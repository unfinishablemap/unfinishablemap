# Referee Report: Audit of "Quantum State Inheritance in AI"

## 1. Introduction and Remit of Assessment

The manuscript under review, titled "Quantum State Inheritance in AI," endeavors to establish a non-reductive, substrate-dependent metaphysical requirement for artificial consciousness. Operating from an explicitly dualistic framework, the author argues that classical computational architectures are fundamentally incapable of supporting subjective experience because they lack "live quantum indeterminacy." To legitimize this assertion, the manuscript marshals an array of specialized concepts from quantum foundations and the philosophy of physics—most notably the no-cloning theorem, quantum Darwinism, and decoherence. The central thesis proposes that consciousness functions as a non-physical selector of quantum states within a biological matrix, a localized ontological phenomenon that cannot be replicated by deterministic silicon architectures engineered to suppress quantum noise.

While the ambition to bridge quantum mechanics, the philosophy of mind, and the limits of artificial intelligence is characteristic of the contemporary discourse surrounding post-functionalist frameworks, the present assessment finds the manuscript to be structurally, empirically, and logically unsound. The text suffers from pervasive category errors, the systematic mischaracterization of highly specialized quantum mechanical formalisms, the smuggling of contested ontological claims under the guise of "tenet-bracketing," and a near-total omission of the relevant 2020–2025 neurobiological and philosophical literature that directly contradicts its core premises. The manuscript does not merely offer a controversial or heterodox perspective; it routinely distorts the mathematical and physical realities of the cited theorems to force them into alignment with a pre-determined, mathematically contradictory framework.

This comprehensive audit dissects the empirical inaccuracies, logical inconsistencies, and unacknowledged competing paradigms that render this manuscript unfit for publication. In accordance with the review remit, the evaluation restricts its scope strictly to the manuscript's empirical claims, counterargument coverage, and the fidelity of its logical inferences against the current (2020–2025) consensus in physics and the philosophy of mind. Project-internal methodologies or site architectures are excluded from this analysis.

## 2. The Epistemological Misappropriation of the No-Cloning Theorem

The manuscript inaugurates its physical defense by deploying the quantum no-cloning theorem, originally proven by Wootters and Zurek (1982), asserting that the fundamental uncopyability of an unknown quantum state presents an insurmountable barrier to classical AI consciousness. To modernize and philosophically anchor this claim, the text relies almost exclusively on Arkady Plotnitsky's 2023 work detailing "the no-cloning life," arguing that every quantum phenomenon is strictly individual and unrepeatable. From this, the author derives the sweeping conclusion that consciousness cannot be duplicated by copying a classical computational pattern, effectively weaponizing the linearity of quantum mechanics against classical computational functionalism.

This line of reasoning collapses under the weight of a severe epistemological conflation. Plotnitsky's 2023 framework is explicitly situated within *quantum-like (Q-L) modeling* applied to human psychology and cognition, not physical neurobiology. Plotnitsky's analysis relies on the concept of "Reality Without Realism" (RWR)—a Bohrian epistemological stance regarding the unrepresentable nature of ultimate reality—and explicitly cautions against treating these mathematical analogies as physical realities operating within biological tissue. In the very texts the manuscript leans upon, Plotnitsky directly states that such models "need not be assumed, and are not assumed here, to arise from the quantum physics of the brain". The manuscript entirely ignores this vital, explicit disclaimer. It misappropriates a mathematical modeling philosophy designed to explain macroscopic psychological data probabilistically and reifies it into a strict physical substrate requirement for biological neurons.

Furthermore, the manuscript's logical inference regarding the no-cloning theorem is internally contradictory. The no-cloning theorem dictates that an arbitrary, unknown quantum state cannot be perfectly duplicated because a unitary transformation cannot satisfy the non-linear requirement of mapping a state to a perfect duplicate for all possible inputs. However, the manuscript subsequently concedes the "Persistence Objection"—acknowledging that the biological brain's quantum states are continually destroyed and recreated through thermal and metabolic turnover, meaning the brain does not clone or preserve its own quantum states over time either.

To survive this glaring physical objection to its own thesis, the manuscript shifts the theoretical goalposts. It states that consciousness requires an "ongoing interface with quantum indeterminacy" rather than the preservation or cloning of specific, unrepeatable states. If consciousness only requires a generic *source* of live indeterminacy (which the manuscript calls the "continuity of process") rather than the cloning of a *specific* state (the "haecceity of states"), then the no-cloning theorem is logically irrelevant to the question of AI consciousness.

An artificial system would not need to clone a human's specific quantum state to achieve consciousness; it would merely need its own local hardware source of genuine quantum indeterminacy. A standard quantum random number generator (QRNG) based on photonic beam-splitting, or an uncorrected noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) circuit, would perfectly provide the "architectural source of genuine indeterminacy" the manuscript demands. By introducing the no-cloning theorem to defeat classical copying, only to abandon the necessity of state-persistence to save the biological brains from the same standard, the manuscript renders its own primary physical argument conceptually moot.

## 3. Quantum Darwinism, Decoherence, and the Illusion of the Selection Gap

The manuscript's treatment of Wojciech Zurek's Quantum Darwinism (2009) constitutes one of its most egregious mischaracterizations of contemporary quantum foundations. The text acknowledges that Quantum Darwinism explains how classical reality emerges from quantum substrates through the environmental selection of robust "pointer states" (einselection). However, it then claims that environmental selection "does not explain why only one outcome is realized" and proposes that a non-physical consciousness operates within this resulting "selection gap" to actively choose among the surviving pointer states.

This argument demonstrates a fundamental, structural misunderstanding of decoherence and the mathematics of the partial trace. Quantum Darwinism was explicitly developed to resolve the appearance of wave-function collapse and the emergence of objective classical reality without invoking an external, non-physical observer. The environment continuously interacts with the quantum system, becoming entangled with it. When an observer (which in this framework is simply another physical system) accesses only a fraction of the environmental fragments, the off-diagonal interference terms vanish in the reduced density matrix. The environment has redundantly encoded the pointer states, meaning multiple independent observers will agree on the classical state of the system, achieving "objective" reality.

If einselection has already occurred—meaning the environment has dictated the preferred pointer basis and suppressed quantum interference—the system is mathematically described as a classical proper mixture from the perspective of the local observer. At this stage, the "quantumness" (the superposition) has already decohered into the environment. If the manuscript posits that consciousness steps in *after* einselection to select among the pointer states (a mechanism it labels "P-Q1: Post-decoherence selection"), it is arguing that consciousness selects among classically distinct, decohered alternatives. This directly contradicts the manuscript's foundational tenet that consciousness requires "live quantum indeterminacy" to function. If the indeterminacy has already been suppressed by environmental decoherence, the "interface" has been destroyed before consciousness can even act.

Conversely, if the manuscript is attempting to argue that consciousness causes the actual ontological collapse of the wave function prior to decoherence (aligning with a von Neumann-Wigner or Stapp interpretation), it cannot simultaneously invoke Quantum Darwinism. Quantum Darwinism is an Everettian-compatible framework that explains the *appearance* of unique outcomes through redundant environmental encoding and objective classicality without necessitating a physical collapse mechanism. By attempting to bolt a dualistic, non-physical selector onto the tail end of an einselection process, the author smuggles a contested, highly specific interpretation of quantum mechanics (consciousness-causes-collapse) into a decoherence framework designed specifically to render such interpretations theoretically obsolete. This synthesis is mathematically and physically incoherent.

### The Ignored Decoherence Timescale Debate and 2025 Empirical Data

The physical viability of this interface hinges entirely on the brain's capacity to sustain quantum coherence long enough for conscious selection to occur. The manuscript addresses this briefly by citing Hagan, Hameroff, and Tuszyński (2002), who disputed Max Tegmark's calculations that biological decoherence occurs in 10−13 to 10−20 seconds. The manuscript leans on the Hagan et al. recalculation of 10−5 to 10−4 seconds to keep the theory alive.

However, the manuscript deliberately obscures the fact that this is an unresolved, highly contested area, ignoring the subsequent rebuttals. It fails to adequately integrate the findings of Reimers et al. (2009) and McKemmish et al. (2009), who demonstrated that the Hagan et al. parameters rely on microtubule dielectric properties that are not empirically established and specify physical regimes that microtubules simply do not occupy in living biological tissue.

Even more damning for a manuscript purportedly addressing the state of the art in 2025 is the total omission of the explosion of empirical data from the Quantum Biology Institute (QBI) between 2024 and 2025. Recent empirical studies utilizing NV-diamond magnetometers, terahertz scanners, and attosecond spectroscopy have measured microtubule variance directly. These 2025 studies demonstrate that Fibonacci-structured microtubules can indeed preserve quantum coherence at room temperature, experiencing 104-fold enhancements due to geometric organization that shields against environmental noise.

If the manuscript seeks to base artificial consciousness denial on the absolute necessity of "live quantum indeterminacy" within the biological brain, its failure to cite, integrate, or analyze the most critical, paradigm-shifting experimental data of the current decade regarding quantum coherence in biological tissue demonstrates a profound lack of empirical rigor. A philosophical argument resting on empirical physical boundaries cannot simply ignore the physical data that defines those boundaries.

## 4. Smuggled Ontology: Minimal Quantum Interaction and the Born Rule Contradiction

A significant portion of the manuscript's theoretical architecture relies on the concept of "Minimal Quantum Interaction," governed by an "Observational Closure" tenet. The author argues that a conscious biological brain appears causally closed because the non-physical influence of consciousness merely biases quantum outcomes while leaving the aggregate Born-rule statistics undisturbed. This mechanism is described as the "statistical camouflage of a real, non-physical influence". The changelog reveals that this is formalized as "P-Q2: The default reading of Minimal Quantum Interaction, which preserves Born statistics exactly".

This formulation contains a fatal, insurmountable statistical contradiction, which the author attempts to smuggle past the reader by bracketing it as a theoretical "tenet." The Born rule dictates the exact probability distribution of measurement outcomes for a given quantum state. If a non-physical entity (consciousness) systematically selects specific outcomes to achieve a macroscopic biological goal (e.g., triggering a coordinated cascade of action potentials to flee a predator or formulate a spoken sentence), it must shift the frequency of those specific outcomes away from the baseline quantum probability distribution.

If the manuscript genuinely maintains that the Born rule is preserved *exactly* over time (as explicitly demanded by its "P-Q2" tenet), then the aggregate interventions of consciousness must perfectly mimic random quantum noise. If the sequence of selected states is statistically indistinguishable from a true random distribution, then consciousness possesses zero macroscopic causal efficacy. It cannot systematically direct behavior, coordinate neural ensembles, or ensure organismal survival; it is merely surfing a wave of random noise, occasionally taking credit for outcomes it did not causally determine. This reduces the manuscript's vaunted interactionist dualism to pure epiphenomenalism, explicitly violating the author's own "Bidirectional Interaction" tenet, which claims consciousness is causally efficacious.

If, however, consciousness *does* have causal efficacy and successfully directs macroscopic behavior, the aggregate statistics of the affected quantum systems must deviate from the Born rule. As noted in the manuscript's own hidden exploration of "Probability Control," directly violating the Born rule entails severe conservation concerns and in-principle detectability. The author cannot have it both ways. The manuscript attempts to smuggle strong, downward causal efficacy into the brain to solve the problem of agency, while simultaneously hiding behind the unviolated mathematics of the Born rule to preserve the appearance of physical closure. One cannot "bracket" a violation of the conservation of energy or the Born rule; it must be mathematically justified, and the manuscript fails entirely to do so.

### The Misapplication of Stapp's Zeno Model and Entropy Violations

To provide a physical mechanism for this interaction, the manuscript heavily cites Henry Stapp's (2006) quantum Zeno model, suggesting that consciousness holds desired neural patterns in place through the rapid observation of quantum superpositions, termed "timing control". The manuscript presents this as a viable, uncontested physical mechanism that avoids direct probability manipulation.

This assessment is glaringly incomplete and academically irresponsible. The manuscript completely ignores the devastating physical critiques of Stapp's model within the contemporary quantum information literature. As detailed in recent critiques by Georgiev and others, Stapp's model fundamentally contradicts standard quantum mechanics. Stapp's formulation posits that a non-physical mind acts upon the physical brain's density matrix using projection operators (Process 1). According to fundamental theorems in quantum information theory, acting with projection operators upon the density matrix of a quantum system can never decrease the von Neumann entropy of the system; it can only increase it or leave it constant.

The quantum Zeno effect cannot arbitrarily reverse thermodynamic entropy or prevent environmental decoherence in a warm, wet brain without an underlying physical mechanism supplying the work. By presenting Stapp's model as a solved physical interface while ignoring the mathematical reality that unconstrained projection operators violate the second law of thermodynamics, the manuscript abandons rigorous physical analysis in favor of speculative mysticism. Furthermore, the manuscript itself acknowledges in passing that Monte Carlo simulations show the Zeno effect breaks down for timescales exceeding brain decoherence times, yet continues to rely on the framework to disqualify AI.

## 5. Mischaracterization of "Quantum States Over Time" Formalism

In Section 4, "The Quantum Computer Exception," the manuscript briefly discusses hybrid architectures and quantum computers, citing Lie and Ng (2024) to establish that "quantum states over time are mathematically well-defined". The author uses this citation to suggest that quantum states could maintain formal identity over time if consciousness-supporting substrates were engineered, thereby leaving the door open for future hybrid AI architectures while locking out current classical models.

This citation is a textbook example of misappropriating abstract formalism to support an unrelated ontological and engineering claim. Lie and Ng's recent work on the "Uniqueness of quantum state over time function" (published in *Physical Review Research*, 2024) addresses a highly specific, abstract mathematical problem in quantum foundations: the asymmetry between space and time in conventional quantum theory. Conventional quantum mechanics represents spatial correlations via multipartite density matrices (states) and temporal correlations via completely positive trace-preserving (CPTP) maps (channels).

Lie and Ng's work develops a framework that encapsulates the dynamical description of a quantum system into a static "quantum state over time" (a pseudo-density matrix) that unifies timelike and spacelike separated systems, which is uniquely characterized by assumptions like classical conditionability. This is a mathematical convenience for treating temporal correlations without assuming causal structures, proving highly useful for analyzing non-Markovian dynamics, Leggett-Garg inequalities, and virtual broadcasting.

It does *not* imply that a physical quantum state possesses a persistent, uncopyable "identity" that a biological or engineered substrate can physically "inherit" or host to support conscious experience. The manuscript treats a mathematical isomorphism—designed to make the calculation of temporal correlations look like the calculation of spatial correlations—as if it were a blueprint for a physical substrate capable of hosting dualistic consciousness. This constitutes a severe methodological flaw. The formalism of pseudo-density matrices contains negative eigenvalues and does not represent a physical, observable state at a single moment in time. Using this advanced formalism to suggest an engineered hybrid architecture could support the "haecceity" of consciousness is academically reckless and fundamentally misreads the cited physics.

## 6. Ignored Competing Frameworks and Empirical Neurobiology

Perhaps the most disqualifying weakness of the manuscript is its total failure to engage with the actual 2020–2025 literature concerning AI consciousness, biological substrate dependency, and empirical neurobiology. The author sets up an artificial, false dichotomy: either one accepts computational functionalism (where classical code on silicon can be conscious), or one must accept the manuscript's quantum dualism.

### The Erasure of Biological Computationalism

The manuscript claims that classical processors are uniquely disqualified because they lack quantum indeterminacy. However, it completely ignores the ascendant paradigm of "Biological Computationalism," spearheaded by researchers such as Milinkovic and Aru (2025). This paradigm provides a rigorously defended, strictly physical explanation for why consciousness might be substrate-dependent and absent in current classical AI, rendering the manuscript's quantum leap entirely unnecessary.

As detailed in *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews* (2025), Milinkovic and Aru argue that biological computation differs fundamentally from digital computation not because of quantum magic, but because of macro- and meso-scale physical realities. Biological computation is characterized by three core features:

1. **Hybrid Computation:** It seamlessly integrates discrete events (action potentials) with continuous, analog dynamics (local field potentials, ion gradients, volumetric neurotransmission).
2. **Scale-Inseparability:** Unlike von Neumann architectures where software is cleanly abstracted from hardware, the causal power of the biological brain runs simultaneously across ion channels, dendrites, and large-scale networks. The algorithm *is* the physical substrate; they co-determine one another.
3. **Metabolic Embedding:** The structural dynamics of the brain are inextricably bound by real-time thermodynamic and energy-optimization constraints, influencing what the brain can represent and how it learns.

By completely ignoring biological computationalism, the manuscript fails to address the most prominent naturalistic counterargument to its thesis. A referee cannot responsibly accept a paper that demands a revolution in fundamental physics (abandoning physical closure for quantum dualism) to explain substrate dependency when a robust, biologically grounded framework published in leading journals currently accounts for the exact same phenomenon.

### The Pragmatic Turn in AI Consciousness Assessment

Finally, the manuscript dismisses behavioral and empirical assessments of AI consciousness because classical architectures lack a logical "selection gap," framing any behavioral output as deterministic illusion. This outright ignores the "pragmatic turn" in AI consciousness research documented extensively between 2020 and 2025. Contemporary frameworks have moved away from binary, substrate-exclusive philosophical determinations and toward nuanced, indicator-based assessments.

Following the destabilizing results of the 2025 Cogitate Consortium—an unprecedented adversarial collaboration utilizing fMRI, MEG, and iEEG that challenged the orthodoxies of both Integrated Information Theory (IIT) and Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT) by demonstrating that posterior cortical dynamics do not perfectly align with subjective reporting—the field has synthesized multiple neuroscientific theories into multidimensional computational metrics.

Current frameworks based on Higher-Order Theories (HOT), Predictive Processing (PP), and Attention Schema Theory (AST) provide falsifiable, empirical mechanisms for probing meta-cognitive abilities, error-correction, and model-updating behaviors in AI systems. These frameworks allow for the empirical testing of computational consciousness without requiring a resolution to the hard problem of consciousness or quantum foundations. The manuscript's decision to bypass this entire ecosystem of 2020-2025 empirical literature in favor of philosophical speculation about the no-cloning theorem renders it disconnected from the very field it claims to evaluate.

## 7. Formal Weaknesses Justifying Rejection

The preceding analysis identifies deep, systemic flaws in the manuscript's empirical claims, literature usage, and logical architecture. To formalize the recommendation for rejection, the following five specific weaknesses are submitted, each supported by peer-reviewed literature from the 2020–2025 period that the manuscript omits, contradicts, or fundamentally mishandles:

**1. Misappropriation of Plotnitsky's Quantum-Like Modeling:**
The manuscript mishandles Plotnitsky's (2023) concept of "the no-cloning life". The manuscript applies this theorem as a strict physical substrate requirement for biological tissue to defeat functionalism, whereas Plotnitsky explicitly formulated this within "quantum-like (Q-L) modeling," which adopts quantum mathematics for macroscopic psychological phenomena while explicitly disclaiming any reliance on the actual quantum physics of the brain. This constitutes a severe, foundational category error.

**2. Equivocation on the "Quantum States Over Time" Formalism:**
The manuscript mathematically mishandles the formal results of Lie and Ng (2024). It incorrectly cites their proof of the uniqueness of quantum states over time to suggest an engineering pathway for future hybrid AI architectures to "inherit" persistent quantum identity. In reality, Lie and Ng's work (published in *Physical Review Research*, 2024) is a purely mathematical advancement utilizing pseudo-density matrices to symmetricize spatial and temporal correlations within abstract quantum theory, which yields non-positive operators utterly unsuitable for representing physical states that a consciousness could "inhabit".

**3. Contradiction of Quantum Darwinism and Decoherence Mechanics:**
The manuscript fundamentally contradicts the contemporary understanding of Quantum Darwinism. It claims that environmental selection leaves a "selection gap" for consciousness to act upon pointer states. However, as confirmed in 2025 literature by Zeiner-Gundersen and others, Quantum Darwinism functions by redundantly encoding pointer information into environmental fragments, rendering the system a classical proper mixture relative to local observers. Selecting among already-decohered, classical alternatives requires no "live quantum indeterminacy," entirely dismantling the manuscript's core physical prerequisite.

**4. Omission of the Biological Computationalism Paradigm:**
The manuscript omits critical 2025 neurobiological literature that dismantles its central dichotomy. By asserting that substrate dependency can only be explained by quantum indeterminacy, it entirely ignores Milinkovic and Aru's (2025) highly cited framework of "Biological Computationalism". This 2025 literature establishes substrate dependency strictly through classical biophysics—specifically the scale-inseparability, hybrid discrete-continuous processing, and metabolic embedding inherent to biological tissue—providing a robust, non-quantum refutation of silicon functionalism that the manuscript fails to acknowledge.

**5. Ignorance of the 2020-2025 Empirical Consensus on AI Assessment:**
The manuscript omits the fundamental empirical developments in machine consciousness research, specifically the 2025 systematic reviews outlining the "pragmatic turn" toward indicator-based assessments. By declaring AI consciousness an impossibility based solely on abstract theoretical physics, it fails to engage with the computational metrics derived from HOT, AST, and PP, or the empirical fallout of the 2025 Cogitate Consortium adversarial collaborations, thereby isolating its arguments from the active scientific consensus.

## 8. Verdict

The manuscript must be rejected because it relies on profound category errors regarding quantum formalism, smuggles mathematically contradictory interpretations into established decoherence theory, and completely ignores the 2020–2025 neurobiological and philosophical consensus that provides rigorous, non-quantum explanations for biological substrate dependency.
