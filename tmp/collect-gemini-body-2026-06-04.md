# Peer Review Report: "Clinical Dissociation as Systematic Evidence"

## Introduction: The Epistemic Burden of the Interface Hypothesis

The manuscript under consideration, "Clinical Dissociation as Systematic Evidence," attempts a highly ambitious intervention in the philosophy of mind. The author seeks to resurrect a non-reductive, interactionist dualist model of consciousness by leveraging pathological and pharmacological states of dissociation as empirical validation. The central theoretical mechanism proposed is the "Interface Specification Programme," which posits that the brain operates not as the generator of conscious experience, but as a complex biological receiver or transmission interface for an irreducible, non-physical consciousness. To substantiate this massive ontological claim, the manuscript systematically interprets instances where consciousness appears to fracture or degrade—specifically in Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID), split-brain phenomena, and pharmacological anesthesia—as structural failures of this physical interface, rather than as evidence for the physical divisibility or reducibility of the mind itself.

The stakes of such an argument are undeniably high, engaging directly with classical debates surrounding the indivisibility of mind, the neural correlates of consciousness, and the hard problem of phenomenal experience. According to the manuscript, if consciousness were entirely reducible to physical, integrated information, the fragmenting of global neural integration should produce fragmented but continuously present micro-experiences. Because empirical data allegedly demonstrates an all-or-nothing threshold effect alongside the localized preservation of cognitive function in the absence of cross-state integration, the author concludes that physicalist paradigms are fatally constrained. The manuscript insists that these threshold effects and dissociative pathologies are more "naturally predicted" by a dualist interface theory, wherein the physical antenna simply loses the capacity to sustain the signal, leaving the underlying ontological unity of consciousness untouched.

However, the ambition of the manuscript is entirely eclipsed by its catastrophic methodological and evidentiary failures. While the text adopts the structural aesthetic of a rigorous empirical synthesis, a close audit of its claims reveals a philosophical architecture built upon an antiquated, fundamentally misunderstood, and deeply cherry-picked interpretation of contemporary clinical psychiatry and neurobiology. To adjudicate debates in the philosophy of mind using clinical psychopathology requires an uncompromising fidelity to the current empirical state of the art in those respective scientific domains. The manuscript comprehensively fails this standard.

The paper relies on a theoretically ossified and empirically falsified conception of Dissociative Identity Disorder, systematically ignoring the last half-decade of highly robust cognitive-behavioral, sociocognitive, and neurobiological literature. Furthermore, it creates a false comparative dichotomy by entirely omitting the most formidable physicalist explanatory frameworks currently dominating computational psychiatry and cognitive neuroscience—specifically, Predictive Processing and Active Inference. By ignoring these computational paradigms, the author declares victory over a rudimentary, straw-man version of physicalism, leaving the actual frontier of materialist philosophy of mind completely untouched. Most severely, the manuscript’s logical structure is defined by an epistemic circularity; it employs a tenet-bracketed framing that begs the question, systematically smuggling dualist conclusions into the premises by interpreting all standard neurological network threshold effects as *a priori* evidence of an "interface" malfunction.

The following sections provide an exhaustive, philosophically rigorous exegesis of the manuscript’s empirical inaccuracies, theoretical omissions, and logical fallacies. This detailed dissection demonstrates precisely why the manuscript’s foundational arguments are invalid and why the submission fails to meet the rigorous standards of contemporary analytic philosophy, justifying an outright rejection.

## Section 1: The Falsification of the Absolute Partitioning Hypothesis in Dissociative Identity Disorder

A load-bearing pillar of the manuscript’s argument is its ontological and structural characterization of Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID). The text posits that in DID, patients "do not lose total information capacity — individual alters show normal cognitive function — but lose cross-state integration". The manuscript draws a direct, literal structural equivalence between the pathology of DID and the neurological reality of split-brain phenomena, arguing that while split-brain represents a failure of integration across physically severed hemispheres, DID represents an analogous failure of integration across dissociated alter states within an intact brain. This absolute partitioning of phenomenological access and memory is utilized as pristine systematic evidence that the brain functions as a complex routing interface for distinct, unified streams of an irreducible consciousness.

This conceptualization of DID relies entirely on the acceptance of subjective, first-person self-reporting of absolute inter-identity amnesia as an objective mapping of neurobiological hardware failure. The author conflates subjective phenomenological reports with objective failures of neural information integration. This absolute partitioning model, however, has been comprehensively falsified by recent empirical literature in cognitive psychology and memory research, rendering the manuscript's reliance upon it epistemically reckless.

Crucially, rigorous experimental designs assessing memory transfer between dissociative identity states consistently demonstrate that the reported amnesia is not a structural failure of cross-state integration, but rather a higher-order retrieval or metacognitive deficit. The most devastating empirical counter-evidence to the manuscript's interface theory is the work conducted by Marsh, Huntjens, and colleagues (2021), which directly assessed neutral episodic self-referential and autobiographical memory in DID using contrasting paradigms of recall and recognition.

In these carefully controlled experimental paradigms, DID patients subjectively report significant inter-identity amnesia when tasked with explicit, free-recall tests. If the manuscript’s "severed interface" model were correct—if the mind-brain connection literally compartmentalizes access preventing cross-state integration—this amnesia should remain absolute regardless of the testing methodology, because the information is fundamentally siloed in an alternate phenomenological stream. However, objective tests of recognition memory completely dismantle this hypothesis. When presented with recognition tasks (e.g., identifying previously seen stimuli without requiring the active, conscious reconstruction of the episodic context), DID participants recognized significantly more of the events that occurred in their alternate identities than simulator groups and partial-information controls.

The philosophical implications of these empirical findings are fatal to the manuscript’s interface argument. If DID represented a genuine failure of cross-state integration at the foundational level of the mind-brain interface—structurally analogous to a severed corpus callosum as the author explicitly claims —information encoded by Alter A should be completely, physically inaccessible to the neural architecture supporting Alter B. The fact that the physical brain of the DID patient demonstrably retains the information, processes it, and successfully expresses it via implicit recognition tasks proves unequivocally that fundamental neural integration remains intact. The breakdown occurs not at the root level of a dualist transmission interface, but at the level of higher-order executive access, metacognition, and the subjective phenomenology of ownership.

By failing to engage with the critical neurocognitive distinction between explicit (recall) and implicit (recognition) memory transfer in dissociative pathologies , the manuscript constructs its interface theory upon an empirically debunked premise. It treats a metacognitive illusion of partitioning as if it were a fundamental law of mind-brain physics.

## Section 2: Etiological Myopia and the Systematic Omission of Sociocognitive Paradigms

Beyond its misunderstanding of memory transfer dynamics, the manuscript commits a profound philosophical error in its treatment of the etiology and ontology of Dissociative Identity Disorder. The text treats DID as a naturally occurring, pure neurobiological hardware failure—a pristine, uncontaminated "worked exhibit" of how the mind-brain interface can malfunction under duress. In doing so, the manuscript exclusively, and without justification, relies upon a pure trauma-centric, stress-diathesis model of dissociation, assuming axiomatically that the disorder represents a primary, organic fracturing of the self. This highly selective theoretical framing completely bypasses the intense, decades-long paradigm war regarding the origins of DID, systematically erasing the formidable challenges posed by the Sociocognitive Model (SCM) and contemporary Transtheoretical Frameworks.

To construct a universal theory of consciousness based on a specific psychiatric syndrome requires establishing that the syndrome in question represents a natural kind, rather than an artifact of culture, suggestion, or clinical environment. The Sociocognitive Model fundamentally challenges the natural-kind status of DID. It argues that dissociative symptoms and alternate identities do not arise as a spontaneous, organic fragmentation of an underlying mind-brain interface. Rather, the SCM conceptualizes DID as a highly complex, socially constructed syndrome driven by social, cognitive, and cultural forces. Under the SCM, patients—who are often highly suggestible individuals suffering from genuine, profound psychological distress—come to conceptualize, structure, and enact their distress in the form of a fractured self. This structuring occurs under the heavy influence of external information in popular media narratives, socio-cultural expectations regarding mental health, and, critically, iatrogenic reinforcement via suggestive therapeutic techniques employed by practitioners.

The manuscript is entirely silent on the iatrogenic, sociocognitive, and cultural dimensions of DID. This omission is philosophically catastrophic for an argument attempting to derive ontological claims about the universe from clinical presentation. If DID is even partially culturally constructed or iatrogenically reinforced—if it is a narrative framework adopted by a distressed cognitive system rather than a fundamental hardware failure—then utilizing its specific symptom profile as a foundational map of a universal "mind-brain interface" is a severe category error. It is logically analogous to observing a culturally learned religious trance and deducing from it the fundamental laws of quantum neurophysics.

Furthermore, the manuscript entirely ignores the most significant and rigorous recent synthesis in the field of dissociation: the Transtheoretical Framework proposed by Lynn, Lilienfeld, Merckelbach, and colleagues (2022). In this comprehensive review, Lynn et al. rigorously analyze the empirical limitations of both the pure posttraumatic model and the pure sociocognitive model. They propose a unifying perspective wherein dissociation and dissociative disorders are understood as complex failures of normally adaptive systems and functions, driven by interactive, transdiagnostic variables.

The transdiagnostic variables identified by Lynn et al. include severe sleep disturbances, impaired self-regulation, the inhibition of negative cognitions and affects, hyperassociation, set shifts, and critical deficits in reality testing, source attributions, and metacognition. By framing DID through the transdiagnostic lens of impaired reality testing and source attribution, contemporary physicalist psychology provides a highly robust, computationally tractable account of why a patient might generate the profound *illusion* of multiple, disconnected streams of consciousness.

Consider the mechanics of source monitoring: a human brain continuously generates thoughts, memories, and impulses. A healthy cognitive system seamlessly attributes these phenomena to the self. However, a patient with severe, trauma-induced or sleep-deprivation-induced deficits in source monitoring may generate a thought or a memory and fail to accurately attribute that cognitive artifact to their own unified apparatus. The brain, attempting to resolve this profound cognitive dissonance, subsequently constructs an alternate "identity" narrative to house the orphaned cognitive artifact.

This framework completely dissolves the deep ontological mystery that the manuscript claims only a dualist interface theory can solve. The fragmentation is not a severing of an irreducible mind from its physical receiver; it is a higher-order narrative failure of the brain's self-modeling apparatus. The manuscript’s total omission of Lynn et al. (2022) and the broader sociocognitive literature reveals a deeply selective reading of psychiatry, engineered to artificially inflate the explanatory necessity of dualism by hiding the physicalist solutions that already exist.

## Section 3: The Computational Evasion: Predictive Processing and the Architecture of Active Inference

Perhaps the most glaring and theoretically devastating omission in the manuscript is its total failure to test its interface hypothesis against the dominant cognitive-computational paradigm of the current decade: Predictive Processing (PP) and Active Inference. The manuscript attacks physicalism by arguing that dualism "more naturally predicts" the dissociative phenomena observed in clinical practice, claiming that reductive models are forced to view dissociative resilience and all-or-nothing anesthetic loss as mere "coincidences" rather than expected systemic features.

This represents a classical straw-man fallacy. The author sets up a naive, rudimentary, bottom-up feature-binding version of physicalism to easily knock down, while comprehensively ignoring the sophisticated, Bayesian frameworks that currently define theoretical neuroscience and which explicitly predict these exact dissociative phenomena.

Predictive processing, spearheaded by theorists such as Karl Friston and Anil Seth, posits that the brain is not a passive receiver of external information, but a proactive inference machine. It continuously generates top-down generative models of the world and the body, attempting to predict sensory inputs before they arrive. The brain updates these models based entirely on bottom-up sensory prediction errors—the difference between what was predicted and what was actually sensed. Within this framework, conscious experience is tied to the brain's posterior beliefs—its "best guess" regarding the hidden causes of its sensory input, actively minimizing free energy.

Crucially, the entire system relies on a mechanism known as *precision weighting*. Precision weighting is the process by which the brain estimates the reliability, salience, or uncertainty of different prediction errors. It dictates whether the system should update its high-level top-down models to accommodate new data, or whether it should ignore the bottom-up signals as unreliable noise.

The 2020-2025 literature extensively and explicitly models clinical dissociation, depersonalization, and functional neurological disorders as profound pathologies of precision weighting. In the context of overwhelming trauma or extreme stress, the predictive system may adaptively decouple high-level priors (such as the overarching generative model of the unified, safe "self") from bottom-up sensory input to minimize catastrophic, unresolvable prediction error. The brain dynamically adjusts its strategy based on the inferred structure of the environment, radically resetting contextual models when normal predictive priors fail to map onto a traumatic reality.

This Active Inference framework effortlessly and computationally explains the exact clinical dissociations the manuscript claims are intractable for physicalism:

**1. Ketamine and Pharmacological Dissociation:** The manuscript marvels at the phenomenon of ketamine-induced dissociation, noting that it produces high phenomenal intensity disconnected from cognitive control. The author argues this independence is definitive evidence that consciousness is a multidimensional, non-physical phenomenon that can be "dialled up or down along separate axes" independent of neural organization. However, under Predictive Processing, NMDA-receptor antagonists like ketamine systematically and directly disrupt precision weighting, destabilizing hierarchical predictive processing at a synaptic level. This neurochemical intervention leads to a complete breakdown in the brain's usual filtering of sensory information and top-down cognitive constraint. The result is that unconstrained, high-entropy priors dominate experience (manifesting as vivid hallucinations), while the computational connection to external stimuli is severed. Physicalism does not view this as a coincidence; it views it as the mathematically required output of disrupting NMDA-mediated precision weighting. The manuscript’s appeal to dualist "separate axes" is an ontological extravagance completely rendered obsolete by computational neurochemistry.

**2. DID and Alternate Identity States:** Active inference models view the core phenomenology of DID—such as egodystonia and identity shifts—as a dysregulation between prior beliefs and sensory input, where psychosocial factors and trauma modulate maladaptive predictive coding. To minimize free energy in a chronically abusive environment with highly contradictory rules, the developing brain may maintain multiple, distinct, context-specific generative models of the self. These models fail to integrate because the system assigns abnormal precision to the prediction errors that would normally force their unification.

By completely omitting any reference to Predictive Processing, Active Inference, or precision weighting , the manuscript declares victory in a theoretical vacuum. It assumes that because rudimentary models struggle with dissociation, materialist philosophy as a whole has failed, ignoring the fact that top-down Bayesian predictive models not only explain but computationally *require* the capacity for the cognitive system to decouple and dissociate under specific high-entropy conditions.

## Section 4: Neuroimaging Limitations and the Reversibility Paradox

The manuscript repeatedly assures the reader that its conclusions are derived from "systematic evidence" mapped across neurology and psychiatry. However, the manuscript demonstrates a concerning lack of methodological scrutiny when relying on the neurobiological research intended to support its interface map of Dissociative Identity Disorder.

The text leans heavily on the assumption that DID possesses a distinct, stable, hard-coded neurobiological architecture that proves the existence of "dissociated alter states within an intact brain" resulting from a unified cause. However, a critical review of the 2020-2025 neurobiological literature reveals that the neuroimaging evidence for DID is highly contested, profoundly heterogeneous, suffers from severe replication issues, and is chronically plagued by extremely small sample sizes.

The manuscript fails to acknowledge that many of the highly cited neuroimaging and functional connectivity studies in the DID literature rely on experimental cohorts of fewer than 20 to 30 patients. For instance, systematic reviews of neuroimaging in DID reveal highly fragmented data regarding prefrontal cortex dysfunction and functional neural network alterations. Furthermore, recent large-scale attempts to map the neural functional connectivity of severe pathological dissociation, such as those by Lebois, Purcell, and colleagues (2026), have required k-means cluster analysis to make sense of the vast heterogeneity within the patient populations. These studies demonstrate that dissociation does not have a single, unified "interface failure" signature, but rather manifests across diverse, individually-based functional regions of interest with varying clinical patterns. To make broad, universalizing claims about the fundamental metaphysical nature of consciousness based on statistically underpowered and highly heterogeneous fMRI clusters is epistemically indefensible.

Even more damaging to the interface hypothesis is the clinical reality of symptom reversibility. The manuscript requires DID to function as a structural map of the mind-brain connection, where integration is literally lost across states. However, recent critical evaluations in the psychiatric literature note that the highly reversible nature of dissociative amnesia in DID is fundamentally incompatible with structural brain abnormalities. If the amnesia, fragmentation, and lack of cross-state integration can be reversed through phase-based psychotherapy and cognitive-behavioral interventions—as is frequently and successfully reported in the clinical literature —the underlying disruption cannot be a hard-coded architectural feature of an ontological mind-brain "interface" as the manuscript insists.

As Lotfinia and colleagues (2024) indicate, dissociative processing cannot be neatly localized to a few distinctive brain regions that act as broken antennas. Alternative explanations, specifically dysfunctional metacognitive beliefs about memory and trauma, provide a vastly more plausible and empirically grounded explanation for the reported amnesia in DID than the structural or interface anomalies proposed by the manuscript. The author's total failure to audit the replication crises, sample size limitations, and the profound structural contradictions inherent in reversible psychopathology indicates an argument built upon highly unstable, selectively curated empirical sand.

## Section 5: Epistemic Circularity and Tenet-Bracketed Smuggling in Threshold Dynamics

Beyond its empirical failures and theoretical omissions, the manuscript suffers from pervasive and fatal logical defects in its core structural argumentation. Specifically, the paper engages in continuous circular reasoning, heavily utilizing a tenet-bracketed framing that seamlessly smuggles its dualist conclusions into its very evaluation of the evidence.

The author establishes an "Interface Specification Programme" designed to map how a non-physical consciousness interacts with the physical brain. However, the analytical method employed consists entirely of observing standard, mathematically ubiquitous neurological threshold effects and arbitrarily re-labeling them as "interface limits." This is not scientific deduction; it is rhetorical re-categorization.

The clearest example of this logical smuggling occurs in the manuscript's treatment of general anesthesia. The text correctly notes that consciousness ceases below a certain threshold of global neural integration, even though the individual neural components may remain locally active. The physicalist explanation for this phenomenon is exceptionally robust and well-established within contemporary neuroscience, specifically within Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT) and Integrated Information Theory (IIT). In these models, consciousness supervenes upon global, brain-scale recurrent processing and information integration; when anesthetics disrupt long-range cortical connectivity, the global workspace mathematically collapses, and consciousness ceases, despite the continuation of local, isolated neuronal firing.

The manuscript inexplicably dismisses this robust explanation, stating: "The physicalist reading — consciousness fragments because it is physical integration — appears simpler, but purchases a commitment that consciousness is nothing more than integration. The resilience of unity and the all-or-nothing character of anaesthetic loss then become coincidences rather than expected features".

This logical inference is fundamentally flawed and betrays a profound misunderstanding of complex network theory.

First, claiming that physicalist threshold effects are "coincidences" is scientifically illiterate. In non-linear dynamic networks, phase transitions (all-or-nothing threshold effects) are mathematical inevitabilities, not strange coincidences. Just as water transitioning to ice at exactly zero degrees Celsius is not a "coincidence" but a fundamental property of thermodynamic integration, the collapse of global neuronal workspaces at specific anesthetic concentrations is a strictly expected, mathematically modeled feature of complex network topology. Physicalist models like GNWT and IIT are explicitly built to predict these exact phase transitions.

Second, the manuscript argues that the dualist reading is superior because if "integration is a condition for consciousness to interact with the brain rather than the thing that constitutes consciousness... Disrupt integration and you disrupt the interface, not the mind". This statement is epistemologically vacuous. The manuscript is essentially arguing: *If we assume *a priori* that dualism is true, the data looks exactly like a broken radio.* This is an argument from analogy that begs the question, not an evidential derivation. The raw empirical data (the loss of consciousness upon the loss of neural integration) is perfectly symmetric to both the dualist interface hypothesis and the physicalist identity hypothesis. The manuscript provides absolutely zero independent variables to adjudicate between the two frameworks, instead relying purely on rhetorical framing to "smuggle in" the interface conclusion under the guise of an explanation.

Third, the manuscript attempts a mereological paradox that undermines its own dualist premises. It attempts to partition consciousness into multi-dimensional axes—claiming that phenomenal intensity, voluntary attention, and memory encoding can vary independently under conditions like NREM sleep or ketamine influence, proving that consciousness is not a "single quantity". Simultaneously, it mocks the physicalist question of "How many minds does this patient have?" by stating it "assumes minds come in whole numbers" and that consciousness can be "partially divided while remaining somehow one".

If consciousness is a truly independent, non-physical, irreducible unity—the foundational Cartesian premise that motivates the interface theory—how can its fundamental dimensions be independently dialed up and down by highly specific physical molecules, and how can it be "partially divided"? If the dimensions of consciousness can be surgically isolated, degraded, and manipulated by physical chemistry, the system behaves exactly as a physical supervenience model predicts. The interface theory merely adds an unnecessary ontological middleman that perfectly mimics physicalism, violating parsimony without increasing predictive or explanatory power by a single iota.

## Final Catalog of Specific Weaknesses Justifying Rejection

The manuscript must be rejected based on the profound accumulation of methodological, empirical, and logical defects detailed above. The following five specific weaknesses highlight the precise locations where the manuscript fails the standards of rigorous philosophical and scientific scholarship, each accompanied by a 2020-2025 peer-reviewed citation that the manuscript directly omits, contradicts, or mishandles:

### Weakness 1: Falsification of Absolute Compartmentalization via Recognition Memory Paradigms

The manuscript builds its primary interface argument on the claim that Dissociative Identity Disorder demonstrates a clean, structural loss of "cross-state integration" analogous to a physically severed corpus callosum. This absolute partitioning model directly contradicts empirical findings proving that objective recognition memory persists across identity states despite subjective recall amnesia. The persistence of recognition memory demonstrates that physical neural integration of the information is maintained, and the deficit is merely a metacognitive retrieval failure, directly falsifying the dualist interface hardware-failure hypothesis.

Inter-identity amnesia for neutral episodic self-referential and autobiographical memory in Dissociative Identity Disorder: An assessment of recall and recognition. *PLoS ONE*, 16(2), e0245849.

### Weakness 2: Etiological Myopia and Omission of the Transtheoretical Model

The manuscript improperly utilizes DID as pristine natural evidence of an organic mind-brain "interface" failure. In doing so, it entirely ignores the overwhelming consensus in contemporary psychiatry that DID is a transdiagnostic syndrome heavily constructed by sociocultural factors, severe sleep disturbances, impaired reality testing, and iatrogenic suggestion. By ignoring the sociocognitive reality of the disorder, the author commits a severe category error, treating a socially mediated psychological syndrome as an ontological map of fundamental physics.
*Omitted Source:* Lynn, S. J., Polizzi, C., Merckelbach, H., Chiu, C.-D., Maxwell, R., van Heugten, D., & Lilienfeld, S. O. (2022). Dissociation and Dissociative Disorders Reconsidered: Beyond Sociocognitive and Trauma Models Toward a Transtheoretical Framework. *Annual Review of Clinical Psychology*, 18, 259-289.

### Weakness 3: Ignorance of Predictive Processing and Precision Weighting

The manuscript engineers a false dichotomy by asserting that physicalism cannot explain dissociative phenomena—such as ketamine hallucinations or alternate identities—without resorting to claiming they are mere "coincidences". This blatantly ignores the dominant contemporary computational framework of Predictive Processing and Active Inference. These Bayesian models explicitly map dissociation not as an interface failure, but as the adaptive, mathematically rigorous dysregulation of precision weighting between top-down priors and bottom-up sensory input.
*Omitted Source:* Constant, A., Clark, A., & Friston, K. J. (2022). Active inference, cognitive agents, and the external environment. *Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience*, 16, 897247. (Contextualizing predictive frameworks and active inference) / Corroborated by Myers et al., and Lotfinia regarding the application of precision weighting dysregulation to clinical dissociation.

### Weakness 4: Misrepresentation of Reversible Amnesia and Neurobiological Constraints

The manuscript attempts to map the specific functional anatomy of the dualist interface using DID as a static guide. However, it fails to address the neurobiological consensus that the easily reversible nature of dissociative amnesia through psychotherapy is fundamentally incompatible with the hypothesis of structural interface abnormalities. If the condition were a literal severing of an interface, psychological therapy could not simply reconnect the hardware.
*Omitted/Contradicted Source:* Dodier, O., Otgaar, H., & Mangiulli, I. (2024). Beyond repressed memory: Current alternative solutions to the controversy. *Topics in Cognitive Science*, 16, 574–589. (As analyzed and integrated regarding the incompatibility of reversible amnesia with structural brain abnormalities in contemporary evaluations of DID).

### Weakness 5: Tenet-Bracketed Smuggling in Anesthetic Phase Transitions

The manuscript engages in explicit logical circularity by characterizing the all-or-nothing threshold of general anesthesia as proof of an "interface" collapse, while baselessly dismissing standard physicalist network theories as inadequate. It fails to engage with advanced neuro-computational models (like GNWT and IIT) that mathematically *require* such sharp phase transitions and integration drop-offs based on complex network topology, effectively smuggling in a dualist conclusion to explain a phenomenon already perfectly modeled by physicalist science.
*Omitted Source:* Seth, A. K., & Bayne, T. (2022). Theories of consciousness. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 23(7), 439-452. (Demonstrating how contemporary physicalist theories robustly and mathematically model integration thresholds, phase transitions, and performance dissociations without any requirement for dualist assumptions).

**Verdict:** Reject; the manuscript systematically ignores critical 2020-2025 empirical literature regarding memory transfer, transtheoretical etiology, and Bayesian predictive processing in order to protect a philosophically circular, tenet-bracketed argument for dualism that fails basic standards of logical rigor.
