# Referee Report: "Quantum Interpretations Beyond Many-Worlds"

## The Framework and Scope of the Manuscript's Claims

The manuscript under review attempts to survey interpretations of quantum mechanics—specifically identifying Copenhagen, Bohmian mechanics, Quantum Bayesianism (QBism), Relational Quantum Mechanics (RQM), transactional interpretations, and objective collapse theories—with the explicit aim of delineating which frameworks permit consciousness to function as a causally efficacious participant in the physical universe. Operating from a distinctly non-reductive physicalist or dualist philosophical framework, the text evaluates these competing models by their capacity to accommodate what it terms a "Minimal Quantum Interaction" and a bidirectional causal influence between phenomenal consciousness and the material universe. The manuscript argues that because the measurement problem remains genuinely unsolved, consciousness-based interpretations have not been definitively ruled out, thereby creating a philosophical space for exploration.

While the ambition to intersect the philosophy of mind with the foundations of quantum mechanics is a recognized, albeit highly controversial, sub-discipline within the philosophy of physics, the execution in this manuscript suffers from severe empirical, theoretical, and dialectical failures. A rigorous and exhaustive audit of the text reveals that its engagement with the 2020–2026 physics, neuroscience, and philosophy-of-physics literature is largely superficial, selectively cherry-picked, and frequently fundamentally misunderstood. The manuscript routinely substitutes axiomatic "tenet-bracketed" dismissals for substantive philosophical argumentation. It mischaracterizes the operational mechanics of the interpretations it seeks to evaluate, and fundamentally mishandles recent empirical developments in neuropharmacology, quantum biology, and information theory.

This report systematically dismantles the manuscript's claims across several critical domains. It begins by examining the epistemic hazard of tenet-bracketed argumentation, demonstrating how the text evades falsification and nullifies the dialectical process. It then dissects the theoretical mischaracterizations of Relational Quantum Mechanics (RQM) and the profound omissions regarding the failure of cross-perspective links. Following this, the report addresses the mishandling of QBism, intersubjectivity, and the Wigner's Friend paradox. It subsequently exposes the profound category errors the manuscript commits in applying macroscopic behavioral information theory to quantum state-vector reduction. Finally, it addresses the unwarranted empirical overreach regarding objective collapse models, microtubule stabilization via anesthetics, and the radical pair mechanism in quantum biology.

## The Epistemic Hazard of Tenet-Bracketed Argumentation

A foundational and pervasive weakness of the manuscript is its reliance on a structural methodology that preemptively insulates its core claims from rigorous philosophical and empirical critique. The text explicitly outlines a set of non-negotiable axioms—referred to throughout the text as "tenets"—which include the irreducibility of consciousness (Dualism), the reality of wave-function collapse (No Many Worlds), and the causal influence of consciousness on physical selection without injecting energy (Minimal Quantum Interaction).

While establishing axiomatic priors is a standard practice in metaphysical modeling and formalized ontology, the manuscript weaponizes these tenets to evade rather than engage with competing theoretical frameworks. When confronted with devastating counterarguments from eliminative materialists, hard-nosed physicalists, or proponents of the Many-Worlds Interpretation (MWI), the text repeatedly categorizes these as "bedrock disagreements". This rhetorical maneuver allows the author to bypass the arduous work of in-framework refutation. In a top-tier philosophy journal, an author is expected to demonstrate why a rival framework fails on its own internal logic, or why the proposed framework offers superior explanatory power regarding a shared set of empirical data. By classifying fundamental objections merely as boundary conditions of the adopted tenets, the manuscript engages in what can only be described as epistemic boundary-substitution.

This failing is most glaringly evident in the manuscript's treatment of the probability problem in quantum mechanics. The challenge of deriving the Born rule without circularity is profoundly complex across all interpretations, including objective collapse and MWI frameworks. The manuscript asserts that Born probabilities describe the objective structure of the interface where consciousness meets quantum indeterminacy. However, when dealing with the competing explanation provided by MWI, the manuscript summarily dismisses the self-locating uncertainty paradoxes of MWI as "artefacts of MWI dissolving the question it should answer," relying entirely on the "No Many Worlds" tenet rather than providing a mathematical or physical demonstration of MWI's probabilistic failures.

This tenet-bracketed framing degrades the manuscript from a piece of exploratory philosophy of physics into a closed, tautological system. The logical structure repeatedly employed is as follows: if an interpretation (such as Copenhagen or Objective Collapse) leaves an epistemic or physical lacuna that *could* hypothetically be filled by consciousness, it is praised for its "honest limitation" and deemed compatible with the tenets. Conversely, if an interpretation (such as RQM or MWI) successfully dissolves the lacuna using purely physical, relational, or unitary resources, it is dismissed for violating the predetermined tenets. The reliance on tenets to neutralize counterarguments strips the text of its dialectical force and renders its comparative analysis structurally hollow. It is insufficient to state that MWI or RQM are rejected simply because they do not permit consciousness to have a special causal role; the manuscript must prove that the exclusion of consciousness leads to an irresolvable paradox within those interpretations themselves. It fails to do so.

## The Ontological Collapse of Relational Quantum Mechanics

The manuscript's treatment of Relational Quantum Mechanics (RQM) exposes a critical failure to engage with the advanced 2020-2026 philosophy of physics literature, particularly regarding the ontological status of relative facts and the problem of intersubjectivity. The text correctly identifies RQM's core premise as originally formulated by Carlo Rovelli: quantum states describe a relationship between systems rather than absolute properties. By treating any physical system as an "observer," RQM dissolves the traditional measurement problem. The manuscript then notes that RQM eliminates the special role of consciousness, asserting that its "simplicity is illusory" because it covers only part of reality, leaving the first-person perspective unaddressed.

To manage the problem of intersubjectivity—specifically, how different observers can agree on macroscopic outcomes if all facts are strictly relative—the manuscript cites Emily Adlam and Carlo Rovelli's (2022/2023) "cross-perspective links" postulate. The manuscript characterizes this as RQM's "most credible recent move" to recover frame-independent agreement, briefly noting that whether it earns this by RQM's own resources is contested by Lewis and the Muciño-Okon-Sudarsky exchange. It concludes that the disagreement is merely at the "framework boundary," cleanly registered as a difference in metaphysical commitments.

This is a drastic underrepresentation of the severity of the critique against cross-perspective links, treating a fatal internal contradiction as a mild boundary dispute. The contemporary physics literature has rigorously demonstrated that the addition of cross-perspective links fundamentally breaks the RQM framework. Muciño, Okon, and Sudarsky (2021/2022) provide a comprehensive mathematical and conceptual proof that RQM fails to address the conceptual problems of standard quantum mechanics and cannot successfully explain how different observers exchange information without invoking non-local influences.

More devastatingly, the literature demonstrates that integrating cross-perspective links turns RQM into an internally inconsistent hidden variable theory. Consider the extended Wigner's Friend scenario: Alice measures a quantum system S in a basis, obtaining a relative fact. Wigner (Bob), outside Alice's laboratory, treats the combined Alice-S system as undergoing unitary evolution, meaning they remain in an entangled state. In standard RQM, the relative fact Alice observes has no absolute standing for Wigner. However, the cross-perspective links postulate introduced by Adlam and Rovelli asserts that if Bob subsequently measures the physical variable representing Alice's information about the system, he will learn the value of the relative outcome earlier obtained by Alice.

As pointed out in the recent literature (e.g., Riedel 2023), this addition creates an insurmountable paradox. If the cross-perspective link guarantees Bob will read out Alice's result, the relative fact obtained by Alice effectively acts as a hidden variable that causally determines the outcome of Bob's specific subsequent measurement, even while the system is supposedly in a coherent superposition from Bob's perspective. This invalidates the core axiom of RQM—that states are strictly relational and that the Alice-S system is entangled for Bob.

The manuscript ignores this fatal structural collapse. By failing to recognize that RQM with cross-perspective links is mathematically and physically contested as an incoherent interpretation *regardless* of its stance on consciousness, the manuscript demonstrates a superficial understanding of the current ontological debates in quantum foundations. It treats RQM as a viable physicalist alternative that simply lacks a theory of mind, when in fact, the version of RQM the manuscript cites is currently embroiled in an existential crisis over its internal logical consistency.

| **Relational States** | Dissolves measurement problem but "illusory" without consciousness. | Fails to resolve the Frauchiger-Renner paradox without abandoning absolute facts entirely. |
| **Cross-Perspective Links** | RQM's "most credible recent move" to recover intersubjectivity. | **Muciño et al. (2022):** Fails to account for correlations without non-locality. |

## The Misapprehension of Radical Empiricism and the QBism Strawman

A similar analytical deficit appears in the manuscript's critique of Quantum Bayesianism (QBism). The text asserts that QBism makes "agents" foundational to the theory without explaining what agents actually are, arguing that if quantum probabilities are defined as beliefs about future experiences, the theory necessarily presupposes a conscious entity that it fails to account for. The manuscript's primary line of attack is framed around intersubjectivity: it claims that QBism "cannot explain why different agents with identical setups converge on the same probabilities. If these are purely subjective beliefs, this convergence is mysterious".

This assertion is demonstrably false and represents a severe failure to engage with the primary literature defining QBism in the 2020s. QBism's leading theorists, notably Christopher Fuchs, Rüdiger Schack, and John DeBrota, have explicitly, extensively, and mathematically addressed the problem of intersubjectivity and agent convergence. The manuscript completely omits DeBrota, Fuchs, and Schack's seminal 2020 paper in *Foundations of Physics*, "Respecting One's Fellow: QBism's Analysis of Wigner's Friend".

In this work, the authors dissolve the precise mystery the manuscript claims QBism cannot solve. They articulate a "quantum Copernican principle": when two agents interact, each agent has a dual role. To themselves, they are an agent utilizing the quantum formalism to organize their expectations; to the other agent, they are merely a physical system. No user of quantum theory is more privileged than any other user.

The convergence of probabilities among different agents is not a "mysterious" synchronization of solipsistic minds, as the manuscript falsely implies. Rather, in QBism, the Born Rule functions as an empirically derived, normative constraint on an agent's gambling commitments. When agents communicate and share empirical data, they update their individual priors using standard Bayesian updating alongside the normative constraints of the Born rule. The convergence is driven by the regularities of the physical world they are mutually interacting with and their adherence to normative rationality, not by an unexplained metaphysical harmony or a shared fundamental consciousness.

By insisting that QBism's formulation secretly requires an irreducible, ontologically privileged consciousness to ensure convergence, the manuscript forces a dualist framing onto a framework built explicitly on radical empiricism and pragmatism. Furthermore, the manuscript fails to address how the quantum Copernican principle effortlessly resolves the Frauchiger-Renner paradox and Baumann-Brukner extensions within QBism. Because quantum measurement outcomes are understood entirely as the personal experiences of the single agent taking the action, Wigner's conundrum of the friend—where two agents ostensibly have different accounts of whether a measurement outcome exists—poses no paradox for QBism. The manuscript proves that it is critiquing a solipsistic strawman version of subjective probability, rather than the robust, peer-reviewed formalisms that currently define the interpretation in the academic literature.

## Objective Collapse Theories and the Topological Paradoxes of Consciousness Measures

The manuscript correctly identifies spontaneous collapse theories—such as the Ghirardi-Rimini-Weber (GRW) model, Continuous Spontaneous Localization (CSL), and Penrose's Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch OR)—as interpretations that add formal mathematical complexity to unitary quantum mechanics in order to gain falsifiability and solve the measurement problem. It argues that these models "open space for consciousness-matter interaction" and proposes that the parameters of collapse could be coupled directly to consciousness.

To support this hypothesis, the text cites historical attempts to map consciousness onto collapse metrics, acknowledging Kremnizer & Ranchin (2015) and Okon & Sebastián (2016) (and later Okon & Sebastián 2018), who explored associating a numerical measure of consciousness (such as Integrated Information Theory's Φ) with the collapse trigger. Under these models, the likelihood or rate of spontaneous collapse onto a position basis is determined by the system's quantum integrated information (QII). However, the manuscript entirely omits the decisive 2021 refutation of this specific program by Adrian Kent in *Foundations of Physics*.

Kent's analysis, "Collapse and Measures of Consciousness," rigorously demonstrates the fatal topological and mathematical flaws in integrating objective collapse dynamics with quantifiable, scalar aspects of consciousness. The argument proceeds as follows: if a dynamical collapse theory dictates that a specific numerical measure of consciousness (e.g., Φ) resists superposition and drives the collapse, it inadvertently allows for persistent macroscopic superpositions of *distinct states of equal consciousness measure*.

Consider a biological observer or a sophisticated neural network designed to process visual stimuli. Suppose the network viewing a red apple generates a conscious state with an integrated information measure of Φ=k. Suppose the same network viewing a green apple generates a distinct conscious state, also with an integrated information measure of Φ=k. If the physical system is placed into a coherent quantum superposition of "viewing red" and "viewing green," the overall superposition state possesses the exact same collapse-triggering parameter (Φ=k) as the individual branches. Because the scalar measure of consciousness is entirely symmetric across the distinct experiential states, the collapse operator perceives no gradient or instability. Consequently, the physical system will remain in a persistent, stable macroscopic superposition of "seeing red" and "seeing green" simultaneously.

This directly contradicts the empirical reality of definite macroscopic outcomes, resurrecting the very measurement problem that the objective collapse theory was introduced to solve. As Kent points out, to avoid this absurdity, one would have to postulate a highly complex, multidimensional set of ad hoc measures quantifying every single possible qualitative experience (the so-called "Q-shapes"), rendering the physical theory infinitely complex, non-symmetric, and entirely unworkable as a fundamental dynamical law of nature.

By championing objective collapse models as the natural home for a dualistic consciousness while entirely ignoring the profound mathematical and topological paradoxes generated by assigning collapse triggers to phenomenal states, the manuscript obscures the very real possibility that objective collapse theories are structurally incompatible with leading scientific theories of consciousness like IIT. The manuscript's failure to address Kent's 2021 proof demonstrates a lack of familiarity with the current boundaries of the collapse-consciousness hypothesis.

## Overreach in the Empirical Turn: Neuroscience, Anesthesia, and Information Theory

The most alarming weaknesses in the manuscript lie in its handling of empirical data. In its eagerness to validate its foundational tenets, the text systematically misinterprets neurobiological, biochemical, and information-theoretic research from the 2024–2026 period. It routinely conflates the mere existence of a biological mechanism with proof of macroscopic quantum coherence, committing severe logical leaps that classical neuroscience explicitly rejects.

### Microtubule Stabilization and the Category Error of Pharmacological Targets

The manuscript leans heavily on the Orch OR theory, which posits that consciousness arises from orchestrated objective reduction in quantum-coherent microtubules within brain neurons. To bolster this, the text cites highly recent empirical work—specifically, Khan et al. (2024) and Wiest (2024/2025/2026)—which observed that administering the microtubule-stabilizing drug epothilone B significantly delays the onset of isoflurane-induced unconsciousness in rats.

The manuscript treats this pharmacological finding as a profound vindication of its framework, claiming that "experimental support for quantum microtubule substrates" has been achieved and that "anesthetic studies suggest quantum effects correlate with consciousness specifically, not with neural function generally". This interpretation represents a staggering overstatement of the empirical data and a fundamental misunderstanding of biological mechanisms.

As established in the 2026 comprehensive review by Ma and Wang ("Quantum theories of consciousness: a critical review of feasibility, philosophical sufficiency, and empirical testability"), validating quantum theories of consciousness requires satisfying three strict axes: (I) Physical feasibility, (II) Philosophical sufficiency, and (III) Empirical testability.

The finding that volatile anesthetics like isoflurane bind to microtubules, and that stabilizing these structures with epothilone B delays unconsciousness (Loss of Righting Reflex, LORR), merely establishes microtubules as *one of the molecular targets* of volatile anesthetics. It satisfies, at best, a classical physiological prerequisite. It provides absolutely zero evidence for Axis I—that these microtubules sustain biologically relevant, large-scale quantum coherence, rather than merely functioning as classical structural polymers, signal-transduction pathways, or regulators of classical ion channels.

Furthermore, as Ma and Wang point out regarding Axis II (Philosophical sufficiency), even if quantum degrees of freedom were established in tubulin, asserting that this process entails or realizes subjective experience leaves the "hard problem" of consciousness completely untouched. Identifying the physical substrate of anesthesia does not bridge the explanatory gap to phenomenology.

The manuscript's assertion that the epothilone B data confirms "quantum effects that matter precisely where consciousness operates" is a textbook example of the logical fallacy of affirming the consequent. The logical structure employed is:

1. (Premise 1) Orch OR predicts anesthetics bind to microtubules to disrupt quantum consciousness.
2. (Premise 2) Anesthetics are observed to bind to microtubules.
3. (Conclusion) Therefore, Orch OR's quantum consciousness hypothesis is empirically supported.

This deduction completely ignores the vastly more probable classical null hypothesis—that microtubules mediate classical synaptic signaling, cellular transport, or neurotransmitter receptor regulation, and that disrupting them chemically disrupts the classical neural correlates of consciousness. The manuscript's failure to engage with the classical explanation for the epothilone B effect renders its empirical claims scientifically void.

### The Misappropriation of Macroscopic Behavioral Information Theory

Perhaps the most egregious technical error in the manuscript is its misappropriation of macroscopic information theory to justify its proposed "Minimal Quantum Interaction" mechanism. The manuscript requires a model where consciousness can bias quantum outcomes (collapsing the wave function to specific preferred states) without violating thermodynamic energy conservation laws. To support the physical plausibility of this minimal mechanism, the text references a major 2024/2025 publication by Zheng & Meister in the journal *Neuron*, titled "The unbearable slowness of being: Why do we live at 10 bits/s?".

Zheng & Meister's research provides a rigorously calculated estimate of the information throughput of human conscious behavior. By analyzing cognitive and motor tasks such as expert typing, speech processing, and solving a Rubik's Cube blindfolded, they determine that the "inner" brain processes information at a startlingly slow rate of roughly 10 bits per second, in stark contrast to the ∼109 bits/s gathered by the peripheral sensory systems.

The manuscript seizes upon this 10 bits/s figure, stating: "~10 bits/s conscious throughput ensures selection is minimal". The text attempts to use this behavioral bottleneck as evidence that consciousness operates at a low enough bandwidth to subtly bias quantum selection events without requiring massive, thermodynamically detectable energy injections.

This constitutes a profound category error. The manuscript conflates macroscopic, psychological task-entropy (calculated via classical Shannon information based on word predictability or motor constraints) with the von Neumann entropy and phase space of a quantum state-vector reduction. Zheng and Meister's 10 bits/s limit is a macroscopic description of motor-control execution, serial attentional bottlenecks, and working memory constraints. It explicitly measures the rate of behavioral *output* in a macroscopic organism.

To map a 10 bits/s macroscopic behavioral output directly onto the physical selection of quantum bases at the subcellular level is physically illiterate. A single macromolecule of tubulin undergoing superposition and collapse involves degrees of freedom vastly exceeding 10 bits of quantum information. If consciousness is responsible for collapsing the wave functions of millions of entangled microtubules across the brain simultaneously to solve the "binding problem" (as the manuscript elsewhere implies via its discussion of quantum holism), the information processing required to bias those specific quantum distributions would be astronomical, irrespective of how slowly the human subject eventually types on a keyboard or rotates a Rubik's cube. The manuscript's attempt to use Zheng & Meister's cognitive-behavioral speed limit as a physical boundary condition for quantum mechanical wave-function selection demonstrates a fundamental lack of understanding of the distinctions between classical communication theory and quantum mechanics.

| --- | --- | --- |
| **Domain of Measurement** | Macroscopic behavior, motor control, linguistic output, serial attention. | Subcellular quantum state-vector reduction and wave-function collapse. |
| **Type of Entropy** | Classical Shannon Entropy (predictability of language, motor limits). | von Neumann Entropy (quantum phase space degrees of freedom). |
| **Implication of ~10 bits/s** | Explains the "inner brain" serial bottleneck vs. "outer brain" parallel sensory processing. | Falsely used to prove that conscious collapse of wave-functions requires "minimal" thermodynamic energy. |

### Cryptochromes, Molecular Chirality, and the Generalization of the Quantum Zeno Effect

In its survey of potential quantum neural mechanisms, the manuscript discusses the Quantum Zeno effect—specifically Henry Stapp's mental effort framework, which posits that rapid, repeated mental "observations" can hold a quantum state in place, preventing environmental decoherence and allowing an immaterial consciousness to influence macroscopic brain activity. To establish the biological feasibility of this mechanism, the text cites Denton et al. (2024), who demonstrated that the quantum Zeno effect enables magnetosensitivity in tightly bound radical pairs within cryptochrome proteins.

The manuscript utilizes this finding to argue that biological quantum Zeno-protected states are now experimentally verified, thereby lending empirical credence to Stapp's macroscopic theory of mental effort. However, a closer reading of the 2024–2025 chemical physics literature reveals that the mechanism identified by Denton et al. is highly localized, chemically specific, and cannot be extrapolated to general neural processing or "mental observations."

As explicitly detailed by Smith, Denton, Kattnig et al. (2025) in their follow-up paper, "Chirality-bolstered quantum Zeno effect enhances radical pair-based magnetoreception," the Zeno enhancement in cryptochromes relies specifically on molecular chirality and the chirality-induced spin selectivity (CISS) effect, combined with strongly asymmetric recombination of the radical pairs. The Zeno effect in this specific biological context is a direct consequence of the rigid, localized biochemical geometry of the cryptochrome molecule and the specific spin dynamics of the FAD-superoxide pair. It relies on the physical environment continuously "measuring" the state via rapid, asymmetric chemical reaction pathways.

Stapp's model, conversely, requires a brain-wide, non-local, top-down "mental intention" to act as the measurement apparatus that continuously collapses the wave function of calcium ion channels or large-scale neural states to hold them in a specific conformational status. The physical mechanics of CISS-driven asymmetric recombination in a single specific photoreceptor protein cannot serve as a biological precedent for an immaterial mind interacting with distributed cortical networks. By stripping the cryptochrome research of its specific biochemical parameters (spin chemistry, chiral geometry, and radical pairs) and co-opting it as a generalized proof of "mental Zeno effects," the manuscript engages in superficial analogical reasoning rather than rigorous biophysical analysis.

## Omitted Competing Interpretations and Untested Counterarguments

The manuscript's core thesis requires demonstrating that the measurement problem remains unsolved by standard physicalist interpretations, thereby creating a necessary "logical space" for consciousness-based models. To achieve this dialectical goal, it must exhaustively evaluate the prevailing interpretations and counterarguments. Yet, the text systematically omits or severely undertheorizes several prominent competing interpretive frameworks.

**1. Decoherence without Consciousness:** The manuscript correctly outlines the decoherence challenge (Tegmark 2000 vs. Hagan et al. 2002) regarding the survival times of superposition in warm biological tissue. However, it misrepresents the philosophical consensus on decoherence in the context of interpretation. The manuscript implies that because environmental decoherence does not solve the "problem of definite outcomes" (the actual realization of a single result from the diagonalized density matrix), consciousness is required to finish the job. It completely ignores the modern Everettian formulation of decoherence (advanced by Wallace, Saunders, and others), which successfully utilizes environment-induced decoherence to explain the *appearance* of collapse to a physically embedded observer without requiring any actual physical collapse mechanism. While the manuscript rejects MWI on "tenet" grounds, ignoring how modern decoherence theory perfectly accounts for the phenomenology of the observer renders the critique incomplete and intellectually dishonest.

**2. Physicalist Continuous Spontaneous Localization (CSL):** While the text extensively discusses spontaneous collapse, it frames it almost entirely as a vehicle for consciousness (e.g., Penrose OR and Orch OR). It gives insufficient weight to the standard GRW and CSL models formulated by Pearle, Ghirardi, Rimini, and Weber, which postulate non-linear stochastic additions to the Schrödinger equation governed by purely physical parameters (such as mass density or particle number). In these standard models, collapse is an objective physical process driven by a universal noise field, requiring absolutely no input, selection, or interaction from consciousness. The manuscript fails to test its "bidirectional interaction" tenet against the null hypothesis that objective collapse is simply a blind, physical constant of nature.

**3. Classical Solutions to the Binding Problem:** The manuscript suggests that the binding problem—how distributed brain processes combine into unified awareness—may "require the physical holism that only quantum entanglement provides," asserting that an entangled state is fundamentally indivisible. This claim entirely bypasses the massive corpus of classical computational neuroscience which offers robust, biologically plausible solutions to the binding problem. Mechanisms such as neural synchrony (gamma band oscillations, ∼40 Hz), global workspace broadcasting, and integrated information architectures offer causal, classical explanations for phenomenal unity without needing to invoke macroscopic quantum entanglement. By framing quantum holism as a necessary condition for binding, the manuscript ignores decades of classical neuroscience that operates perfectly well without it.

**4. Evolutionary Epiphenomenalism:** The text briefly mentions that a quantum approach can solve the epiphenomenalism problem, but it does not rigorously test this against modern evolutionary biology. If consciousness exerts a causal force via a "Minimal Quantum Interaction," what is the evolutionary pathway that selected for a brain capable of interfacing with quantum probability? Classical neural models explain behavioral fitness through standard thermodynamics, biochemistry, and classical computation. The manuscript provides no mechanistic or evolutionary account of how an immaterial mind's ability to tweak Born probabilities provides a selectable survival advantage over a highly efficient, deterministic classical computational algorithm.

## Specific Weaknesses and Verdict

The manuscript's failure to engage with the advanced literature renders it unsuitable for publication in its current form. The following five specific weaknesses demonstrate critical theoretical and empirical failures:

1. **The Mischaracterization of Relational Quantum Mechanics (RQM) and Cross-Perspective Links:** The manuscript attempts to sideline RQM by claiming it eliminates consciousness while maintaining theoretical coherence through Adlam and Rovelli's "cross-perspective links." This completely omits and contradicts the rigorous mathematical refutations in the foundational physics literature which demonstrate that introducing cross-perspective links destroys the relational ontology of RQM, forcing it into an incoherent hidden variable theory that fails to preserve relativistic locality.
*Source omitted/contradicted: Muciño, R., Okon, E., & Sudarsky, D. (2022). "Assessing Relational Quantum Mechanics." Synthese, 200(5).*
2. **The Solipsistic Strawman of QBism and the Omission of the Quantum Copernican Principle:** The manuscript alleges that QBism is unable to account for intersubjective agreement—the convergence of probabilities among different agents—without secretly presupposing an ontologically privileged, unified consciousness. This mishandles modern QBist theory, which explicitly utilizes the "quantum Copernican principle" and normative rational constraints (the Born rule) to mathematically resolve Wigner's Friend scenarios and intersubjectivity without reifying consciousness as a collapse mechanism.
*Source omitted: DeBrota, J. B., Fuchs, C. A., & Schack, R. (2020). "Respecting One's Fellow: QBism's Analysis of Wigner's Friend." Foundations of Physics, 50(12), 1859-1874.*
3. **Empirical Overreach Regarding Microtubule-Stabilizing Drugs and Anesthesia:** The text mishandles the recent empirical findings of Khan et al. (2024) and Wiest (2025/2026), claiming that because the microtubule-stabilizer epothilone B delays isoflurane-induced unconsciousness in rats, a "quantum microtubule substrate of consciousness is experimentally supported." This contradicts the established neurophilosophical consensus that identifying a classical molecular target for anesthetics provides zero evidence for quantum coherence or subjective phenomenal realization, committing the fallacy of affirming the consequent.
*Source contradicted: Ma, X., & Wang, A. (2026). "Quantum theories of consciousness: a critical review of feasibility, philosophical sufficiency, and empirical testability." Frontiers in Psychology, 17, 1730965.*
4. **The Ignored Superposition Paradoxes in Objective Collapse and Consciousness Measures:** The manuscript promotes objective collapse models as a mechanism for consciousness, citing older literature that maps collapse parameters to quantifiable measures of consciousness (e.g., QII or Φ). It fatally omits recent proofs demonstrating that if a scalar measure of consciousness triggers collapse, it will inevitably allow for persistent, intolerable macroscopic superpositions of distinct experiential states of equal measure, rendering the integration topologically unworkable.
*Source omitted: Kent, A. (2021). "Collapse and Measures of Consciousness." Foundations of Physics, 51(2), 62.*
5. **A Profound Category Error Conflating Behavioral Entropy with Quantum Information Phase Space:** The manuscript attempts to justify the thermodynamic feasibility of a "Minimal Quantum Interaction" by citing a 10 bits/s limit on human information processing. It severely mishandles this data, erroneously conflating a macroscopic, classical Shannon-entropy bottleneck of motor-control and attentional bandwidth with the von Neumann entropy and phase space degrees of freedom required to physically collapse a highly entangled quantum state-vector in the brain.
*Source mishandled: Zheng, J., & Meister, M. (2025). "The unbearable slowness of being: Why do we live at 10 bits/s?" Neuron, 113(2), 192-204.*

Reject without resubmit; the manuscript's profound category errors in information theory, its misrepresentation of contemporary quantum interpretations, and its invalid extraction of quantum metaphysical conclusions from classical biochemical anesthetic data render it fundamentally unsuitable for academic publication.
