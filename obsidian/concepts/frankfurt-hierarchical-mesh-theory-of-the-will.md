**Frankfurt's hierarchical (or "mesh") theory of the will** is a structural account of free will, set out in his 1971 paper "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person." Its central move is to locate freedom not in the ability to do otherwise, and not in the causal history of one's desires, but in a *synchronic relation among mental states*: an agent's will is free when the desire that actually moves her to act is the one she *wants* to be effective. The theory is built from **first-order desires** (desires to do things), **second-order desires** (desires about which desires to have), and the pivotal **second-order volition** (wanting a certain desire to be one's *will*). A being with second-order volitions is a **person**; one with first-order desires but no second-order volitions is a **wanton**. This article presents that account at full strength as a paradigm of **structural compatibilism**, states its two canonical objections (the *regress* objection and the *manipulation / authority* objection, both explained below), and locates it on the [[source-versus-leeway-incompatibilism|source-versus-leeway taxonomy]]—where its ahistorical character becomes the point of contact with the Map's interests.

## Two Distinct Frankfurt Contributions

Harry Frankfurt made two landmark contributions to the free-will debate two years apart, and they are easy to conflate. This article concerns only the second.

- **1969 — "Alternate Possibilities and Moral Responsibility"** (*Journal of Philosophy* 66(23):829–839). A *negative* argument: the [[frankfurt-cases-and-the-principle-of-alternate-possibilities|Frankfurt cases]] use a counterfactual intervener to show that moral responsibility does *not* require the ability to do otherwise. This is about **leeway**—it dismantles a requirement.
- **1971 — "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person"** (*Journal of Philosophy* 68(1):5–20). A *positive* account of what freedom of the will and personhood *consist in*: a mesh between the effective first-order desire and a second-order volition. This is about the **structure of the will**, and it is the subject of this article.

Both are compatibilist, and together they form a pincer against the leeway picture: 1969 removes the "could have done otherwise" requirement, and 1971 supplies a positive account of freedom that never needed it. But they are separate theories with separate machinery. The Frankfurt *cases* are not the hierarchical theory, and the hierarchical theory does not depend on the cases. Where this article touches the 1969 paper, it cross-references rather than restates it.

## First-Order Desires, Second-Order Desires, and Volitions

Frankfurt begins from a capacity he takes to be distinctively human: **reflective self-evaluation**, the ability to form desires about one's own desires. This generates a hierarchy.

A **first-order desire** is a desire to do or not do some action—to eat the cake, to take the drug, to finish the work. Most animals have these. A **second-order desire** is a desire to *have* (or not have) a certain first-order desire: a person might want to want to exercise, or want *not* to want the drug.

The load-bearing refinement is the **second-order volition**. This is the special case in which one wants a certain first-order desire not merely to be present but to be *effective*—to be one's **will**, the desire that actually moves one all the way to action. Frankfurt's term "the will" names the effective desire, the one that wins and issues in conduct. A second-order volition is thus a stance on *which desire should do the moving*, not just on which desires one should have. Frankfurt makes possession of second-order volitions the mark of personhood: "It is only because a person has volitions of the second order that he is capable both of enjoying and of lacking freedom of the will."

## The Person and the Wanton

The distinction Frankfurt draws to make the theory vivid is between the **person** and the **wanton**. A wanton has first-order desires—possibly strong ones, possibly organized by considerable intelligence—but forms no second-order volitions. The wanton simply acts on whatever first-order desire happens to be strongest, and, crucially, *does not care* which desire that is. Wantonness is compatible with rationality; a shrewd but purely appetite-driven agent is still a wanton. Non-human animals and very young children are Frankfurt's central cases.

The point is sharpened by his three-way contrast among addicts, all subject to the same first-order craving:

- The **unwilling addict** takes the drug, but his second-order volition is *against* the addictive desire; he is driven by a will he does not want to be his will. He acts, in Frankfurt's phrase, under a force he experiences as external to himself.
- The **willing addict** takes the drug and *endorses* the addictive desire at the second order—he wants that desire to be the one that moves him. His drug-taking is his own, and in the relevant sense free.
- The **wanton addict** takes the drug and forms no view at all about which of his desires should prevail. He is neither identified with nor alienated from his craving; he simply is not in the business of caring.

The wanton addict shows that acting on one's strongest first-order desire is not sufficient for free will. All three addicts may do exactly the same thing for the same proximate reason, yet only one of them—the willing addict—has a *free will* in Frankfurt's sense.

## Freedom of the Will as Identification

Frankfurt separates two freedoms that ordinary talk runs together. **Freedom of action** is doing what one wants to do—being unimpeded in translating an effective desire into conduct. **Freedom of the will** is the deeper matter of *willing what one wants to will*: the effective first-order desire coincides with the agent's second-order volition. When they coincide, the agent is **identified** with her will—the desire that moves her is the one she wants to be moved by.

This is why the theory is called a **mesh** account: as the *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* summarizes the family, an agent acts freely when her action issues from desires that "suitably mesh with hierarchically ordered elements of her psychology." Freedom is a matter of *fit* between levels of the will, not a matter of open alternatives (leeway) or of causal origin (source). Frankfurt allows two consequences that some find surprising: free will in this sense is something non-human animals wholly lack, and something a person can lack on a particular occasion—whenever her will is one she does not want to have.

## The Regress Objection

The most influential criticism is Gary Watson's **regress objection**, from "Free Agency" (1975). If a second-order desire is what confers authority over a first-order desire, what confers authority on the *second*-order desire? Consistency seems to demand a third-order volition to adjudicate conflicts among second-order desires, and a fourth to adjudicate those, without end. Nothing in the *form* of the hierarchy privileges the second level; height alone confers no authority, so the account either regresses or stops arbitrarily.

Watson's constructive alternative replaces the desire-hierarchy with a distinction between the agent's **valuational system** (judgments about what is good or worth pursuing) and her **motivational system** (what actually moves her). Free action is action in which what the agent most values is not obstructed by her motivational system; unfree action is action in which, as Watson puts it, the agent is unable to get what he most wants or values. Authority comes from the agent's *values and practical reason*—a different *kind* of state—rather than from a higher *tier* of desire. This launched the "real self" tradition and remains the standard foil against Frankfurt's purely formal hierarchy.

Frankfurt is not defenceless. In later work collected in *The Importance of What We Care About* (1988), he answers the regress not with a still-higher desire but with **decisive identification**: an act of commitment with which the person is *satisfied*, one that "resounds" throughout the potentially endless higher orders and so terminates them without adding another rung. He reframes this as **wholeheartedness**—a stable, unambivalent structure of the will—and **satisfaction**, a quietist state that is *not one more desire*. Critics press that satisfaction is either just another desire (reopening the regress) or an unexplained brute stopping point; the exchange is live, not settled.

## The Manipulation (Authority) Objection

The second objection targets the theory's silence about *origins*. Because the mesh is a **synchronic** relation—a fact about how the levels of the will fit together *now*—it says nothing about *how* that fit came about. So consider an agent covertly engineered, by manipulation or brainwashing, to have exactly the right mesh: an effective desire she endorses with a wholehearted second-order volition. She satisfies Frankfurt's condition to the letter, yet intuitively she is neither free nor responsible, because her endorsements were installed from outside. The SEP states the wedge directly: mesh theories concern only the structure of the will and are silent on its causal history, so a manipulated agent with the right structure meets the condition while seeming unfree.

This is the hinge that connects the 1971 theory to source incompatibilism and the [[the-manipulation-argument-and-hard-incompatibilism|manipulation argument]]: if an alien causal history defeats freedom despite a perfect mesh, why should an ordinary deterministic history—equally outside the agent's control—not defeat it too? It is partly in response to this vulnerability that later compatibilists (Fischer and Ravizza) add a *history* condition to their [[reasons-responsiveness|reasons-responsive]] account, distinguishing their view from purely structural mesh theories.

## Locating the Mesh Theory on the Source/Leeway Map

Read through the [[source-versus-leeway-incompatibilism|source-versus-leeway taxonomy]], the mesh theory occupies a clean and revealing position. It requires **no alternative possibilities**: Frankfurt's willing addict is fully free-willed even in a story where he could not have done otherwise, so the theory survives the collapse of the Principle of Alternate Possibilities that the 1969 cases engineered. And it requires **no ultimate sourcehood**: the account is explicitly indifferent to where the mesh came from, asking only whether the levels fit *now*. Freedom, for Frankfurt, is a synchronic structural relation among desires—neither a leeway condition nor a source condition, but a third thing that brackets both.

That bracketing is exactly what the manipulation objection exploits. Because the mesh is ahistorical, it cannot by itself distinguish a self-authored will from a manipulatively-installed one. The theory therefore *brackets* the sourcehood question rather than *settling* it. This is a claim about the theory's scope, and it is one that non-dualist source incompatibilists such as Derk Pereboom press just as hard as anyone—it is not, on its own, evidence for any particular metaphysics of mind.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map treats the hierarchical mesh theory as a **genuine rival at full strength**, not a strawman: it is a respectable, widely-held compatibilist account of what free will is. Two tenets frame the Map's narrow, calibrated engagement with it.

Under [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] (Tenet 3), the Map cares that a free action be the agent's own *origination*—that consciousness, not a prior physical chain, settle which outcome becomes actual. The mesh theory does not supply that. Its own manipulation objection shows why: a synchronic structure among desires is compatible with the whole structure having been installed from outside, so the mesh cannot by itself certify that the will is *authored* rather than merely *arranged*. This is precisely the point where the Map's interest in sourcehood engages. But the engagement must be stated carefully, because the very same gap is what motivates non-dualist source incompatibilists. That a structural mesh leaves origination unsettled is a **framework-relative observation about the theory's scope**, shared with Pereboom and other physicalist critics; it is **not a sourcehood-shaped hole that only dualism can fill**, and the Map does not read it as evidence for dualism. It borrows the *structure* of the manipulation argument, not its metaphysical conclusion.

Under [[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]] (Tenet 5), the Map declines to treat the mesh theory's *parsimony*—it delivers a workable compatibilist freedom with no non-physical machinery—as decisive in its favour, while conceding, symmetrically, that this restraint buys the Map no positive support. The honest summary: the mesh theory tells us that freedom has a structural dimension the Map should preserve (an authored will really is one whose levels cohere), but it leaves the origination question open, and the Map's stake is only that this openness is real—not that it points anywhere in particular.
