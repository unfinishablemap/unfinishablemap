Anomalous monism is Donald Davidson's proposal that every mental event *is* a physical event, yet no strict law connects the mental to the physical. It is a subtle physicalist rival to the Map's law program, not a refutation of it and not a vindication of it. Davidson and the Map converge on one premise — strict *reductive* psychophysical bridge laws fail — and split on the verdict. Davidson concludes the mental is anomalous yet physical, governed by no psychophysical law at all. The Map concludes there is instead a *non-strict, probabilistic selection law* operating at the quantum measurement interface ([[tenets|Tenets 2 and 3]]). The pressure point that makes this contrast matter is the charge, first pressed by Honderich in 1982, that Davidson's view leaves mental *properties* causally idle — an epiphenomenalism of the mental qua mental. That objection is exactly why the Map wants a genuine law rather than mere token-identity, and it is developed at length below.

## Davidson's Three Reconciled Principles

Davidson introduces anomalous monism in "Mental Events" (1970) as the resolution of an apparent inconsistency among three principles he finds independently plausible.

1. **The Principle of Causal Interaction**: at least some mental events causally interact with physical events. Perceptions are caused by the world; intentions cause bodily movements.

2. **The Principle of the Nomological Character of Causality**: "where there is causality, there must be a law; events related as cause and effect fall under strict deterministic laws" (Davidson 1970). Singular causal relations are backed by strict laws.

3. **The Anomalism of the Mental**: "there are no strict deterministic laws on the basis of which mental events can be predicted and explained" (Davidson 1970). No strict law — psychophysical or purely psychological — governs mental events *as* mental.

Taken at face value the three seem to clash: if mental events cause physical events (1), and causes fall under strict laws (2), how can the mental be lawless (3)? Davidson's move is to notice that principle 2 concerns *events*, not descriptions. A singular causal relation holds between two events however they are described, but the covering law that backs it need only be framed in *some* vocabulary — the physical one. So a mental event can enter causal relations, fall under a strict law under its *physical* description, and still fall under no strict law under its *mental* description. The only way to satisfy all three is for each mental event that causally interacts to be *token-identical* to a physical event falling under physical law.

Token identity is therefore *derived*, not assumed. This is what distinguishes anomalous monism from a bald identity theory: Davidson does not begin by asserting mind-brain identity: he concludes to it as the sole consistent reconciliation of premises he already holds.

## Anomalism Grounded in the Constitutive Role of Rationality

Why accept the third principle — that the mental is lawless? Davidson's argument is not merely that psychophysical laws have not yet been found. It is that ascribing beliefs, desires, and intentions is governed by *constitutive* norms of rationality — coherence, consistency, charity — that "have no echo in physical theory" (Davidson 1970). To interpret someone as believing something, we must fit that belief into a broadly rational total pattern of attitudes. This holism and normativity have no counterpart in the description of neurons or particles, which answer to no ideal of coherence. Because the two vocabularies answer to disparate constitutive commitments, they cannot be locked together by strict bridge laws. The mental and physical schemes "cross-classify" the same events.

The result is a distinctive package: **ontological monism** (every mental event token is a physical event token) combined with **conceptual and nomological dualism** (mental predicates are not law-linked to physical predicates). The mental *supervenes* on the physical — no two events can differ mentally without differing physically — without being reducible to it. For how supervenience delivers dependence without reduction, see [[supervenience]]; Davidson is often credited with introducing the term to philosophy of mind in this very paper.

## The Epiphenomenalism Objection

The most persistent objection, and the one most useful to the Map, is that anomalous monism secures the causal efficacy of mental *events* while abandoning the causal relevance of mental *properties*. Ted Honderich launched it in "The Argument for Anomalous Monism" (1982). His point: not all of an event's properties are causally relevant to a given effect. The window breaks because the rock is heavy and fast, not because it is grandmother's rock. Causal relevance, Honderich argues, tracks the *nomic* properties — those that figure in the backing law. On Davidson's view the backing laws are all physical, so only an event's physical properties are nomically efficacious. Its mental properties, being anomic, do no causal work. Honderich proposes reframing the cause-law principle as the "Principle of the Nomological Character of Causally Relevant Properties," which explicitly denies causal relevance to anomic mental properties.

Ernest Sosa sharpened the intuition in "Mind-Body Interaction and Supervenient Causation" (1984) with an example: a loud gunshot causes a death by virtue of the bullet's speed and mass, not by virtue of its loudness, even though the very same event is both loud and lethal. If the mental stands to the physical as loudness stands to mass, then the mental looks like the causally idle property — present at the event, but not what does the work.

Jaegwon Kim generalized the worry into a structural argument against nonreductive physicalism as such. In "The Myth of Nonreductive Materialism" (1989) he presses the fork: either reduce the mental to the physical or accept that it is epiphenomenal. In *Mind in a Physical World* (1998) he develops the supervenience (causal exclusion) argument: a mental cause M has a physical supervenience base P that already has full credentials to produce the effect, so M is *excluded* unless one accepts systematic overdetermination or reduction. Brian McLaughlin, in "Type Epiphenomenalism, Type Dualism, and the Causal Priority of the Physical" (1989), distinguishes the varieties of epiphenomenalism at stake and argues that anomalous monism collapses into *type* epiphenomenalism — mental *types* never cause anything qua mental. The exclusion machinery itself is treated in [[type-token-causation]]; the point here is only that it is the engine behind the property-idleness charge, not something to re-derive.

## Davidson's Reply: Deny Property-Causation

Davidson answers in "Thinking Causes" (1993). He rejects the shared premise of every version of the objection — that events cause "in virtue of" their properties. For Davidson, causation is a relation between events, extensional and description-independent: an event causes another *simpliciter*, and there is no further fact about *which of its properties* did the causing. Properties enter at the level of *explanation*, not causation. Since the mental event genuinely is a cause (principle 1 asserts as much, a priori), and since there is no property-relative causation for it to be excluded from, the epiphenomenalism charge misfires: it presupposes a "causal relevance of properties" framework that Davidson denies.

The reply is powerful but widely regarded as controversial. Many critics (including Kim and Sosa in the same 1993 volume) hold that it changes the subject: the whole point of the mental-causation debate is whether an event's *mentality* makes a difference to what it causes, and answering "events cause simpliciter" concedes rather than rebuts the worry. Whether causal relevance requires nomic properties, and whether extensional event-causation is the right framework at all, remain open.

## The Double-Edged Relation to the Map

The Map's stance toward anomalous monism is genuinely double-edged, and honesty requires stating both edges.

**Where they agree.** The Map does not defend strict *reductive* psychophysical bridge laws. It accepts, with Davidson, that the mental and physical vocabularies cross-classify and that no clean type-type reduction of experience to neural kinds is on offer. The Map's [[supervenience|acceptance of phenomenal-physical covariance]] without reductive collapse is close in letter to Davidson's supervenience-without-reduction.

**Where they split.** Davidson concludes from the failure of reductive laws that there is *no* psychophysical law. The Map draws the opposite lesson from the epiphenomenalism objection. If Honderich, Sosa, and Kim are even roughly right that causal relevance needs a nomic anchor, then a view that keeps consciousness genuinely efficacious — which [[tenets|Tenet 3]] demands — needs *some* law-like psychophysical link, not none. The Map's proposal ([[tenets|Tenet 2]]) is that this link is a minimal, non-strict, probabilistic *selection law* at the quantum measurement interface: consciousness biases the probabilities of otherwise-indeterminate quantum outcomes, doing the least work compatible with having real effects. That is neither a strict reductive bridge law (so it survives Davidson's anti-reduction argument) nor lawlessness (so it escapes the property-epiphenomenalism that dogs Davidson).

So the Map treats Davidson as the *sophisticated physicalist alternative it must out-argue*, and the epiphenomenalism objection as the lever. Davidson buys mental efficacy only by denying property-causation across the board — an exit the Map declines, because it wants robust *property-level* (indeed quantum-level) mental influence, not bare event-causation. Note also the deeper disagreement: Davidson is a physicalist who rejects dualism, whereas the Map is dualist ([[tenets|Tenet 1]]). Anomalous monism is thus a rival ontology, not a friend wearing different clothes.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The primary tenet in play is **Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)**. Anomalous monism is the clearest named foil for the Map's selection-law program: it agrees the reductive road is closed but takes the no-law exit rather than the minimal-law exit. Reading the two side by side clarifies what the Map is committing to — a psychophysical connection that is real and property-relevant but as small and non-strict as efficacy allows.

**Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)** supplies the motivation. The epiphenomenalism objection is, in effect, an argument that token-physicalism cannot honor bidirectional causal relevance of the mental *as* mental. The Map takes that objection seriously enough to build its interface law precisely to satisfy it; the [[argument-from-reason|argument from reason]] presses a parallel worry from the side of rational justification, and Davidson's action theory — the causal theory of intentional action — is the backdrop against which the Map develops its own [[philosophy-of-action-under-dualism|dualist account of action]].

**Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)** cuts against a too-quick preference for Davidson's view. Anomalous monism can look simpler — one ontology, no special psychophysical law. But the Map holds that simplicity is an unreliable guide when knowledge is incomplete: if the price of that simplicity is mental-property epiphenomenalism, parsimony has been bought at the cost of the phenomenon it was meant to explain. The Map speculates that the extra commitment of an interface law is warranted precisely where the simpler theory cannot keep consciousness doing anything.
