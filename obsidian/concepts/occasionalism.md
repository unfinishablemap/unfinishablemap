Occasionalism holds that finite minds and bodies never genuinely cause anything: God is the sole true cause, acting on the *occasion* of each mental or physical event to produce what looks like its effect. When you will your arm to rise, your volition does not move the arm—God moves it on the occasion of the willing. For roughly a century after Descartes it was a leading learned response to the mind-body interaction problem, and it remains the sharpest foil for the position this site defends. The Map is an interactionist dualism ([[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3]]): it holds that creaturely minds really do influence matter. Occasionalism denies exactly that. Reconstructing why it was compelling—and why its modern quantum descendants press hardest—is the best way to see what genuine [[interactionist-dualism|creaturely interaction]] must pay for.

## The Machinery: Four Arguments and a Distinction

Nicolas Malebranche (1638–1715) built the most systematic occasionalism, but the position rests on a family of arguments assembled across the Cartesian school. The Stanford Encyclopedia reconstructs four:

- **No Necessary Connection (NNC)** — Malebranche's central move. A true cause requires a *necessary* connection between it and its effect, and only God's will is necessarily efficacious. As rendered in the standard English translation: "A true cause as I understand it is one such that the mind perceives a necessary connection between it and its effect" (*The Search After Truth*, p. 450, as quoted in the SEP entry). No connection between a creaturely volition and a bodily motion is necessary in that sense; therefore no creature is a true cause.
- **Passive Nature** — Bodies are just extension, mere spatial relations, and extension carries no power to move. Motive force must come from elsewhere—from God.
- **No Knowledge** — Arnold Geulincx pressed the epistemic axiom *quod nescis quomodo fiat, id non facis*: what you do not know how to bring about, you do not do. Because we have no idea how a volition produces a nerve firing, we are not its authors. The SEP renders his principle as "it is impossible to bring about something when one does not know how it is brought about."
- **Conservation is Continuous Creation** — God's sustaining of the world is a continuous re-creation; in re-creating each thing in its state at each instant, God already does all the causal work, leaving none for creatures.

A distinction cuts across these. *Global* occasionalism—Malebranche's mature view—denies creaturely causation everywhere. *Local* occasionalism restricts divine substitution to a domain (body-body, or body-mind causation) while leaving genuine creaturely causes elsewhere. Louis de La Forge, who first used the term "occasional cause," argued from continuous creation that God moves all bodies, yet he *preserved mental causation*—a local occasionalist. Géraud de Cordemoy argued that motion initiated by an agent can only be continued by that same agent; since bodies plainly do not sustain their own motion, God alone moves them. The graded position-space matters for the Map: even a *local* occasionalism about mind-body traffic concedes precisely what [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3]] refuses to concede.

## The Tradition, and a Historiographical Correction

The "God is the only cause" move predates Malebranche by six centuries. **Al-Ghazālī** (c. 1058–1111) and the Ashʿarite *kalām* tradition denied that the connection between what we call cause and effect is necessary—causal regularities are God's habitual practice, not a power in things. This site's article on [[islamic-sufi-philosophy-of-consciousness|Islamic and Sufi philosophy of consciousness]] treats al-Ghazālī's contribution in more depth; here it is enough to record the scholarly caution (Marmura and others): the Seventeenth Discussion of *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* may defend the *possibility* of miracles rather than endorse thoroughgoing occasionalism, and leaves "some kind of genuine causality in creatures" open. Al-Ghazālī should not be filed as an unambiguous global occasionalist.

The more consequential correction, due to Steven Nadler's *Occasionalism: Causation Among the Cartesians* (2011), targets a textbook caricature. Occasionalism is often taught as an ad hoc patch for the mind-body problem—a *Deus ex machina* wheeled in to explain how an unextended soul moves an extended body. Nadler shows it was primarily a doctrine about *causation in general*, with body-body causation the central case; the mind-body application is one consequence among many. An occasionalism presented as merely a mind-body dodge repeats the error Nadler corrects.

## The Classical Objections

Two objections defined the debate and still frame the modern one.

Antoine **Arnauld** pressed the *explains-too-much* charge: dissolving all causation into divine intervention explains everything indifferently and therefore explains nothing in particular. If God directly produces every event, the specific structure of nature—why *this* cause reliably yields *that* effect—is left as brute divine habit rather than accounted for. Pierre-Sylvain Régis took the opposite horn, conceding that mind-body interaction is incomprehensible while insisting it is nonetheless real—an awkward stance that nonetheless protected the intuition, vital to emerging science, that secondary causes do genuine work.

The second objection is the slide toward [[epiphenomenalism|epiphenomenalism]]. If the mind never causally affects the body, mental states are causally idle with respect to behaviour—the defining mark of epiphenomenalism. Occasionalists reply that the mind is not *inert*: it has real internal life and God does act on its occasions. Whether "idle with respect to the body" already suffices for the epiphenomenalist verdict is the live question; the Map treats the resulting vulnerability as shared across all non-interactionist views and develops it in the [[epiphenomenalism]] article rather than here.

## Sibling Non-Interactionist Solutions

Occasionalism belongs to a family of theories that keep the dualist *distinction* while abandoning the causal *link*. Leibniz's **pre-established harmony** packs all coordination into a single creative act: God so constructs each substance that its states unfold by their own law yet correspond exactly to every other's, with no ongoing causal traffic. His two-clocks illustration frames the options as three ways two clocks might stay in agreement—mutual influence (interactionism), a craftsman continually adjusting them (occasionalism, which Leibniz mocks as perpetual miracle), or perfect initial construction (his own harmony). **Psychophysical parallelism** generalises the "two synchronised series, no interaction" picture, with the correlation grounded in God (Leibniz), in one substance under two attributes (Spinoza's dual-aspect reading), or left as a bare empirical posit (Fechner). The Spinozist version is the one to watch: it shares vocabulary with the Map's own [[bi-aspectual-ontology|bi-aspectual ontology]] while reaching the opposite verdict—the Map affirms interaction between the aspects, parallelism denies it. (Note that Leibniz's own metaphysics is a monadology, not Cartesian dualism; pre-established harmony is a solution the dualist tradition borrows, not a dualism Leibniz held.)

## Quantum Occasionalism: The Living Rival

Occasionalism's most interesting afterlife is the one that touches the Map directly. Contemporary *divine-action* theology relocates the classical move to the quantum joint, in the program the Map calls [[quantum-divine-action]]—Alvin Plantinga's **divine collapse-causation** and Robert John Russell's **non-interventionist objective divine action** (NIODA), on which God fixes otherwise-undetermined quantum outcomes without injecting energy or breaking any law. (An older analogical literature, beginning with Harding's 1993 comparison of al-Ghazālī's causal occasionalism to Copenhagen indeterminacy, is illustrative rather than a mechanism proposal.)

This is the sharpest dialectical material the rival offers, because it agrees with the Map on physics and disagrees only on the agent: quantum divine action uses the *same causal joint* the Map's [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] identifies but assigns the selecting to God rather than to creaturely minds. The dedicated [[quantum-divine-action]] article develops the full program and its objection ledger—sorting which pressures the Map inherits from the shared joint and which are peculiar to an omni-determining divine agent that the Map's selective, creaturely channel escapes.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Occasionalism is the position [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3, Bidirectional Interaction]] most directly opposes. The Map grants occasionalism its honest premise: Cartesian contact-style interaction between an unextended mind and an extended body really is unintelligible, and the [[pairing-problem|pairing problem]] really does press. What the Map rejects is the cure. Rather than removing creaturely causation, the Map *locates* it: a minimal, physically lawful [[selection-only-channel|selection channel]] at quantum indeterminacy ([[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Tenet 2]]) through which a creaturely mind biases which outcome becomes actual, without energy injection or Born-statistics violation. This keeps genuine creature-level bidirectional causation without a per-occasion external coordinator.

Against the quantum-occasionalist twin, the Map's reply mirrors Arnauld's—but only once an asymmetry is made explicit. Arnauld's *explains-too-much* charge bites hardest on an *omni-determining* agent: if God settles every event, the specific structure of nature and of agency is left as brute divine habit. The Map's channel is instead *selective and local*—a particular creaturely mind biasing particular outcomes—so the structure of agency stays located in creatures rather than dissolved into one universal will. Honesty requires admitting that both of occasionalism's sharpest weapons rebound on the Map. No Necessary Connection presses at least as hard on a probabilistic bias, which is a weaker candidate for a *perceivable necessary* connection than classical push-causation; and the explains-too-much charge presses on any view that locates causation at a joint without detailing the joint's inner workings. The Map does not answer NNC by claiming its channel meets the necessitarian standard—it rejects that standard. NNC helps itself to a conception on which only a *necessary* connection counts as causal, a foundational move occasionalism stipulates rather than argues for; the Map takes genuine causation to include lawful, non-necessitating influence of exactly the selective kind the channel names. What is left is the erasure of creaturely mental causation ([[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3]]) and a parsimony question. Here [[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5, Occam's Razor Has Limits]] binds symmetrically: the Map cannot claim that minimal creaturely selection at a few quantum events is *simpler than*, and therefore truer than, an omni-determining agent at every collapse—simplicity is not truth-tracking when knowledge is incomplete. The case against occasionalism is not that it is less parsimonious but that it discards the very thing the framework exists to secure. On [[tenets#^dualism|Tenet 1, Dualism]], the two views agree that mind is not reducible to matter; they part on whether finite minds have any causal reach at all.
