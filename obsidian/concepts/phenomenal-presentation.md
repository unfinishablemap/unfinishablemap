**Phenomenal presentation** is the passive, felt showing-up of qualities to a conscious subject—the way the world and one's own states are simply *there* for experience, prior to any act of attending, judging, or doing. When light fills a room, the brightness is presented before you decide to notice it; when a low ambient hum persists, it is felt as a background even while unattended. Phenomenal presentation names this category: experience as undergone rather than performed. The Unfinishable Map treats it as the most basic datum of consciousness—the bare fact that there is a felt *given*—and holds that this passivity is exactly what structural and functional descriptions cannot reconstruct.

## What Presentation Is

Presentation is the receptive or passive aspect of [[phenomenal-consciousness]]. Some content is *presented* to a subject: it appears, it is undergone, it has a felt character whether or not the subject does anything about it. This contrasts with the active dimensions of mental life—attending, comparing, classifying, choosing—which operate *on* what is presented.

Three features mark presentation as a distinct category:

**Passivity.** The presented is received rather than produced. You do not decide that the wall looks blue or that the ache in your shoulder hurts; these qualities are delivered to experience. Husserl's phenomenology calls this the dimension of *passive synthesis*—the pre-reflective constitution of a felt field that is already organised before active engagement begins.

**Background standing.** Much of what is presented sits at the periphery, felt but unattended: the pressure of the chair, the tone of the light, the low-level mood that colours a moment. This is the "passive-felt-background"—present in experience without being a target of attention. It is not absent and later summoned; it is there all along, awaiting notice.

**Pre-judgemental character.** Presentation precedes the formation of beliefs about it. The shade is presented before it is classified as "teal"; the warmth is felt before it is judged "too hot." Judgement, classification, and report are downstream operations that take the presented as their material.

## How Presentation Differs from Neighbouring Concepts

Phenomenal presentation is easy to conflate with several adjacent ideas. Keeping them distinct sharpens what the concept does.

**Versus [[phenomenal-acquaintance]].** Acquaintance is an *epistemic relation*—the direct, non-inferential knowing that a subject has of a quality, the relation that grounds *knowing what it is like*. Presentation is the *phenomenal fact* that the relation has something to relate to: that a quality shows up at all. Acquaintance is the subject's grasp of the given; presentation is the given's being-there. One can describe presentation without yet making any epistemic claim about what it yields.

**Versus [[phenomenal-depth]].** Depth concerns the layered internal *structure* within a presented state—the warmth-within-the-colour, the unfolding articulation revealed under closer attention. Presentation concerns the *mode* in which any such content is had: passively, as background, before articulation. A presented state may be deep or shallow; depth describes its richness, presentation describes its givenness.

**Versus [[phenomenal-overflow]].** Overflow concerns *breadth*—that more is phenomenally present than [[access-consciousness|access]] can report at a given moment. Presentation is the more general category: overflow is, in effect, a claim that the *presented* exceeds the *accessed*. Overflow presupposes presentation, because the overflowing content must be presented (felt) even where it is not accessed. Presentation is what overflow is overflow *of*.

**Versus active phenomenal character.** Not all experience is passive. Attending, willing, and effortful thinking have their own felt character (see [[phenomenology-of-choice-and-volition]] and [[cognitive-phenomenology]]). Phenomenal presentation isolates the *receptive* pole: the felt given that active phenomenal character operates upon. The two poles are continuous in lived experience but conceptually separable, and presentation is the one that does not depend on the subject's agency.

This map of distinctions matters because debates about consciousness often run them together—treating "what it is like" as a single undifferentiated phenomenon. Presentation isolates the most minimal layer: that there is a felt given, before anything is done with it.

## Why Presentation Resists Reduction

The passivity of presentation is what makes it hard for functional accounts. A functional description specifies what a system *does*—how inputs are transformed, which states cause which behaviours, what dispositions are realised. But presentation is precisely the dimension of experience that is *not* a doing. The brightness presented to you is not a behaviour, a disposition, or an information-processing step; it is a felt appearing that would remain even if no downstream processing occurred.

The challenge sharpens the [[explanatory-gap]]. One can grant a complete account of how a brain detects, encodes, and routes a signal—how it *processes*—and still have said nothing about why anything is *presented* to a subject in the course of that processing. Detection and routing are activities. Presentation is the felt standing-there that accompanies (or, on materialist accounts, somehow constitutes) them. To explain the activity is not yet to explain the appearing.

[[minimal-consciousness|Minimal consciousness]] may be little more than bare presentation: a felt field with scarcely any depth, breadth, or active engagement—just *something being given*. If such a state is possible, it shows that presentation is the floor of consciousness, the last thing that could be subtracted while leaving experience at all. That floor is what reductive accounts must reach, and the passivity of presentation is what they keep failing to capture: every functional story explains a doing, while presentation is an undergoing.

A related observation: presentation is closely tied to [[phenomenal-transparency-opacity-spectrum|transparency]]. In ordinary perception the presented qualities seem to belong to the world, not to the experience—you seem to see the blueness *of the sky*, not a blue patch *in your mind*. This transparency is a feature of how presentation works: the given is offered as world-directed (see [[intentionality]]) even though it is, as presented, a phenomenal fact about the subject's experience.

The strongest objection to any talk of a "pre-judgemental given" is Wilfrid Sellars's "Myth of the Given": the charge that nothing can be both non-conceptual and capable of justifying a belief, so a bare given that is supposed to ground knowledge while owing nothing to concepts is a confused posit. The Map grants Sellars his target and is untouched by it. Sellars attacks an *epistemic* given—a self-authenticating foundation that confers justification without conceptual mediation. Phenomenal presentation makes no such epistemic claim. It asserts only that something is *felt* before it is judged—a claim about phenomenal character, not about warrant. One can accept that all *justification* is conceptually structured (Sellars's point) and still hold that the warmth is felt before any concept classifies it (presentation's point). The felt showing-up is a phenomenal fact; whether and how it justifies anything is a separate question, handled by [[phenomenal-acquaintance|acquaintance]]. Keeping presentation distinct from acquaintance is exactly what lets the concept absorb the Sellarsian objection: presentation owes nothing to the epistemology Sellars dismantles.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: Phenomenal presentation is, for the Map, the cleanest statement of the core datum that resists physicalism. Materialism describes reality as structure and dynamics—what things are made of and what they do. Presentation is neither structure nor dynamics; it is the passive showing-up of a felt quality to a subject. A complete account of the brain's processing specifies activities exhaustively yet leaves untouched the question of why those activities are accompanied by anything being *given*. The Map holds that this is an ontological gap, not a temporary explanatory one: the felt given belongs to a domain that the vocabulary of doing cannot reach.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: Presentation might seem purely receptive and therefore [[epiphenomenalism|epiphenomenal]]—a passenger that registers without affecting. The Map resists this. That a subject can *report* what is presented, and act differently because of how the background feels, indicates that the presented does causal work: what is felt as background shapes mood, attention's next move, and choice. A purely passive phenomenal field that made no difference to any downstream output would leave unexplained why reports track presentation so reliably.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: A reductive theory can achieve apparent economy by treating presentation as just a way of talking about information being available. But this redescribes the passive given as an active disposition—precisely the move that drops what was to be explained. Where simplicity is bought by renaming the explanandum, the Map reads it as oversimplification rather than insight.
