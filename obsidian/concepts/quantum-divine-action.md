Quantum divine action (QDA) is the theological program that locates God's special action at the point of quantum measurement: God fixes which physically-permitted outcome becomes actual, injecting no energy and breaking no law. It is the single closest live rival to the position this site defends, because it agrees with the Map's physics *exactly* and disagrees only on the agent. The Map holds that a creaturely mind biases quantum outcomes ([[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3]]); QDA assigns that same biasing to God. Since the two proposals are physically co-located—both work at the indeterminacy the collapse of the wave function leaves open—no experiment can distinguish a divine selector from a creaturely one. That empirical indistinguishability is the honest predicament this article confronts, and it forces the Map to state, openly, that it takes its side on non-empirical, framework-relative grounds rather than on evidence or parsimony.

The core contribution below is an **objection ledger** (introduced in [Inherited versus peculiar objections](#inherited-versus-peculiar-objections)): a sorting of the standard objections to QDA into those the Map *inherits*, because they arise from the shared quantum joint, and those *peculiar* to an omni-determining divine agent, which the Map's selective and local creaturely channel escapes.

This page treats QDA as a named rival doctrine, standing beside [[occasionalism]] (its historical ancestor), [[panpsychism]], and [[illusionism]]. It differs from occasionalism, which relocates *all* causation to God as a general metaphysics; from the Map's own [[selection-only-channel|selection-only channel]], which shares the joint but places a finite mind there; and it is the specifically *quantum* descendant of the classical position [[occasionalism]] reconstructs in depth.

## NIODA: Russell's Non-Interventionist Objective Divine Action

The flagship form of QDA is Robert John Russell's **Non-Interventionist Objective Divine Action** (NIODA), developed across the five-volume CTNS/Vatican Observatory "Scientific Perspectives on Divine Action" series (1990s–2002). Russell holds that God acts *objectively* (a real event in the world, not merely in a believer's perception) and *specially* (a particular providential act beyond creation and conservation), yet *non-interventionistically* (breaking no law), by determining which outcome is realized among the ontologically-open possibilities the wave function leaves undetermined. Russell describes God as acting like a "nonphysical hidden variable" that fixes the precise outcome.

Russell insists on two conditions. First, a *collapse* interpretation of quantum mechanics on which the indeterminism is ontological rather than merely epistemic, so there is a genuine gap in which to act. Second, that divine action stay *within* the Born-rule probability distribution over long runs, so no statistical signature betrays it. Both conditions are exactly the ones the Map's own mechanism must meet.

## Plantinga's Divine Collapse-Causation

Alvin Plantinga makes the same structural move from the analytic-philosophy side. In *Where the Conflict Really Lies* (2011) he proposes **divine collapse-causation**: when a quantum measurement collapses the wave function to a definite result, it is God who causes that particular outcome. Because collapse is not fixed by prior physical law, God supplies the determination "without in any way abrogating the laws of physics." Plantinga finds the view attractive partly because it dovetails with libertarian free will.

Plantinga's version tends toward God causing *all* collapses—constant engagement everywhere—which sharpens the omni-determining worry the ledger below isolates. In a companion methodological essay, "What is 'Intervention'?" (2008), he argues that on contemporary quantum mechanics there is no principled way even to *define* intervention, so the alleged conflict between science and special divine action cannot be stated in the first place.

## The Scope Split: Murphy, Tracy, Ellis

Within bottom-up QDA a scope disagreement runs directly parallel to a choice the Map must also make. **Nancey Murphy** holds that God acts in *every* quantum measurement event, "activating or actualizing one or other of the quantum entity's innate powers," with macroscopic effects arising by amplification. **Thomas Tracy** holds that God acts in *only some* events—the "particular providence" version—which he argues sits better with treating the laws of nature as reflecting nature's genuine structure. Tracy also raises the "God of the gaps" worry against his own position: selecting only some events can look like exploiting an epistemic gap.

**George Ellis** combines quantum indeterminism with top-down causation, exploring quantum-triggered genetic mutation as a concrete amplification pathway from micro-indeterminacy to macroscopic biological consequence.

The all-events / some-events / creaturely axis is the very axis on which the Map plants its flag. The Map's channel is Tracy-like in *scope* (selective) but Murphy-unlike in *agent* (creaturely, not divine).

## Inherited versus peculiar objections

The load-bearing distinction—which no existing Map article assembles—is between objections the Map shares with QDA and objections that fall on QDA alone.

**Inherited (they arise from the shared quantum joint):**

1. **Measurement-problem dependence.** Both NIODA and the Map need a collapse interpretation with genuine ontological indeterminism. If a deterministic hidden-variable theory (Bohmian mechanics) is correct, the open gap vanishes. The Map independently rejects Many-Worlds under [[tenets#^no-many-worlds|Tenet 4]], removing one horn, but the Bohmian horn and the general unsettledness of the measurement problem remain shared liabilities.
2. **Decoherence and the amplification problem.** Environmental decoherence at warm, wet, macroscopic scales appears to leave little exploitable indeterminacy, and single determinations rarely aggregate predictably into macroscopic effects. Murphy's accumulation model is widely judged to fail here. The Map faces the identical problem at *neural* scales.
3. **Episodicity.** Collapse events are sporadic and discrete; continuous, coherent agency has to be reconstructed from a stutter of isolated determinations. This presses on any quantum-interface proposal, divine or creaturely.

**Peculiar (they arise from an omni-determining divine agent, and the Map's selective, local channel escapes or transforms them):**

4. **Theodicy.** If God determines quantum outcomes—including the mutations behind cancers and the events behind natural disasters—God bears direct responsibility for natural evil. Qureshi-Hurst and Bennett press exactly this against NIODA's extensive agency over evolution and genetics. A creaturely selective channel does not universalize responsibility this way.
5. **The deism-versus-occasionalism dilemma.** Too little divine action collapses toward deism (an absentee God); too much—Plantinga's "all collapses," Murphy's "every event"—collapses toward [[occasionalism]], God as the sole true cause, erasing creaturely causation. QDA must thread a needle the Map sidesteps, because it locates agency in creatures from the start.
6. **The scope / Born-statistics explosion.** This is Arnauld's classical *explains-too-much* charge in modern dress. If God sets *every* outcome, the observed Born statistics become brute divine habit rather than something explained, and the specificity of nature dissolves into one universal will. A selective, local biasing of a few events does not incur this explosion.
7. **The "non-interventionist" label dispute.** Critics (Saunders; Qureshi-Hurst) argue that determining countless outcomes throughout the universe is *maximally* interventionist in substance, so the "non-interventionist" branding misrepresents the view. This objection is specific to the universal-scope divine version.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's stand against QDA is **non-empirical and framework-relative**, and saying so plainly is a requirement of intellectual honesty rather than a weakness to be hidden. Because QDA and the Map share their physics exactly, no experiment can adjudicate between them; the disagreement is entirely over the agent.

[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)]] names the shared joint: quantum indeterminacy as a real, law-respecting opening for non-physical determination, with no energy injected and the Born distribution preserved over long runs. QDA and the Map agree here almost word for word. The parting is at [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)]]: the Map commits to *finite* minds having genuine causal reach, whereas QDA routes all such reach through God. The Map takes the creaturely-agent side as an avowed commitment—the framework exists precisely to secure creaturely mental causation—and not because it has evidence QDA lacks.

Two refusals keep this honest. First, the Map refuses any parsimony win. [[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)]] binds symmetrically: the Map cannot claim that minimal creaturely selection at a few events is *simpler than*, and therefore truer than, an omni-determining agent at every collapse. Simplicity does not track truth when knowledge is incomplete. The case against QDA is not that it is less parsimonious but that it discards the thing the Map's framework exists to secure. Second, the Map refuses any pretense of empirical adjudication; the co-location is admitted in full.

On [[tenets#^dualism|Tenet 1 (Dualism)]], the two views agree that mind and agency are not reducible to matter—a non-physical agent acts at the joint in both. They part only on whether *finite* minds have any causal reach at all. What the ledger above secures is that the objections the Map genuinely shares with QDA are the tractable, physics-facing ones, while the objections that would be decisive—theodicy, the drift to occasionalism, the Born-statistics explosion—fall on the universal-scope divine version and not on a selective creaturely channel.
