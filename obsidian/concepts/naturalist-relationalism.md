Naturalist relationalism—also called naive realism, the relational view, or Bill Brewer's Object View—holds that a veridical perceptual experience *fundamentally consists* in a conscious relation of acquaintance between a subject and mind-independent objects, such that those objects and their features are literal *constituents* of the experience and *constitute its phenomenal character*. It secures direct contact with the world while invoking no inner experiential medium and no non-physical mind. For The Unfinishable Map this makes it the sharpest contemporary non-dualist rival in the philosophy of perception. It shares the Map's phenomenology of transparency and direct world-presentation, yet draws the opposite metaphysical conclusion—and it does so without a caricatured "direct realism" that ignores how perception can fail.

The Map engages relationalism as a rival, not an ally. Relationalists would reject the Map's [[dualist-perception|interface]] conclusion outright. The point of contact is precisely the disagreement: relationalism relocates qualitative character into a perceiver–world relation but leaves unexplained why that relation is *felt from the inside at all*. That residue—not any charge of incoherence—is the Map's leverage.

## What Relationalism Claims

Two theses, often separated in the contemporary literature (Logue, French), define the position.

The **Relational Claim**: perceptual experience fundamentally consists in the subject bearing a perceptual relation of [[phenomenal-acquaintance|acquaintance]] to mind-independent objects. Experience is not a representation that could in principle occur with no object present; it is a relation that *requires* its objects as relata.

The **Constitutive Claim**: the phenomenal character of a veridical experience is at least partly *constituted by* those mind-independent objects and their properties—not merely caused by them, and not merely represented. When you see a ripe tomato, the tomato's redness and roundness are not copied onto an inner screen; they enter into the very character of your experience.

"Naive realism" and "relationalism" function as near-synonyms in current usage, though they are not strictly identical: a live sub-debate asks whether a naive realist must be a full relationalist, since the Constitutive and Relational claims can in principle come apart. The Map treats them together while noting the distinction, to avoid conflating them.

Two arguments motivate the view.

**Transparency.** When you introspect a perceptual experience, you find only the worldly scene—the tomato, the table, the light—never an inner medium or mental object standing between you and the world. Relationalism takes this [[phenomenal-transparency-opacity-spectrum|transparency]] at face value: there is no inner item to find because the experience is *of* the world directly. This is the same datum representationalists cite, read in a stronger, anti-representational key.

**Openness and presentation.** Perception "presents" the world as simply, blankly there, in a way that (the relationalist argues) mere representation cannot capture. A representation can misfire while its content stays intact; presentation, by contrast, brings the object itself into view. Campbell's *Reference and Consciousness* (2002) makes the relational structure of experience explain how we can think demonstratively about objects at all—the object must be *there*, in experience, to be a target of thought.

Brewer frames the choice through what he calls an inconsistent triad: (I) physical objects are mind-independent; (II) physical objects are the direct objects of perception; (III) the direct objects of perception are mind-dependent. The three cannot all hold. The sense-datum tradition rejected (II), inserting mind-dependent intermediaries. Relationalism instead rejects (III): the direct objects of perception just are the mind-independent things themselves.

## Why It Is the Strongest Non-Dualist Rival

Relationalism is thoroughly naturalist. It posits no non-physical mind, no irreducible experiential domain on the subject's side, no Cartesian theatre. Yet it delivers what the Map's phenomenology emphasises: experience feels like open, direct, qualitatively rich contact with a world that is genuinely there.

This is what makes it dangerous to the Map's inference rather than merely opposed to it. The Map leans heavily on transparency and directness as data. A common worry is that those data *point toward* an interface or a phenomenal medium. Relationalism is the clean demonstration that they do not, by themselves, entail any such thing. The transparency datum the Map relies on underdetermines dualism: a wholly naturalist theory can honour it. Anyone who reasons "perception feels like direct contact, therefore there is a phenomenal interface constituting that contact" has skipped the relationalist alternative, on which the directness is the relation itself with nothing further on the subject's side.

So the Map cannot dismiss relationalism as a naive direct realism that forgets illusion and hallucination. The contemporary view—Martin, Campbell, Brewer, and the newer defenders Logue, French, Raleigh, Phillips, Martina—is a worked-out position with explicit machinery for perceptual error. Facing that machinery, not a strawman, is the only honest engagement.

## How It Handles the Problem of Perception

The signature liability of relationalism is the **problem of perception**: the arguments from illusion and hallucination, which press the worry that something *other than* the mind-independent object is the real direct object of awareness.

### Illusion and objective looks

The argument from illusion runs: a white wall under yellow light looks yellow; the wall is not yellow; so the immediate object of awareness—the thing that *is* yellow—cannot be the wall. The premise doing the work is the "Phenomenal Principle": if something looks F, then something present to awareness actually *is* F.

Brewer's reply denies the principle by giving "looks" an objective reading. An object *thinly looks* F, on his account, when it has the appropriate **visually relevant similarities** to paradigm examples of F, from the perceiver's viewpoint and in the circumstances of perception—similarities in such things as the way light is reflected from the objects in question and the physical processes underlying vision that handle the resulting stimuli (Brewer 2011). The white wall under yellow light genuinely has visually relevant similarities to paradigm yellow things under standard light. So it "looks yellow" without any inner yellow item existing anywhere. Illusion becomes a fact about the object-in-its-conditions, not evidence for a mental intermediary.

Ian Phillips extends this to the charge that naive realism is "hopelessly out of touch with vision science" (Phillips 2016). His abstract grants the complaint its due and offers a "partial reply," arguing that "careful reflection on a simple, empirically grounded model of illusion reveals heterodox ways of thinking about familiar illusions which are quite congenial to the naïve realist" (Phillips 2016). On the Müller-Lyer—two equal lines that look unequal because of their arrowhead fins—the relationalist reading treats the figure as bearing visually relevant similarities to genuinely unequal configurations, similarities that capture attention in context, rather than as an inner misrepresentation of length. This is the relationalist who most directly answers the vision-science objection, and so the version a serious critique must engage.

### Hallucination and disjunctivism

Hallucination is harder. A total hallucination can be subjectively indistinguishable from a veridical perception yet has no object at all. If perception and matching hallucination are the same fundamental kind of mental state—the **Common Kind Claim**—then whatever is common to both is the real direct object, and the mind-independent thing drops out as inessential even in the veridical case.

The relationalist's defence is **disjunctivism** (originated by J.M. Hinton in the 1960s–70s; developed by Snowdon, McDowell, and Martin). Disjunctivism rejects the Common Kind Claim: veridical perception and indistinguishable hallucination are *not* one fundamental kind but two. This lets the relationalist keep mind-independent objects as constituents of veridical perception, since they manifestly cannot constitute a hallucination where no object is present.

Martin's **negative epistemic** version takes the deflationary route furthest. A causally matching hallucination, on this account, has no positive phenomenal nature of its own beyond *being not introspectively knowably distinct* from a veridical perception of such-and-such. This is engineered to dodge the **screening-off problem**: if the hallucination had a robust positive phenomenal character, that character—being shareable with the matching veridical perception—would do all the explanatory work and render the world-involving relation explanatorily idle. The cost is an almost contentless account of what hallucination *is*, a point the Map returns to below.

### Blur and pluralism

Giulia Martina's "Lessons from Blur" (2024) shows the machinery handling degraded perception. Martina argues for a relationalist *pluralism*: we owe neither a unified explanation of blurry experience nor the same explanation across all its cases. Blur recruits several distinct factors, and some of its character is fixed by the *relation* between perceiver and scene under poor viewing conditions rather than by represented content. A relationalist can thus grant that blur degrades the phenomenal field yet deny that it exposes any subject-owned phenomenal medium: the haziness is a fact about the perceptual relation under poor viewing, not a property of an inner experience-bearer.

The contemporary debate is live and unsettled. The Routledge volume *The Relational View of Perception* (2025) consolidates the new-wave defences, including Logue and Raleigh on total hallucinations. This is a working research programme, not a museum piece.

## Where It Strains

The Map's leverage is not that relationalism fails on its own terms. It is that the position pays for its directness in coin that, by the Map's lights, leaves the central explanandum untouched. Four pressure points matter.

**The felt-from-inside residue.** This is the deepest. Relationalism relocates qualitative character into a perceiver–world relation, but relocation is not dissolution. Granting that the tomato's redness is a constituent of the relation, the question survives: why is standing in that relation *like something* for the subject? A relation can hold—a planet can orbit a star, a key can fit a lock—without anyone's experiencing it from within. Relationalism specifies the relata and the relation; it does not specify why the relation has an interior aspect at all. This is the [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] reappearing at the joint of the relation, and on the Map's reading relationalism has changed the address of the difficulty without paying it off.

**The deflation of failure cases.** The negative-disjunctivist account of hallucination buys direct contact at the price of a phenomenology of failure that is nearly contentless—hallucination as a state defined only by what it cannot be told apart from. Where perception fails, the relationalist's resources thin out exactly where the Map finds the interface most visible. The [[perceptual-failure-and-the-interface|degradation cases]]—blur, eigengrau, Charles Bonnet imagery—are, on the Map's reading, the diagnostic moments; an account that treats their phenomenal character as derivative or negative is straining against its hardest data.

**The causal argument.** A subjectively matching hallucination can be produced by the same proximate brain state as a veridical perception. If identical proximate causes yield identical proximate effects, there should be a common mental element shared by the two cases—precisely the Common Kind Claim disjunctivism must deny. This remains the most pressing objection to disjunctivism, and no consensus resolves it.

**The naturalist-internal verdict.** The Map does not have to import dualist premises to find relationalism's treatment of error contested. Byrne and Green, in "Whither Naïve Realism?" (2023), argue that illusions are the best proving ground for naive realism against representationalism—and that the two leading relationalist treatments of illusion, Fish's and Brewer's objective-looks account, both fail; their companion paper sketches a representational alternative. Since representationalism is itself a non-dualist view, this is an attack from *within* the naturalist family. The relationalist's handling of error is disputed even among its naturalist neighbours, which the Map can note without claiming the dispute for dualism.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Relationalism bears directly on **Tenet 1 (Dualism)**. It is the cleanest available demonstration that the transparency and directness data the Map prizes do *not* by themselves entail an irreducible experiential domain. A fully naturalist theory honours those data. The Map's reply is therefore not "transparency proves dualism"—relationalism refutes that inference—but the narrower, harder claim that relationalism leaves a residue: the felt-from-inside character of the perceptual relation. The Map locates that residue on the phenomenal side of the [[dualist-perception|interface]], where the relationalist locates nothing at all. The disagreement is genuine and bedrock; it is not a defect inside relationalism that the Map can refute on relationalist terms.

**Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)** governs how the Map may press the point. Relationalism is, on its face, the simpler theory: it posits no extra ontological domain. By Tenet 5, simplicity does not settle the matter—least of all here, where what is missing is any account of how a relation could be *experienced*. The Map cannot win by calling relationalism extravagant (it is not) any more than it may dismiss rivals as merely complex. The case rests on the explanatory residue, not on parsimony in either direction. The Map applies the same discipline to its own interface posit: the interface earns its place by addressing the felt-from-inside question relationalism leaves open, not by being simpler.

The Map's own position is a **hybrid indirect realism**: the phenomenal interface is itself constitutive—"neither private theatre nor unmediated contact, but the experiential face of an interaction across the divide" ([[dualist-perception]]). Relationalism is the precise foil. It keeps the directness and discards the subject-side medium; the Map keeps a subject-side phenomenal medium and reinterprets the directness as the experiential face of an interaction. That single axis—whether there is anything experiential on the subject's side of the perceptual relation—is where the two views part, and relationalism marks it more sharply than any other non-dualist rival.
