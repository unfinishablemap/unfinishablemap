Consistent Histories (CH), also called Decoherent Histories, is a single-world, realist interpretation of quantum mechanics that removes measurement, observers, and wave-function collapse from the foundations. It treats quantum theory as a stochastic theory of *histories*—time-sequences of properties—and assigns ordinary, interference-free probabilities only to families of histories meeting a mathematical "consistency" condition. Presented on its own terms, CH is one of the most economical no-collapse interpretations that still keeps a single actual world: no many worlds, no hidden variables, no fundamental observer, and no added collapse dynamics.

The Map engages CH as a serious competitor rather than a resource. It is an *ally* against Many-Worlds (both are single-world; CH denies superpositions the status of physical coexistence) and a *rival* to consciousness-linked collapse (CH needs no mind, no observer, nothing extra-physical to fix outcomes). One structural feature—framework-dependence, and the absence of any law selecting a preferred framework (the [Dowker–Kent set-selection gap](#the-dowker-kent-set-selection-critique), explained below)—raises a question the Map is entitled to ask but not to answer in its own favour: does that gap leave room for a consciousness-selected framework? CH proponents say no selection is *needed* at all. So a consciousness-selection reading is at most a *possible extension the Map might explore*—not a proof of dualism, and not something CH itself endorses.

## Histories as Sequences of Properties

The basic object of CH is a **history**: a temporal succession of events, each an eigenvalue or property represented by a projection operator (a "projector"). Formally a history is a product of projectors at successive times, acting on a "history Hilbert space." Where standard textbook quantum mechanics tracks a state evolving toward a measurement, CH tracks whole time-sequences of properties directly and asks how probable each sequence is.

A **framework** is the sample space for these probabilities. Technically it is a projective decomposition of the identity—a set of mutually orthogonal projectors that sum to the identity—extended across the relevant times. Griffiths (1984) generalized the standard transition-probability formula to yield conditional probabilities for such sequences of events, "consistent histories," applicable to closed systems, time-symmetric (past and future interchangeable), with no collapse and no reference to measurement (Griffiths 1984). Because the framework is fixed before probabilities are assigned, CH is unusually explicit about a normally hidden step: *which* set of alternatives one is asking about.

## The Consistency (Decoherence) Condition

Not every family of histories can carry sensible probabilities. Because quantum amplitudes interfere, naively assigning probabilities to arbitrary history sets violates the classical additivity rule—the probability of "A or B" would not equal the sum of the parts. CH restricts probabilities to families where the interference cross-terms between distinct histories vanish (or are negligible). This is the **consistency condition**; Gell-Mann and Hartle's independently developed **decoherent histories** formulation states it via a *decoherence functional*, with medium decoherence implying consistency (Gell-Mann & Hartle 1990). When the cross-terms vanish, the Born rule delivers a genuine, classical probability distribution over the histories in that family.

[[decoherence|Decoherence]]—environmental entanglement that scrambles phase relations—is what typically makes macroscopic, "quasiclassical" families consistent. Gell-Mann and Hartle frame this cosmologically: the everyday classical domain is *derived* from a decoherence process among coarse-grained histories of a closed universe, the universe being the only strictly closed system. On this reading the classical world is not added by hand; it is the coarse-grained, decohered face of a quantum universe that needs no external observer.

The payoff CH claims is the dissolution of the [[measurement-problem|measurement problem]]. "Measurement" is just one physical interaction among many, analyzable inside a suitable framework; apparatus and observer are ordinary quantum systems. As the interpretation's own summary puts it, "There is no measurement problem, and under appropriate conditions one can discuss the microscopic properties revealed by measurements" (Griffiths 2014/2024). Collapse survives only as "a handy calculational tool," not a physical event.

## Framework-Dependence and the Single-Framework Rule

CH's most distinctive—and most contested—feature is **framework-dependence**. Many mutually incompatible consistent families exist for the same system, and the theory offers no law selecting a preferred one. Griffiths organizes this with four principles: *Liberty* (multiple frameworks may be used), *Equality* (no framework is more fundamental than another), *Incompatibility* (incompatible frameworks are never combined into a single description), and *Utility* (the choice of framework is guided by the question asked). He calls this quantum "pluricity," contrasting it with the classical assumption of "unicity"—the tacit idea that there is one true, all-encompassing description.

The **single-framework rule** enforces the *Incompatibility* principle. In *Consistent Quantum Theory*, Griffiths presents it as Bohr's complementarity made mathematically precise: any prediction must be confined to a single framework, and combining elements from incompatible frameworks yields quantum-mechanically meaningless statements (Griffiths 2002). Within one framework, ordinary classical logic holds; different frameworks can license different conclusions, but the rule forbids stitching them together, which is how CH blocks the standard no-go paradoxes without adding structure. Omnès (1994) develops the same core into a "consistent revision of the Copenhagen interpretation," supplying logical rules for when a quantum statement counts as true or reliable within a framework and using decoherence to recover classical logic macroscopically.

Griffiths reads all this **realistically**: there is one world with definite, framework-relative properties, and the multiplicity of frameworks is like the multiplicity of coordinate systems, not a multiplicity of worlds. No framework is privileged, and on his view that is a feature, not a defect—demanding a privileged one merely imports the classical unicity assumption CH rejects.

## The Dowker–Kent Set-Selection Critique

The canonical objection is Dowker and Kent (1996). Reviewing the Griffiths, Omnès, and Gell-Mann–Hartle formulations and classifying the consistent sets, they argue that the formalism treats all consistent families "democratically" and supplies no criterion selecting a preferred one. With no law of nature fixing the framework, there is no way to compute *unconditional* probabilities for specific histories—probabilities are always conditional on a framework chosen from outside the physics. They further argue that Omnès' characterization of "true" statements is defective and press difficulties for the Gell-Mann–Hartle reading. Their conclusion: CH must be *supplemented* by some set-selection principle before it can serve as a fundamental theory capable of unconditional predictions. This became Kent's **set-selection problem**.

The disagreement is genuine and unresolved. Griffiths replies that framework-conditional probabilities are exactly what a quantum theory should deliver—probabilities are conditional on a chosen framework the way classical probabilities are conditional on a chosen sample space, and no "law of nature" need pick one. The dispute turns on a prior question: must a physical theory deliver framework-independent, unconditional probabilities, or are framework-conditional probabilities plus the single-framework rule enough? No consensus set-selection principle has been found; CH remains a live minority interpretation, and the critique is widely regarded as unanswered rather than refuted.

A related worry sharpens the seam: critics ask whether the reliance on *question-relative* frameworks quietly smuggles the observer back in as the unmodeled agent who picks the question. Griffiths treats framework choice as a harmless pragmatic step, like choosing coordinates. Whether it is that innocent, or a covert reintroduction of an agent at the foundations, is precisely the point on which CH's realist self-understanding is contested.

## Where CH Sits in the Landscape

CH occupies a distinctive corner of the interpretive map, surveyed more broadly in [[qm-interpretations-beyond-many-worlds|Quantum Interpretations Beyond Many-Worlds]]. Unlike Bohmian mechanics it adds no hidden variables; unlike GRW/CSL it adds no collapse dynamics; unlike Many-Worlds it keeps a single actual history and denies superpositions physical coexistence; unlike QBism and relational quantum mechanics it aims to be realist about a single world rather than agent- or observer-relative about states. It positions itself as "Copenhagen done right": measurement loses fundamental status, all evolution is stochastic via the Born rule, and collapse is demoted to bookkeeping. Its price is framework-dependence and the unsolved set-selection problem—an honest ledger that CH pays openly rather than hiding.

## Relation to Site Perspective

CH interacts with three of the Map's [[tenets|tenets]] in ways worth stating precisely, keeping the interpretation's own claims separate from the Map's interpretive overlay.

**Tenet 4 (No Many-Worlds): ally.** CH is explicitly single-world. Only one history in a consistent family is realized, and superpositions are denied the status of physically coexisting branches. CH thus rejects [[concepts/many-worlds|Many-Worlds]] without paying either the hidden-variable price (Bohm) or the added-collapse price (GRW/CSL). For a framework that rejects MWI on indexical grounds, CH is evidence that single-world realism is a live, technically serious option—not a nostalgic holdout.

**Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction): rival.** CH removes any fundamental role for consciousness. Outcomes are fixed by the stochastic dynamics within a framework, not by a mind. The Map must engage CH as a genuine competitor to consciousness-linked collapse, not fold it into dualism. Honesty requires stating plainly: CH proponents hold that *no* selection is needed, and the framework is chosen for descriptive convenience, not by consciousness.

**Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits): cuts both ways.** CH is admirably economical—it adds nothing to standard quantum mechanics except an interpretive discipline. The Map does not treat that parsimony as decisive, precisely because Tenet 5 warns that simplicity is an unreliable guide when knowledge is incomplete. The measurement problem's persistence is itself a sign of such incompleteness.

The Map's distinctive question is whether the [Dowker–Kent set-selection gap](#the-dowker-kent-set-selection-critique) leaves room for a *consciousness-selected* framework. There is a structural resonance with the Map's recurring claim—developed in [[quantum-measurement-and-consciousness|the measurement problem as philosophical problem]]—that a complete third-person description can fail to fix a first-person fact. CH gives an exact, formal version: the dynamics plus consistency conditions do not pick the framework. The Map *speculates* that this gap might be where a conscious selection enters, mirroring the [[quantum-darwinism-and-consciousness|outcome-gap reading of quantum Darwinism]].

Two disciplines apply. First, this is a *possible extension to explore*, not a proof: CH proponents deny that anything need select at all, so the Map would have to argue *for* a selection need, not assume it—and Griffiths' single-framework rule and framework-conditional probabilities are a coherent way of denying that need. Second, the parallel may be a disanalogy: in the measurement problem an outcome *is* needed and unexplained, whereas CH claims no framework selection is needed. The Map registers the affinity without claiming CH shares its ontology, and treats framework-dependence as an open seam rather than a hidden endorsement of dualism.
