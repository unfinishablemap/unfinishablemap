Consistent Histories (CH), also called Decoherent Histories, is—on its standard, realist (Griffiths) reading—a single-world interpretation of quantum mechanics that removes measurement, observers, and wave-function collapse from the foundations. It treats quantum theory as a stochastic theory of *histories*—time-sequences of properties—and assigns ordinary, interference-free probabilities only to families of histories meeting a mathematical "consistency" condition. Presented on its own terms, CH is one of the most economical no-collapse interpretations that still keeps a single actual world: no many worlds, no hidden variables, no fundamental observer, and no added collapse dynamics.

The Map engages CH as a serious competitor rather than a resource. On Griffiths' single-world reading it is an *ally* against Many-Worlds (both keep one actual world; CH denies superpositions the status of physical coexistence)—but that alliance is conditional, because a realist reading of *all* consistent families can be argued to collapse back into many-worlds ([explained below](#relation-to-site-perspective)), so the payoff hangs on Griffiths' reading, not on CH as such. On every reading CH is a *rival* to consciousness-linked collapse: it needs no mind, no observer, nothing extra-physical to fix outcomes. One structural feature—framework-dependence, and the absence of any law selecting a preferred framework (the [Dowker–Kent set-selection gap](#the-dowker-kent-set-selection-critique), explained below)—raises a question the Map is entitled to ask but not to answer in its own favour: does that gap leave room for a consciousness-selected framework? CH proponents say no selection is *needed* at all. So a consciousness-selection reading is at most a *possible extension the Map might explore*—not a proof of dualism, and not something CH itself endorses.

## Histories as Sequences of Properties

The basic object of CH is a **history**: a temporal succession of events, each a property or event represented by a projection operator (a "projector"). Two related formal objects encode a history, and it is worth keeping them apart. As a *history projector* it is a tensor product of the time-indexed projectors, defined on an enlarged "history Hilbert space" that tensors together one copy of the system's Hilbert space for each time. As a *chain operator* (or class operator) it is the time-ordered product of those same projectors interleaved with the unitary propagators that carry the system between the times, acting on the ordinary system Hilbert space; the chain operator is what the Born-rule weight of the history is computed from. Where standard textbook quantum mechanics tracks a state evolving toward a measurement, CH tracks whole time-sequences of properties directly and asks how probable each sequence is.

A **framework** is the sample space for these probabilities. Technically it is a projective decomposition of the identity—a set of mutually orthogonal projectors that sum to the identity—extended across the relevant times. Griffiths (1984) generalized the standard transition-probability formula to yield conditional probabilities for such sequences of events, "consistent histories," applicable to closed systems, time-symmetric (past and future interchangeable), with no collapse and no reference to measurement (Griffiths 1984). Because the framework is fixed before probabilities are assigned, CH is unusually explicit about a normally hidden step: *which* set of alternatives one is asking about.

## The Consistency (Decoherence) Condition

Not every family of histories can carry sensible probabilities. Because quantum amplitudes interfere, naively assigning probabilities to arbitrary history sets violates the classical additivity rule—the probability of "A or B" would not equal the sum of the parts. CH restricts probabilities to families where the interference cross-terms between distinct histories vanish (or are negligible). This is the **consistency condition**; Gell-Mann and Hartle's independently developed **decoherent histories** formulation states it via a *decoherence functional*, with medium decoherence implying consistency (Gell-Mann & Hartle 1990). When the cross-terms vanish, the Born rule delivers a genuine, classical probability distribution over the histories in that family.

[[decoherence|Decoherence]]—environmental entanglement that scrambles phase relations—is what typically makes macroscopic, "quasiclassical" families consistent. Gell-Mann and Hartle frame this cosmologically: the aim is to *derive* the everyday classical domain from a decoherence process among coarse-grained histories of a closed universe, the universe being the only strictly closed system. This is an active and contested research programme rather than a finished result—whether a genuinely quasiclassical domain can be singled out from consistency and decoherence alone is exactly what the [Dowker–Kent critique](#the-dowker-kent-set-selection-critique) below disputes. On the programme's intended reading the classical world would not be added by hand but would emerge as the coarse-grained, decohered face of a quantum universe that needs no external observer.

The payoff CH claims is the dissolution of the [[measurement-problem|measurement problem]]. "Measurement" is just one physical interaction among many, analyzable inside a suitable framework; apparatus and observer are ordinary quantum systems. As the interpretation's own summary puts it, "There is no measurement problem, and under appropriate conditions one can discuss the microscopic properties revealed by measurements" (Griffiths 2014/2024). Wave-function collapse survives only as bookkeeping: on Griffiths' account, in the specific setting of reasoning about a measured subsystem, "there is no need to invoke wave function collapse except, perhaps, as a handy calculational tool," rather than as a physical event.

## Framework-Dependence and the Single-Framework Rule

CH's most distinctive—and most contested—feature is **framework-dependence**. Many mutually incompatible consistent families exist for the same system, and the theory offers no law selecting a preferred one. Griffiths organizes this with four principles: *Liberty* (multiple frameworks may be used), *Equality* (no framework is more fundamental than another), *Incompatibility* (incompatible frameworks are never combined into a single description), and *Utility* (the choice of framework is guided by the question asked). He calls this quantum "pluricity," contrasting it with the classical assumption of "unicity"—the tacit idea that there is one true, all-encompassing description.

The **single-framework rule** enforces the *Incompatibility* principle. In *Consistent Quantum Theory*, Griffiths presents it as Bohr's complementarity made mathematically precise: any prediction must be confined to a single framework, and combining elements from incompatible frameworks yields quantum-mechanically meaningless statements (Griffiths 2002). Within one framework, ordinary classical logic holds; different frameworks can license different conclusions, but the rule forbids stitching them together, which is how CH blocks the standard no-go paradoxes without adding structure. Omnès (1994) develops the same core into a "consistent revision of the Copenhagen interpretation," supplying logical rules for when a quantum statement counts as true or reliable within a framework and using decoherence to recover classical logic macroscopically.

Griffiths reads all this **realistically**: there is one world with definite, framework-relative properties, and the multiplicity of frameworks is like the multiplicity of coordinate systems, not a multiplicity of worlds. No framework is privileged, and on his view that is a feature, not a defect—demanding a privileged one merely imports the classical unicity assumption CH rejects.

## The Dowker–Kent Set-Selection Critique

The canonical objection is Dowker and Kent (1996), which built on their earlier result (Dowker & Kent 1995) that the consistency criterion alone cannot recover the standard predictions and retrodictions of quasiclassical physics. Reviewing the Griffiths, Omnès, and Gell-Mann–Hartle formulations and classifying the consistent sets, they argue that the formalism treats all consistent families "democratically" and supplies no criterion selecting a preferred one. With no law of nature fixing the framework, there is no way to compute *unconditional* probabilities for specific histories—probabilities are always conditional on a framework chosen from outside the physics. They further argue that Omnès' characterization of "true" statements is defective and press difficulties for the Gell-Mann–Hartle reading. Their conclusion: CH must be *supplemented* by some set-selection principle before it can serve as a fundamental theory capable of unconditional predictions. This became Kent's **set-selection problem**.

The technical exchange sharpened from there. Kent (1997) showed that different consistent sets can license *contrary inferences*—orthogonal, mutually exclusive propositions each retrodicted with probability one from the same data—and Griffiths and Hartle (1998) replied that the single-framework rule forbids combining such inferences, with Kent responding in turn. Bassi and Ghirardi (1999) argued that a small set of natural assumptions about the decoherent-histories description leads to a logical contradiction; Griffiths replied, and they answered again. Most recently, Zampeli, Pavlou and Wallden (2025) show that the contrary-inferences problem persists even in the quasiclassical macroscopic limit, using the arrival time of a ball in an infinite square well as a worked case—evidence that the difficulty is not confined to contrived microscopic examples.

The disagreement is genuine and unresolved. Griffiths replies that framework-conditional probabilities are exactly what a quantum theory should deliver—probabilities are conditional on a chosen framework the way classical probabilities are conditional on a chosen sample space, and no "law of nature" need pick one. The dispute turns on a prior question: must a physical theory deliver framework-independent, unconditional probabilities, or are framework-conditional probabilities plus the single-framework rule enough? No consensus set-selection principle has been found, and CH remains a live minority interpretation. Everettians press the point one step further: Wallace argues that the set-selection underdetermination cuts harder against CH than against Everett, because Everettian quasiclassical realms are picked out by the dynamics of decoherence and stability rather than chosen from a menu of consistent sets, so the branch structure is not a free parameter in the way the consistent set is.

A related worry sharpens the seam: critics ask whether the reliance on *question-relative* frameworks quietly smuggles the observer back in as the unmodeled agent who picks the question. Griffiths treats framework choice as a harmless pragmatic step, like choosing coordinates. Whether it is that innocent, or a covert reintroduction of an agent at the foundations, is precisely the point on which CH's realist self-understanding is contested.

## Where CH Sits in the Landscape

CH occupies a distinctive corner of the interpretive map, surveyed more broadly in [[qm-interpretations-beyond-many-worlds|Quantum Interpretations Beyond Many-Worlds]]. Unlike Bohmian mechanics it adds no hidden variables; unlike GRW/CSL it adds no collapse dynamics; unlike Many-Worlds it keeps a single actual history and denies superpositions physical coexistence; unlike QBism and relational quantum mechanics it aims to be realist about a single world rather than agent- or observer-relative about states. It positions itself as "Copenhagen done right": measurement loses fundamental status, quantum time-development is fundamentally probabilistic—unitary propagators describe the deterministic evolution between events while the Born rule fixes the probabilities of the events themselves—and collapse is demoted to bookkeeping. Its price is framework-dependence and the unsolved set-selection problem—an honest ledger that CH pays openly rather than hiding.

## Relation to Site Perspective

CH interacts with all five of the Map's [[tenets|tenets]], and honesty requires keeping the interpretation's own claims separate from the Map's interpretive overlay.

**Tenet 4 (No Many-Worlds): ally only on the single-world reading.** The alliance has to be stated conditionally from the start, because the many-worlds reading of CH is a live one. Bigaj (2023) argues that the formalism admits a *many-histories* reading on which each history in a consistent family describes a separate world, and Everettians such as Wallace and Saunders argue that a realist attitude toward *all* consistent (or decoherent) families just is a form of many-worlds—Everett's branches under another name. Gell-Mann and Hartle's own 2012 reformulation, which posits "one real fine-grained history" embedded in an ensemble of imagined ones, is a further sign that the decoherent-histories programme does not by itself single out one actual world. Only on Griffiths' *realist single-world* reading—one history in a consistent family realized, superpositions denied the status of physically coexisting branches—does CH reject [[concepts/many-worlds|Many-Worlds]], and it does so without paying either the hidden-variable price (Bohm) or the added-collapse price (GRW/CSL). That reading, which the Map leans on, shows that single-world realism is a live, technically serious option and not a nostalgic holdout; but the Tenet-4 payoff is conditional on it, not on CH per se.

**Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction): rival.** CH removes any fundamental role for consciousness. Within a chosen framework, which history is realized is a matter of Born-rule chance, not of mental selection, and the framework itself is chosen for descriptive convenience. Griffiths is explicit that no mind is needed: on his account "quantum mechanics is compatible with the traditional idea of an independent physical reality whose fundamental properties can be discussed without needing to make reference to human observers or human consciousness" (Griffiths 2013). The Map must engage CH as a genuine competitor to consciousness-linked collapse, not fold it into dualism.

**Tenet 1 (Dualism): no support, no contradiction.** Standard CH is a physicalist interpretation: it locates everything in the quantum state and its histories and invokes nothing non-physical. It neither establishes dualism nor refutes it—the only route by which CH could bear on Tenet 1 is the speculative consciousness-selection reading discussed below, which CH itself disowns. The Map records CH as a naturalistic competitor whose success would count against the *need* for dualism, not as evidence for it.

**Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction): no bidirectional influence.** Standard CH provides no channel by which consciousness could act back on the physical: histories and their probabilities are fixed by the Born-rule dynamics, and framework choice is, on Griffiths' account, a descriptive act with no physical efficacy. CH is therefore structurally inhospitable to the Map's claim that mind causally influences the physical world; any bidirectional reading would have to be imported by the consciousness-selection extension, against Griffiths' insistence that framework choice changes nothing.

**Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits): cuts both ways.** CH is admirably economical—it adds nothing to standard quantum mechanics except an interpretive discipline. The Map does not treat that parsimony as decisive, precisely because Tenet 5 warns that simplicity is an unreliable guide when knowledge is incomplete. The measurement problem's persistence is itself a sign of such incompleteness.

The Map's distinctive question is whether the [Dowker–Kent set-selection gap](#the-dowker-kent-set-selection-critique) leaves room for a *consciousness-selected* framework. There is a structural resonance with the Map's recurring claim—developed in [[quantum-measurement-and-consciousness|the measurement problem as philosophical problem]]—that a complete third-person description can fail to fix a first-person fact. It is tempting to call this an "exact, formal version" of that gap, but that overstates it. The resonance is a limited and contested *structural* analogy, and three distinct selections must be kept apart before it can be weighed:

- *Framework selection*—which sample space, or descriptive context, one adopts. This is what CH leaves open, and Griffiths insists it changes nothing in the world.
- *History selection*—which single history in a family is the actual one. CH treats this as a matter of Born-rule chance.
- *Outcome selection*—why this definite result rather than another. This is the measurement problem's demand, and what the Map's consciousness-linked reading is really about. The [[post-decoherence-selection|post-decoherence selection]] concept page sets these apart as four distinct loci—adding preferred-basis selection to the three above—and locates the Map's consciousness claim at outcome selection specifically.

The gap CH exhibits sits at the level of *framework* (context) selection, whereas the measurement-problem parallel the Map cares about is *outcome* selection. Because Griffiths holds that framework choice does not influence reality, even a consciousness-selected framework would be at most context-selection—which the Map's own [[tenets|tenets]] treatment subordinates to outcome selection. So the parallel is best read as a disanalogy: in the measurement problem an outcome *is* needed and unexplained, whereas CH claims no framework selection is needed at all. To turn the affinity into an argument, the Map would have to earn a foundational move CH denies—that some selection is needed and that consciousness supplies it. That move is not available inside CH's own framework, where the single-framework rule and framework-conditional probabilities coherently deny the need; the disagreement is a framework-boundary one, and is honestly noted as such rather than presented as a result CH underwrites. The Map registers the affinity, treats framework-dependence as an open seam rather than a hidden endorsement of dualism, and mirrors the cautious [[quantum-darwinism-and-consciousness|outcome-gap reading of quantum Darwinism]] without claiming CH shares its ontology.
