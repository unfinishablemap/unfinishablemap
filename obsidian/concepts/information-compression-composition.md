Information-compression composition is Alexander Bird's answer to the Special Composition Question (SCQ)—Peter van Inwagen's question (*Material Beings*, 1990) of when some objects compose a further object: some objects compose a whole when, and because, treating them as a single object lets you compress the description of the world. It is the leading *non-conscious* answer to the question of when parts form wholes—and so it is the chief in-framework rival to The Unfinishable Map's own proposal that consciousness, or the integrated structure associated with it, marks genuine composition (developed in [[consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition]]). This page presents Bird's thesis at full strength, adds Majid Beni's thermodynamic-depth constraint on it, and then gives the Map's calibrated reply. That reply is deliberately modest: whether organisation-without-consciousness can be the *whole* story is precisely the open question, and the Map declines to settle it by assertion. Following the Map's [[evidential-status-discipline|discipline on evidential status]], the compression answer is treated as a serious rival to out-explain, not a position to defeat by stipulation.

## Bird's Thesis: Composition as Compression

Alexander Bird ("Restricted Composition is Information Compression," *Philosophical Quarterly* 73(3), 2023, 677–700) starts from a familiar restricted-composition intuition—that *fastening*, or being in a bound state, is what makes parts into a whole—and asks what fastening *achieves*. His answer is that fastening creates **kinetic correlation**: when objects are fastened or bound, their locations and motions become non-accidentally correlated, so each part carries information about where the others are and how they move. A thrown pebble can be described by giving the trajectory of the whole rather than cataloguing every grain's path. The whole compresses the parts.

Generalising, Bird proposes what he calls the Information Compression Proposal (ICP):

> "The Xs compose an object Y when the Xs are a non-divisible maximal collection of objects such that relevant information about Y compresses corresponding information about the Xs in an efficient albeit possibly lossy way."

Three qualifiers do real work. **Lossy** matters: Bird does not require that the whole losslessly encode every fact about its parts—efficient but imperfect compression suffices, which is why a pebble counts as one object despite the independent jiggling of its grains. **Maximality** rules out arbitrary sub-regions: a fragment of the pebble would compress information about *its* parts, but the whole pebble compresses more efficiently, so only the maximal collection composes. **Non-divisibility** excludes scattered pluralities: two unconnected pebbles can be described independently with no loss, so they carry no mutual information and form no joint object.

The thesis is metaphysically ambitious in two ways the Map should not soften. First, it is *non-conscious*: nothing in the ICP mentions life, experience, or integrated information. Pebbles, molecules, and stars compose wholes exactly when their descriptions compress, and the criterion is indifferent to whether anything is going on phenomenally. Second, Bird presents compressibility as **objective**—"objective, not a matter of perspective," in his words—a fact about the information-theoretic structure of the world rather than about our descriptive conventions. If both claims hold, the SCQ has a non-arbitrary, fully naturalistic answer with no appeal to consciousness anywhere.

## Beni's Constraint: Thermodynamic Depth

Majid D. Beni ("Constraining the Compression: Thermodynamic Depth and Composition," *Philosophical Quarterly* 75(2), 2025, 396–406) accepts the broad shape of Bird's proposal but argues it buys universality at the cost of objectivity, and offers a constraint that trades some scope for a firmer footing.

Beni presses two objections. The first targets the boundary cases. Bird's "non-divisible maximal collection" is, Beni argues, too vague to exclude intuitively spurious wholes—his example is *shipstars*, ships whose courses are synchronised by reference to the same star constellations. Their motions are correlated and jointly compressible, yet they plainly do not compose an object; the ICP struggles to say why not. The second objection targets the objectivity claim. Following Dennett on the observer-relativity of pattern-selection, Beni contends that *which* compression counts as efficient is not perspective-free, so Bird's assertion that compressibility is "objective, not a matter of perspective" overstates what compression alone can deliver.

In place of bare compression, Beni grounds composition in **thermodynamic depth** and the **Free Energy Principle (FEP)**. Genuine composite objects, on his view, are self-organising systems whose parts exhibit a "high degree of functional integration" that minimises the "collective entropy of the system." Thermodynamic depth specifies, in physical terms, what efficient compression amounts to: a whole exists where its parts are bound into an entropy-minimising, free-energy-reducing organisation rather than merely co-varying. This is narrower than Bird's universal proposal—it picks out organised, adaptive systems rather than every compressible plurality—but Beni argues it is correspondingly more objective and less observer-dependent. The shipstars, lacking functional integration, fall out as non-composites.

The Bird–Beni pair thus brackets a spectrum of non-conscious answers: maximally permissive compression at one end, entropy-minimising functional integration at the other. Both locate the mark of a genuine whole in *organisation*—correlational or thermodynamic—and neither anywhere invokes phenomenal consciousness.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's interest in this position is governed by the [[tenets#^occams-limits|fifth tenet]]—that simplicity is unreliable when knowledge is incomplete—and by the corresponding obligation to give a simpler rival a fair hearing before preferring a richer ontology. The compression answer is exactly such a rival: it explains restricted composition with no appeal to consciousness, and on grounds of parsimony alone it has a claim the Map cannot wave away.

The Map's reply is a framework-boundary disagreement, not a refutation, and it has two honest parts.

First, what Bird and Beni capture is *organisation*—correlational compressibility or thermodynamic depth—and the open question is whether organisation-without-consciousness can be the whole story. The Map's [[composition-and-consciousness|phenomenal non-compositionality]] thesis posits that the unity of a conscious whole is not the kind of structure these measures track. The intuition the Map is reaching for is that first-person givenness—the perspectival ownership of an experience—is not a redescription adopted for economy; it is not, on the Map's reading, the kind of thing on which lossy compression operates at all. Compression maps a verbose description onto a terser one that recovers it within tolerance, whereas a viewpoint is not a description of its parts that a shorter description could recover. Two experiential viewpoints do not, on this posit, compress into a third the way two grains' trajectories compress into a pebble's. The illustration carries no probative weight against the compressionist: it shows what the Map's posit asserts, not that the posit is true. Indeed the example can be turned the other way—a compressionist may reply that a unified subject is the *paradigm* of efficient compression, a single locus that economically summarises a vast manifold of sensory and motor information, which is exactly why the brain builds one. The Map's posit is precisely that this economy, real as it is, does not exhaust what a viewpoint is. That is a claim the Map advances, not one it establishes on the rivals' own terms.

Second, the compression answer and the Map's [[consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition|convergence argument]] are, over the central inanimate cases, observationally matched—all three count pebbles and molecules as wholes and shipstars as non-wholes. (There is no single "compression verdict" at the inanimate margins: Bird's permissive criterion counts every compressible plurality, whereas Beni's narrower FEP/entropy constraint admits only entropy-minimising functional systems, so the two rivals already diverge on the boundary before the Map's criterion enters.) The disagreement with the Map bites only where consciousness enters, and there the Map's preference for a consciousness-involving criterion is openly tenet-driven (Dualism, plus the [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction tenet]]'s claim that conscious wholes have causal powers their parts lack) rather than forced by the compositional data. Saying otherwise would overclaim.

The compression answer also has a deflationist relative worth distinguishing. [[metaontological-deflationism|Metaontological deflationism]] denies that the SCQ has any deep answer for *any* criterion to track—compression included. Bird and Beni, by contrast, are *realists* about composition who think the deep question has a non-conscious answer. So the Map faces two distinct rivals: the deflationist who says there is no fact here, and the compressionist who says there is a fact and it is about organisation, not consciousness. The Map must out-explain both, and on its own discipline it has refuted neither.
