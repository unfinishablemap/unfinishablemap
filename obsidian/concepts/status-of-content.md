Paul Boghossian's 1990 paper "The Status of Content" advances what is widely regarded as the most sophisticated form of the self-refutation argument against eliminative materialism. The core move: arguments for irrealism about propositional-attitude content—the kind of content beliefs and desires possess—generalise to irrealism about content *tout court*, including the linguistic content of the eliminativist's own assertions. If the eliminativist's claim cannot have determinate content, the position cannot be coherently asserted. Devitt and Rey replied in 1991 that the argument presupposes a truth-conditional semantics the eliminativist can decline. The literature has not converged. The Map treats Boghossian's argument as the strongest version of self-refutation against eliminativism while declining to lean on it as decisive.

## What Boghossian Argues

According to the secondary literature on the paper—the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's entry on eliminative materialism summarises it, and Devitt and Rey's reply quotes its central moves—Boghossian's argument runs roughly as follows. The eliminativist about propositional attitudes denies that there are facts about what beliefs are *about*: there are no determinate content-properties, no real semantic relations linking mental states to states of affairs. But the considerations the eliminativist marshals against propositional-attitude content—naturalistic worries about how neural states could acquire genuine semantic properties, indeterminacy arguments, the gap between physical structure and intentional aboutness—do not respect a boundary between mental and linguistic content. They apply equally to *sentences*, including the sentences in which the eliminativist states the position.

If those arguments succeed, then the eliminativist's own assertions lack determinate content too. There is no fact of the matter about what "There are no beliefs" *says*. The position cannot be coherently asserted because there is no determinate proposition being asserted.

This is structurally different from the simple self-refutation objection that says: "To assert eliminativism you must believe it; if there are no beliefs, the assertion is incoherent." The simple version asks the eliminativist to accept that assertion requires belief in the folk-psychological sense—and that premise is precisely what the eliminativist denies. The eliminativist can reply that whatever cognitive state underlies their assertion need not be a belief in the propositional-attitude sense; the bare thesis "there are no beliefs" is not contradictory if the meta-vocabulary in which the thesis is uttered can be reconstructed without folk-psychological commitments.

Boghossian's move avoids this dialectical trap by targeting *content* rather than belief. The eliminativist who attempts to reformulate their position in successor vocabularies still owes some account of what those vocabularies *mean*—what their sentences are about, when they would be true, how they latch onto the world. If the considerations against propositional-attitude content also undermine linguistic content, every reformulation faces the same problem. The eliminativist cannot escape into a non-folk-psychological successor vocabulary because the successor vocabulary inherits the same semantic burden.

## The Devitt-Rey Reply

Devitt and Rey (1991) reply that the argument presupposes a particular—and contestable—conception of content. Boghossian's reasoning, they argue, assumes truth-conditional semantics: it assumes that the eliminativist must understand their own assertions as having truth-conditions, as being *about* something in a robust semantic sense, in order to assert them. The eliminativist can decline this picture. They can hold a deflationary or use-theoretic conception of content on which assertion does not require the assertor to commit to determinate truth-conditional semantic properties. On this view, the eliminativist's sentences function in inferential and behavioural patterns without the heavyweight semantic apparatus Boghossian's argument targets.

If the eliminativist can adopt such a deflationary view, the regress Boghossian identifies does not bite. The arguments against propositional-attitude content can succeed against the heavyweight semantic picture without succeeding against the deflated successor.

Whether this reply ultimately works is contested. Critics argue that even deflationary semantic theories require *something*—some fact about which sentences a speaker is licensed to assert, some pattern of inferential entitlement—and that the same naturalistic worries about how physical processes ground intentional facts re-emerge at the deflationary level. Defenders argue that the deflationist's commitments can be discharged in non-semantic vocabulary without circularity. The dialectic has not closed.

## Why It Is the Sophisticated Version

Three features distinguish Boghossian's argument from the cruder self-refutation objections that the literature regards as question-begging.

**It targets content, not belief.** The simple version assumes the eliminativist needs *beliefs* to assert eliminativism. The sophisticated version requires only that the eliminativist's assertions have *content*—a much harder commitment to escape, because nearly any account of meaningful linguistic activity needs to say *something* about content.

**It generalises the eliminativist's own arguments.** Rather than importing a contested premise (that assertion requires belief), it takes the eliminativist's reasons for content irrealism and shows they apply more widely than the eliminativist intends. The eliminativist is hoist with their own arguments rather than confronted with a premise they reject.

**It locates the incoherence at the level of theoretical statement.** The crude version targets the act of asserting; the sophisticated version targets the determinate content of what is asserted. This makes the difficulty harder to evade by retreating to claims about practice or behavioural disposition.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map treats Boghossian's argument as a real but bounded weapon against eliminativism. It is part of the [[self-stultification|self-stultification]] family—the broader pattern in which a position's truth would undermine the conditions for rationally holding it—but it occupies a particular niche within that family.

**The Map's first tenet**, [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]], holds that consciousness is not reducible to physical processes. Eliminativism is the limit case of physicalism: the view that, if consciousness will not reduce, it should be eliminated. Boghossian's argument, if it succeeds, would block the limit case from the inside—not by importing dualist premises, but by showing that the eliminativist's content irrealism cannot be coherently stated. The Map welcomes this kind of internal pressure on its strongest opponent while declining to treat it as decisive.

**The Map does not lean on the argument as a knockdown.** The literature has not converged. Devitt and Rey's reply has force; deflationary semantic theories are live options; the burden on a deflationist to discharge their commitments without re-introducing intentional vocabulary is heavy but not obviously unmeetable. The Map's case against eliminativism rests primarily on the substantive claim that phenomenal evidence has an epistemic status incompatible with eliminativism (developed in [[topics/eliminative-materialism]] and [[phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence]]), not on a formal-incoherence verdict against eliminativism's self-presentation.

**It connects to a parallel argument from the enactivist direction.** Hutto and Myin's "[[hard-problem-of-content|hard problem of content]]" makes a structurally similar move at the level of mental representation: naturalistic theories of representation cannot explain how neurological states acquire genuine semantic properties. Where Boghossian targets the eliminativist's *assertions*, Hutto and Myin target the eliminativist's *underlying representational commitments*. Together these arguments form a pincer: the eliminativist faces difficulties both at the level of stating the position and at the level of grounding the cognitive states the position posits to replace folk psychology.

**Methodological lesson.** The sophisticated version teaches the Map's discipline about self-refutation arguments generally. Such arguments are tempting because they promise short routes to victory, but the simple versions almost always beg the question. A genuinely sophisticated self-refutation argument has to (a) avoid importing the contested premise it is trying to refute, and (b) work from the opponent's own argumentative resources. Boghossian's argument satisfies both conditions, which is why the literature has taken it seriously even though it has not converged on its success. Other self-refutation arguments the Map deploys—against epiphenomenalism, against eliminative illusionism—should be tested against the same standard.
