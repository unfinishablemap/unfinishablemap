Conceptual role semantics (CRS), also called inferential role semantics, holds that the content of a mental state is constituted by the state's role in inference, perception, and action among other states, rather than read off its covariation with the world. It is the strongest naturalisation of content the Map has not yet engaged, and the reason is precise: because CRS does not derive content from world-covariance, it steps out from under the "covariance does not constitute content" dilemma (Hutto and Myin 2013) that drives the Map's [naturalisation-failure argument](the-naturalisation-failure-for-content). The Map therefore owes CRS a dedicated response, distinct from the deflationary functionalist reply it already rebuts. This article states the view at its strongest, gives the standard internal objection its due, and then offers the Map's considered counter on independent grounds.

## What Conceptual Role Semantics Claims

The core CRS thesis, in the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy's formulation, is that "the meaning or propositional content of an expression or attitude is determined by the role it plays in a person's language or in her cognition." Content is a matter of inferential position: to grasp the concept AND is to be disposed to the transitions it licenses (from "p and q" to "p"); to hold a perceptual concept is to be governed by the language-entry, intra-linguistic, and language-exit transitions that fix its use. Reference and truth-conditions, on the purest versions, are downstream of role rather than its source.

This distinguishes CRS sharply from the covariance-based families the naturalisation-failure argument targets. [Teleosemantics](teleosemantics), informational semantics, and predictive-processing accounts all try to build content out of some tracking relation between an inner state and a worldly condition — and all are caught by the dilemma that covariance is either too thin to be content (mere correlation) or, when enriched enough to be content, presupposes the very semantic notions it was meant to explain. CRS makes no tracking claim at the ground floor. It is this refusal to start from covariance that makes it the conspicuous missing member of the naturalisation survey.

### One-Factor and Two-Factor Versions

CRS comes in two structurally different forms, and the distinction matters for any reply.

**One-factor (constitutive) CRS** holds that inferential role is the whole story: there is no separate representational factor left over to naturalise. Sellars is the root — his attack on the Myth of the Given locates knowing in "the logical space of reasons, of justifying and being able to justify what one says," an inferentially articulated space with no non-inferential semantic bedrock beneath it. Brandom's *Making It Explicit* (1994) is the mature descendant: propositional content is conferred by a state's position in a web of material inferential relations — the commitments and entitlements it licenses and precludes — and that web is instituted by social-normative practices of giving and asking for reasons. Harman's "(Nonsolipsistic) Conceptual Role Semantics" (1987) externalises the role, letting perceptual input and behavioural output and worldly relations figure inside it rather than as a separate factor. Peacocke's *A Study of Concepts* (1992) is a close cousin: a concept is individuated by its possession conditions — the "primitively compelling" inferences a thinker must find compelling to have it.

**Two-factor CRS** splits content in two. Block's "Advertisement for a Semantics for Psychology" (1986) is canonical: a narrow, internal conceptual-role factor, individuated solipsistically by inferential role, is paired with a wide, referential/truth-conditional factor for externalist cases. The narrow factor is meant to do the psychological-explanatory work — it supervenes on the internal state and drives cognition — while the wide factor secures reference. Field's "Logic, Meaning, and Conceptual Role" (1977) prefigures the structure, pairing a probabilistically modelled conceptual role with a deflationary, disquotational treatment of truth. Two-factor CRS is the more common accommodation of the Twin Earth externalism that motivates [content externalism](content-externalism); the cost, as its critics note, is a concession that inferential role alone cannot deliver the world-directed dimension of thought.

## The Holism Objection (and CRS's Reply)

CRS faces a serious problem before any dualist ever raises one, and honesty about the view requires stating it. Fodor and Lepore's *Holism: A Shopper's Guide* (1992) presses the objection: if content is inferential role, and inferential roles are holistic — depending on the whole surrounding web of beliefs — then no two thinkers ever share a concept exactly, and each thinker's concepts shift as their beliefs change. Shared meaning, communication, psychological generalisation, and learning all look threatened. Inferential roles are also non-compositional: the role of "brown cow" is not built from the roles of "brown" and "cow" in the way a truth-conditional semantics would predict. The natural escape is an analytic/synthetic distinction — a line between meaning-constitutive inferences and merely collateral ones — but Quinean holism is widely taken to deny any such line.

CRS has resources here, and a fair presentation records them. Two-factor theorists (Block) can locate stability in the shared wide referential factor while treating narrow role as the explanatory-but-idiosyncratic component. Inferentialists can adopt a moderate holism, or anaphoric and normative constraints that pick out a privileged subset of role-constituting inferences without a full analytic/synthetic distinction. The debate is unresolved: holism is a standing cost of CRS, not a refutation. For the Map's purposes the point is that this is an intra-naturalist quarrel — it shows CRS is embattled on its own terms, but it does not touch the question of whether inferential role could naturalise content in principle. Notably, Fodor's own positive alternative (informational atomism) is covariance-based and so falls straight back under the dilemma CRS escapes.

## Relation to Site Perspective

CRS matters to the Map because of Tenet 1: consciousness, and with it the intentional content of thought, is not reducible to physical processes. The Map's naturalisation-failure argument leans on the Hutto-Myin dilemma to show that covariance-based programmes cannot constitute content. CRS is the case that dilemma does not reach. If content could be naturalised by inferential role instead, Tenet 1's claim that content resists physicalist reduction would need exactly the dedicated response this article supplies. The reply below is offered as the Map's considered line — a construction the Map defends, not a result found pre-made in the literature — and it is aimed at the constitutive core of the view, with the two-factor variant handled as a corollary.

The Map's counter turns on a dilemma internal to CRS itself, and it is important that it not simply assume the anti-naturalist conclusion. Grant the inferentialist everything the holism debate leaves standing: grant that a privileged set of inferential transitions can be identified and that content is their product. The question the Map presses is what makes those transitions the *correct* ones. Inferential role does constitutive work only if it is the role in *correct* inference — a state's licensing the move from "p and q" to "p" contributes to content because that move is valid, not merely because thinkers happen to make it. But "correct inference" is itself a semantic and normative notion. So constitutive CRS faces a fork:

**(a) The normativity horn.** If the constituting norms of correct inference are taken as real and irreducible — as Brandom's appeal to genuine commitments and entitlements, or Peacocke's "primitively compelling" transitions, seem to require — then content has not been naturalised but relocated. The account presupposes an already-contentful, already-normative structure (the ought of inference) to do its constituting. This is not a covert re-run of the covariance failure; it is a distinct point. It connects to the Map's normativity-of-reason thread, on which the normative force of "you ought to infer thus" is a second irreducible register alongside phenomenal character — as resistant to physicalist reduction as qualia, and for structurally similar reasons. The strongest inferentialist rejoinder is that the norms are implicit in practice and need not themselves be explicitly contentful. The Map's response is that a norm with genuine correctness-conditions — one that can be violated, that distinguishes what a thinker does from what they ought to do — already carries the normative-semantic character at issue, whether or not it is ever made explicit.

**(b) The deflation horn.** If instead the inferentialist grounds correctness in the actual dispositions of a community — what practitioners are disposed to treat as correct — then correctness has been traded for regularity, and the theory collapses into interpretationism: roles are individuated only relative to an interpreter's or community's stance. This reproduces the covariance dilemma one level up. Community dispositions covary with inferential transitions exactly as inner states covary with worldly conditions; and covariation of dispositions no more constitutes the correctness of an inference than covariation of states constitutes the content of a representation. The deflationary wing of CRS thus inherits precisely the failure the constitutive wing was meant to escape — this is the same deflation the Map rebuts elsewhere, meeting it here in inferentialist dress.

Two-factor CRS does not evade the fork. Its narrow inferential factor faces the same normativity regress on horn (a); and its need for a *separate* wide referential factor is, on the Map's reading, a concession that the world-directed, truth-evaluable dimension of thought is exactly what inferential role cannot supply — which is the naturalisation-failure diagnosis restated in the theory's own architecture. Brandom's Sellarsian pedigree (see [Sellars' manifest and scientific images](sellars-manifest-and-scientific-images)) is instructive here: the autonomy of the space of reasons that inferentialism prizes is congenial to the Map's anti-reductionism, but Sellars aimed to naturalise the manifest image within a scientific one, and it is that final reductive step the Map resists. The space of reasons, the Map holds, is real and irreducible — which is why inferential role illuminates the *structure* of content without naturalising its existence.

The upshot for the [hard problem of content](hard-problem-of-content) and for [intentionality](intentionality) generally: CRS earns its place as the naturalisation programme that does not start from covariance, and it survives the holism objection well enough to remain a live rival. But it constitutes content only by helping itself to correct-inference norms that are themselves either irreducibly normative-semantic or, when deflated, no more content-constituting than the covariance it was meant to replace. On the Map's considered view, that is not a naturalisation of content but a redescription of where the irreducibility lives.
