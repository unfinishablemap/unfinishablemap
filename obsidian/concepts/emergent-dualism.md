Emergent dualism is William Hasker's position that the conscious self is a genuinely new, non-composite mental *substance*—a single subject of experience with novel causal powers—that is *generated by* and *depends on* the brain once its functional organization reaches sufficient complexity. It occupies a deliberately narrow middle ground. Against Cartesian and Thomistic [[substance-property-dualism|substance dualism]], the mind is not a pre-existing soul but is produced by brain function, so it is emergent and brain-dependent. Against property dualism and emergent materialism, what emerges is a new individual *thing*, not merely a set of irreducible properties borne by the brain. Hasker's signature image is the magnetic field: the mind stands to the brain as an electromagnetic field stands to the magnet that generates it—qualitatively different from its physical source, spatially located where the source is, and able to act back on it. For The Unfinishable Map, emergent dualism is a live rival and close cousin: it shares the Map's rejection of reduction and its commitment to downward causation, but it makes consciousness ontologically *downstream* of the brain—which is precisely the dependence-versus-independence question the Map must sharpen for its own [[interactionist-dualism|interactionist]] account.

## The Position: A New Substance, Not a New Property

Hasker develops emergent dualism most fully in *The Emergent Self* (Cornell University Press, 1999). The core move is to accept everything the neuroscience of mind establishes about brain-dependence while denying that brain-dependence entails materialism.

Two contrasts fix the view precisely. The first is with Cartesian and Thomistic substance dualism. Those views hold that the soul either pre-exists the body or is separately created and merely joined to it; the mind does not owe its existence to the brain. Emergent dualism rejects this. The conscious self is *produced by* the brain: no sufficiently organized nervous system, no mind. In that respect the position is continuous with a broadly naturalistic, brain-respecting picture of how minds come to exist in the world.

The second contrast is with property dualism and emergent materialism. Those views hold that what emerges from neural complexity is a set of irreducible *properties*—new features of the brain—with no new individual added to the world's inventory. Emergent dualism insists that a new *individual* emerges: a subject, a bearer of experience, a thing that has properties rather than a property that things have. This is the load-bearing distinction. The claim is not that brains acquire strange new attributes but that brains, at sufficient complexity, *generate* a further entity.

Hasker's field analogy carries the intuition. A magnet generates a magnetic field. The field is not identical to the magnet or to its parts; it is qualitatively a different kind of thing; it occupies the region around its source; and it can act causally on the matter that produces it. Mind, on this picture, stands to brain as field stands to magnet—generated by the physical base, spatially co-located with it, ontologically distinct from it, and causally efficacious upon it. The analogy is meant to make emergence of an *individual* seem less mysterious: physics already recognizes generated fields that are not reducible to the objects generating them.

## The Unity-of-Consciousness Argument

The engine driving Hasker toward a *substance* rather than mere properties is the [[unity-of-consciousness|unity-of-consciousness]] (UOC) argument, which he traces to Leibniz and Kant. The argument runs roughly as follows. A conscious experience is genuinely unified: when you see a red apple while hearing a voice, there is one experience that encompasses both, had by one experiencer. But a physical brain is a *composite*—a vast manifold of parts, constantly being replaced, many rather than one. Nothing that is a mere aggregate of many parts can *be* the single, unified subject that a unified experience requires. Therefore the subject of experience is not the composite brain, nor any set of properties distributed across it, but a non-composite substance.

Crucially, the UOC argument is what forces the step from properties to substance. If unity required only that certain properties be co-instantiated, an emergent-property view might suffice. Hasker's contention is that co-instantiated properties borne by a manifold still leave you with a manifold, not a single subject—so unity demands a genuinely new, simple *bearer*. This is where emergent dualism parts company with every view that stops at emergent properties.

The 2010 essay "Persons and the Unity of Consciousness" (in Koons and Bealer's *The Waning of Materialism*, Oxford University Press) develops this line against the empirical hard cases—commissurotomy (split-brain) and dissociative phenomena—that appear to threaten the very unity the argument relies on. Hasker treats the person as a unified, coherent center of consciousness functioning as a rational agent, and argues these cases complicate but do not overturn the requirement for a single subject.

## The Pairing Problem and Interaction

Emergent dualism claims a distinctive advantage over Cartesian dualism on the [[pairing-problem|pairing problem]]—Kim's challenge that a non-spatial mind has nothing to ground its connection to *this* particular body rather than any other. Because the Cartesian soul is non-spatial, no spatial relation can pair it with a brain, and the interaction looks unintelligible.

Emergent dualism dissolves this by locating the mind *where its generating brain is*. Just as a magnetic field is found at the location of its magnet, the emergent self is spatially co-located with the brain that produces it. The pairing question—why is *this* mind joined to *this* brain?—is then no more puzzling than why *this* field belongs to *this* magnet: the field is simply where its source is. Interaction, likewise, is unmysterious in the way a field's action on its source is unmysterious. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's "Emergent Properties" entry (O'Connor and Wong) notes exactly this: Hasker claims for emergent dualism the advantages of Cartesian dualism while overcoming the difficulty of explaining how particular brains and mental substances come to be persistently linked.

## Standard Objections

Emergent dualism draws sustained criticism, including from fellow dualists.

**The emergence of a substance is unprecedented.** Ordinary emergence—solidity, liquidity, the wetness of water—is grounded in micro-parts obeying standard physics; nothing genuinely new is added to the world's ontology. The emergence of a *new substance* with *new causal powers* and, arguably, new causal laws is a different order of claim. Critics argue it is unprecedented and mysterious—a distance Hasker himself concedes, granting that his emergent substance stands far removed from garden-variety emergence. Whether this is a difference of degree or an unexplained ontological leap remains contested. The Map treats this general puzzle as an instance of the [[emergence-void|emergence void]]: cognition may face a standing limit at level transitions.

**Causal exclusion (Kim).** If every physical effect already has a sufficient physical cause, an additional emergent-mental cause is either excluded or generates systematic overdetermination. This [[causal-closure|exclusion]] pressure bears on emergent dualism as it does on all interactionist views. Emergentists reply variously—denying that the emergent is merely supervenient, or embracing genuine [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|downward causation]]. Because the objection is shared across interactionism, it functions less as a distinctive strike against Hasker than as a common battleground.

**The combination/constitution worry.** Brandon Rickabaugh's "Against Emergent Dualism" (in the *Blackwell Companion to Substance Dualism*, Wiley, 2018) presses a dualist-internal critique: emergent dualism is not more attractive than non-emergent substance dualism. His sharpest point is a constitution worry that mirrors the panpsychist [[combination-problem|combination problem]]—how can bits of non-conscious matter combine so as to *generate* a unified conscious subject? If the UOC argument shows that no manifold can constitute a single subject, then the burden reappears at the emergence step: how does the composite brain manage to *produce* the very unity a composite cannot itself possess? The [[composition-and-consciousness|metaphysics of composition]] frames this tension directly.

**Is "emergent substance" even coherent?** Underlying the specific objections is the worry that "generated-yet-simple substance" combines commitments that pull apart—brain-dependence pointing toward the physical, non-composite subjecthood pointing away from it. Defenders regard the field analogy as showing the combination is intelligible; skeptics regard the analogy as promissory, since a magnetic field is a physical entity governed by known laws, whereas a conscious subject with novel causal powers is exactly what is in question.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Emergent dualism is one of the closest cousins to The Unfinishable Map's own position, which makes locating the disagreement precisely more important than overstating it.

**Shared ground (Tenets 1 and 3).** Emergent dualism affirms the Map's [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]] tenet: consciousness is not reducible to physical processes. It also affirms the [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] tenet—Hasker's field "acts back on the brain that generates it," which is downward causation of just the kind the Map requires. At the level of anti-physicalism and mental causation, Hasker is an ally, not an opponent. His UOC argument is one the Map can largely endorse, and his spatial-location solution to the pairing problem closely parallels the Map's own [[brain-interface-boundary|interface-locality]] response, on which consciousness is located where it causally interfaces with the brain.

**The decisive divergence—dependence versus independence.** The friction is ontological priority. Emergent dualism makes consciousness *generated by and dependent on* the brain: no brain of sufficient complexity, no mind. The Map's [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] tenet points the other way. On the Map's picture, non-physical consciousness is a *distinct pole* that couples to the physical at a minimal quantum interface, rather than a product the brain manufactures. This sharpens a question the Map should answer in its own voice: does the Map's consciousness *emerge from* neural complexity, moving it toward Hasker, or is it *independent* and merely *coupled* to the brain, moving it toward the Cartesian and interactionist tradition?

The Map's answer leans toward independence-with-coupling, but the case is argued, not asserted. Three considerations favor treating consciousness as a distinct pole rather than a neural product. First, the [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|minimal-interaction]] mechanism supplies a *coupling* site—the quantum interface—that does not require the brain to *generate* a new substance; the brain provides indeterminacies for consciousness to bias, not raw material from which a subject is fabricated. Second, if the emergent self is generated by a composite, Rickabaugh's constitution worry lands squarely: the Map would inherit the burden of explaining how a manifold produces the unified subject the UOC argument says a manifold cannot be. Locating consciousness as an independent pole that *couples* to the manifold, rather than one *built from* it, sidesteps that particular regress. Third, the Map's commitments elsewhere—[[agent-causation|agent causation]] and the persistence of the subject—sit more comfortably with a consciousness whose existence is not hostage to a particular configuration of neural parts.

None of this refutes Hasker. Emergent dualism remains a coherent and well-defended horn, and a Map that discovered consciousness *does* depend on neural complexity would move toward it rather than away. The honest statement is comparative: the Map borrows Hasker's downward causation and his spatial pairing solution, declines his generation-and-dependence thesis, and treats the choice between "emerges from the brain" and "independent and merely coupled" as a substantive open commitment it resolves, provisionally, in favor of coupling. The [[tenets#^occams-limits|limits of parsimony]] apply on both sides: neither an emergent substance nor an independent coupled pole is the "simple" option, and simplicity alone cannot adjudicate between them.
