Wilder Penfield (1891–1976), the neurosurgeon who founded the Montreal Neurological Institute and pioneered awake cortical-stimulation surgery for epilepsy, ended his career an interactionist dualist. What makes his case distinctive is its evidential base: rather than a conceivability argument, Penfield rested his dualism on regularities he observed across a very large clinical series of conscious-patient stimulations. Whether that evidence licenses dualism is a further question—on the reading defended here it is suggestive rather than decisive, because a mainstream physicalist account of agency can absorb the same clinical data. Penfield is best read as an empirically grounded interactionist whose observations are congenial to the Map's commitments while remaining, like all such evidence, framework-relative.

## The clinical setting

From the late 1920s Penfield developed the "Montreal procedure": operating on epilepsy patients under local anaesthetic, keeping them awake and responsive, and electrically stimulating the exposed cortex to map functional areas before excising seizure foci. Over decades this produced an unusually direct dataset on what stimulation of the human cortex does and does not evoke. The systematic mapping work appears in *The Cerebral Cortex of Man* (Penfield & Rasmussen, Macmillan, 1950)—the source of the familiar sensory and motor homunculus—and in the larger series of *Epilepsy and the Functional Anatomy of the Human Brain* (Penfield & Jasper, Little, Brown, 1954). The philosophical conclusions Penfield eventually drew came only later, and by his own account reversed the materialist expectation he had begun with.

## The ownership asymmetry

Penfield's first anchoring observation concerns the *ownership* of movement. When he stimulated the motor cortex, patients would move—a hand would rise, the head would turn—but they characteristically declined to claim the movement as their own, attributing it instead to the surgeon and the electrode. Penfield reported that patients drew a sharp line between such evoked movement and the movement they willed: the evoked action was experienced as something done *to* them, not *by* them.

This asymmetry between disowned evoked movement and owned voluntary movement is Penfield's central clinical datum. (The often-quoted patient remarks circulate in variant wordings across secondary sources and are not reproduced verbatim here; the substance—systematic disownership of stimulation-evoked movement—is robustly attested.) Penfield took the asymmetry to show that agency is not simply the occurrence of a motor act in the cortex: the same overt movement could be present with or without the sense of authorship, and stimulation supplied the movement while leaving the authorship absent.

## No cortical seat for the will

Penfield's second observation is negative and, for his argument, the more important. Across the series, stimulation reliably evoked movements, sensations, and—famously, from the temporal lobe—vivid experiential "flashbacks" of past scenes. What it never evoked, he maintained, was a decision, a belief, or an act of reasoning. An electrode could make a patient move or perceive or re-experience, but it could not make a patient *decide* to do something, or *come to believe* a proposition, or engage in abstract thought. Penfield concluded that the operations most characteristic of mind were absent from the map of stimulable functions.

From this he drew an inference: the deciding, believing, reasoning mind is not localizable to any stimulable cortical site, and is therefore not straightforwardly a product of the "highest brain-mechanism" alone. This is an argument from the *structure of the stimulation map*—from what is conspicuously missing from it—rather than from introspective certainty about the will.

## The late dualist turn

Penfield set out this position in *The Mystery of the Mind* (Princeton University Press, 1975), published the year before his death and standing as his final statement. There he distinguished the brain's "highest brain-mechanism"—an automatic, computer-like apparatus—from the mind, and argued that the highest mechanism does not by itself fully account for the mind. His governing analogy casts the brain as a computer and the mind as a programmer that directs it: a distinct element operating *through* the neural machinery without being identical to it.

Two features of the book bear on how much weight the metaphor can carry. First, Penfield framed his argument primarily as a *negative* one—that materialism is insufficient to the clinical facts—rather than as a positive theory of how mind and brain interact. Second, he explicitly declined to specify the interaction mechanism, conceding it was unexplained and handing the problem to future neurophysiology. The "programmer/computer" image is therefore best read as a heuristic for a levels-and-control distinction and a placeholder for an unexplained interaction, not as a fully worked-out substance dualism. Over-reading it as the latter is a standing risk.

## The neuroscientist-dualist lineage

Penfield sits within a small lineage of twentieth-century neuroscientists who defended dualism against the materialist tide. The *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* entry on dualism notes that "some distinguished neuroscientists, such as Charles Sherrington (1951), Wilder Penfield (1975) and John Eccles (1994) continued to defend dualism throughout the second half of the twentieth century."

The pairing with Eccles is especially apt, and the two are complementary. Eccles (with Popper, and later in the psychon/dendron quantum-probability-field proposal) offered a candidate *mechanism* for mind-to-brain action at the synapse. Penfield offered *clinical evidence* of the will's non-localizability but pointedly declined to propose any mechanism. Between them they occupy the two halves of the interactionist case: Eccles the mechanism side, Penfield the phenomenological-clinical side. Penfield's contribution matters to a project like the Map precisely because it is *independent* of the contested quantum-interface machinery—an empirical foothold for the same conclusions that does not ride on the quantum bet.

## The physicalist reply

The clinical facts Penfield reported are not seriously contested; their interpretation is. The strongest challenge to his dualist reading comes from the comparator, or efference-copy, model of the sense of agency developed in cognitive neuroscience (Blakemore, Wolpert, and Frith and collaborators around 2000–2002; refined by Synofzik, Vosgerau, and Newen in 2008). On this model, a voluntary motor command dispatches an *efference copy* to a forward model that predicts the movement's sensory consequences; when prediction matches the actual sensory feedback, the system generates the feeling of ownership.

This account handles Penfield's headline datum directly and on purely physical terms. An electrode-evoked movement bypasses the normal motor-command pathway, so no efference copy is generated, so no prediction is available to match the feedback—and the comparator correctly flags the movement as not-self. The disowned evoked movement is exactly what a physical prediction-based agency system should produce. The negative observation is similarly absorbable: that no single stimulation site yields a "decision" is consistent with the will being a *distributed, prediction-dependent process* rather than a non-physical element—there would be no point-stimulable will-site even if agency were wholly physical.

The upshot is that Penfield's evidence *underdetermines* the choice between dualism and the comparator account. It converts his datum from "proof of a non-physical will" into "a fact about the machinery of predictive agency" without strain. That is why the honest verdict is: suggestive, not decisive.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Penfield is congenial to the Map. His clinical case bears directly on **Tenet 1** (consciousness and will are not reducible to physical mechanism) and **Tenet 3** (the mind exercises downward, bidirectional causal influence—the "programmer" directing the "computer"). The Map values him because his is a *clinical, first-person-surgical* case for the irreducibility of the will rather than an armchair one, and because it diversifies the Map's interactionist evidence base beyond the [[the-interface-problem|Eccles/quantum-interface line]].

That endorsement must stay framework-relative. Following the Map's [[evidential-status-discipline]], the comparator-model reply shows that Penfield's data do not by themselves discriminate dualism from physicalism, so the Map presents his inference as evidence congenial to Tenets 1 and 3, not as a demonstration of them. The parallel with the [[objections-to-interactionism|standard objections to interactionism]] is instructive: here too the dualist reading survives, but only as one live option among rivals that fit the same facts.

There is a **Tenet 5** twist worth flagging. The comparator reading is simpler on its face, and simplicity would ordinarily favour it. But Penfield's point—that decision, belief, and reasoning never appear on the stimulation map—is a genuine explanandum the comparator model must also address, not a datum simplicity alone can dissolve. Under Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits), the greater parsimony of the physicalist reading should not be treated as settling the question while the interaction between prediction machinery and genuine agency remains this poorly understood.
