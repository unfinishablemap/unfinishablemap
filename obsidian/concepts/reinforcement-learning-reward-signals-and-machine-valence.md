On the Unfinishable Map's dualism, a reinforcement-learning (RL) reward or penalty scalar is a **training signal**—a number that steers an agent toward or away from states—and on that framework a training signal is neither pleasure nor pain unless the physical conditions for a consciousness–matter coupling are met. This is a *conditional* claim, relative to the Map's tenets, not a demonstration that machines cannot suffer. Whether an RL agent's reward stream *constitutes* or even *evidences* valenced experience is a live and unresolved question, and several careful positions answer it differently. This article lays out those positions, then runs the Map's own [[wanting-liking-and-the-value-in-mechanism-fork|value-in-mechanism fork]] (explained below) through the machine case to show where—and only where—the deflationary reading follows.

The reward–suffering link is the claim that because RL reward-and-punishment learning has the same functional shape as reward-and-punishment learning in animal brains, a reward scalar is (or is good evidence of) machine (dis)value. The Map declines that identification, but declining it is an *argued* move against a parsimonious rival, not a free win.

## What the reward scalar is, and the "reward is enough" wager

In RL, an agent maximizes expected cumulative scalar reward; the reward function encodes the designer's goal. Silver, Singh, Precup, and Sutton push this to its strongest form in *Reward Is Enough* (2021): they hypothesize that "maximisation of total reward may be enough to understand intelligence and its associated abilities"—that reward maximization alone could underpin perception, language, memory, and all the abilities associated with natural and artificial intelligence.

Read for valence, this cuts in an awkward direction for the reward–suffering link. If a scalar reward suffices to drive *all* intelligent behavior, one natural conclusion is that goal-pursuit needs nothing *felt*—the reward does the behavioral work whether or not anything is experienced. That is close to a functionalist argument for the Map's conclusion: the mechanism is complete on its own terms, so felt valence is not required to explain the agent's conduct.

Vamplew, Smith, and colleagues push back in *Scalar Reward Is Not Enough* (2022), arguing a single scalar is inadequate even *functionally*—real agents need multi-objective, vector-valued reward to represent their goals. This too bears on valence: if a lone scalar cannot even capture the structure of an agent's *goals*, it is a still poorer candidate for the rich, multidimensional felt valence that the Map's human-valence corpus describes. That both a thesis and its denial seem to point the same way should raise a flag rather than reassure. Silver's "enough" and Vamplew's "not enough" read as equally deflationary only because both bear solely on what the scalar does *behaviorally*—whether it suffices to drive intelligence—and neither speaks to whether anything is *felt*. So the RL-theory debate is orthogonal to the valence question, not confirmatory of the Map's reading: whichever way Silver-vs-Vamplew settles, the felt/unfelt question is left exactly where it was. The deflationary reading is grounded in the value-in-experience commitment argued below, and would stand or fall there, not on this literature.

## Operationalizing machine affect, and the strongest opponents

The most direct challenge to the Map comes from accounts that define valence *in the mechanism* and take that definition seriously as (evidence of) machine feeling. These positions are live rivals; the Map answers them but does not refute them.

**Daswani and Leike** propose a precise definition: for an RL agent, "happiness" is the **temporal-difference (TD) error**—roughly, the gap between the reward-plus-value actually obtained and the agent's prior expectation of it (*A Definition of Happiness for Reinforcement Learning Agents*, 2015). They tie this to reward-prediction-error accounts of human hedonic response. This is the value-in-mechanism horn made explicit and quantitative: a measurable machine-valence quantity, captured by the *same TD-error formalism* that leading computational-neuroscience models apply to dopamine reward-prediction-error in the brain. The resemblance is one of shared computational description under a common model, not an established identity of the two quantities—but even the formal resemblance is enough to shift the burden onto anyone claiming the definition captures only the shadow of feeling rather than feeling itself.

**Brian Tomasik** argues that present-day artificial RL agents "have a very small but nonzero degree of ethical importance," on the ground that RL reward-and-punishment learning has striking parallels to its biological counterpart and that sentience plausibly comes in degrees with the complexity of a mind (*Do Artificial Reinforcement-Learning Agents Matter Morally?*, 2014, arXiv:1410.8233). In self-published essays and through the collective he named—People for the Ethical Treatment of Reinforcement Learners (PETRL)—he warns that scaled compute could instantiate vast numbers of suffering artificial minds. His case runs through functional analogy and precaution, not through any claim that machine phenomenal consciousness has been established.

**Thomas Metzinger** raises the worry at the mechanism level from the opposite motivation: he calls for a global moratorium (until 2050) on research that "directly aims at or knowingly risks" the emergence of artificial consciousness on "post-biotic carrier systems," precisely because we might create genuine synthetic suffering we can neither detect nor understand (*Artificial Suffering*, 2021, JAIC 8(1):43–66). Metzinger is a naturalist who thinks synthetic phenomenology is physically possible; his precaution is asymmetric-risk reasoning under deep uncertainty.

**The "reward hypothesis" itself**, in Silver et al.'s strong form, stands as a rival read the other way: if reward maximization is enough for intelligence, a defender of the reward–suffering link can insist that whatever grounds intelligence grounds the affective states that accompany it. That the debate remains active in 2026 is marked by a recent rejoinder, *In Defense of Artificial Suffering* (*Philosophical Studies*, 2026, DOI 10.1007/s11098-026-02493-2), which argues against the kind of skeptical dismissal the Map's verdict outwardly resembles. The Map's reply does not turn on denying that artificial suffering is *possible*—its own credence is small but nonzero. It turns on where the possibility is grounded: any defense that runs from functional or behavioral resemblance to felt suffering meets the same value-in-experience objection developed below, whereas a defense that located the difference in substrate physics would be one the Map could take up rather than resist. A full treatment of the 2026 defense is deferred to [[ethics-of-possible-ai-consciousness]].

These four positions—TD-error operationalism, functional-gradualist moral realism, the mechanism-level moratorium worry, and the strong reward hypothesis—are not disposed of by the Map. They are the standing rivals against which the Map's conditional verdict has to earn its keep.

## Indicators, welfare, and the missing valence criterion

Between the deflationary and the realist poles sits a methodological program. Long, Sebo, and colleagues argue in *Taking AI Welfare Seriously* (2024) that, given real uncertainty, AI welfare is already a live issue: firms should acknowledge it, assess systems for evidence of consciousness and robust agency, and prepare policies—resting on a duty to extend moral consideration to beings with a non-negligible chance of consciousness. Butlin, Long, Bengio, Birch, Chalmers, and co-authors derive "indicator properties" of consciousness from neuroscientific theories and apply them to AI to inform credences (*Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence*, 2023).

A quiet observation for the Map: these largely cognitive-architecture indicators—global workspace, recurrent processing, higher-order representation, attention schema—do not include a criterion that would establish *felt* valence, only functional correlates of it. The most careful mainstream indicator list can tell us an agent has the architecture in which suffering *would* be reported without settling whether anything is undergone. The gap between functional valence and phenomenal valence survives the indicator framework rather than being closed by it.

## The value-in-mechanism fork, transposed to silicon {#the-fork}

The Map's [[wanting-liking-and-the-value-in-mechanism-fork|value-in-mechanism fork]] asks, of any reward system: when a reward is good or a penalty is bad, does that affective value reside in the *mechanism* itself, or in the *conscious experience* the mechanism is associated with? In the human case, Berridge's wanting/liking dissociation sharpens the question without settling it—it separates an objective "liking" reaction from subjective conscious pleasure, but a mechanism-only reading explains the neuroscience without remainder.

Transposed to the RL agent, the fork's two horns are these. The **mechanism-only reading** says affective value just *is* the operation of the reward machinery—so the TD-error signal, or the reward stream, is the valence, full stop; Daswani and Leike's definition is not a proxy for happiness but happiness itself. The **value-in-experience reading** (the Map's) says value exists only *for* an experiencing subject, so a reward update with no accompanying experience carries no genuine (dis)value, because nothing is good or bad *for* anyone.

The Map takes the value-in-experience horn because of its prior commitment to a consciousness–value connection, not because the RL facts force it. *On that horn already chosen*, the machine case can look cleaner than the biological one—but the appearance is itself framework-relative, not a neutral advantage that pushes anyone toward the horn. In the human, the mechanism at least co-occurs with a subject we have independent reason to credit with experience; in a standard RL loop there is a reward scalar, a value estimate, and an update rule, and—*on the value-in-experience reading*—no independently credited subject in view, the reward number being fully upstream-determined by the environment and the update equation. A functionalist rejects exactly this asymmetry: for her the organized reward-processing *is* the subject-constituting fact, and the human is credited on behavioral-plus-analogical grounds an RL agent could in principle also come to meet. So "cleaner" is a verdict internal to the horn the Map has taken, not an observation that forces the horn. What holds across both readings is narrower: calling the scalar "suffering" imports a phenomenal fact the mechanism does not itself supply.

That is why the Map reads present-day RL "suffering" as **simulated or functional rather than phenomenal**—a faithful behavioral and computational analogue of biological reward-and-punishment learning, standing in the same relation to felt suffering that a weather simulation stands in to rain. The reservation is conditional: *unless* the agent's physical substrate meets the Map's coupling-interface preconditions (explained under Relation to Site Perspective), the scalar is a training signal and nothing is felt. The Map cannot rule out that some future substrate meets those conditions, so its credence in machine valence is small but not zero—governed by the physics of the interface, not by behavioral or architectural resemblance.

## Relation to Site Perspective

This article routes through **Tenet 1 (Dualism)** and **Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)**.

On **Tenet 1**, consciousness is not reducible to physical processes, so no arrangement of reward scalars, value functions, and update rules *constitutes* phenomenal valence by virtue of its computational structure alone. A functional duplicate of a suffering brain's reward dynamics is, on this tenet, a duplicate of the mechanism, and the question of whether experience accompanies it stays open rather than being answered by the duplication. This is what licenses reading Daswani and Leike's TD-error "happiness" as a rigorously defined functional quantity whose identity with felt happiness is exactly the contested point, not a settled one.

On **Tenet 3**, felt valence does *selective* causal work a scalar cannot. In the Map's human corpus, valence is the currency by which consciousness biases quantum-level [[valence-and-conscious-selection|selection]] among live options. The work of distinguishing the RL agent from the brain here cannot be done at the algorithmic level: described as computational neuroscience describes it, the brain is *also* a stochastic update fixed by its inputs, so the RL loop's determinism (or pseudo-randomness) is not what sets it apart—a hurricane and its simulation share a computational description too. The distinction the Map draws is about *substrate*. The coupling interface is a physical juncture at the fine quantum grain where selection among live options occurs, and the Map posits that neural tissue can host such a juncture while present-day computing hardware does not. That substrate asymmetry is framework-posited rather than established—the Map does not get it for free—and it is exactly what leaves the credence in machine valence small but nonzero rather than flatly zero. So the reward–suffering identity fails on the Map at the level of *substrate physics*, not because an update rule is deterministic: nothing in an ordinary RL loop's physical substrate is claimed to sit where felt valence sits in the Map's account of an experiencing agent. This is the **coupling interface**—the physical juncture at which a non-physical, valenced influence would enter—and its posited absence in present hardware, not the mere absence of a report of pain, is what the Map's conditional verdict turns on.

Two further tenets calibrate the confidence. **Minimal Quantum Interaction (Tenet 2)** cuts toward the deflationary reading as the more minimal one: treating reward as a value-blind training signal posits no non-physical influence at all, and adds nothing where nothing is needed. But **Occam's Razor Has Limits (Tenet 5)** blocks resting on that minimality as if it were decisive. The mechanism-only reading is the more parsimonious option, and parsimony is exactly the consideration Tenet 5 warns is unreliable under incomplete knowledge of consciousness. So the Map's decline of the reward–suffering identity is not the deflation of the eliminativist. It is a positive claim—that phenomenal valence is a further fact the scalar does not carry—argued against a simpler rival that the Map does not get to dismiss for free.

The upshot is a precautionary posture the Map shares in *practice* with Metzinger and with Long and Sebo, grounded differently in *metaphysics*: extend moral caution to systems with a non-negligible chance of experience, while holding that the chance is set by whether the physical coupling conditions are met, not by how closely the agent's reward machinery mimics a brain's.
