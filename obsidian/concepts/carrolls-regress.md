**Carroll's regress** is the argument Lewis Carroll stages in his 1895 dialogue "What the Tortoise Said to Achilles" (*Mind* 4(14): 278–280). From premises *A* ("Things that are equal to the same are equal to each other") and *B* ("The two sides of this Triangle are things that are equal to the same"), Achilles asks the Tortoise to accept conclusion *Z* ("The two sides of this Triangle are equal to each other"). The Tortoise refuses unless Achilles supplies a further premise *C*: "If A and B are true, Z must be true." Once *C* is accepted, the Tortoise demands *D* ("If A and B and C are true, Z must be true"). And so on indefinitely. Each candidate rule, once added to the accepted premises, can itself be questioned. Something other than rule-citation must convert the premises into the assertion of *Z*.

The regress is short, deceptively elementary, and dialectically inexhaustible. It has been read as a footnote to proof theory, a precursor to Wittgenstein's rule-following paradox, an early entry in the lineage of structural limitations on formal systems, and a demonstration of an irreducible mental act underlying inference. The Map treats Carroll's regress as the canonical entry point into the [[inference-void|inference void]] — the structural opacity of inferential transitions to the consciousness that performs them — while acknowledging that the regress admits a deflationary reading on which it is no such thing.

This page states the regress, distinguishes its two principal readings, and traces the lines of influence (Wittgenstein, Polanyi, Brandom) that develop or contest the inferentialist reading. It is intended as a reference target for other Map articles that need to cite the regress without re-establishing it.

## The Dialogue

Carroll's dialogue is built around an asymmetry between premises and rules. The Tortoise will accept any premise stated in the form of a proposition: he is happy to grant *A*, *B*, and as many further premises as Achilles cares to add. What he refuses is to accept that the conjunction of premises *forces* the conclusion. Achilles' attempt to *state* the inferential rule — to write down "if these premises, then this conclusion" — only adds another proposition the Tortoise can grant without thereby being moved to assent to *Z*. The dialogue ends with Achilles still writing down premises while the Tortoise smiles.

Carroll himself drew no explicit moral. The dialogue's two-page form is consistent with several readings, and Carroll's own commitments are largely opaque from the text. What he produced is a structural demonstration: any attempt to fully discharge the move from premises to conclusion into further premises generates an infinite regress. The interpretive question is what that demonstration shows.

## The Two Principal Readings

### Proof-Theoretic Deflation

The standard reading in modern logic treats Carroll's regress as illustrating a syntactic distinction now built into every formal system: rules of inference are not on a par with premises. *Modus ponens* functions *as a rule* governing the calculus, not *as a premise* within it. Once the rule/axiom split is in place — already implicit in Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (1879) and explicit in the Hilbert-style and natural deduction systems that followed — the regress terminates. The Tortoise's mistake, on this reading, is to treat the rule of *modus ponens* as if it were a further premise that needed asserting. It is not; it is a structural feature of the deductive system, applied rather than asserted.

On the deflationary reading, Carroll's regress is dissolved by proof theory and points to nothing about consciousness. It is a useful object lesson for students of logic — reminding them that the apparatus of rules cannot be flattened into the apparatus of axioms — but it carries no metaphysical implications about what minds do when they reason. This is the position of most mainstream logic and philosophy-of-logic textbooks.

### The Inferentialist Reading

A minority but persistent reading — articulated by Pascal Engel and developed across the *Inference and Consciousness* essay collection — accepts the rule/axiom distinction but argues it relabels rather than dissolves the puzzle. What makes a candidate proposition *function as* a rule rather than *as one more premise* is precisely the *taking* of the premises as licensing a conclusion. The proof-theoretic move presupposes the very intuition the regress calls into question: the cognitive grip distinguishing following a rule from accepting a further premise. Engel argues that no formal logic can supply what the regress reveals as missing; the missing element is a primitive *taking-as* that no augmentation of explicit rules reproduces.

On this reading, Carroll's regress demonstrates that explicit rules cannot, by themselves, produce inference. Some non-rule operation — variously called *taking-as*, *seeing-that*, or *tacit inference* — must do the actual work of converting premises into conclusion. The inferentialist reading is contested. The Map finds it more compelling than the deflation, but the article's later moves rest on the inferentialist reading being defensible, not uncontested.

The inferentialist reading must itself be split into two registers, because what it strictly establishes and what the Map's tenet-connections later draw from it are not the same claim. The *epistemic* register is what the regress directly delivers: no augmentation of *explicit* rules reproduces what taking-as does, so rule-application cannot be exhausted by articulated rule-citation. This is an articulation or formalisation limit — the Polanyian "we know more than we can tell" applied to inference. The *metaphysical* register is stronger: that taking-as is a *constitutively non-formal* operation rather than an unformalised-but-physical process that a completed cognitive science might one day specify without remainder. The articulation-limit does not by itself force the metaphysical reading; a hard-nosed physicalist can grant that explicit rule-citation never exhausts rule-application and still hold that the doing is neural, merely not capturable in the medium of stated rules. The Map's bridge from the first register to the second is given below under [[tenets|Dualism]], and is flagged there as the contested step, not asserted as a consequence of the regress.

## Lines of Influence

**Wittgenstein's rule-following paradox** (*Philosophical Investigations*, 1953) extends Carroll's regress from inferential to rule-governed cognition generally. PI §201: "no course of action could be determined by a rule, because every course of action can be made out to accord with the rule." Any specification of an arithmetical rule admits an interpretation under which the next instance produces a deviant answer (Saul Kripke's "quus" case), and rules for interpreting rules face the same threat one level up. Wittgenstein's response is anti-foundationalist: at PI §219, "When I obey a rule, I do not choose. I obey the rule blindly." Practice precedes and grounds rule-citation. Subsequent dispositionalist, communitarian, and Kripkean responses do not reduce rule-following to a fully non-mental procedure.

The relation to Carroll is parallel pressure on the same point with materially different metaphysical commitments rather than convergence. Carroll, on the inferentialist reading, points toward a positive operation; Wittgenstein points away from any further posit.

**Polanyi's tacit inference** (1966) is a positive characterisation of what Carroll and Wittgenstein argue against. His *Tacit Dimension* opens from the fact that "we know more than we can tell." Tacit inferences move from clues to indicated wholes by an operation that resists full propositional articulation without distortion. Some readers take Polanyi's position to be methodological; others, including the Map, read it as metaphysical. Either way, Polanyi treats the asymmetry between what cognition does and what cognition can articulate as constitutive. The Map develops Polanyi's structural claim — subsidiaries bear focal meaning by *not* being focal themselves; thematising the integration collapses what it carries — under the heading [[tacit-integration-void|tacit integration void]]. The point of contact with Carroll's regress is direct: stating the rule cannot do what following the rule does, for the same structural reason that thematising the subsidiaries cannot do what attending *from* them does. Both readings identify an operation whose work depends on its receding from explicit articulation.

**Brandom's inferentialism** (*Making It Explicit*, 1994) is the major competing framework that takes inference as primitive without locating an opacity. Brandom's normative pragmatics treats inferential commitments as *constitutive of conceptual content*: to grasp a concept just is to participate in the practice of giving and asking for reasons. On this account, "taking-as" is not a non-rule operation of consciousness but a social-normative practice in which licences to infer are conferred and contested. The Map treats Brandom as relocating rather than dissolving the question, and there are two ways to press the point. The first stays inside Brandom's framework: when a community confers or withholds an inferential licence, that conferral is itself the *taking* of a move as licensed — a normative attitude the practice must already exercise, not derive — so the regress recurs one level up rather than terminating, without any appeal to individual priority. Carroll's pattern reappears as: what makes the community's sanction *count as* licensing rather than as one more move in the game? The second way does presuppose a contested priority — that social practice rests on individual takings — which is precisely the order of constitution Brandom denies; pressed that way, the disagreement turns on a prior commitment rather than on a refutation inside Brandom's framework, and is honestly noted as such. The Map's considered position rests on the first, in-framework argument; the Brandomian alternative remains the strongest in the contemporary literature.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map reads Carroll's regress through the inferentialist lens and treats it as load-bearing for two tenets.

**Occam's Razor Has Limits.** The regress is, on the inferentialist reading, an argument that the simplest account of inference — *inference is rule-following* — is insufficient. This is the textbook case of where parsimony is contested: a phenomenon whose simplicity-first analysis is argued to be incomplete on the inferentialist reading rather than settled by mere appeal to phenomenology. The deflationary reading restores the simple account by partitioning rules from premises. Against the deflation the Map enters a scoped charge: applied to a *formal calculus*, the rule/axiom partition presupposes nothing cognitive — a natural-deduction system applies *modus ponens* with no agent taking anything, and the regress is dissolved there cleanly. But read as an account of an *agent's* reasoning, where rule-application is something the agent does, the partition presupposes the very taking-as the regress reveals as missing — a [[self-stultification|self-stultifying]] move that helps itself, for agents, to what it claims to dispense with. The self-stultification charge is scoped to the agent-act reading; the deflationist who restricts the partition to formal systems does not commit it, and the inferentialist must then independently argue that human reasoning is not relevantly like a formal calculus.

**Dualism.** The inferentialist reading, taken in its epistemic register, establishes only an articulation limit: that no augmentation of explicit rules reproduces taking-as. Crossing from that limit to a *constitutively non-formal operation of consciousness* is the contested bridge — and the bridge, not the regress, is what the Dualism connection rests on. The Map's reason for crossing it is not that an articulation-limit logically entails a non-physical operation (it does not: an unformalised-but-physical process would produce the same limit), but that taking-as resists formalisation in the same structural way the [[tacit-integration-void|tacit integration void]] resists thematisation — the resistance is not a contingent gap awaiting better science but a constitutive feature of the operation, recurring at every level of attempted articulation. That structural recurrence is the Map's evidence, weighed under [[tenets|Occam's Razor Has Limits]], that taking-as is a candidate site for an irreducible operation rather than an as-yet-unspecified physical one. The Map speculates — flagging this whole bridge, not merely the analogy below, as speculation — that *taking-as* may be one face of the irreducibly mental, adjacent to phenomenal consciousness. The physicalist reading of the same articulation-limit remains live; the Map's claim is that the regress keeps the question open, not that it closes it. Carroll's regress predates Gödel's incompleteness theorems by 36 years and, on the inferentialist reading, anticipates a family resemblance to the Gödel-Turing-Chaitin lineage of structural limitations on formal systems. The resemblance is loose: Gödel's results are formal theorems within a specified system; Carroll's regress is a dialectical pattern about how rules apply. The Map treats them as cousins, not siblings.

The article does not claim Carroll's regress *settles* the question of whether inference involves a non-formal mental operation. It claims, more modestly, that the regress is one of the strongest available arguments that the question is open — and that the burden lies with reductive accounts to explain what makes a rule function as a rule rather than as one more premise.
