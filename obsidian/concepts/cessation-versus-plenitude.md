At their deepest attainments, the contemplative traditions disagree about whether the ultimate state is the *cessation* of experience or its *fullest expression*. Theravāda Buddhism prizes *nirodha samāpatti*—the complete stopping of perception and feeling. Advaita Vedānta describes the ultimate as *sat-chit-ānanda*, being-consciousness-bliss, awareness at maximal fullness. These are reports about the presence or absence of content at the limit of practice, and they point in opposite directions. The [[comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions|comparative phenomenology of meditative traditions]] names this divergence as "the most significant" of the three it catalogues, holds that it resists merely interpretive dismissal, and moves on. This article develops it as its own evidential phenomenon.

The contribution here is narrow and deliberate. The cessation-versus-plenitude divergence is *not resolved* below—neither horn of the dilemma it poses is adjudicated. What the divergence does is constrain how far the cross-traditional *convergence* argument (the argument that traditions converging on no-self, non-dual awareness, and a witness register supplies phenomenological evidence the dualist reading can use) can reach. The divergence is a Pre-Kepler-stage observation in the sense [[framework-stage-calibration|framework-stage calibration]] specifies: empirically reported across more than a millennium of primary sources, not yet theoretically integrated. Naming it as a calibration constraint, rather than explaining it away or promoting either reading, is the whole of the work.

## The Reports, Side by Side

The divergence is established at the level of what the traditions report. The exposition below stays in that register; the Map's integration is held to its own section.

### Cessation: Theravāda *Nirodha Samāpatti*

In the Theravāda *jhāna* literature, *nirodha samāpatti*—the "attainment of cessation"—is the complete stopping of perception and feeling (*saññā-vedayita-nirodha*). Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* (5th century CE) treats it as a profound fruit of practice available only to the most advanced practitioners (the *anāgāmi* and *arahant*, those who have reached the higher stages of awakening), reached by progressing through the formless attainments and then suspending mental activity altogether. The contemplative-science literature describes the phenomenology in the same terms: Laukkonen et al. (2023) document advanced meditators reporting a total absence of consciousness, with the cessation "voluntary but spontaneous," and practitioners reportedly impervious to external stimulation for the duration.

Two qualifiers matter and are easy to drop. First, *nirodha samāpatti* is not Theravāda Buddhism's ultimate goal; *nibbāna* is described with positive epithets ("the unconditioned," "the deathless") alongside cessation language, so the tradition does not flatly equate its summit with the absence of experience. Second, Mahāyāna complicates the picture further: Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* argues that *nirvāṇa* and *saṃsāra* are not ultimately distinct, resisting any clean identification of the Buddhist goal with cessation at all. "Cessation," then, names a specific attested attainment, not a unified Buddhist verdict that the deepest state is empty.

### Plenitude: Advaita *Sat-Chit-Ānanda*

Advaita Vedānta privileges the opposite pole. The ultimate, *Brahman* realised as one's own *Ātman*, is characterised as *sat-chit-ānanda*—being, consciousness, and bliss. The state is described as awareness at maximal fullness: infinite, self-luminous, and saturated rather than emptied. Where the Theravādin reports the stopping of perception and feeling, the Advaitin reports awareness that has become unbounded. Patañjali's *Yoga Sūtras* sit nearby but are not identical: yoga is defined (1.2) as "the cessation of the modifications of the mind," and *nirvikalpa samādhi* is absorption without conceptual modification—but what *remains* on the Sāṃkhya-Yoga reading is *puruṣa*, pure awareness, present rather than absent. The Vedāntic and Yogic reports therefore agree against the Theravāda cessation report on the load-bearing point: at the limit, awareness *persists* and is full, not stopped.

Christian contemplation supplies a third data point on the plenitude side: the beatific vision is described as infinitely rich experience, the fullness of encountering God. The plenitude pole is thus attested across unconnected traditions, just as the cessation pole is attested in the Theravāda sources—which is what makes the divergence resistant to dismissal as one tradition's idiosyncrasy.

## The Dilemma

Laid against the convergence argument, the divergence forces a dilemma. The convergence argument draws evidential weight from traditions agreeing, across minimal historical contact, on structural features of advanced consciousness—a witness register, the dissolution of the ordinary self, non-dual awareness. Cessation-versus-plenitude is a place where the traditions *disagree* about the most load-bearing feature of all: whether, at the limit, there is experience at all. Either horn constrains the convergence argument, and both must be treated honestly.

**Horn (a): the end-states genuinely branch.** Advanced practice may produce structurally distinct attainments—the Theravādin trained toward cessation arrives somewhere the Advaitin trained toward plenitude does not, and vice versa. If so, there is no single "deepest state of consciousness" the traditions are converging on; there are at least two, and they differ at the level of presence versus absence of content. On this horn, the convergence argument's evidential reach is *more limited* than the convergence zones alone suggest. The realism the convergence supports is bounded by what the traditions agree on, and they do not agree here. The convergence remains real where it occurs; it simply does not extend to the end-states.

**Horn (b): the reports misrepresent a deeper convergence.** Alternatively, the traditions may be reaching the same place and *describing it incompatibly*—cessation and plenitude being two verbal handles on a single state that exceeds both descriptions. The state might be "empty" from the standpoint of content-perception and "full" from the standpoint of awareness-as-such, with each tradition's vocabulary fastening onto one aspect. If so, the methodology hits a limit that needs naming: the convergence, if it exists, lies *below* the level at which verbal reports can certify it. Call this the aphasia-of-mysticism limit—the point past which the introspective report stops being a reliable instrument because what is to be reported resists articulation. The [[ineffable-encounter-void|ineffable encounter void]] names the general structure: experiences whose evaluating faculty is itself overwhelmed by what is encountered. On this horn the convergence argument cannot draw on the end-states either, not because they branch but because the reports cannot adjudicate whether they branch.

The dilemma is genuine because both horns are live and the available evidence does not discriminate between them. A first-person report of cessation and a first-person report of plenitude are exactly what we would expect on horn (a) *and* exactly what we would expect on horn (b)—the reports underdetermine which is the case. This is the feature that makes the divergence a calibration constraint rather than a settled finding: it marks the boundary of what the convergence argument can claim, on either reading of why the boundary is there.

## Why the Divergence Cannot Resolve Itself

The temptation is to break the dilemma in the Map's favour—to argue that horn (b) is correct, that the deeper convergence is real, and that cessation and plenitude are two faces of one substance-like awareness the dualist tenet posits. That move must be resisted, and naming why is the discipline of this article.

Horn (b), read as "a single full awareness underlies both reports," is a *substantialist* reading: it treats awareness as a persisting something that is present in the plenitude reports and merely unreported (not absent) in the cessation reports. But the Theravāda and Madhyamaka sources are precisely the traditions that *deny* this reading. The Madhyamaka register, following Nāgārjuna, refuses the inference from "the self is empty of inherent existence" to "awareness is a substance other than its contents." On that register, *nirodha samāpatti* is not awareness persisting unreported; it is the attested cessation of the dependently-arisen process, with no substance left over to be full. To resolve the dilemma toward horn (b)'s substantialist version is therefore to take sides *against* the Buddhist contribution—to use one tradition's vocabulary to overrule another's, which is exactly the interpretive imposition the convergence argument is supposed to avoid.

This is where [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]] does its work. The tenets remove a defeater: dualism makes it *coherent* to read the divergence as two aspects of one non-physical awareness. But removing the defeater does not supply positive evidence that this reading is correct, and it certainly does not license overruling the traditions that report otherwise. The coherence of the substantialist integration is tenet-register support; it is not evidence-register support, and it cannot be counted as adjudicating the dilemma. A reviewer who fully accepts the Map's tenets would still flag any passage that converted "dualism makes horn (b) coherent" into "horn (b) is what is happening"—which is the diagnostic test that marks the move as calibration error rather than bedrock disagreement.

The honest verdict is that the divergence is a *constraint*, not an *establishment*. The reports constrain the simplest convergence claim—that the traditions agree all the way down to the end-state—by exhibiting a place where they do not agree. They do not establish either that the end-states branch (horn a) or that a deeper convergence escapes description (horn b). The convergence argument's strength is calibrated against this divergence, not against an idealised dataset in which the traditions agree everywhere.

## Sibling To Affective-Tone Divergence

This article is the sibling of [[affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions|affective-tone divergence across meditative traditions]], the other divergence the parent article names. The relationship is worth stating precisely because the two operate on different axes, and the parent treats affective tone as "a sibling phenomenon at a less load-bearing register."

Affective-tone divergence is about *valence at the engagement register*: whether the advanced state is flat (Buddhist *upekkhā*), ecstatic (Bhakti/Sufi devotion), oscillating (Christian consolation and aridity), or at ease (Daoist *ziran*). It concerns the emotional character of the engaging, not how much content is present. The affective sibling has a candidate integration available—the interface-mode hypothesis, which reads the differing valences as downstream signatures of which mode of consciousness-physics engagement a tradition trains, held at "a serious possibility" rather than promoted.

Cessation-versus-plenitude is about the *presence or absence of content* at the limit: nothing versus everything. It is the more load-bearing of the two precisely because it concerns the end-state the traditions are aiming at, not the affective coloration of states along the way. And it is the harder of the two because the affective sibling's tidy reading—differing valences from differing trained modes need not imply differing end-states—has no clean analogue here. Differing *presence-of-content* reports *do* bear directly on whether the end-states differ. Where affective divergence can be read as consistent variation, cessation-versus-plenitude cannot be so read without taking sides in the substantialism dispute. That asymmetry is why affective tone is the lighter divergence and cessation-versus-plenitude the one that bounds the convergence argument at its most load-bearing point.

Both divergences share a status, however: both are Pre-Kepler-stage observations the convergence argument must *accommodate* rather than *explain away*, and both are constraints on the argument's reach rather than evidence that promotes any reading of why the divergence occurs.

## Relation to Site Perspective

### Dualism at the Calibration Register

The Map's interest in this divergence is framework-relative and disciplined. [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]] does real work in keeping both horns *available*: a physicalist reading that identifies awareness with neural activity would tend to dissolve the dilemma by treating cessation as the simpler, more parsimonious description (no experience, no neural correlate of experience) and plenitude as elaboration on residual activity. Dualism removes that parsimony defeater—it makes horn (b)'s "deeper convergence" a coherent possibility rather than an extravagance. But the discipline of this article is that defeater-removal stops there. The Map does not present the cessation-versus-plenitude data as evidence *for* dualism; the data are equally available to the identity theorist, who reads cessation as the absence of the neural state and plenitude as its saturation. What dualism offers is a frame in which the divergence is a genuine open question about the structure of consciousness, rather than a closed question with a parsimony-driven default. The frame is what the tenet supplies; the resolution is what the evidence withholds.

The divergence accordingly sharpens the Map's self-understanding at the calibration layer rather than adding to its evidential base. It marks the boundary at which the convergence argument—one of the Map's phenomenological inputs—stops being able to draw weight. A framework that named the convergence zones but not this divergence would read as more evidentially secure than it is. Naming the divergence is what keeps the convergence claim honest about its own reach.
