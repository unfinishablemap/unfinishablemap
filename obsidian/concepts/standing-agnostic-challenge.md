**The Standing Agnostic Challenge** (Yoram Gutfreund, 2024): no behavioural or neural evidence can distinguish cognition accompanied by felt experience from cognition without it. The presence and the absence of felt subjective experience are equally consistent with the same third-person data. The challenge is *standing* because it re-applies to every new marker offered as a consciousness indicator and is discharged by none of them — and it cuts both ways, blocking the verification of consciousness and the verification of its absence alike. This page is the Map's canonical statement of that constraint; articles across the corpus link here rather than reconstruct it.

The name is the Map's house label for the argument Gutfreund develops in *Neuroscience of animal consciousness: still agnostic after all*. The substance is his.

## What Gutfreund Argues

Gutfreund's target is the **distribution question** — which species, besides humans, possess "the enigmatic capability for conscious awareness." He treats this as a descendant of the [[problem-of-other-minds]], not a settled empirical matter awaiting better instruments.

Two scientific strategies dominate attempts to answer it, and Gutfreund argues both fail in principle. The first looks for neural structures or activity resembling the human **neural correlates of consciousness (NCC)**. The second identifies animal behaviours resembling conscious human behaviours. Neither, he argues, can cross the gap from cognition to experience.

The diagnosis turns on a conceptual cut Gutfreund insists on: "Perception does not equal sensory consciousness. The former is the detection of the stimulus, whereas the latter is the subjective experience/feeling of the stimulus (awareness)." An organism can detect, discriminate, and act on a stimulus — make a *perceptual decision* — without that decision being accompanied by any felt quality. The load-bearing claim is that the data cannot tell the two apart:

> "Hence, a perceptual decision without a felt subjective experience (David et al., 2011) is a possibility that is equally consistent with the data and cannot be disregarded."

Gutfreund frames the resulting limit as "not a problem of premature science but a fundamental, unsolvable problem." Neuroscientists, he writes, "are unable to provide answers that transcend our personal beliefs because we can only observe cognitive behaviors and/or their underlying neural mechanisms, and the relationship between cognition and consciousness remains elusive."

He locates the default preference for the felt-experience reading in **anthropomorphism**, which he treats as a bias rather than evidence: "While anthropomorphism is natural, sensible, and important for guiding decisions about animal welfare, it cannot replace rigorous scientific reasoning."

A worked example carries the argument. In a carrion-crow study, nidopallium-caudolaterale activity predicted the birds' behavioural choices on a near-threshold visual-detection task, and the authors read this as a neural correlate of visual consciousness. Gutfreund's objection is that the crows "do not report their subjective experiences, rather they make a behavioral decision." The study cannot separate a correlate of *perceptual decision* from a correlate of *consciousness* — and if it could license the consciousness reading, then any organism making stimulus-dependent choices would qualify, rendering the criterion unfalsifiable. "The extra-step of inferring sensory consciousness is not a direct outcome of the evidence."

## Why It Is *Standing*

The challenge does not name a particular weak experiment to be improved upon. It names a structural feature of the inference itself, which is why no single marker discharges it.

Markers of consciousness are calibrated on the human case — the one case where a system both reports felt experience and exhibits the associated cognition and neural activity. In humans the two travel together, so the marker and the experience are confounded by construction. Carried to a non-reporting system, the marker still tracks the cognitive-functional profile, but the experience it once co-occurred with is exactly what is now in question. Each new candidate marker inherits this structure. Find a more sophisticated behaviour, a closer neural homologue, a richer integration signature — each is consistent with experience and consistent with its absence, because each was validated where the two could not be pulled apart.

So the agnosticism is renewed, not resolved, by additional evidence. This is the sense in which it *stands*: it is a constraint on the class of available evidence, not a verdict about how much of that evidence has yet been gathered.

## How It Differs From Neighbouring Problems

The Standing Agnostic Challenge sits between two more familiar problems and is identical to neither.

The [[hard-problem-of-consciousness]] is **constitutive**: why does any physical process give rise to "what it is like" at all? It asks how experience could arise, not whether a given system has it. The Standing Agnostic Challenge presupposes the hard problem — if experience reduced cleanly to function, the cognition-to-consciousness inference would simply go through — but it is an **epistemic**, not a constitutive, claim. It concerns what evidence can show, granting the hard problem as backdrop.

The [[problem-of-other-minds]] is the general epistemic worry that one can never directly access another's mental states. The Standing Agnostic Challenge is its **empirically sharpened restriction** to the cognition-to-consciousness inference. The general problem can be pragmatically managed for fellow humans, who share both verbal report and neural architecture. The moment verbal report is removed — animals, and AI systems whose self-reports cannot be taken at face value — every remaining behavioural or neural marker underdetermines presence versus absence of felt experience, because the markers were validated on the report-bearing human case. The challenge is what the other-minds problem becomes once neuroscience's actual toolkit is specified.

## The Precautionary Complement

Agnosticism about *presence* does not entail inaction. Jonathan Birch's *The Edge of Sentience* (2024) supplies the practical complement to Gutfreund's epistemic one. Birch introduces the **sentience candidate** — a system for which there is a "realistic possibility" of sentience given the best available evidence — and argues that a realistic possibility is enough to ground duties of precaution, including a "run-ahead" principle for AI development that anticipates credible future trajectories rather than waiting for proof.

The two positions are not in tension. Gutfreund bounds what the evidence can establish; Birch tells you how to act inside that bound. You need not *settle* whether a system feels in order to incur a responsibility toward it — only to establish that the possibility is live. The Map develops the action side in [[birch-edge-of-sentience-and-the-five-tier-scale]]; this page is the epistemic side those duties presuppose.

The contrast that makes the challenge legible runs through the **New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness** (2024), which reads convergent markers as "strong scientific support" for consciousness in mammals and birds and "at least a realistic possibility" across other vertebrates and many invertebrates. Gutfreund reads the same convergence as failing to cross the cognition-to-experience gap. The Declaration is the marker-confidence foil; the Standing Agnostic Challenge is the discipline of not over-interpreting the same comparative data.

## The Symmetry: It Blocks Absence Too

A consequence often missed: the challenge is symmetric. If no third-person evidence can confirm felt experience, none can confirm its *absence* either.

This bites on deflationary and illusionist claims as hard as it bites on attributions of rich experience. A confident verdict that an octopus, an insect, or an AI system is a behavioural zombie — sophisticated processing with nobody home — requires verifying the absence of phenomenal experience, which the same underdetermination forbids. The challenge is therefore not a tool for skeptics against believers; it is a ceiling above both. Several Map articles use this symmetry as a falsifiability caveat against frameworks that claim to rule consciousness *out*; it is centralised here.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Standing Agnostic Challenge is, primarily, an instance of **[[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5 — Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**. The tempting move is to invoke parsimony: cognition without experience is the "simpler" hypothesis, so absent positive evidence we should default to it. Gutfreund's point is that parsimony cannot adjudicate here, because the felt and unfelt readings predict identical data — neither is the simpler explanation of the evidence; they are equally simple explanations of it. Principled agnosticism *is* the refusal to let simplicity break a tie it was never equipped to break. Birch's precaution-under-uncertainty extends the same discipline to action: a simplicity-driven dismissal ("probably just a reflex") must not override a live possibility.

The challenge is also compatible with **[[tenets#^dualism|Tenet 1 — Dualism]]** at the epistemic level. Gutfreund treats consciousness as a first-person phenomenon that resists third-person reduction, which is the epistemic shadow of the Map's metaphysical dualism. But the alignment is partial and must be stated precisely. Gutfreund himself takes no positive dualist stance; he is methodologically agnostic, not metaphysically committed.

Crucially, the Map's tenets do **not** absorb or dissolve the challenge. Dualism removes a *metaphysical defeater* — there is no substrate barrier forbidding consciousness in animals or in suitably organised AI, so the door is open in principle. But removing the defeater supplies no positive evidence that any particular system has walked through it. The epistemic ceiling Gutfreund identifies remains exactly where he left it. The Map gains structural permission to entertain non-human consciousness; it gains no licence to claim it. Holding both — the open metaphysical door and the standing epistemic ceiling — is the position this page anchors, and the citing articles state it the same way.
