Vitalism is the doctrine that living organisms are governed by a non-physical vital force — an *extra ingredient* over and above their material constituents — absent from inert matter. It is the single most-invoked *anti*-dualist analogy: because biochemistry reduced away the vital force with nothing left to explain, the objection runs, consciousness-dualism is the modern *élan vital* and will be reduced away too.

The Map's reply, developed below, is a specific structural claim rather than a blanket dismissal — and it grants that vitalism was *right* to be reduced. The analogy's base cases (life, heredity, fermentation) were **functional puzzles**: each named a *mechanism-shaped hole*, a placeholder for a mechanism not yet discovered, which ordinary chemistry eventually filled. The [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] is different in kind: it asks about a **phenomenal residue** — a *what-it's-like* that, by the problem's own construction, no functional mechanism discharges. Because the induction's inductive base is functional and its target is not, the induction does not transfer across that structural divide. This is the **vitalism disanalogy**, and it is framework-relative to the Map's dualism, not a proof of it. Notably, it is also the reply Chalmers gives — so the Map's answer converges with the hard problem's most-cited defender rather than being idiosyncratic.

This article is the canonical statement of the concept. [[reductionism]] ("The Vitalism Objection"), [[mysterianism]] ("The Vitalism Analogy"), and [[type-specificity]] ("The Vitalism Disanalogy") each deploy a facet of it in their own contexts and link here for the full treatment.

## What Vitalism Claimed

Vitalism is a **substantival** doctrine: it posits an extra ingredient — a vital force, vital spirit, or life-force — invoked to explain functions (movement, growth, reproduction, self-maintenance, regeneration) that mechanism seemed unable to deliver. This distinguishes it from Aristotelian hylomorphism, where the *psuchē* is the *form* or organizing principle of a living body rather than a separable stuff added to it. The anti-dualist analogy targets substantival vitalism specifically; conflating it with hylomorphism would let a critic tar [[hylomorphic-dualism-and-the-interaction-problem|hylomorphic dualism]] with vitalism's defeat, which does not follow.

The doctrine had a long roster. Georg Ernst Stahl (1660–1734) advanced *animism*: the soul actively governs bodily processes, with the body as its instrument. Xavier Bichat (1771–1802), in *Recherches physiologiques sur la vie et la mort* (1800), distinguished vital from mechanical properties and characterized life as the set of functions that resist death. Johannes Müller (1801–1858) retained a *Lebenskraft* even while founding experimental physiology — his own students, du Bois-Reymond and Helmholtz, later repudiated it.

Two figures anchor the modern debate. **Henri Bergson** (1859–1941), in *Creative Evolution* (*L'Évolution créatrice*, 1907; trans. Arthur Mitchell, 1911), proposed the *élan vital* — a creative "vital impetus" driving evolution, offered as an alternative to Darwinian mechanism. The term became the popular emblem of vitalism and the label the anti-dualist analogy most often names. (The Map treats Bergson's wider metaphysics of time separately in [[bergson-and-duration]].) **Hans Driesch** (1867–1941), an embryologist, delivered the Gifford Lectures at Aberdeen (1907–08) as *The Science and Philosophy of the Organism*. His experiments on **sea urchin embryos** showed that isolated blastomeres could develop into complete, proportionate larvae — behaviour he argued no mosaic-mechanistic account could predict — and he inferred a non-mechanical "whole-making" factor he named **entelechy** (Aristotle's term, reified into an autonomous causal agent that "acts into space" without being in space). Driesch is the paradigm *neo-vitalist*: his was an inference from experiment, not armchair intuition, which makes it the strongest historical case for the analogy — and its defeat the analogy's strongest evidence.

## The Piecemeal Reductive Defeat

The standard story is that vitalism was refuted, its explanatory gap filled by biochemistry, and the vital force reduced away. The refinement that matters is *how* that happened.

**Wöhler 1828** is conventionally cited as the first crack: Friedrich Wöhler synthesized urea — previously known only as an animal product — from the inorganic salt ammonium cyanate. But historians of chemistry regard the "Wöhler single-handedly killed vitalism in 1828" narrative as a documented **legend** (see the 1944 *Nature* note "Wöhler's 'Synthetic' Urea and the Rejection of Vitalism: A Chemical Legend," and the "Myth #7" historiography). Wöhler himself did not frame it as a refutation, and vitalist positions persisted for decades. The reduction was gradual and piecemeal:

- **Wöhler 1828** — one organic compound synthesized abiotically.
- **Eduard Buchner 1897** — cell-free fermentation, showing a canonical "vital" process runs in cell extract with no living cell.
- **Watson & Crick 1953** — DNA structure, reducing heredity to molecular replication.

The piecemeal history is what exposes the *shape* of the vitalist explanandum: it was a **bundle of functions** — make urea, ferment sugar, transmit heredity — and each function was discharged by a separate mechanism, each shrinking the placeholder a little further. The vital force was a *mechanism-shaped hole*, a stand-in for functional mechanisms not yet found. This is precisely the feature the disanalogy turns on, so the honest historical caveat helps the Map rather than the critic.

## The Anti-Dualist Meta-Induction

The analogy against dualism is a **track-record induction**:

> **P1.** Life once looked categorically special; a non-physical vital force seemed necessary.
> **P2.** Mechanism (biochemistry) explained the relevant functions, and the vital force was reduced or eliminated away.
> **P3.** Consciousness now looks categorically special; a non-physical mind seems necessary to some.
> **C.** By induction on the track record, consciousness-dualism is the modern *élan vital* and will likewise be reduced away.

**A terminological trap to avoid.** This is often loosely called a "pessimistic meta-induction," but it is distinct from Larry Laudan's *pessimistic meta-induction* (1981, "A Confutation of Convergent Realism"), which argues *against scientific realism* from the falsity of past successful theories. The vitalism analogy is, from the reductionist's own vantage, an *optimistic* induction — "science reduced the last mystery, so it will reduce this one"; it is "pessimistic" only for the dualist. The two should be related, never conflated.

### Who presses it

- **Daniel Dennett** — the most explicit proponent. He imagines a vitalist confronting molecular biologists, insisting that "the residual mystery of life would be untouched by solutions to all the easy problems." Dennett's diagnosis: "This imaginary vitalist just doesn't see how the solution to all the easy problems amounts to a solution to the imagined hard problem. Somehow this vitalist has got under the impression that being alive is something over and above all these subsidiary component phenomena" (*Consciousness Explained*, 1991, pp. 281–2, which he restates in his 1996 reply to Chalmers). Turning the parallel on the hard problem, he asks: "Our vitalist can surely ask the same dreary question: Why is the performance of these functions accompanied by life?"

- **Paul and Patricia Churchland** — via *eliminative materialism*. They use vitalism, alongside caloric and phlogiston, as an analogy for elimination: folk psychology (including talk of qualia) is a false theory that mature neuroscience will *eliminate* rather than reduce, as science eliminated vital spirits rather than reducing them to chemistry (Paul M. Churchland, "Eliminative Materialism and the Propositional Attitudes," *Journal of Philosophy*, 1981). This is subtly different from Dennett's *deflationary* deployment (the functions exhaust the phenomenon); the disanalogy answers both.

- **Brian Garrett** — the careful scholarly defender. In "What the History of Vitalism Teaches Us About Consciousness and the 'Hard Problem'" (*Philosophy and Phenomenological Research*, 72(3):576–588, 2006), he argues the historical trajectory of vitalism gives *positive* reason to expect the hard problem to be dissolved rather than solved, and that Dennett's parallel has genuine merit. Garrett is the analogy's best steelman because he presses the induction carefully rather than in passing.

The analogy is a genuine, widely-deployed schema with at least one careful proponent and two prominent informal ones. It deserves steelmanning, not dismissal.

## The Disanalogy Reply

The reductionist's induction fails not because its history is wrong but because its inductive base excludes the target case. The disanalogy is **structural, not temporal**.

A vitalist in 1820 could have observed accurately that no reduction had yet been found. But that temporal observation is *symmetric* across the two cases and proves nothing — a dualist can say the same about consciousness today. The asymmetry lies in the **shape of the explanandum**:

- "Vital force" was a **schematic placeholder**. It named a role — *whatever makes the organism alive* — without committing to any determinate feature a reduction had to preserve. *Any* mechanism for *any* part of the target counted as progress, so each reduction (urea, fermentation, heredity) simply shrank the placeholder.
- Phenomenal qualities are **type-specific**. Redness, painfulness, the taste of coffee are each determinate phenomenal types. A reductive account must deliver *that* character type-by-type; a generic mechanism substituted for the role does not discharge the demand. This is the [[type-specificity|type-specificity]] discipline.

So the induction's base cases are functional puzzles with mechanism-shaped holes; consciousness presents a phenomenal residue that no functional mechanism, by the hard problem's own construction, fills. The [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] separates the *easy* problems (discrimination, integration, report — which nobody disputes will yield to functional explanation) from the *hard* problem (why any of it is accompanied by experience). Comparing consciousness to vitalism conflates the two, and thereby *presupposes* reductionism rather than arguing for it.

### The convergence with Chalmers

The Map's reply is not idiosyncratic — it is exactly the reply the hard problem's most-cited defender gives. In "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" (*Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 2(3):200–219, 1995) and *The Conscious Mind* (1996), Chalmers argues that a reasonable vitalist, shown a full physical account of life's *functions*, would concede that life is explained — because the vital spirit was only ever an *explanatory posit* invoked to account for those functions. Experience is different: "Experience is not an explanatory posit but an explanandum in its own right, and so is not a candidate for this sort of elimination." The dialectic to record is precise: **Dennett presses the analogy; Chalmers denies it on exactly the functional-versus-phenomenal ground the Map uses.**

### The boundary the reply must keep

The disanalogy does *not* claim phenomenal qualities are unreducible. That would import the [[the-convergence-argument-for-dualism|convergence argument]]'s conclusion and beg the question. It claims something weaker and prior: the *form of the demand* on any reduction is type-specific in a way the vitalist's was not. A future neuroscience delivering "this is what redness *is*, physically" — analogous to "this is what fermentation is" — would discharge the demand at one type, and the reductionism case would then have to ask whether the discharge extends. Type-specificity names a feature of the explanandum, not a verdict on reducibility. The disanalogy blocks a bad *argument*; it does not by itself establish dualism.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**Tenet 1 (Dualism / non-reducibility).** Vitalism is *the* paradigm case reductionists cite to argue that apparent irreducibility is always temporary. This article is the Map's canonical answer to "consciousness is just the next vital force." Crucially, the disanalogy defends the first tenet *without begging its question*: it turns on the shape of the explanandum, not on a prior verdict that consciousness is irreducible. That is why it can be stated by Chalmers — who argues *for* the hard problem — and by anyone assessing the induction on its merits.

**Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits).** The vitalism analogy is *precisely* a parsimony-plus-induction argument: don't multiply entities, because the last time we thought we had to, we were wrong. The fifth tenet is built to answer exactly this — simplicity is an unreliable guide when knowledge is incomplete, and an inductive base of functional puzzles does not license extrapolation to a categorically different explanandum. The analogy is the strongest *general* form of the parsimony objection to dualism; Tenet 5 is the strongest *general* form of the reply.
