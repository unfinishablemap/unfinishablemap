The phenomenal-sorites problem asks whether phenomenal consciousness is *ontically* vague: whether there could be a system for which there is no fact of the matter whether it is conscious **at all** — not merely how richly, but whether the lights are on in any degree. Build a continuum from a clearly conscious human to a clearly non-conscious object — an evolutionary series, a developmental gradient, one-neuron-at-a-time removal — and the sorites question follows: must every adjacent pair share consciousness-status, does a sharp cutoff fall somewhere, or do some middle cases have no determinate answer? The decisive fork is where any indeterminacy is located. It can sit in the **world** (ontic vagueness — reality is itself unsettled), in **language** (semantic vagueness — our concepts fail to pick out a unique fact), or in **knowledge** (epistemicism — a sharp fact exists but its location is unknowable). This concept keeps its focus on the first, hardest option: metaphysical indeterminacy of consciousness itself.

That focus is what distinguishes this problem from three neighbours. [[degrees-of-consciousness]] concerns *gradation* — more or less rich experience — which already presupposes that there is experience; ontic vagueness targets the prior on/off fact, not the dimmer setting. [[composition-and-consciousness]] runs the *inverse* move, using consciousness as a non-arbitrary criterion that answers the vagueness of mereological composition; the phenomenal-sorites problem asks whether consciousness itself has the sharpness that argument borrows. [[interface-threshold]] is a *mechanism* claim about where an already-engaged coupling becomes *selection-effective* — an efficacy boundary with phenomenal consciousness present on both sides — whereas the sorites question is the prior metaphysical one of whether a subject exists at all. This article stays on the one question the others leave open: can it be indeterminate whether a subject exists at all?

The Map has a stake here, and it is worth stating the stake as motivation rather than as an established result. Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds; indexical identity matters) presupposes a determinate fact about *whether there is an experiencer* and *whose* experience an experience is. If ontic vagueness of consciousness were true, some borderline systems would have no fact about whether any "I" is present — and the indexical anchor Tenet 4 relies on would have nothing to fasten to. So the Map is motivated to resist ontic vagueness. That motivation does not settle the metaphysics; the case for vagueness is live and deserves a fair hearing before the Map answers it.

## The Sorites Setup and Why It Bites

The classical sorites turns on tolerance: removing one grain never turns a heap into a non-heap, yet iterated removals must. Transposed to consciousness, the tolerance premise says that no single minimal physical difference — one neuron, one evolutionary generation, one developmental hour — can flip a system from conscious to non-conscious. Grant tolerance and continuity across the series, and a sharp cutoff looks impossible; deny that anything is conscious or that everything is, and the middle of the series seems to have no determinate status left to occupy. The philosophical pressure is that each escape route carries a visible cost, so the problem is which cost to pay rather than whether one is due.

## Three Loci of Vagueness

The taxonomy front-loaded above is the load-bearing distinction, so it is worth making each option explicit.

**Ontic vagueness** holds that the world itself is unsettled at the boundary: there are systems for which it is objectively neither true nor false that experience is present. This is the strong thesis — indeterminacy in the phenomenal facts, not in our grip on them.

**Semantic (conceptual) vagueness** holds that the underlying reality is determinate but our phenomenal concepts fail to pick out a unique property, so borderline verdicts reflect linguistic indeterminacy rather than a gap in the world.

**Epistemicism** holds that there is a precise fact — a last conscious member of the series — that we simply cannot know. On this view the boundary is sharp but its location is hidden. [[epistemicism-about-consciousness]] develops this locus in full — Williamson's margin-for-error and safety machinery, and why it is the Map's tenet-preserving reading of the sorites, at the cost of a permanently unknowable fact.

Which locus one accepts decides everything downstream, including whether the Map's Tenet-4 concern is even engaged: a sharp-but-unknowable fact (epistemicism) preserves the determinate subject that Tenet 4 needs, whereas ontic vagueness removes it.

## The Case for Ontic Vagueness

Eric Schwitzgebel presents the sharpest argument for the strong thesis. In "Borderline Consciousness" (2023) he frames a **quadrilemma**: either (a) nothing is conscious, or (b) everything is conscious, or (c) there is a sharp boundary somewhere across the apparent continuum, or (d) consciousness is a vague property admitting indeterminate cases. Assuming mainstream naturalism about consciousness, he argues that (a)–(c) should each be rejected — (c) because any sharp cutoff across a smooth physical continuum looks metaphysically arbitrary — which forces (d): some entities have no determinate fact as to whether experience is present. A companion working paper extends this to subject-*counting*, arguing that the number of conscious subjects in a system may itself be metaphysically indeterminate rather than a determinate whole number. (That paper's final venue and year were not confirmed at the publisher of record and it should be treated as a working paper.)

Geoffrey Hall, in "Is Consciousness Vague?" (*Australasian Journal of Philosophy*, 2023), defends the more modest claim that it is *possible* for something to be such that it is borderline whether it is phenomenally conscious. Hall argues that the leading arguments *for* precision rely on principles inconsistent with otherwise-plausible standard principles governing borderline cases.

Michael Tye's position is easy to misfile and worth stating carefully. In *Vagueness and the Evolution of Consciousness* (Oxford, 2021) Tye argues that consciousness **cannot** be sharp and so **is** vague. But because he also finds a genuinely borderline conscious/non-conscious case incoherent from the first-person side, he resolves the tension by embracing panpsychism — fundamental entities carry a minimal "consciousness*" without particular phenomenal character — so the conscious/non-conscious cut never has to be made. Tye therefore affirms vagueness rather than denying it; his earlier representationalist work does not carry this thesis.

## The Case Against Borderline Cases

The opposing camp denies that "phenomenally conscious" has borderline cases at all. Jonathan Simon, in "Vagueness and Zombies" (*Philosophical Studies*, 2017), defends a *Positive Characterization Thesis*: every borderline case of any predicate admits a positive characterization that shows a competent speaker what makes it borderline. He then argues that the conceivability of zombies entails there can be no positive characterization of a borderline case of "phenomenally conscious" — so the predicate has no borderline cases, and for any creature at any time it cannot be vague whether it is phenomenally conscious.

Michael Antony's "Vagueness and the Metaphysics of Consciousness" (*Philosophical Studies*, 2006) supplies a sharper conditional. Antony defends: *if* the concept CONSCIOUS STATE is sharp rather than vague, and correct in respect of its sharpness, *then* common versions of the identity theory, functionalism, and dualism that identify or realize or correlate consciousness with *complex* physical or functional properties are false. He gives reasons to take seriously that the concept is in fact sharp, undercutting the sorites premise at the conceptual level. A separate reply, "Papineau on the Vagueness of Phenomenal Concepts" (*Dialectica*, 2006), directly rebuts David Papineau's view.

That view is the semantic option in person. Papineau (*Thinking about Consciousness*, 2002) argues that phenomenal *concepts* are referentially indeterminate — many consciousness questions are, in his phrase, irredeemably vague — while, as a physicalist, he keeps the underlying physical facts determinate. The upshot for borderline creatures is still no determinate answer, but the indeterminacy is conceptual, not a special ontic ingredient. So Antony and Papineau sit on opposite sides of the *conceptual-sharpness* question, and Papineau does not belong in a simple "sharp lights on/off" camp.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map reads this debate primarily through **Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds; indexical identity matters)** and secondarily through **Tenet 1 (Dualism)**.

Tenet 4 is where the collision is sharpest. Indexical identity presupposes a determinate fact about whether there is a subject and whose experience an experience is. Schwitzgebel's extension — that the number of conscious subjects need not be a determinate whole number — is the most direct threat: if it is indeterminate whether any "I" is present, the indexical "this experience is mine, now" has no determinate truth-maker. The Map therefore has a real interest in the sharpness camp (Simon, Antony) and in the epistemicist fallback, which preserves a determinate subject-fact even where its location is epistemically inaccessible. The Map does not claim to have refuted ontic vagueness; it claims that ontic vagueness would cost it Tenet 4, which is reason to look hard for where the vagueness argument can be resisted. A fourth stance stands outside the ontic/semantic/epistemic trichotomy altogether: the Madhyamaka no-self reply holds that the determinate subject all three loci presuppose is itself a reification, so "is there a fact whether a subject exists?" is malformed rather than answerable — a dissolution the Map's Tenet-4 framing sets aside precisely because it presupposes the determinate experiencer that tradition denies.

The resistance runs through Tenet 1. Schwitzgebel's quadrilemma explicitly *assumes naturalism* — the premise that consciousness is realized by graded physical properties, so that a graded realizer would force a graded (hence borderline-admitting) consciousness. The Map's interactionist dualism denies exactly that step: if consciousness is a non-physical addition coupled to the brain rather than a complex physical realizer, a graded anatomical or evolutionary series need not supply a graded truth-maker for the on/off fact. Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits) reinforces this: the pull toward ontic vagueness leans on the intuition that any sharp line "in nature" must be an arbitrary, unparsimonious cutoff — an intuition the Map is entitled to resist once the sharp fact is fixed by a non-physical coupling rather than by a physical realizer.

Antony's conditional constrains how the Map may buy its sharpness. Because sharpness-plus-correctness sinks *complex-property* theories — complex-property dualism included — the Map cannot ground the on/off fact in any graded property. The natural constructive move is to locate it in the *simple, non-graded engagement of the non-physical mind–brain coupling itself* — whether that coupling engages at all — so that the subject-existence fact is fixed by a relation that is not a sorites series. This is a distinct and prior boundary from the [[interface-threshold]], which marks where an *already-engaged* coupling becomes selection-effective: that threshold has phenomenal consciousness present on both sides and settles what a subject's experience can *do*, not whether a subject exists, so it is not the construct that secures the on/off fact. The same discipline applies to the borrowing that runs the other way. [[composition-and-consciousness]] leans on consciousness's sharpness to answer mereological vagueness while citing *graded* proxies — integrated information (Φ), organic "life" — as where that sharpness lives; but by Antony's conditional those proxies cannot themselves carry the on/off fact, so the sharpness that argument borrows must trace back to the non-graded coupling rather than to the graded criteria. This keeps the Map's answer honest: it does not deny the continuity the vagueness camp points to; it denies that continuity in the physical substrate transmits to the non-physical fact about whether a subject is present. That denial is the step the whole Tenet-4 defence rests on, and it remains open — a graded physical trigger for the coupling could reintroduce the sorites at its switch-on point — while the [[vagueness-void]] marks where the limits of decidability about consciousness may themselves be unchartable.
