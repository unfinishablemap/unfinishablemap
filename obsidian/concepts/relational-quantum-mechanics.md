Relational quantum mechanics (RQM) is Carlo Rovelli's interpretation of quantum mechanics in which physical properties exist only as relations between systems, not as intrinsic features of things. There is no "view from nowhere"—all quantum facts are relative to specific observers, where "observer" means any physical system, not necessarily a conscious one. RQM explicitly removes any special role for consciousness in quantum measurement.

This interpretation directly conflicts with The Unfinishable Map's [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] tenet. Understanding why RQM works—and what it sacrifices to work—clarifies what's at stake in adopting a consciousness-based alternative.

## The Core Idea

Rovelli's insight begins with an analogy to special relativity. Just as Einstein showed velocity is always relative to a reference frame, RQM holds there is no absolute quantum state, only states relative to particular observers.

When system S interacts with system O, the interaction produces definite values—but only relative to O. Another system O′ that hasn't interacted with S may still describe S as in superposition. Both descriptions are correct; apparent contradiction dissolves because neither claims absolute truth.

Wigner's friend illustrates this. A physicist inside a lab measures a spin and sees "up"; outside, Wigner hasn't yet interacted with the lab. The spin has a definite value relative to the physicist but remains in superposition relative to Wigner. When Wigner opens the lab, he finds results consistent with the physicist's measurement. No paradox arises because "the spin is up" was never absolute—it was always "up relative to the physicist."

## How RQM Dissolves the Measurement Problem

The [[measurement-problem|measurement problem]] asks: what causes quantum superpositions to collapse into definite outcomes? Standard quantum mechanics leaves "measurement" undefined, creating a conceptual gap.

RQM dissolves the problem by denying its premise. Collapse isn't a special process requiring explanation—it's what interaction looks like from the interacting system's perspective. Every physical interaction is a "measurement" that produces definite values for the interacting systems; no special observer status and no quantum/classical boundary are needed. The wave function is a calculational tool, not a real entity that collapses. The ontology is a web of discrete relative events—systems interacting and properties actualizing relative to the interacting partner—rather than an absolute state evolving in time.

## Challenges to RQM

RQM faces several objections of unequal weight. Two—the intersubjectivity problem and the solipsism dilemma it generates—are the centre of gravity in the 2020s literature; the others are older.

### The intersubjectivity problem and cross-perspective links

If facts are relative to observers, how does science work? The standard formulation of RQM appears to leave each observer trapped in their own perspective—the "island universes" problem. If your measurement of an electron's spin is a fact relative to you, and my later measurement of your apparatus is a separate fact relative to me, nothing in the original RQM postulates guarantees the two cohere.

Adlam and Rovelli (2023) address this with a new postulate, Cross-Perspective Links (CPL), proposed as a replacement for RQM's original fourth postulate ("Relativity of comparisons"). CPL states that when observer Alice measures variable V on system S, any subsequent observer Bob who measures Alice's physical record of V obtains a matching result—provided the record has not been destroyed through measurements in a non-commuting basis (the information-destruction condition is specified via the Heisenberg uncertainty relation). The aim is to recover a "shared observable reality composed of a large number of variables whose values all the observers agree on" while leaving observer-relativity intact at the level of single quantum events.

CPL is the most serious technical move RQM has made in twenty years, and the interpretation now turns on it. If CPL succeeds on its own terms, RQM has answered its sharpest contemporary objection; if it fails, RQM faces a deeper structural problem than the original formulation acknowledged.

### The solipsism dilemma

Peter Lewis (2024) argues CPL generates a dilemma RQM cannot escape. Without CPL, RQM collapses toward epistemic solipsism: an observer cannot in principle know anything beyond their own immediate present experience, since every claim about another observer or the past is just another relative fact with no postulate connecting facts across perspectives. With CPL, the solipsism dissolves—but the cure removes what was distinctively relational. CPL effectively reinstates frame-independent facts about observers' records: Alice's reading of the spin becomes something Bob can measure and find. The relational ontology has been quietly augmented with absolute facts about measurement outcomes, softening the "no view from nowhere" selling point until RQM and a standard observer-independent reading look structurally close.

Muciño, Okon and Sudarsky (2022) press a related objection: on standards RQM should accept, Rovelli's claim to a satisfactory realist non-solipsistic description is untenable—either the relational ontology collapses toward solipsism, or what is added to prevent it is ad hoc. Rovelli's reply (2021) contends the assessment presupposes the framework RQM rejects. The exchange is now a stable impasse located at the foundations, not at empirical predictions.

The Map's reading: CPL is RQM's most credible attempt to recover frame-independent facts, but it does work the original postulates did not earn—importing frame-independent agreement as a postulate where the original framework treated such agreement as derivative. Whether this is a successful repair or a quiet abandonment of what made the interpretation distinctive is exactly what Lewis's dilemma and the Muciño-Okon-Sudarsky exchange leave open.

### The locality question

RQM claims to preserve locality despite Bell violations, treating EPR correlations as "common cause in indeterministic context" rather than faster-than-light influence. Critics like Pienaar argue this empties locality of content: if it is compatible with perfect correlations between spacelike-separated measurements, the term no longer constrains anything.

### What counts as an observer

RQM says any physical system can be an observer—but this leaves unclear what determines the boundaries of a "system." When does a collection of particles become an observer with its own perspective? RQM provides no principled way to individuate observers; Laudisa (2019) catalogues this among RQM's standing open problems, alongside the "third person" question of what relativising states to observers actually achieves. The Map's distinct objection to this universalisation appears in the next section.

## What RQM Removes and the Map's Specific Disagreement

Rovelli is explicit: "There is nothing subjective, idealistic, or mentalistic, in RQM." This is the point—RQM succeeds by eliminating the special status of consciousness that other interpretations invoke.

The consequence: RQM provides no mechanism for [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|mental causation]]. If any physical interaction is a "measurement," a rock measuring a photon is equivalent to a physicist measuring one. Consciousness adds nothing, and the [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] tenet has no place in RQM's framework.

The disagreement is precise. The Map does not object to observer-relativity as such—the [[post-decoherence-selection-programme]] makes the actualised outcome relative to the conscious system whose neural states are differentiated, and the [[brain-interface-boundary]] picture localises each subject's collapses to their own brain. What the Map rejects is RQM's universalising "observer" to mean *any* physical system. That move buys RQM broad applicability—any interaction is a measurement, the early universe collapses itself, the [[multi-mind-collapse-problem]] and [[prebiotic-collapse|prebiotic collapse problem]] dissolve automatically—but it detaches the relativity from the only kind of observer the Map's tenets identify as load-bearing.

So both RQM and the Map treat measurement outcomes as relative to *something*; they differ on what. RQM says: any physical system. The Map says: the conscious system at whose interface the actualisation occurs. This is honest framework-boundary disagreement—neither refutes the other from inside; each makes a foundational commitment the other declines. What can be argued at the boundary is whether each side has earned its move by its own standards—the burden the CPL discussion above assesses for RQM, and which [[brain-interface-boundary]] and the [[post-decoherence-selection-programme]] attempt to discharge for the Map.

## The Illusionist Reading and the Phenomenology of Observation

[[illusionism|Illusionists]] such as Dennett and Frankish are RQM's most natural allies. If phenomenal consciousness is an introspective illusion, then RQM's dismissal of consciousness's special role costs nothing—there was never anything special about consciousness to dismiss. On this reading, RQM is the interpretation the hard problem would have us choose if the hard problem itself were dissolved.

The Map's standing objection to illusionism applies. To say consciousness plays no role in collapse, the illusionist must explain why we *believe* it does. Against Dennett's heterophenomenology and Frankish's "quasi-phenomenal" properties, Raymond Tallis's objection—"Misrepresentation presupposes presentation"—is the Map's reply: for consciousness to *seem* special, something must be doing the seeming, and that something resists purely dispositional analysis. Frankish contests the move; the dispute is live, and the Map's commitment to dualism is what tips the assessment, not a stand-alone refutation.

Independently, RQM treats observation as a purely physical relation. The phenomenology of observation—accessible through [[introspection]] and developed in the [[witness-consciousness|contemplative literature]] on subject-object structure—suggests observation involves something the physical-relation account cannot capture: the qualitative character of being an observer, not merely functioning as one. The weight one gives this first-person evidence depends on prior commitments about first-person methods.

## Why Understanding RQM Matters

RQM shows there is a coherent interpretation where consciousness plays no role; the Map's choice is therefore a philosophical commitment, not a deduction from physics. RQM also illuminates a real problem for consciousness-based readings—if Alice's and Bob's consciousnesses both participate in collapse, how are their selections reconciled? The Map's [[brain-interface-boundary|brain-interface-boundary]] picture answers by locality: each consciousness modulates only its own neural substrate. RQM's insight that intermediate states need not be coordinated until observers interact carries over—before Alice and Bob communicate, their brain states need not be compared, and agreement is verified only through physical interaction.

## Process Philosophy Perspective

Whitehead's process philosophy illuminates what RQM sacrifices. For Whitehead, reality consists of "actual occasions"—momentary events that arise, achieve definiteness through "concrescence," and perish, with both physical and experiential aspects. Both Whitehead and RQM deny a view from nowhere, but the denials differ in structure: RQM relativises facts to observers, while for Whitehead definiteness is *achieved* through an internal process of becoming. On RQM there is no moment of genuine becoming where relativity yields to definiteness—which Whitehead would say redescribes the measurement problem rather than solving it, since something must still account for why each occasion achieves the definiteness it does.

The Map does not endorse Whitehead's panexperientialism—experience running all the way down raises the [[combination-problem|combination problem]]. But the insistence that definiteness is *achieved* rather than merely *relative* aligns with the Map's view that collapse involves something beyond physical interaction.

## What Would Challenge This View?

The Map's position on RQM would need revision if:

1. **RQM solves the hard problem.** If an account emerged of why relative facts between physical systems are accompanied by phenomenal experience—if CPL could explain not just physical agreement but qualitative experience—RQM would become more attractive. Currently it ignores the hard problem rather than solving it.

2. **[[decoherence]] proves sufficient.** If decoherence explained not just the suppression of interference but the emergence of definite outcomes, the role for consciousness at collapse would diminish. But [[decoherence|decoherence doesn't solve the measurement problem]]—it selects preferred bases without explaining why we see specific outcomes.

3. **Contemplative reports prove unreliable.** The phenomenology of observation supports consciousness-causes-collapse over RQM. If this phenomenology were shown to be systematically misleading (as illusionists claim), RQM's dismissal of observer specialness would lose its cost.

4. **[[haecceity|Indexical identity]] proves illusory.** RQM handles multiple observers by relativizing facts to each—which works if there's no deep fact about *which* observer you are. If the haecceitistic fact of being *this* subject proves reducible to physical relations, RQM's treatment of observers as mere physical systems becomes more defensible.

5. **The cross-perspective-links proposal is shown to be internally earned.** If a future technical development could show CPL recovering frame-independent agreement without quietly importing frame-independent facts—answering Lewis's dilemma and the Muciño-Okon-Sudarsky objection on RQM's own resources—the case for RQM strengthens considerably. The Map's current reading is that this has not been shown.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]:** RQM is compatible with physicalism and finds no support for dualism, though its emphasis on observer-relativity shares something with the Map's rejection of "view from nowhere" physicalism.

The deeper issue: RQM's relativization applies to *physical* properties. The phenomenal character of being an observer—what it's like to see the measurement result—is neither relative nor absolute on RQM because RQM doesn't address it at all. By treating observers as purely physical systems, RQM assumes away the dualist's central datum. Its silence on phenomenology is exclusion, not neutrality.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]:** RQM eliminates the need for any special interaction—consciousness plays no role in collapse. This directly conflicts with the tenet's proposal that consciousness biases quantum outcomes.

Note, however, that RQM faces its own version of the "how does collapse happen?" question: saying collapse is relative to observers doesn't explain *why* each relative fact takes the value it does. Neither framework provides a complete dynamical mechanism—the Map's proposal lacks specified coupling constants, just as RQM treats relative facts as brute. The difference is not mechanistic completeness but which variable each framework identifies as relevant: the Map holds that an experiential variable (consciousness) belongs in the account; RQM excludes it by construction.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]:** **Direct conflict.** RQM explicitly removes consciousness from physics; if it is correct, consciousness cannot influence physical outcomes through quantum mechanisms, and the Map's central claim fails.

The [[epiphenomenalism|epiphenomenalism concern]] cuts both ways, however. RQM makes consciousness epiphenomenal by default—experience accompanies physical interactions (if it exists at all) but plays no causal role—raising the question of why we talk about consciousness if it does nothing. The bidirectional interaction tenet addresses this by giving consciousness genuine causal work; RQM leaves the gap unexplained.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]:** RQM also rejects many-worlds, treating collapse (relative to each observer) as real. Here the Map and RQM agree: we don't branch into multiple copies.

But the agreement is superficial. RQM's rejection of MWI stems from denying that the wave function represents reality—it's a calculational tool. The Map's rejection stems from the [[haecceity|indexical identity problem]]: the meaningfulness of asking "which branch am I in?" presupposes something RQM cannot accommodate—that being *this* observer is a genuine fact, not merely a relative one. RQM and the Map reject MWI for different reasons, and those reasons reveal incompatible commitments about the nature of observers.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]:** RQM appears simpler—no need for consciousness in physics—but simplicity isn't truth. It solves the measurement problem while ignoring the hard problem, never explaining why physical processes are accompanied by experience. This parsimony is achieved by restriction of scope, not genuine explanation: refusing to address the hard problem is not showing it doesn't exist. If consciousness exists and is irreducible (as the dualism tenet holds), RQM's simplicity is illusory—it covers only part of reality.

## Summary

Relational quantum mechanics offers a coherent interpretation where quantum facts are relative to observers, and "observer" means any physical system. It dissolves the measurement problem and avoids multi-observer paradoxes—but only by eliminating consciousness's special role and universalising "observer" in a way that severs the relativity from what the Map's framework requires. Its most credible recent move, Adlam and Rovelli's cross-perspective-links postulate, attempts to recover frame-independent agreement without abandoning observer-relativity at the level of individual events; whether it earns this by RQM's own resources, or quietly imports the frame-independent facts the framework was supposed to do without, is what Lewis's dilemma and the Muciño-Okon-Sudarsky exchange leave contested.

The Map's disagreement is at the framework boundary, honestly registered rather than presented as in-framework refutation. Engaging with RQM clarifies what the Map adds beyond bare quantum mechanics: a commitment to consciousness as real, irreducible, and causally effective—not merely another physical system among many.
