Egocentric presentism is Caspar Hare's metaphysical thesis that, of all conscious beings, exactly one — oneself — has experiences that possess the irreducible, monadic, non-relational property of *presence*. Other people are genuinely conscious, but their experiences are simply *not present*. From the inside, my experiences are present *simpliciter*, not present-relative-to-me. The view's structural core is an analogy with the A-theory of time: just as temporal presentism privileges *now* over past and future, egocentric presentism privileges *me* over other selves. The privileged "now" of time has a cousin — a privileged "me."

The Unfinishable Map gives egocentric presentism a dedicated entry for one reason above all others: it is the leading *one-world* response to the [[vertiginous-question|vertiginous question]] ("why, of all conscious beings, am *I* this one?"). Christian List has shown that the family of theories surrounding this question forms a [[#the-quadrilemma|quadrilemma]] (explained below): four individually plausible claims of which at most three can hold. Egocentric presentism keeps **one world** while taking the first-person perspective seriously — exactly the horn List's own preferred resolution, a *many-worlds theory of consciousness*, abandons. That places egocentric presentism in direct structural alignment with the Map's [[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]] tenet, and makes List's many-worlds resolution the foil the Map rejects.

## The Thesis Stated

Hare introduced the view in "Self-Bias, Time-Bias, and the Metaphysics of Self and Time" (*Journal of Philosophy*, 2007) and developed it fully in the monograph *On Myself, and Other, Less Important Subjects* (Princeton University Press, 2009).

The 2007 paper begins not with metaphysics but with ethics. We are all, Hare observes, "mild egocentric hedonists": all else equal, we prefer that pleasure befall ourselves and pain befall others. Can this self-bias be rationally grounded? Hare names the difficulty the **Grounding Problem**: to think my comfort makes an especially weighty contribution to the value of a state of affairs, my picture of who I am must make me metaphysically unique in some way that bears on the intrinsic value of my suffering — yet, as he puts it, "I am mundane in all measurable respects." His tentative conclusion is that "perhaps my only hope is to believe that I am extraordinary in some immeasurable respect, that I am *metaphysically unique* in some way that bears upon the intrinsic value of my suffering."

The metaphysical payoff arrives as a parity claim. The same move that makes the present *time* metaphysically privileged (the A-theory) can make the present *self* metaphysically privileged. Self-bias and time-bias are treated as two faces of one metaphysics. *Presence* is the property that does the work: in temporal presentism only the present moment is real or present; in egocentric presentism only my experiences are present, indexed to the self rather than to time.

Two features of the property matter. First, it is **monadic** — a one-place property, "present" full stop, not the two-place relation "present *to* x." Hare insists my experiences are present *simpliciter*, not present-relative-to-me, because a relational reading would make presence symmetric across subjects and dissolve the privilege. Second, it is **immeasurable** — no instrument, and no third-person description however complete, detects which subject is the present one. The privileged subject is *me*, and (Hare maintains) I am the only one whose point of view corresponds with the facts.

## Perspectival Realism (Hare's Weaker Sibling) {#perspectival-realism}

Hare pairs egocentric presentism with a closely related, slightly weaker fallback he calls **perspectival realism**. Other perspectives genuinely exist, but *my* perspective has a defining intrinsic, non-relational property: the world is present, the objects around me are *here*, *simpliciter* rather than relative to a point of view. Hare frames it as a "weak version of metaphysical solipsism." Where egocentric presentism extends the privilege through time (events simply happening now, for me), perspectival realism concerns the spatial and perceptual perspective (the scene centred, intrinsically, on me). The weaker view is the more defensible fallback: it claims less and so survives more objections.

**Disambiguation — a critical one.** Hare's "perspectival realism" is unrelated to the philosophy-of-science doctrine of the *same name* associated with Ronald Giere and Michela Massimi. That doctrine concerns scientific representation — the claim that models, measurements, and theoretical claims are perspectival, made from a particular vantage of instruments and concepts, while still latching onto a mind-independent world. It is a thesis about *scientific knowledge*, not about the metaphysics of the self. The two share only a label. Throughout this article, "perspectival realism" means Hare's sense exclusively.

## The Quadrilemma {#the-quadrilemma}

Christian List has given the cleanest map of the logical space egocentric presentism inhabits. In "A Quadrilemma for Theories of Consciousness" (*The Philosophical Quarterly*, 2024/25), he isolates four individually plausible claims that cannot all be true; at most three can hold:

1. **First-person realism** — there are irreducible first-personal facts: a fact about which subject is *the* present or lived one, not capturable by any third-person description.
2. **Non-solipsism** — more than one conscious subject is real.
3. **Non-fragmentation** — the facts true in any single world are jointly compossible; no contradictory "centred" facts coexist in one world.
4. **One world** — reality is a single world, not many.

Any three are mutually consistent; accepting all four generates contradiction. The quadrilemma is therefore a forced choice: every theory of consciousness that engages the first-person fact must sacrifice one claim.

Egocentric presentism, with its sibling perspectival realism, keeps first-person realism, non-fragmentation, and one world — by **dropping non-solipsism**. Only my experiences are present; others are conscious but not present. This is the "weak solipsism" Hare avows: not the denial that other minds exist, but the denial that their experiences share the privileged property mine have. The cost is paid in the one place the Map can most easily afford it relative to its rivals, because the alternative horn is the one the Map's tenets forbid.

List's own preferred resolution drops **one world** instead. In a separate paper, "The Many-Worlds Theory of Consciousness" (*Noûs*, 2023), he associates each conscious subject with its own first-personally centred world — a distinct first-personal realiser of a shared third-personal world — and treats all these centred worlds as equally real, in a modal-realist register, with exactly one *present* for each subject. The first-person privilege the indexical intuition demands is built into the ontology: my centred world is the one that is present, by construction. The puzzle the [[vertiginous-question|vertiginous question]] poses is answered by multiplying worlds rather than by privileging a self within one world.

These are the two principled rivals. Hare keeps one world and privileges the self; List keeps non-solipsism's full strength and multiplies worlds. The structural cousin of this contrast — *this* outcome from many possibilities, *this* viewpoint from many subjects — is developed further at [[indexical-identity-quantum-measurement|indexical identity and quantum measurement]].

## The Communicability Objection

The standard objection is that the privileged property cannot be coherently stated. The worry: a non-relational "present, but present *to* no one in particular" seems either unintelligible or covertly relational. When I try to say what is special about my experiences, I say they are present *to me* — and that "to me" reintroduces a relation, restoring symmetry across subjects and dissolving the very privilege the view asserts. If presence is genuinely monadic, it appears unstatable; if it is statable, it appears relational and so universal. Reviewers press related pressure from different angles: Kris McDaniel, in a critical study of the book (2012), argues the evidence supports presence being a two-place relation rather than a monadic property, and Ned Markosian, reviewing the book in "Are You Special?", holds both that the inside/outside intuitions can be reconciled in other ways and that the view supplies no account of why presence should attach to one subject rather than another.

Hare's reply is that monadic presence is a genuine property whose intelligibility does not require relational expressibility — the difficulty of saying it is a feature of any deeply first-personal fact, not evidence against its reality. The debate is live. Giovanni Merlo's "subjectivism" (2016) develops a near-neighbour position that differs precisely on the status of the privileged fact, and Vincent Conitzer's "personalized A-theory of time and perspective" (2020) independently reconstructs Hare's self–time parity from a decision-theoretic starting point — which together show egocentric presentism is one member of a small family of "metaphysically privileged first-person" theories rather than an isolated curiosity.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Egocentric presentism is, for the Map, an exploratory ontological *fit* — a metaphysical rival that maps the same terrain the tenets occupy — rather than positive evidence for the Map's framework. Its structural alignment with the tenets is real and load-bearing; its status as confirmation is not. The distinction is the [[project/evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]] applied: a congenial metaphysics that keeps one world and takes the self seriously removes a *defeater* (it shows the indexical privilege need not force many worlds), but it does not *upgrade* the evidence for dualism. The Map adopts the structure as a worked-out option, not as a result.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]** — the sharpest connection. Tenet 4 rejects the many-worlds interpretation primarily on the indexical objection: many-worlds dissolves "why am *I* this branch?" by making every branch equally real, so the question has no answer. List's many-worlds theory of consciousness is the harder case the Map must face directly, because it *answers* the vertiginous question — by giving each subject a present centred world — rather than dissolving it. Egocentric presentism is the rival that honours the same first-person realism List honours while keeping the one world Tenet 4 insists on. The Map shares List's diagnosis (the bare one-world picture struggles to say why *this* subject is mine) but rejects his remedy: his modal realism makes every other subject's centred world a *real* world, abandoning the global uniqueness Tenet 4 requires. Against List specifically, the Map's case rests on the rejection of modal realism, with egocentric presentism standing as the constructive one-world alternative. The Map's own settled position is more cautious still: it treats the privileged "I" as a genuine [[vertiginous-question|void]] — a primitive within one world that [[haecceity|haecceity]] marks but does not cross — rather than fully endorsing Hare's metaphysic. Egocentric presentism shows the one-world horn is *occupiable*; the Map occupies it without claiming the communicability objection is answered.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** — *presence* is an immeasurable, non-physical feature of experience: no instrument detects it, and no third-person description settles which subject bears it. That places it congenially alongside the Map's dualism, which holds that consciousness has features physical description cannot capture. The fit requires care: Hare himself pairs egocentric presentism with a lean, broadly physicalist ontology, so the Map *repurposes* the structure under a dualist reading rather than enlisting Hare as an ally. He would not accept the Map's metaphysics, and the view is engaged as a rival whose structure the Map borrows, not as corroboration of dualism.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** — egocentric presentism posits a primitive, theoretically tidy-looking property (monadic presence) whose explanatory adequacy is contested. Anti-haecceitists will call it an unparsimonious addition to a world that qualitative facts already describe completely. By Tenet 5 the Map declines to let parsimony decide: the apparent simplicity of the deflationary view may reflect the inadequacy of our concepts for the first-person fact, not insight into it. The same discipline that blocks parsimony against dualism blocks it against a privileged self.
