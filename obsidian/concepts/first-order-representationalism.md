First-order representationalism is the reductive-physicalist thesis that the phenomenal character of an experience—its "what it is like"—just *is*, or is wholly exhausted by, that experience's **first-order representational content**: what the experience represents the world or the body as being like. On this view there is no phenomenal residue left over once the represented properties are fixed—no intrinsic, non-representational "feel" beyond the tracking relation itself. Its paradigm defenders are Michael Tye and Fred Dretske, and it is the Map's central reductive rival: the leading attempt to naturalize phenomenal character as a representational relation and thereby dissolve the [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]]. This article presents the view on its own terms, gives it its strongest case, and then locates where the Map resists it. The Map engages and resists first-order representationalism; it does not claim to refute it.

The theory should be distinguished from the general notion of [[intentionality|intentionality]] (the "aboutness" of any mental state), which is broader and older. First-order representationalism is the specific claim that consciousness *is* intentionality of a certain kind—that phenomenal character reduces to first-order world-directed content. It should also be distinguished from **higher-order** representationalism (see [[higher-order-theories]] below), on which a state becomes conscious by being represented by a *further* mental state.

## The Core Thesis

Representationalism about consciousness comes in strengths. **Strong (reductive) representationalism**—the version treated here—holds that phenomenal character is *identical to* or *supervenes on and is grounded in* representational content, with no further phenomenal fact. **First-order** representationalism adds that the relevant content is the experience's own outward-directed content (a visual experience represents the tree as green and there), not any second-order content about the experience itself.

The motivation is naturalistic economy. If phenomenal character reduces to a representational relation, and representation can in turn be naturalized (via tracking, information, or biological function), then the hard problem has no remainder to explain. There is no separate "why does it feel like anything" once feel is identified with content.

## Tye's PANIC Theory

Michael Tye's *Ten Problems of Consciousness* (1995) offers the flagship statement. Tye identifies phenomenal character with **PANIC**: **P**oised, **A**bstract, **N**onconceptual **I**ntentional **C**ontent.

- **Poised**: the content stands ready to make a direct impact on the belief/desire system—it is available to cognition without needing to be conceptualized first.
- **Abstract**: no particular object need exist for the content to be as it is (hallucination has phenomenal character without a tracked object).
- **Nonconceptual**: the subject need not possess the concepts required to specify the content.
- **Intentional Content**: it is representational—it says the world is thus-and-so.

For Tye this is strong representationalism: there is no intrinsic quale over and above such content. Qualia, understood as intrinsic non-representational features of experience, are denied. (Tye later moved away from the PANIC formulation in subsequent work; the 1995 statement remains the canonical first-order text and is what the Map engages here.) *Consciousness, Color, and Content* (2000) defends the view against the objections that accumulated after *Ten Problems* and extends it to color experience.

## Dretske's Representational Naturalism

Fred Dretske's *Naturalizing the Mind* (1995, the Jean Nicod Lectures) supplies the other pillar. Dretske's Representational Thesis has two parts: (1) all mental facts are representational facts, and (2) all representational facts are facts about informational functions. The qualitative aspects of a perceptual experience are identical to the external, real-world properties the experience *represents* objects as having—paired with a teleological account of what gives a state its representational function (roughly, what it was recruited by learning or evolution to indicate).

The advertised payoff is a "completely naturalistic" account of phenomenal consciousness. Where the [[intentionality|naturalization of intentionality in general]] has a long record of difficulty, Dretske's wager is that experience is the *easy* case, because perceptual content is anchored to worldly properties an organism is built to track.

## The Transparency Argument

The primary positive case for first-order representationalism is the **transparency** (or diaphanousness) of experience, associated with Gilbert Harman ("The Intrinsic Quality of Experience," 1990) and endorsed by Tye. The observation: when you introspect a visual experience of a tree and try to attend to features *of the experience itself*, the only features you find are features of the presented tree—its green, its shape, its location. You look *through* the experience to its object; you never seem to encounter intrinsic non-representational properties of the experience. As Harman's example runs, in attending to the experience "the only features there to turn [your] attention to will be features of the presented tree."

The representationalist's inference: the best explanation of transparency is that phenomenal character consists *entirely* in represented worldly properties. If experience had intrinsic non-representational features, introspection should be able to find them—and it cannot.

## First-Order versus Higher-Order and Non-Reductive Variants

Three distinctions keep "representationalism" from collapsing into a single position:

- **First-order (Tye, Dretske, Harman; often Dennett)**: phenomenal character is the first-order content of the experience. No higher-order state is needed for feel; the feel is already present in the world-directed content.
- **Higher-order ([[higher-order-theories|Rosenthal's HOT, Lycan's inner-sense theory]])**: a state is *conscious* in virtue of being represented by a suitable further mental state. William Lycan is a useful hybrid—representationalist about qualia at the first order, but higher-order about a state's *being conscious*—which is why he is not filed as a pure first-order theorist.
- **Non-reductive (Tim Crane; David Chalmers's Fregean representationalism)**: phenomenal character covaries with representational content, but the content-property is not thereby *physically reduced*. Chalmers pairs representational structure with property dualism or panprotopsychism.

The Map opposes the *reductive* first-order version specifically. It does not oppose the weaker claim that experience has representational structure—which a dualist can grant—only the identification of feel with naturalized tracking content.

## The Standard Objections

The literature's main battleground is a cluster of cases alleging that phenomenal character and representational content can come apart.

**Inverted spectrum / [[inverted-qualia|Inverted Earth]]**: Ned Block's "Inverted Earth" (1990) imagines a planet where every color is the complement of Earth's *and* the color vocabulary is inversely swapped, so speech stays "correct." A person fitted with color-inverting lenses and transported there eventually comes to *represent* (on a wide, externalist semantics) grass as red and sky as yellow—while, Block argues, their phenomenal character stays put. Different represented content, same phenomenal character: so phenomenal character is not wide representational content.

**Mental paint**: Block's positive counter-thesis (developed in "Mental Paint and Mental Latex," 1996, and later work) is that experience has introspectable, intrinsic, non-representational features—"mental paint"—that persist across cases of identical represented content. Afterimages, phosphenes, and blurry vision are offered as cases where phenomenal features seem to outrun any matching difference in represented worldly properties.

Representationalists reply by going "wide" on content, by denying the intuition, or by arguing the cases are incoherent. The dispute is unresolved and remains a defining fault line. One point matters for the Map's use of it: Block is himself a *physicalist*. His objections press strong representationalism from *inside* physicalism, so they function as dialectical pressure rather than as dualist allies.

## Relation to Site Perspective

First-order representationalism is a direct denial of the Map's [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]] tenet: it holds that phenomenal character is nothing over and above physical/functional tracking content. The Map treats it as the strongest reductive rival on offer and resists it on two fronts.

**The relocation objection (the Map's core move).** Identifying feel with content does not close the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]]; it relocates it. Replace "why does this neural state feel like anything?" with "why does representing-thus feel like anything?" and the question survives intact. No tracking relation, however teleologically refined, *entails* a felt character—one can specify the full informational-functional profile of a state representing red without thereby saying why occupying that state is like anything at all. The reduction of feel to content reads, on the Map's view, as a re-description of the mystery rather than an explanation of it. This is the same structural point the Map presses against every reductive account: fixing the functional or representational facts leaves the phenomenal fact untouched.

**A dualist re-reading of transparency.** The Map can accept the transparency datum in full and decline the representationalist's inference. Transparency shows that introspection attends *through* experience toward worldly objects; it does not show that experience *is* nothing but its represented content. On a dualist reading, experience is transparent *to* its objects precisely because phenomenal awareness is a non-physical coupling *directed at* the world—a relation of acquaintance with worldly properties, not an identity with a naturalized content-property. Transparency undercuts a naive picture of qualia as inner mental objects we inspect; it does not establish reduction. Read this way, the datum representationalism leans on is compatible with—arguably even suggestive of—the Map's dualism.

The honest counterweight: the representationalist has real advantages the Map should not paper over—naturalistic economy, a direct account of perception's world-directedness, and the genuine phenomenological pull of transparency. The Map's claim is not that these fail on their own terms but that they leave the felt character of experience unexplained, which is exactly the irreducibility Dualism takes as its starting point. The view is resisted, not defeated.
