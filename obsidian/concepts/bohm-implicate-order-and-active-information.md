David Bohm's work supplies one of the four standing quantum-mind rivals the Map catalogues (alongside [[stapp-quantum-mind|Stapp's quantum Zeno model]], Penrose–Hameroff Orch-OR, and [[quantum-biology-and-neural-consciousness|quantum biology]]). It is the one the Map borrows from most and hosts least. Bohm developed three interlocking bodies of thought — the 1952 pilot-wave interpretation, the reading of the "quantum potential" as *active information*, and the *implicate order* metaphysics — that together offer a candidate channel for the kind of small, energy-conserving influence the Map's tenets require. Against this, Bohm's own reading is a neutral monism the Map must decline, and his mechanics is deterministic in a way that sits in genuine tension with conscious selection. This article separates what the Map takes from what it leaves.

## The Pilot-Wave Foundation

Bohm's causal interpretation appeared as a two-part paper in *Physical Review* in 1952 (85(2): 166–179 and 180–193), reviving Louis de Broglie's abandoned 1927 pilot-wave idea as a working theory. Its claim is that particles have definite positions and trajectories at all times, guided by the wavefunction acting as an objectively real field. There are two dynamical equations: Schrödinger's equation for the wavefunction, and a *guiding equation* fixing each particle's velocity from the wavefunction at its actual location. Given the initial positions, the evolution is fully deterministic.

The theory is a genuine hidden-variable model — it was constructed partly as a counterexample to claims that no such theory could reproduce quantum mechanics. The statistical predictions of standard quantum theory re-emerge from ignorance of the exact initial configuration: if the initial distribution of positions matches the Born measure (*quantum equilibrium*), that agreement persists under the dynamics, and the usual `|psi|^2` probabilities follow. On this reading the Born rule is derived from the dynamics rather than postulated. The Stanford Encyclopedia entry on Bohmian mechanics notes a terminological irony flagged by John Bell: the "hidden variables" (particle positions) are the manifest, observed quantities, while the wavefunction is the more genuinely hidden object.

Two structural features matter for the Map. The interpretation is manifestly *nonlocal*: a particle's guidance depends, through the joint wavefunction, on the instantaneous configuration of distant entangled particles. Bohmians treat this Bell-nonlocality as a virtue that honestly reflects entanglement rather than a defect. And it is *contextual*: measured values depend on the full experimental arrangement, not on pre-existing context-free properties. There is also no collapse — apparent collapse is the effective wavefunction conditioning on the actual particle configuration, which dissolves the measurement problem at the price of determinism.

## Active Information: Form, Not Force

In *The Undivided Universe* (Routledge, 1993), Bohm and Basil Hiley reworked the quantum potential — the term in the guiding dynamics that encodes the wavefunction's influence — as *active information*. The central observation is that the quantum potential's effect depends only on the *form* of the quantum field, not on its intensity or amplitude. A field of arbitrarily small energy can strongly direct a particle, because what steers the particle is the shape of the information, not its magnitude. Bohm and Hiley describe this as the field *in-forming* the particle: information that is physically active, guiding motion from within rather than pushing from without.

They illustrate the idea with analogies (paraphrased here rather than quoted): the way a radio signal's *form*, not its slight energy, can direct a ship steered by autopilot, its power drawn from the ship's own engines; or the way the form encoded in DNA guides cellular processes whose energy comes from the surrounding cell. In each case a small quantity of form-bearing information organises a much larger flow of energy. Crucially, the active information is about the *whole* experimental context — it carries the nonlocality and contextuality of the underlying theory.

This form-directs-energy character is the single most borrowable piece of Bohm's apparatus for the Map, because it describes a way for something to steer a physical process *without supplying energy to it*. That is exactly the profile a conservation-respecting minimal influence would need.

## The Implicate Order and the Holomovement

In *Wholeness and the Implicate Order* (Routledge, 1980), Bohm set the physics inside a broader metaphysics. He distinguished the *explicate order* — the familiar world in which each thing occupies its own region of space and time — from the *implicate order*, in which everything is enfolded into everything else. Underlying both is the *holomovement*: an unbroken, undivided, flowing totality, a universal flux from which the relatively stable structures of the explicate world are unfolded as recurrent sub-patterns.

Bohm's recurring image is the hologram, where information about the whole scene is enfolded into every region of the plate, so that any fragment reconstructs (a blurred version of) the entire image. Reality, on this picture, is whole-in-part: separateness is a secondary appearance abstracted from a prior wholeness. The nonseparability of entangled systems is offered as physical motivation for taking enfoldment seriously rather than as mere metaphor.

For present purposes the implicate order is a metaphysics of the *physical* side of things — a holistic reading of what the quantum world is like. The difficulty, addressed below, is that Bohm did not stop there.

## The Mind Connection: Soma-Significance

Bohm extended the implicate order to the mind–matter relation, most directly in "A New Theory of the Relationship of Mind and Matter" (*Philosophical Psychology*, vol. 3, 1990). There he argued that mind and matter are distinguishable but not separate — two relatively autonomous orders enfolded in a common implicate ground. His term *soma-significance* couples the somatic (physical) and the significant (meaning-bearing) as two aspects of one process: physical states carry significance, and significance shapes physical states, in a reciprocal loop.

Paavo Pylkkänen developed this program furthest in *Mind, Matter and the Implicate Order* (Springer, 2007), applying active information, soma-significance, and the implicate order to mental causation, the hard problem, and time-consciousness. On Pylkkänen's reading, active information is the *bridge* between mind and matter: because it already carries something like meaning or semantic content in a primitive form, it is a candidate proto-mental or proto-experiential property, making the mind–matter transition less abrupt than dualism suggests.

Pylkkänen and Hiley pressed the interaction question directly in "Can Mind Affect Matter Via Active Information?" (*Mind and Matter*, 3(2), 2005). Their argument is that mental processes, treated as non-classical physical processes, could act on classically describable neural processes without violating energy conservation — precisely because active information directs energy rather than injecting it. This is structurally close to what the Map wants, but its framing is designed to *avoid* dualist interaction by keeping mind and matter within a single informational ontology.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's engagement with Bohm is deliberately selective, and it splits along the site's [[tenets]].

**Borrow the active-information channel (Tenets 2 and 3).** [[tenets|Tenet 2]] calls for the *smallest possible* non-physical influence on quantum outcomes, and Tenet 3 requires that influence to be causally real and bidirectional. Active information is a natural candidate implementation: a channel where *form directs energy* lets a conscious contribution shape the information guiding a physical process without supplying energy to it, respecting conservation while remaining maximally minimal. The Map interprets this as the strongest reason it keeps citing Bohm — the machinery describes the shape of the influence the tenets posit. The nonlocal, whole-context character of that information also aligns with Tenet 2's framing of influence as operating on a whole configuration rather than a local push.

**Decline the monism (Tenet 1).** Bohm's own reading is a neutral (or dual-aspect) monism: mind and matter as two orders unfolding from one implicate ground. Pylkkänen's development is non-reductive but still monist. Both conflict with [[tenets|Tenet 1]], the Map's commitment to a genuine dualism on which consciousness is not reducible to, or a mere aspect of, the physical. The Map therefore adopts the *mechanism* — active information as a form-directs-energy channel — while rejecting the claim that the information is intrinsically physical or that mind and matter share a single ground. Treat the implicate order as a metaphysics of the physical side, not as a dissolution of the mind/matter distinction.

**The determinism-versus-selection tension (a genuine cost).** This is where honesty matters most, because it is easy to paper over. Bohmian mechanics is deterministic and hidden-variable: a fully specified pilot wave plus definite initial positions leaves *no* room for a Born-rule-preserving conscious selection at measurement, because the outcome is already fixed by the initial configuration. This is in direct tension with the Map's picture of consciousness selecting among quantum-compatible outcomes. Adopting Bohmian ontology wholesale would therefore *undercut* the Map's selection mechanism, not support it — unless consciousness is deliberately located in one of two layers:

1. **In the guidance itself.** If the active information partly constituting the quantum potential has a mental or proto-mental component, then consciousness enters through the guiding field rather than at a collapse event. This keeps the theory's structure but abandons the strict physicalism of the field — closer to what the Map wants, though it makes "active information" carry heavy metaphysical weight.
2. **In the choice of initial configuration.** There is a live debate over whether Bohmian mechanics is *genuinely* deterministic. A recent argument (arXiv:2202.12279) holds that reproducing Born statistics requires an external stochastic sampling of the quantum-equilibrium distribution, reintroducing irreducible contingency at the level of *which* initial condition obtains. If that is right, the selection of the actual initial configuration is a candidate seam where a Born-preserving conscious contribution could live without breaking the dynamics.

Neither seam is free, and the Map should not pretend otherwise: each buys compatibility with the tenets at a real theoretical price, and the second depends on an unsettled foundational dispute. The Map's stance is to catalogue Bohm's apparatus as a borrowable channel and an instructive rival, not to host it as its own mechanism. What survives the borrowing is the form-directs-energy idea; what does not is the determinism, the no-collapse ontology, and the single-ground monism.
