The *contingency thesis* is the single most damaging objection to [[russellian-monism|Russellian monism's]] claim that consciousness does genuine causal work. Russellian monism was motivated partly by escaping [[concepts/epiphenomenalism|epiphenomenalism]]: if phenomenal *quiddities* — the intrinsic natures that categorically ground the dispositions physics describes — are what make charge charge and mass mass, then consciousness is woven into physical causation rather than left idle beside it. Robert Howell (2015) identifies the flaw, and Robinson (2018) presses the same epiphenomenalist verdict from a complementary angle. The grounding relation appears *contingent*: if quiddity Q1 grounds negative charge in the actual world but a distinct quiddity Q2 could have grounded the very same dispositional profile in another possible world, then phenomenal character makes no distinctive causal contribution *in virtue of being phenomenal*. The causal work is carried entirely by the structural-dispositional profile, which any suitable quiddity could realise. Epiphenomenalism returns wearing monist vocabulary.

This article develops *why* the contingency thesis bites, what would have to be true to escape it, and how the Map's [[delegatory-causation|delegatory causation]] avoids the defeater. One framing must be fixed at the outset to keep the argument honest: the Map does not *inherit* this specific defeater, because its causation is delegatory and interactionist rather than quiddity-grounded. That is a **structural** claim about which objections apply to which theories — not **evidence** that interactionism is true. Russellian monism's failure here is no point in favour of the Map; both views could be false. The contingency thesis tells us where one bridge is weak, not that any other bridge will bear weight.

## What the Contingency Thesis Claims

Russellian monism, in Howell's reconstruction, holds that "consciousness is constituted at least partly by quiddities: intrinsic properties that categorically ground the dispositional properties described by fundamental physics" (Howell 2015). Negative charge, on this picture, is a disposition — to repel like charges, attract unlike ones — and that disposition is *grounded in* some categorical intrinsic property that physics never names. The Russellian identifies (or partly identifies) those categorical grounds with phenomenal or proto-phenomenal properties.

The contingency thesis is a claim about the *modality* of that grounding relation:

> The contingency thesis says that the connection is contingent — the dispositional property associated with negative charge might have been categorically grounded by a quiddity distinct from the one that actually grounds it.

If the thesis holds, the quiddity and the disposition can come apart across possible worlds. The same causal role — the same pattern of attractions, repulsions, and field interactions — could have been realised by a different intrinsic filler. Whatever phenomenal character Q1 carries is not what does the causal work, because Q2, with whatever different character it carries, would have done exactly the same work. The disposition is *multiply realisable* by quiddities, and that multiple realisability is precisely what drains phenomenal character of causal relevance.

Howell builds the argument on Jaegwon Kim's exclusion argument (see [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|mental causation and downward causation]]). The structural-dispositional profile is causally sufficient for the physical effects. If the phenomenal quiddity is something *over and above* that profile — something that could vary while the profile stays fixed — then it is excluded from the causal story by the very profile it was supposed to ground. The quiddity becomes a passenger.

## Why Quiddity-Grounding Fails to Make Consciousness Causal "In Virtue of Its Phenomenality"

The crucial phrase is *in virtue of*. Russellian monists can readily grant that quiddities are *causally relevant* in some thin sense — they are, after all, the categorical grounds without which the dispositions would not exist. What the contingency thesis denies is that they are relevant *qua phenomenal*. Howell's verdict:

> "The best Russellian monism can hope for is that phenomenal properties are causally relevant, but not in virtue of their phenomenality."

The distinction is the whole game. Consider two readings of "the quiddity does causal work":

1. **Grounding relevance.** The quiddity is the categorical base of a disposition; remove all quiddities and you remove the dispositions. In this sense any quiddity that grounds the right disposition is relevant.
2. **Phenomenal relevance.** The *specific felt character* of the quiddity — its being *this* experience rather than that — makes a difference to what happens physically.

The contingency thesis grants (1) and denies (2). Because Q1 and Q2 ground the *same* disposition, swapping one for the other changes nothing physical. But Q1 and Q2 differ phenomenally. So the phenomenal difference is causally inert — it floats free of the physical effects. That is epiphenomenalism about phenomenal character, which is exactly the conclusion Russellian monism was built to avoid. The view set out to integrate consciousness into causation and ends up integrating only the *categorical-ground role*, a role any non-phenomenal filler could occupy just as well.

This is why the objection is sharper than the parallel worries Russellian monism faces. The [[russellian-monism-versus-bi-aspectual-dualism|instability]] arguments (Cutter 2019, Kind 2015) say the view collapses toward dualism or physicalism; the parity argument (Pautz 2017) says it needs the same brute linking laws as dualism. The contingency thesis is more pointed: it grants the view its quiddities and *still* shows the phenomenal aspect doing nothing — the failure is internal to the monist's own causal ambitions.

## What Would Defeat the Objection — and the Cost

The contingency thesis can be resisted by denying its modal premise: insist the grounding is **necessary**, so that *only* Q1 could ground negative charge. If the quiddity-disposition link is necessary, Q2 is not a genuine possibility, multiple realisability fails, and phenomenal character is no longer detachable from the causal role.

This is the live defence in the literature. Alter and Coleman (2021) argue that the integration-failure arguments do not go through — that Russellian monism can locate consciousness within physical causation after all, partly by resisting the contingency framing. But the necessitarian move carries costs the Map's own commitments make visible:

- **It imports brute necessities.** A necessary quiddity-disposition link is a further modal commitment with no independent evidence bearing on it — the same predicament Pautz's [[russellian-monism|parity argument]] diagnoses. Why *this* quiddity necessarily grounds *this* disposition becomes a primitive.
- **It strains against modal intuitions about phenomenal properties.** The [[modal-structure-of-phenomenal-properties|modal structure of phenomenal properties]] is distinctively *loose*: zombies and inverted qualia are conceivable precisely because phenomenal character seems detachable from structural-functional role. A necessitarian Russellian must hold that *this* phenomenal-to-dispositional link is rigid while conceding the looseness elsewhere — a tension, not a contradiction, but a cost.
- **It may collapse the view.** If the quiddity is necessarily tied to the disposition, the disposition arguably *is* (or rigidly individuates) the quiddity, and the categorical/dispositional distinction that motivated quiddities in the first place begins to dissolve.

None of these is a knock-down refutation of necessitarianism. The point is that escaping the contingency thesis is not free: it trades an epiphenomenalism worry for a modal-commitment worry. The objection is "the single most damaging" precisely because every exit costs something the view was trying to keep.

## Contrast with the Map's Delegatory Causation

The Map's response to the exclusion problem runs through [[delegatory-causation|delegatory causation]] (Bradford Saad 2025), and the contrast with quiddity-grounding is instructive — provided the contrast is read structurally, as set out above, not as evidence for the Map.

Quiddity-grounding tries to make consciousness causal by making it the *categorical base* of physical dispositions. The contingency thesis attacks at exactly that join: the base is swappable, so the phenomenal character of the base is idle. Delegatory causation does not place consciousness at that join at all. On Saad's mechanism, a conscious experience *preempts* the [[trumping-preemption|default causal profile]] of the physical state — it takes over the causal work the brain state would otherwise perform, by trumping rather than grounding. The experience is not a categorical filler behind a disposition; it is a distinct causal contributor that displaces the physical state's default profile (see [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|mental causation]] and [[causal-closure|causal closure]]).

Why this matters for the contingency thesis specifically:

- **No grounding relation to be contingent.** The contingency thesis needs a quiddity that *grounds* a disposition, so that a different quiddity could ground the same one. Delegation posits no such grounding. Experience does not realise the disposition; it preempts the profile. There is no "could a different filler have done the same job" question of the relevant kind, because experience is not a filler.
- **Phenomenal character is what preempts.** On the delegatory picture, the *specific* experience — this pain, this decision — is what takes over the causal work. Swap the experience and you change what is preempted. Phenomenal relevance (sense (2) above) is built in rather than drained away.
- **The cost is relocated, not eliminated.** Delegation has its own hard questions — most pointedly [[the-epiphenomenalist-threat|ensemble-level epiphenomenalism]]: if selection only ever stays within the Born-rule distribution, a critic can ask whether the experience is doing token-causal work or merely riding the statistics physics already fixes. The Map answers this on *trumping* grounds (efficacy is structural, not statistical), and rates that answer as framework-internal rather than empirically demonstrable.

So the honest summary is structural and modest: **the contingency thesis is a defeater for theories that make consciousness causal by grounding dispositions; the Map does not adopt that strategy, so the defeater does not transfer.** This is not a victory of interactionism over monism on the evidence. Both delegatory dualism and Russellian monism share the same empirical profile — neither makes distinctive testable predictions — and both could be wrong. What the contingency thesis establishes is narrower: one specific route to mental causation is blocked, and the Map happens not to take that route.

## Relation to Site Perspective

This concept bears most directly on two of the Map's [[tenets]].

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]].** The contingency thesis is, in effect, an argument that Russellian monism cannot have both its monism and genuine phenomenal causation. Howell's reconstruction shows the felt character of quiddities floating free of physical effects unless the grounding is made necessary — at which point the categorical/dispositional distinction strains toward collapse. The Map reads this as further pressure toward explicit dualism: if the phenomenal must be *added* to the causal story rather than identified with a swappable ground, the cleaner option is to treat it as ontologically distinct and causally interacting. This is an interpretive reading, not a proof; a necessitarian Russellian will resist it.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]].** The tenet asserts that consciousness causally influences the physical world. The contingency thesis is precisely a demonstration that *one* popular way of honouring something like this tenet — quiddity-grounding — fails to deliver phenomenal efficacy. The Map's delegatory route is designed to deliver it (the specific experience is what preempts), but the article must not overclaim: the success of delegation rests on the broader case for [[delegatory-causation|trumping-style mental causation]], not on Russellian monism's troubles. **Russellian monism's failure is not evidence for the Map.** It shows that the field of live options is smaller than it looked, which is a different and weaker thing.

The discipline here matters because the temptation is real: it is easy to write as though a rival's defeater were a credit to one's own view. The evidential-status-discipline the Map imposes forbids that move. The most the contingency thesis licenses is: *the Map's interaction thesis does not inherit this particular objection.* Non-inheritance of a defeater is a structural property of the theory's architecture, not a point of confirmation.
