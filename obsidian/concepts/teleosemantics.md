Teleosemantics—Ruth Millikan's coinage was *biosemantics*—is the leading *positive* naturalistic theory of mental content: the strongest attempt to explain how a physical state can be genuinely *about* the world using only resources a working scientist recognises. Where bare causal or informational theories fix content by covariation (state S means P because S reliably co-occurs with P), teleosemantics adds one ingredient that covariation lacks: **biological proper function fixed by selection history**. A state means P because the mechanisms that produce or consume it were *selected for* their role relative to P. That addition is meant to earn what a correlation can never earn on its own—the possibility of *misrepresentation*, of a state being *wrong*—and so to cross from mere correlation to genuine aboutness. This article gives that programme its strongest form across its four major proponents (Millikan, Neander, Dretske, Papineau) and its canonical objections *with* their best replies, before locating the Map's dissent: proper function delivers at most *selection-relative* correctness, which is not the rational, content-constituting normativity the Map's [dualism tenet requires](#relation-to-site-perspective). This page is the positive rival's home. The negative master argument against it—Hutto and Myin's [[hard-problem-of-content|Hard Problem of Content]]—lives elsewhere and is engaged here only by reference.

## The Problem: From Covariation to Aboutness

Every naturalistic theory of content confronts one demand: explain how a neural state comes to *say* something that can be true or false. The natural sciences supply causal relations, statistical correlations, and evolutionary history. The trouble is that correlation is normatively inert. A tree ring correlates with rainfall without being *about* rainfall; a thermometer tracks temperature without representing it. What content adds to correlation is *correctness conditions*—a state can be tokened when its worldly condition fails to obtain, and then it *misrepresents*. Bare covariation cannot mark that difference, because whatever a state actually correlates with is, trivially, what it correlates with.

Teleosemantics' central move is to source the missing normativity from biology. Proponents adopt an **etiological** theory of function: a trait's function is not what it happens to do but what past tokens of its type were *selected for*. A heart's function is pumping blood—not making thumping sounds—because pumping is why hearts were favoured by selection. Transfer this to inner states: a representation's content is the condition its producing or consuming mechanism was selected to operate relative to. Because selection favoured performance under one specific condition, tokening the state *outside* that condition is genuine failure. The "ought" of representing-truly is glossed as the "ought" of functioning-as-selected. Whether that gloss succeeds is the pivot on which the whole programme—and the Map's dissent—turns.

## Millikan's Biosemantics: Proper Functions and the Consumer

Ruth Millikan founded the programme in *Language, Thought, and Other Biological Categories* (MIT Press, 1984) and stated it most accessibly in the essay "Biosemantics" (collected in *White Queen Psychology and Other Essays for Alice*, MIT Press, 1993). Two ideas do the work.

First, **proper function** is etiological: an item's proper function is the effect that explains why its ancestors were reproduced. This makes function a natural, historically-grounded relation—no appeal to present dispositions, designers, or minds—yet a normative one, since an item can exist while failing to perform the function that explains its existence. That is malfunction, and malfunction is the biological template for misrepresentation.

Second, and distinctively, Millikan locates content on the **consumer** side. Every representation is a sign passed from a *producer* mechanism to one or more *consumer* mechanisms downstream. What the sign *means* is fixed not by what tends to produce it but by the world-condition onto which it must map for the consumer to carry out *its* proper functions in the way that historically succeeded—Millikan's term of art is performing them the "Normal" way, where "Normal" means the way that explains past success, not the statistically usual way. On this reading a bee-dance means *nectar in such-and-such direction* because that is the mapping the dance-reading consumer relies on to forage successfully; a frog's inner sign means whatever its snapping-and-digesting consumer was selected to have it map onto. Producers confer representational *status*; consumers confer *content*.

(The consumer-primacy reading is contested, including by Millikan herself, who in later work stresses complementary producer and consumer roles; some commentators speak of "two Millikans." The Map treats the consumer-side emphasis as the canonical statement while noting the scholarship is live.)

## Neander's Informational Teleosemantics: The Producer Side

Karen Neander's *A Mark of the Mental: In Defense of Informational Teleosemantics* (MIT Press, 2017) develops the rival, **producer-side** account for sensory-perceptual representation. Here content is fixed by what the sensory system was selected to be *caused by*—the distal condition it has the function to *detect*—rather than by what a downstream consumer does with the result.

Neander's sophistication is to weave together three elements she argues are complementary rather than competing: biological **function**, **causal-information** relations, and **second-order similarity** (the inner state changes in ways that are structurally analogous to changes in what it represents). A sensory system has the function of producing inner states that are both reliably caused by, and analogs of, their referents. To break ties between candidate contents she invokes a **distality principle**: a visual system responds to proximal features (retinal patterns) *because* doing so historically enabled detecting distal ones (prey), and that explanatory asymmetry fixes the distal cause as the content. This is the producer-side programme in its strongest, most defended contemporary form.

## Dretske's Indicator-Function Account

Fred Dretske supplies the theory's information-theoretic ancestor. In *Explaining Behavior: Reasons in a World of Causes* (MIT Press, 1988) he argues that an internal state acquires the **function of indicating** a condition when a consumer system is recruited—through learning or selection—to use that state in controlling behaviour. Natural information (nomic dependence: the state would not be present unless the condition obtained) supplies the raw correlation; recruitment for behavioural control supplies the function; content is *what the state has the function to indicate*.

Dretske's ambition later widened. In *Naturalizing the Mind* (MIT Press, 1995) he extended representationalism to *phenomenal* character itself, arguing that qualia are the represented external properties—that what it is like to have an experience is exhausted by what the experience non-conceptually represents. This makes Dretske the most ambitious figure for the Map's purposes: he aims to naturalise not only aboutness but experience, bringing the account into direct contact with the Map's [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] and [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] reasoning.

## Papineau's Success-Semantic Version

David Papineau, in *Philosophical Naturalism* (Blackwell, 1993), gives an output-side account tied to **success semantics**. A desire's satisfaction condition is the effect it is the desire's biological purpose to produce; a belief is true when, combined with desires, it would tend to yield the success the underlying states were selected to deliver. Truth and satisfaction conditions are thus read off the selection history of behaviour-guiding states. Papineau is also notable for the frankest concession-and-reframe on the Swampman objection, discussed [below](#swampman).

## The Objection Battery and Its Best Replies

A genuine steelman must field the standard objections *with* the replies teleosemanticists actually give. Presenting the objections alone would smuggle in a refutation.

### The Disjunction Problem (the Founding Success)

Jerry Fodor's disjunction problem is the difficulty teleosemantics was built to solve. On a pure causal theory, a belief-state caused by cats and, on dark nights, by small foxes means *cat-or-small-fox*—so the theory cannot say the fox-triggered tokening is an *error* rather than a correct representation of a disjunction. Teleosemantics blocks this: if the consumer (or detector) was selected for its role relative to *cats*, then a fox-triggered tokening is a malfunction, not part of a disjunctive content. Misrepresentation is recovered. This is widely regarded as teleosemantics' strongest card, and the debate has moved on from *whether* function can distinguish error to *which* condition function picks out.

### The Frog Case (Neander versus Fodor)

That "which condition" question is sharpest in the frog. A frog's prey-detector was selected because it responded to small dark moving things, *and* because that enabled catching prey, *and* because prey yielded nutrition. So does the inner state represent *fly*, *frog food*, *nutrition*, or *small dark moving thing*? Fodor presses that selection is silent among locally co-instantiated candidates, so content is indeterminate and teleosemantics fails to deliver a definite answer. Neander's reply is the producer-side one: content is what the system was selected to be *causally sensitive* to, and a frog's visual system cannot be causally sensitive to *nutritional value* or to *dipteran taxonomy*—only to visible motion and shape. So the content is determinately *small, dark, moving thing*. The frog case remains the flagship test, still actively litigated, and it is a principal reason producer-side informational teleosemantics has gained ground on benefit-based versions.

### Swampman

Donald Davidson's Swampman is a lightning-formed molecular duplicate of a person, assembled by cosmic accident with no evolutionary or learning history. Having no selection history, Swampman has no proper functions—so teleosemantics entails his inner states have no content, which many find plainly false. Teleosemanticists reply in two main ways. The **bite-the-bullet** strategy (Millikan; Neander) holds that "intentionality" rigidly designates a historical-selectional kind, much as "water" designates H₂O: the intuition that Swampman thinks is a mistaken pre-theoretic intuition, like insisting water needn't be H₂O. The **actual-world-reduction** strategy (Papineau) grants that teleosemantics reduces the *actual* property of belief, so a merely possible Swampman with content is no counterexample to an actual-world identity claim—Papineau reports being moved off pure bullet-biting partly by the ethical discomfort of denying that a Swamp-person has a mind. Both replies keep the theory intact at the price of a commitment about what content *is*.

### The Indeterminacy of Function (Pietroski's *kimu*)

Paul Pietroski's *kimu* thought experiment targets *benefit*-based versions specifically. Imagine colour-blind creatures whose brain-state B, keyed to a certain wavelength, spread because it led them uphill toward safety from a dawn predator (the *snorf*)—not because it tracked *red*. A benefit-based teleosemantics that reads content off downstream advantage seems forced to say B means *snorf-free space*, when intuitively B represents *red*. The pressure generalises via the observation (pressed by Neander's own antelope example and by indeterminacy critics broadly) that selection acts on whole complexes of co-implicated properties, so function may underdetermine content. Millikan's response distinguishes *represented* from merely *causing* properties: a state can represent an invisible property (safety, nutrition) that is caused by visible features. And crucially, *kimu* is fuel for the producer-side turn: tying content to the distal *cause* the system detects, rather than the *benefit* it confers, sidesteps the case—one reason informational teleosemantics is now the more defended wing.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Teleosemantics is the most credible reductive rival to the Map's first tenet, **Dualism** (the irreducibility of the mental). Its appeal is exactly that it seems to derive normativity—correctness, error, misrepresentation—from mind-independent selection facts, with no appeal to consciousness. If it succeeds, aboutness is naturalised and one of the Map's central claims is undercut. So the Map owes it a fair hearing, which the sections above supply, before dissenting.

The Map's dissent is a claim about *two normativities*. Proper function yields **biological** normativity: a trait can malfunction relative to what selection favoured. Content requires **semantic-rational** normativity: a state can be *false*, and a thinker *ought*, given evidence, to judge one way rather than another. The Map's contention is that the first does not constitute the second. A malfunctioning heart is not *mistaken about* anything; it fails a selection-relative standard without thereby entering the space of reasons. Reading the "ought" of representing-truly off the "ought" of having-been-selected trades a rational relation for a historical-statistical one, and the two come apart—not over what *fixes* correctness, which is world-involving on any theory including the Map's, but over what earns a state *answerability to evidence*, which selection-relative malfunction never amounts to. This is a sibling of the Map's [[rational-normativity|normativity-of-reason]] argument, where the prescriptive force of inference likewise resists reduction to what brains were selected to do.

This dissent is deliberately kept distinct from the two standing arguments the Map already holds against naturalised content, which it here cross-references rather than repeats. Hutto and Myin's [[hard-problem-of-content|Hard Problem of Content]] presses the *intensionality* gap—that even granting proper function, biology cannot fix the *guise* under which a state represents (as *food* versus as *nutritionally-optimal-nearby-item*). The [[the-naturalisation-failure-for-content|naturalisation failure for content]] topic develops that dilemma as a stand-alone parallel to the phenomenal hard problem. The present objection is upstream of both: it questions whether *selection-relative malfunction* is the *right kind* of normativity to begin with, before intensionality is even reached. Where the Map's [[intentionality|phenomenal intentionality]] tradition locates genuine aboutness in consciousness, teleosemantics locates it in history—and history, the Map holds, is the wrong place to find an *ought*.

Two honest limitations. First, the bite-the-bullet reply is principled: a teleosemanticist can simply insist that "content" *names* the historical kind, so the Map's appeal to a rational normativity beyond selection begs the question against the reduction. The disagreement is over what content is, and neither side refutes the other by stipulation. Second, teleosemantics is a live and improving research programme; producer-side informational versions have answered several objections that sank cruder ancestors, and the Map's dissent is a reasoned bet about the limits of reduction ([[tenets|Occam's razor has limits]]), not a demonstration. What the Map claims is that the crossing from covariation to aboutness, even with proper function added, stops one step short of the normativity that content-bearing thought requires—and that the missing step is where consciousness enters.
