## Outer-review verdict

**The article is strongest as an argument for functional separability, not as evidence for dualism.** Its best-supported claim is that dissociative, depersonalization, amnestic, and functional-neurological phenomena often involve selective disruptions of access, ownership, agency, memory, or motor control without a gross destructive lesion. Its weakest move is treating that selective separability as if it gives positive differential support to a non-physical “interface” rather than to already-standard network, functionalist, predictive-processing, and clinical-neuropsychiatric accounts. The article itself sometimes says the right thing — that the data “earn the architecture” but leave dualist significance to be argued separately — yet the title, section heading “Why This Favours Dualism,” trauma-protection language, and Minimal Quantum Interaction paragraph pull harder than the evidence warrants.

## 1. Central claims audit

**Claim 1: Clinical dissociation supplies intact-substrate evidence.**

Partly supported, but overstated. The article says that in DID, DPDR, dissociative amnesia, and conversion/FND, the “neural substrate remains anatomically intact” while aspects of experience “go offline,” with neuroimaging showing altered functional connectivity rather than regional degradation. This is a fair high-level contrast with destructive lesions, but “substrate intact” is too blunt: if the physical substrate includes dynamic connectivity, predictive control, interoceptive inference, and network state, then the substrate is precisely what has changed. The safer claim is: **no gross destructive lesion is required; functional network dynamics can be selectively altered.** Current reviews support the existence of functional neuroimaging correlates in dissociative disorders, but also stress small samples, heterogeneity, conflicting conclusions, and the need for further studies.

**Claim 2: The four conditions map onto stable “interface channels.”**

Useful as a Map-internal taxonomy, but not yet an empirically established taxonomy. The table mapping DID to unity/cross-state integration, DPDR to self-model ownership, amnesia to memory access, and conversion/FND to motor command is conceptually tidy. But current clinical literature does not yet vindicate one clean channel ontology. Dissociation is transdiagnostic and multidimensional; the trauma-versus-sociocognitive debate has broadened into transtheoretical accounts involving sleep disturbance, self-regulation, inhibition, hyperassociation, set shifts, source attribution, reality testing, and metacognition. The article’s “same structural logic repeats” formulation should be downgraded to: **the cases can be interpreted as selective access failures, but the clinical literature does not yet show that they instantiate one shared channel architecture.**

**Claim 3: The pattern favours dualism.**

Weak. It favours, at most, **anti-localization** and **anti-crude-identity** views. It does not discriminate strongly between an interface dualism and sophisticated physicalist accounts. Functionalism is not an ad hoc retreat from “neural activity” to “connectivity”; it is classically the view that mental states are individuated by what they do, not by what material they are made of, and it is widely treated as a central philosophy-of-mind position rather than a patch. The article’s claim that physicalism “progressively add[s] complexity” by moving toward functional organization is therefore unfair: functional organization is exactly where functionalist physicalism began.

**Claim 4: Recovery or “snap-back” supports reconnection rather than reconstruction.**

Suggestive rhetorically, but evidentially thin. The article argues that when connectivity restores, full function may return “immediately,” which could suggest reconnection rather than rebuilding. But a connectivity physicalist does not need a separate “rebuilding” step: if a conscious state is constituted by a dynamic network relation, then restoring the relevant relation just is restoring the state. The article acknowledges this reply, but then continues to treat snap-back as a positive bridge signature. The claim should be labelled speculative and should not carry argumentative weight without detailed clinical data on time course, relapse, partial recovery, memory accuracy, and reconstruction.

**Claim 5: Trauma explains purposive selectivity.**

Overdrawn. Trauma is important, especially in DID and trauma-related dissociation, and a Cambridge editorial by Reinders and Veltman argues that DID remains controversial while neurobiological evidence supports clinical observations linking DID to severe PTSD-like trauma. But current reviews also emphasize that neither the posttraumatic model nor the sociocognitive model is sufficient, and that broader transdiagnostic variables are needed. For FND, current work stresses positive diagnostic signs, brain-network abnormalities, agency, predictive coding, action inference, stress sensitivity, emotion-motor linkage, distorted action selection, and top-down control; psychological stressors are clinically relevant but not a sufficient explanatory key. The target article’s “mind protects itself by disconnecting specific interface channels” should be presented as a metaphorical dualist interpretation, not as something the clinical data show.

## 2. Citation strength and accuracy

I did not find an obvious fabricated reference in the target article’s reference list. The main problem is **not fake citations**; it is **stale, sparse, and overextended citation support** for claims that now need recent review-level anchoring.

**Janet 1889.** Bibliographically real and relevant as historical background: *L’automatisme psychologique* was published in Paris by Félix Alcan in 1889. But the article’s claim that Janet “anticipates the Map’s interface architecture” is interpretive. Janet supports a historical dissociation/integration frame, not the Map’s dualist interface model.

**Sierra & Berrios 1998.** Correctly identified and relevant to DPDR. Their model links depersonalization to prefrontal attentional systems, anterior cingulate inhibition, amygdala inhibition, hypoemotionality, and unreality/detachment. The article’s DPDR section is broadly aligned with that framework, but it should not rely on a 1998 DSM-IV-era theoretical review as the main anchor for a 2026 empirical synthesis. It needs newer DPDR and dissociation network literature.

**Reinders et al. 2003.** Real and relevant, but too load-bearing as used. The ScienceDirect record confirms “One brain, two selves,” DOI 10.1016/j.neuroimage.2003.08.021, and reports different regional cerebral blood-flow patterns for different senses of self in DID. But the study used eleven female DID participants and comes from an early PET paradigm; the article should say this explicitly and pair it with later reviews and controversy.

**Vuilleumier 2005.** Real and relevant to conversion/FND, but the target article should preserve more of its caution. Vuilleumier describes conversion symptoms as functional neurological deficits not explained by organic lesions and reviews imaging findings in motor, somatosensory, and visual conversion symptoms. Importantly, the abstract also says the exact nature of the disorder remains largely unknown and that the data point toward dynamic limbic–sensorimotor modulation, not a simple exclusion-from-awareness story.

**Brown 2004.** Real and relevant, but broad. Brown’s paper is about medically unexplained symptoms as an integrative cognitive-psychological model, not specifically a modern FND rule-in framework. The target uses Brown to support inhibitory blocking of representations from awareness; that is plausible background, but the article should add current FND sources because “medically unexplained symptoms” is not the preferred framing for contemporary FND diagnosis.

**Missing current anchors.** A 2022 systematic review of functional neuroimaging in dissociative disorders included only 13 studies, across 51 DID patients, 28 depersonalization patients, 24 dissociative-amnesia patients, and 6 other/unspecified cases; it found recurring prefrontal dysfunction but also noted small samples, heterogeneity, conflicting conclusions, and need for further studies. A 2025 DID treatment review states that reliable biological markers have not been identified and that evidence-based guidelines remain unavailable because solid empirical data are lacking. Those two points should materially lower the article’s confidence level.

## 3. Counterarguments: present but underpowered

The article does include a physicalist response: it repeatedly says modern physicalism can accommodate dissociation through connectivity and that the dualist reading is not forced. That is good calibration. But the article does not fully steelman the opponent. A serious physicalist response is not merely “connectivity does it somehow.” It is a developed cluster of functionalist, global-workspace, predictive-processing, agency, interoceptive, salience-network, and self-model accounts. In FND specifically, recent work frames symptoms through abnormal sensorimotor processing, action inference, emotion-motor linkage, self-monitoring, top-down control, and brain network dynamics.

The biggest missing counterargument is that **the phenomena are exactly what functionalist and network accounts predict**. If consciousness is availability, integration, self-modeling, action inference, affective salience, or global access, then selective disruptions of access, ownership, memory, or motor agency are not surprising edge cases. They are diagnostic probes of a functional architecture, not evidence that a non-physical mind is failing to couple to the brain.

The DID section especially needs stronger counterweight. A sibling Map article explicitly says DID carries a live sociocognitive-alternative caveat, but the target article does not import that caveat with enough force. Current DID literature includes trauma-model arguments, sociocognitive critiques, schema/metacognitive approaches, warnings against reifying identity states as separate persons, and claims that inter-identity amnesia may reflect dysfunctional beliefs about memory rather than literal compartmentalized storage. The target’s “single non-physical mind expressing itself through a trauma-disrupted interface” is philosophically possible inside the Map, but it should not be presented as a natural reading until these clinical alternatives are locally discussed.

The amnesia section is the most overconfident. “The information was stored, encoded, and retained” and “access returns immediately and fully” are too strong. In dissociative amnesia, reversibility does not by itself prove intact storage, accurate preservation, or non-reconstructive recovery. The article should distinguish absence of detectable trace destruction from positive evidence of retained content, and it should address memory reconstruction, suggestibility, source monitoring, state-dependent retrieval, and personally relevant semantic memory.

## 4. Tenet-protective bracketing and falsifiability

The article has better bracketing than many advocacy pieces: it says the same data are compatible with connectivity physicalism, says the convergence mainly earns architecture rather than dualist significance, and says neither reading is forced. That is the article’s strongest methodological feature.

The risk is that the bracketing is not fully honored. The “Minimal Quantum Interaction” paragraph says psychological disruption of coordinated neural activity would produce “exactly the dissociative pattern observed,” but the article offers no clinical evidence linking dissociation to quantum-level interaction sites. The site’s Tenets page itself classifies Minimal Quantum Interaction as a consistency claim rather than a novel-prediction claim, and says it trades empirical risk for explanatory completeness. That should prevent the target article from presenting dissociation as positive quantum-interface confirmation.

The deeper falsifiability problem is this: if every physical connectivity finding is redescribed as “the physical side of the bridge,” then the bridge hypothesis risks becoming insulated. To avoid tenet-protective interpretation, the article should state what would reduce confidence in the interface reading. Examples: successful purely intra-cerebral models that prospectively predict onset, symptom switching, recovery, and relapse; stimulation or therapy studies that selectively toggle symptoms through known network mechanisms; robust evidence that dissociative amnesia recovery is graded, reconstructive, or belief-mediated; and failure of the Map’s proposed channel taxonomy to outperform standard clinical/neuroscientific taxonomies. The site already has a Tenet-Dependency Matrix warning against hidden inheritance of stronger commitments; this article should apply that discipline locally.

## 5. Integration with surrounding articles

The article is well-linked internally. It points readers to neurological dissociations, anesthesia, pain asymbolia, contemplative pathology, conversion disorder, dissociative states, and targeted lesion tests. That gives it a coherent place in the Map’s wider architecture.

However, the surrounding articles are often more careful than the target article. The “Dissociative States as Intact-Substrate Interface Evidence” article explicitly says the interpretation is serious but not forced and flags a live sociocognitive caveat for DID. The “Conversion Disorder as the Consciousness-Side-Fault Paradigm” article distinguishes robust architecture from live-hypothesis metaphysics, notes DSM-5’s positive rule-in framework, and says no single test is decisive across presentations. The convergence-methodology article explicitly warns that framework commitments are not independent evidence routes. The target article should inherit those caveats more visibly.

The integration failure is therefore not lack of links; it is **asymmetric inheritance**. The target imports the architecture and evidential ambition of neighbouring articles, but not all of their limiting conditions. This matters because the article’s title and framing encourage readers to count clinical dissociation as systematic evidence for dualism, while the best surrounding methodology would classify it more cautiously as systematic evidence for selective functional dissociability, with dualist interpretation downstream and separately argued.

## Concrete potential improvements

1. **Retitle or subtitle the target article.** Change “Clinical Dissociation as Systematic Evidence” to something like “Clinical Dissociation as Evidence of Selective Functional Dissociability,” or add a subtitle: “Architectural evidence first; dualist significance contested.” This would align the title with the article’s own two-tier concession.
2. **Replace “anatomically intact substrate” with a stricter formulation.** Use: “No gross destructive lesion is required; functional connectivity, agency, salience, and predictive-control networks may be altered.” This prevents “intact substrate” from smuggling in the conclusion that physicalism lacks a substrate-level explanation.
3. **Add an evidence-status box near the top of the article.** Three rows would help: “Clinical datum,” “What it supports,” and “What it does not support.” For example: DPDR supports dissociability of felt ownership from cognition; it does not show a non-physical self-model channel.
4. **Update the reference base.** Add recent review anchors: Modesti et al. 2022 on functional neuroimaging in dissociative disorders; Lynn et al. 2019 and 2022 on competing and transtheoretical models; Bachrach & Huntjens 2025 on DID treatment/evidence limits; Dworetzky & Baslet 2025 on practical FND management; Lo et al. 2025 on motor FND mechanisms; Weber et al. 2025 on rTPJ network dynamics in motor FND.
5. **Revise the DID section to import the sibling article’s caveat.** State locally that DID is a contested exhibit; discuss trauma, sociocognitive, fantasy/suggestion, schema-mode, metacognitive, and non-reification models before offering the Map’s “single mind, fragmented interface” interpretation.
6. **Soften the amnesia claims.** Replace “stored, encoded, and retained” and “access returns immediately and fully” with: “Recovery is often reported, sometimes abruptly, but reversibility does not by itself establish intact storage, accuracy, or non-reconstructive retrieval.” Add discussion of source monitoring, state-dependent retrieval, suggestibility, and reconstruction.
7. **Modernize the conversion/FND section.** Use “functional neurological disorder” as the primary term, with “conversion disorder” as historical/DSM-related terminology. Include the rule-in diagnostic framework, heterogeneity of FND subtypes, and current action-inference/predictive-processing accounts.
8. **Narrow the trauma claim.** Replace “clinical dissociation is overwhelmingly associated with psychological trauma” with a disorder-by-disorder breakdown. DID and trauma-related dissociation can be treated as strongly trauma-linked but contested; DPDR and dissociative amnesia need more qualified language; FND should not be framed as requiring psychological distress.
9. **Remove or demote “purposive selectivity.”** Reframe it as “apparent clinical selectivity” and then compare competing explanations: trauma-protective interface, affective salience, predictive processing, learned expectations, attention, agency disruption, and metacognitive belief.
10. **Rewrite “Why This Favours Dualism.”** A better heading would be “What the Dualist Reading Adds — and What It Does Not Add.” The section should say that the evidence challenges crude localization and region-identity views but does not strongly discriminate between interface dualism and sophisticated physicalist network theories.
11. **Delete “exactly” from the Minimal Quantum Interaction paragraph.** Replace “would produce exactly the dissociative pattern observed” with: “could be made consistent with the pattern, but the clinical evidence does not currently discriminate quantum-interface explanations from ordinary network-level explanations.”
12. **Add a local falsification/disconfirmation subsection.** Include concrete confidence-lowering conditions: prospective physical-network prediction of symptoms and recovery; effective symptom toggling through known neural mechanisms; evidence that dissociative amnesia is reconstructive or belief-mediated; and failure of the channel taxonomy to outperform standard clinical models.
13. **In the surrounding article “Dissociative States as Intact-Substrate Interface Evidence,” add a reciprocal warning.** It should point back to this target article while stating that “intact substrate” means no gross lesion, not absence of physically realized dynamic dysfunction.
14. **In “Conversion Disorder as the Consciousness-Side-Fault Paradigm,” add a short “terminology inheritance” note.** The target article should be required to inherit the FND article’s rule-in, no-single-test, and intra-cerebral-inhibitory-process caveats.
15. **In “The Epistemology of Convergence Arguments,” add clinical dissociation as a worked example of double-count risk.** The example should ask whether lesion, pharmacology, contemplative pathology, and clinical dissociation independently converge on a taxonomy, or whether the Map’s channel taxonomy is partly imposing the common structure.
16. **In the Tenets or methodology pages, add an “evidence ladder” for empirical articles.** Suggested tiers: clinical observation; network-mechanistic evidence; architecture constraint; metaphysical underdetermination; dualist interpretation; discriminating evidence. The target article currently slides between tiers.
17. **Create a citation-staleness rule.** Any empirical article relying on pre-2010 clinical neuroscience should require at least one post-2020 systematic review or major review unless the older source is purely historical.
18. **Separate external evidence from Map-internal support in reference lists.** Internal Map articles should appear under “Map integration,” not under the same “References” heading as clinical literature, so readers do not mistake cross-links for independent corroboration.
19. **Add a source-provenance table for empirical references.** For each cited study: study type, sample size, diagnosis, modality, main finding, limitation, and whether it supports the clinical datum, the channel taxonomy, or the dualist interpretation.
20. **Adopt a “steelman before interface” rule.** Before any article says a phenomenon favours the interface model, it should first present the strongest current physicalist/clinical account in enough detail that a specialist would recognize it.
