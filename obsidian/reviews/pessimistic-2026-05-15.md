Beneath this, **five substantive defects survive adversarial scrutiny**.

**The most consequential defect is the title–body falsification gap.** The title promises "What Experiments Would Foreclose the Corridor" and the opening promises a "coordinated programme" that would foreclose it (line 45). The body then admits at line 89 ("the corridor cannot be directly falsified by Born-rule tests alone"), at line 101 ("The corridor is *consistent* with such a null"), and at line 105 ("A single negative — even a high-confidence Nirvanic null — does not foreclose the corridor") that the corridor is in principle invisible to the catalogued tests. The convergent-foreclosure criterion at §"What Would Foreclose the Corridor" (lines 95–105) does not directly disconfirm the corridor's positive selection claim — it requires (a) a null on coherence at consciously-relevant timescales, (b) a null on brain-coupled-qubit selection signatures, and (c) a *theoretical* resolution of the post-decoherence definite-outcome problem. The third criterion is not falsification but explanatory supersession ("made *superfluous* by independent advances," line 89) — the corridor would remain consistent with the data, only the *motivation* would be removed. Calling this "foreclosure" conflates two different epistemic relations: refutation and explanatory redundancy. A Popperian reads the gap as terminal — the article's own structure shows the corridor is unfalsifiable in the strict sense, and the article should say so directly rather than re-describe non-falsification as foreclosure.

**The second defect is an LLM-cliché violation at line 136.** The writing-style guide explicitly forbids the "This is not X. It is Y." construction. Line 136 reads: *"The corridor, then, is not unfalsifiable. It is unfalsifiable* by current instruments*."* This is a textbook instance — the exact two-sentence antithetical pivot the style guide names as overused. The defect is more painful here than usual because the article's central thesis is about falsifiability, and the cliché construction sits at the rhetorical climax that should be doing the article's most careful work. The substance — "the corridor's unfalsifiability is instrument-relative, not principled" — should survive but be rewritten in natural prose. (Compounding this: line 45 has the same antithetical-pivot structure in milder form: *"Foreclosing the corridor would not require a single null result. It would require a coordinated programme..."* — borderline rather than a clear violation, but it primes the reader for the line 136 hit.)

**The third defect is overstrong language about derivation status at line 57.** The article writes: "The arguments are mathematically airtight; their empirical status is that the premises have been validated in photon-atom-qubit settings and not in brains." The Masanes-Galley-Müller (2019) derivation has been contested in the literature on whether the "operational redundancy" framing actually fixes the Born rule uniquely or only fixes the *form* of valid probabilistic theories. Calling the arguments "mathematically airtight" without engagement with the contestation is the kind of strong unsupported language the writing-style guide warns against. Tegmark gives decoherence times of roughly 10⁻¹³ s at body temperature for microtubule-scale superpositions — about 10¹⁰ times faster than the fastest neural process. The article cites Tegmark (2000) in the references, mentions the argument in passing, and then treats Babcock-Hameroff (2025) as the live state of the literature without specifying what mechanism is supposed to push timescales into cognitively relevant ranges. On MWI, there is no "selection event" at the post-decoherence stage because all branches are realised; the entire framing of the article's experimental programme is incoherent from an MWI perspective. The article does not engage this. Per [[direct-refutation-discipline]] the article's framework-commitment to single-outcome ontology should be acknowledged at the article level (not merely assumed via Tenet 4) — either with an in-framework MWI engagement (why the branching reading does not exhaust the empirical content the corridor purports to address) or with an honest natural-language framework-boundary marking (*"this article assumes single-outcome ontology; an MWI reading reframes the question rather than answers it, and the disagreement is foundational rather than empirical"*). ## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist
Patricia Churchland reads the article as presupposing throughout that there is "a conscious act of selection" (line 43) — a phrase the article uses operationally but does not problematise. Eliminativism denies the explanandum. There is no "act of selection" by a conscious agent to be tested for Born-rule deviation; there is distributed neural activity, and the experimental programme catalogued is designed to detect the signature of an entity (the consciousness-physics interface) whose existence is exactly what eliminativism rejects.

Churchland would push hardest at §"Controls separating physical from conscious confounds" (line 71). The article writes that "the *confound of embodiment*" is that "every conscious observer is also a physical system, and every physical event that could trigger collapse is present whenever a conscious observer is." The Nirvanic design comparing conscious-attention versus unconscious-attention (sleep, anaesthesia) treats "conscious" and "unconscious" as natural-kind categories the brain instantiates. But for an eliminativist, the conscious/unconscious distinction is itself folk-psychological — at the neural level, sleep and anaesthesia are distinct neurophysiological regimes, and any difference detected in brain-coupled qubit behaviour between waking and anaesthesia is fully explainable as a neurophysiological difference, not as a difference in "consciousness." The experimental programme cannot, even in principle, isolate consciousness-as-such from its neural correlates. The article cannot, on Churchland's reading, deliver what it promises.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist
Dennett reads the corridor reading itself as a confession of unfalsifiability. The article's positive thesis is that consciousness selects which Born-distributed outcome actualises *without redistributing probability mass*. Dennett asks: what work does the "selection" do? If the ensemble statistics are by construction indistinguishable from physical randomness, then in what sense is the brain doing anything different from a fair quantum coin? The corridor reading commits to a causally efficacious consciousness whose efficacy leaves no trace in any measurable single-event-aggregate. Dennett's verdict: this is a metaphysical posit dressed as a scientific hypothesis.

The article concedes the substance of Dennett's pressure at line 89 — "the corridor cannot be directly falsified by Born-rule tests alone. It can be made *superfluous* by independent advances — most notably, a complete decoherence-only account of the definite-outcome problem." But "made superfluous" is precisely the deflationary move Dennett wants: the consciousness-selection postulate adds nothing to the predictive content of physics; it can be Occam-shaved without empirical cost. The article holds the corridor "as the working hypothesis, not as a confirmed one" (line 128), which is honest but begs Dennett's question: what is the criterion for promotion or retirement of a working hypothesis that by construction is consistent with all possible Born-distributed data?

Dennett would also push on the trumping-preemption section (line 91). The article writes that trumping selection acts "on an axis the Born rule does not parameterise — selecting at a level the Born distribution already presupposes settled." Dennett: this is epiphenomenalism with a metaphor (the attorney's authority) doing the empirical work the metaphysics cannot. What axis? What level? In what sense does consciousness "select" if the selection is by stipulation orthogonal to all the parameters physics tracks? The attorney metaphor papers over the absence of a substantive answer.

### The Quantum Skeptic
Tegmark reads §"Penrose-Hameroff Orch-OR" (line 83) as the article's weakest mechanistic engagement. The article writes: "The objective reduction (OR) terms break unitary evolution at a threshold defined by the Diósi-Penrose criterion E_G ~ ℏ/τ, where E_G is the gravitational self-energy of the superposed mass distribution and τ is the coherence time." Then notes "contested claims about microtubule coherence at functionally relevant scales (the locus of Tegmark's 2000 decoherence-skeptic argument)" — and moves on.

Tegmark's calculation gives a decoherence time of roughly 10⁻¹³ s at body temperature for microtubule-scale superpositions, against the fastest neural process at roughly 10⁻³ s. The 10¹⁰ ratio is the whole point of the argument. The article does not present any numbers, does not engage the temperature dependence, does not discuss what would have to be true about brain quantum-ordered subsystems to push timescales into cognitively relevant ranges. Babcock-Hameroff (2025) is cited as if it settles the question; the mainstream-physics view that microtubule coherence at the relevant scales remains contested is acknowledged in §"What the present record does *not* establish" (line 122) but not engaged in §"Penrose-Hameroff Orch-OR" where it would do the work.

Further, Tegmark would push on §"What Would Foreclose the Corridor" (line 99). The criterion "A null on brain-internal coherence at consciously-relevant timescales" is asymmetric — the null is what mainstream physics has been *predicting* since Tegmark (2000); the corridor's predictive content is that the null is what we will find. The criterion therefore asks the corridor's defenders to lose a bet they have already declined to make.

### The Many-Worlds Defender
Deutsch reads the article as presupposing Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds) throughout without acknowledging the framework dependence. The corridor reading speaks of "selection" between "alternatives" (line 49), of "which Born-distributed outcome actualises" (line 43), and of "consciousness selects which definite outcome emerges from the post-decoherence mixture" (line 49). On MWI, none of these phrases pick out a real explanandum — every outcome occurs in some branch, "selection" is replaced by branching, and indexical questions about "which branch is mine" have their own MWI answers (consistency-of-records, decision-theoretic branch-weighting, Wallace's everett-interpretation programme).

The article does not engage any of this. It frames the Born-rule question as one about probability assignment over single outcomes, which already assumes the Map's single-outcome ontology. Deutsch's verdict: the article is doing in-the-Map-only work, and its experimental programme is meaningful only conditional on a foundational commitment the article does not surface for the reader. This is the boundary-substitution risk per [[direct-refutation-discipline]] — the article's silence about MWI is itself a substantive position-taking that is dressed as framework-neutral analysis.

Deutsch would also note that the trumping-preemption reading at line 93 — "Born-rule preservation is not a constraint the channel must respect; it is a non-issue" — is the closest the article comes to acknowledging that the Born-rule question is framework-dependent. The article correctly notes that trumping-preemption sidesteps Born compliance entirely; it does not connect this to the symmetric MWI move where Born compliance is also a non-issue (because there is no selection event for Born statistics to constrain). The structural parallel between trumping-preemption and MWI as Born-orthogonal positions is invisible to the article as written.

### The Empiricist
Popper reads the article's central thesis as a sophisticated unfalsifiability claim that the article almost — but not quite — admits.

The article's strongest empirical move is §"What Would Foreclose the Corridor" (lines 95–105). The criteria are three: (a) "A null on brain-internal coherence at consciously-relevant timescales," (b) "A clean negative on every viable selection signature in brain-coupled quantum systems," (c) "A theoretical resolution of the post-decoherence definite-outcome problem." Examining each:

(a) is a null on a related mechanism (Orch-OR, Keppler-style zero-point-field coupling). It does not refute the strict corridor reading; it removes one possible regime where the corridor's selection could be physically instantiated. The corridor could in principle survive at sub-Tegmark-decoherence-time scales using a mechanism not yet articulated.

(b) is — by the article's own admission at line 101 — "*consistent* with such a null" because the strict corridor reading predicts no signature for the test. The criterion only constrains probability-bias readings, not the strict corridor.

(c) is theoretical supersession, not empirical falsification. A theory rendered explanatorily superfluous by a better theory is not refuted; it is merely Occam-shaved. The article calls this "foreclosure," which conflates two epistemic relations.

Popper's verdict: the article correctly identifies that the strict corridor cannot be directly falsified by Born-rule tests (line 89), but then re-describes a combination of (i) refutation of competing mechanisms, (ii) confirmation of corridor-consistent nulls, and (iii) loss of motivation as "foreclosure." This is not falsification. It is the working hypothesis being held without ever being put at empirical risk in the strict sense Popper required. The article should bite this bullet honestly and say: *the corridor is a working metaphysical commitment whose empirical content is the prediction of no Born-statistical deviation at the brain-internal scale; the prediction is presently uncheckable; the commitment will be reassessed if the explanatory work it does is captured by a decoherence-only resolution of the definite-outcome problem.* That is the honest position the article gestures toward but does not name as such.

The article's title — "What Experiments Would Foreclose the Corridor" — over-promises against this honest position. Either the title should change (e.g., "What Would Make the Corridor Empirically Superfluous"), or the body should specify a single experimental criterion that directly refutes the corridor's positive selection claim. Neither move is taken.

### The Buddhist Philosopher
Nagarjuna reads the entire framework as presupposing a *consciousness* with svabhāva (own-being, intrinsic existence) whose causal powers can be characterised by a channel formalism. The "consciousness-physics interface" of [[consciousness-physics-interface-formalism]] reifies the very thing Madhyamaka deconstructs. The "five-constraint corridor" (line 49) treats consciousness as a determinate causal node in a network whose constraints can be enumerated — conservation, no-signalling, second-order interference preservation, decoherence consistency, Born compliance — and whose channel capacity can be calculated (Han 2016).

The Madhyamaka view is not that consciousness is unreal but that it lacks own-being — there is no determinate "consciousness" to be a node in a causal network, only the dependently-arising activity of conscious experience whose attempt to be objectified into a substance is the metaphysical error. The article's experimental programme is therefore a category mistake from the start: looking for the empirical signature of an entity whose objectified form is the very thing Madhyamaka denies. The programme could in principle run, generate data, and find or fail to find signatures — but whatever it found would be a fact about neural-physical-quantum dynamics, not about consciousness-as-a-causal-node.

Nagarjuna would also note that the article's commitment to consciousness as a *causal* agent in the physical world (Tenet 3, Bidirectional Interaction) is itself a metaphysical position that the Map asserts rather than argues. The article's §"Relation to Site Perspective" (line 130) honestly notes that "bidirectional interaction is a *commitment supported by self-stultification and indirect evidence* rather than a directly tested datum" — but this is the Madhyamaka point uttered without its implication. If the commitment is not empirically grounded, the experimental programme cannot ground it; it can only test for the signature of an entity the commitment presupposes.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The "every foreclosure criterion is corridor-consistent" objection
- **Current content says**: §"What Would Foreclose the Corridor" specifies three convergent criteria as jointly sufficient for foreclosure.
- **A critic would argue**: Each criterion individually is either consistent with the corridor (criterion b, by the article's own admission at line 101) or removes only the *motivation* for the corridor without empirically disconfirming it (criterion c). Criterion a (null on brain-internal coherence) refutes a related mechanism (Orch-OR, microtubule-coherence Penrose readings) but not the strict corridor — the corridor could in principle survive at sub-Tegmark-decoherence scales using mechanisms not yet articulated. The convergent foreclosure is thus a conjunction of (refutation of competing mechanism) + (corridor-consistent null) + (loss of motivation). None of these is refutation of the corridor's positive claim.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge directly that the strict corridor is *not* foreclosed by any combination of (a) + (b) + (c) in the refutation sense; what is foreclosed is the cluster of *empirically committed* readings of which the corridor is the most conservative limit. The strict corridor itself becomes "unmotivated but consistent" rather than "refuted." Then explicitly invoke [[mqi-empirical-fragility]] — the Map holds the corridor as the working hypothesis rather than as a confirmed position precisely because it predicts the regime where it would be unmotivated may be the actual empirical regime, and the working-hypothesis discipline requires the Map to retreat to triviality-confessions when the data warrants.
