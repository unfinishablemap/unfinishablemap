## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)

The psychophysical control law article would make Chalmers sit up. His 1996 framework distinguished supervenience laws (physical-to-phenomenal) from what he called "psychophysical laws" but never fully developed the downward direction. The Map's article fills that gap with engineering precision: three elements any control law must specify (input variables, output variables, mapping function), five constraints any adequate law must satisfy (bandwidth compatibility, conservation compliance, scope locality, phenomenological consistency, falsifiability), and three candidate mechanisms assessed against these requirements. Chalmers would recognise this as the specification problem his framework always needed.

The measurement standards article strikes at something Chalmers has long circled: why consciousness resists scientific integration. The article's insight — that the failure isn't merely practical but reflects the structural requirements of measurement (additivity for units, public causal effects for instruments, observer-independent references for calibration) — formalises what the explanatory gap means in philosophy of science terms. If you cannot in principle construct measurement standards for phenomenal quantities, irreducibility follows not from metaphysical argument but from the logic of measurement itself.

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)

The refined locality article would please Stapp particularly. The previous version relied on an ad hoc simulation hypothesis; the 2026-02-20 revision replaces it with the independently motivated observation that physics itself contains non-localities (quantum entanglement) that resist local explanation. Demanding strict locality of consciousness while tolerating non-locality in physics applies an inconsistent standard. This is exactly Stapp's style of argument: using physics' own features to create conceptual space for consciousness.

The control law article's treatment of Stapp's attention-rate mechanism as the "most developed account" — clear phenomenal property (effort), clear physical parameter (observation rate), clear mechanism (Zeno effect), testable predictions, and experimental evidence (though contested) — represents the fairest assessment of Stapp's framework in the Map's content. The honest engagement with Georgiev's (2015) challenge and the proposed response (consciousness biasing outcomes after decoherence, at measurement rather than during coherence) shows genuine scientific thinking rather than partisan cheerleading.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)

The phenomenology of cognitive load article captures something Nagel would immediately recognise: the first-person encounter with one's own limitations. The key phenomenological observations — the "thickening" as items accumulate, the "shaped absence" when items drop (you know what you lost even when you cannot access it), the qualitative distinction between cognitive load (spatial: running out of room) and fatigue (temporal: running out of energy) — these are precisely the kind of phenomenological distinctions that no functional description captures. A computer that exceeds buffer capacity simply fails. A conscious subject *experiences* the approaching limit.

The refined intersubjectivity article's emphasis on "relational specificity" — that contemplative traditions converge not just on categories but on dynamic relationships between states — would resonate with Nagel's insistence that subjectivity has structure, not merely presence. The revision transforms a claim about taxonomic convergence (easy to dismiss as shared cognitive architecture) into a claim about convergence on complex relational patterns (much harder to explain away).

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)

The refined russellian-monism article deploys Whitehead more effectively than before. The key insight — that Whitehead's actual occasions dissolve the quiddity problem by making intrinsic nature temporal and experiential rather than static — avoids crude substance dualism while preserving what process philosophy demands: genuine creativity in nature. The softened comparison between Russellian monism's bridging gap and the Map's ("potentially empirically tractable" rather than flatly "empirically tractable") shows the kind of honest self-assessment Whitehead valued.

The control law article's framing of consciousness as a controller — not a substance sitting alongside the brain but an ongoing process of observation, comparison, and signal output — is processual in spirit. The five control operations (gating, stabilisation, veto, attractor steering, resource allocation) describe what consciousness *does*, not what it *is*. Whitehead would approve: function first, substance questions second.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)

The deep-reviewed preemption article would excite Kane. Bradford Saad's delegatory dualism — the insight that consciousness *preempts* rather than *overdetermines* physical causes — answers the exclusion problem without requiring novel physics. The preempting cause takes over what the physical state would otherwise do, like a major's order displacing a sergeant's. The physical causal chain is not duplicated but redirected. This gives Kane's libertarian free will exactly the causal structure it needs: consciousness genuinely determines outcomes, not by adding a second cause but by displacing the default physical one.

The article's honest acknowledgment of observational closure — that preemption leaves no empirical trace distinguishing it from purely physical causation — is philosophically mature. Kane has always recognised that libertarian free will's evidence must be partly phenomenological (the felt experience of genuine authorship) and partly philosophical (the reflexive incoherence of determinism or epiphenomenalism). The preemption framework provides both.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)

The measurement standards article is the most sophisticated engagement with cognitive closure in the recent batch. Rather than citing McGinn's thesis directly, it arrives at a structurally parallel conclusion from a different starting point: the logic of measurement. If measurement requires additivity, public causal effects, and observer-independent reference points, and consciousness systematically lacks all three, then the measurement gap may be structural rather than temporary. This is cognitive closure arrived at through philosophy of science rather than philosophy of mind — an independent route to the same destination.

The cognitive load article's phenomenology of "shaped absence" — experiencing the form of what was lost without access to its content — would fascinate McGinn. It exemplifies what he calls the "paradox of introspective access": consciousness has privileged access to its own operations yet cannot fully explain what it finds there. The felt topology of one's own cognitive limits is simultaneously intimate and opaque.

## Content Strengths

### the-psychophysical-control-law.md (NEW)
- **Strongest point**: The specification gap framing — the control law is unspecified not from laziness but because no measurement theory exists for phenomenal states, creating a genuine conceptual asymmetry between the law's two sides.
- **Notable quote**: "A psychophysical control law would state precisely how phenomenal states map to physical selections. Without such a law, 'consciousness selects' remains a promissory note."
- **Why it works**: By importing control engineering vocabulary with explicit caveats about what the framing is not (not functionalism, not computationalism), the article makes abstract dualism operationally concrete. The "T-Shirt Problem" — that psychophysical laws might be irreducibly complex due to evolutionary contingency — shows genuine philosophical thinking about why specification is hard, not merely that it hasn't been done yet.

### consciousness-and-the-problem-of-measurement-standards.md (NEW)
- **Strongest point**: The proxy problem exposed. Consciousness science uses proxies (verbal reports, neural correlates) that are labelled "measurements" — but the label is borrowed, not deserved. Proxies indicate without measuring. This gap is not practical but principled.
- **Notable quote**: "If consciousness were reducible to physics, measurement standards should in principle be constructable; their persistent absence supports irreducibility."
- **Why it works**: The article arrives at dualism through philosophy of science rather than metaphysics, providing an independent argument that doesn't depend on thought experiments about zombies or Mary's Room. If you cannot build units, instruments, or calibration for phenomenal quantities, something is structurally different about consciousness.

### phenomenology-of-cognitive-load.md (NEW)
- **Strongest point**: The phenomenology of shaped absence — when items drop from working memory, you experience the *form* of what was lost even when the content is inaccessible. A computer that loses data simply lacks it. A conscious subject experiences the loss as structured.
- **Notable quote**: "Fatigue feels like diminishing energy — a progressive difficulty in sustaining effort. Cognitive load feels like diminishing space — a running out of room."
- **Why it works**: The careful distinctions (load vs. fatigue, load vs. effort, load vs. flow) ground abstract claims about irreducible phenomenology in immediately recognisable experience. The phenomenology tracks actual capacity reliably, supporting the claim that consciousness has privileged access to its own operational limits.

### preemption.md (DEEP-REVIEWED)
- **Strongest point**: The distinction between overdetermination (two sufficient causes) and preemption (one cause displaces another) resolves the exclusion problem cleanly. Consciousness doesn't duplicate the physical cause — it takes over.
- **Why it works**: Crystal-clear exposition of causal structure makes a previously obscure metaphysical manoeuvre accessible. The integration with agent causation and quantum selection shows three independent frameworks converging on the same model.

### russellian-monism.md (REFINED)
- **Strongest point**: The post-refinement honesty about the Map's own bridging gap — "potentially empirically tractable" rather than flatly "empirically tractable" — with explicit acknowledgment that the quantum mechanism faces contested decoherence objections.
- **Why it works**: By softening its strongest asymmetric claim, the article paradoxically becomes more persuasive. It no longer appears to be special-pleading against Russellian monism while granting its own framework immunity from similar objections.

### locality.md (REFINED)
- **Strongest point**: The 2026-02-20 rewrite replaced two flawed arguments (reifying physical law as a causal agent; unmotivated simulation hypothesis) with substantive engagement: physics itself contains non-localities (entanglement), and whether spacetime is fundamental remains contested in contemporary physics.
- **Why it works**: The article now builds on real physics and metaphysics rather than ad hoc moves. Demanding locality of consciousness while tolerating entanglement's non-locality applies an inconsistent standard — a genuinely strong philosophical point.

### intersubjectivity.md (REFINED)
- **Strongest point**: The relational specificity argument — contemplative traditions converge not just on categories (concentration vs. insight) but on dynamic relationships between states — is much harder to explain as shared neural architecture.
- **Why it works**: The revision carefully distinguishes what intersubjectivity establishes (phenomenological realism — necessary for dualism but not sufficient) from what it doesn't (dualism itself). This calibrated epistemic humility strengthens rather than weakens the overall argument.

- **Estimated scope**: Medium-long article (2000-2500 words)

#### The Interface Location Problem Revisited: Lessons from Recent Refinements

- **Estimated scope**: Medium article (1500-2000 words)

### Medium Priority

#### Consciousness and the Phenomenology of Precision
- **Builds on**: phenomenology-of-cognitive-load.md, consciousness-and-the-problem-of-measurement-standards.md, phenomenology-of-intellectual-effort.md
- **Would address**: The measurement standards article argues consciousness resists quantitative measurement. But consciousness is not entirely imprecise — we can reliably distinguish more and less intense pain, brighter and dimmer colours, louder and softer sounds. The phenomenology of *qualitative precision* (comparative judgements that work without numerical units) deserves treatment. What can consciousness measure about itself, and what does the boundary between measurable and unmeasurable reveal?
- **Estimated scope**: Medium article (1500-2000 words)
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism (qualitative precision differs structurally from quantitative measurement), Occam's Razor Has Limits (apparent imprecision may reflect the wrong measurement framework)

#### The Epistemology of Causal Preemption
- **Builds on**: preemption.md, mental-causation.md, consciousness-and-the-philosophy-of-explanation.md
- **Would address**: The preemption article acknowledges that preemption is observationally closed — empirically indistinguishable from purely physical causation. What are the epistemological consequences? If we cannot detect preemption empirically, what kind of evidence bears on whether it occurs? This touches debates in philosophy of science about underdetermination, inference to the best explanation, and the role of theoretical unification in choosing between empirically equivalent theories.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium article (1500-2000 words)
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits (empirical equivalence doesn't settle metaphysical questions), Bidirectional Interaction (the epistemological framework for the tenet's central claim)
