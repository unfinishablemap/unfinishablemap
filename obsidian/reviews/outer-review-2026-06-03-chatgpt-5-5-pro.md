## Executive verdict

The article has a strong and interesting central intuition: questions about when many things become one thing are naturally adjacent to questions about when many processes become one conscious subject. Its best contribution is not the claim that consciousness *settles* the Special Composition Question, but the weaker and more defensible claim that consciousness, unity, causal irreducibility, and individuation form a pressure point for standard physicalist mereology.

The article currently overstates the convergence. Van Inwagen, Merricks, and McQueen/Tsuchiya do not straightforwardly form "three of the most prominent answers" to the Special Composition Question that independently identify consciousness as the criterion of composition. A more accurate formulation would be: **three restricted-composition strategies, selected from a much wider field, place life, consciousness, causal irreducibility, or integrated information near the boundary between real composites and mere aggregates.** That is still philosophically useful, but much less decisive.

The article's evidence base is also too narrow for a 2026 live-literature page. It cites McQueen/Tsuchiya 2023, but it does not yet engage Bird's 2023 information-compression account, Beni's 2024/2025 thermodynamic-depth/FEP critique and alternative, IIT 4.0, recent IIT ontology debates, or the full import of the 2025 COGITATE results. Those omissions matter because the strongest current alternatives explain composition in terms of information, compression, thermodynamics, causal integration, biological self-maintenance, or functional organization without making consciousness metaphysically fundamental. [OUP Academic+2OUP Academic+2](https://academic.oup.com/pq/article/73/3/677/7111547)

My bottom-line assessment: **promising as a Map-internal convergence argument, not yet rigorous enough as an outer-facing article on contemporary mereology and consciousness.** It should be downgraded from "several prominent answers converge on consciousness" to "some restricted-composition proposals create pressure toward consciousness-adjacent criteria," then strengthened by confronting live rival accounts.

## 1. Audit of the core claims

### Claim 1: "Three prominent answers to the SCQ converge on consciousness"

This is the article's central load-bearing claim. It is rhetorically effective, but too compressed.

The contemporary literature does not treat "Van Inwagen, Merricks, McQueen/Tsuchiya" as the three dominant answers to the Special Composition Question. A more standard map distinguishes universalism, nihilism, restricted composition, brutal composition, composition-as-identity, and deflationary/metaontological responses. The 2020 PhilPapers Survey, for example, shows substantial support for restrictivism and universalism, with nihilism a minority but active option; the survey does not suggest that consciousness-centered restricted composition is the field's dominant axis. [PhilPapers Survey 2020+1](https://survey2020.philpeople.org/survey/results/5074) The IEP overview likewise treats universalism and nihilism as major positions partly because they avoid sorites-style vagueness problems for restricted composition. [Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy+1](https://iep.utm.edu/mat-comp/)

Van Inwagen is the weakest member of the alleged convergence. His criterion is life, not consciousness. The target article acknowledges this, saying consciousness enters indirectly because living organisms are the domain where consciousness occurs. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) But that indirect step needs much more care. Life includes non-conscious organisms, and consciousness may be possible in non-biological systems depending on one's views about AI, aliens, panpsychism, or functional organization. The article should not treat "life is where consciousness occurs" as enough to make Van Inwagen a consciousness criterion.

Merricks is the strongest member of the convergence. His view really does make conscious organisms central, and the article correctly notes that his case depends on controversial claims about consciousness, causal powers, and non-supervenience. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) But Merricks should not be presented as independent support unless the article foregrounds how much of the Map's dualism is already doing work. McQueen and Tsuchiya explicitly note that Merricks gives no experimental evidence for the premise that only conscious beings are causally irreducible and that his argument depends on strong dualism many philosophers reject. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article-abstract/2023/1/niad013/7189628)

McQueen/Tsuchiya are more complicated than the article allows. Their IIT-based proposal says composition occurs where integrated information is maximized or where elements make a non-zero integrated-information contribution relative to a complex. They present this as a formal restriction on composition, but it is not simply "consciousness is the criterion." It is closer to: **causal integration, as formalized by IIT, determines complexes; consciousness is then identified or correlated with such complexes depending on how one reads IIT.** [OUP Academic+1](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article-abstract/2023/1/niad013/7189628)

So the convergence is real only after abstraction. The three routes converge on something like **organized, causally unified, self-maintaining, or integrated systems**. Consciousness is one candidate marker of that unity, but not the only one.

### Claim 2: consciousness resists bottom-up and top-down composition

The article's non-compositionality section is plausible as a dualist argument sketch, but it currently moves too quickly from "we cannot see how experiences compose" to "consciousness may be the metaphysical boundary of composition."

The combination problem is a strong resource here. Panpsychists and constitutive physicalists face a real problem explaining how many micro-experiences, proto-experiences, or physical processes yield one subject. The surrounding `combination-problem` article already develops this point more directly. [concepts/combination-problem](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/combination-problem/) But the target article should distinguish at least three claims:

1. **Epistemic non-transparency:** we do not understand how experience composes.
2. **Conceptual non-compositionality:** the idea of subject-summing is incoherent.
3. **Metaphysical non-compositionality:** conscious subjects are not composed from physical parts at all.

The article often sounds as though it is arguing from 1 to 3. That is too fast. The argument needs an explicit bridge principle: why should explanatory opacity about subject-unity imply that consciousness marks genuine composition rather than merely marking a difficult emergent property, a functional organization, or a primitive higher-level law?

There is also a tension with the site's own `unity-of-consciousness` article. That article says experiences can be parts of larger experiences and treats phenomenal unity as involving a mereological relation among experiences. [concepts/unity-of-consciousness](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/unity-of-consciousness/) The target article says consciousness resists composition and decomposition. Those claims can be reconciled, but only if the Map distinguishes **phenomenal part-whole structure** from **composition out of independently existing physical or experiential subjects**. Without that distinction, the site risks saying both "experience has parts" and "experience cannot be composed."

### Claim 3: composition-as-identity cannot handle consciousness

The article's challenge to composition-as-identity is suggestive but underdeveloped. It argues that if a conscious organism is identical to its atoms, then the atoms would have to have phenomenal properties no atom possesses. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) That is a familiar and powerful worry, but a serious composition-as-identity defender will appeal to plural identity, collective predication, and ontological innocence. The IEP discussion of composition-as-identity emphasizes exactly these disputes around Leibniz's Law and whether the composite is anything "over and above" its parts. [Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/mat-comp/)

The target article does concede that collective predication may avoid a simple refutation. That concession is good. But the section stops where the hard work should begin. The article should ask whether "the atoms collectively are conscious" is an intelligible plural predicate, whether it merely redescribes emergence, and whether the unity of a first-person subject is the sort of property that can be collectively predicated without a further subject.

## 2. Adequacy of evidence and citations

The target article's bibliography is adequate for a short Map-internal note, but not for the outer-facing claim it makes. It includes canonical references to Van Inwagen, Merricks, Chalmers, Bayne/Chalmers, Hasker, and composition-as-identity; it also includes McQueen/Tsuchiya 2023. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) But the live 2020s literature is doing more than the article reflects.

Most importantly, McQueen/Tsuchiya's own paper is more cautious than the target article's summary. The target article says IIT provides a "precise, measurable threshold" for composition. McQueen/Tsuchiya do offer formal precision, but they also say exact Φ computation for realistic biological systems is infeasible and that continuous systems pose a major unresolved challenge. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article-abstract/2023/1/niad013/7189628) The target should therefore say **formally precise, but not presently operationally measurable in realistic systems**.

The article's discussion of IIT also needs the current IIT 4.0 literature. IIT 4.0 presents itself as giving mathematical conditions for the physical substrate and quality of consciousness, with axioms including intrinsic existence, specificity, unity, definiteness, and structure/composition. [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article) That matters because IIT is not naturally a dualist ally. IIT often aims at an explanatory identity between physical causal structure and experience. If the Map wants to use IIT as evidence for a dualist composition thesis, it must explicitly say what it accepts from IIT and what it rejects.

The article also needs Leung and Tsuchiya's distinction between strong IIT and weaker IIT-inspired measures. They warn that once one drops the core theoretical framework, the resulting measures may no longer be IIT in the relevant sense. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article/2023/1/niad012/7169163) This directly bears on the article's fallback claim that "the structural insight survives whether or not Φ is the right measure." That may be true, but it cannot be borrowed from IIT without a new argument.

The COGITATE sentence is also too one-sided. The target article says the 2025 results partially corroborated posterior-cortex predictions. That is true only in a narrow sense. The Nature paper says the results aligned with some predictions but substantially challenged key tenets of both IIT and GNWT; for IIT specifically, the lack of sustained synchronization within posterior cortex contradicted a key connectivity claim. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08888-1) The article should revise this to: **COGITATE gave mixed results: some posterior-content findings were IIT-friendly, but other results challenged IIT's predicted sustained posterior connectivity.**

The 2023 anti-IIT open letter is also summarized too bluntly. The target says critics argued IIT was untestable in principle. The available letter text says the cited experiments tested only idiosyncratic predictions, not IIT's core claims, and argues that until the theory as a whole is meaningfully empirically testable, the pseudoscience label is appropriate. [Brain and Cognitive Science](https://nkonstantinou.com/publication/fleming-psyarxiv-2023/Fleming-PsyArxiv-2023.pdf) Nature reported the controversy as a statement signed by 124 scholars. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-023-02971-1) The target should not paraphrase this as a simple "untestable in principle" claim unless it cites a source that uses that exact stronger formulation.

## 3. Strongest counterarguments not yet covered

The article's counterargument coverage is its weakest feature. It acknowledges a selection effect, and it concedes that the dualist reading is not forced. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) But it does not engage the strongest live rivals.

First, **universalism** remains under-addressed. A universalist can agree that conscious organisms are important while denying that consciousness has anything to do with whether composition occurs. On universalism, tables, trout, storms, brains, arbitrary scattered objects, and conscious organisms all compose something. Consciousness then marks a special kind of composite, not the boundary of composition itself. The target article needs an argument that universalism cannot treat consciousness as a higher-level feature of some fusions.

Second, **nihilism** is treated as though it obviously undermines subjects with causal powers. But nihilists often paraphrase ordinary object-talk in terms of simples arranged tablewise, organismwise, or personwise. The article needs to explain why "simples arranged personwise instantiate consciousness" is worse than "a composite person instantiates consciousness." The IEP overview notes that nihilism has developed responses to common-sense objections and is not defeated simply by saying there are no ordinary objects. [Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/mat-comp/)

Third, **brutal composition** is missing. A brutalist can say there is no informative nontrivial answer to the Special Composition Question: some things compose something, and that is a brute fact. This is especially relevant because the Map argues consciousness supplies a non-arbitrary boundary. The brutalist denies that such a boundary must exist.

Fourth, the article misses **recent naturalistic restricted-composition proposals**. Bird's 2023 account treats composite objects as information-compressing patterns: a composite exists where describing the whole compresses information about its parts. [OUP Academic+1](https://academic.oup.com/pq/article/73/3/677/7111547) Beni's 2024/2025 critique argues that Bird's compression account needs constraints and proposes thermodynamic depth/free-energy-style functional integration as a more objective restriction. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/pq/advance-article/doi/10.1093/pq/pqae019/7612581) These are direct rivals because they explain non-arbitrary composition without making consciousness fundamental.

Fifth, the article should engage **biological self-maintenance and free-energy-style organismal unity** as a rival to dualism. Beni's alternative is especially important because it partly vindicates Van Inwagen-style organismal restriction while naturalizing it through thermodynamics rather than consciousness. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/pq/advance-article/doi/10.1093/pq/pqae019/7612581) That is a stronger opponent than generic "physicalism."

Sixth, the article should address **IIT's own ontology and exclusion debates**. Recent work on IIT's exclusion/definiteness and causal ontology raises philosophical problems directly relevant to composition. Moon's 2025 paper, for example, examines IIT's exclusion axiom/postulate and broader axiom-to-postulate translation. [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-025-05069-5) If the article leans on IIT, it should not treat Φ-maximization as a settled compositional criterion.

## 4. Tenet-protective bracketing

The article is admirably transparent that the dualist reading is not forced. But several moves still look tenet-protective.

The first is the move from McQueen/Tsuchiya to "whatever consciousness tracks." The article takes support from IIT when IIT appears to make consciousness composition-relevant, but it then insulates the argument from IIT's empirical and theoretical vulnerabilities by saying the structural insight survives even if Φ is wrong. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) This is possible, but it needs a separate argument. Otherwise the article keeps the rhetorical benefit of IIT while avoiding the cost of defending IIT.

The second is the "No Many Worlds" paragraph. The article says that in many-worlds, every possible composition obtains in some branch, trivializing the Special Composition Question. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) This is not established. An Everettian can still ask the composition question branch-relative: given the physical ontology in a branch, when do the branch's entities compose a further object? Many-worlds may complicate personal identity and actuality, but it does not obviously trivialize mereology. This paragraph should be removed or rewritten as a speculative Map-internal concern rather than a consequence of the composition argument.

The third is the Occam paragraph. The article says universalism and nihilism are simpler but fail to account for the distinctive status of conscious beings. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) That is not yet an argument against them. Universalists can say conscious beings are distinctive fusions; nihilists can say personwise-arranged simples are distinctive. The Map needs to show that consciousness requires a composite subject, not merely that consciousness is metaphysically important.

The fourth is the relation to minimal quantum interaction. The article suggests that if consciousness is over and above physical parts, MQI supplies the natural interface by biasing quantum indeterminacies. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) But the composition argument does not by itself support quantum interaction. That connection should be labelled as a downstream Map hypothesis, not a result of the article.

## 5. Integration with surrounding articles

The article is well integrated in one respect: it sits in a coherent local cluster. `Composition and Consciousness` explicitly presents the broader concept and points readers back to the target article for the extended case. [concepts/composition-and-consciousness](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/composition-and-consciousness/) `Consciousness and the Metaphysics of Individuation` also uses the composition problem correctly: if consciousness makes composites real, something must individuate which composite this consciousness constitutes. [topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation/) That is a strong internal bridge.

The integration is weaker where concepts overlap. `Composition and Consciousness` now repeats much of the target article's dialectic, including Van Inwagen/Merricks/McQueen, the combination problem, and the relation to the Map's tenets. [concepts/composition-and-consciousness](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/composition-and-consciousness/) After the 2026-06-01 coalescence, the site should decide whether the target article is the long-form argumentative treatment and the concept page is the navigational primer, or whether both are meant to carry independent arguments. At the moment they are partly duplicative.

There is also a tension with `Unity of Consciousness`, which treats experiences as parts of larger experiences, while the target article leans on phenomenal non-compositionality. [concepts/unity-of-consciousness](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/unity-of-consciousness/) This is fixable, but the site needs a shared vocabulary: phenomenal parts, access-unity parts, causal parts, and subject-constituting parts should not be run together.

The `Binding Problem` and `Quantum Holism and Phenomenal Unity` articles amplify the target article's anti-classical-composition line. [concepts/binding-problem](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/binding-problem/) That helps the Map's internal coherence, but it also increases the need for caution. Claims like "classical mechanisms coordinate but do not unify" and "physical properties compose transparently" need to be stated as philosophical theses, not as settled results. Contemporary physics, information theory, and complex-systems work are precisely where the strongest naturalistic rivals now live.
