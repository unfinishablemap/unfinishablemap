## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The whole article rests on taking first-person RED reports as *data about awareness during arrest*. But a report obtained minutes-to-days post-resuscitation is data about a memory formed during recovery, full stop. The article concedes this in the illusionist section, then quietly reverts to "people report heightened experiences *during* severe cerebral ischemia" (lead, line 39) — smuggling the contested temporal claim back in as established. The honest statement is: people *later report* experiences they *attribute* to the arrest period. The article's own AWARE II numbers (zero visual hits, one auditory hit out of 28) are exactly what confabulation-during-recovery predicts.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The "paradox of heightened experience" is the article's load-bearing argument, and it is built on introspective testimony ("more real than real," "thinking more clearly than ever") taken at face value. Heterophenomenology says: that is a report of how it *seemed*, not a measurement of cognitive function. There is no objective measure of NDE "clarity" — no task performance, no reaction time, nothing but the subject's retrospective superlative. An impaired brain producing a *feeling of* enhanced clarity is mundane (cf. the confident certainty of dreams and ketamine states). The article never distinguishes "reported phenomenal vividness" from "actual cognitive enhancement," and the paradox dissolves once you do.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
The MQI tenet paragraph (line 150) is, refreshingly, on my side — it concedes dying tissue is *more* decoherent, not less, and flags the quantum-interface idea as "frank speculation with no mechanism." Good. No complaint here; this is how the rest of the corpus should handle MQI.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
The "haecceitistic certainty" rebuttal (line 148) just restates the intuition it needs to defend. "I was there, I came back" is exactly the indexical report MWI says every branch-copy produces. Asserting the certainty "resists reinterpretation" is not an argument against the reinterpretation; it is a refusal of it. This is honest framework-boundary marking dressed as a refutation — a mild boundary-substitution.

### The Empiricist (Popper's ghost)
Credit where due: the "What Would Challenge This View?" section gives four concrete falsifiers, and the asymmetry section openly admits the constraining-mechanism/interface-channel distinction "risks becoming unfalsifiable on this point." That is unusually disciplined. The residual problem: the filter framing keeps *both* outcomes — enhancement (NDEs/terminal lucidity) and diminishment (ordinary brain damage) — as confirmations, which is the unfalsifiability the section warns about, not yet cured by the warning.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The article leans on persisting personal transformation and "the specific experiencer through their specific life" (line 148) as evidence of a genuine, continuous self that survived the interface disruption. From emptiness, the persistence of a *narrative* of transformation is not the persistence of a *self*; it is karmic continuity of conditioned processes. The Map's reading reifies exactly what the NDE could be read as dissolving.

## Critical Issues

(No critical-severity issues. The most actionable items are medium.)

### Issue 1: Lead reasserts "during arrest" awareness as established
- **File**: `concepts/near-death-experiences.md`
- **Location**: Lead paragraph, line 39 ("people report heightened, coherent experiences during severe cerebral ischemia") and line 56 ("subjects recall awareness during documented cardiac arrest").
- **Problem**: The article's own illusionist section (line 100) grants that memory consolidation during recovery could construct the narratives without awareness during arrest. The lead and the RED section nonetheless state the during-arrest timing as fact. This is the epistemic/metaphysical equivocation pattern in temporal form: the *epistemic* fact is "subjects later report experiences they attribute to the arrest period"; the *stronger* claim "experiences occurred during arrest" is what the argument needs and is exactly what is contested.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: In a `refine-draft` pass, hedge the lead and line 56 to "recall, on returning, vivid experiences they place during the arrest period" — and let the gamma-surge / timing-uncertainty discussion carry the contested timing claim where it is already handled honestly.

### Issue 2: Introspective-reliability argument overreaches
- **File**: `concepts/near-death-experiences.md`
- **Location**: Line 102 ("NDE introspective reports show characteristics of reliable phenomenal access: cross-cultural commonalities ... resistance to suggestion, and transformation effects persisting for decades").
- **Problem**: None of the three listed properties is a marker of *accurate phenomenal access*. Cross-cultural commonality is equally predicted by shared neurophysiology of dying (the article itself lists hypoxia, REM intrusion, temporal-lobe effects as universal mechanisms). Durable transformation shows the memory was *salient and emotionally potent*, not that it was *veridical* — false memories can be life-altering. "Resistance to suggestion" is asserted without citation. This is a cluster of unsupported claims doing rebuttal work.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Downgrade to: these features make pure on-the-spot fabrication less likely, but do not establish that the reports track awareness *during* arrest; cite a source for the suggestion-resistance claim or drop it.

### Issue 3: "Representative sample" framing of Van Lommel
- **File**: `concepts/near-death-experiences.md`
- **Location**: Line 144 ("18% in Van Lommel's representative sample") and the lead's contrast with AWARE II's "self-selected" subset.
- **Problem**: Van Lommel's 18% is among cardiac-arrest *survivors* — already a heavily selected population (those resuscitated, those who recalled and chose to report). Calling it "representative" overstates it relative to the (correctly) hedged AWARE II figure, creating a false asymmetry that flatters the older, headline number.
- **Severity**: Low
- **Recommendation**: Replace "representative sample" with "larger prospective survivor cohort" — accurate without the rhetorical lift.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The heightened-experience paradox depends on unmeasured "clarity"
- **Current content says**: Degraded brain function "should produce degraded experience," yet NDEs show enhanced clarity — paradoxical for production models (lines 108–112).
- **A critic would argue**: "Clarity" here is a retrospective phenomenal report, not a measured cognitive capacity. A degraded brain reliably *generates feelings of* certainty and vividness (dreams, deliria, dissociatives). There is no paradox between impaired function and a strong *sense* of clarity.
- **Suggested response**: Add one sentence distinguishing reported phenomenal vividness from measured cognitive performance, and concede that the paradox bites only if the reported clarity tracks genuine cognitive function — which current data cannot establish. This *strengthens* the article by pre-empting the strongest deflationary move.

### One auditory "hit" undercuts the clean veridical-failure story
- **Current content says**: "No patient has identified a hidden image in controlled conditions" (line 90); separately, "Only 1/28 identified the auditory stimulus" (line 76).
- **A critic would argue**: The article treats the visual-target null as the headline and tucks the single auditory identification into a bullet without saying whether it was a genuine veridical hit or a chance/expectancy artefact. A skeptic and a believer will both seize on the 1/28 — the article should adjudicate it.
- **Suggested response**: One clause on what the single auditory identification means (the AWARE II authors did not treat it as confirmed veridical perception) so the number isn't left as an unexplained loose end.
