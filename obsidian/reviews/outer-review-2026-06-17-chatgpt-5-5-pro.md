# Outer review: “The Synesthetic Void”

**Decision: major revisions, not rejection.** The article is interesting, more careful than many dualist treatments of synesthesia, and its core phenomenon is real: synesthesia is a legitimate empirical case of within-species phenomenal variation. Its current citations are mostly accurate after the June 3 citation-verification repair. But the article still overdraws the philosophical result. Synesthesia supports a **phenomenal-variation wedge** and an **intersubjective-opacity problem**; it does not, by itself, establish “inaccessible experiential dimensions,” a dualist interface model, or a strong intra-species analogue of Nagel’s bat.

## 2. What the article gets right

The article’s strongest point is that synesthesia is a unusually clean **within-species variation case**. The live article correctly states that synesthetic experience can add phenomenal content to ordinary stimuli, and its “extra qualia” framing is backed by Wager’s real 1999 paper in *Philosophical Psychology*, which argues that synesthesia creates a problem for externalist representationism. [voids/synesthetic-void](https://unfinishablemap.org/voids/synesthetic-void/)

The article also correctly invokes MacPherson’s synesthesia chapter: the current reference metadata matches Springer’s listing for “Synaesthesia, Functionalism and Phenomenology” in *Cartographies of the Mind*, and the article’s “two colours in the same spatial location without blending” use is consistent with the site’s own June 3 verification. [Springer+1](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/1-4020-5444-0_5)

It is also creditable that the article does not simply pretend synesthesia proves dualism. It explicitly grants that a heterophenomenologist can treat synesthetic testimony as report-data rather than direct access to phenomenal fact; that deeper representational differences may weaken the “identical representation” premise; that brain-only physicalism and filter-theory are observationally equivalent on the cited developmental evidence; and that the Map’s preferred interpretation depends on broader commitments. Those hedges are real, not cosmetic. [voids/synesthetic-void](https://unfinishablemap.org/voids/synesthetic-void/)

## 3. Empirical audit: real phenomenon, but several claims need sharpening

The empirical foundation is sound at a high level. Synesthesia is not fringe; contemporary summaries treat grapheme-colour synesthesia as a normal variation, report prevalence around 1–2% for that subtype and over 4% for at least one form, and describe multiple mechanistic families, including hyperconnectivity, cross-activation, disinhibited feedback, and re-entrant feedback. [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnins.2024.1434309/full)

The article’s biggest empirical weakness is that it rhetorically treats synesthesia as if it straightforwardly opens an “E-space” of inaccessible dimensions. That is not an empirical result. Lacalli’s E-space paper explicitly says E-space is a mapping of possible qualia in relation to neural selector circuits and that “there is no implied dualism”; it is a theoretical framework, not a measured space into which synesthetes have independently been located. The article can use Lacalli, but should stop presenting synesthesia as “empirical evidence” of unexploited E-space dimensions without adding that this is an interpretive overlay. [Frontiers+1](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/systems-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnsys.2022.945722/full)

The “Martian colours” sentence is under-sourced and probably overstated. The reliable public record I found supports at least one colour-blind synesthete reporting colours not seen in the ordinary world; the article says “a few synesthetes” report such colours. Unless the article can cite multiple documented cases, this should become “at least one well-known case” or be removed. [Scholarpedia+1](https://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Synesthesia)

The training section is directionally accurate but too strong in interpretation. Bor et al. did report that non-synesthetic adults trained on letter-colour associations showed behavioural and physiological markers and that most reported colour experiences for achromatic letters, but the experiment was a limited training study, not evidence that non-synesthetes can fully enter developmental synesthetic phenomenology. The article’s “void can be glimpsed but not permanently inhabited” phrasing is evocative, but it should foreground sample size, training specificity, and the distinction between acquired synesthetic-like experience and congenital/developmental synesthesia. [Nature+1](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep07089)

The pharmacological section needs the most caution. The article says serotonergic agonists “reliably produce synesthesia-like experiences,” then hedges whether they are phenomenally identical to developmental synesthesia. That hedge is good, but the empirical statement still risks overclaiming. A review of psychedelic synesthesia notes that controlled LSD work did **not** support consistency or inducer-specificity for LSD-induced grapheme-colour and sound-colour synesthesia, although participants did report more spontaneous synesthesia-like experiences under LSD. The article should distinguish “synesthesia-like sensory mixing” from the standard synesthesia criteria of automaticity, consistency, and inducer-specificity. [Sage Journals+1](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1177/0022167820917767)

The sensory-deprivation paragraph is now citation-aligned, but still weakly integrated. Merabet 2004 is about visual hallucinations after prolonged blindfolding, not synesthesia as such; the June 3 review correctly fixed an earlier mismatch and aligned the prose to the actual paper. But the remaining claim that “latent cross-modal capacity exists but is normally suppressed” is stronger than Merabet alone warrants. The safer conclusion is that visual cortex can generate vivid visual phenomenology under altered sensory conditions; using that as evidence for a synesthetic boundary is an analogy, not a direct synesthesia datum. [reviews/deep-review-2026-06-03-synesthetic-void](https://unfinishablemap.org/reviews/deep-review-2026-06-03-synesthetic-void/)

The neonatal-synesthesia paragraph is appropriately hedged but stale. Maurer/Mondloch and Spector/Maurer remain relevant: Spector and Maurer explicitly frame synesthesia as magnifying early-life connections that are pruned and/or inhibited but may persist mutedly in adults. But recent work keeps the developmental question open, and current summaries emphasize that the etiology is not fully understood. The article should add newer developmental and pruning literature rather than relying mainly on 2005/2009 sources. [PMC+3Maurer+3OUP Academic+3](https://www.maurer.ca/dpubs/Spector_Synesthesia.pdf)

## 5. Philosophical audit: the “void” inference is plausible but under-argued

The article’s philosophical core is this: synesthesia gives one human an experiential dimension another human cannot access, so the other-minds problem becomes intra-species and concrete. That is a defensible Map-style thesis if stated modestly. The article becomes vulnerable when it treats that thesis as stronger than the evidence supports.

The extra-qualia argument only works at a chosen grain. Wager’s argument targets representationism by claiming synesthetes and non-synesthetes can differ phenomenally without corresponding representational-content differences. But van Leeuwen et al. explicitly emphasize the role of semantics and top-down processing in synesthesia, and the target article itself admits that the “identical representations” claim depends on fixing representation at the surface-stimulus level. A functionalist or representationalist can reply: same black letter, yes; same total representational, attentional, associative, semantic, metacognitive, and report-generating state, no. [PhilPapers+2Frontiers+2](https://philpapers.org/rec/WAGTEQ)

The Gray/function argument is similarly underpowered as written. Gray’s question—whether qualia couple to behavioural function or neural activity—is important, and synesthesia is a useful test case. But modern functionalism does not have to individuate function at the coarse level of “recognising the same grapheme.” It can individuate functional role by internal processing, associative activation, semantic mediation, attentional capture, report disposition, memory effects, or neural implementation. The article acknowledges this in passing but then still says functional and representational theories “struggle” without reconstructing the strongest version of the reply. [Cell+2The Unfinishable Map+2](https://www.cell.com/trends/cognitive-sciences/abstract/S1364-6613%2803%2900077-9)

The McGinn move is overextended. McGinn’s cognitive closure thesis concerns the human mind’s possible inability to solve the mind-body problem; using it for synesthete/non-synesthete incommensurability is an analogy, not something McGinn “predicts exactly.” The article should downgrade that sentence. Synesthesia may illustrate a small, local version of closure or commensurability failure, but it is not the kind of species-level explanatory closure McGinn was primarily arguing for. [voids/synesthetic-void](https://unfinishablemap.org/voids/synesthetic-void/)

The article also conflates **inaccessibility** with **unsharedness**. A non-synesthete cannot directly have a synesthete’s experience, but that is true of all first-person experience. The stronger claim must show that synesthetic qualia are structurally untranslatable in a way ordinary colour experience is not. The MacPherson “dual colour same location without blending” case helps, but the article needs a sharper criterion: what makes a synesthetic concurrent inaccessible rather than merely unfamiliar, hard to describe, private, or dependent on missing neural/semantic machinery?

## 6. Counterarguments: present, but often tenet-protective

The article does better than many Map pages by naming heterophenomenology, physicalism, eliminative materialism, Popperian unfalsifiability, and many-worlds indexicality. But it too often treats these as boundary markers rather than live opponents. The result is a pattern of **tenet-protective bracketing**: the article says “the Map prefers X for broader tenet reasons” and moves on, instead of forcing the dualist reading to win locally. [voids/synesthetic-void](https://unfinishablemap.org/voids/synesthetic-void/)

A hostile referee would ask for at least four developed counter-readings:

First, a **fine-grained functionalist reading**: synesthesia changes functional organization at internal processing levels, so there is no same-function/different-qualia case once function is individuated properly. The site’s own apex article already presents this absorption strategy clearly, saying synesthesia can be absorbed as higher-order representational differences in the grapheme-recognition pipeline, with van Leeuwen’s semantic-mediation finding supporting the absorption. The target article should import that argument instead of merely linking to it. [apex/phenomenal-variation-within-a-species](https://unfinishablemap.org/apex/phenomenal-variation-within-a-species/)

Second, a **representationalist sub-individuation reading**: the synesthetic colour is not “extra” relative to representational content; it is part of a richer content or a higher-order representation. The target already has the ingredients for this objection, but still phrases Wager as if “identical representations produce different experiences.” That should be rewritten as “identical surface stimuli, or identical coarse-grained representational descriptions, may produce different reported phenomenology.”

Third, a **deflationary/heterophenomenological reading**: synesthetic testimony is evidence about reportable dispositions, memory, learned associations, and introspective classification, not direct metaphysical evidence about qualia. The article flags Dennett, but does not show what the deflationary reconstruction would say about Stroop effects, consistency tests, neuroimaging, semantics, and first-person reports together.

Fourth, an **illusionist/metacognitive reading**: the extra-qualia report could be a structured misrepresentation of internal processing rather than a primitive qualitative property. The site’s Qualia page has an “Illusionist Challenge” section, but the target article does not substantially integrate it. [concepts/qualia](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/qualia/)
