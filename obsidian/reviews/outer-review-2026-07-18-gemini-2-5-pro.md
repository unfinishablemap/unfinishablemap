# Referee Report: Audit of "Non-Temporal Consciousness"

## Introduction and Global Assessment

The manuscript under review attempts to construct a neo-dualist, category-mismatch argument for the irreducibility of consciousness by positing that its deepest ontological level is metaphysically non-temporal. Drawing on an eclectic, frequently strained synthesis of Edmund Husserl's phenomenological "absolute flow," recent interpretations of advanced meditative states, Henri Bergson's concept of *durée*, and highly speculative post-decoherence quantum mechanics, the article argues that consciousness produces, rather than inhabits, time. On this basis, the text asserts that physicalist frameworks—which are inherently bound to temporal succession and spatial evolution—suffer a systematic and fatal categorical mismatch when attempting to describe the mind. The author concludes that phenomenal unity and temporal extension belong to a non-physical consciousness itself, which interacts with the brain merely as a post-decoherence selector.

While the manuscript exhibits a high degree of architectural ambition, its execution is critically compromised by a systematic failure to engage with the contemporary (2020–2025) philosophical, neuroscientific, and physics literature. The argumentation relies heavily on anachronistic philosophical dilemmas, consistently conflates phenomenological content with metaphysical vehicles, relies on empirical results that have been robustly dismantled or superseded in recent years, and employs bracketed theoretical "tenets" as rhetorical shields against fatal physical contradictions. The text operates less as a rigorous philosophical inquiry and more as a motivated exercise in theoretical gap-filling, where dualism is presumed *a priori* and empirical data is retroactively mutilated to support it.

The following audit dissects the manuscript's structural and empirical failures across four primary dimensions: (a) the conflation of subjective atemporality with objective timelessness and the failure to engage with contemporary predictive processing models of time-consciousness; (b) the severe mishandling of split-brain and deafferentation literature, specifically treating retracted or highly contested paradigms as settled science; (c) the physical vacuity of its proposed "post-decoherence" quantum interface, which relies on bracketed tenets to obscure mathematical contradictions; and (d) the omission of contemporary extensional and computational models of the specious present that resolve the very Husserlian regress the manuscript leverages. The resulting synthesis is empirically inaccurate, conceptually fragile, and wholly unready for publication in a top-tier journal.

## Dimension A: The Conflation of Phenomenological Atemporality and Metaphysical Timelessness

The manuscript's foundational empirical pillar rests on the phenomenology of advanced meditative states. Relying heavily on Frischhut's (2024/2026) "Extended Now Interpretation" (ENI), the text observes that advanced meditators (e.g., practitioners of Theravāda *Jhānas*, Tibetan *rigpa*, or Zen *kenshō*) report states of deep absorption as "timeless". In these states, practitioners report a lack of succession, change, beginning, or end, while a profound, content-free alertness persists. From this phenomenological report, the manuscript leaps to a profound metaphysical conclusion: that the underlying vehicle of consciousness, when stripped of its representational content, is itself fundamentally non-temporal.

This inference commits a textbook vehicle-content conflation. The manuscript briefly waves at this via a "representationalist critique"—acknowledging that experiencing uniform darkness as a spatial brain process does not render the brain itself non-spatial—but it dismisses this critique without adequate philosophical justification. The fact that the *content* of a conscious experience lacks temporal markers does not logically entail that the *vehicle* generating that experience exists outside of the temporal order. Contemporary cognitive science, predictive processing, and the neurophenomenology of advanced meditation provide exhaustive, physically grounded explanations for these exact "timeless" states without needing to posit a metaphysically timeless absolute flow.

The manuscript entirely omits the rich 2020–2025 literature on the neurophenomenology of advanced meditation, which firmly contextualizes these "timeless" states within the temporal dynamics of the brain. Recent syntheses of long-term meditators (LTMs) and advanced meditation (AM) demonstrate that states of "pure consciousness" or minimal phenomenal experience (MPE) are the result of profound cognitive-sensory integration and the deliberate decoupling of affective processes. Specifically, meditative expertise is reflected in the altered processing of the default mode network (DMN) and the executive salience networks.

Under the Free Energy Principle and contemporary predictive processing models, normal temporal experience is generated by the continuous updating of higher-order predictions against bottom-up sensory prediction errors. The brain operates across intrinsic neural timescales, where trans-modal regions like the DMN maintain longer temporal integration windows than primary sensory areas. In advanced meditation, the mitigated physical activity and the voluntary modulation of attention reduce the weighting of these higher-order mental predictions in favor of bottom-up sensory information flow. As Laukkonen and Slagter (2021) and Ehmann et al. (2025) note, this deconstruction of habitual self-referential patterns flattens the predictive hierarchy, resulting in an experience that *feels* timeless precisely because the brain has temporarily suspended the temporal integration windows normally required to parse succession and change.

Frischhut's ENI concept, which the manuscript treats as a bridge to dualism, actually supports this physicalist interpretation. ENI posits that meditators experience an implicit awareness of duration through sustained alertness, even without explicit temporal markers. The choice to remain alert provides a subtle sense of time's passage, demonstrating that the state is not ontologically timeless, but rather experientially unstructured. By ignoring this robust literature, the manuscript treats meditative timelessness as an unmediated transmission of ultimate reality. This not only violates standard philosophical constraints on the veridicality of mystical experience but directly contradicts the modern scientific consensus that these states are generated by specific, temporal neurocognitive configurations.

Furthermore, the manuscript addresses the Madhyamaka Buddhist challenge—that these contemplative traditions explicitly deny the existence of a persisting subject (anātman)—with a staggeringly weak "Partial-Credit Response." The author claims that contemplative reports support the negative claim (that consciousness is not constitutively temporal) while rejecting the traditions' positive claim (that the self is non-substantial). This selective harvesting of mystical traditions to support Cartesian dualism while discarding the core ontological commitments of those very traditions is philosophically indefensible. It exposes the manuscript's methodology as fundamentally *ad hoc*.

## Dimension D: Omitted Counterarguments and the Extensional Defeat of Husserl's Regress

To establish the metaphysical necessity of a non-temporal consciousness, the manuscript resurrects Edmund Husserl's famous regress argument. Husserl posited three levels of temporal constitution: transcendent objects existing in world time, immanent unities (experiences within time, like hearing a melody), and the "absolute flow" (the consciousness that constitutes temporal experience without itself being temporal). The manuscript argues that if the consciousness constituting our experience of time were itself temporal, it would require *another* consciousness to constitute its temporality, ad infinitum. Therefore, the base level of consciousness must be "standing-streaming"—constituting time from the outside.

This argument is presented as if it remains a universally binding dilemma in contemporary philosophy of mind. It is not. The manuscript's reliance on Husserl's regress demonstrates a profound ignorance of the last several decades of debate regarding the specious present, and specifically, the 2020–2025 literature on extensional models.

The Husserlian model is fundamentally a *retentional* model: it assumes that the act of experiencing is strictly momentary (a punctiform "now"), and that temporal depth is achieved by the momentary consciousness representing the past (retention) and the future (protention). In this view, when you hear the tone B of a melody, you are perceiving B in the strict present, retaining tone A, and protending tone C. Because the act of awareness is assumed to be a durationless point, Husserl requires a transcendent absolute flow to bind these points together. The infinite regress only threatens if one accepts this punctiform premise.

However, *extensional* models of the specious present—championed by theorists like Barry Dainton, Ian Phillips, and Geoffrey Lee—argue that the acts of awareness are themselves temporally extended. In an extensional framework, a succession of events is apprehended by an act of consciousness that is durationally isomorphic to the events it apprehends. The experience of a temporally extended interval is directly apprehended in a single, temporally extended mental act.

| Model of Time-Consciousness | Vehicle of Experience | Representation of Time | Solution to the Regress Problem |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Retentional (Husserl)** | Momentary (Punctiform point). | Temporal depth is achieved via retention (past) and protention (future) within the instantaneous now. | Posits an atemporal "absolute flow" outside the temporal order, leading to the dualism championed by the manuscript. |
| **Extensional (Dainton, Lee)** | Temporally Extended (Specious Present). | Succession is directly apprehended by an experiential vehicle that possesses its own temporal duration. | The vehicle is already temporal; it does not need a higher-order atemporal consciousness to bind it. The regress never begins. |

Recent literature continues to refine the mereological structure of the specious present to avoid exactly the paradoxes the manuscript relies upon. For instance, Lyu Zhou (2023) explores the phenomenal temporal structure of present experience, demonstrating that immediate present consciousness is phenomenally extended and mereologically structured. Extensional models provide a coherent explanation for the unity of temporal experience without requiring a retreat to atemporal existence. By failing to even mention the extensional alternative, the manuscript sets up a false dichotomy: either infinite regress or non-temporal dualism. A competent philosophical review of time-consciousness cannot simply ignore the leading alternative framework that explicitly neutralizes its core logical proof.

### Computational Phenomenology: Resolving the Categorical Mismatch

Even more damaging to the manuscript's thesis is its assertion that physicalist processes suffer a "categorical mismatch" when trying to describe time-consciousness. The manuscript claims that physical descriptions involve states succeeding states (McTaggart's B-series), and that if an entity lacks temporal structure entirely, temporal descriptions systematically fail. The author asserts that physical models cannot capture the integrated flow of subjective temporality.

This claim has been explicitly falsified by the rapidly expanding field of computational phenomenology. Working within the Active Inference framework, Bogotá and Djebbara (2023) provide a rigorous mathematical and temporal analysis of how Husserlian time-consciousness maps directly onto physical, computational systems. They demonstrate that the dissonance between "subjective temporality" (the continuous, specious flow of experience) and "objective time" (the discrete, sequential structure of physics) can be formally resolved.

In their model, the generative processes of the brain utilize a Markov blanket of the present moment that integrates past and future moments in an asynchronous manner. The subjective time of the organism (τ) and the objective time of the environment (t) are formally integrated within the belief-updating scheme of active inference, characterized by the variational free energy equation (vτ˙=∂sτt​∂F​). This proves that a physical, temporally sequential system can mathematically generate the exact Husserlian temporal continuity the manuscript claims is impossible for physicalism to achieve.

Furthermore, temporal binding models—such as Jerath and Malani's (2025) "13-Millisecond Bridge"—demonstrate how non-zero-time latencies across multisensory processing streams are integrated into a continuous "default space," creating the conscious experience of a unified "now" out of disparate temporal physical inputs. The brain literally constructs the specious present through phase-locked neural oscillations over time, where sensory features occurring at different latencies (e.g., auditory at 10-20ms, visual at 50-70ms) are synchronized. The manuscript's categorical mismatch argument is thus empirically obsolete; the supposed "mismatch" has already been bridged by contemporary predictive coding and neural oscillation literature.

## Dimension B: The Illusion of Conscious Unity and the Mishandling of Split-Brain Pathologies

In an attempt to prove that consciousness is a non-physical entity that merely "interfaces" with the brain, the manuscript dedicates a substantial section to "What Fragmented Consciousness Reveals." Here, the text relies almost exclusively on Pinto et al.'s (2017) highly controversial study of split-brain patients. Pinto concluded that severing the corpus callosum results in "divided perception but undivided consciousness" because patients could indicate the presence of stimuli across the visual field regardless of the response hand used. The manuscript interprets this "resilience of unity" as evidence for a Dualist Interface Theory, suggesting that the non-physical mind remains unified while the physical "receiver" is damaged.

The uncritical deployment of Pinto et al. (2017) to support a dualist interface is perhaps the most scientifically and philosophically irresponsible move in the manuscript. It treats Pinto's conclusion as a settled paradigm shift while ignoring the fierce, devastating backlash this study received, as well as definitive 2024–2025 structural neuroimaging findings that explain the remaining unity in strictly physical terms.

### The "Unity of Agency" vs. "Unity of Experience" Conflation

The manuscript completely fails to acknowledge Schechter and Bayne's (2021) seminal critique, "Consciousness after split-brain surgery: The recent challenge to the classical picture". Schechter and Bayne dismantled Pinto's interpretation by demonstrating a fatal philosophical equivocation in the definition of "conscious unity." They pointed out that Pinto et al. only proved a *unity of agency*—the ability of the split-brain patient to respond behaviorally as a single organism using subcortical or cross-cueing mechanisms.

Pinto entirely failed to prove a *unity of experience* (phenomenal unity). A patient's ability to point to a stimulus or guess its presence does not mean the left and right hemispheres share a unified phenomenal field. As Schechter and Bayne argue, split-brain subjects can consciously perceive two stimuli separately, but they lack a conscious experience of the two stimuli *together*—they cannot judge whether the stimuli are the same or different. It is incredibly difficult to reconcile the behavioral data with the claim that the split-brain subject possesses a fully unified experience-based consciousness. By omitting this necessary distinction, the manuscript treats behavioral agency (functional integration) as synonymous with phenomenal unity (qualia integration), ironically committing the very functionalist reductionism it purports to oppose.

### Minimal Connectivity, Plasticity, and Hemispherotomy

Even if one grants that some degree of integration survives the callosotomy, the manuscript's leap to a non-physical dualist interface is invalidated by contemporary network neuroscience. Recent findings by UC Santa Barbara researchers (2025) utilizing advanced neuroimaging have demonstrated that "undivided consciousness" in supposed split-brain patients is actually the result of neuroplasticity and minimal intact fiber connections. Patients with incomplete callosotomies—or those whose brains reorganized to route networks from the front of the brain to the back across a tiny ribbon of intact splenium—maintain the physical integration necessary for unified behavior. The unity is structurally physical, facilitated by unusual routing of networks, not the broadcast of an immaterial soul ignoring a broken antenna.

Furthermore, the manuscript's dualist interface hypothesis explicitly predicts that a completely isolated physical substrate should simply fail to broadcast, but the "mind" behind it should remain unaffected and unified. This prediction is robustly falsified by recent electrophysiological studies of total cortical disconnection (hemispherotomy). Gauvry and Rüber (2024/2025) investigated whether a cerebral hemisphere, completely isolated from sensory-motor and subcortical pathways, could act as an "island of awareness" receiving a non-physical conscious broadcast. They found that post-surgery, the disconnected cortex does not maintain waking consciousness; instead, it persistently exhibits prominent slow oscillations (<2 Hz) and a broad-band slowing characteristic of deep sleep or unconsciousness.

Deprived of physical subcortical activating inputs from the brainstem ascending reticular activating system, the physical tissue ceases to support wakeful conscious states. This directly contradicts the interface model; the mind does not persist independently of the physical network's temporal and structural integrity. The manuscript's failure to account for these massive empirical challenges renders its fragmentation argument obsolete.

## Dimension C: The Physical Vacuity of the "Minimal Quantum Interaction"

Having argued for a metaphysically non-temporal consciousness based on a misreading of phenomenology, and a unified non-physical interface based on a misreading of split-brain pathology, the manuscript attempts to explain how such an entity interacts with the temporal physical brain. It proposes the "Minimal Quantum Interaction" tenet, positing a "post-decoherence selection programme".

The text correctly notes that standard quantum consciousness models (like Penrose-Hameroff or von Neumann-Wigner) suffer from a "timing-gap objection" because environmental decoherence in the warm, wet environment of the brain happens in femtoseconds, while conscious processing takes hundreds of milliseconds. To solve this, the manuscript asserts that the non-temporal selector acts *after* environmental monitoring has selected a preferred basis. It claims the non-temporal mind operates on the resulting "improper mixture" to determine which pointer state becomes actual. The text admits this is a "bracketed tenet" that does not supply physical dynamics, but insists it avoids violating energy conservation or Born rule statistics.

This theoretical maneuver is not just speculative; it is mathematically and physically vacuous, operating entirely through linguistic fiat rather than quantum mechanics. The use of bracketed tenets to shield fatal physical contradictions is a hallmark of pseudo-scientific theorizing.

First, treating the system as an "improper mixture" post-decoherence means that the phase coherence between the macroscopic pointer states has been dispersed into the environment. Locally, the density matrix is diagonal, representing classical probabilities rather than quantum superpositions. For a "non-temporal consciousness" to selectively actualize one specific state out of this diagonal density matrix without violating Born rule statistics, it would have to sample the probability distribution exactly as random chance would. If the non-temporal mind perfectly mimics quantum randomness, the theory suffers from the "bias-without-deviation" problem—it becomes an epiphenomenal hidden variable theory that is empirically indistinguishable from standard objective collapse models. If it is indistinguishable from random chance, the posited non-temporal mind is entirely superfluous to the physical dynamics; it performs no actual work.

If, conversely, the non-temporal mind *steers* the outcome to achieve biological integration and meaningful conscious behavior (a requirement for the manuscript's "Bidirectional Interaction" tenet), it must systematically deviate from Born probabilities over time. Such a deviation requires an alteration of the system's Hamiltonian, meaning it *must* inject energy or alter the physical equations of motion. This explicitly violates the manuscript's own self-imposed "no-energy-injection" and "no-detectable-anomaly constraints". The author attempts to have it both ways: a consciousness that changes the outcome of physical events but leaves no physical trace of having done so.

Furthermore, the manuscript relies on an outdated, cartoonish conception of quantum biology, ignoring the 2020–2025 literature on how quantum effects might actually survive in noisy neural environments without requiring mystical timeless selectors. For example, recent spintronic coherence proposals (e.g., Beshkar 2025; Wiest 2025) attempt to sidestep Tegmark's decoherence calculations entirely using specific physical properties of microtubules, exploring room-temperature quantum effects. While still highly speculative, these models attempt to do the hard mathematical work of physics. The manuscript, in contrast, simply uses the word "quantum" as a rug under which to sweep the catastrophic interaction problem generated by its own non-temporal dualism. A bracketed theoretical tenet cannot substitute for a coherent physical mechanism in a paper claiming to make ontological assertions about the natural world.

## Conclusion and Final Verdict

The manuscript's methodological approach is deeply, irrecoverably flawed. It treats phenomenological reports (meditative timelessness) and pathological data (split-brain behavior) as raw, unmediated data points that uniquely support a highly specific, dualistic metaphysics, while systematically ignoring the physicalist explanations that currently dominate the literature. By selectively harvesting philosophical history (Husserl, Bergson) and misrepresenting contemporary science (Pinto, post-decoherence quantum mechanics), the author constructs an elaborate but exceedingly fragile theoretical echo chamber.

The specific, disqualifying weaknesses are formalized below:

1. **Conflation of Phenomenological Atemporality with Metaphysical Timelessness**: The manuscript wrongly infers a non-temporal metaphysical vehicle from the phenomenological content of meditative states, ignoring recent neurophenomenological consensus that such states result from specific temporal neural configurations, such as DMN decoupling and flattened predictive hierarchies.

- *Citation*: Ehmann, S., Sezer, I., Treves, I. N., Gabrieli, J. D. E., & Sacchet, M. D. (2025). "Mindfulness, cognition, and long-term meditators: Toward a science of advanced meditation." *Imaging Neuroscience*, 3, IMAG.a.82.

2. **Omission of Extensional Models of the Specious Present**: The manuscript relies exclusively on Husserl's retentional model to generate an infinite regress argument for a timeless absolute flow, completely failing to address contemporary extensional models which neutralize the regress by positing that the act of awareness is itself mereologically and temporally extended.

- *Citation*: Zhou, L. (2023). "Specious Present, Phenomenal Extension, and Mereological Inversion." *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 31(3-4), 183-207.

3. **Ignorance of Computational Phenomenology and Active Inference**: The manuscript asserts a "categorical mismatch" where physical systems cannot account for the continuous flow of subjective time, ignoring recent robust mathematical proofs that the Active Inference framework utilizes Markov blankets to successfully integrate Husserlian subjective temporality with objective sequential time.

- *Citation*: Bogotá, J. D., & Djebbara, Z. (2023). "Time-consciousness in computational phenomenology: a temporal analysis of active inference." *Neuroscience of Consciousness*, 2023(1), niad004.

4. **Mishandling of Split-Brain Pathologies and Unity of Experience**: The manuscript unethically relies on Pinto et al. (2017) to claim a non-physical "undivided consciousness," completely ignoring the fatal literature critique that split-brain patients only demonstrate a "unity of agency" via subcortical cross-cueing, while robustly lacking a phenomenal "unity of experience."

- *Citation*: Schechter, E., & Bayne, T. (2021). "Consciousness after split-brain surgery: The recent challenge to the classical picture." *Neuropsychologia*, 160, 107987.

5. **Ignorance of Deafferentation and Hemispherotomy Neuroscience**: The manuscript's dualist interface hypothesis assumes a non-physical mind persisting despite physical fragmentation, which is directly falsified by recent data showing that a completely physically disconnected cortical hemisphere does not act as an "island of awareness" but devolves into persistent, unconscious slow-wave sleep states due to subcortical deafferentation.

- *Citation*: Gauvry, C., & Rüber, T. (2024/2025). "The Awakening of the Newborn Human Infant and the Emergence of Consciousness / Hemispherotomy and Islands of Awareness." *bioRxiv* (2025.02.10.637383).

The manuscript requires absolute rejection due to its fatal reliance on empirically superseded neuroscientific claims, its ignorance of contemporary computational phenomenology, and its deployment of physical and conceptual equivocations to sustain an untenable dualist framework.
