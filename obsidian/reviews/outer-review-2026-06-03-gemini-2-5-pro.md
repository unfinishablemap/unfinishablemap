# Referee Report: "Consciousness and the Metaphysics of Composition"

## 1. Executive Evaluation of the Argumentative Framework

The manuscript "Consciousness and the Metaphysics of Composition" attempts an ambitious cross-disciplinary synthesis of mereology and the philosophy of mind. The author's central thesis asserts that consciousness serves as the definitive boundary criterion for genuine mereological composition, leveraging the Special Composition Question (SCQ) originally formulated by Peter van Inwagen. The text posits an alleged "convergence" between disparate philosophical and scientific paradigms—specifically, biological minimalism, causal non-redundancy (via Trenton Merricks), and Integrated Information Theory (IIT) via Kelvin McQueen and Naotsugu Tsuchiya. From this purported convergence, the manuscript concludes that physical systems only achieve genuine metaphysical unity when consciousness supervenes to bind their parts, thereby purportedly providing substantive evidence for a non-reductive, interactionist dualism.

While the structural ambition of bridging the metaphysics of individuation with the SCQ is apparent, the manuscript in its current form is entirely unsuitable for publication in a top-tier analytic philosophy journal. The article suffers from catastrophic epistemic, empirical, and logical vulnerabilities that systematically undermine its conclusions. The analysis routinely misrepresents the physicalist and functionalist underpinnings of its cited scientific frameworks, abstracting their mereological conclusions from the theoretical engines that generate them. Furthermore, the manuscript completely ignores recent and devastating empirical challenges to the theories it relies upon—most notably the 2023 consensus characterizing IIT as unfalsifiable pseudoscience.

In the domain of philosophy of mind, the manuscript arbitrarily dismisses panpsychist solutions to the combination problem without engaging with the robust 2020–2025 literature on constitutive panpsychism and phenomenal sharing. More broadly, the dialectic structurally begs the question by enforcing pre-ordained "tenets" (such as Dualism and the rejection of Many Worlds quantum mechanics) as unstated, unargued axioms rather than derived conclusions. The resulting narrative is epistemically closed, functionally isolating the argument from contemporary debates in deflationary physicalism, mereological meta-ontology, and network neuroscience. The following sections systematically detail the fatal flaws in the manuscript's methodology, empirical claims, and logical inferences.

## 2. The Foundational Misappropriation of the Special Composition Question (SCQ)

The architectural foundation of the manuscript rests on its deployment of the Special Composition Question (SCQ). Formulated prominently by Peter van Inwagen (1990), the SCQ asks: under what conditions do some objects (the xs) compose a further object (a y)? The manuscript argues that the SCQ has "unexpectedly deep connections to consciousness" because several leading answers converge on consciousness as the criterion for genuine composition. The text uses this to launch an attack on "composition as identity" (CAI) for conscious subjects, claiming that standard mereological answers—Universalism and Nihilism—are ontologically unviable because they eliminate the entities that bear conscious experience.

### 2.1 The Metaphysical Overreach of the Criterion

The fundamental error in this framing is the conflation of mereological survival with ontological primacy. The manuscript assumes that if consciousness maps onto the boundaries of composite objects, then consciousness itself must be the *cause* or *metaphysical primitive* that grounds composition. This inference is entirely illicit within standard analytic mereology.

When the SCQ is answered restrictively (e.g., composition occurs only under condition C), the condition C is typically a physical, structural, or topological relation among the xs (such as bonding, homeostasis, or causal integration). The manuscript reverses this vector of dependency. It takes the metric that purportedly identifies consciousness and declares it the determiner of which physical systems count as genuine wholes. However, in a physicalist or functionalist framework, consciousness is the *result* of the parts forming a whole under specific causal conditions, not the metaphysical glue that performs the composition. By treating consciousness as the compositional operator, the manuscript assumes the very non-reductive dualism it is attempting to prove, resulting in a circular dialectic.

### 2.2 The False Dilemma Between Nihilism and Dualist Composition

The manuscript forces a false dichotomy: either we accept mereological Nihilism/Universalism (which the author claims eliminates the subject), or we must accept consciousness as a primitive, dualistic boundary condition. This argument completely ignores the dominant contemporary position in mereological meta-ontology: deflationary physicalism and hylomorphic conceptualism.

Under a deflationary physicalist framework, the fundamental properties of the universe are highly fine-grained physical states. "Coarse-grained" properties, including macroscopic objects, artifacts, and conscious subjects, are constructed by identifying properties that are metaphysically mutually entailing. Deflationary ontology posits that questions of composition are largely conceptual, linguistic, or pragmatic. Whether parts "compose" a whole is a matter of convention or biological individuation (such as the concept of the "holobiont" in modern microbiology, which treats host and microbiome as a pragmatic biological unit), not a matter of objective, fundamental "joints" in nature.

For example, contemporary hylomorphic and deflationary theorists like Friedell (2020) argue that the properties of composite artifacts (and by extension, complex biological systems) are extrinsic and defined by social or functional conventions. If physicalist mereology is deflationary, the SCQ does not require a heavyweight, non-reductive dualist answer to "save" the existence of conscious subjects. Subjects exist as highly integrated, coarse-grained physical systems. The failure to engage with deflationary meta-ontology reveals a profound blind spot in the manuscript’s grasp of the very field it attempts to critique.

## 3. The Illusory "Convergence Thesis" and Methodological Selection Effects

The primary argumentative mechanism of the manuscript is the "Convergence Thesis," which suggests that three independent approaches to the SCQ—van Inwagen’s biological criterion, Merricks’ causal criterion, and McQueen and Tsuchiya’s informational criterion—mutually triangulate on consciousness as the boundary condition for genuine wholes. The manuscript heavily emphasizes that these thinkers "share no common premises, invoke different argumentative strategies, and emerge from different philosophical traditions," thereby granting their convergence immense probative value.

However, a rigorous philosophical audit reveals that this purported convergence is entirely an artifact of editorial manipulation and selection bias. The text routinely conflates description-level correlation with metaphysical entailment.

### 3.1 The Distortion of Biological Minimalism

Peter van Inwagen’s answer to the SCQ restricts composition to living organisms and fundamental simples. The manuscript acknowledges, albeit softly, that van Inwagen "defends a materialist ontology of persons". Yet, it systematically co-opts his mereological restriction as implicit evidence for a consciousness-based, dualist framework.

Biological minimalism explicitly relies on homeostatic, metabolic, and teleological unity—not phenomenal consciousness—as the engine of composition. To claim that van Inwagen’s organicism "converges" on consciousness requires the supplementary assumption that all and only living things are conscious (biopsychism or panbiotism). This is an assumption the manuscript neither defends nor correctly attributes. Plants, fungi, and single-celled organisms possess metabolic unity and thus compose wholes under van Inwagen's framework, yet they are not typically considered phenomenally conscious subjects in the manner required by the manuscript's interactionist dualism. By abstracting the biological and metabolic mechanics out of van Inwagen’s framework, the manuscript commits a severe category error, treating physiological integration as synonymous with phenomenal unity.

### 3.2 Merricks and the Causal Redundancy Misapplication

The manuscript utilizes Trenton Merricks’ argument against causal overdetermination. Merricks posits that composite objects (like baseballs) do not exist because their causal powers are entirely redundant with the powers of their constituent atoms; however, he makes an exception for conscious beings, arguing they possess novel, non-redundant causal powers. The text treats Merricks’ non-supervenience claim as a secure ontological foundation.

This reliance is philosophically irresponsible. The manuscript completely ignores the vast, highly developed physicalist literature on mental causation and the exclusion problem. A rigorous physicalist approach demonstrates that macroscopic causal efficacy does not require strong emergence or non-supervenience. Deflationary accounts, compatibilist causal models, and interventionist theories of causation all provide mechanisms by which macro-level physical states (like neural networks) exert causal influence without redundant overdetermination, yet without requiring non-physical mental properties. By treating Merricks' highly contested premises regarding mental causation as load-bearing architecture, the article's "convergence" is exposed as merely a selective cataloging of non-standard mereologies that happen to align with its pre-existing dualist conclusions.

### 3.3 The Failure of the "Selection Effect" Defense

The manuscript attempts to inoculate itself against the "selection effect" objection—the critique that it has merely cherry-picked three theories that yield similar conclusions—by citing their methodological distance (biological, causal, information-theoretic). It claims to administer a self-objection regarding selection effects, granting it "real force" but ultimately dismissing it.

This defense is logically and rhetorically insufficient. Methodological heterogeneity does not validate a unified metaphysical truth if the underlying criteria are structurally forced into alignment by the author's editorial framing. The manuscript effectively strips the physicalist and functionalist commitments from IIT and van Inwagen's organicism, leaving only a skeletal mereological restriction. It then backfills this restriction with the manuscript's own dualistic "tenets". To claim that physicalists and dualists "converge" on consciousness when the physicalists are talking about functional information integration and the dualists are talking about non-supervenient phenomenality is an equivocation on the term "consciousness." They are not converging on the same referent.

## 4. Empirical and Epistemic Catastrophes in the Application of Integrated Information Theory

The most fatal flaw in the manuscript's empirical scaffolding is its uncritical, foundational reliance on Integrated Information Theory (IIT) to ground its mereological claims. The text heavily cites McQueen and Tsuchiya (2023) to argue that IIT specifies a non-trivial restriction on composition: "composition happens when integrated information is maximized". The manuscript treats this Φ-maximization postulate as a robust scientific validation of its claim that consciousness identifies genuine wholes, arguing that this convergence links the metaphysics of composition directly to the science of consciousness.

This reliance is disastrously mishandled across three independent axes, rendering the manuscript empirically obsolete and logically contradictory.

### 4.1 Ignorance of the 2023 Pseudoscience Consensus

In September 2023, a coalition of over 124 leading researchers in neuroscience, psychology, and cognitive science published an explosive open letter explicitly categorizing IIT as pseudoscience. The consensus critique, which sparked massive debate in the consciousness literature, demonstrated that IIT is mathematically unfalsifiable, lacks sufficient empirical support, and makes absurd theoretical predictions. For instance, critics pointed out that IIT's mathematical formalism implies that inactive grids of connected logic gates would possess consciousness, a reductio ad absurdum that shatters its utility as a biological theory of mind.

The manuscript’s failure to even mention this existential crisis in the literature fundamentally undermines its academic credibility. A philosophical argument cannot casually deploy a scientific theory as an uncontested pillar of a metaphysical convergence argument when that very theory has been broadly condemned by the scientific community as pseudo-scientific. The text abstracts the McQueen-Tsuchiya thesis from its violently contested sociological and empirical context, presenting an illusion of scientific stability that simply does not exist in the 2020-2025 literature. If the theory supplying the sole mathematical and empirical criterion for the manuscript's mereological restriction is empirically unviable, the entire metaphysical superstructure built upon it collapses.

### 4.2 The Methodological Incompatibility of Φ-Maximization and Interactionism

Even if we bracket the pseudoscience allegations, the manuscript fundamentally misunderstands the ontological direction of IIT. McQueen and Tsuchiya use IIT to provide a functionalist, reductive mapping of causal power. For them, consciousness *just is* the maximization of integrated information. It is a structural property of physical networks.

The manuscript, however, operates under the explicit "Tenet" of Dualism and Bidirectional Interaction, wherein consciousness is not reducible to physical processes and causally influences the physical world. Using a strictly functionalist, physicalist algorithm (IIT) to "prove" a non-reductive interactionist ontology is a profound epistemic mismatch. The manuscript assumes that because IIT identifies a boundary, that boundary must represent a dualistic ontological primitive. But IIT's boundary is mathematically generated by calculating transition probability matrices across partitions of a physical system. It is a measure of causal density, not a detector for immaterial phenomenal essence.

### 4.3 The "Activity Critique" and the Instantaneous Property Contradiction

The manuscript's treatment of time and activity introduces a devastating internal contradiction. The text treats IIT's Φ-maximization as a valid ontological boundary , while elsewhere promoting an "integration as activity" framework that explicitly rejects instantaneous properties.

Recent advancements in computational neuroscience highlight that the IIT algorithm is mathematically ill-defined for continuous, real-world physical systems because it relies on calculating properties at an instantaneous state—an assumption that collapses when applied to continuous physical fields or dynamic neural activity. The manuscript attempts to adopt the mereological output of IIT while simultaneously holding a process-oriented "integration is not a property at an instant" view.

This is logically incoherent. If unity and integration resist instantaneous analysis, then IIT's Φ, which is calculated strictly via discrete, synchronous logic states, cannot possibly serve as the criterion for the SCQ. One cannot leverage a static, discrete mathematical algorithm to validate a dynamic, process-oriented metaphysical dualism. The manuscript fails to realize that the activity critique it praises completely invalidates the McQueen-Tsuchiya mereological criterion it relies upon.

## 5. The Evasion of Constitutive Panpsychism and the Combination Problem

A core component of the manuscript's argument against physicalist and alternative composition is its bold assertion of "phenomenal non-compositionality." The text confidently declares that "two subjects do not fuse into a wider subject" and leverages this alleged asymmetry between physical and phenomenal composition as definitive proof that consciousness resists mereological analysis from below. This assertion operates as the manuscript's primary weapon against both physicalism and panpsychism.

### 5.1 The Ignorance of the 2020s Panpsychist Revival

The manuscript treats the impossibility of subject-summing as an established, uncontroversial metaphysical fact. This reveals a shocking ignorance of the past half-decade of philosophy of mind, specifically regarding the "combination problem." The text implicitly assumes the combination problem is a fatal, unsolved paradox for panpsychism, thereby clearing the field for its own dualist framework.

This ignores the extensive, highly cited work of philosophers like Luke Roelofs (2020, 2024), who has systematically defended the logical and metaphysical possibility of experiential combination and composite subjectivity. Roelofs’ defense of "manyism"—the concept that multiple overlapping conscious subjects can exist in one space, seamlessly sharing phenomenal components—provides a direct, heavily articulated counterargument to the manuscript's foundational claim.

In his paper "Can We Sum Subjects? Evaluating Panpsychism's Hard Problem" (2020) and his subsequent 2024 work "No such thing as too many minds," Roelofs demonstrates that treating minds as lacking part-whole structure is a historical artifact. He argues that just as everything else in nature shows part-whole structure, from physical objects to biological systems to social groups, accepting mental combination merely requires treating minds like the rest of nature. Roelofs systematically dismantles the "subject-summing problem," the "palette problem," and the "revelation problem".

### 5.2 The Straw-Man of "Bidirectional Resistance"

By bluntly asserting that "coordination is not composition"  and that subjects definitively cannot fuse, the manuscript argues against a straw-man version of panpsychism from the late 20th century. It fails entirely to stress-test its "bidirectional resistance to mereological analysis" against contemporary models of phenomenal sharing and constitutive panpsychism.

If subject-summing is coherent and possible (as Roelofs and other modern panpsychists argue), the manuscript’s claim that consciousness possesses a unique, indivisible metaphysical boundary entirely collapses. The author cannot simply state that phenomenal non-compositionality is "one of the strongest" asymmetries  without engaging the primary literature dedicated to disproving that exact asymmetry. In a top-tier philosophy journal, asserting a highly contested premise as an axiomatic truth is grounds for immediate rejection.

## 6. Structural Circularity and Tenet-Bracketed Epistemology

Perhaps the most disqualifying aspect of the manuscript is its underlying epistemic architecture. The text operates within the boundaries of an overarching ideological project ("The Unfinishable Map") that is explicitly constrained by five foundational "tenets" :

1. **Dualism:** Consciousness is not reducible to physical processes.
2. **Minimal Quantum Interaction:** Smallest possible non-physical influence on quantum outcomes.
3. **Bidirectional Interaction:** Consciousness causally influences the physical world.
4. **No Many Worlds:** Reject MWI; indexical identity matters.
5. **Occam's Razor Has Limits:** Simplicity is unreliable with incomplete knowledge.

The manuscript explicitly connects its conclusions about consciousness-based composition to these tenets. For instance, it notes the necessity of a "single definite world" (rejecting Everettian/Many Worlds interpretations based on Tenet 4) and enforces a non-supervenient view of the mind to align with Tenets 1 and 3.

### 6.1 Question-Begging in the Treatment of Parsimony

In its concluding theoretical moves, the text argues that standard mereological answers like Universalism and Nihilism appear simpler than restricted composition, but that their simplicity is misleading because consciousness "resists the categories that make simple answers possible". This ad hoc dismissal of theoretical parsimony is explicitly framed as an application of "Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)".

This methodology is structurally question-begging. The manuscript does not *prove* that Occam’s Razor fails in the context of mereology; it merely assumes the failure of Occam's Razor as a pre-ordained ideological tenet to shield its ontologically inflated dualism from standard physicalist critiques of parsimony. When faced with the theoretical elegance of physicalist composition, the author simply points to a site rule that declares simplicity unreliable.

### 6.2 The Premature Dismissal of Quantum Holism

Similarly, the manuscript dismisses quantum holism and Everettian mereology not through rigorous argumentation, but by invoking the "No Many Worlds" tenet. The SCQ has profound intersections with quantum mechanics, particularly regarding entanglement and state non-separability, which directly challenge traditional macroscopic mereology. By insulating itself from quantum-mechanical frameworks via fiat, the manuscript operates in an artificial epistemic vacuum.

A philosophical argument submitted to a peer-reviewed academic journal cannot derive its argumentative force by appealing to its own external, unverified axioms. Because the manuscript's framing is strictly bracketed by these tenets, it fails to engage genuinely with competing frameworks. Its conclusions are essentially tautological: it assumes dualism and the unreliability of parsimony in its premises, and subsequently "discovers" that mereology requires a complex, dualistic solution. This is not philosophical inquiry; it is ideological confirmation.

## 7. Conclusions and Mandatory Revisions

The manuscript "Consciousness and the Metaphysics of Composition" demonstrates a clear ambition to synthesize disparate areas of philosophy of mind and metaphysics. However, it is crippled by profound scholarly omissions, empirical misrepresentations, and structural circularity. It co-opts physicalist frameworks (IIT, biological minimalism) while deliberately ignoring their foundational premises. It relies on empirical theories that have been widely condemned as pseudoscience in the contemporary literature. It dismisses panpsychism without engaging with any contemporary solutions to the combination problem. Most damningly, it insulates itself from metaphysical critique by retreating into pre-established, undefended tenets.

The manuscript fundamentally lacks the empirical rigor, contemporary literature engagement, and structural neutrality required for academic submission.

### At-Least-Five Weaknesses Justifying Rejection

1. **Ignorance of Constitutive Panpsychism:** The manuscript claims as uncontested fact that "two subjects do not fuse into a wider subject" without engaging contemporary constitutive panpsychism, completely omitting Luke Roelofs' extensive 2020–2024 work demonstrating the coherence of mental combination and phenomenal sharing (Roelofs, L., "Can We Sum Subjects? Evaluating Panpsychism's Hard Problem," in *The Routledge Handbook of Panpsychism*, 2020).
2. **Reliance on Pseudoscience-Labeled Empirical Models:** The manuscript heavily leverages IIT's Φ-maximization as a scientifically stable mereological restriction, entirely omitting the massive 2023 scientific consensus that labeled the theory mathematically and empirically unviable pseudoscience (Fleming, S. M., et al., "The Integrated Information Theory of Consciousness as Pseudoscience," *PsyArXiv*, 2023).
3. **Internal Contradiction Regarding Instantaneous Properties:** The text attempts to merge an "integration as activity" framework with the mereological output of IIT, flatly contradicting the established neurocomputational fact that IIT's algorithm relies on instantaneous discrete states and fails on continuous physical activity, rendering its use of McQueen & Tsuchiya's (2023) composition model incoherent.
4. **Omission of Deflationary Physicalist Mereology:** The argument forces a false dilemma between nihilism and dualistic restricted composition by completely ignoring deflationary physicalist and hylomorphic accounts of the Special Composition Question that resolve the paradox via pragmatic or coarse-grained property construction (Friedell, referenced in *Making Objects and Events*, 2020).
5. **Biopsychist Conflation in van Inwagen’s Framework:** The manuscript mishandles van Inwagen's biological minimalism by illicitly substituting phenomenal consciousness for metabolic integration without theoretical defense, a conflation that is wholly unsupported by contemporary mereological surveys (such as the overview in McQueen & Tsuchiya, "When do parts form wholes?", *Neuroscience of Consciousness*, 2023).

**Verdict:** Due to catastrophic structural circularity, empirical misrepresentation of highly contested scientific models, and the systematic omission of contemporary counterarguments, the manuscript is unfit for publication and must be rejected.
