# Audit: “Phantom Limb Phenomena”

[The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/)

Its main weakness is empirical recency. For an article last substantively modified on 2026-05-09, the public reference list is too old and too Flor/Ramachandran-centered: the external references run from 1992 to 2014, with no Makin-group work, no recent prevalence meta-analysis, no recent predictive-processing/pain review, and no 2025 longitudinal cortical-map evidence. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) This is especially notable because the site’s own research notes already flagged the Makin challenge, mixed-to-negative mirror-therapy evidence, and a need to check 2024–2026 literature before major refinement. ## 1. Empirical claims and citations

The opening prevalence claim is broadly defensible but should be tightened. The article says roughly 60–80% of amputees experience phantom sensations and a similar proportion experience phantom limb pain. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) A 2020 systematic review/meta-analysis pooled phantom limb pain prevalence at about 64%, with high heterogeneity and risk-factor caveats; that supports the general range, but the article should distinguish “phantom sensations” from “phantom limb pain” more explicitly and cite the meta-analysis rather than relying mainly on older classic sources. [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article)

The cortical-remapping section is the most empirically dated part. The article presents adjacent cortical invasion and remapping as the “standard mechanism,” then says the Map accepts that mechanistic story while adding an explanatory-gap point. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) That framing is now too simple. Makin and Flor’s 2020 review says traditional remapping is incomplete and that preserved limb representation, behavioral factors, and mechanisms beyond primary sensorimotor cortex all matter. [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1053811920304298) Even more directly, 2025 longitudinal work in *Nature Neuroscience* tracked people before and after arm amputation and found stable hand and lip representations, with no evidence of the classic lip-into-hand-region shift in those cases. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41593-025-02037-7) The article should treat the Flor model as historically important, not as the settled “standard mechanism.”

Peripheral and spinal mechanisms are acknowledged, but too briefly. The article mentions neuromas, dorsal-root-ganglion activity, spinal sensitisation, and affective modulation in one compressed passage. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) Current review-level discussions treat the central-versus-peripheral debate as unresolved and give serious weight to neuromas, ectopic firing, dorsal-root-ganglion excitability, spinal sensitisation, and combined bottom-up/top-down maintenance of pain. [JCI Insight](https://www.jci.org/articles/view/94003) The article should not imply that “no peripheral signal” means no relevant peripheral contribution; the safer formulation is “no signal from the felt limb or felt site.”

The mirror-therapy section is better calibrated than many popular treatments pages, but still needs sharper evidence language. It says the clinical literature is mixed but real, with moderate effects in some trials and weak effects in others. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) The site’s own research notes say higher-quality placebo-controlled evidence is weaker and that a strong “mirror therapy works” claim is not supported at the highest evidence tier. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/research/phantom-limb-phenomena-2026-05-08/) The article should separate two claims: mirror feedback can modulate phantom experience in some patients, but mirror therapy’s general clinical efficacy remains uncertain.

The congenital-phantom discussion is appropriately cautious, but under-sourced. The article uses Brugger’s congenital case, then says innate body-schema or predictive-processing defaults can explain such phenomena without mysticism. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) That is the right direction, but the section should add developmental body-schema sources and make clear that single-case reports are not enough to carry broad metaphysical weight.

## 2. Counterargument coverage

The article does cover mainstream neuroscientific explanations, but it does not steelman them enough.

It covers cortical remapping, but not the strongest current counterargument: that some phantom phenomena may depend on preserved cortical body maps rather than cortical invasion. The 2025 longitudinal evidence is especially important because it directly weakens the older “deafferented cortex gets invaded, therefore phantom pain” story. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41593-025-02037-7) A stronger article would present three live models side by side: classical remapping, preserved representation, and distributed multilevel pain-network accounts.

It mentions peripheral mechanisms, but does not fully show how much philosophical pressure they remove. If stump neuromas, dorsal-root-ganglion excitability, spinal sensitisation, and central amplification interact to generate pain, then phantom pain is not “pain without physical cause”; it is pain without tissue damage at the represented body location. [JCI Insight](https://www.jci.org/articles/view/94003) That distinction is crucial for a platform evaluating dualism.

Predictive coding is only gestured at. The article says congenital phantoms are consistent with predictive-processing defaults, but does not give predictive coding a full naturalistic explanatory role. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) A mainstream predictive-processing account can explain phantom limbs as persistence of a generative body model under altered sensory input, with pain emerging from hierarchical inference, prediction error, affective salience, and action constraints. Contemporary pain-prediction accounts explicitly frame pain as distributed, inferential, and shaped by both bottom-up and top-down signals without requiring dualism. [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neural-circuits/articles/10.3389/fncir.2023.1073537/full)

The article should also explicitly say that “top-down” does not mean “nonphysical.” Visual feedback, attention, expectation, body schema, and affective appraisal are all standard neural-level variables. They may be philosophically interesting, but they do not by themselves support interactionist dualism.
