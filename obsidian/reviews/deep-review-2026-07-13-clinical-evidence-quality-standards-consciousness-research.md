## Pessimistic Analysis Summary

### Critical Issues Found (both fixed)

1. **Wrong statistic attributed to Van Dam et al. (2018)** — the article claimed "only approximately 1% of mindfulness studies compare against active treatments." Verified at PMC5758421: Van Dam (applying Dimidjian & Segal's NIH stage model) reports **9%** of mindfulness research reached Stage 2b ("Efficacy in research clinic: compared to active control"); the **1%** figure is a *different* statistic — "only 1% of all research has been conducted outside research contexts" (Stage 3+ real-world effectiveness). The article conflated the two. **Resolution**: corrected to "only approximately 9% of mindfulness studies reach the stage of testing efficacy against an active control (and only 1% have been tested outside research settings)," which now cites both statistics faithfully and preserves the article's point (few studies use active controls).

2. **Orphan reference (#9)** — Schwartz, Stapp & Beauregard (2005), *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B* 360(1458), 1309-1327, appeared in the References list but was never cited inline (skill §2.4 step 5: orphans in either direction are critical). The paper is real, verified at the publisher, and directly on-topic (it is where Schwartz's OCD-mental-causation reading is formalized within a quantum mind–brain model). **Resolution**: cited inline at the Schwartz OCD-PET passage — "the reading Schwartz, Stapp & Beauregard (2005) formalise within a quantum mind–brain model" — rather than deleting a real, relevant work. Attribution is non-over-claiming (they "formalise" the mental-causation reading; the article does not present the quantum model as established).
