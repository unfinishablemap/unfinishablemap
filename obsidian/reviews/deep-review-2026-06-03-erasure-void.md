- **Misattribution + wrong venue metadata — Reference #2 (Mazzi 2024).** The article cited the *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* metacognition review as "Mazzi, C., et al. (2024) ... 28(11)." Publisher verification (Cell/ScienceDirect, PubMed 39353838) shows the actual paper is **Michel, M., Gao, Y., Mazor, M., Kletenik, I., & Rahnev, D. (2024), *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 28(12), 1066-1077.** First author is **Michel, not Mazzi** (likely confusion with C. Mazzi, an unrelated visual-metacognition author), and the issue was 12 not 11, with page range absent. → **Resolved**: replaced with full correct byline, issue 12, pages 1066-1077. The body's in-text reference ("A 2024 review in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*") names no author and the verbatim quote "a failure in any of these three steps may lead to unawareness" matches the paper, so no body edit was needed.

- **Misattribution + wrong year/volume/pages — Reference #3 (Mograbi & Morris 2018).** The article cited a paper titled "Examination of the metacognitive errors that contribute to anosognosia in Alzheimer's disease" as "Mograbi, D. C., & Morris, R. G. (2018), *Cortex* 88, 24-37." Publisher verification (PubMed 27750070, DOI 10.1016/j.cortex.2016.08.003) shows that **exact title** is **Cosentino, Zhu, Bertrand, Metcalfe, Janicki & Cines (2016), *Cortex* 84, 101-110** — not Mograbi & Morris, not 2018, not vol 88, not 24-37 (every metadata field was wrong, and the byline was the wrong research group). However, the body claim the reference supports — the executive/mnemonic/primary three-mode taxonomy — IS the **Cognitive Awareness Model**, whose canonical statement is **Morris, R. G., & Mograbi, D. C. (2013), "Anosognosia, autobiographical memory and self knowledge in Alzheimer's disease," *Cortex* 49(6), 1553-1565** (PubMed 23117055). → **Resolved**: replaced #3 with the correct Morris & Mograbi (2013) CAM citation, which genuinely originates the three-mode framework the body attributes to it. The body text (which names no author/year in-line) is faithful to the CAM and needed no change.

- **Quote/stance inexactness — Gertler (2003).** The article placed in quotation marks "...a much more fragmentary character than normally attributed to it" and framed Gertler as *concluding* it. Publisher abstract (Springer 10.1023/B:PHIL.0000030435.87499.d4) gives the exact wording "...a much more fragmentary character than the one we normally attribute to it," and Gertler in fact *defends a moderate unity* against the strong fragmentation reading — so "concludes" overstated her endorsement. → **Resolved**: restored the exact quote wording and changed "concludes that" to "engages the claim that," which is faithful both to the quotation and to her dialectical stance without weakening the article's point (the fragmentation evidence still stands on the clinical cases).
