## §2.4 Verbatim Quote-Fidelity Web-Verify (Primary Publisher)

All four external direct quotations checked character-for-character against the publisher of record, not aggregators, and not unfinishablemap.org ([[quote-verify-self-contamination-via-own-page]]):

- **Nagel 1974** (L116, bat quote: "the fact that an organism has conscious experience at all means, basically, that there is something it is like to be that organism… fundamentally an organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something that it is like to be that organism—something it is like *for* the organism") — **real-correct**. Both clauses confirmed verbatim against *What Is It Like to Be a Bat?* (The Philosophical Review 83(4), reprinted in *The Mind's I*). No change.
- **Dennett 1991** (L92, neutral-path quote: "the neutral path leading from objective physical science and its insistence on the third-person point of view, to a method of phenomenological description that can (in principle) do justice to the most private and ineffable subjective experiences, while never abandoning the methodological scruples of science") — **real-correct**. Confirmed verbatim against *Consciousness Explained* (1991), p. 72 (independently sourced via Zahavi's "Dennett and Phenomenology," which quotes the passage with the p. 72 locator). No change.
- **Varela 1996** (L86) — **quote-fidelity defect, FIXED**. The article rendered, in quotation marks with "As Varela wrote," the sentence *"There is no theoretical fix for the absence of a disciplined approach to experience."* This is NOT verbatim Varela; it is a paraphrase dressed as a direct quotation. Varela's actual words (*Neurophenomenology: A Methodological Remedy for the Hard Problem*, JCS 3(4):330–349, confirmed across three independent reads of the primary text): *"I claim there is no 'theoretical fix' or 'extra ingredient' in nature that can possibly bridge this gap."* Corrected to quote Varela's genuine phrase ("no 'theoretical fix' or 'extra ingredient' in nature that can possibly bridge this gap") and to state his actual point (the remedy is a disciplined method, not a theory that dispenses with experience). The article's rhetorical hook — heterophenomenology offers "precisely such a theoretical fix" — is preserved and, if anything, sharpened: "theoretical fix" is genuinely Varela's term.
- **Dennett 2003 / Sherlock Holmes** (L58) — **unconfirmable-as-verbatim, DE-QUOTED**. The article presented as one continuous verbatim Dennett quotation: *"Just as it can be an objective fact that Sherlock Holmes resided at 221B Baker St., despite the great detective's fictitious nature, we can treat the subject's introspective report as establishing a set of objective facts about a fictional world."* This exact string appears only in secondary expository sources discussing Dennett's method; it could not be traced to Dennett's primary text across multiple targeted searches. The Wikipedia Heterophenomenology entry and the primary *Consciousness Explained* explainers carry no such quotation, and the second clause ("we can treat the subject's introspective report as establishing a set of objective facts about a fictional world") has the register of expository summary, not Dennett's prose. Per the de-quote discipline for strings unconfirmable at the primary publisher ([[quote-verify-self-contamination-via-own-page]]), the sentence was converted from a direct quotation to the article's own paraphrase of Dennett's (genuinely attested) Holmes analogy. The substance — true-within-the-fiction facts about a fictional world, applied to the subject's heterophenomenological world — is faithful and preserved; only the false verbatim framing was removed.
