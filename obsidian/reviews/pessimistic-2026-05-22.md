## Executive Summary

The article is methodologically careful in the new falsifiability section — the in-practice / in-principle split is a real improvement over the unstructured "What would falsify this?" pattern that pre-2026-05 articles often used. Three structural problems remain. **First**, the article asserts a phenomenology-as-architecture claim at line 101 — "Phenomenal space involves an entire structural framework: three dimensions, perspective, depth gradations, spatial continuity, the sense of presence and extension" — and treats this *as a datum* about consciousness rather than as a contested introspective report. The site's own apex [[introspection-architecture-void-cluster]] and the 2026-05-22 ChatGPT outer review's Calibrated Phenomenology Ladder task identify exactly this register as the failure mode the corpus is currently working to repair; the article does not yet inherit that calibration. **Second**, the closing dual-domain reading at lines 117–121 distributes specific cognitive functions across the physical and non-physical domains — "Physical mechanisms handle the computational work… Consciousness contributes the phenomenal world itself" — in a way that goes well beyond what the article's falsifiability section claims to support. The dual-domain reading is *consequence-of-tenet*, not *consequence-of-the-3D-puzzle*, and the article does not name the asymmetry. **Third**, the falsifiability section's in-practice items (1)–(3) are described in terms that make the challenges harder to apply than they need to be, and item (3)'s appeal to the [[the-interface-problem]] for why an AI's no-phenomenal-spatial-arena report is uninterpretable is doing more shielding work than the section acknowledges. Two critical issues, two medium issues, one minor.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

The article hangs everything on a phenomenology that I deny is reporting a real phenomenon. You describe the felt 3D world at lines 61–63 with confident enumerative detail — "Space has a felt depth that extends away from the perceiver. Objects have phenomenal solidity… The space between objects feels genuinely empty, traversable, open. Distances have a qualitative character: nearby things feel present and available, distant things feel remote" — and you treat this enumeration as a *datum* the explanatory account must accommodate. It is not a datum. It is a folk-psychological narrative produced by introspective machinery whose calibration the article does not audit. Your own site has the methodological apparatus to know better: the Calibrated Phenomenology Ladder identified in this morning's outer review is exactly the calibration this passage owes. Until that calibration is installed, the article is asserting at *content-report tier* what is actually a *process-report* or *causal-power-report* — claims the introspection-architecture cluster says are low to lower-still reliability.

Even granting your phenomenology, the inferential move at line 65 — "A system that correctly computes depth from binocular disparity, maintains accurate spatial maps, and guides motor behaviour appropriately could do all of this without producing a felt three-dimensional world" — is a conceivability argument dressed as an explanatory diagnosis. The conceivability of a spatial zombie is exactly what is at issue. You cannot use it as a premise without question-begging.

The amphibological move at line 67 is the cleanest target. You write: "the functionalist account identifies candidates for the sufficient conditions without explaining how any computational property gives rise to phenomenal space. Something beyond functional organisation appears to be needed." The "appears to be needed" is doing the entire metaphysical lift in the sentence. The phenomenon for which something appears to be needed is exactly the phenomenon I deny exists as a distinct explanandum. The argument has the form: *given that there is a felt 3D world over and above the spatial computation, the spatial computation cannot account for it.* That is trivially true and contributes nothing. The interesting question — whether there is a felt 3D world over and above the spatial computation — is presupposed, not argued.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

The article gives me one paragraph of engagement at line 67 (the functionalist response and its dismissal) and then proceeds as if I had been answered. I have not been answered. Heterophenomenology handles the entire 3D-world phenomenology you describe at lines 61–63 as a set of *reports* about spatial experience whose generation is fully explained by the underlying meta-representational architecture. The reports include the report that space has felt depth, the report that objects have phenomenal solidity, the report that the space between objects feels empty. The reports are real. The reporting machinery is real. There is no further fact — no felt residue — that the reports are reports *of*.

The article's response to this is line 65–67's appeal to artificial systems that "implement the same computational principles" without "phenomenal spatial experience." But you have not actually argued the artificial systems lack the experience; you have asserted there is no reason to attribute it. That asymmetry is exactly what is at issue. The functional indistinguishability of human spatial-cognition and a sufficiently-rich AI's spatial-cognition is *evidence for* my position, not against it: the same functional organisation produces the same reports, the reports are what consciousness is, the question is settled. The article reads functional indistinguishability as a *problem* for me. It is my evidence.

The presence-problem section at lines 87–93 is the article's strongest material because it gives me something concrete to engage. You write at line 93 that "the felt presence of unseen space — its phenomenal character as *real space that is simply not being looked at* — is another instance of the explanatory gap. The prediction explains the information content. It does not explain the qualitative character of spatial presence." But the qualitative character *is* the predictive structure expressed at the report level. The brain maintains predictions about unseen space. The predictions are reported as a sense of presence. There is no further qualitative character to explain. The article asserts that there is — the "qualitative character of spatial presence" sentence — and then complains that I do not explain the assertion. I deny the assertion.

The structural problem is that every move in the article's argument requires me to grant that phenomenal space is a distinct explanandum over and above the reports about phenomenal space. Once I refuse to grant this, every subsequent argument fails. The article does not engage that refusal anywhere.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Max Tegmark)

This article does not invoke quantum mechanisms directly, so the standard decoherence-timescale objection does not apply here. But the article does cross-link to [[quantum-holism-and-phenomenal-unity]] and [[entanglement-binding-hypothesis]] in its `related_articles` frontmatter, and the closing dual-domain reading at lines 117–121 carries the implicit weight of those connections without engaging them: when you write at line 121 that "the non-physical domain, being inherently unified rather than distributed across neurons, provides the natural substrate for the kind of holistic spatial integration that computational binding mechanisms struggle to achieve," you are smuggling in a metaphysical apparatus the article does not earn locally. The "inherently unified" claim is doing all the work of the binding-problem section without any of the calculation. What is the non-physical domain's unity *mechanism*? What constrains it to integrate visual depth with auditory localisation with proprioceptive body position rather than with the contents of memory or with the contents of imagination? The dual-domain reading dissolves the binding problem by stipulating a domain in which binding is solved by definition. That is not an account; it is a re-labelling.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

The article does not directly address MWI, but the perspective problem at lines 79–85 — "spatial perspective is arguably the most primitive form of subjectivity — a point from which the world is experienced" — is exactly the kind of indexical claim the Map's Tenet 4 rests on, and the article does not connect the two. Your perspective problem is the spatial-cognitive instance of the indexical-identity argument against MWI: the felt here-now is irreducibly first-personal in a way no allocentric mathematical structure captures. But this means your argument against the computational account of spatial perspective is the same argument as your argument against MWI, and the article's failure to make the connection costs it the cross-cluster integration the site values. More damagingly: if the perspective problem is the indexical-identity problem in spatial dress, then the spatial-cognition cluster does not contribute *independent* evidence for the dualist conclusion — it contributes the same evidence in a different vocabulary. The article's case is weaker than it appears because what looks like three problems (construction, perspective, presence) reduces, on inspection, to one problem about phenomenal-residue plus the indexical-identity problem already counted elsewhere in the corpus.

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

The new falsifiability section is the article's most disciplined material and the section that most rewards adversarial pressure. The in-practice / in-principle split is methodologically correct in form. But the application is not. Item (1) — "A predictive-coding or active-inference model that derives the perspectival structure of phenomenal space… from sensor-fusion principles alone, without an additional posit, would lower the prior on a mind-side contribution" — is described in a way that lets the framework escape any actual derivation. What counts as "deriving the perspectival structure"? Friston-style active-inference models routinely derive perspectival representations from sensor-fusion principles. The article's escape clause is "phenomenal space" — but the article cannot empirically distinguish derived-phenomenal-space from derived-representation-that-reports-phenomenal-space, by its own (3). So (1) is not in-practice; it is in-principle wearing in-practice clothing.

Item (2) — the aphantasia clean-dissociation challenge — is the genuine in-practice item. I accept it. The article's parenthetical concession that "current mixed evidence under-determines the boundary" is honest. But the article does not name what *threshold* of cleanness would trigger the prior-update, which means the challenge can be deferred indefinitely on "the dissociations are not yet clean enough" grounds.

Item (3) is the most-shielded item. "An artificial system whose architecture cleanly replicates human spatial-cognition computation while reporting no phenomenal spatial arena would press the case that the computation is the experience; the [[topics/the-interface-problem|interface problem]] catalogues why such a report is currently uninterpretable as evidence." The interface-problem appeal is doing all the shielding. The article is saying: even if such a system exists and reports no phenomenal spatial arena, the report cannot be interpreted as evidence. So (3) is not a challenge the framework can lose; it is a challenge the framework has already insulated itself against by appeal to a separate framework commitment. The article's honest closing paragraph — "the framework's net falsifiability is genuinely modest" — is correct in form but understates the asymmetry. Two of the three in-practice items are in-principle items in disguise; only (2) is genuinely in-practice.

The in-principle items (4)–(6) are honestly named as inaccessible by framework commitments. Fine. But once we acknowledge that (1) and (3) collapse into the same category, the in-practice column is genuinely just (2), and the article's net falsifiability is *weaker* than the article's own honest framing acknowledges.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

The article reifies the spatial experiencer at line 81: "the world is always *someone's* world, seen from *somewhere*." The someone is exactly the metaphysical posit the analysis should deconstruct, not assume. Your perspective problem asks why spatial representation has a "centred, first-person character" — but the centred character is what arises when consciousness mistakes its own conventional designations for substantial entities. There is no perceiver at the centre of the spatial arena; the arena and the perceiver are co-arising conventional designations with no independent existence on either side.

The dual-domain reading at lines 117–121 makes the reification worse, not better, by giving the perceiver an entire ontological domain to live in. The non-physical domain inherits all the metaphysical problems the substantial-self framing had — what individuates it, what persists across time, what relations it has to other non-physical domains, why it has the spatial structure it has — without solving any of them. The Buddhist analysis dissolves the 3D-world representation problem by dissolving both the world and the representer; the dualist reading preserves both and adds a domain.

The presence problem at line 91 — "It is an all-encompassing spatial envelope that contains the perceiver" — is exactly the kind of phenomenological description that contemplative practice undoes. Sustained attention to the felt envelope reveals it as conceptually constructed: the envelope is generated by the continuous attention to the boundary between perceived and unperceived space, not by an antecedent spatial container that the perceiver is "in." The article treats the envelope as a fact about consciousness. It is a fact about a particular trained attentional habit.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Phenomenology-as-architecture asserted at content-report tier
- **File**: `obsidian/topics/three-dimensional-world-representation-problem.md`
- **Location**: lines 61–63 (the felt-3D-world enumeration in §"The Phenomenal Residue"); line 101 (the "structural framework" formulation in §"Why Computational Accounts Fall Short"); §"The Presence Problem" at lines 87–93 (the "sense of presence" and "spatial envelope" claims)
- **Problem**: The article asserts an enumerated phenomenology of 3D experience — felt depth, phenomenal solidity, traversable emptiness, qualitative distance-character, ambient spatial envelope — and treats the enumeration as a datum the explanatory account must accommodate. The 2026-05-22 ChatGPT outer-review's Calibrated Phenomenology Ladder task (queued this morning, see todo.md P2) identifies exactly this register as the corpus's current methodological gap. The reliability ladder the calibration would install: (a) content reports — high reliability, (b) agency/passivity reports — mid, (c) process reports — low, (d) causal-power reports — lower still, (e) metaphysical inferences — framework-internal only. The article's enumerated phenomenology operates at (b)–(d) register but is presented at (a) register. Eliminative materialism, illusionism, and the Map's own [[introspection-architecture-void-cluster]] each independently raise the reliability concern; the article does not engage any of them at this passage.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Add a calibration insert near the §"The Phenomenal Residue" opening — one to two paragraphs — that locates the enumerated phenomenology on the ladder, distinguishes content-report claims (felt-depth, felt-presence) from process-report claims (the introspective description of *how* the experience is structured), and names the contested-register claims as such. The article's argument can survive — the explanatory-gap framing does not require process-report-tier confidence — but the current passage operates at content-report-tier confidence on claims that are not at that tier.

### Issue 2: Dual-domain reading at line 117–121 is consequence-of-tenet, not consequence-of-the-3D-puzzle
- **File**: `obsidian/topics/three-dimensional-world-representation-problem.md`
- **Location**: §"Relation to Site Perspective" lines 117–121 (the closing paragraph and the binding-problem paragraph that follows)
- **Problem**: The closing paragraphs distribute specific cognitive functions across the physical and non-physical domains: physical handles "depth estimation, sensor fusion, spatial mapping, motor guidance"; consciousness contributes "the phenomenal world itself: the felt three-dimensionality, the sense of presence, the perspectival organisation, the qualitative character of spatial experience." The distribution is presented as the article's preferred reading of the 3D-world puzzle. It is not what the 3D-world puzzle supports. Interactionist dualism, panpsychism, neutral monism, Russellian monism, and even sophisticated functionalism all accommodate the explanatory-gap data without the specific division of labour the closing paragraph asserts. - **Severity**: High

## Counterarguments to Address

### The phenomenal-3D-world enumeration is reliable testimony
- **Current content says**: lines 61–63 enumerate the felt characteristics of 3D space (depth, solidity, emptiness, qualitative distance, ambient envelope) and treat the enumeration as an accurate description of conscious experience the explanatory account must accommodate.
- **A critic would argue**: Introspection of the structural features of spatial experience is exactly the kind of report the introspection-architecture cluster ([[introspection-architecture-independence-scoring]], [[apex/introspection-architecture-void-cluster]]) flags as systematically unreliable. The contents may be roughly accurate; the structural description of the contents almost certainly is not.
- **Suggested response**: Install the Calibrated Phenomenology Ladder insert (Issue 1 recommendation) and qualify the enumerated phenomenology with the appropriate reliability tier. The argument does not depend on process-report-tier confidence and survives the qualification.

### The functional indistinguishability of human and AI spatial cognition is evidence for physicalism, not against it
- **Current content says**: lines 65 and 105 use artificial systems implementing depth-estimation and 3D-mapping as evidence that the computation alone does not produce the experience.
- **A critic would argue**: The functional indistinguishability of human and AI spatial cognition is *exactly* what heterophenomenology predicts. Same functional organisation, same reports, same consciousness. The article cannot use functional indistinguishability as evidence against physicalism without first establishing that the AI lacks the experience, which the article does not establish — it merely asserts "there is no reason to attribute phenomenal spatial experience to these systems." The asymmetry is the question, not the answer.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge the heterophenomenological reading explicitly. The article's argument can route through the [[the-interface-problem]] — the asymmetric attribution is grounded in the bootstrapping issue, not in observed functional difference — but the routing should be made explicit so the reader can see the move.

### In-practice falsifiability items (1) and (3) collapse into in-principle items
- **Current content says**: §"What Would Challenge This View?" lines 105–109 lists (1) predictive-coding derivation, (2) aphantasia dissociation, (3) AI-no-phenomenal-spatial-report as in-practice challenges; (4)–(6) as in-principle.
- **A critic would argue**: Item (1)'s "deriving the perspectival structure of phenomenal space" cannot be empirically distinguished from "deriving a representation that reports phenomenal space" — by the article's own (3) escape clause. So (1) is in-principle. Item (3) explicitly appeals to the interface problem to insulate against any AI report. So (3) is in-principle. Only (2) is genuinely in-practice. The article's "modest net falsifiability" closing is more accurate than its three-in-practice / three-in-principle list suggests.
- **Suggested response**: Either (a) tighten item (1) by specifying what empirical criterion would distinguish a derivation of phenomenal space from a derivation of a representation reporting phenomenal space — if no such criterion exists, demote (1) to in-principle; or (b) restate the in-practice column as "(2) is the genuinely in-practice item; (1) and (3) become in-practice only conditional on instrumentation the framework does not currently have" and let the asymmetry be visible.

### Construction, perspective, and presence are not three independent problems
- **Current content says**: §"Three Dimensions of the Problem" presents construction, perspective, and presence as three distinguishable aspects "each posing its own challenge."
- **A critic would argue**: The perspective problem is the indexical-identity problem in spatial dress and is already counted in the Map's case against MWI (Tenet 4) and in the [[topics/voids/haecceity-void]]-adjacent material. The construction problem is the binding problem applied to space and is already counted at [[the-binding-problem]] and [[quantum-holism-and-phenomenal-unity]]. The presence problem is the closest to a genuinely independent contribution. Counting all three as independent puzzles overcounts the spatial-cognition cluster's contribution to the overall dualist case — exactly the convergence-double-counting pattern [[concession-convergence]] and [[common-cause-null]] are designed to discipline.
- **Suggested response**: Add a short paragraph in §"Three Dimensions of the Problem" naming the overlap. The construction and perspective problems can be presented as *spatial-cognitive instances* of corpus-wide arguments (binding, indexical identity) rather than as independent contributions; the presence problem can be presented as the article's distinctive addition. The honest framing strengthens the article by clarifying what it uniquely contributes.
