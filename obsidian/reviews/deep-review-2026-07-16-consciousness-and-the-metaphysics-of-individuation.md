## Pessimistic Analysis Summary

### Critical Issues Found

- **Nagel paraphrase-as-quote cited to the wrong work (quote-fidelity defect)** — FIXED. The split-brain paragraph rendered Nagel's position as a direct quotation: *"too much unity ... to say 'two', yet too much separation ... to say 'one'."* attributed to Nagel, "Brain Bisection and the Unity of Consciousness", *Synthese* 22 (1971). Verbatim-checked against the primary source and the wording is **not Nagel's**. It is the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy "Unity of Consciousness" entry's characterization of Nagel (§4.3.1, "How Many Centres of Consciousness?"): *"there is too much unity (for example in life outside the laboratory and even in behaviour within) to say 'two', yet too much separation in the specially contrived laboratory situations to say 'one'."* The article's two ellipses correspond exactly to the elided SEP parentheticals — a clear tell that the "quote" was lifted from the SEP summary, not Nagel's text. The substance is genuinely Nagel's (SEP faithfully summarizes him), so per quote-fidelity discipline the fix is to **de-quote to an attributed paraphrase** rather than delete: now reads *"…display what Thomas Nagel characterised as too much unity to count them as two subjects, yet too much separation to count them as one—for him there is no whole number of centres of consciousness in such cases."* Attribution to Nagel preserved; false quotation marks removed; genuine Nagelian content ("no whole number of centres of consciousness") retained.
