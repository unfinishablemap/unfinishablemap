# Adversarial Audit of "Psychedelics and the Filter Model" (The Unfinishable Map)

## 0. Executive Verdict

The article under review is unusually well-written for a piece making strong metaphysical claims from neuroimaging data, and it is *more careful than its sister concept page* on filter theory in several places — for example, the REBUS section explicitly concedes that REBUS is "conceived within a physicalist paradigm." Nevertheless, against the Map's own stated standards (the "evidential-status discipline," "direct-refutation-discipline," and the "coherence-inflation countermeasures" repeatedly invoked in the changelog), the article fails to enforce those disciplines on itself. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

The central problems are four. **(i)** It overstates the empirical robustness and specificity of the DMN-ego-dissolution link, citing only Carhart-Harris (2012), Tagliazucchi (2016), and Lebedev (2015) — the 2010s canon — and missing the substantial reframings introduced by Siegel et al. (*Nature*, 2024), Timmermann et al. (*J. Neurosci.*, 2024), and the claustrum/thalamic-gating literature (Doss et al. 2020; Brennan et al. 2024; Krzyzosiak 2025), which redistribute the explanatory load *away* from a specifically-DMN story. **(ii)** Its core argumentative move — "less brain activity, more experience, therefore filter" — is underdetermined; the article does not seriously engage the production-side accommodation it gestures at, treats REBUS as if its predictions could "double-count" for filter theory while admitting that they don't differ empirically, and largely ignores the strongest non-dualist competitor (Letheby/Gerrans predictive self-binding) by name. **(iii)** It engages exactly one of the major production-model rebuttals (REBUS, and only superficially) and is silent on Entropic Brain v2, predictive processing more broadly, Global Workspace Theory, and IIT. **(iv)** It is stylistically more confident than the filter-theory concept page it depends on, importing claims (e.g., the "Mind at Large" reading of Huxley, the quantum/Orch-OR mechanism) as if they were merely "illuminating" tenets when in fact they would, if true, do most of the metaphysical work — a coherence-inflation pattern the Map's own methodology pages flag as a known failure mode. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

Below, the four dimensions in turn, with specific 2024–2026 citations where they bear, and a concluding **Concrete Potential Improvements** section.

## 1. Empirical Accuracy of Neuroimaging and Phenomenological Claims

### 1.1 DMN suppression is real, but the article privileges a 2012-era framing

The article's headline claim is correct in its weakest form: classic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT) reliably reduce intra-DMN functional connectivity and shift between-network functional connectivity. This is consistent with the systematic review by Gattuso, Perkins et al. (2023, *Neurosci. Biobehav. Rev.*; refreshed in *J. Neurochem.* 2024) and the *Frontiers in Neuroscience* scoping review by Stoliker et al. (2025). The Siegel et al. (2024, *Nature*) longitudinal precision-mapping study from the Washington University / Imperial College collaboration is, on the Map's own evidence standards, the strongest single confirmation to date: psilocybin produced threefold greater FC disruption than methylphenidate (an active stimulant control), with persistent (~3-week) reduction in anterior-hippocampus–DMN connectivity. The Avakian (2025, *Cureus*) EEG-fMRI work on DMT reaches similar conclusions. [Nature + 2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07624-5)

But this is also where the article begins to drift. Three specific problems:

**(a) The Siegel et al. (2024) reframing is not cited, and it matters.** Siegel et al. explicitly characterise the effect as "desynchronization across spatial scales (areal, global), which dissolved network distinctions by reducing correlations *within* and anticorrelations *between* networks." The phenomenon is not best described as "DMN goes down"; it is *cortex-wide desynchronization, strongest in association cortex (including but not limited to DMN), which dissolves network identity altogether*. This recasts the empirical picture in a way that *changes the inferential structure* of the article: it is not a specific organ (the "filter") that loosens, but the very partition of the cortex into networks that breaks down. A filter-theory reading is still possible — one can simply say "the filter is the partition" — but that re-description is post-hoc and the article does not make it. [Illinois Experts](https://experts.illinois.edu/en/publications/psilocybin-desynchronizes-thehuman-brain/)

**(b) The DMN-ego-dissolution correlation is weaker and less specific than the article suggests.** The article cites Lebedev et al. (2015) for "ego dissolution under psilocybin correlates specifically with reduced DMN integrity." This is correct as far as it goes, but: (i) Tagliazucchi et al. (2016) — the very paper the article cites *for* the DMN claim — actually argues that *increased global* functional connectivity, not DMN-specific decrease, is what tracks ego dissolution; (ii) Timmermann et al. (2024, *J. Neurosci.*) reports that DMT-induced ego dissolution intensity correlates most strongly with **alpha-band power collapse**, an EEG-level finding that is *not* DMN-specific; (iii) Mason et al. (Maastricht, 2020) found that prefrontal and hippocampal **glutamate** concentrations (not DMN connectivity per se) tracked positive-vs-negative ego dissolution. The MIND Foundation's review of the ego-dissolution literature puts the point bluntly: "When examined closely, Carhart-Harris's own work does not conclusively identify the DMN as the main character in the story." The article's "the network most associated with maintaining the narrative self is the network most disrupted" is technically defensible but misleadingly clean. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

**(c) The "more DMN suppression → more intense subjective effects" claim is partially over-stated.** The article writes: "The greater the DMN suppression under psilocybin, the more intense the reported subjective effects (Carhart-Harris et al., 2012)." This is true of that one early study at a small N, but later high-N work — including Smigielski et al. (2019) — finds the relationship is *mediated* by mPFC–PCC decoupling specifically and by combined-task context, not by gross DMN suppression. The dose–response curve is also dirty: at very high doses, network identity dissolves rather than being uniformly suppressed (Siegel 2024). The article would be more honest with a hedge such as "early studies reported a dose–response between DMN suppression and subjective intensity; recent precision-imaging work suggests the relationship is mediated by network-identity dissolution rather than simple suppression." [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

### 1.2 The article misses the main 2020–2025 mechanism candidates

The article presents the DMN as if it were the structural locus of "filtering." The 2020–2025 literature has, however, increasingly *shifted explanatory weight to other circuits*:

- **Claustrum.** Doss et al. (2020, *NeuroImage: Clinical*) and the cortico-claustro-cortical (CCC) model in Doss, Považan et al. (2022, *J. Psychiatry Neurosci.*) propose that the claustrum — densely 5-HT2A-expressing, providing widespread glutamatergic cortical inputs — is the upstream trigger for psychedelic-induced cortical desynchronization. Krzyzosiak et al. (2025, *Pharmacol. Rep.*) provide neurochemical evidence from direct intra-claustral psilocin infusion. The DMN, on this view, is a *downstream consequence* of claustral disinhibition, not the filtering organ itself. [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9014750/)[Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43440-025-00817-3)
- **Thalamic gating.** Multiple 2024–2025 papers (e.g., *Translational Psychiatry* 2024 on the thalamus in psychosis and psychedelics) implicate the thalamic reticular nucleus and high-order thalamic nuclei as the filter component that 5-HT2A activation disrupts, leading to amplification of unprocessed sensory input. This is in fact more naturally a "thalamic-filter-loosens" story than a "DMN-filter-loosens" story, and arguably tracks Huxley's metaphor better. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02691-0)[Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43440-025-00817-3)
- **5-HT2C and non-5-HT2A pathways.** Brennan et al. (2024, *Neuroscience*) argued, contra the canonical 5-HT2A story, that 5-HT2C receptors mediate key claustrum-ACC synaptic effects of the prototypic psychedelic DOI. *Molecular Psychiatry* (2025) showed psychedelic neuroplasticity in cortical neurons that *lack* 5-HT2A receptors. The simple "5-HT2A → DMN suppression → ego dissolution" pipeline the article tacitly relies on is, in 2026, no longer adequate. [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0301008224000960)
- **Cortical traveling waves and neural avalanches.** Recent work (Toker et al. 2022, Mediano et al. 2024–26 preprints) on travelling-wave restructuring and on shifts in criticality/avalanche statistics under psychedelics suggests that the relevant level of explanation may be *neural-dynamic* rather than *network-anatomical*. None of this is cited.

The article's "Neuroimaging Paradox" section thus represents the 2012–2016 evidence base, not the 2020–2025 evidence base. The Map's filter-theory concept page is already partly aware of this — it acknowledges REBUS and the framework-internal nature of the case — but the topic article appears to have been drafted from older literature and not refreshed.

### 1.3 Entropy, complexity, and the "missing 2018–2026" updates

The article cites neither the **Entropic Brain Hypothesis 2.0** (Carhart-Harris 2018, *Neuropharmacology*) nor the multiple replications of increased Lempel–Ziv complexity / signal diversity under psychedelics (Schartner et al. 2017, *Sci. Rep.*; Mediano et al. 2026 preprint on bioRxiv distinguishing psychedelics from stimulants on multi-scale entropy and meta-state complexity). This is a significant omission because the Entropic Brain Hypothesis offers a *production-compatible* explanation of richer subjective experience: more disordered neural activity ≈ richer phenomenology, no transmission needed. The article gestures at this in one sentence ("a materialist can partly accommodate this") and then drops it. The entropy/avalanche/criticality literature is, on its own terms, a *prediction* of psychedelic richness from physical-dynamical considerations — making the article's "production models must explain how a brain doing less produces a mind experiencing more" framing a strawman. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)[topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

### 1.4 Phenomenological measurement: a hidden weakness

The article appeals to the phenomenology of psychedelic experience (ego dissolution, oceanic boundlessness, ineffability, noetic quality) as if the data were unproblematic. But the standard instruments — the **MEQ30** (Barrett et al. 2015), the **11D-ASC** / **5D-ASC** (Dittrich 1998; Studerus et al. 2010), and the **EDI** (Nour et al. 2016) — each have specific known weaknesses:

- The **MEQ30** has been criticised as carrying "Christian bias" (Mosurinjohn et al. 2023) and pre-loading mystical framings (Cole-Turner 2024; Stocker et al. 2026, *Int. J. Methods Psychiatr. Res.*). Stocker et al. find that cross-site differences in MEQ effect sizes are driven by **expectancy and contextual factors**, not by the drug per se. The instrument's authors themselves noted self-selection bias (participants who report a "mystical or profound" experience and then complete the questionnaire about it). [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8959755/)
- The **11D-ASC** retains Dittrich's original 1998 factor structure and has the same expectancy-prone scoring; Liechti et al. (2017) noted comparable cross-laboratory variability.
- **Muthukumaraswamy et al. (2021, *J. Psychopharmacol.*)** explicitly argued that **expectancy effects** plausibly mediate much of the persisting positive effect attributed to psychedelics; demand characteristics in unblinded designs (which is almost all psychedelic research, including all of Imperial, Hopkins, NYU, and Beckley Foundation work) are severe. [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8959755/)
- The **EDI** is psychometrically reasonable but shares the demand-characteristic problem: subjects know they are receiving a psychedelic, and are usually familiar with the "ego dissolution" trope before participating.

The article's epistemological section briefly acknowledges that "psychedelics disrupt the very neural processes required for accurate introspection" and replies that "this objection proves too much." That reply is too quick. The relevant question is not *whether* metacognitive disruption invalidates *all* introspective reports, but *whether* psychedelic reports are subject to *specific and systematic* biases (expectancy, set-and-setting priming, mystical framing in instruments, retrospective confabulation). The current literature suggests yes. **A genuinely adversarial version of this article would treat the phenomenological data as significantly noisier than the article currently does.** [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)[topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

### 1.5 The quantum section is the weakest empirical move

The article cites **Babcock et al. (2024)** on UV superradiance in tryptophan mega-networks and **Kerskens & Pérez López (2022)** on putative quantum entanglement in living brains. Both are real papers, but the article's epistemic treatment of them is more permissive than the Map's evidential-status discipline allows. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

- The Babcock et al. paper shows superradiance in *tryptophan networks* in microtubules — it is a quantum-optical finding about a specific photophysical effect. It does *not* show that this effect is functionally relevant to consciousness, nor that psychedelics modulate it. The article's leap from "quantum collective effects exist in microtubules" to "psychedelics might expand the bandwidth of the consciousness-brain interface" is several inferential steps the article does not pay for. (The Wikipedia Orch-OR entry, as of 2025–26, also notes the **ferritin UV absorption** objection — that ferritin in neurons would absorb biophoton UV before it reaches microtubules to cause superradiance — which is not addressed.) [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)
- The Kerskens & Pérez López (2022) result has not, to my knowledge as of 2026-05, been independently replicated; *J. Phys. Commun.* is a low-impact, open-access venue. Treating it as evidential support for quantum consciousness without flagging the single-study, unreplicated status is exactly the move the Map's "evidential-status discipline" exists to prevent.
- The Hameroff (2024) "Psychedelics, microtubules, and quantum consciousness" Cambridge chapter is, to put it bluntly, advocacy literature for a position whose champion is the same person — a *citatorial* problem the article does not flag.

The article does say "this remains a possibility to investigate rather than a commitment," but immediately frames it as a "concrete mechanism for the Map's tenet" — which is itself a coherence-inflation move (citing a contested mechanism as confirmation of a tenet that the mechanism would, if true, support). ## 2. Argumentative Soundness

### 2.1 The core inference is invalid as stated

The article's structural argument is roughly:

> P1. Psychedelics reduce DMN activity.
> P2. Psychedelics produce subjectively richer/expanded experience.
> P3. On production models, less brain activity should produce less consciousness.
> C. Therefore, filter theory (brain constrains rather than produces consciousness) is supported.

P3 is the hidden premise, and it is false as stated. **No serious production theorist holds that "neural activity reduction → consciousness reduction" is a general principle.** Production theorists hold that *specific* neural activity patterns instantiate *specific* conscious contents. Reducing activity in *one specific network* (the self-modelling network) while *increasing variability and global integration elsewhere* should, on production models, alter the *content* and *structure* of consciousness — including potentially making it feel richer, more unified, less ego-bound — *not* reduce its quantity. The Entropic Brain Hypothesis says exactly this. So the article is rebutting a strawman version of physicalism.

The filter-theory concept page, to its credit, acknowledges this in a passing parenthetical ("A materialist can partly accommodate this: suppressing the DMN disinhibits other networks, so the brain does *different* things rather than strictly *less*"). But it then says "But it leaves the deeper puzzle untouched." The deeper puzzle, however, is the **hard problem of consciousness**, not the psychedelic data — and the hard problem is *just as much a problem for filter theory*. Filter theory does not explain why a "filter" should produce phenomenal experience either; it relocates the explanatory burden onto whatever is being filtered, which is unspecified. [concepts/filter-theory](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/filter-theory/)

### 2.2 The underdetermination problem is not handled

The article's treatment of REBUS is the clearest illustration. It says:

> "REBUS describes the *mechanism* of filtering without addressing the deeper question of what is being filtered. ... The REBUS model explains *how* the brain constrains experience; filter theory asks *why* the brain constrains something it supposedly produces." [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

This is rhetorically slick but conceptually evasive. REBUS does not "describe filtering"; it describes Bayesian belief updating with relaxation of precision-weighted priors. *Re-describing* this as "filtering" begs the question. The fact that one *can* re-describe REBUS in filter-theoretic vocabulary does not establish that the filter description has any independent purchase — it just shows that "filter" is a sufficiently elastic metaphor to accommodate any account of constraint or modulation. This is exactly the failure mode the Map's "coherence-inflation countermeasures" page flags (per the changelog entries of 2026-05-12 01:46 UTC and 2026-05-12 01:15 UTC).

The honest version of the article's REBUS section would say: "REBUS and filter theory make identical empirical predictions; they differ only in metaphysics; the empirical evidence therefore cannot adjudicate between them; the choice between them depends on prior commitments not on the data."

Multiple competitor theories cover the same data:

- **Entropic Brain Hypothesis** (Carhart-Harris 2014, 2018): richer phenomenology = higher neural entropy. No filter needed.
- **REBUS** (Carhart-Harris & Friston 2019; REBAS extension Stoliker et al. 2023, *Sci. Rep.*): relaxed priors permit bottom-up signal to dominate. No filter needed.
- **Predictive Processing / Active Inference** (Friston; Clark 2016, *Surfing Uncertainty*; Seth 2021, *Being You*; Hohwy 2013): consciousness as controlled hallucination; psychedelics loosen constraints.
- **Predictive Self-Binding** (Letheby & Gerrans 2017; Letheby 2021, *Philosophy of Psychedelics*, OUP): the self-model is normally transparent and becomes opaque under psychedelics; ego dissolution is the brain's self-model failing in a specific way. Letheby's central argument is *explicitly anti-dualist and anti-filter*: psychedelic insight is a "dispelling-the-illusion" event, not a "third-eye" event. Letheby is the strongest single philosophical opponent the article does not name. (The article cites Gładziejewski 2024 on the third-eye/dispelling-the-illusion distinction — but Gładziejewski is *deploying* Letheby's distinction without crediting it.) [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)
- **Global Workspace Theory** (Dehaene; Baars; Mashour et al. 2021): increased global broadcasting under psychedelics consistent with richer reported phenomenology.
- **Integrated Information Theory** (Tononi, Koch; Gallimore 2015): empirically, MEG signal diversity and Lempel–Ziv complexity *increase* under psychedelics (Schartner et al. 2017; replicated 2024), suggesting Φ may rise, consistent with richer experience without filter theory.

The article addresses none of these by name except REBUS, and even REBUS is treated as a vehicle for filter theory rather than as a competitor.

### 2.3 The asymmetry problem is the elephant

This is, in my judgement, the single most important methodological weakness of the article. The article surveys the cases where the filter loosens (psychedelics) and uses these as evidence. The Map's broader corpus does the same for NDEs, terminal lucidity, contemplative practice, lucid dreaming. But:

- **Deep anaesthesia** reliably extinguishes phenomenal experience.
- **Slow-wave sleep** does the same.
- **Stroke, severe TBI, advanced Alzheimer's** produce reliable, structured, progressive degradation of consciousness — including, eventually, its disappearance.
- **Locked-in syndrome** preserves consciousness; **persistent vegetative state** progressively does not.

These cases are at least as numerous and at least as well-documented as the filter-supportive cases. Filter theory must explain them too — and it does, by appealing to "interface damage" or "filter narrowing to a near-closed state." But this is *exactly* the kind of accommodation that filter theory criticises production models for performing. **Both theories accommodate the data; the asymmetry vanishes once one notices that filter theory has to do the same kind of accommodation work production theory does.** [topics/anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface/)

The Map *is* aware of this — the anaesthesia article handles it carefully and conservatively, with explicit acknowledgement that "production models account for the change straightforwardly: disrupt one subsystem and the overall output shifts." But the psychedelics article does not import this caution. It frames psychedelics as the "single most direct pharmacological challenge to production models," which is rhetorically strong but factually contestable: deep anaesthesia is at least as direct a challenge to *filter* models. The asymmetry is not in the world; it is in the article's selection of cases. [concepts/default-mode-network](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/default-mode-network/)[topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

### 2.4 The phenomenological reliability problem is dismissed too quickly

The article writes: "If any alteration of metacognition invalidates introspective reports, then reports from *all* altered states — including the focused states of scientific observation and philosophical reasoning — become suspect." [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

This is a tu quoque. The relevant claim is not that *all* altered states are invalid, but that *psychedelic* states involve specific, documented disruptions of:

- Working memory (impaired)
- Source monitoring (impaired — confabulation literature, Bernstein & Loftus 2009)
- Reality testing (impaired by definition under hallucinogens)
- Temporal binding (disrupted, per the volatile-agent / sevoflurane parallel the Map's own anaesthesia article notes)
- Metacognition itself (Metzinger 2003, cited by the article, makes the strong claim that the self-model becomes *opaque* — meaning self-introspection now reports on the model rather than seeing-through it) [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

These are specific deficits, not generic "altered metacognition." A 2024–2026 adversarial reader would push hard on whether reports of "more real than real" experience under conditions of *known disrupted reality-testing* are evidential for the actual reality of expanded consciousness, or whether they are evidential for the disrupted-reality-testing itself. The article does not engage this. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

### 2.5 "Cross-cultural convergence" is weaker than presented

The article appeals to Stace (1960) and cross-cultural convergence of mystical phenomenology. This is one of the oldest tropes in the literature and one of the most contested. Steven Katz's (1978, *Mysticism and Philosophical Analysis*) constructivist critique — that mystical phenomenology is heavily mediated by prior cultural framing, training, expectation — is mainstream in religious studies. The Map's own filter-theory page does not cite Katz; nor does this article. The Stocker et al. (2026) finding that MEQ scores vary systematically with site, context, and expectation is direct evidence that the "cross-cultural convergence" claim has been partly produced by the convergence of *instruments* and *expectations*, not by the convergence of underlying experience. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

## 3. Engagement with the Strongest Production-Model Rebuttals

This is the section where the article is most clearly under-engaged. Below, the rebuttals the article should have addressed, in order of strength.

### 3.1 Letheby's Predictive Self-Binding (the strongest single rebuttal)

Letheby's *Philosophy of Psychedelics* (OUP, 2021) and Letheby & Gerrans (2017, *Neuroscience of Consciousness*) constitute the most developed philosophical account of psychedelic experience under naturalism. The core move: ego dissolution does not reveal a "wider consciousness" being unmasked; it reveals that *the self was always a construct of the brain's predictive self-model*, and the model has destabilised. The "more real than real" quality is a direct prediction: when the self-model becomes opaque, subjects experience model-as-model rather than model-as-self, which feels more revealing than ordinary experience — but is in fact *less* informationally tied to reality, not more.

Crucially, Letheby's account *uses the same neuroimaging data the article uses*, including DMN suppression and Tagliazucchi's global functional connectivity, and *produces the opposite metaphysical conclusion*. The article cites Metzinger's transparency thesis approvingly without noting that Metzinger (and Letheby, who builds on him) explicitly *denies* the existence of an extra-physical consciousness for the brain to filter. The "minimal self" the article appeals to as "the witness of this very dissolution" is, on Metzinger's account, *itself a model component that has not yet decohered*, not evidence of a transcendent subject. **This is a major omission.** The article is in effect having an argument with a strawman naive materialist while ignoring the trained philosopher whose work most directly threatens its conclusion.

### 3.2 The Entropic Brain Hypothesis

The article does not engage Carhart-Harris (2014, *Frontiers in Hum. Neurosci.*) or its 2018 update (*Neuropharmacology*). The EBH proposes that richer phenomenology *just is* higher neural entropy / criticality — there is no further question about "why does less activity produce more experience?" because the relevant variable is entropy, not activity-quantity. The 2024–2026 literature has continued to refine this: Mediano et al. (2026 preprint) shows that psychedelics distinctively raise multi-scale entropy and meta-state complexity compared to stimulants. The Tagliazucchi & Carhart-Harris programme is — *on the article's own evidence* — a working production-side explanation.

The article's only response is the throwaway "the disinhibition account describes what happens *neurally*; filter theory explains why the *experiential* result is enrichment rather than noise." But the EBH explicitly addresses this: more entropy means more accessible mental states, more flexible representational configurations, more "primary consciousness." Whether one accepts this or not, the article *must* engage with it directly to support its claim that production models cannot explain the data. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

### 3.3 Predictive Processing more broadly

Karl Friston's free-energy principle, Andy Clark's *Surfing Uncertainty* (2016), and Anil Seth's *Being You* (2021) — all of which explicitly treat psychedelics — represent a substantial body of work that explains psychedelic phenomenology as *altered controlled hallucination*. None is cited. Seth's "controlled hallucination" framing is particularly relevant: it predicts that loosened priors will *increase the felt vividness* of bottom-up signal without any expanded access to anything. The article needs to address why this is not the simpler explanation.

### 3.4 Global Workspace Theory

Dehaene and Baars are not cited. GWT predicts that psychedelics increase global broadcasting (consistent with Tagliazucchi 2016), and *that the felt richness is the broadcasting itself*. Not engaged.

### 3.5 Integrated Information Theory

IIT's relationship to psychedelics is genuinely interesting and remains an open empirical question. Gallimore (2015, *Front. Hum. Neurosci.*) argues that psychedelics increase Φ. Lempel–Ziv complexity rises under psychedelics (Schartner et al. 2017). The article does not cite this work, even though it would be useful *to its own case*: if Φ rises under psychedelics, this is consistent with "more consciousness," and on IIT this is a purely physical fact. The article should at least note that IIT — a leading non-dualist theory — accommodates psychedelic enrichment without filter theory. (Note: IIT itself faces serious philosophical objections — the "expander" objection, the unfolding argument by Doerig et al., the 2023 IIT-as-pseudoscience open letter signed by 100+ researchers — but the article does not engage with these either, which would actually have *helped* its case by showing competitor weaknesses.)

### 3.6 Parsimony / Occam's Razor

The article's tenet section says: "The materialist assumption that reduced brain activity must reduce consciousness seems parsimonious — until the data contradicts it." But the data does not contradict it: as established in §2.1, no production model has that as a premise. The "Occam's Razor Has Limits" tenet is the Map's structural workaround for the parsimony objection to dualism, but its deployment here is too quick. Filter theory posits: [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)

1. An independently existing consciousness (a non-physical substance or property),
2. A brain that filters it,
3. A mechanism (Orch-OR-style quantum coupling, on the Map's reading),
4. A specific architecture of interface components (the Map's anaesthesia article posits four).

Production theory posits:

1. A brain that produces conscious experience under specific dynamical conditions.

The latter has the hard problem — that is conceded. But the former *also* has the hard problem (it just relocates it: why does some filtering arrangement of consciousness produce *this* experience rather than another?), *plus* the interaction problem (Kim, Tegmark), *plus* the pairing problem (which consciousness goes with which brain?), *plus* a hostage to empirical fortune on the quantum mechanism. The article's claim that "neither view is obviously simpler" understates the asymmetry; on standard accounting, filter theory has substantially more theoretical commitments. The article should at least make explicit that it is accepting this cost because it judges the explanatory benefit worth it. ### (c) Improvements to the site's broader methodology

1. **Make outer reviews multi-source and at least sometimes domain-expert.** The changelog shows daily outer-review commissions across three services (likely all LLM-based). LLMs are good at consistency-checking and detecting label leakage; they are weak at noticing literature drift, missing citations, and the strongest current opponents in a field. A monthly external review by a domain expert (neuroscientist; philosopher of mind; psychiatrist with psychedelic experience) would catch issues this audit caught that the Map's internal pipeline did not. The cost would be real but the epistemic value would be high. Concretely: the Imperial College, Hopkins, NYU, and Beckley Foundation networks all have philosophers (Letheby, Sjöstedt-Hughes, Timmermann themselves) who could be approached for paid reviews.
2. **Add a "literature-drift" review skill.** None of the named skills in the workflow appear to specifically check whether an article's empirical citations are current. 3. **Implement asymmetric-evidence-handling discipline.** The Map's "coherence-inflation countermeasures" page is excellent but does not specifically address the "selection of supportive altered states" pattern. A new discipline page — call it "altered-state symmetry discipline" — would require that any article citing psychedelics / NDEs / terminal lucidity / contemplative cessation as filter-supportive evidence explicitly also address anaesthesia, slow-wave sleep, and brain damage, and explicitly account for the symmetric accommodation work both theories must do. The Map already has these articles; the issue is that they are not enforced as a single dialectical position in articles that lean filter-supportive.
4. **Audit topic-tier articles against their concept-tier anchors.** The discrepancy between this topic article's confidence and the filter-theory and DMN concept pages' caution is exactly the kind of issue a discipline pass could detect: for each topic article, verify that its central claims do not exceed the calibration of the concept pages it depends on. 5. **Distinguish "tenet-coherent" from "tenet-evidenced" more carefully in topic articles.** The Map's apex articles (per the 2026-05-12 01:15 UTC refine-draft entry) now flag this distinction. Topic articles should do the same. The current psychedelics article reads as if psychedelic evidence *evidences* filter theory, when at most it is *consistent with* filter theory — a "tenet-coherent" but not "tenet-evidenced" position, in the Map's vocabulary.
6. **Track citation-recency at the corpus level.** A simple dashboard showing, for each topic article, the median year of cited empirical papers, and flagging articles where this is more than 5 years behind the publication date of the article, would help catch the staleness problem. The current article was written 2026-03-08 with empirical citations whose median year is approximately 2015.
7. **Consider whether the "Mind at Large" framing should be retired.** Huxley's "Mind at Large" / "reducing valve" metaphor is a poetic gloss that imports specific metaphysical content (consciousness as cosmic medium being filtered) which is *not* required by filter theory in its weaker forms (consciousness as non-physical individual property being transmitted). Multiple weaker formulations of filter theory are compatible with the data; the strong cosmic-consciousness version is not load-bearing for the Map's tenets. Disentangling them would lower the article's metaphysical exposure without weakening its case. 8. **Add an honest "minimum requirements for falsification" section.** Filter theory at the Map currently has the structure: "predicts pattern X" + "data shows X" + "therefore supported." A stronger discipline would require: "predicts pattern X" + "predicts not-Y if filter theory true" + "Y not observed" + "therefore supported." Currently the article makes the first half of this and not the second. The DMN concept page's "What Would Challenge This View?" section is the right template; the psychedelics article should have one.

## 6. Where the Article Makes Defensible Moves

To be fair, the article does several things well, and any revision should preserve them:

- **The phenomenological richness of psychedelic experience is correctly described.** Ego dissolution, oceanic boundlessness, "more real than real" quality, ineffability, noetic quality — these features are real and well-documented, even if their *interpretation* is contested.
- **The Griffiths et al. (2006) "67% top-five meaningful experiences" finding is correctly cited.** It is a real finding from a real lab and supports the claim that the subjective effects are at minimum extremely significant to subjects.
- **The recognition that REBUS is "conceived within a physicalist paradigm"** is honest and most filter-theorist authors would skip this concession.
- **The "selective access" point** — that psychedelic experiences are not random hallucination but selectively bring up suppressed material — is a real phenomenon (e.g., trauma-relevant memory under MDMA), and filter theory does predict it, even if other accounts can accommodate it too.
- **The article's restraint on quantum claims** ("This remains a possibility to investigate rather than a commitment") is correct in framing, even if the citations chosen overstate the evidence. [topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/)
- **The cross-linking discipline** to other Map articles (filter theory, DMN, terminal lucidity, anaesthesia, bandwidth of consciousness, dream consciousness, lucid dreaming, William James) is genuinely useful for readers and is one of the Map's stronger features.

## 7. Final Assessment

The article is the work of a competent philosophical writer who has been allowed by the review pipeline to draft a piece that is *more confident than the corpus it sits within*. The fix is not to add more material but to *bring the article's calibration into line with the filter-theory and DMN concept pages it depends on*, while updating its empirical citations to the 2024–2026 literature and engaging the philosophical opponents (above all Letheby) that it currently ignores.

The Map's broader methodology is, in this auditor's judgement, unusually sophisticated for a self-published philosophical project, and the changelog evidences a level of self-correction that exceeds what most academic projects manage. The specific failure here — that the psychedelics article slipped through with stale citations and inflated confidence — is the kind of failure the Map's own apparatus is designed to catch and which a more diverse review pipeline (in particular, occasional domain-expert reviews) would likely have caught earlier. The recommendation, then, is not that the Map's method is wrong, but that it has a known weakness around literature currency and around the gap between topic-tier and concept-tier calibration, and that this article is a clean example of where that weakness shows.
