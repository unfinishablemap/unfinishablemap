### Empirical-Claim-Fidelity ledger (findings, not metadata)

- **Britton (2019) inverted-U / dose-response — FAITHFUL, no change.** Article: "contemplative practice follows something like an inverted U-curve: beneficial in moderate doses but capable of producing genuine disturbance at extreme intensities." Verified against the paper (PMC6612475): Britton argues positive phenomena "follow a non-monotonic or inverted U-shaped trajectory where their typically positive effects eventually turn negative," mindfulness is "unlikely to be an exception," and an optimal dose exists "between extremes." The article's paraphrase is accurate and appropriately hedged ("something like"). No superlative/currency issue.
- **Lindahl et al. (2017) recovery/grounding attribution — FAITHFUL, no change.** Article attributes to Lindahl 2017 that "effective treatment … typically involves guided return to ordinary interface function — grounding practices …". Verified: the study's influencing-factors/remedies taxonomy explicitly lists **"Grounding activity"** as a management strategy (reported by 38% of practitioners, 55% of experts). Attribution sound.
- **Lindahl et al. (2017) taxonomy binning — CORRECTED (minor findings-fidelity error).** Article previously grouped "depersonalisation, derealisation, thought disruption" under **"cognitive disturbances."** The paper's 7-domain taxonomy (cognitive, perceptual, affective, somatic, conative, sense of self, social) places **derealisation in the Perceptual domain**, and depersonalisation/derealisation are standardly *dissociative / sense-of-self* phenomena (DSM dissociative disorders), not cognitive. Fixed: split the bracket into "dissociative disturbances of the sense of self (depersonalisation, derealisation)" and "cognitive disturbances (thought disruption, impaired executive function)." Length-neutral; no finding reversed, just correctly binned.
- **Lindahl et al. (2017) sample description — SHARPENED.** "practitioners from multiple traditions" → "three Western Buddhist traditions (Theravada, Zen, and Tibetan)" — the study's actual sample (equal representation, n=20 each). "Multiple traditions" risked reading as multiple *religions*; the study is titled "…in Western Buddhists." Faithful sharpening.
- **Sass & Parnas (2003) ipseity/hyperreflexivity/diminished self-affection — FAITHFUL, no change.** Terms and embodiment-polarity framing ("disembodied mind" vs "corporealized" body) match the paper's account; carried forward from prior metadata verification, no findings-fidelity issue.
