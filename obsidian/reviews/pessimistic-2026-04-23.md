# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-04-23
**Content reviewed**: [[topics/four-quadrant-dualism-taxonomy]] — substantially modified today (2026-04-23 11:47 UTC) to integrate [[trumping-preemption]] as the Q1-stabilizer mechanism; 2420 words; three prior deep reviews but no prior pessimistic review of this piece. The article proposes a two-axis taxonomy (mind-side thickness × physical-side thickness) organising dualist positions across four quadrants, with the Map's tenets used as a worked example of quadrant-restriction.

## Executive Summary

The taxonomy is organisationally useful and the "thickness" axis is a genuine conceptual move. But the piece has three deeper problems that the deep-review loop has not surfaced because deep-review operates from *within* the framework. First, the "thickness" axis itself bundles two things (ontological extravagance vs. causal authority) that the Stapp illustration shows can come apart — the axis needs either disambiguation or a principled argument that it really is one dimension. Second, several load-bearing empirical claims about the field ("heavily populated," "cleanest Q1 inhabitant," "dominates contemporary analytic philosophy") are asserted without citation and some of them are in tension with the article's own hedging. Third, the taxonomy is constitutively unfalsifiable: no observation moves a position between quadrants, and the reasons offered for Q1-stability, Q3-collapse, and Q4-under-population are metaphysical-aesthetic judgements, not empirical ones — the article should say so, not present them as quasi-empirical observations about the landscape.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Churchland would observe that *every* quadrant in this taxonomy presupposes the reality of phenomenal vocabulary. The min-mind pole still commits to qualia — "the felt redness of red, the minimum of the cogito." A genuinely eliminativist position has no place on the mind axis at all, because the mind axis *starts* with folk psychology already vindicated. The article acknowledges this only obliquely, by excluding eliminativism from the taxonomy per the 2026-04-21 stability note. But the exclusion is itself a commitment the article never defends. A serious taxonomy of mind-matter positions should have eliminativism on it as the limit where the mind axis goes to zero; treating eliminativism as outside the axes is to assume that consciousness is a datum rather than a disputed explanandum. Churchland would add: the "thickness" metaphor imports a substance-like picture — mind as a fluid that can be thick or thin — precisely the picture her work argues is obsolete. The taxonomy thus smuggles in its conclusion.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would press the single hardest question the article does not answer: *what does "ontological weight" measure?* The article offers three different hints — how much causal work the side does; how many properties the side has; how much structure beyond introspection/physics the side posits. These come apart. Saad's Q1 position arguably has *more* causal work on the mind side than Chalmers' Q2 epiphenomenalism (Chalmers' qualia do nothing; Saad's experiences preempt) but is classified as thinner because it has less *subliminal structure*. If "thickness" is subliminal structure, the Stapp illustration breaks: Stapp's thickness (rich agent, rich quantum) is *structural* richness but his *channel* is minimal — and the article moves between these senses freely. The paragraph that treats the Stapp case as vindicating the axis ("minimal intervention and rich mind are not the same dimension") actually undermines it: if channel width and ontological weight are independent dimensions, there should be three axes, not two. Dennett would also note that his own illusionism has no home in the taxonomy — which again signals that the mind axis is measuring "how much phenomenal realism you concede" rather than anything deeper.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Tegmark's decoherence calculation (2000) shows that coherence times for neural microtubules and synaptic processes fall in the 10⁻¹³–10⁻²⁰ s range — many orders below the 10⁻² s timescale of conscious cognition. The article's "physical-thin" pole is characterised as "causal closure *only at the indeterminacy level*: physics specifies probabilities but leaves outcomes undetermined." But at brain scales, standard decoherence says there *is* no residual indeterminacy above thermal noise; the would-be "indeterminacy level" is already closed by the environment. Tegmark would read the article's Stapp treatment and the Q1 physical-thin characterisation as leaning on an indeterminacy-room that physics denies exists in warm wet tissue. The article mentions MQI without engaging this objection; the Map's [[born-rule-violation-brain-interface-empirical-status-2026-04-23]] research note acknowledges it, but the taxonomy article does not borrow that acknowledgement. Tegmark would also press the Stapp placement in Q4: calling Stapp's quantum physics "ontologically rich" without noting that the richness is the unfalsifiable Process-1 / observer-state-vector structure concedes too much respect to a view the physics community has largely set aside.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

Deutsch would dispute the claim that the "No Many Worlds" tenet is "largely orthogonal to the thickness axes." This is false in at least two directions. First, on MWI there is no "probabilistic indeterminacy" for a min-physical position to lean on — the Schrödinger equation is fully deterministic and all outcomes are realised. The Q1 and Q4 min-physical readings the article lists all require an interpretation on which physics is incomplete at the probabilistic level. Rejecting MWI does not re-open that space; one also needs to adopt a specific *non*-MWI interpretation (GRW, Bohm, Copenhagen with a real collapse), each of which re-positions the physical axis. Second, if MWI is correct, the thickness axis itself is misspecified: the entire physical "thickness" question is about how much structure we attribute above the universal wavefunction, and branches-as-real dissolves the min-physical reading into a max-physical one. Deutsch's complaint is that the article treats MWI as just one rival tenet-target rather than as a position that *reshapes* the taxonomy. He would additionally note that the article's appeal to parsimony ("Q2 dominates contemporary analytic philosophy") is tendentious in exactly the way MWI theorists object to: parsimony of mathematical structure, in MWI's case, is what motivates *rejecting* the tenet-set's preferred quadrants.

### The Empiricist

Popper's verdict: the taxonomy is unfalsifiable in the strong sense. Not one observation would move a position between quadrants; the article relies on metaphysical-aesthetic arguments ("Q3 collapses into idealism under sustained pressure," "Q1 faces stability pressure," "Q4 is under-populated not because it fails but because a parsimony norm makes it hard to teach") that are not testable hypotheses about the landscape of positions but commitments of the taxonomer. The article makes this worse by using quasi-empirical language ("Q2 is over-populated," "Q1 looks attractive," "Q4 is under-populated in contemporary analytic philosophy") without citing any actual bibliometric or survey evidence. If the empirical claim is "most papers in philosophy of mind defend Q2 positions," then show the survey; if the claim is aesthetic ("Q2 is the default unreflective option"), then say so. The mixing of registers lets the article sound empirical while being evaluative. Popper would also note that the article's central hedging move — "stability is the live question" for Q1 — is itself an observation about internal consistency, not about evidence; importing "stability" language from dynamical systems into metaphysics is at best a metaphor and at worst a confusion about what philosophical positions can fail at.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nāgārjuna would read the thickness axis as an exemplary case of reification. Both the mind-side and the physical-side are treated as fluids with measurable density; the taxonomer's question becomes "how much of each substance exists." But the Madhyamaka critique is that *neither* side is a substance in the sense the axis presupposes — both are dependently-arisen flows whose "amount" is a question-mal-posed. The very existence of a well-defined "min-mind" pole (what is *bare* qualia, absent cognitive framing?) is philosophically contentious; phenomenology after Husserl and Merleau-Ponty has argued that isolable qualia-atoms are a theoretical abstraction, not an experiential given. If bare qualia are not a stable terminus, the mind axis has no zero. Nāgārjuna would add that the "thickness" metaphor inherits material imagery (density, weight, heft) from the very physicalist picture dualism is trying to contest; using it to frame dualism is to import the enemy's ontology into one's own taxonomy.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: "Thickness" axis conflates at least two distinct dimensions

- **File**: `topics/four-quadrant-dualism-taxonomy.md`
- **Location**: §"The Two Axes" (lines 45–65), §"Why the Thickness Axis Matters" (lines 115–121), Stapp illustration
- **Problem**: The article characterises thickness variously as "how much ontological work each side does" (line 47, 56), "how much structure beyond introspection" (thick mind bullet, line 53), "counterfactual sufficiency and authority-law selection" (Q1 delegatory bullet), and "channel width vs. observer ontology" (Stapp illustration). These are not the same dimension. The Stapp paragraph (line 119) is presented as the cleanest illustration *of* the thickness axis, but it in fact shows that at least two dimensions have been bundled: Stapp is min-channel, max-observer, max-physics — three values, not two. The article's response is to classify him as Q4 by bundling the two "max" values, but this is a choice, not a derivation.
- **Severity**: Medium (high conceptual stakes; the axis is the article's central contribution)
- **Recommendation**: Add a short §"Limits of the Thickness Metaphor" subsection (~150 words) acknowledging that "thickness" is a family of related but distinct measures (structural richness, causal work, counterfactual role, channel width), and specifying which the taxonomy is tracking. One honest move is: "The axis tracks structural richness above the minimum each side's proponents are forced to concede; it does not track causal work or channel width. The Stapp illustration shows that these dimensions can come apart — the taxonomy compresses them deliberately, at the cost of placing some positions (Stapp) by judgement rather than definition." This preserves the taxonomy's usefulness while disarming the Dennett-style objection.

### Issue 2: Empirical landscape claims lack evidence

- **File**: `topics/four-quadrant-dualism-taxonomy.md`
- **Location**: §41 opening ("three of which are heavily populated in the literature"), Q1 closing ("currently the cleanest Q1 inhabitant in contemporary philosophy"), Q2 closing ("Q2 dominates contemporary analytic philosophy of mind"), Q4 closing ("under-inhabited in contemporary analytic philosophy")
- **Problem**: These are empirical claims about the distribution of positions in a discipline. They are asserted without citation (no PhilPapers survey reference, no bibliometric count, no canonical Stanford Encyclopedia landscape citation). "Heavily populated" for Q1 is in direct tension with the article's own hedging just a few paragraphs later, which lists Saad (2025) as the main inhabitant, describes neutral monism as "technically monist," qualifies some Russellian readings, and says "minimal interactionism... *drifts toward* Q1." If Q1 has one clear contemporary inhabitant (Saad), calling three quadrants "heavily populated" misleads the reader about the opening claim's support.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either (a) replace "heavily populated" with "extensively discussed" (softer, and defensible from the SEP entries alone), or (b) name specific inhabitants for each quadrant with citations, or (c) explicitly reframe as an aesthetic-intellectual landscape claim rather than a bibliometric one ("Q2 is the default reflex of contemporary naturalism"). The "cleanest Q1 inhabitant" phrasing about Saad should be softened to "the most worked-out recent Q1 candidate in the literature known to us," which hedges the "cleanest" superlative and names the epistemic limitation.

### Issue 3: Monist positions placed in a dualist taxonomy without a clear rule

- **File**: `topics/four-quadrant-dualism-taxonomy.md`
- **Location**: Q1 bullet on neutral monism (line 74), Q3 bullets on Berkeley and Kastrup (lines 95–96)
- **Problem**: Neutral monism, Berkeleyan idealism, and Kastrup's analytic idealism are repeatedly placed in quadrants of a dualist taxonomy, with hedges like "technically monist but behaves like deflationary dualism," "limit case toward which Q3 points," and "rigorously monist rather than dualist, but illustrates the edge of the quadrant." No principled rule is given for when a monist position is admitted to the taxonomy and when it is not. Functional behaviour? Historical influence on dualism? Being a "limit case"? The inclusion is useful but it makes the scope of the taxonomy unclear. A critic could reasonably ask: is this a taxonomy of *dualisms*, or of *non-reductive positions on mind-matter*? If the latter, the title misleads; if the former, the monist entries do not belong.
- **Severity**: Low–Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add a single sentence to the §"The Two Axes" or §"The Four Quadrants" introduction: "The taxonomy ranges over non-reductive positions on mind-matter, including monist limit-cases, because their structure often illuminates what adjacent dualisms commit to. Monist entries are marked as such; their inclusion is for contrast, not classification-as-dualism." This makes the inclusion principled rather than ad hoc.

### Issue 4: "Stability" of Q1 is asserted as a philosophical outcome, not an argument

- **File**: `topics/four-quadrant-dualism-taxonomy.md`
- **Location**: Q1 closing paragraph (line 78), §"Where the Taxonomy Points Next" Q1 stability (line 137)
- **Problem**: The article treats "can Q1 be stabilised by trumping-preemption?" as an open empirical-sounding question, with directional answers ("toward Q2 if the law needs thick physics; toward Q4 if it needs thick mind"). But "stability" here means "can a non-stipulative authority-selecting law be articulated?" — a philosophical question about the availability of an argument, not an observation about which way a position drifts under pressure. The dynamical-systems connotations of "stability" ("pressure," "drift") add nothing to the analysis and invite the reader to think in terms of landscape forces rather than argumentative burdens. The prior pessimistic review of [[trumping-preemption]] (2026-04-22, Issue 1) flagged the same fiction-to-nature gap; the current article imports trumping as a stabilizer without inheriting that review's caveats.
- **Severity**: Medium (load-bearing for the cross-review's central integration)
- **Recommendation**: Rephrase "Q1 stability" as "Q1 articulability" or "Q1 specifiability." Then one sentence: "The question is not whether Q1 is stable under some philosophical force, but whether a non-stipulative authority-selecting law can be articulated without inflating either axis. The [[trumping-preemption#costs-of-the-template|prior review]] flags that such a law requires evidence beyond Schaffer's fictional cases — a burden Q1 inherits." This ties the axes-article's Q1 claim to the concept-page's concession rather than restating the promise.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The thickness axis is a re-parameterisation of Chalmers' Type-D/E/F, not a new dimension

- **Current content says**: "The thickness axis is orthogonal to the substance/property distinction and to Chalmers' Type-D/E/F classification" (line 65).
- **A critic would argue**: Chalmers' Type-D/E/F already distinguishes positions by how much ontological work consciousness does (Type-D: full causal power; Type-E: none; Type-F: intrinsic). A thick-mind position *is* a Type-F or a substance-inflated Type-D; a thin-mind position *is* a Type-E or a deflationary Type-D. The thickness axis thus *correlates strongly* with the Chalmers classification, which raises the suspicion that it is a relabelling, not an orthogonal dimension.
- **Suggested response**: Add a short paragraph — not a full defence, just an acknowledgement — showing at least one concrete case where two positions agree on the Chalmers classification but disagree on thickness (or vice versa). The Nida-Rümelin case is one candidate (she is a Type-D substance dualist but mind-medium rather than mind-thick, whereas Cartesian substance dualism is also Type-D but mind-thick). Without such a case, the article's orthogonality claim is underdetermined.

### Q4 under-population is artefact of training, or an actual philosophical weakness

- **Current content says**: "Q4 is under-populated not because it fails but because a parsimony norm makes it hard to teach" (line 121).
- **A critic would argue**: This attributes the distribution to sociology-of-the-profession rather than to philosophical quality. But parsimony norms in the profession track (imperfectly) actual philosophical virtues — maximally committing on both sides *does* cost something, and the cost is not reducible to training. The article's "Occam's Razor Has Limits" tenet licenses Q4, but the license is internal to the Map; it does not vindicate Q4 for everyone else. Presenting Q4's under-population as *merely* a sociological accident underweights the real parsimony case against it.
- **Suggested response**: Rephrase the line as: "Q4 is under-populated partly because maximum commitments on both sides *do* carry real parsimony costs, and partly because training conventions reinforce the sense that such costs are decisive. The Map's 'Occam's Razor Has Limits' tenet is a specific defeater for the second factor; the first factor remains live." This acknowledges the legitimate weight of parsimony while preserving the article's framing.

### "None of this is visible without the thickness axis" is an overreach

- **Current content says**: "None of this is visible without the thickness axis" (line 121).
- **A critic would argue**: Chalmers (2003) and Goff (2017) both distinguish "thin" and "rich" readings of Type-F positions; Pereboom's "qualitative inertness" arguments turn on ontological weight; Schaffer's own priority monism debates involve ontological-weight axes. The claim that this axis is newly visible is strong and borderline self-congratulatory.
- **Suggested response**: Soften to: "The thickness axis is rarely named explicitly as a taxonomic dimension, though adjacent distinctions (Chalmers' Type-F vs Type-F*, Goff's 'rich' vs 'austere' panpsychism, Pereboom on qualitative inertness) partially track it. Naming it as a primary axis rather than an incidental feature is what allows the four-quadrant structure." This preserves the contribution while locating it in conversation with the literature.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite the critiques above, the article has real virtues worth preserving:

1. **The Stapp illustration is genuinely clarifying** — isolating the "minimal channel, thick observer" pattern as *one configuration* rather than the essence of interactionism is an analytically productive move. Even if it undermines the single-axis claim (see Issue 1), the illustration itself should be kept.
2. **The min-physical disambiguation (line 63) is pre-emptive and honest** — explicitly distinguishing "physics' own ontology treated as minimal" from "fewer physical things existing, in the eliminativist sense" heads off a cheap misreading.
3. **Tenet-by-quadrant mapping is operational** — rather than gesturing at compatibility, the §"Relation to Site Perspective" section matches each tenet to specific quadrant-restrictions, showing the Map's commitments narrow the taxonomic space in a determinate way.
4. **The "Q1 stability" open question is real philosophical work** — naming the requirement for a non-stipulative authority-selecting law, and specifying both failure directions (toward Q2 or Q4), is exactly the kind of signposting a taxonomy should provide.
5. **The article acknowledges its own limits on Q4 and Q3** — calling Q3 "unstable" and Q4 "under-inhabited for reasons worth examining" is better than simply populating the quadrants and declaring victory.

Issues 1–4 and the language tweaks would leave the article substantially stronger without altering its core contribution. The thickness axis is a real conceptual move; it just needs a clearer account of what it measures and honest empirical hedging on the landscape claims.
