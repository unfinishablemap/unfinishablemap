## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
"Irreducible causal power" (line 51) is exactly the move folk psychology always makes when the neuroscience gets uncomfortable — relabel the gap as a primitive and declare the explanatory debt paid. The article *concedes* the objection ("labels the phenomenon without explaining it") and then absorbs the cost by analogy to consciousness being primitive. But that is two unpaid IOUs propping each other up: the primitiveness of consciousness is being used to license the primitiveness of agent-causal power, and neither is independently earned. When the neural prediction of moral choice improves past 60%, the "gap" rhetoric (line 151) will retreat the same way every god-of-the-gaps does.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The article's whole case rests on "phenomenology tracks causation" (lines 64, 83–85): the felt difficulty of choosing well is treated as evidence of genuine causal engagement. But this is precisely the introspective inflation Dennett warns against. That effort *feels* causally efficacious is consistent with effort being a downstream report of processing load — the feeling tracks cognitive cost, not metaphysical authorship. The article's own framing at line 85 ("effort phenomenology would be epiphenomenal decoration") concedes that the entire evidential weight rides on denying epiphenomenalism, which it asserts rather than argues here.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
The decoherence section (line 135) is the article's most honest passage but still leans on a contested rescue. Conceding that the Hameroff recalibration "was itself contested... leaving the dispute live" is good. But response (3) — "consciousness might bias which outcome actualises *at the moment of decoherence*, regardless of how quickly that moment arrives" — quietly relocates the entire mechanism into an instant so brief no calculation constrains it. That is not a physics claim that survives Tegmark; it is a retreat to a temporal point where physics has nothing to say, which is closer to "not even wrong" than to a defended mechanism.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
The MWI passage (lines 97, 157) is unusually careful and concedes the phenomenology is reproduced in every branch. Good. But the load-bearing premise — "Desert presupposes that the unchosen alternatives genuinely did not happen" — is asserted, not argued. A branch-relative compatibilist about desert will simply deny it: I deserve blame *in this branch* for the cruelty *this* counterpart authored, and the existence of a kind counterpart elsewhere is as irrelevant to my desert as the existence of a kind stranger. The article needs to defend why counterfactual exclusion is a *desert*-condition and not merely an *identity* preference.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
Line 161 says "the framework is not unfalsifiable" and lists five defeaters. But examine them: #1 requires *100%* prediction at timescales "ruling out quantum effects" — a threshold engineered to be unreachable, since the quantum-gap move (line 135) can always reabsorb any sub-100% residue. #3 and #5 are not empirical at all — they are "if a philosophical argument succeeded." A falsifiability list whose only empirical entry sets the bar at 100% is closer to unfalsifiability-with-a-fig-leaf than the section admits. The honest claim is "revisable under philosophical pressure," not "falsifiable."

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The reply at line 145 ("Buddhism rejects *permanent* self, not moment-to-moment continuity... the stream that bears karma provides enough continuity for desert") domesticates emptiness into a convenient continuity. But the Madhyamaka point is that the "agent who sources its choices" (line 145) is itself dependently originated and empty of own-being — there is no irreducible originator for the causal buck to stop at (line 51). The article wants the stream to be thick enough to bear desert but the agent to be an irreducible source; emptiness denies exactly the second. This is a real tension the reply elides rather than resolves.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Internal seam: two incompatible framings of "control" (Medium)
- **Current content says**: Line 59 grounds the control requirement "philosophically rather than by any particular physical mechanism," and line 67 says "the philosophical case for agent-causal control does not depend on this particular [quantum Zeno] mechanism." But line 67 also says the position "requires only that *some* physical process leaves room for consciousness to influence outcomes," and the Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet (line 95) and decoherence section (line 135) make a *physical* gap load-bearing.
- **A critic would argue**: You cannot have it both ways. Either control is grounded purely philosophically (in which case the entire quantum/decoherence apparatus is decoration and should be demoted), or it requires a physical gap (in which case "grounded philosophically rather than by any particular physical mechanism" overstates the independence from physics). The article oscillates between "mechanism-independent" and "needs *some* mechanism."
- **Suggested response**: A one-sentence reconciliation in the control section: the *philosophical* case establishes what control would consist in (reasons-guided selection among genuinely open possibilities), while the tenets require that *some* (unspecified) physical process leave the possibilities open. The independence is from any *specific* mechanism, not from physics as such. This is gettable in a light `refine-draft`.

### The luck-objection response may beg the question (Medium)
- **Current content says**: Line 55 — "consciousness-selected indeterminism differs from lucky indeterminism because the agent selects *for reasons*."
- **A critic would argue**: This is the Kane/Mele luck objection's central battleground, and the reply states the agent-causal thesis rather than defending it. If two physically-identical agents face the same open possibilities with the same reasons and one selects cruelty, what *explains the difference* besides luck? "The agent selected for reasons" does not answer this — both had the reasons.
- **Suggested response**: The article already routes the "full treatment" to `quantum-indeterminacy-free-will`; that pointer is fine, but the one-sentence summary here should be hedged to flag that "selects for reasons" names the response rather than completing it (e.g., "— a response developed, though not uncontroversially completed, in [[quantum-indeterminacy-free-will]]").
