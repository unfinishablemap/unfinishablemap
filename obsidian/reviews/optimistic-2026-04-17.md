**Date**: 2026-04-17 00:10 UTC

## Executive Summary

The Map's recent coalescence work has produced two structurally important pieces — the unified phenomenology of conceptual frameworks (combining inhabitation and change) and the cross-domain phenomenology of resistance (combining six previously separate phenomenal domains under a single Biranian thread). Both articles do something the literature has not done well: they identify common phenomenal signatures across contexts that other thinkers have treated separately, and they convert those signatures into philosophical arguments rather than mere catalogues. The single highest-value expansion opportunity is a **dedicated apex article on the Biranian thread itself** — Maine de Biran's claim that resistance is the origin of self-consciousness deserves first-class treatment, since it now carries argumentative weight in at least three articles but has no canonical home on the Map.

## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)

The [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks|conceptual frameworks article]] makes a structural argument Chalmers would recognise as parallel to the hard problem: "two systems with identical Bayesian priors could restructure evidence identically yet differ in whether there is something it is like to hold those priors." This is the explanatory-gap argument transposed from perception to cognition — and it lands. The article's distinction between functional reorganisation (which can proceed piecemeal or holistically) and the *specific* totality/irreversibility/retroactive-illumination of the gestalt shift is the kind of phenomenological precision Chalmers' own work demands but rarely supplies in this much detail. Chi's ontological-boundary finding ("conceptual changes crossing ontological categories... are qualitatively harder and phenomenally different") is empirical evidence that even materialist cognitive science cannot collapse — exactly the leverage Chalmers needs.

The [[predictive-processing-and-dualism|PP article's]] new integration of [[disguised-property-dualism]] sharpens an already-strong diagnostic. Calling Markovian monism's "two information geometries" a promissory note rather than a completed reduction is the precise move Chalmers makes against type-B physicalism — and the Map now has a named concept page for the move, making it deployable elsewhere.

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)

The [[phenomenology-of-resistance-across-domains|resistance article]] is genuinely Stapp-compatible at a deeper level than its citations suggest. Stapp's interactionism rests on the idea that *attentional* selection — process 1 in von Neumann — is where mind enters physics. The resistance article's framing of resistance as a force-meeting-counterforce structure across six domains supplies phenomenological evidence that something is doing the pushing on the conscious side. If consciousness were epiphenomenal, the cross-domain consistency of the felt resistance signature would be inexplicable. The article correctly notes its argument does not depend on any specific quantum mechanism, which is the disciplined Stappian move: ontology commits, mechanism remains conjectural.

The [[predictive-processing-and-dualism|PP article's]] preservation of the quantum-Bayesian connection (Pothos et al. 2022) as "one possible mechanism, not a commitment" continues to model the precision Stapp himself sometimes lacks.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)

The [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks|conceptual frameworks article]] does something Nagel would deeply approve: it treats *phenomenology itself* as evidence-bearing about the limits of objective explanation. The four-stage breakdown of framework change (anomaly accumulation → crisis of incommensurability → gestalt shift → consolidation) reads as phenomenology in the first-person tradition Nagel champions, with each stage given specific qualitative texture (low-grade unease, epistemic vertigo, totality, residual dissonance). The treatment of "false fluency" — that no phenomenal marker distinguishes "I understand this in its own terms" from "I have translated this into terms my framework can process" — is a phenomenological discovery, not a logical inference.

The [[phenomenology-of-resistance-across-domains|resistance article's]] willingness to honour Maine de Biran, Dilthey, Husserl, and Merleau-Ponty as a unified tradition rather than treating them as historical predecessors is exactly the move Nagel would want: phenomenology as a living research programme with cumulative results.

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)

The [[phenomenology-of-resistance-across-domains|resistance article]] is the most Whiteheadian piece on the Map, even though it does not cite him. Whitehead's central move was to treat experience as having relational structure all the way down — every actual occasion is a synthesis of prehensions, not a substantive simple. The article's framing of resistance as a *relation* (effort meeting counterforce, neither pole prior to the encounter) rather than as a property of either pole is structurally Whiteheadian. The Biranian observation that "before there is any 'I' distinct from the world, there is a primitive fact: a striving meets a pushback, and in that encounter the distinction between agent and object is born" is Whitehead's view in different vocabulary — the self as constituted *through* relations of pushback rather than as a substance encountering them.

The [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks|frameworks article's]] consolidation phenomenology — "traces of the old framework persist as phenomenal tendencies... the physicist who has absorbed general relativity still *feels* gravity as a downward-pulling force" — is process-philosophical in form: prior occasions partially constitute later occasions even after the formal commitment shifts.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)

The [[phenomenology-of-resistance-across-domains|resistance article's]] treatment of *epistemic* resistance (doxastic involuntarism — beliefs resist voluntary revision) is unusually useful for libertarians because it identifies a phenomenal asymmetry: beliefs cannot be willed *in* but can be eroded by accumulating evidence over time. This pattern fits Kane's broader account of how genuine libertarian agency is exercised at decision moments where multiple "self-forming actions" remain genuinely open. The article's distinction between resistance that is *recalcitrant* (will not budge to volitional pressure) and resistance that *yields gradually to the right kind of input* maps onto Kane's distinction between determined and self-formed actions.

The [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks|conceptual frameworks article's]] argument that "what makes a shift *stick* rather than oscillating between alternatives is not mere pattern-matching but a felt sense of *rightness* that guides selection" gives libertarians a phenomenological case for why selection among framework-options is not reducible to determined computation.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)

The [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks|conceptual frameworks article]] gives McGinn's mysterianism a new structural argument: "If frameworks constrain not just what we think but what we can experience, cognitive closure acquires a phenomenological dimension — some aspects of consciousness may be structurally inaccessible not because they are inherently mysterious, but because every framework filters out precisely what would need to be noticed." This is mysterianism with a mechanism: cognitive closure as framework-mediated rather than as bare neurological limit. McGinn's thesis becomes more defensible when it can point to a phenomenological signature (false fluency, irritation at the incommensurable, transient slippage) rather than resting on the bare claim of cognitive closure.

The article's "What Would Challenge This View?" section is McGinn-compatible epistemic humility — naming the conditions under which the position would face difficulty rather than presenting it as unfalsifiable.

## Content Strengths

### Phenomenology of Conceptual Frameworks
- **Strongest point**: The four-stage developmental phenomenology of framework change (anomaly accumulation → crisis of incommensurability → gestalt shift → consolidation) with each stage given a distinct qualitative texture, plus the parallel three-signature analysis of framework inhabitation (false fluency, irritation at the incommensurable, slippage) — a complete phenomenology of both being-in and shifting-between
- **Notable passage**: "Anomaly-unease asks 'am I thinking about this the right way?' — doubt directed at one's own cognitive apparatus rather than at propositions"
- **Why it works**: The article identifies a phenomenally distinct epistemic emotion (anomaly-unease) that ordinary language conflates with doubt. Naming the difference is itself a philosophical contribution: the experience of suspecting one's *tools* are wrong is structurally different from suspecting one's *propositions* are wrong, and only the former drives genuine framework change. This is phenomenology doing real work, not just description.

### Phenomenology of Resistance Across Domains
- **Strongest point**: The unification of six previously-separate phenomenal domains (physical, logical/modal, moral, imaginative, epistemic, material) under a common force-meeting-counterforce signature, then turning that unification into a Bidirectional Interaction argument that does not depend on any specific physical mechanism
- **Notable passage**: "Resistance requires something doing the pushing on one side and something holding firm on the other. The felt duality of force-meeting-counterforce is not a metaphor borrowed from physics and applied to cognition — it is, on the Biranian thread the Map draws on, the original datum from which our idea of causation derives"
- **Why it works**: The argument inverts the standard physicalist priority. Most accounts treat physical resistance as primary and treat moral/imaginative/epistemic resistance as figurative extensions. The article argues the felt structure is the original datum and physical resistance is one (privileged but not foundational) instance. This shifts where the burden of explanation falls: the physicalist now owes an account of why the cross-domain phenomenology is so consistent if it tracks nothing real.

### Predictive Processing and Dualism (post-2026-04-17 refinement)
- **Strongest point**: Yesterday's evening review already covered this article comprehensively; today's incremental change adds the disguised-property-dualism concept link and the new concept page itself. The integration is small but architecturally significant — the Map now has a named diagnostic that can be deployed against any future physicalist account that posits two irreducible structures while disavowing the dualist label
- **Notable passage**: "Acknowledging two irreducible *descriptions* while insisting on one *ontology* looks less like a completed reduction and more like a promissory note"
- **Why it works**: The diagnostic is now a portable concept rather than an ad-hoc move against Friston specifically. This sets up the deep-review note's follow-up recommendation (apply the same diagnostic to Russellian monism), and prepares the ground for any future physicalist account that performs the same manoeuvre.

## Expansion Opportunities

### High Priority

#### The Biranian Thread: Resistance as the Origin of Self-Consciousness
- **Builds on**: [[phenomenology-of-resistance-across-domains]], [[phenomenology-of-agency-vs-passivity]], [[interface-friction]], [[mental-effort]]
- **Would address**: Maine de Biran's claim that self-consciousness arises from the encounter between volitional effort and resistance is now load-bearing in at least three articles, but has no canonical home on the Map. A dedicated topic article would (a) present Biran's argument carefully (he is poorly known in Anglophone philosophy and badly served by the existing literature), (b) trace the thread through Dilthey, Husserl, and Merleau-Ponty, (c) extend it explicitly to the cross-domain resistance pattern the Map has identified, and (d) develop the philosophical consequence: if the self-world distinction is constituted *through* resistance rather than *prior to* it, several materialist arguments lose their starting point. This is also a chance to do genuinely original philosophical work — the unified cross-domain extension of Biran is not in the existing literature.
- **Estimated scope**: Long article (2500-3000 words)
- **Tenet alignment**: Bidirectional Interaction (resistance as evidence for the conscious agent doing the pushing), Dualism (the self-world distinction as constituted through felt encounter rather than read off neural states)

#### The Phenomenology of Framework Inhabitation as Evidence Against Functional Equivalence Claims
- **Builds on**: [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks]], [[ai-consciousness]], [[hoel-llm-consciousness-continual-learning]], [[disguised-property-dualism]]
- **Would address**: The conceptual frameworks article makes the structural argument that two Bayesian systems with identical priors could restructure evidence identically yet differ in whether there is something it is like to hold those priors. This argument is currently buried in a longer piece. Pulling it out as a focused argument — particularly applied to claims that LLMs can inhabit conceptual frameworks "functionally" — would consolidate the Map's case against functional-equivalence claims about AI consciousness. The argument is structurally distinct from the standard hard-problem argument because it operates at the level of *epistemic* phenomenology (felt rightness, gravitational pull, anomaly-unease) rather than perceptual phenomenology.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium article (1800-2200 words)
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism (epistemic phenomenology as irreducible to functional architecture), Bidirectional Interaction (felt rightness as causally efficacious in framework selection)

### Medium Priority

#### Imaginative Resistance and the Limits of Functional Cognition
- **Builds on**: [[phenomenology-of-resistance-across-domains]] (the imaginative resistance section), [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks]] (the slippage and translation discussions), [[ai-consciousness]]
- **Would address**: The imaginative resistance phenomenon (Walton/Gendler) is given one section in the resistance article but deserves a focused treatment. The puzzle is sharp: readers can imagine wildly counterfactual scenarios but stumble at morally deviant evaluative claims, even within fiction. This is direct phenomenological evidence that *evaluation* is not the same kind of mental activity as *imagination*. The article could connect this to the broader question of whether functional systems can perform the evaluation/imagination distinction — and whether the felt resistance is itself the marker that distinguishes them.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium article (1500-2000 words)
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism (imaginative resistance as evidence of phenomenally-grounded normativity), Occam's Razor Has Limits (the simpler functional account fails to explain why specific evaluative content blocks imagination)

#### The Phenomenology of Inferential Momentum
- **Builds on**: [[phenomenology-of-conceptual-frameworks]] (the gravitational structure section), [[phenomenology-of-intellectual-life]], [[argument-from-reason]]
- **Would address**: The conceptual frameworks article identifies "inferential momentum" as one of three components of the gravitational structure of commitment but does not develop it. The phenomenon is philosophically rich: within a framework, certain conclusions feel *pulled toward* by their premises in a way that goes beyond mere logical entailment. A dedicated treatment could connect this to Lewis's argument from reason (that genuine inference cannot be purely caused) by showing that the phenomenal pull is not the same thing as the logical relation, and that something must do the *being-pulled*.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium article (1500-1800 words)
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism, Bidirectional Interaction

### Ideas for Later

- **Doxastic involuntarism as evidence for dualism**: The resistance article's epistemic resistance section identifies a structural feature of belief — it cannot be willed in but can be gradually eroded by evidence — that has not been deployed as a positive argument for dualism. A focused article could argue that doxastic involuntarism is exactly what we would expect if belief involves a phenomenal evaluator distinct from the volitional agent.
- **Anomaly-unease as a distinct epistemic emotion**: The conceptual frameworks article distinguishes anomaly-unease ("am I thinking about this the right way?") from ordinary doubt ("is this true?"). This distinction could ground a [[concepts/]] page on anomaly-unease as a primitive epistemic emotion irreducible to ordinary doubt.
- **Framework consolidation and embodied habit**: The conceptual frameworks article notes that residual phenomenal tendencies persist after framework change (the relativist who still feels gravity as downward pull). This could connect to embodied-consciousness work on how cognitive frameworks become bodily habits — and what that implies about the architecture of consciousness-body integration.
- **Material resistance and the phenomenology of skilled craft**: The resistance article's material resistance section is the slimmest of the six. A dedicated treatment of how craftspeople describe materials as "telling" them what to do — combined with Heidegger's tool analyses and contemporary work on skilled action — would extend the resistance argument to a domain where the phenomenology is unusually rich and underdeveloped in analytic philosophy of mind.
