## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The article is fairer to me than most. It concedes the connectionist argument (Ramsey-Stich-Garon) "has theoretical weight," grants the predictive-processing descendant, and even admits self-refutation "does not obviously succeed." My genuine complaint is that the article's positive case ultimately reduces to "phenomenal evidence is bedrock and I decline to give it up" (line 130). That is not an argument against me; it is a refusal to be moved. The article says exactly this — which I respect as honesty but note as a confession that no refutation has been delivered. The article's own "Honest Limitations" section concedes the point.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The treatment of illusionism is accurate and the regress objection (line 122) is the right pressure point. But the article's verdict that my quasi-phenomenal/phenomenal distinction "requires the appearance of phenomenality to have *no* phenomenal character whatsoever, which is the position it was meant to explain" assumes the very thing at issue — that a higher-order misrepresentation must itself be phenomenal. I deny that. The article registers my reply and then asserts its dissatisfaction; that is a standoff, not a defeat, and the article could say so more plainly. (It nearly does: "The disagreement at this point is over whether the distinction can be drawn cleanly enough.")

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
Not engaged; out of scope for this article. No quantum claims are made, so no decoherence objection applies. Correctly avoided.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
Not engaged; out of scope. No objection.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
The article's central positive claim — that "failure to reduce... is at least as much evidence for irreducibility as it is for fictionalism" (line 128) — is symmetric and therefore non-discriminating: it tells us no experiment could break the tie. The article is admirably candid that the dispute is "foundational rather than... derived," but a foundational commitment that no evidence could overturn is exactly what I call metaphysics, not science. To its credit, the article does not pretend otherwise — it explicitly disclaims dislodging the eliminativist "by argument from prior premises they accept." That honesty is itself a (philosophical, not empirical) virtue.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The article gestures at me via `[[buddhism-and-dualism]]` and concedes "limited eliminativism about *some* folk-psychological categories" including the self (it endorses a "no-self analysis"). It resists my extension of *pratītyasamutpāda* to phenomenal events. Fair enough — but the article's bedrock datum, "there is something it is like," is precisely the reified phenomenal substrate I would dissolve. The article does not argue the phenomenal is non-empty; it stipulates it. Same observation as the eliminativist's, from the other direction.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The "two-edged evidence" symmetry (line 128)
- **Current content says**: "Failure to reduce, in a domain whose structure is not yet understood, is at least as much evidence for irreducibility as it is for fictionalism."
- **A critic would argue**: A symmetric evidential claim cannot, by itself, favour the Map's side. If failure-to-reduce is *equally* evidence for both, the article needs an *additional* asymmetry to license choosing irreducibility — and the only asymmetry on offer (phenomenal evidence is bedrock) is the contested premise itself.
- **Suggested response**: The article already supplies the asymmetry holistically (first tenet + explanatory-gap track record + Moorean confidence-asymmetry, line 130) and openly flags it as foundational. No change required — but a one-clause forward-pointer at line 128 ("the asymmetry that breaks the tie is given below as a foundational commitment, not derived here") would prevent a reader from mistaking the symmetric sentence for the whole argument.

### Toon-fictionalism attribution precision (line 136)
- **Current content says**: Mental fictionalism (Toon 2023) "den[ies]" the causal-efficacy commitment — "they treat belief-talk as as-if discourse whose usefulness does not require real intentional causation."
- **A critic would argue**: Toon's view is subtler than a flat denial of mental causation. His thesis is that *mental representations* (inner sentence-like items) are a fiction and that belief-attribution is a pretence about inner representations — not necessarily that the physical states the pretence is layered over are causally inert. Lumping Toon with "full phenomenal-and-causal eliminativism" as both "deny[ing] the first [causal-efficacy] commitment" may overstate.
- **Suggested response**: Confirm against the book (or the Springer *Philosophia* discussion of it) whether Toon denies real intentional causation or only denies inner representation. If the latter, narrow the attribution: it is the *representational-realist* commitment Toon denies, which the article's own next sentence already says it is neutral on. As written, Toon may be mis-sorted onto the wrong side of the article's own distinction.

The exact wording traces to Searle's **1995 NYRB exchange with Dennett** ("The Mystery of Consciousness: An Exchange," 1995-12-21): *"where the existence of conscious states is concerned, you can't make the distinction between appearance and reality, because the existence of the appearance is the reality in question."* The appearance-reality collapse is genuinely Searle's and central to *Rediscovery* ch.5, so the fix is either re-attribute the verbatim quote to the 1995 NYRB piece (add a reference) or soften to a paraphrase of the 1992 position — unless the exact 1992 page is confirmed. Folded into the P2 refine-draft task.

- **Churchland "twenty-five centuries" (paraphrase-grade).** Churchland (1981) does write that folk psychology "suffers explanatory failure on an epic scale" and argues FP has not advanced since Aristotle (~25 centuries); the specific string "stagnant for at least twenty-five centuries" is faithful in substance but could not be locked as verbatim — acceptable as paraphrase, no fix required.
