## Executive Summary

The article does many things well — front-loaded opening, clean attributions, explicit independence proofs, honest "what the axis is not" section. But three real weaknesses survive the deep-review pass, and one of them is critical and structural: **the article's chosen "cleanest case" (the imagery exemplar) does not actually fit the axis pattern as articulated**. The axis defines its second pattern as "identical function, different phenomenology", but the Kay/Keogh/Pearson 2024 data the article cites describes aphantasics and controls using *demonstrably different strategies* (analytic vs object-based mental rotation) — i.e., different functions producing the same task output. This is precisely the functionalist's standard reply ("the function differs at finer grain") already empirically realised, not a hypothetical falsifier. The article's own newly-added falsifiability paragraph (line 105) names "if aphantasic mental rotation turns out to deploy a functionally distinct subroutine" as a de-weighting condition, apparently unaware that the cited data already meets that condition. Secondary issues: (2) the "two patterns" framing introduced at lines 45–47 is never explicitly applied to the four exemplars, leaving the reader to infer pattern membership, and on inspection three of the four exemplars do not unambiguously fit either pattern; (3) the article repeatedly says "four voids" but one of the four (cognitive-phenomenology-and-the-irreducibility-of-thought) is a topic article, not a void — a small but factual misclassification that propagates from the opening paragraph. Tertiary issues: a "same processing pipeline" claim about synaesthesia (line 67) directly contradicts a hedge in the synesthetic-void source article (line 53 there), the Strawson "sensory function held constant" framing ignores top-down predictive-coding effects on auditory cortex, the Dualism-support claim uses "predicts" too strongly (ontological distinctness is compatible with strict covariance), and the multi-realizability resource is not engaged anywhere despite being functionalism's canonical answer to such cases. Strengths to preserve: the falsifiability paragraph itself (good move, even if it surfaces the issue it doesn't fully see), the cross-cluster argument for treating the axis as a structural slot, and the disciplined refusal to overclaim against functionalism in the body.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
"You've labelled four reportage-difference cases as evidence of an ontological gap between phenomenology and function. Each case dissolves under cross-activation plus differential disposition-to-report. Aphantasics report not-having images and use a different verbal-analytic strategy; their *processing* differs as well as their *reports*, and there's no further phenomenal-extra residue to dissociate from anything. Synaesthetes have V4 cross-activation triggered by grapheme-form area; the 'extra qualia' is the activation pattern, not a metaphysical surplus. Wegner's I-Spy is a misattribution rate in a noisy authorship-detector; treating it as a 'function-phenomenology dissociation' rather than an ordinary cognitive error inflates a calibration result into a metaphysics. The whole axis is folk psychology with citations." Status: bedrock per deep-review stability notes, but worth surfacing because the article concedes this in §What the Axis Is Not without noting that *the eliminativist concedes nothing* — they don't read these as functionalist absorptions, they read them as no-dissociations-at-all.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Imagery exemplar undermined by article's own data and falsifier
- **File**: concepts/phenomenology-vs-function-axis.md
- **Location**: §The Four Canonical Exemplars → Imagery: The Cleanest Case (lines 55–59); §What the Axis Is Not falsifier paragraph (line 105)
- **Problem**: The axis defines pattern (b) as "Identical function, different phenomenology" (line 47). The imagery exemplar is presented as "the cleanest case" (line 55). But the cited Kay/Keogh/Pearson 2024 finding explicitly describes aphantasics and controls using *different cognitive strategies*: "Aphantasics work 'slower but more accurately' using analytic strategies; controls use object-based mental rotation" (line 57). Different strategy = different functional role under any functionalist taxonomy. This makes the imagery case "different function, different phenomenology, equivalent task performance" — neither of the article's two patterns. The article's own newly-added falsifiability clause (line 105) names "if aphantasic mental rotation turns out to deploy a functionally distinct subroutine" as a de-weighting condition, but the cited data already establishes a functionally distinct subroutine. The functionalist absorption the article concedes as a hypothetical possibility (line 103) is empirically realised in the article's lead exhibit. The article needs either (a) an explicit argument for why "analytic vs object-based" isn't the relevant grain of function (and therefore why "function" should be defined at the level of the task, not the strategy), or (b) a demotion of the imagery case from "cleanest" to "weakest", or (c) a reframing of the axis to allow "different strategy, equivalent task, divergent phenomenology" as a distinct third pattern.
- **Severity**: High (load-bearing on the article's strongest exemplar, internally incoherent with its own falsifier)
- **Recommendation**: Add a paragraph to §Imagery: The Cleanest Case acknowledging the strategy-difference and arguing — if it can be argued — for a functional-grain consideration: e.g., "the relevant function is the task-level operation (mental rotation as such), under which both groups perform equivalent work; the strategy-level difference is the phenomenological accompaniment of that function rather than a function-difference." Alternatively, demote the imagery case and lead with synaesthesia as the cleanest case (Gray 2003's "function and qualia come apart in two ways" really is a within-pipeline dissociation in a way Kay/Keogh/Pearson is not).

## Counterarguments to Address

### Multi-realizability is functionalism's canonical answer to the imagery case
- **Current content says**: "the function may differ at a finer grain than the experiments resolve" (line 103) — concedes the response without engaging it.
- **A critic would argue**: Multi-realizability is *the* foundational functionalist commitment (Putnam 1967, Block & Fodor 1972). It says different physical realisations of the same function are still functionally identical. The aphantasic case is *exactly* a candidate multi-realization: same task-level function (mental rotation), different lower-level realisation (analytic vs object-based). Functionalism doesn't need to push the function-grain finer to absorb this — it needs to argue that the grain at which function should be individuated is the task level, where both groups are functionally identical. The article never names multi-realizability, never engages it, and so doesn't see that its "subtler functionalism" gesture is really pointing at the central functionalist resource.
- **Suggested response**: Add a paragraph engaging multi-realizability directly. The article's wedge against function-phenomenology identity needs an argument that the relevant grain of function is *not* the multiply-realisable task level but a finer level that tracks strategy. This is a substantive argument the cognitive-phenomenology literature (Pitt, Bayne, Smithies) has actually made; the article could borrow it explicitly.

### Top-down predictive coding undermines "sensory function constant" in the Strawson case
- **Current content says**: "Acoustic stimulation is identical; the French speaker's experience differs because they understand. The sensory function is held constant; the phenomenal character varies." (line 71)
- **A critic would argue**: "Sensory function constant" is empirically contestable. Predictive-coding accounts of perception (Friston, Clark, Hohwy) and well-replicated neurolinguistic findings show that comprehension *modulates* sensory processing top-down: a comprehending listener exhibits different auditory cortex activation patterns than a non-comprehending listener exposed to the same acoustics. So the sensory function is not constant; it is modulated by comprehension. This doesn't kill Strawson's argument but it complicates the clean "sensory same, phenomenology different" framing.
- **Suggested response**: Hedge the framing: "Acoustic stimulation is identical; downstream auditory processing is partially modulated by comprehension, so the wedge is sharper at the level of *what is acoustically delivered* than at the level of *what is acoustically processed*." Strawson's argument still works for cognitive phenomenology, but the cleanness shifts from "sensory function held constant" to "acoustic input held constant."

### Strict covariance is compatible with property dualism — "predicts dissociation" is too strong
- **Current content says**: "Property dualism — and the Map's stronger tenet-package position — predicts exactly this kind of dissociation, because phenomenal character is held to be ontologically distinct from the functional roles it accompanies." (line 111)
- **A critic would argue**: Ontological distinctness does not predict observable dissociation. Property dualism is fully compatible with psychophysical laws that yield strict covariance — phenomenal property P is distinct from functional property F but always covaries with F. Parallelism (Leibniz) is the limiting case. So observed dissociations are *consistent with* property dualism but are not *predicted by* it. The dualist needs the additional commitment that the psychophysical laws permit dissociation, which is a substantive claim beyond bare ontological distinctness.
- **Suggested response**: Soften "predicts" to "is consistent with and renders intelligible." Add a sentence acknowledging that strict covariance is also compatible with property dualism, so the dissociation observations are evidence in favour of dualist frameworks that *permit* dissociation rather than against physicalism *tout court*.

### "Same processing pipeline" claim contradicts the synesthetic-void's own hedge
- **Current content says**: "where aphantasia shows the same task done with or without imagery, synaesthesia shows the same processing pipeline yielding extra phenomenal content in some minds and not in others." (line 67)
- **A critic would argue**: The synesthetic-void source article (line 53) explicitly flags: "van Leeuwen et al.'s 'mediated through semantics' finding (developed below) suggests that the synesthete's processing pipeline is *not* identical to the non-synesthete's at the deeper grain that representationalists track—so the 'identical representations' claim relies on fixing the level of representation at the surface stimulus." The axis article's "same processing pipeline" assertion is more confident than the source article it's drawing from.
- **Suggested response**: Replace "same processing pipeline" with "the same surface-level processing pipeline" or carry the hedge across: "synaesthesia shows divergent phenomenology under conditions where the surface processing is held constant; deeper-grain pipeline differences exist (van Leeuwen et al. 2015) but the pattern survives at coarser representational levels."
