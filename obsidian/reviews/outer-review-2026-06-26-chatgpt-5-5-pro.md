## Outer review: “Empirical Phenomena of Mental Causation: Placebo and Choking”

**Referee verdict: major revision.** The article is not empirically careless; its current version already makes several important concessions: it says the data do not force dualism, treats the argument as “cost-of-explanation” rather than unique prediction, acknowledges open-label-placebo limits, and admits future neuroscience could weaken the empirical leverage. The problem is narrower but important: the empirical evidence supports **expectation-, conditioning-, attention-, reward-, and control-related causal effects**, while the article’s differentiating philosophical conclusion often slides to **phenomenal character itself is doing causal work**. That bridge is under-argued.

The live article says it was created on 2026-02-02, last modified 2026-06-19, and frames placebo plus choking as mutually reinforcing evidence for mental causation, while explicitly stating that the Map reads phenomenology as load-bearing but that the data alone do not force that reading. ## 1. What the article gets right

The placebo section is strongest where it is careful. The article accurately reports the main limitation of the Cochrane placebo review: placebo effects are not large general clinical effects, and the most robust signal is modest, patient-reported, and especially pain-related. The live Cochrane page reports 202 trials, no major general health benefit, modest patient-reported effects such as pain, substantial heterogeneity, and difficulty separating patient-reported placebo effects from biased reporting. [Cochrane](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003974_placebo-interventions-all-clinical-conditions) The article’s summary of Fendel et al. is also largely accurate: the 2025 Scientific Reports meta-analysis included 60 open-label-placebo RCTs, found an overall SMD of 0.35, a self-report SMD of 0.39, and an objective-outcome SMD of 0.09. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-14895-z)

The Benedetti point is also handled better than many dualist treatments would handle it. The article explicitly says expectation dominates for analgesic and motor channels but not for hormonal responses, where unconscious conditioning drives secretion; that is faithful to Benedetti et al.’s abstract-level conclusion. [topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation/) The immune-conditioning example is real: Goebel et al. found that re-exposure to the conditioned drink with placebo capsules suppressed IL-2, IFN-γ, and lymphocyte proliferation. [Amsterdam UMC](https://pure.amsterdamumc.nl/en/publications/behavioral-conditioning-of-immunosuppression-is-possible-in-human/) The article is right to treat this as objective physiology rather than mere verbal report.

The choking section is also not fabricated. The older explicit-monitoring literature is a real line of evidence, and the article responsibly flags that some findings await replication. It also correctly notes that dual-task paradigms can sometimes improve expert performance under pressure. A 2019 systematic review reports that choking interventions based on both distraction and self-focus models, plus acclimatisation, generally benefit performance under pressure, while dual tasks can help under pressure but hurt when used in training. [Universität Wien](https://ucrisportal.univie.ac.at/en/publications/choking-interventions-in-sports-a-systematic-review/)

## 2. Main evidential overreach: “mental causation” becomes “phenomenal causation”

The article’s central evidential move is not licensed by the empirical studies as cited. The empirical literature supports a weaker claim: **beliefs, expectations, learning histories, attentional strategies, reward context, and control policies can causally modulate pain, immune markers, dopamine, and motor performance**. That is already interesting. But it does not, by itself, show that **phenomenal character**, as opposed to the physical/functional state that implements expectation or attention, is causally load-bearing.

The article often states the weaker and stronger readings in adjacent sentences. It says the physiological response tracks what the subject consciously understands, then links this to phenomenal intentionality and says the felt quality of expecting pain relief is doing explanatory work. [topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation/) That is a philosophical thesis, not an empirical result. The functionalist rival has a simple first-pass response: different representational states are different computational-neural states, and different computational-neural states have different downstream effects. The article mentions that response, but too quickly converts the residual hard problem into positive evidence for causal phenomenology.

The site already has a method for preventing exactly this inflation. Its “Evidential-Status Discipline” says that when evidence fits both the Map’s tenets and a leading rival model, the article must classify the evidence as compatibility rather than support unless it names an independent discriminator. [project/evidential-status-discipline](https://unfinishablemap.org/project/evidential-status-discipline/) The same document warns against borrowing a stronger metaphysical reading while paying only the weaker evidential price. [project/evidential-status-discipline](https://unfinishablemap.org/project/evidential-status-discipline/) This target article needs that rule applied more strictly.

## 3. Placebo: strong psychophysiology, weak dualist discrimination

The article’s placebo material is empirically plausible but philosophically over-assimilated.

First, open-label placebo weakens the article’s clean “propositional belief selects cascade” model more than the article admits. Fendel et al. report the strongest effect in self-reports and a near-null objective-outcome effect. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-14895-z) The article says this “shifts the argument rather than dissolving it” and that “some mental causation operates survives intact.” [topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation/) That wording is too protective. The safer conclusion is: **open-label placebo supports ritual, expectation, demand, self-interpretation, symptom appraisal, and therapeutic-context effects; it does not robustly support objective content-specific physiological control.**

Second, the article underuses the 2020s mechanistic literature. A 2021 participant-level fMRI meta-analysis of placebo analgesia found small, widespread reductions in pain-related activity, frontoparietal increases with high heterogeneity, and concluded that placebo analgesia is multifaceted, involving mechanisms that differ across paradigms. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-21179-3) A 2024 Nature mouse study identified an rACC→pontine-nucleus pathway in placebo-like anticipatory pain relief, with opioid-receptor evidence and causal pathway manipulation. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07816-z) A 2026 Neuron paper reports cortical-brainstem circuitry, vlPAG opioid gating, and multimodal placebo analgesia in mice. [Cell](https://www.cell.com/neuron/fulltext/S0896-6273%2826%2900216-3) These sources strengthen placebo as a psychobiological phenomenon but weaken the article’s suggestion that the crucial residue is phenomenology rather than increasingly mapped top-down control circuitry.

Third, the predictive-processing rival is too lightly treated. Büchel et al.’s predictive-coding account explicitly models placebo analgesia as recurrent top-down/bottom-up inference in the pain system, with expectation magnitude and precision influencing hypoalgesia. [Cell+1](https://www.cell.com/neuron/fulltext/S0896-6273%2814%2900192-5) The target article mentions predictive processing, but treats it as compatible and then returns to the hard problem. That is not enough. A skeptical referee would require a paragraph explaining why predictive coding, active inference, or nonreductive physicalist mental causation does not already explain the empirical pattern at the relevant grain.

## 4. Choking: the article is too dependent on explicit monitoring

The choking discussion is the article’s biggest currency problem. It relies mainly on Baumeister, Masters, Beilock & Carr, and Beilock et al.; those are legitimate classics, but the article’s 2026 version should engage modern reviews and animal/neural work. Yu’s review distinguishes three families of explanation: distraction, explicit monitoring, and over-arousal, and says neuroimaging implicates motivation and top-down attention-control regions. [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/behavioral-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnbeh.2015.00019/full) The 2019 systematic review likewise treats distraction, self-focus, and acclimatisation as live intervention families rather than confirming a single explicit-monitoring model. [Universität Wien](https://ucrisportal.univie.ac.at/en/publications/choking-interventions-in-sports-a-systematic-review/)

The animal literature is especially important. A 2021 PNAS paper found that rhesus monkeys also choke under unusually high reward, and interpreted the failures as overly cautious reaching consistent with explicit monitoring; it also says choking is not unique to humans and may have conserved neural mechanisms. [NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8536322/) A 2024 Neuron study reports a motor-cortex mechanism in which reward drives movement-preparation activity into and then past an optimal zone. [Cell+1](https://www.cell.com/neuron/fulltext/S0896-6273%2824%2900608-1) These findings do not refute the Map’s interpretation, but they strongly undercut the article’s claim that choking “tracks the felt mode rather than cognitive load.” A monkey model can be compatible with animal consciousness, but it does not supply the article’s required evidence of reportable human phenomenology as the causal variable.

The line “You cannot confabulate interference” is rhetorically effective but too quick. A skeptic need not deny the interference; the skeptic can deny that introspection correctly identifies the causal property as phenomenological self-monitoring rather than reward pressure, arousal, control-policy shift, motor-preparation collapse, or task-specific explicit control. The article needs to move from “interference occurs” to “phenomenology itself, not merely the neural-control regime correlated with it, is causally indispensable.” It currently does not.

## 5. The conjunction argument overstates independence

The article claims the two domains share little except conscious processing, with “no shared neurotransmitter system, anatomical target, or regulatory loop.” [topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/empirical-phenomena-mental-causation/) That is too strong.

Placebo and choking differ in distal effectors: pain modulation, dopamine, immune pathways, and motor execution are not the same endpoint systems. But the live literature repeatedly points to overlapping families of mechanism: prefrontal/ACC control, expectation, reward, precision, top-down modulation, frontoparietal control, and motor/reward interaction. Placebo fMRI meta-analysis implicates frontoparietal regions and multiple cognitive-affective mechanisms; the 2024 Nature placebo paper centers rACC circuitry; Yu’s choking review emphasizes motivation and top-down control; the Neuron choking work emphasizes reward and motor-preparation interaction. [Cell+3Nature+3Nature+3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-21179-3)

A better formulation would be: **placebo and choking involve different output systems but overlapping top-down control, expectation, reward, and precision-like architectures.** That would preserve the article’s comparative structure without making an empirically fragile “no shared machinery” claim.
