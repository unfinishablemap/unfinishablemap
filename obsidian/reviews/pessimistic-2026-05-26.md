# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-05-26
**Content reviewed**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md` (3,394 words; created 2026-01-29, `ai_modified: 2026-05-25T14:43:24+00:00`, `last_deep_review: 2026-04-22` — over a month stale; `ai_contribution: 100`). Selected as a load-bearing core-architecture article (the delegation framing is the Map's primary structural answer to the exclusion problem) that was edited today by a calibration refine-pass but has not had a deep review in 34 days. Only one prior pessimistic review (2026-04-05-evening), and the 2026-05-25 refine substantially rewrote the strongest passages — exactly the window where freshly-installed calibration prose should be adversarially stress-tested. This review deliberately does **not** re-litigate the empirical-indistinguishability critique that dominated the April review and that the May 25 refine pass has largely answered in-text; it targets what survives those fixes and what the fixes introduced.

## Executive Summary

The May 25 refine pass genuinely answered the April review's central complaint: the article now states plainly that delegatory dualism is "empirically equivalent to its rivals." But three weaknesses survive or were created by that honesty. First, the article repeatedly asserts the theory's *intra-dualist* advantages (it beats epiphenomenalism and overdeterminism) without noticing that the very feature it just conceded — empirical equivalence to physicalism — also dissolves most of those intra-dualist advantages, because the advantages it claims are all explanations of phenomena physicalism explains more cheaply. Second, the "default causal profile" — the load-bearing primitive of the whole theory — is admitted to be never directly observable and to rest on the contested possibility of zombies, yet the article continues to deploy it as though it does real work. Third, the Part II integration with quantum interactionism and entanglement-binding, while now hedged, still smuggles in a coherence claim it cannot earn: combining two empirically-silent speculative frameworks is presented as potentially *mutually strengthening* when the multiplication of unverifiable assumptions should, by the article's own Occam's-limits discipline, count against it.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist
Churchland's April attack on folk-psychological natural kinds (pain, wincing) still lands, because the May refine did nothing to address it — and it is now *sharper*. The Subset Law* requires an experiential property E whose causal profile is "a subset of" a physical property P's default profile. But "subset" presupposes that both profiles are individuated at the same grain. Neuroscience individuates nociceptive-affective-motor processing at a grain that has no clean counterpart in the folk category "pain." If "pain" decomposes into a dozen dissociable neural processes, the article owes an account of *which* experiential property is the subset-bearer, and it has none. The whole formalism inherits the indeterminacy of its folk relata. This is unaddressed by the refine and should be flagged as a standing gap, not a settled matter.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist
Dennett's "relabelling, not discovery" charge is now in an awkward position relative to the article's own concessions. The article concedes (Testability and Limitations) that "the neural pathways are identical" in delegation and physicalism — it says all parties predict the same observable causal patterns. But then it claims delegation has an *advantage* over epiphenomenalism: "delegation explains why introspective reports track experiences." Dennett's response: physicalism also explains this (self-modelling), and does so without the extra ontology — the article admits as much two paragraphs later. So the article is left claiming an advantage over one dualist rival (epiphenomenalism) that is *simultaneously not an advantage over the physicalist*. The honest conclusion is that delegation's entire value proposition is "if you have already bought dualism, here is the least-bad way to keep mental causation." The article says this once ("genuine advantages *for someone already committed to dualism*") but its tone elsewhere — "Saad's work arguably strengthens the philosophical foundations for the Map's interactionist position" — overstates it. The strengthening is conditional on a prior commitment the strengthening cannot itself justify.

### The Quantum Skeptic
Tegmark's decoherence objection was partially absorbed: the entanglement-binding paragraph now says the integration "presupposes ... that macroscopic neural entanglement is physically sustainable ... [which is not] settled." Good. But the hedge is doing suspicious work. The article still writes "An entangled quantum state spanning many neurons *would constitute* a single non-decomposable physical state with a collective default causal profile." The conditional "would constitute" presupposes the antecedent is live; Tegmark's whole point is that femtosecond decoherence makes the antecedent not merely unsettled but, on current physics, false at brain temperatures. "Unsettled" understates the burden. The article should not be allowed to treat "macroscopic neural entanglement" as a possibility-on-equal-footing with its negation when the physics consensus is sharply against it.

### The Many-Worlds Defender
Deutsch's April point — that the article's MWI rejection "begs the question" — has a new wrinkle. The article's "Many-Worlds Considerations" section says the Map's quantum interactionism "requires outcomes to be *selected* (not merely branched), so rejection of MWI is necessary for the combined view." This is fine *as a statement of the Map's commitment*, but the article then lists "No Many-Worlds tenet" under "Relation to Site Perspective" as though delegatory dualism *supports* it. It does not — the article itself says three times that Saad's theory is neutral on quantum interpretation. So the No-MWI tenet entry is honest in content ("relevant only to our quantum integration proposal, not to Saad's theory") but mis-placed: a tenet the article admits the subject theory does not bear on should not appear in a section titled "Delegatory dualism supports the Map's core tenets." That heading promises support the body then retracts.

### The Empiricist
Popper's ghost is now mostly satisfied — the article concedes unfalsifiability-against-rivals explicitly, which is exactly the candour the April review demanded. The residual Popperian complaint is narrower: the article says the theory "*would* be falsified by an observed violation of Closure," then immediately says Observational Closure "is precisely the constraint that ensures such violations don't occur at observable scales." This is presented as a "trade-off ... stated plainly," but logically it is closer to self-sealing: the theory's one falsification condition is one the theory's own central constraint guarantees will never be met. The article gestures at this ("the constraint that makes the theory coherent also makes it empirically silent") but does not draw the sharper conclusion — that this is not a falsification *condition* at all in any operative sense, and calling it one is generous.

### The Buddhist Philosopher
Nagarjuna would note that the entire delegation picture reifies a "default causal profile" — a fully-determinate set of effects a physical state *would* produce in the counterfactual absence of experience. The article admits this baseline "is never directly observed" and that the only indirect access (a zombie functional-duplicate) "presupposes that such zombies are metaphysically possible, itself a contested claim." Nagarjuna's deeper point: a causal profile that is never instantiated as such, accessible only via a contested metaphysical possibility, and defined entirely by its relations to other never-isolated profiles, is precisely an empty (svabhava-lacking) posit dressed as a fundamental. The theory's central primitive is a counterfactual that, by the theory's own account, can never obtain wherever the theory applies (because "wherever experience is present, delegation is already occurring"). The Map should ask whether it is building on a foundation the article itself describes as permanently unobservable.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Intra-dualist advantages collapse under the article's own equivalence concession
- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "Testability and Limitations" and "Relation to Site Perspective" sections (lines ~151-157, ~239)
- **Problem**: The article claims delegation's advantages are "primarily *intra-dualist*" — it beats epiphenomenalism (explains report-tracking) and overdeterminism (avoids redundancy). But it also concedes physicalism explains the same phenomena more cheaply. The two strongest advantage-claims (report-tracking, function-tracking) are therefore advantages over *one* rival while being neutral-or-worse against another, which the article does not net out. The closing "Saad's work arguably strengthens the philosophical foundations" overstates a benefit the body has already shown is conditional on a prior dualist commitment.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add one sentence making the netting explicit: the report-tracking and function-tracking "advantages" are advantages *only* against epiphenomenalism, not against physicalism, so they presuppose rather than support the choice of dualism. Soften "strengthens the philosophical foundations" to "offers the most coherent internal option for a position already adopted on other grounds."

### Issue 2: The "default causal profile" is treated as load-bearing while admitted to be permanently unobservable
- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "The Subset Law*" (lines ~87-95)
- **Problem**: The article concedes the default profile "is never directly observed," that indirect access requires metaphysically-possible zombies (contested), yet the entire delegation mechanism is defined in terms of it. This is a foundational vulnerability: a fundamental posited entity that cannot in principle be observed wherever the theory applies. The article notes the counterfactual character but then says "Despite this counterfactual character, the default baseline enables the delegation mechanism" — asserting the mechanism works without addressing whether an unobservable-in-principle primitive can do explanatory work.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add an explicit acknowledgment that the default profile is a theoretical posit whose only access route depends on the contested possibility of zombies, and that this makes the Subset Law* a postulate rather than an independently-motivated law. This is a candour fix, consistent with the May 25 calibration discipline.

### Issue 3: Part II presents combining two empirically-silent frameworks as potentially mutually strengthening, against the Map's own Occam's-limits discipline
- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "Relation to Quantum Interactionism," "entanglement-binding" paragraph (lines ~199-201)
- **Problem**: The article says entanglement-binding "may strengthen this integration" and that together delegation + entanglement "might address complementary aspects of the mind-body problem." Even with the added hedges, the framing is that combining two speculative, empirically-silent frameworks could *increase* explanatory force. The Map's own Occam's-Razor-Has-Limits tenet (correctly read) says parsimony cannot adjudicate either way under incomplete knowledge — it does not license treating assumption-multiplication as a strengthening. The article elsewhere admits "combining two speculative frameworks multiplies assumptions rather than reducing them" (line 187), which directly contradicts the "may strengthen" framing two sections later.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Reconcile the line-187 disclaimer with the "may strengthen" language. Either qualify "may strengthen" to "may cohere with (without thereby gaining independent support)," or explicitly note that the coherence of the combination is not evidence for either component.

### Issue 4: The single falsification condition is guaranteed unmet by the theory's central constraint
- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "Testability and Limitations" (line ~153)
- **Problem**: The article offers one falsifier (an observed Closure violation) and immediately notes Observational Closure "ensures such violations don't occur at observable scales." This is acknowledged as a trade-off but not as the stronger structural point: the stated falsification condition is one the theory's defining constraint guarantees will never obtain, so it is not an operative falsifier.
- **Severity**: Low
- **Recommendation**: Sharpen the existing sentence to note that, because Observational Closure is a constitutive constraint, the "falsification condition" is not a live empirical test of *this* theory but a test of dualist interactionism in general — and even there only against the universal-closure denier.

### Issue 5: "No Many-Worlds tenet" listed as supported in a section the article's own text contradicts
- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "Relation to Site Perspective" (lines ~237)
- **Problem**: Under a heading stating "Delegatory dualism supports the Map's core tenets," the No-MWI entry says Saad's theory "does not depend on any particular quantum interpretation" and the rejection is "relevant only to our quantum integration proposal, not to Saad's theory." This is correct but means the article is listing, as supported, a tenet it says the subject does not bear on.
- **Severity**: Low
- **Recommendation**: Either move the No-MWI note out of the "supports" framing into a short "Tenets the subject theory is neutral on" note, or reword the section heading to "Relation to the Map's tenets" (neutral) so neutral entries are not mis-read as support.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Claim: Delegation explains why introspective reports are reliable
- **Current content says**: On a causal theory of reference, reports are reliable because caused by what they describe (lines ~135).
- **A critic would argue** (Dennett): Physical self-modelling delivers the same reliability without non-physical causes; the article concedes this. So "explains" is doing comparative work it cannot sustain against physicalism — only against epiphenomenalism.
- **Suggested response**: Restrict the reliability claim explicitly to the intra-dualist comparison and note the physicalist parity already conceded in the Testability section.

### Claim: Entanglement provides physical holism, delegation provides causal efficacy
- **Current content says**: The two "might address complementary aspects" — unity via entanglement, efficacy via delegation (line ~201).
- **A critic would argue** (Tegmark): The entanglement premise is not merely "unsettled" but contradicted by decoherence physics at brain temperatures; pairing it with delegation does not rescue it.
- **Suggested response**: Upgrade the hedge from "unsettled" to acknowledge the decoherence consensus explicitly, cross-linking the article that handles it (e.g. `decoherence-and-macroscopic-superposition`).
