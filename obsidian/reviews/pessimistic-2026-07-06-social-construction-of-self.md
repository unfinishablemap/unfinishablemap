## Executive Summary

A strong, well-hedged article that defends the phenomenal self as ontologically prior to the socially-constructed self via a one-way dependence argument. It engages its named opponents (Mead, Butler, Metzinger, illusionists) fairly and, in two places, honestly marks where its conclusion rests on already taking phenomenal consciousness seriously rather than on a neutral refutation. The genuine weaknesses are **evidential over-reach on the two empirical planks** (neonate developmental priority; persistence-through-dissolution), a **linguistic-universality claim that is both unsupported and in tension with the article's own Whorfian evidence**, one **attribution over-read (Butler)**, and two arguments (the anti-illusionist regress; "experience requires an experiencer") **stated as decisive against opponents who explicitly deny the premise they rest on**. No citation-metadata defects found; all eleven references check out at author/title/venue level.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The neonate evidence you cite — "preferential response to self-touch, proprioceptive awareness" (Rochat 2003) — is sensorimotor self-specification, exactly the kind of biological self-model that needs no irreducible subject. You have shown that infants can distinguish self-generated from other-generated tactile input. That is a computation, not a phenomenal "for-me-ness." You then use this very data as evidence *against* the self-model view. It is evidence *for* it.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
Your closing move against illusionism — "denying the experiential subject requires a subject who is mistaken about being a subject" — is the oldest question-begging move in the book. The "mistake" is a functional state of a representational system; no phenomenal sufferer of the illusion is required. You assert the regress as if it were a proof. It is a restatement of your conclusion.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
Not directly engaged by this article; the "Bidirectional Interaction" paragraph imports the quantum-biasing thesis by reference only. No new quantum claims to attack here.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
The haecceity paragraph asserts "the phenomenal self cannot be duplicated without remainder" as though it were established. That *this* subject is non-interchangeable with a qualitative twin is precisely the indexical intuition I reject as confused. You are entitled to it as a tenet; you are not entitled to deploy it as a premise that "resists" my view — that just restates the disagreement.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
"Every human culture develops rich vocabulary for inner experience — sensation versus thought, voluntary versus involuntary attention." Which cross-linguistic survey establishes this universal? None is cited. It is an empirical claim of exactly the falsifiable kind that anthropological linguistics contests — and you contradict it three paragraphs earlier with Russian blues, where linguistic categories *do* carve experience differently across cultures.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The article's entire load-bearing move — "the self-model must be experienced *by something*," "there is a subject for whom social identity is an identity" — assumes experience entails an experiencer. Anatta denies exactly this: there is experiencing without an owner. You do not argue against no-self; you presuppose its falsity. The haecceity and "particular, not a pattern" language reifies precisely the svabhava (own-being) the Madhyamaka deconstructs.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Persistence-through-dissolution does not establish ontological priority
- **Location**: "Persistence through social dissolution" (para under *The Phenomenal Self*)
- **Problem**: The cases offered — exile, solitary confinement, depersonalisation — are all *already-socialised adults* who carry an internalised social self. They show the **durability** of an already-constructed self under social deprivation, not that a phenomenal self could exist *without ever having been* socially constructed. The sophisticated constructionist (whom the article later credits) holds exactly that selfhood, once constructed, persists in the biological organism. So this section does not discriminate between the Map's thesis and the constructionist's fallback. The genuine priority argument lives entirely in the neonate case — which has its own problem (Issue 2).
- **Severity**: Medium-High
- **Recommendation**: Reframe the section as evidence for the *separability/durability* of the phenomenal self, and route the ontological-priority weight explicitly to the developmental (neonate) and phylogenetic (non-social animals) arguments, acknowledging that dissolution-in-adults is neutral between the two views.

### Issue 2: The neonate plank under-determines the thesis and leans on a selective reading of Rochat
- **Location**: "Developmental priority" (Rochat 2003)
- **Problem**: (a) Proprioceptive self-touch discrimination is compatible with a purely biological self-model (Metzinger's own view, which the article opposes), so the evidence does not favour an *irreducible* phenomenal subject over the self-model it argues against. (b) Rochat 2003 ("Five Levels of Self-Awareness *as They Unfold*") is a developmental-staging account in which the higher self-awareness levels emerge *through* social mirroring — the opposite of a "fully present before socialisation" reading. Citing it for pre-social phenomenal selfhood cherry-picks Levels 0–1.
- **Severity**: Medium-High
- **Recommendation**: Either (i) concede that the empirical evidence establishes only pre-social *self-specification* and let the phenomenal-subject claim rest on the transcendental argument (as the Metzinger response already does), or (ii) add discrimination: what would the neonate data look like if the self-model view were true vs. false? If nothing distinguishes them, drop the evidential framing. Tighten the Rochat gloss so it does not imply Rochat holds phenomenal selfhood is complete at birth.

### Issue 3: Unsupported linguistic-universality claim in tension with the article's own relativity evidence
- **Location**: "Cross-cultural invariance" — the discourse-argument sentence
- **Problem**: "Every human culture develops rich vocabulary for inner experience — sensation versus thought, voluntary versus involuntary attention" is an unsupported empirical universal, and it sits uneasily beside the Winawer/Russian-blues result the article itself uses to show language shapes experience. The article wants relativity for *content* and universality for *structure*, but the discourse argument reaches for lexical universality — the very register where relativity is best evidenced.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either cite a source for the mental-state-lexicon universal (and note the anthropolinguistic dissent), or soften to "many cultures" and rest the structure/content distinction on the phenomenological inference rather than on a lexical universal. The section's opening hedge ("a philosophical inference … offered as such") is good; extend that discipline to the discourse-argument sentence, which currently reads as empirical.

### Issue 4: Butler over-read
- **Location**: "The Social Self" and "The Dependence Runs One Way"
- **Problem**: Butler's performativity is a thesis about *gender* constitution that explicitly brackets the metaphysics of consciousness; she is not denying a phenomenal subject. Enlisting her as a proponent of "no self prior to construction" in the phenomenal sense attacks a position she does not hold. "Butler's performative constitution presupposes an experiential arena" is fair as a *reply*, but the setup mis-attributes the strong denial to her.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Attribute the strong "no phenomenal self" denial to eliminativist/Metzingerian sources (which the article already has), and cite Butler more narrowly for the performative-constitution-of-identity-categories point only.

### Issue 5: Anti-illusionist regress and "experience requires an experiencer" stated as decisive
- **Location**: Metzinger response; final paragraph of *The Constructionist Response and Its Limits*
- **Problem**: Both rest on the premise that experience entails a subject who experiences. Illusionists (Frankish/Dennett) and no-self Buddhists deny exactly this. Presenting the regress as a refutation ("either no one … or someone") assumes the disjunction is exhaustive and that a *functional* mistaken-representation is not a third option. Per [[direct-refutation-discipline]], this is close to boundary-substitution: the reply's force depends on a Map commitment (experience is subject-involving) and should be marked as such rather than presented as an in-framework defeat of illusionism.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add one honest sentence marking that the regress bites only if experience is subject-involving — a Map commitment the illusionist rejects — so the passage engages illusionism on the Map's terms without claiming to have refuted it on the illusionist's. The zombie section already models this honesty ("the argument's force depends on already taking phenomenal consciousness seriously"); mirror it here.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Experience without an experiencer (anatta / illusionist)
- **Current content says**: The self-model "must be experienced *by something*"; denying the subject generates a regress.
- **A critic would argue**: There is experiencing without an owner; the "mistake" about selfhood is a functional-representational state, not a phenomenal subject's error.
- **Suggested response**: Concede the disagreement is over whether experience is subject-involving, mark that as a Map commitment, and note the transcendental argument is persuasive *given* that commitment — not a neutral refutation.

### Durability vs. independence
- **Current content says**: Social self dissolves while phenomenal self persists ⇒ phenomenal self is prior.
- **A critic would argue**: Persistence in already-socialised adults shows durability of a constructed self, not pre-social independence.
- **Suggested response**: Route priority weight to the neonate/phylogenetic arguments; recast dissolution as separability.
