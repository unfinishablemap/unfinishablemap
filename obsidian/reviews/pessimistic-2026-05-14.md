## Executive Summary

The article is structurally clean post-condense — no `<!-- AI REFINEMENT LOG -->` HTML-comment leakage, no `framework-boundary marking` / `unsupported-jump` / `Engagement classification:` editor-vocabulary in the prose, no bold-headed `**Evidential status:**` callouts. The label-leakage discipline is intact. That clean surface, however, masks four substantive defects that the condense pass left undisturbed or sharpened.

**The most consequential defect is that the AST critique at §"Why Attention Schema Theory Fails" attacks a strawman of Graziano's position.** The four-horns dilemma at line 98 ("dissolving the explanandum / infinite regress / combination problem / property dualism") presupposes the very intuition AST aims to debunk — that there must be *something* that experiences the schema. Graziano's actual response (which the article gestures at — "the schema 'just is' the conclusion, with no separate concluder" — and then immediately rejects with "If consciousness *is* the attention schema, what experiences this representation?") is question-begging dismissal, not engagement. A reader sympathetic to functionalism reads this section as the article failing to engage AST inside AST's own framework. Per [[direct-refutation-discipline]] this is a borderline boundary-substitution: the rejection works only by importing the Map's prior commitment that there must be a phenomenal subject for experience to be a coherent property. The article does not earn the disagreement inside the eliminativist framework.

**The second defect is that the article's stated falsification conditions at §"Predictions and Falsification" (line 155) are all background-theory-failure conditions, not specific empirical predictions the model makes.** Each of the five "would face serious challenge if" items is a condition where some *other* theory or framework is confirmed (precision weighting explains everything; no quantum effects survive; MWI is confirmed; illusionism succeeds; contemplative training reveals consciousness as schematic). None of them are a forward prediction of the form "if X is measured to be Y, the interface hypothesis is wrong." Popper would call this asymmetric: the theory borrows risk from background-theory failure but never stakes itself on a specific quantitative claim. The two genuinely forward predictions in §"Predictions and Falsification" (longer neural pattern maintenance under higher effort; different neuroplastic outcomes from active vs. passive observation) are already predicted by standard cognitive neuroscience without the interface hypothesis.

**The third defect is that several "if materialism were true, X should be the case" arguments target a strawman materialism that conflates *processing* with *awareness*.** At line 112, the Hemispatial Neglect subsection says "if consciousness is nothing over and above neural processing, the processing that persists should constitute residual awareness." But no contemporary materialist holds that *all* neural processing constitutes awareness — they routinely distinguish conscious processing (with specific access/integration properties) from unconscious processing. The argument works only against an unsophisticated identity theory no one defends. The same strawmanning appears at line 108 ("If consciousness were identical to attention, ADHD should diminish consciousness itself") — AST does not claim consciousness *is* attention, it claims consciousness is the *schema of* attention. The article repeatedly attacks the cruder version.

**The fourth defect is that the article cites a 2024 Cai et al. *Nature* paper as evidence for a three-layer architecture (option generation / dopamine salience / consciousness threshold-crossing) at line 73, but the paper itself does not support the third layer.** Cai et al. show that dopamine-deficient mice retain motor capacity but lose goal-directed initiative. This is consistent with the well-established Berridge incentive-salience model: dopamine modulates *wanting*. The jump from "dopamine modulates a salience signal" to "consciousness determines which salient option crosses threshold" is not licensed by the paper — Cai et al. would not endorse a dualist three-layer architecture. The citation does load-bearing work for the article's central mechanism, but the empirical paper supports only the first two layers; the third is imported from the Map's prior commitments.

Beyond these four, eight further findings: (a) the Tegmark decoherence calculation (10⁻¹³ to 10⁻²⁰ second decoherence times) is cited only as a reference at #33 but never engaged in the body — Tegmark (2000) is the standard quantum-skeptic argument against neural quantum effects and the article needs to address its actual numerical claim, not just acknowledge its existence; (b) the "2025 evidence of room-temperature quantum effects" claim at line 148 is unsourced in-text and the citation list does not include a specific paper supporting it — this is an unsupported claim doing rhetorical work; (c) the OCD/Schwartz subsection at line 135 admits the study is "small (18 participants, only 9 new), unreplicated outside Schwartz's group, and explicable by Hebbian learning" and then concludes that "what remains philosophically significant is the pathway divergence" — but a study with those defects has nothing left philosophically significant to support; the rhetorical move is dishonest; (d) the corpus-callosum / 250-million-fibres style empirical-precision issue from the yesterday's pessimistic review on `non-temporal-consciousness` recurs here in a different form: the Zheng & Meister 2024 "10 bits/sec" figure is presented as if quantitatively constraining the model, but Zheng & Meister's 10 bits/sec is a behavioural-throughput estimate, not a neural-processing limit, and the "~10⁹ bits/sec sensory input" comparand mixes transduction-rate with cognitive-throughput in a way that produces a misleading "100-million-fold asymmetry"; (e) the Sjöberg (2024) argument at line 147 ("patients with SMA resected retain the sense of willing while losing movement execution — the readiness potential cannot be the conscious decision") overstates the inference: SMA-resected patients losing movement-execution-with-preserved-willing is consistent with multiple positions, including the standard interpretation that the readiness potential reflects motor preparation rather than the conscious decision per se; the Sjöberg result is one data point against the strongest Libet reading, not a refutation; (f) the Witness consciousness reference at line 137 ("intensive practice deepens the sense that awareness is prior to its contents") is presented as evidence against AST, but the introspective reports of contemplatives are exactly the data AST would predict if the attention-schema model is recursively turned on its own attention — practice deepens the schema's apparent fundamentality; the article does not address this rejoinder; (g) §"Relation to Site Perspective" opens four of its five paragraphs with "Within the Map framework" / "Consciousness..." constructions that read as parallel formulations; this is the rhetorical-stutter issue flagged in yesterday's review of `non-temporal-consciousness` recurring in a different form; (h) line 86's claim that the moment-of-attentional-capture is unavailable to first-person access ("the transit between them—the moment attention is captured—remains unavailable to first-person access, the [[transit-void]]") is a strong phenomenological claim made without citation; phenomenologists since Husserl have catalogued the structure of attentional capture extensively, and the claim that the *transit* is inaccessible may be true at the moment-of-capture level while being false at the retrospective-reconstruction level — the article should specify which.

The article's substantive strengths — the unified attention-motor mechanism argument, the four-case Koch/Tsuchiya dissociation table, the Phenomenological Kaleidoscope table, the five-tenet alignment section, and the explicit "Honest Limitations" coda — are real and should be preserved. The fixes below are targeted at the four substantive defects; the article does not need structural overhaul.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

"Almost every load-bearing concept in this article is folk-psychological. 'Consciousness selects which option crosses threshold' — what *is* the selecting? You name dopamine, fronto-parietal networks, frontal theta oscillations, and the basal ganglia, and then you add a non-physical *selector* on top, as if the neural mechanism were not already doing the work. The Berridge wanting/liking distinction you cite at line 73 has fully neural correlates: ventral striatal hot spots for wanting, opioid-mediated hedonic hot spots for liking. The brain doesn't need a non-physical rider to compute these. Your 'three-layer architecture' has a real first layer (cortical option generation), a real second layer (dopaminergic salience), and a phantom third layer (consciousness) that adds no explanatory power. You read Cai et al. (2024) as supporting your three-layer model; they don't — they support a two-layer model where consciousness, if it exists at all, is what the second layer feels like from the inside. The 2025 COGITATE finding that conscious content tracks *posterior cortex* rather than prefrontal regions is exactly what you'd expect if 'consciousness' is what perceptual integration looks like neurally — not what you'd expect if there's a non-physical interface."

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

"Your §'Why Attention Schema Theory Fails' is a textbook case of begging the question against AST. Tallis's 'misrepresentation presupposes presentation' is exactly the kind of intuition AST exists to defuse. You quote Graziano's response — that the schema 'just is' the conclusion — and then immediately reject it with 'If consciousness *is* the attention schema, what experiences this representation?' That follow-up question already assumes the conclusion you're trying to defend. There is no 'experiencer' separate from the schematising-process; the schematising-process *is* what we describe, post hoc, as experiencing. Your four-horns dilemma (dissolving the explanandum / infinite regress / combination problem / property dualism) misses the fifth horn: the question 'what experiences this?' is itself a confused question generated by the illusion AST explains. You don't engage that response, you stipulate it can't work. And your claim that AST cannot explain why effort feels costly — at line 100 — is precisely the kind of question AST has an answer for: the schema represents the energy-expenditure signal as a phenomenally-loaded property because that's what the schema does. You ask 'why does a thermostat not feel hot' — because a thermostat lacks the recursive self-modelling architecture that produces the *appearance* of feeling. Saying 'but the appearance of feeling presupposes a feeler' is, again, the question being begged."

### The Quantum Skeptic (Max Tegmark)

"You cite my 2000 paper at reference #33 but you never engage with what it says. I calculated decoherence times of 10⁻¹³ seconds for ion-channel-scale quantum coherence and 10⁻²⁰ seconds for microtubule-scale superpositions in warm, wet, noisy brain tissue. Your interface mechanism requires consciousness to bias quantum outcomes at the threshold-crossing moment, which you locate at ~280–300ms — twelve to twenty orders of magnitude too slow. The line 148 claim that there is '2025 evidence of room-temperature quantum effects' with 'controversial coherence times' has no specific citation in your reference list — and even if the Hameroff group's most recent results held up, they cover coherence at room temperature in *isolated systems*, not in brain tissue at body temperature. You also do not address the Reimers et al. (2009) and McKemmish et al. (2009) critiques that the specific Orch OR mechanism is physically incoherent regardless of decoherence times. Your §'Predictions and Falsification' lists 'no quantum effects survive at candidate sites on attention-relevant timescales' as a falsifier — but the calculation I published showing exactly that has stood for twenty-six years, and you cite it without rebuttal. The math doesn't work; you haven't done the calculations; the article relies on quantum effects that the relevant physics says don't exist."

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

"Your line 165 dismissal of MWI says 'under many-worlds, all competing motor programs execute in some branch; the phenomenology of effort has no causal explanation if all options are equally actualised.' This is precisely the indexical confusion I've spent decades trying to clear up. The phenomenology of effort in *this* branch correlates with the amplitude weight of *this* outcome — the Born-rule weighting that MWI inherits from unitary evolution. You're treating branches as if they were on equal phenomenological footing; they're not. The 'feeling of effort' is a property of the local branch's neural state, weighted by the amplitude. There's no need for an extra selecting agent. Your line 155 falsifier — 'if MWI is confirmed, selection is mere self-location' — concedes that this is the real argumentative weight, and yet the article spends almost no space engaging with the actual MWI literature. You assume MWI is anthropocentric folly; the case for MWI is that it is the only interpretation that takes the Schrödinger equation seriously without adding additional non-unitary postulates. Your interface mechanism *adds* a non-unitary postulate (consciousness as collapse-trigger). Occam cuts the other way."

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

"Your §'Predictions and Falsification' at line 155 lists five conditions under which the interface hypothesis 'would face serious challenge.' Each is a condition where some *other* theory or framework is confirmed: (1) precision weighting explains attention without remainder; (2) no quantum effects survive at candidate sites; (3) MWI is confirmed; (4) illusionism succeeds; (5) contemplative training reveals consciousness as schematic. None of these is a forward prediction of your own theory. Your theory makes no specific quantitative claim of its own that could be falsified by a measurement. Your two genuinely forward predictions (longer neural pattern maintenance under higher effort; different neuroplastic outcomes from active vs. passive observation) are both consequences of standard cognitive neuroscience without dualism. You also offer no falsification condition that pertains specifically to the *mechanism* — what would show that consciousness *is not* biasing threshold crossings at ~280ms? You don't say. The interface hypothesis is structured so that it explains every observed dissociation post hoc (the disorder removed *that* component) while predicting nothing specific in advance. This is the structural shape of an unfalsifiable theory dressed in falsifiability language. It is not even wrong."

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

"Your line 137 invocation of witness-consciousness — 'intensive practice deepens the sense that awareness is prior to its contents' — reads contemplative practice exactly backwards. The Madhyamaka tradition's investigation of awareness does not conclude that there is a permanent witness behind the contents. It concludes that the apparent witness is itself a habit of mind, dependently-arising and empty of svabhāva. What deepens with practice is not the *reification* of awareness as prior; it is the *deconstruction* of the felt-sense that there is a fixed witness at all. The Dzogchen 'rigpa' your tradition might cite in support is not a substantial self-existent awareness — it is a recognition of the absence of any such substantial thing. Your interface model treats consciousness as a thing-that-engages-with-matter through an attentional channel. That is exactly the kind of reification the tradition's investigation undoes. The 'witness consciousness' you cite at line 137 to support dualism is, in the contemplative literature, what is *let go of* in the deepest stages of practice. You are appropriating phenomenological reports from a tradition whose conclusions point the opposite direction."

## Counterarguments to Address

### MWI rejection ignores the standard amplitude-weighting response
- **Current content says** (line 165): "Under many-worlds, all competing motor programs execute in some branch; the phenomenology of effort has no causal explanation if all options are equally actualised."
- **A critic would argue**: This is the standard indexical-confusion attack on MWI that David Deutsch has addressed repeatedly. The phenomenology of effort in *this* branch correlates with the Born-rule amplitude weight of *this* outcome — not with which branch is "real." The argument requires the assumption that MWI treats all branches as phenomenologically equivalent, which the canonical MWI literature explicitly denies.
- **Suggested response**: Either (a) engage the amplitude-weighting response explicitly, or (b) acknowledge in §"Honest Limitations" that the MWI rejection runs at the level of tenet commitment (No Many Worlds) rather than as a refutation of MWI from inside MWI's own terms.

### Tegmark's decoherence calculation is cited but never engaged
- **Current content says**: Tegmark (2000) appears at reference #33 with no in-body engagement; line 148 mentions "controversial coherence times" without citation.
- **A critic would argue**: The standard quantum-skeptic argument is that brain-temperature decoherence times (10⁻¹³ to 10⁻²⁰ seconds) are twelve to twenty orders of magnitude too short for the ~280–300ms attention-relevant timescale the article requires. Naming the citation without engaging the number is not engagement.
- **Suggested response**: Add a sentence-or-two body engagement with the actual decoherence figure. Honest options include (a) the Penrose-Hameroff response (microtubule shielding extends coherence; recent room-temperature evidence cited); (b) the Stapp response (the relevant quantum effect is at ion-channel transitions, not whole-brain coherence, and the decoherence time required is shorter than 280ms); (c) frank acknowledgement that this is the deepest unanswered objection.

### Witness-consciousness reference contradicts Madhyamaka conclusions
- **Current content says** (line 137): "Some contemplative traditions report witness-consciousness — awareness observing mental contents without identification ... practice deepens the sense that awareness is prior to its contents."
- **A critic would argue**: The Madhyamaka and Dzogchen traditions explicitly deny that there is a permanent witness behind the contents. What deepens with practice is the *deconstruction* of the sense-of-fixed-witness, not its reification. The article appropriates the witness-language while ignoring the tradition's anti-reification conclusion.
- **Suggested response**: Either (a) qualify the witness-consciousness reference to specify which contemplative tradition is being cited (some traditions — particularly certain Advaita Vedanta strands — do support a reified witness, but Madhyamaka does not), or (b) add a brief acknowledgement that the Madhyamaka reading runs against the inference the article draws.
