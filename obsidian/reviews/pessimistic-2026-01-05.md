### Issue 2: Bidirectional Interaction Argument May Be Circular

- **File**: `tenets.md` (Bidirectional Interaction section)
- **Location**: "The very fact that we discuss consciousness proves it is not epiphenomenal"
- **Problem**: This argument assumes what it seeks to prove. An epiphenomenalist would say our discussions of consciousness are themselves physical processes caused by other physical processes—we *think* we're talking about qualia, but this belief is itself just neurons firing. The argument needs to address why the content of our reports (not just the fact of reporting) requires non-physical causation.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Strengthen by addressing the epiphenomenalist response directly. Perhaps: "If consciousness were epiphenomenal, our beliefs about consciousness would be causally disconnected from actual conscious states, making them unreliable—yet we trust introspection enough to have this debate."

### Issue 3: Minimal Quantum Interaction Lacks Mechanism

- **File**: `tenets.md` (Minimal Quantum Interaction section)
- **Location**: Definition and Rationale
- **Problem**: The tenet posits that consciousness "biases" quantum outcomes without specifying how this is different from hidden variables or how it avoids being testable. If consciousness systematically biases outcomes in brains, this should be statistically detectable in principle. The claim of undetectability needs justification.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Address why neural-level quantum biasing wouldn't aggregate into detectable statistical anomalies. Perhaps the bias is too small, or cancels out at macro scales, or is indistinguishable from noise—but this needs stating.

### Issue 4: No Many Worlds Rationale is Underdeveloped

- **File**: `tenets.md` (No Many Worlds section)
- **Location**: Rationale paragraph
- **Problem**: The indexical argument ("why am I this instance?") is asserted but not developed. Many-worlds proponents have responses: the question is malformed, or answered by anthropic reasoning, or simply not a problem. These aren't addressed.
- **Severity**: Low-Medium
- **Recommendation**: Engage with at least one MWI response to the indexical problem. David Deutsch and Sean Carroll have written on this.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Physicalist Response to Dualism

- **Current content says**: The explanatory gap proves consciousness isn't reducible to physics.
- **A critic would argue**: The explanatory gap is epistemic, not ontological. We may simply lack the cognitive capacity to intuit how consciousness arises from physics—"mysterianism" (Colin McGinn). The gap doesn't prove dualism, only that we don't yet understand.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge mysterianism but note it still leaves the hard problem unsolved. the project's tenets are *chosen starting points*, not claimed proofs.

### Quantum Decoherence Objection

- **Current content says**: Quantum indeterminacy in the brain is the interface for mind-matter interaction.
- **A critic would argue**: Decoherence in warm, wet brains is extremely rapid—quantum superpositions collapse in femtoseconds. There's no time for consciousness to "choose" among options. (Max Tegmark's calculations)
- **Suggested response**: Address decoherence directly. Perhaps: the interaction occurs at a timescale faster than decoherence, or at a level where decoherence is less dominant (microtubules per Penrose-Hameroff?), or the objection applies to naive versions but not minimal interaction.

### Occam's Razor Defense

- **Current content says**: Occam's Razor has repeatedly pointed away from truth.
- **A critic would argue**: The examples given (heliocentrism, atoms) are cherry-picked. Occam's Razor has also pointed toward truth countless times. It's a useful heuristic *despite* occasional failures.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge this more explicitly. The point isn't that Occam's Razor is useless, but that it shouldn't be *decisive* against dualism when we're in a domain of fundamental ignorance.
