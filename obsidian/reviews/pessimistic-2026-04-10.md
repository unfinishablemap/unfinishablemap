# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-04-10
**Content reviewed**: `topics/biological-computationalisms-inadvertent-case-for-dualism.md`, `concepts/causal-delegation.md`, `topics/cognitive-phenomenology-and-the-irreducibility-of-thought.md`

## Executive Summary

These three articles share a common rhetorical strategy — interpreting physicalist concessions as inadvertent dualist admissions — but each contains logical gaps where the conclusion outruns the premises. The biological computationalism piece equivocates between "not abstractly computable" and "non-physical." The causal delegation piece presents an unfalsifiable mechanism as though unfalsifiability were a feature. The cognitive phenomenology piece generalises arguments beyond what the evidence supports and conflates phenomenal character with irreducibility.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would target the biological computationalism article most directly. The article assumes that if computation alone cannot explain consciousness, something non-physical must. But Churchland would insist this is a false dichotomy: substrate-dependent biological properties are still *physical* properties. The fact that consciousness requires specific neurobiological substrates is a victory for neuroscience, not a concession to dualism. Ion flows, electromagnetic fields, and metabolic processes are all physical — learning that consciousness depends on their specific configurations rather than abstract computation just tells us which physics matters.

On cognitive phenomenology, she would note that "what it is like to understand" may simply be our folk-psychological gloss on complex neural processing. Tip-of-the-tongue states and aphantasia cases can be explained by differential neural activation patterns without positing irreducible phenomenal properties. The introspective reports are real; the metaphysical conclusions drawn from them are not.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would focus on the cognitive phenomenology article's reliance on phenomenal contrast arguments. The felt difference between hearing French when you understand it and when you don't is real — but Dennett would argue it is a difference in *processing*, not in irreducible phenomenal character. Understanding involves activating semantic networks, generating predictions, priming responses — all of which alter the processing of the auditory input. The subject's report of a phenomenal difference is a report about these processing differences, not evidence of something beyond them.

On causal delegation, Dennett would be characteristically dismissive: the theory postulates invisible entities doing work that observable entities already explain. The "delegation" is a metaphysical ghost story — you cannot tell the difference between a world with delegation and one without, which means the concept does no explanatory work. The military analogy is misleading because actual military hierarchies are observable; causal delegation is postulated precisely because it *isn't*.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Tegmark would focus on the biological computationalism article's suggestion that quantum effects in biological substrates could provide an "interface for consciousness." The article mentions avian magnetoreception and photosynthetic energy transfer, but these are evolved quantum effects in specialised molecular structures — they are not evidence that brains use quantum effects for consciousness. The warm, wet neural environment decoheres quantum states in femtoseconds. Citing quantum biology in birds' eyes as support for quantum consciousness in human brains is a non sequitur.

On causal delegation, Tegmark would note that if consciousness "biases quantum outcomes," this would be detectable as a statistical anomaly in quantum measurements on neural tissue. The article claims this leaves "no empirical trace," but systematic biasing of quantum outcomes would violate Born rule statistics. You cannot bias outcomes and leave no trace — the trace is in the statistics.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

Deutsch would challenge the biological computationalism article's claim that substrate specificity "implicitly supports" rejection of Many-Worlds. The argument — that if consciousness depends on particular physical dynamics, indexical identity has "real physical content" — is a non sequitur. In MWI, each branch has its own specific substrate dynamics; the question of which branch "I" am on does not arise because there is a copy of me on each branch experiencing that branch's specific substrate dynamics. Substrate dependence is perfectly compatible with MWI.

On cognitive phenomenology, Deutsch might note that the experience of "grasping" a mathematical proof is the experience of a computation reaching a result — it feels like something because the information processing is complex enough to include self-modelling. Nothing here requires anything beyond physics.

### The Empiricist

A Popperian would find the causal delegation article the most problematic of the three. The article explicitly acknowledges that delegation generates no distinctive testable predictions and is "in the same predictive boat" as epiphenomenalism and physicalism. By Popper's criterion, a theory that makes no predictions different from its competitors is not a scientific theory. The article tries to redeem this by claiming "theoretical virtues," but Popper would insist that theoretical elegance without empirical content is metaphysics, not science.

The biological computationalism article also raises falsifiability concerns. The "convergence pattern" — every physicalist theory that specifies consciousness requirements allegedly converges on dualism — is unfalsifiable because any specification counts as convergence and any lack of specification counts as evasion. What physicalist finding would the article accept as *not* supporting dualism?

On cognitive phenomenology, the mathematical Mary variant is designed to produce only one answer. But the Popperian would ask: what would count as evidence *against* cognitive phenomenology? If people with aphantasia still have rich cognitive phenomenology, and people with inner speech also have rich cognitive phenomenology, and tip-of-the-tongue states demonstrate cognitive phenomenology — what *wouldn't* count as evidence for it?

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nagarjuna would question all three articles' foundational assumption: that there is a stable, unified subject whose experience is at stake. The biological computationalism article worries about whether silicon could be conscious "like us" — but what is this "us"? The self that worries about substrate dependence is itself a construction arising from interdependent processes. Reifying it as something that either does or doesn't transfer across substrates misunderstands its nature.

On causal delegation, the Buddhist critique is pointed: delegation presupposes a substantial self that "takes over" causal work from the physical. But if the self is empty of inherent existence — if it arises dependently from physical and mental conditions — then there is nothing to do the taking-over. The major-sergeant analogy assumes the major exists independently of the command structure. In dependent origination, there is no major — only the pattern of commanding itself.

The cognitive phenomenology article's claim that "what it is like to understand" is irreducible would face the two truths doctrine: conventionally, yes, there is a phenomenal character to understanding; ultimately, the character is empty — dependent on conditions, impermanent, without fixed essence. Building metaphysical dualism on phenomenal character is like building a house on a river.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Equivocation between "substrate-dependent" and "non-physical"
- **File**: `topics/biological-computationalisms-inadvertent-case-for-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "What 'The Algorithm Is the Substrate' Really Says" section
- **Problem**: The article slides from "properties that resist abstraction" to "non-physical properties" in a single paragraph. Substrate-dependent properties can be entirely physical — they are just physical properties of a specific substrate rather than abstract properties of any substrate. The inference that resisting abstraction = non-physical is the article's central move, and it is a non sequitur. The article partially hedges with the tripartite label ("non-physical," "irreducible higher-order physical," "non-computational biological") but then asserts "the logical structure is the same" — which is false. Property dualism and biological computationalism have fundamentally different ontological commitments.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Explicitly acknowledge that substrate-dependent properties can be physical. The argument should be: biological computationalism's concessions make it *structurally analogous* to dualism, not that they *entail* dualism. This is a weaker claim but a defensible one.

### Issue 2: Observational closure may be self-undermining
- **File**: `concepts/causal-delegation.md`
- **Location**: "Observational Closure" section
- **Problem**: The article presents observational closure as a feature — delegation is empirically invisible because experiences cause exactly what physics would. But this means delegation is explanatorily idle in exactly the way epiphenomenalism is. The article acknowledges they share a "predictive boat" but doesn't confront the deeper problem: if no observation distinguishes the theories, choosing between them is a matter of preference, not argument. The claimed advantage over epiphenomenalism (explaining why introspective reports track experiences) is a theoretical virtue, but theoretical virtues are precisely what you appeal to when you *lack* empirical evidence — the same move the article criticises physicalists for making.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Address the tension between claiming theoretical superiority over epiphenomenalism while sharing its empirical profile. Consider whether delegation's theoretical virtues genuinely exceed those of simpler alternatives, or whether the article is special-pleading.

### Issue 3: Mathematical Mary begs the question
- **File**: `topics/cognitive-phenomenology-and-the-irreducibility-of-thought.md`
- **Location**: "From Sensation to Cognition" section
- **Problem**: The article presents Mathematical Mary as extending the knowledge argument to cognition, then acknowledges the physicalist objection ("if Mary truly knows everything physical about mathematical understanding, perhaps she already understands") and dismisses it by asserting the gap is "starker" for cognition than for colour. But this is exactly what needs to be argued, not asserted. The physicalist's point is that knowing everything physical about mathematical understanding *includes* the physical processes that constitute understanding. Saying "no amount of neuroscientific description seems to constitute the grasp" restates the intuition rather than defending it.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either provide an argument for why the physicalist objection fails in the cognitive case (beyond restating the intuition), or frame Mathematical Mary as an illustration of the cognitive hard problem rather than an independent argument for irreducibility.

### Issue 4: Adaptive computational depth argument is circular
- **File**: `topics/biological-computationalisms-inadvertent-case-for-dualism.md`
- **Location**: Final paragraph of "What 'The Algorithm Is the Substrate' Really Says"
- **Problem**: The argument that "the allocator must stand outside the computation it directs" presupposes dualism to argue for dualism. The premise assumes consciousness is an allocator of computational resources that cannot itself be computational — but this is precisely the conclusion being argued for. A physicalist would say the allocation is itself a computation within the system, with no need for an external allocator.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Flag this as depending on the Map's prior commitment to consciousness as allocator, not as an independent argument from biological computationalism's own premises. The biological computationalism article should argue from what biological computationalists concede, not from what the Map independently believes.

### Issue 5: Misrepresentation of Piccinini's "noncomputational functionalism"
- **File**: `topics/biological-computationalisms-inadvertent-case-for-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "The Relocated Hard Problem" section
- **Problem**: The article claims Piccinini's admission of noncomputational functionalism is "structurally identical to the dualist claim." But Piccinini's noncomputational functionalism is still physicalist — it just denies that the relevant physical functions are computational ones. Physical but non-computational is not the same as non-physical. The article is treating "not computational" as though it means "not physical," which repeats the equivocation identified in Issue 1.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Accurately represent Piccinini's position as physicalist-but-non-computational and argue explicitly for why this is unstable — why non-computational physical functionalism faces pressures toward dualism — rather than asserting equivalence.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Biological Computationalism as Physicalist Success
- **Current content says**: Biological computationalism's concessions build the dualist case by narrowing what can be conscious.
- **A critic would argue**: Narrowing is exactly what good science does. Physics narrowed "things that burn" from phlogiston to oxidation — that didn't make combustion non-physical. Consciousness requiring specific substrates is a *physicalist* prediction being confirmed, not a dualist concession.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge this as a legitimate interpretation. Argue that the analogy breaks down because combustion was never defined by subjective experience — the narrowing of consciousness-capable substrates is different in kind because the explanandum (experience) has no physical definition to narrow toward.

### Delegation's Theoretical Superiority Is Assumed, Not Argued
- **Current content says**: Delegation has advantages over overdetermination, joint causation, and constitution.
- **A critic would argue**: The comparison is rigged. Overdetermination is presented as making the mental "redundant," but redundancy is a feature of robust causal systems. Joint causation is dismissed for "denying physical sufficiency," but dualists already deny physical sufficiency. Constitution is dismissed as "collapsing into reductionism," but only if you've already decided the mental is non-physical.
- **Suggested response**: Present the comparison more even-handedly. Acknowledge that each alternative has defenders who would not accept the characterisation given.

### Cognitive Phenomenology May Be Real but Reducible
- **Current content says**: If thought has phenomenal character, materialism cannot quarantine the hard problem to sensation.
- **A critic would argue**: Cognitive phenomenology could be real and still be physical — higher-order neural representations of cognitive processes. Having phenomenal character doesn't entail irreducibility. The hard problem is about the gap between physical description and experience, not about the *scope* of experience.
- **Suggested response**: Distinguish between the existence of cognitive phenomenology (which the article argues well) and its irreducibility (which requires separate argument). Currently the article slides from one to the other.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite the issues above, these are substantive articles with genuine philosophical merit:

- **Biological computationalism**: The "concession by concession" structure is effective and original. Tracing each of biological computationalism's claims to a classical dualist argument is a legitimate and illuminating comparison, even if the inference to actual dualism overreaches. The engagement with Milinkovic & Aru (2025) is timely and specific.
- **Causal delegation**: The clearest exposition of Saad's theory I've encountered on the site. The comparison table of causal structures (overdetermination, joint causation, constitution, delegation) is genuinely useful. The connection to skill-delegation as an inverse parallel is creative.
- **Cognitive phenomenology**: The argument from aphantasia is powerful and underutilised in the literature. The mathematical Mary variant is thought-provoking even if it needs tighter argument. The integration with the argument from reason and self-stultification creates genuine philosophical momentum.
