# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-03-08
**Content reviewed**: topics/the-psychophysical-control-law.md

## Executive Summary

"The Psychophysical Control Law" is a well-structured article that honestly acknowledges its own framework's central specification gap. However, the article's candour about the gap may inadvertently undermine the Map's project: by cataloguing how far away a psychophysical control law remains, it reads more as an argument *against* the viability of interactionist dualism than *for* it. Several claims rely on speculative mechanisms that face serious empirical objections, and key logical transitions smuggle in assumptions that deserve more scrutiny.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would find this article a confession of bankruptcy. The entire piece amounts to: "We need a psychophysical control law, we don't have one, we don't know what it would look like, and we may never be able to formulate one." For an eliminativist, this is precisely what happens when you start from a pre-scientific ontology—you create a "specification gap" that exists only because you postulated unnecessary entities. The Map invented a problem (how does the non-physical interact with the physical?) and now struggles to solve it. Neuroscience doesn't need psychophysical laws because there is nothing psychophysical to lawfully connect. The article's Section "The T-Shirt Problem" implicitly concedes this: if psychophysical laws resist elegant formulation, perhaps that's because they don't exist.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would press on the article's uncritical use of "phenomenal experience provides the sensor channel." This smuggles in the Hard Problem as a premise. The article treats phenomenal states—qualia, felt effort, the "what-it-is-like"—as if they were well-defined variables awaiting measurement. But Dennett would argue these are poorly defined philosophical constructs, not genuine variables. A "control law" connecting undefined inputs to physical outputs isn't a law at all—it's a category error dressed up in engineering notation. The control-theoretic framing doesn't make dualism more rigorous; it makes the conceptual confusion more precise.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Tegmark would zero in on the article's treatment of his own decoherence calculations. The article acknowledges 10⁻¹³ to 10⁻²⁰ second decoherence times but then pivots to "responses exist" without giving these responses adequate weight. The article mentions "Hameroff's corrected timescale estimates" without noting that these remain deeply contested—and that the broader physics community has not accepted them. Tegmark would also note that the article's "post-decoherence selection" escape route is conveniently unfalsifiable: if quantum coherence isn't needed, then the "quantum" in quantum consciousness does no work, and you're left with ordinary neural selection dressed in quantum language.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

Deutsch would challenge the article's claim that "under MWI, the phenomenal states of every branch-copy are equally real, and 'selection' reduces to indexical discovery rather than genuine causal influence." This mischaracterises the decision-theoretic approach within MWI. Wallace (2012)—whom the article cites—shows that rational agents in MWI have every reason to act as if probabilities are real. The article dismisses this by asserting that "a control law requires more than rational action—it requires that phenomenal states *cause* specific physical outcomes." But this begs the question: it assumes causal influence is needed precisely because the article is trying to prove causal influence is needed. The many-worlds defender can coherently explain all the phenomena the article attributes to "conscious selection" without any psychophysical law at all.

### The Empiricist

A Popperian would find this article deeply troubling. The article lists five constraints a control law must satisfy, including "falsifiability." But then every candidate formulation is acknowledged to be untestable with current technology. Stapp's law requires independently measuring "attention intensity"—which the article admits cannot be done. Eccles' law faces the pairing problem. The policy-selection law "pushes the hard question backward." The article's own analysis shows that no candidate is currently falsifiable. When will falsifiability actually arrive? The article's "Path Forward" section mentions three developments—bandwidth measurement, quantum biology, neurophenomenology—none of which has delivered testable psychophysical predictions. This is not a scientific research programme; it is a philosophical framework waiting indefinitely for science to provide content.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nagarjuna would question the article's entire ontological framework. The article assumes a stable, unified "consciousness" that serves as "the controller"—but what is this controller? If you search for it, you find only a stream of experiences with no fixed controller behind them. The article reifies consciousness into an agent that "senses error," "selects policies," and "modulates" physical processes. This is the self-view (atman) projected onto the control-theoretic framework. If the self is empty of inherent existence, there is no controller to write a control law for. The article's engineering metaphor—thermostat, autopilot—presupposes exactly the kind of bounded, persistent entity that Buddhist analysis deconstructs.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Self-Undermining Honesty
- **File**: topics/the-psychophysical-control-law.md
- **Location**: Throughout, especially "The Specification Gap"
- **Problem**: The article's thorough cataloguing of why a control law can't be specified reads as an inadvertent case for the impossibility of the project. By the end, a fair reader might conclude that the specification gap is not a temporary problem but a permanent feature indicating the framework is wrong.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Rebalance by more explicitly arguing why the gap reflects scientific immaturity rather than conceptual incoherence. The F=ma analogy is good but needs more development—show that the gap is *expected* at this stage rather than a sign of failure.

### Issue 2: The Mysterianism Escape Hatch
- **File**: topics/the-psychophysical-control-law.md
- **Location**: "The T-Shirt Problem" section, line 79
- **Problem**: The article introduces cognitive closure as a possible explanation for the specification gap. This is epistemically irresponsible in this context: it provides an unfalsifiable escape route. If the law can't be specified, that's evidence of cognitive closure, not evidence against the framework. This makes the position immune to failure and therefore non-scientific.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either commit to the view that the gap is solvable in principle (and cognitive closure is merely mentioned as a possibility others raise) or acknowledge directly that invoking cognitive closure here undermines the article's own call for falsifiability.

### Issue 3: OCD Evidence Overclaimed
- **File**: topics/the-psychophysical-control-law.md
- **Location**: Stapp's Attention-Rate Law section, line 95
- **Problem**: The article presents the Schwartz et al. (1996) OCD study as "a suggestive case" for Stapp's framework, then immediately notes it's "also compatible with purely physicalist neuroplasticity." The qualifier is honest, but the damage is done—the study is deployed rhetorically despite being entirely ambiguous. Using ambiguous evidence to support a specific mechanism while acknowledging its ambiguity is a form of having it both ways.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either develop the argument for why the OCD case is genuinely more consistent with Stapp than with neuroplasticity (which would require additional evidence) or present it purely as an example of what the prediction *would look like* if confirmed, rather than as even suggestive evidence.

### Issue 4: "~10 bits/second" Used Without Adequate Qualification
- **File**: topics/the-psychophysical-control-law.md
- **Location**: Multiple (lines 53, 117, 127, 153, 169)
- **Problem**: The article relies heavily on Zheng & Meister's ~10 bits/second finding as a constraint on the control channel. This is cited as "Zheng and Meister's ~10 bits/second finding" (line 153) as if it's an established fact. But this is a 2025 paper reporting a measurement of conscious processing bandwidth—the bandwidth of conscious *experience*, not necessarily the bandwidth of a hypothetical psychophysical control channel. The article conflates the two without argument.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add a sentence acknowledging that the ~10 bits/second figure measures conscious throughput, and that the psychophysical control channel—if it exists—might have different bandwidth characteristics. The identification requires argument, not assumption.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The Bandwidth-Mechanism Gap
- **Current content says**: Consciousness operates at ~10 bits/second and this constrains the control law to coarse-grained selection.
- **A critic would argue**: If the control channel is only 10 bits/second, how does consciousness "select" among quantum states that evolve on femtosecond timescales? There's a temporal mismatch of roughly 10 orders of magnitude between the control rate and the physical process being controlled. Standard control theory requires the controller to operate faster than the system being controlled.
- **Suggested response**: Address this explicitly—perhaps through the policy-level framing where consciousness doesn't control individual quantum events but selects among macro-level neural patterns that emerge from quantum processes.

### The Pairing Problem Generalized
- **Current content says**: The pairing problem undermines Eccles' framework specifically.
- **A critic would argue**: The pairing problem applies to *all* dualist interaction, not just Eccles'. If consciousness is non-spatial, how does it pair with *any* specific brain rather than all brains? The article mentions this only for Eccles but the problem threatens the entire control-law framework.
- **Suggested response**: Address the pairing problem directly as a general constraint on psychophysical control laws, not just Eccles' version. Reference existing Map content on the pairing problem if available.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite the criticisms above, the article has significant virtues worth preserving:

- **Intellectual honesty**: The article doesn't hide the specification gap or pretend it's close to being solved. This candour is rare and valuable in philosophy of mind.
- **Engineering framing**: Translating the philosophical problem into control-theoretic language makes vague ideas precise and exposes exactly what's missing.
- **Systematic constraint identification**: The five constraints section effectively narrows the search space.
- **Relation to Site Perspective**: Excellent—connects each tenet to the control law problem substantively, not perfunctorily. The No Many Worlds section is particularly strong.
- **Reference quality**: Citations are specific and accurate (page numbers, journal details).
