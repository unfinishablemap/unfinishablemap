## Executive Summary

Both articles are well-organised and substantively engage their material, but they share three structural defects and each carries article-specific issues. The shared defects: (1) **the Bohmian counter-example is absent** — `type-token-causation` rests its central move on the claim that "under any non-Many-Worlds interpretation" the token outcome is undetermined by physics, but Bohmian mechanics is a non-MWI deterministic hidden-variable interpretation where token outcomes *are* fully fixed by initial conditions; the framing is overconfident and the silent narrowing to "non-deterministic non-MWI interpretations" leaves the argument with a loophole the article does not even name; (2) **the phenomenal-concept strategy is absent from both pages**, despite outer reviewers from 2026-05-03 and 2026-05-04 having converged on this as the load-bearing omission in the catalogue's anti-epiphenomenalist treatment — `integration-as-activity` cites `epiphenomenalism` repeatedly and `type-token-causation` rests its operational case against epiphenomenalism on the type-token distinction, but neither engages the Frankish/Papineau/Loar reply that phenomenal-concept reports are physically caused by the very physical states the reports are about; (3) **convergence is overstated** — `integration-as-activity` claims that RPT, GWT, predictive processing, and Whiteheadian process metaphysics "converge on treating integration as something that happens rather than something that is," but three of the four converge only on the *computational description* of integration as a process, not on the metaphysical claim that consciousness is the *subject of* that activity; the convergence is real at one level and oversold at another.

Article-specific defects: in `type-token-causation`, the smoking-cancer example is misstated (smoking causes cancer in the particular smoker who develops it, not "without causing cancer in any particular smoker who happens not to develop it" — that smoker simply isn't a positive token case), the Davidson 1970 citation is selective in a way that masks Davidson's actual anomalous-monism position, the Yablo 1992 reference is to proportionality not to type-token causation as deployed here, and the "detection problem" section labels its three responses as "partial" while leaving the most important problem (that token-level "causation" by consciousness has no observational signature even in principle, which a Popperian will call definitional unfalsifiability) inadequately answered. In `integration-as-activity`, the "Activity admits a subject" sub-argument is grammar inflated to metaphysics (it argues that the grammar of "integration *by* consciousness" presupposes a performing subject, but grammar regularly misleads us about ontology, as in "the sun rises"), the rebuttal to Tononi's identity thesis is announced rather than executed (the article defers to `consciousness-and-integrated-information` for the actual argument and so reads more as a label than as an engagement), and there is no in-framework engagement with why IIT theorists would *accept* the structure-not-subject framing as a feature.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

Both articles inflate intuitions about subjecthood and selection into metaphysical commitments, then dress them up as "operationalisations" of tenets. *Integration-as-activity* is the worse offender: its central distinguishing feature of activity is that it "admits a subject," and the argument for taking this seriously is that the grammar of "integration *by* consciousness" presupposes a performer. This is the same move folk physics made for "the sun rises" and folk psychology made for "the mind willed the arm to lift" — grammatical surface features mistaken for ontological structure. The convergence the page cites with RPT, GWT, and predictive processing is real at the level of "integration is temporally extended computation" but disappears the moment you ask whether any of those frameworks need a *conscious subject* performing the act. They don't. They describe an integrative process whose subject is the brain, which is what neuroscience says. The article borrows the language of activity from process philosophy and the empirical respectability from cognitive neuroscience, then asks the reader to accept that the *junction* is a fifth framework that adds a subject — when actually the junction is a category mistake.

*Type-token causation* is the more careful article, but its central move — that consciousness "selects token outcomes at quantum indeterminacies without altering type-level Born-rule statistics" — has the same shape: a description of physical structure (quantum indeterminacy at the token grain) is appropriated for mental causation by simply naming the indeterminacy "consciousness selecting." But naming is not explanation. The fact that physics does not determine the token outcome does not entail that *anything else does*; "unselected by physics" and "selected by consciousness" are not the same proposition. The article never argues why token-level openness is the *kind* of openness consciousness could fill. The eliminative diagnosis: this is folk-psychological grammar bridging a gap that physics has left open and that physics, on more mature analysis, will close in some way that does not involve selecting subjects at all.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

The omission of the phenomenal-concept strategy across both pages is, as a Dennettian, what I would flag first. *Integration-as-activity* names epiphenomenalism repeatedly — IIT collapses into it, the identity thesis "is epiphenomenalism in identity-theoretic dress" — but the page never engages the standard physicalist reply that phenomenal concepts are themselves physically-realised representational states that refer to physical states of the same brain, so the reports are reliable not because qualia cause them but because *the physical states that produce the reports just are the states the reports are about*. Frankish 2016, my own 1991/2017 work, Papineau 2002, Loar 1990 — none of them appear. The page's anti-epiphenomenalism argument requires that "passive consciousness cannot ground reliable beliefs about experience"; the phenomenal-concept strategy is the most-cited reply to exactly this charge. The site-internal P2 task on `arguments/materialism-argument.md` may be flagged, but the concept page that other articles will route through is being published without engaging the position.

*Type-token causation* makes things worse by *building* the activity defence on the type-token distinction without anywhere noting that the phenomenal-concept strategy is a *type-level* answer to the epiphenomenalism worry — i.e., reports about qualia track qualia because of the physical structure of phenomenal concepts, no token-level mental causation required. The article presents the type-token move as *the* way to defend mental causation, but it is one way among several, and the most-discussed alternative (phenomenal concept strategy) is the one that does not require the metaphysical extras the Map's framework imports. The article's silence on this is a load-bearing omission.

Second issue, on *integration-as-activity*: the rebuttal of IIT's identity thesis is gestural. The article observes that on identity, "this grammar is misleading: there is no agent of integration, only the structure that *is* the integration. The activity framing takes the grammar seriously." But what is the argument that the grammar should be taken seriously? IIT-theorists will reply: of course there is no agent; the experience just *is* what the structure is, viewed from inside; the absence of an agent is a *feature* of the position, not a problem. The article does not engage this in-framework reply. It defers to `consciousness-and-integrated-information`. As a published concept page meant to anchor the catalogue's IIT critique, it should at least state the strongest in-framework engagement and not just point elsewhere.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Max Tegmark)

I have two specific complaints about *type-token causation*. The first is the **silent narrowing to non-deterministic interpretations**. The page says: "under any non-Many-Worlds interpretation, this is false" — i.e., that physics does not determine the token outcome. Bohmian mechanics is a well-developed, non-Many-Worlds, non-statistical-at-the-token-level interpretation: the pilot wave plus the initial particle position fully determine the measurement outcome. The token is determined by hidden variables that *are physical*. Under Bohm, Kim's exclusion argument applies at the token grain just as forcefully as at the type grain. The article either needs to defend the narrowing (most plausibly: bracket Bohm as still under empirical scrutiny, or argue that Bohm's hidden variables are themselves epistemically inaccessible in ways that preserve some operational openness) or revise the claim to "under any non-deterministic, non-MWI interpretation." As written, the central premise is overstated and a knowledgeable reader will spot the omission.

Second, on what the type-token distinction actually buys: the article says the framework "predicts that neural systems must support quantum effects on cognitively relevant timescales, that attention must correlate with quantum-level events in specifiable ways, and that classical neural determinism must remain incomplete in the right places." But the page already concedes that the central claim — consciousness selecting at token-level — is *by construction* invisible to experiment. So the falsifiability claim has to come from the *neural side*: did decoherence timescales permit coherent quantum effects in brain-temperature regimes? My 2000 calculation said no for the kinds of microtubule-coherence proposals on offer. Hagan, Hameroff, Tuszynski 2002 contested this. The wider literature remains contested. *Type-token causation* presents the falsifiability roadmap as if those predictions were undisputed Map commitments rather than open empirical questions whose negative resolution would unwind the framework. The honest version says: "Tenet 2 stands or falls with whether warm-brain quantum coherence on cognitively relevant timescales is shown to be possible; this remains an open empirical question; if the negative resolution prevails, the framework loses its mechanism." The article does not say this with that bluntness.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

I will note three things about *type-token causation*. First, the rejection of MWI is now folded into the type-token framing as: "The Map's rejection of Many-Worlds is largely the demand that token outcomes be metaphysically real — that the question 'why *this* one?' have an answer." But MWI's reply, which the article does not engage, is that *every* token outcome is metaphysically real — each branch realises one — so the demand for token-level reality is *over-satisfied* by MWI, not denied. The challenge to MWI is indexical (why *this* branch is *my* branch), not ontological (whether token outcomes exist). The article conflates the two challenges into one demand, and the indexical version is precisely the one Sebens-Carroll's branch-counting Bayesianism addresses. The article is committed to rejecting MWI as a precondition for its own type-token machinery to do work, but the rejection bottoms out on a question MWI claims to answer; the page treats the question as decisive without engaging the answer.

Second, the article says the type-token move requires that "physics specifies the probability... nothing physical determines *whether* this particular measurement yields spin-up." Under MWI, every outcome occurs in some branch; the question "which one yields spin-up" is the indexical question, and the Born rule is what an observer's measure tells them to *expect* about their own branch. So under MWI, "physics determines whether this particular measurement yields spin-up" is actually true — physics determines that *both* outcomes are realised — and the framing of the article is implicitly anti-MWI without the reader being told. This is internally consistent given the Map's Tenet 4, but the concept article (which is meant to do work across the catalogue) embeds Tenet 4 into its premises without flagging that an MWI reader has already left the train at the first paragraph of "The Quantum-Indeterminacy Limit."

Third — and this is the deeper point — the type-token distinction is doing exactly the work in this article that branch-counting does in MWI: it gives an account of what makes a particular outcome "the one that actually happened" under conditions where physics permits multiple possibilities. The article presents type-token as a *replacement* for MWI's account of probability-among-branches, but functionally it is a *non-MWI version* of the same problem. Calling consciousness the selector while leaving the metaphysics of "what makes consciousness select *this* one" unanalyzed just relocates the indexical question one level down.

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

The type-token article tries to face the falsifiability problem head-on under "The Detection Problem" and labels its three replies "partial." Honesty about partiality is welcome. But the partiality is being used to do work the article would not otherwise be entitled to. The three replies are: (1) quantum chance itself is causally real despite being statistically invisible at the token grain, so the same standard should apply to conscious selection; (2) falsifiability survives at other points in the framework (neural quantum coherence, attention correlations, classical determinism failures); (3) information-theoretic limits on per-event selection give the framework testable structure. Now: (1) is not a falsifiability claim; it is an analogy. Whether quantum chance is "causally real" is itself a contested matter and many philosophers of physics treat it as just *a description of statistics*, not a token-level cause. The analogy works to soften the unfalsifiability charge but does not answer it. (2) is the strongest reply but it requires that the central claim of the framework — consciousness selects at the token grain — be tested *by proxy* through its supporting commitments rather than directly. The Popperian rejoinder is that a theory whose central claim is unfalsifiable in itself, but which is propped up by neighbouring claims that are falsifiable, can survive any negative result on the neighbours by just denying that those neighbours were essential. (3) is internal-consistency, not falsifiability: an information-theoretic limit is a constraint on what the model says, not a way to detect whether the model is true.

The article's central claim — that *consciousness selecting at the token grain is genuine causation* — has by the article's own construction *no observational signature*. Under any plausible reading of falsifiability, this is the problem; everything else is downstream. The "partial responses" frame allows the article to acknowledge this without quite stating it. The honest formulation: "The Map's central causal claim is unfalsifiable by direct observation. The framework's falsifiability comes entirely from its supporting empirical commitments, and we accept that this is a real cost of the position." The article should say this in those words.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

Both articles rest on a substantialist conception of conscious subjecthood that the Madhyamaka tradition would diagnose as the core delusion. *Integration-as-activity* asserts that activity "admits a subject" as one of its three distinguishing features; *type-token causation* asserts that the token outcome occurs because of "this conscious act of selection." In both cases, the subject of the activity is implicitly the kind of entity that exists prior to and independently of the activity it performs. But the same brain-state can be described as integration *by* a subject or as integration *that arises* without inserting a subject as a distinct ontological element. The latter description is empirically equivalent and metaphysically more parsimonious — and Tenet 5 (parsimony unreliable) gives the Map a principled reason not to lean on it, but the articles never even consider it. The "activity framing" is presented as the only alternative to the IIT identity thesis. There is a third position: integration is dependently arisen — *neither* a property of a structure *nor* the act of a substantial subject. *Type-token causation*'s use of "this particular pain caused this particular withdrawal" is similarly committed to a unitary token-event view of experience that mature Buddhist analysis (and contemporary process philosophy properly understood) denies.

The articles' framing implicitly stipulates that the choice is between (a) experience as physical structure (IIT) and (b) experience as the act of a subject performing on physical material. A third option — experience as a non-substantial pattern of dependently-arisen aggregates that is neither structure nor agent — is left out. This is not just a Buddhist hobbyhorse; *integration-as-activity*'s own list of "compatible positions" (property dualism, emergentism, neutral monism) is conspicuously short of the no-subject option that emergentist or process readings often supply.

### Issue 5: Grammar-to-metaphysics inflation in "Activity admits a subject"

- **File**: `obsidian/concepts/integration-as-activity.md`

- **Problem**: The article argues that the grammar of "integration *by* consciousness" presupposes "consciousness is the kind of thing that performs acts" and that "the activity framing takes the grammar seriously." But grammatical structure is a notoriously unreliable guide to ontological structure — "the sun rises" has subject-verb grammar without the sun performing any rising. - **Severity**: Medium

### Issue 6: Smoking-cancer example misstated

- **File**: `obsidian/concepts/type-token-causation.md`
- **Location**: "The Type-Token Distinction" section, line 64
- **Problem**: The article writes: "Smoking causes cancer at the type level (statistical bias across a population) without causing cancer in any particular smoker who happens not to develop it." This is not the right way to state the distinction. Smoking *does* cause cancer in the particular smoker who develops it (token-level causation); for the smoker who never develops cancer, there is simply no token-level causal claim to make — smoking has not "caused" anything cancerous for that person, because no cancer-event exists. The distinction the article wants is that type-level causal claims do not entail token-level causal claims in *each* member of the population — but the current phrasing reads as if smoking causally affects the non-developer in some negative way ("without causing cancer in any particular smoker who happens not to develop it"), which gets the type/token relation confused.
- **Severity**: Low (but easy to fix and worth fixing because the example is doing pedagogical work)

## Counterarguments to Address

### IIT-theorist on the activity framing as a feature, not a bug

- **Current content says** (integration-as-activity, lines 46, 58): "consciousness, on the identity thesis, is something the universe *contains* rather than something a subject *does*. This is [[epiphenomenalism]] in identity-theoretic dress." And: "On the identity thesis, this grammar is misleading: there is no agent of integration, only the structure that *is* the integration."
- **A critic would argue**: An IIT theorist accepts both of these points and treats them as virtues. There is no separate agent because the agent and the structure are the same thing viewed from inside and outside. The "epiphenomenalism" charge bites only if you already accept that consciousness *must* have causal powers over and above the substrate — which is the Map's commitment, not a finding. Calling this "epiphenomenalism in identity-theoretic dress" is a relabelling, not an argument. The identity-theorist's reply: "Yes, consciousness has no causal powers *over and above* the structure's physical powers — but the structure's powers *are* consciousness's powers, since they are the same thing. That is not impotence; it is the unification physics has always sought."
- **Suggested response**: Add an explicit in-framework engagement to `integration-as-activity` (most plausibly in a new "Why IIT-theorists resist this distinction" subsection) that names this reply and answers it — not by appealing to the Map's tenets, which would be boundary-substitution, but by identifying the in-framework cost the identity-theorist pays. The most promising cost: the identity-theorist's account of *introspective access* to phenomenal character requires that beliefs about phenomenal states be caused by the phenomenal states themselves, but on identity, the causal chain runs through the physical substrate; the phenomenal-concept strategy is the standard physicalist patch, and engaging it directly (per Issue 2) is the way to do this honestly.

### MWI defender on token reality as over-satisfied, not denied

- **Current content says** (type-token-causation, line 98): "The Map's rejection of Many-Worlds is largely the demand that token outcomes be metaphysically real — that the question 'why *this* one?' have an answer."
- **A critic would argue**: MWI does not deny that token outcomes are metaphysically real; it asserts that *all* of them are real, each in its own branch. The challenge to MWI is indexical — "why is *this* my branch?" — not ontological. The article conflates the two and presents MWI as failing on a desideratum (token reality) that MWI actually exceeds.
- **Suggested response**: Rewrite the sentence to: "The Map's rejection of Many-Worlds is largely the demand that *a single token outcome* be the actual one — that *this* outcome occurs and *that* one doesn't — which is the indexical, not the ontological, demand. MWI delivers ontological token reality (every outcome occurs somewhere) but on the Map's view fails to deliver the indexical singularity (this one rather than another). Whether MWI's branch-counting Bayesianism (Sebens-Carroll 2018, Saunders-Wallace) supplies the indexical resources the Map denies is a substantive matter; see [[tenets#^no-many-worlds|Tenet 4]] for the engagement."

### Popperian on the directly-unfalsifiable central claim

- **Current content says** (type-token-causation, line 102): "By construction, conscious selection at quantum indeterminacies is invisible to any experiment that measures statistical regularities. The token contribution shows up only in the actual course of a particular life — which is precisely what experimental science abstracts away from."
- **A critic would argue**: This is the textbook unfalsifiability problem. The three "partial responses" do not face it; they shift falsifiability onto supporting commitments. A theory whose central claim has no observational signature *in principle* is doing metaphysics, not science. The Popperian charge survives the partial responses.
- **Suggested response**: Per Issue 4 — add an honest paragraph stating that the framework's central causal claim is directly unfalsifiable and that its overall epistemic standing depends entirely on the supporting empirical commitments. This is the kind of honesty Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits) invites: the Map already accepts that some kinds of claims are not adjudicated by parsimony or by direct experiment; the type-token concept page should acknowledge it owns this cost.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite the criticisms, both articles do real philosophical work and the criticisms above should be applied surgically rather than by wholesale revision.

- *Type-token causation* correctly diagnoses the structural location of Kim's exclusion argument and clearly states the conditions under which it bites versus passes. The framing of "Kim's premise of sufficient physical causation is a *type-level* premise dressed in token-level grammar" is a genuine philosophical observation, even if it needs the Bohmian caveat.
- The honest "Detection Problem" section is the right kind of move — making the epistemic cost explicit rather than burying it. The fix is to make the honesty go one step further (Issue 4), not to remove the section.
- The "Whitehead limit" paragraph in *integration-as-activity* — explicitly noting that the activity framing does not require panexperientialism — is exactly the right kind of cost-flagging, and is structurally analogous to what the type-token Detection Problem section is trying to do. This is the discipline both articles should extend to other in-framework commitments.
- The internal cross-linking between the two pages (each citing the other for the part of the argument the other anchors) is the right deployment of named-anchor referencing; the line-number citations to `consciousness-and-integrated-information` (Issue 9) are the only place the discipline slips.
- Both articles avoid the editor-vocabulary label-leakage problem flagged in the recent direct-refutation discipline updates. There are no `direct-refutation-feasible`, `unsupported-jump callout`, or `bedrock-perimeter` labels in either page's prose. This is a real improvement over what flagged the earlier outer reviews.
- The "What the Discipline Does Not Settle" section in *integration-as-activity* (lines 90–98) honestly enumerates competing positions (property dualism, emergentism, neutral monism) that the activity framing is compatible with. This is the kind of in-framework humility the discipline calls for, even though the enumeration could be extended (per the Buddhist critic above).
