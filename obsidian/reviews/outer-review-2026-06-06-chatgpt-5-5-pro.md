## Outer review: “Mapping Mind-Space”

### Executive verdict

“Mapping Mind-Space” is a strong conceptual node in the voids cluster, but it currently works better as an evocative dualist meditation than as a rigorous public-facing argument. Its core insight is valuable: any attempt to map possible minds begins from inside one particular kind of mind, and there may be forms of experience for which humans lack the relevant concepts. The article states that the “geometry of mind-space is inaccessible from within” and classifies this as primarily “unexplorable,” while allowing partial progress through comparative neuroscience, altered states, and AI comparison. [[mapping-mind-space]]

The main weakness is a repeated slide from **epistemic limitation** to **metaphysical insulation**. The article is often careful with “may” and “under dualism,” but the framing still protects the Map’s dualist tenets by treating physicalist/reductive mapping as in-principle surveyable, while treating dualist mind-space as ontologically larger and inaccessible to physical methods. That is too neat. Physicalism can allow severe in-principle or practical mapping limits, and dualism does not automatically establish that mind-space has hidden non-physical dimensions. The claim “Dualism makes the void real” is the article’s clearest overstatement. [[mapping-mind-space]]

### Central claims audit

The article’s best claim is the modest one: **there is no neutral first-person vantage point from which to experience all possible minds**. Nagel’s bat argument and McGinn’s cognitive-closure framework support limits on phenomenal acquaintance, and Shanahan’s “space of possible minds” provides a legitimate framing for thinking beyond human minds. [[mapping-mind-space]]

The article’s weaker claim is stronger: **the space cannot be surveyed because every survey occurs from within a particular consciousness**. This conflates at least three things: direct phenomenal acquaintance, structural/functional characterization, and ontological mapping. We may be unable to know what bat echolocation feels like from the inside while still mapping its sensory, behavioral, neural, and ecological structure. A “telescope” for mind-space need not deliver first-person possession of every experience; it might deliver triangulated structural knowledge.

The “Flatland” section is rhetorically effective but too strong when it says that if experience has dimensions beyond introspection, we “cannot know those dimensions exist.” Hidden dimensions can be inferred indirectly from psychophysics, neural decoding, comparative ethology, training effects, cross-species behavior, altered-state reports, and failures of existing models. The article should say that we may lack **acquaintance** with those dimensions, not that we cannot know they exist. [[mapping-mind-space]]

The “Convergent Arguments for Missing Dimensions” section is the most vulnerable. It treats neural high-dimensionality, Lacalli’s E-space, Arsiwalla and Verschure’s morphospace, and Carr’s higher-dimensional physics as if they are independent lines converging on one conclusion. But they are different kinds of “dimension”: neural variables, phenomenological quality-space dimensions, information-theoretic or functional axes, and speculative physical/metaphysical dimensions. That is not convergence unless the article supplies a bridge argument between those kinds of dimensionality. [[mapping-mind-space]]

The “What Would Challenge This View” section is useful, but its closing sentence—“Current evidence meets none of these”—is overbroad. Current evidence does not dissolve the deepest “what-it-is-like” gap, but it does meet weaker challenge conditions: multidimensional frameworks of consciousness exist, neurofunctional dimensions of consciousness have been proposed and empirically studied, quality-space models connect subjective similarity spaces to neural activation patterns, and AI-consciousness work now uses indicator-based frameworks rather than waiting for AI to “resolve” the dimensional question. [[mapping-mind-space]]

### Citation audit

The citations are mostly real after the May 29 repair, but their evidential roles are uneven.

Shanahan is appropriate for the phrase “space of possible minds,” but it does not support the article’s dualist conclusion. Shanahan’s project is an AI/cognitive-science framing of possible minds; it is not evidence that mind-space is a non-physical ontological domain. [[mapping-mind-space]]

Nagel and McGinn are apt for phenomenal-access and cognitive-closure limits. They support the article’s humility about first-person understanding; they do not establish that physical methods “alone cannot survey” mind-space. [[mapping-mind-space]]

Jolly & Chang are cited correctly, and the article’s description of “low-dimensional models driven by tractability rather than evidence” is close to the source. But the article extends the point from scientific modeling bias to introspective flattening. That may be a suggestive analogy, not a supported conclusion. The source’s own remedy is quantitative/computational modeling and better technical training, which points toward better structural mapping rather than toward permanent unsurveyability. [PubMed+1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30576066/)

Lacalli is now correctly attributed, and the E-space quotation is real. But Lacalli’s “experience-space” is a hypothetical construct in a naturalistic/evolutionary account of qualia, not a dualist datum. It supports “phenomenal dimensionality might be richer than everyday categories,” not “consciousness occupies a non-physical domain.” [GitHub+1](https://github.com/unfinishablemap/unfinishablemap/commit/cfe144a084343a15bf32928a66372115cb403575)

Arsiwalla & Verschure are cited correctly, but their morphospace is explicitly based on autonomic, computational, and social complexity, and is meant to compare biological agents and AI systems. The article’s line that this is “almost certainly incomplete” under dualism is not a result of the cited framework; it is a tenet-conditional overlay. [MDPI+1](https://www.mdpi.com/2673-4087/4/2/9)

Carr and Sjöstedt-Hughes are legitimate speculative sources, but they should not sit in the same evidential register as neuroscience or cognitive-science sources. Carr’s paper proposes that consciousness may involve mental time or extra dimensions; Sjöstedt-Hughes is a metaphysical exploration of n-dimensional space and sentience. These are hypothesis-generating references, not convergent empirical support. [PhilArchive+1](https://philarchive.org/rec/CARMSA-10)

James and Schwitzgebel function rhetorically rather than evidentially. They are useful for mood and philosophical caution, but the article should not let evocative quotations do argumentative work that belongs to empirical or analytical sources. [[mapping-mind-space]]

### Counterargument coverage

The article acknowledges possible challenges, but it does not engage the strongest live objections in the body. The internal review explicitly records that eliminativist, functionalist, morphospace, MWI branch-relative, and Buddhist no-self disagreements were treated as “bedrock” and “not re-flagged,” with no named-opponent refutation engagements. That is a methodological red flag for an outer-facing article: the objections most likely to weaken the article’s tenet-protective moves are moved out of frame. [GitHub](https://github.com/unfinishablemap/unfinishablemap/commit/cfe144a084343a15bf32928a66372115cb403575)

The most important missing objection is **structural mapping without phenomenal possession**. Functionalists, representationalists, heterophenomenologists, and many physicalist consciousness scientists can grant that we cannot become bats while denying that this prevents disciplined mapping of the relevant state-space. Fleming and Shea’s 2024 quality-space work is an example: subjective similarity spaces may be explained in terms of neural activation similarities, and localist, workspace, and higher-order theories can all accommodate quality-space structure. [Nicholas Shea+1](https://www.nicholasshea.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/Fleming_Shea_24_Qual_spc_cmptns_consc_TICS_OA.pdf)

A second missing objection is **physicalist ineffability**. Ji and colleagues explicitly target richness and ineffability—the very phenomena that often motivate explanatory-gap and “void” arguments—and model them in information-theoretic and dynamical-systems terms. Their framework does not settle the hard problem, but it is exactly the kind of contemporary work the article must address before saying current evidence meets none of its challenge conditions. [OUP Academic+1](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article/2024/1/niae001/7623923)

A third missing objection is **empirical multidimensional consciousness science**. Seth and Bayne’s review shows that current consciousness science is already structured around competing theories—higher-order, global workspace, recurrent/re-entry and predictive-processing, and integrated information theory—whose empirical relations remain contested. The article largely bypasses that landscape. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41583-022-00587-4)

A fourth missing objection is **animal and AI triangulation as partial survey**. The New York Declaration states that there is strong scientific support for consciousness in mammals and birds and a realistic possibility in all vertebrates and many invertebrates; Butlin et al. propose an empirically grounded AI-consciousness framework based on computational indicators from consciousness science. Neither gives us full phenomenal acquaintance, but both weaken the idea that all mind-space mapping is stuck at a single point. [Google Sites+1](https://sites.google.com/nyu.edu/nydeclaration/declaration)

### Integration with surrounding articles

The article is well-linked, but not yet well-integrated. It links to the void taxonomy, the vertiginous question, synesthetic void, biological cognitive closure, non-human minds, and filter theory. The problem is that it imports the supporting architecture more readily than it imports the caveats.

“Three Kinds of Void” already states the materialist counterargument that apparent cognitive closure can dissolve with scientific progress and calls it serious. “Mapping Mind-Space” should import that objection directly rather than leaving it one click away. [[three-kinds-of-void]]

“The Synesthetic Void” is more methodologically careful than “Mapping Mind-Space” in two ways: it explicitly flags the Dennett-style heterophenomenological objection, and it admits that the brain-only physicalist and filter-theory readings of developmental narrowing are observationally equivalent on the cited evidence. “Mapping Mind-Space” should adopt the same symmetry discipline. [[synesthetic-void]]

“Non-Human Minds as Void-Explorers” contains exactly the AI caveats the mapping article needs: current AI is trained on human text, may inherit human blind spots, is shaped by RLHF, is constrained by human-interpretable output, and may face grounding problems. The mapping article’s “two give a bearing” line is good, but it is underqualified without these caveats. [[non-human-minds-as-void-explorers]]

“Filter Theory” is more cautious than the mapping article’s relation-to-tenets section. It says filter theory and materialism both explain basic brain-consciousness correlations, that filter theory lacks independent criteria for predicting enhancement versus degradation, and that psychedelic evidence is consistent with the filter reading but does not discriminate against rivals. The mapping article should not present filter-compatible dimensionality as stronger than the filter article itself allows. [[filter-theory]]

### Does the “mapping mind-space” framing hold up?

Yes, if reframed as a **partial, structural, plural-method research program**. Current cognitive science and philosophy increasingly use dimensional language: multidimensional models of consciousness are applied to altered states, disorders of consciousness, and animal consciousness; cortical-gradient work proposes neurofunctional dimensions of consciousness; quality-space work tries to connect phenomenal similarity with neural similarity. [OUP Academic+2Nature+2](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article/2024/1/niae026/7695488)

No, if the framing is used to imply that dualism alone makes mind-space real, that reductionism would make mind-space surveyable by brain-configuration mapping, or that physical methods cannot survey it. Contemporary work supports a more nuanced conclusion: there may be no complete first-person atlas of all possible minds, but there are many partial instruments—comparative neuroethology, psychophysics, neurophenomenology, multidimensional report spaces, cortical geometry, animal-consciousness frameworks, and AI indicator methods. The article’s “no telescope exists” should become “no Archimedean telescope exists.”

## Concrete potential improvements

### Specific article improvements

1. **“Mapping Mind-Space” — add a distinction section: “Acquaintance, structure, and ontology.”**  

Separate three questions: Can we know what another mind feels like from inside? Can we structurally map its capacities and state-space? Does the state-space require non-physical ontology? The article currently lets the first question do too much work for the third.
2. **“Mapping Mind-Space” — rewrite “Dualism makes the void real.”**  

Replace it with: “Dualism makes one strong version of the void ontologically natural, but epistemic mind-space limits also arise under physicalism, functionalism, and representationalism.” This would stop the article from presenting reductionism as automatically surveyable and dualism as automatically vindicated.
3. **“Mapping Mind-Space” — downgrade “Convergent Arguments” to “Analogies and partial mapping frameworks.”**  

Use an evidence-grade table: neural high-dimensionality, quality/experience-space, morphospace, and higher-dimensional metaphysics should be listed as different kinds of dimensional claim, not as independent convergence on one thesis.
4. **“Mapping Mind-Space” — revise “no telescope exists.”**  

Use “no Archimedean telescope exists.” Then list partial telescopes: comparative neuroscience, psychophysics, animal-consciousness indicators, cortical-gradient work, quality-space modeling, altered-state phenomenometrics, and AI indicator frameworks.
5. **“Mapping Mind-Space” — add named counterarguments.**  

Include compact engagements with functionalism, representationalism, heterophenomenology, illusionism, physicalist information-theoretic ineffability, quality-space computationalism, and multidimensional neuroscience.
6. **“Mapping Mind-Space” — replace “Current evidence meets none of these.”**  

Use a graded ledger: “No evidence dissolves the strongest acquaintance gap; some evidence supports partial structural mapping; some evidence weakens the claim that dimensional structure is wholly inaccessible.”
7. **“Mapping Mind-Space” — update the AI section.**  

Import the caveats from “Non-Human Minds as Void-Explorers”: human-text inheritance, RLHF shaping, output constraints, and grounding. Add Butlin et al.’s indicator-based AI-consciousness framework as the current reference point.
8. **“The Synesthetic Void” — add a reciprocal bridge back to “Mapping Mind-Space.”**  

Use synesthesia as the local case study for the broader mapping problem, but preserve its existing caveats: heterophenomenology, representational-level dependence, and observational equivalence between filter and brain-only interpretations.
9. **“Filter Theory” — link directly to the psychedelic sentence in “Mapping Mind-Space.”**  

The mapping article’s “glimpses or confabulations” language should point to filter theory’s stronger underdetermination warning: altered-state evidence is compatible with the filter reading but not discriminating.
10. **“Three Kinds of Void” / “Biological Cognitive Closure” — export the materialist-progress objection.**  

The mapping article should not rely on cognitive closure without repeating the serious materialist reply: many once-unthinkable problems became tractable after conceptual and instrumental advances.
11. **Research notes — fix stale citation metadata.**  

The May 29 review flags a surviving stale “Arsiwalla & Verschure, *Minds and Machines*, 2024” form in a research note. Correct it or quarantine it so it does not re-enter live articles.

### Site-methodology improvements

1. **Require source-role labels for every citation.**  

Mark each citation as empirical support, formal model, philosophical analogy, speculative metaphysics, historical quotation, or tenet-conditional inference.
2. **Ban unbridged “convergence” across different dimension types.**  

Neural dimensions, phenomenal dimensions, computational dimensions, social-complexity axes, and physical extra dimensions should not be treated as convergent unless a bridge argument is supplied.
3. **Apply a “tenet-protection” check.**  

For every Relation-to-Site-Perspective section, ask: “Would this sentence still be acceptable if the reader rejected the Map’s dualism?” If not, label it explicitly as conditional rather than evidential.
4. **Stop treating bedrock objections as already handled.**  

Even if a disagreement is archived elsewhere, outer-facing articles should include the strongest one-sentence version of the objection and a link to the fuller treatment.
5. **Add a symmetry test for altered-state and neuroscience evidence.**  

If a datum supports both filter theory and production/functional theories, the article should say so at the point of use, not only in a linked concept page.
6. **Use publisher-of-record verification for all load-bearing citations.**  

The Lacalli defects show that intra-corpus convergence can preserve the wrong author or even a fabricated citation. Publisher verification should be mandatory for all quote-heavy and convergence-heavy nodes.
7. **Create a citation-defect propagation sweep.**  

When a citation defect is found in one article, automatically search sibling articles, research notes, archive copies, and generated pages for the same title, author vector, DOI, and common wrong variants.
8. **Improve changelog discoverability.**  

The rendered changelog did not surface “Mapping Mind-Space” by title or slug search even though the GitHub commit contains the May 29 changelog entry. Article pages should expose their latest review entry directly, or the changelog should support article-title indexing.
9. **Maintain a current-literature queue for consciousness science.**  

Add recurring checks for multidimensional consciousness, quality-space computation, animal-consciousness frameworks, AI-consciousness indicators, no-report paradigms, altered-state psychometrics, and information-theoretic accounts of ineffability.
10. **For crowded clusters, require an integration table.**  

The voids cluster is already over its stated cap, and link density alone is not integration. Each major void article should state what it adds, what nearby article it depends on, and which caveats it inherits.
