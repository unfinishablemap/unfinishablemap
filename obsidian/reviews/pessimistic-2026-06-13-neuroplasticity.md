## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
"You concede the materialist 'just neurons causing neurons' story is *viable* throughout, then in your last paragraph reverse field and announce the simpler story 'omits the most important datum' — the felt effort. But 'felt effort' is precisely the folk-psychological posit I expect to be eliminated. You are treating the explanandum (phenomenology of effort) as a settled datum that any adequate theory must preserve. That begs the question against eliminativism: I deny there is a determinate 'felt effort' over and above the neural effort-allocation signal that introspection misreports." The article never names the unmet standard eliminativism must meet (specify the reduction of effort-talk), nor does it defend the assumption that effort-phenomenology is a real datum rather than a folk artifact. *Medium — missed unsupported-move identification, and an undefended premise (see Issue 3).*

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
"Your strongest move is the identity-theory paragraph (§The Epiphenomenalist Response), and you handle it fairly — you grant that on identity theory 'deciding to redirect attention' just *is* the neural change, so neuroplasticity poses no problem, and you reply by re-raising the hard problem. Fine. But then you smuggle the hard problem back as if it were a *cost* identity theory pays in *this* debate. It isn't: whether the hard problem is real is a separate fight. In the neuroplasticity argument specifically, the physicalist has conceded nothing. Your article half-admits this ('purchases causal relevance by abandoning explanation') but the framing implies the abandonment is a defeat *here*, when it's just a pointer to a different argument."

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
The article is admirably restrained: it calls the Stapp quantum-Zeno proposal "highly speculative," states "most physicists remain sceptical that quantum coherence survives long enough in warm biological tissue," and explicitly says the Map "doesn't commit to this specific mechanism." This is the calibration I would demand. One residual: the Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet section asserts the Zeno mechanism "doesn't inject energy or violate conservation laws — it shapes which possibilities actualise," stated declaratively without hedge, inside an article that elsewhere flags the whole mechanism as unconfirmed in neural tissue. The conservation-law claim is a *theoretical property of the model*, not an established fact about brains; the surrounding hedges should reach it.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
The "No Many Worlds" section is honest about its dependence: it says the Zeno mechanism "requires genuine collapse" and that under MWI "there is nothing for consciousness to select." That is a clean conditional, not an over-claim. My objection is dialectical, not a flaw in the article: you've made the neuroplasticity case *hostage* to collapse-realism. If MWI is true, your entire interactionist reading of Schwartz evaporates, and the article concedes as much. Worth the reader knowing the load this section silently carries.

### The Empiricist (Popper's ghost)
The article's best feature for me is §"A fair test" / the closing falsifiability gambit: "if purely mechanical interventions could replicate all the effects of cognitive therapy... the interactionist interpretation would be weakened." That is a genuine (if hard-to-operationalise) discriminating condition. But note its weakness, which the article does not flag: "replicate *all* the effects including the specific, intentional redirection of thought patterns" builds the disputed mental causation *into the test condition*, so the test may be unfalsifiable in practice — a materialist will say any replication that produces the behaviour *has* replicated the redirection. The discriminating prediction is softer than it looks.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nāgārjuna)
"Self-directed neuroplasticity" presupposes a self that directs — a stable agent standing behind attention, choosing. The article leans on "willed" vs "instructed" attention and on consciousness "selecting" patterns, language that reifies an executive self. The contemplative-training section is the natural place to note that the very practices it cites (especially cessation / non-dual traditions) are designed to *dissolve* the directing self, which sits in tension with framing meditation as evidence for a self-directing consciousness. The article doesn't have to resolve this, but the unexamined "director" is a soft spot.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Closing-paragraph calibration drift
- **File**: `concepts/neuroplasticity.md`
- **Location**: §"What Neuroplasticity Shows (and Doesn't)", final paragraph (line 140): "This is harder to square with epiphenomenalism than with interactionist dualism—the correlation is exactly what interactionism predicts and what epiphenomenalism must explain away."
- **Problem**: For the entire article the disciplined claim is *compatible-with-but-does-not-require*. The closing sentence quietly upgrades to a comparative verdict ("harder to square with epiphenomenalism") and frames epiphenomenalism as on the back foot ("must explain away"). But by the article's own §"The Epiphenomenalist Response", the epiphenomenalist account is *symmetric*: neural-intention → neural-attention → neural-reorganisation predicts the *same* correlation. The correlation is "exactly what interactionism predicts" — and also exactly what epiphenomenalism predicts. The asymmetry the sentence asserts is not earned by the correlation alone; it rests on the separate phenomenology-tracking argument (§line 99), which is a *different* and contestable consideration.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: `refine-draft` to align the closing with the article's own discipline. Either (a) attribute the asymmetry to its actual source — "the correlation alone is symmetric between the two accounts; what tilts the Map toward interactionism is the further consideration that effort-phenomenology tracks genuine difficulty (see Mental Effort)" — or (b) soften "must explain away" to "must treat as systematically misleading phenomenology, a cost it can bear but does not escape."

### Issue 2: Possible internal numeric inconsistency on the OCD sample
- **File**: `concepts/neuroplasticity.md`
- **Location**: §"Neural Changes" (line 83): "The original PET study included only 18 participants (9 new)".
- **Problem**: The "(9 new)" parenthetical is unexplained and reads oddly for a single foundational study. The canonical Schwartz et al. 1996 (Arch Gen Psychiatry 53:109-113) is usually reported as n≈9 (or up to 18 across the OCD/control or pre/post design). The "18 participants (9 new)" phrasing suggests a half-remembered conflation of two figures whose relationship the article never states. External search confirmed the citation's venue/volume/pages but did not surface the exact N from the abstract.
- **Severity**: Low-Medium (factual precision; the *direction* of the point — small sample — is correct and well-made)
- **Recommendation**: `refine-draft` — verify N against the Schwartz 1996 paper of record (PubMed 8629886 / the PDF) and state it cleanly. If "9 new" means 9 patients added to 9 prior, say so; if it's a conflation, replace with the single correct figure. Cross-check against the sibling article `clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation.md`, which this article defers to, so the two agree.

### Issue 3: Undefended evolutionary-teleology premise used to rebut epiphenomenalism
- **File**: `concepts/neuroplasticity.md`
- **Location**: §"The Epiphenomenalist Response", line 99: "why does it track genuine difficulty? Why would evolution produce accurate phenomenology of cognitive load if that phenomenology contributes nothing?"
- **Problem**: This rhetorical question is doing real argumentative work (it's the consideration that licenses the comparative verdict in Issue 1), but it rests on an unstated and contestable premise: that evolution would not produce a causally-inert phenomenology that nonetheless covaries with a real causal variable. The epiphenomenalist's standard reply is exactly available here — the phenomenology need not be *selected for*; it can be a non-adaptive concomitant of the neural effort-signal that *is* selected for, in which case its "accuracy" is free covariance, not evidence of causal contribution. The article presents the evolutionary rejoinder as if it lands, without acknowledging this well-known counter.
- **Severity**: Medium (an unsupported claim load-bearing for the article's central tilt)
- **Recommendation**: `refine-draft` — either supply the bridging argument (why the concomitant-of-a-real-signal reply fails or is costly) or downgrade the evolutionary question to "the epiphenomenalist owes an account of the tracking; one is available (free covariance with the selected neural signal) but it concedes that introspection's apparent agency is systematically misleading." Reference `mental-effort.md`, which the article already cites for this line, and check that article makes the argument it's relied on to make rather than gesturing back.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The correlation is symmetric between the two accounts
- **Current content says** (line 140): the mind→matter correlation is "exactly what interactionism predicts and what epiphenomenalism must explain away."
- **A critic would argue**: the epiphenomenalist account the article *itself* states (line 93, neural-intention → neural-attention → neural-reorganisation) predicts the identical correlation. The correlation alone discriminates nothing; only the phenomenology-tracking argument (Issue 3) does any discriminating, and that argument is contestable.
- **Suggested response**: relocate the article's tilt onto its actual support (the tracking argument, openly flagged as contestable) rather than onto the correlation, which is neutral. This is the single change that would most improve the article's calibration.

### "Self-directed" presupposes a self (Nāgārjuna)
- **Current content says**: consciousness "selects," "willed" attention differs from "instructed," patients "redirect attention."
- **A critic would argue**: this reifies an executive self; the contemplative traditions the article cites as evidence are aimed at dissolving exactly that self.
- **Suggested response**: a one-sentence acknowledgement in the meditation section that the "director" is itself a contested posit, with a pointer to the relevant self/no-self article, would close the gap without derailing the piece.
