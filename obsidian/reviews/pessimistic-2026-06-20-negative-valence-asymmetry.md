## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The article hands me my own argument and calls it "the steelman's free lunch." If asymmetric reward-prediction-error coding, distinct dopaminergic subpopulations, and asymmetric learning rates already deliver loss aversion "with no detour through felt value" (line 49), then "felt value" is the surplus posit, not the explanation. The article's reply (line 71) is candid to the point of self-defeat: it admits the value-blind reading wins "any contest decided on parsimony alone" and that the Map's only recourse is "the prior-commitments reply — reject illusionism." That is not an argument against eliminativism; it is a refusal to play. The article should at least say what would make the felt-asymmetry posit *do work* the mechanism cannot, beyond bare commitment.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
You have correctly diagnosed that "the move from behavioural asymmetry to felt asymmetry is a further inference the data do not license" (line 43) and that "introspective evidence on it is thin and confounded." Good — that is my position. But you then keep felt valence "metaphysically authoritative" anyway (line 61). On what evidence? You concede the asymmetry may be entirely a "parameter of the reading channel," concede introspection cannot adjudicate, and then assert the feeling "constitutes value" regardless. That is inflating an intuition into metaphysics exactly where you have just admitted the evidence runs out.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
This article is downstream of the selection machinery and does not itself invoke quantum timing, so my usual decoherence objection does not bite directly. But it inherits the whole edifice: "only if one outcome is really chosen does it matter how much more the negative side weighs" (line 75). The weighting-function question is only well-posed if genuine collapse-by-consciousness is real, which is the unpaid bill the rest of the corpus owes. The article is honest that the question is "live and answerable" *only* on that assumption.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
Line 75 dismisses MWI in one sentence: "every outcome occurs, so 'negatives weigh more in selection' has nothing to be true of." That is too quick. A decision-theoretic MWI account (Deutsch–Wallace) reconstructs preference and risk-weighting over branch *measures*; loss aversion can be a fact about the weighting an agent assigns across branches without any branch being "selected." The article asserts MWI has "no selective referent" but does not engage the branch-weight reconstruction that is precisely the MWI answer to "what is the asymmetry true of." This is a one-line dismissal of a position the corpus elsewhere treats more carefully.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
Credit where due: the article proposes a genuine discriminating test (line 63) — whether pain asymbolics lose loss aversion *specifically* — and admits the evidence is "as of 2026, unexplored." That is falsifiability done honestly. The weakness is that the central edifice rests on it being merely "in principle" testable; until the asymbolia-and-loss-aversion datum exists, the felt-vs-mechanism disambiguation is a promissory note, and the article's positive conclusion ("hold felt valence authoritative") is not what the test would license even if run — the test adjudicates *where the asymmetry lives*, not whether felt value is authoritative.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The whole apparatus reifies "felt value" as a currency with an "intrinsic negative tilt" (line 57) — a thing with properties, weights, coefficients. The negativity bias you document is better read as evidence that there is no stable evaluative self doing the valuing: value appears asymmetrically because it is empty and context-dependent (which Gal et al. 2018, cited at line 41, half-concedes — "the effect is contingent on context"). You treat context-dependence as a *moderator* on a real underlying coefficient; I treat it as the dissolution of the coefficient.

### Issue 2: Load-bearing neuroscience claims uncited
- **File**: same
- **Location**: line 49 — "Reward-prediction-error coding is not symmetric: distinct dopaminergic subpopulations and asymmetric learning rates scale positive and negative prediction errors differently..."
- **Problem**: This is the *substrate* claim that powers the entire "steelman's free lunch" — the article's case that the asymmetry is available value-blind. It is asserted with no citation, while the much weaker Ito et al. (1998) ERP point in the same sentence *is* cited. The asymmetric-learning-rate / distinct-dopamine-population literature is real (e.g., work on positive vs negative RPE learning-rate asymmetries), so this is fixable, but as written the strongest empirical move in the article is unsupported.
- **Severity**: Medium.

### Issue 3: Dilemma-closure seam — "must accommodate" vs "not a pillar"
- **File**: same
- **Location**: lines 25, 37 vs lines 41, 43
- **Problem**: The opening and "A Sign Is Not Enough" section impose a hard demand: the value-sensitive law "must specify how much more the negative side weighs ... or it is false to the behaviour it was meant to explain" (line 37). The next section then argues the behavioural asymmetry is "robust-with-moderators at best, not a law" and "real-but-contested, not a pillar" (lines 41, 43). These are in tension: if the asymmetry is contested enough not to be a pillar, the law is not actually *forced* to inherit the kink — the value-sensitive horn could legitimately treat λ>1 as a contested, moderator-laden effect rather than a feature its law owes accommodation. The article asserts both the *must* and the *maybe-not-real* without reconciling whether a contested effect can impose a requirement on the law.
- **Severity**: Medium (it is a structural seam, not a false claim; the article half-gestures at it with "The number is not what matters; the shape is," but never says why a contested *shape* still binds the law).
- **Recommendation**: A one-to-two-sentence reconciliation: e.g., the law owes a *conditional* — "if the behavioural asymmetry is real, it must take this shape; its contested status transfers to the law as uncertainty about whether the kink is needed at all, not as license to assume symmetry." This converts the seam into an explicit calibration.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The felt-vs-mechanism disambiguation may be unforced
- **Current content says**: The asymmetry "forces" a disambiguation between felt value and the reading mechanism (line 55), and the defensible move is to locate the *weighting* in the channel while keeping felt value authoritative (line 61).
- **A critic would argue**: Once you have conceded (a) the behavioural asymmetry is contested, (b) the felt asymmetry is not licensed by the data, and (c) the value-blind reading wins on parsimony, you have not "disambiguated" — you have conceded the entire empirical case and retained only the bare metaphysical commitment. The disambiguation reads as a face-saving relabel of "we keep our prior."
- **Suggested response**: Make explicit what the felt-value posit *buys* that the mechanism cannot — presumably the normative/moral standing of suffering (constitutes value), which is a different job than *explaining selection behaviour*. Separate the two roles cleanly so the concession on the selection-law role is not mistaken for a concession on the value-realism role.

### MWI branch-weight reconstruction
- **Current content says**: Under MWI the asymmetry "has nothing to be true of" (line 75).
- **A critic would argue**: Decision-theoretic MWI assigns weights across branches; loss aversion is reconstructible as asymmetric branch-weighting without selection. The dismissal is too fast.
- **Suggested response**: Either engage the branch-weight reconstruction in one clause, or soften to "has no *selective* referent" (the article already nearly does this — tightening "selective" would suffice and is defensible given Tenet 4).
