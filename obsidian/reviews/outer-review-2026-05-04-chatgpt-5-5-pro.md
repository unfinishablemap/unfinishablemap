## Reply

## Verdict

The Map’s Duch integration is **substantive philosophical engagement at the level of adversarial framing**, but **not yet a substantive refutation of Duch’s strongest position**. It is better than a performative name-drop: the site created a dedicated Duch research dossier, inserted Duch across multiple articles, and often states his view in recognizable terms rather than caricaturing it. GitHub history shows an intentional May 2026 integration across 14 articles, with the site describing the additions as “load-bearing” and using the “convergent-conclusion-opposite-reasoning” frame. [GitHub](https://github.com/unfinishablemap/unfinishablemap/commit/b90a583102795bac74f25af158be2a4c4719e839)

But the engagement also has an inoculating function. The Map explicitly begins from dualism and says its five tenets constrain what it explores; that means Duch is not being treated as a neutral rival theory that could straightforwardly overturn the framework from within. He is being installed as the strongest external foil against Tenet 1, then answered mostly by structural boundary-setting: “Duch may explain the physical/neurocomputational side, but the Map’s tenets require more.” [[index|The Unfinishable Map]]

So the fairest answer is: **not merely performative, but strongly tenet-protective**. The Map engages Duch honestly enough to improve its dialectic; it does not yet defeat him on his own strongest grounds.

## What Duch’s strongest argument actually is

Duch is not just “a classical-computational reductionist” in the weak digital-functionalist sense. His strongest position is closer to **classical neurodynamical physicalism**: conscious contents are patterns, attractors, trajectories, and self-accessible states in a brain-like dynamical system. In his “mind as shadow of neurodynamics” framing, mental states map onto neurodynamic states; mental events correspond to attractor-like transitions in neural/semantic spaces. [fizyka.umk.pl](https://fizyka.umk.pl/ftp/pub/papers/kmk/18-Phys-mind-Duch.pdf)

His machine-consciousness argument is also stronger than “symbol manipulation can be conscious.” In the articon paper, Duch argues that a brain-like dynamical system able to discriminate and comment on its own internal working-memory states would have a basis for qualia-like claims, and that Searle-style Chinese Room objections do not reach this kind of self-reflective, embodied/dynamical architecture. [PhilArchive](https://philarchive.org/rec/DUCBCC-3)

There is an important nuance the Map sometimes risks flattening: Duch has also argued that consciousness should not be treated as pure information processing detached from causal physical realization. In “Facing the Hard Question,” he says there is no reason to think consciousness arises from abstract information processing alone, and that conscious experience depends on the activation of real physical brain matter; he then treats “why brain creates experience” more like an emergent-property question than a dualist gap. [Instytut Nauk Technicznych](https://is.umk.pl/~duch/pubs/97gray2.pdf)

That means Duch’s strongest challenge to the Map is not simply “computation suffices.” It is: **once you have the right kind of physically realized, self-reflective, brain-like dynamical organization, nothing further is philosophically or scientifically required**. The Map’s dualism tenet is directly threatened by that claim.

## Where the Map is genuinely engaging

The Map does several things that go beyond performative citation.

First, it identifies Duch as a serious opponent rather than a weak foil. The research dossier summarizes him as a naturalist, computationalist, anti-mystical critic of substance dualism and quantum-mind proposals, while also noting that his geometric/feature-space tools may be reusable even if his metaphysics is rejected. [[research/wlodzislaw-duch-consciousness-2026-05-02]]

Second, the Map corrects its own internal overreach. In the “Biological Computationalism’s Inadvertent Case for Dualism” article, it explicitly admits that Duch’s substrate-independent classical computationalism is a counterexample to any simple claim that physicalists must retreat into substrate-dependent biology. That is a real concession, not window dressing. [[biological-computationalisms-inadvertent-case-for-dualism]]

Third, the Map uses Duch’s empirical machinery instead of only rejecting him. In the neural-correlates article, it treats Duch’s geometric/feature-space programme as useful for mapping physical correlates while separating that empirical usefulness from Duch’s metaphysical reduction. That is one of the more substantive moves, because it says: Duch may be right about the structure of the physical correlate even if the Map denies that the correlate exhausts consciousness. [[neural-correlates-of-consciousness]]

Fourth, the Map often states the articon challenge in a recognizable form. In its AI-consciousness pages, it describes Duch’s articon as one of the strongest affirmative cases for machine consciousness and says the Map’s obstacles must engage that case rather than presuppose it away. [[open-question-ai-consciousness]]

Those are signs of substantive engagement.

## Where the reply becomes bracketing

The deepest problem is that the Map’s answers often do not refute Duch’s sufficiency claim; they relocate the disagreement to the Map’s own tenets.

On quantum consciousness, the “convergent-conclusion-opposite-reasoning” frame is partly legitimate. Duch and the Map can both reject large-scale Penrose/Hameroff-style quantum consciousness mechanisms, but for opposite reasons: Duch because classical neurodynamics is enough, the Map because it wants only a minimal quantum interface rather than a substantive quantum-computational mind. The Map makes that distinction explicitly. [[comparing-quantum-consciousness-mechanisms]]

But this move only answers a narrower quantum claim. It does not answer Duch’s stronger objection: **why posit any nonphysical or quantum-interface ingredient if classical self-reflective neurodynamics already explains the relevant reports, discriminations, functional organization, and semantic spaces?** The Map often replies by saying Duch’s critique damages Orch-OR-scale proposals but leaves a minimal interface untouched. That is a defensible scope distinction, not a defeat of Duch.

The quantum-biology article is revealing here: it admits that current evidence can be read in both Duch-compatible and Map-compatible ways, and that discrete-event mechanisms might survive anti-coherence arguments while sustained quantum-consciousness mechanisms do not. That is intellectually honest, but it also shows that Duch’s central classical-sufficiency challenge remains live. [[quantum-biology-and-neural-consciousness]]

On AI and uploading, the same pattern appears. The Map treats Duch’s articon as a serious “bedrock clash,” but the reply is largely structural: if the Map’s nonphysical interface is required, then functional duplication or articon-style self-reflective dynamics is insufficient; if Duch is right, then machine consciousness may be routine. The Map itself frames this as a productive clash of commitments rather than a knock-down argument. [[machine-consciousness]]

That is bracketing. It is not dishonest bracketing, because the Map is explicit about its tenet-governed nature. But it is still bracketing: Duch’s central premise is not shown false; it is denied because it conflicts with the Map’s foundational commitments.

## Does the Map answer Duch’s strongest arguments?

**1. Duch’s anti-quantum critique:**

The Map addresses this relatively well. It concedes that Duch is a strong critic of substantive quantum-mind mechanisms and tries to preserve only a minimal interface view. This is the clearest case where “convergent conclusion, opposite reasoning” does real philosophical work. The weakness is that it mostly narrows the target rather than proving that the minimal interface is needed. [[quantum-consciousness]]

**2. Duch’s articon / machine-consciousness argument:**

The Map states the argument fairly, but its reply is mostly cost-assignment: accepting Duch means accepting articon consciousness, possibly strong machine-consciousness claims, and perhaps uncomfortable conclusions about advanced AI. That raises the price of Duch’s view; it does not show the view is false. [[machine-question]]

**3. Duch’s “mind as shadow of neurodynamics” reduction:**

The Map engages this as the sharpest conflict with Tenet 1, especially in the strong-emergence article. Its core reply is that a “shadow” cannot do causal work, so Duch risks epiphenomenalism, whereas the Map wants bidirectional causation. That is a substantive objection, but it depends on whether Duch’s “shadow” metaphor is being read too literally. Duch can reply that mental causation just is neurodynamic causation under another description. [[the-strong-emergence-of-consciousness]]

**4. Duch’s hard-question strategy:**

This is the least answered. Duch does not simply ignore the explanatory gap; he treats it as something a physical theory can normalize without reducing first-person perspective into third-person description. The Map’s hard-problem response says, in effect, that this still leaves the structural gap untouched. That is a familiar and serious dualist reply, but not a decisive answer to Duch’s emergentist/identity-style move. [Instytut Nauk Technicznych](https://is.umk.pl/~duch/pubs/97gray2.pdf)

**5. Duch’s empirical geometric programme:**

The Map handles this well by borrowing it while refusing its metaphysical conclusion. This is probably the most philosophically mature part of the integration: it distinguishes “Duch may be right about the physical map” from “Duch is right that the map is all there is.” [[neural-correlates-of-consciousness]]

## Bottom line

The Duch integration is **not merely performative citation**. It does more than name a critic for inoculation; it improves the Map’s adversarial structure, corrects overbroad claims, and identifies a genuinely strong classical opponent.

But it is also **not a full confrontation with Duch’s strongest argument**. The Map’s deepest reply is usually: Duch’s account may be the best classical-neurocomputational story, but the Map’s tenets require nonphysical consciousness, bidirectional causation, and a minimal quantum interface. That is a principled framework boundary, not an independent refutation.

The strongest criticism is therefore:

**The Map engages Duch substantively as an external foil, but often brackets his central sufficiency claim as tenet-incompatible rather than defeating it.**

A more decisive engagement would need to argue directly that self-reflective neurodynamics, feature-space organization, attractor dynamics, and articon-style internal-state discrimination still cannot generate consciousness even in principle—not merely that accepting them would violate the Map’s dualist architecture.
