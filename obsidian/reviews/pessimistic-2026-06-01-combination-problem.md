# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-06-01
**Content reviewed**: `obsidian/concepts/combination-problem.md`

## Executive Summary

A mature, well-cited concept page (deep-reviewed 2026-05-18; never previously the subject of a dedicated pessimistic review). Both load-bearing empirical citations verify cleanly against the live literature (Kerskens & Pérez 2022; Khan/Wiest 2024). The article's core philosophical move — "panpsychism's combination problem is conceptual and unresolved; interactionism's interaction problem is merely empirical and unspecified, hence better-positioned" — is the genuine soft spot. The article hedges this move repeatedly and even names the worry, but the framing still smuggles a presumed asymmetry past several critics. One medium-severity equivocation around "empirical vs conceptual" and one unsupported-move gap on the interactionist side are the only items worth a refine pass; neither is critical and a human may reasonably decline both.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The whole essay is an intramural dispute between two pre-scientific research programs. "Micro-experience," "macro-subject," "phenomenal bonding," "quantum selection" — none of these terms will survive a mature neuroscience. The article treats the combination problem as a *discovery* about reality; it is an artifact of taking folk-psychological subject-talk as metaphysically load-bearing. When the article says interactionism's problem is "empirical — we can in principle look for it at quantum indeterminacies," I'll take that bet: the look will turn up neurons, not selection. The article's own C. elegans paragraph concedes the combination mechanism "doesn't manifest in observable correlates" — exactly what eliminativism predicts for a non-referring posit.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The article grants Strawson's "emergence can't be brute" as if it were established (line 169, listed under "What Panpsychism Gets Right" and endorsed). That is precisely the inflated intuition I deny. Weak/non-radical emergence is ubiquitous and unmysterious; the demand that consciousness-emergence be "intelligible" in a stronger sense than liquidity-emergence is the question being begged. The article's strongest section ("Why the Problem Is Serious") leans entirely on the non-compositionality intuition — "two perspectives do not merge into a third perspective" — stated as flat fact (line 122). That is an introspective report dressed as a metaphysical law. Split-brain and DID data make subject-unity far less obviously atomic than the article assumes.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
The article is admirably restrained here — it explicitly calls the quantum-selection mechanism "unspecified," flags the cited studies as "preliminary," and never claims decoherence is solved. My standing objection (warm-brain decoherence in femtoseconds) is not rebutted, only deferred to `[[entanglement-binding-hypothesis]]` and `[[causal-closure]]`. Fair enough for a concept page — but the "Empirical Distinguishability" section leans on Kerskens & Pérez 2022 as the one positive datum, and that paper drew a published Comment disputing the entanglement-witness interpretation. The article cites the result without noting it is contested even within the niche that produced it.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
Tangential to this article (combination is a panpsychism problem, not an interpretation problem), so little purchase. One note: the article's appeal to "indexical identity matters" via interface locality (`[[multi-mind-collapse-problem]]`) is asserted, not argued, here — but that is properly the job of the linked page, not this one.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
The load-bearing claim is that interactionism's problem is "empirical" and "we can in principle look for it." But the article simultaneously concedes "no one has specified which quantum states consciousness selects, what the selection dynamics are, or how to detect the selection occurring" (line 145). A problem that is empirical *in principle* but for which no one can state a falsifying observation is, operationally, in the same epistemic position as the conceptual problem it claims to improve upon. The article's honesty is its defence — it asks the question outright at line 147 and 183 ("whether this distinction represents genuine progress or merely relocates the mystery is itself debatable") — but it still lets the favourable framing stand as the section's organizing contrast.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The article does better than most here: it explicitly lists Buddhist no-self and Annaka Harris's subject-elimination as live responses (lines 58, 110-114). But it then dismisses them as "a radical departure from ordinary conceptions" and "dissolving the explanandum." From the emptiness view, that is exactly backwards — the "explanandum" (a unified, persisting subject who *has* experiences) is the reification, and dissolving it is the solution, not an evasion. The article's own interactionist alternative depends on a fundamental, unified subject ("Unity is fundamental — no combination needed," line 141) — i.e. it takes the strongest possible stand on the very atomic-self that the combination problem ought to make suspect. This is the deepest unexamined commitment in the piece.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The "conceptual vs empirical" asymmetry does real argumentative work without earning it
- **Current content says**: The combination problem is conceptual ("unclear what it would even mean for micro-experiences to combine"); the interaction problem is empirical ("we know what causal influence means, and can in principle look for it"). This contrast organizes the comparison table (lines 138-143) and the closing tenet section.
- **A critic would argue**: Both problems are presently unsolved and neither yields a stated falsifier. Labeling one "empirical" trades on the in-principle/in-practice gap: an undetectable-in-practice empirical posit is epistemically indistinguishable from a conceptual one. The asymmetry is asserted by category-label, not demonstrated by exhibiting an actual discriminating experiment.
- **Suggested response**: The article already hedges this twice. A refine pass could go one step further: state plainly *what observation would count against interactionist selection* (e.g., a clean null on quantum-coherence/consciousness correlation surviving decoherence controls), so the "empirical" label is cashed out rather than asserted. If no such observation can be stated even schematically, downgrade the contrast from "conceptual vs empirical" to "conceptual-and-stalled vs empirical-but-currently-unprobeable" — which the prose at line 183 already gestures toward but the table does not.

### Strawson's "emergence can't be brute" endorsed without defence
- **Current content says**: Lists "Emergence from non-experience is unintelligible — Strawson's observation that 'emergence can't be brute' is correct" as a settled insight the Map affirms (line 169).
- **A critic would argue**: This is the central contested premise of the entire panpsychism debate, asserted here as a parenthetical "is correct." Dennett/Churchland deny it outright; many non-reductive physicalists accept brute-ish weak emergence.
- **Suggested response**: Soften "is correct" to mark it as a premise the Map shares with panpsychists rather than an established result — e.g. "the Map agrees with Strawson that..." This is a one-line edit; it removes an unjustified flat assertion of the single most load-bearing intuition in the field.

## Epistemic/Metaphysical Equivocation Check

One **medium** flag. The non-compositionality claim at line 122 — "two perspectives do not merge into a third perspective" — is presented as a metaphysical law (how subjects are constituted). The supporting evidence (introspective unity, the Special-Composition-Question convergence) supports at most an *epistemic/phenomenological* reading: we cannot conceive or introspect such merging, and our best composition theories use consciousness as a criterion. Whether perspectives *cannot* merge (metaphysical) or *cannot be conceived by us* to merge (epistemic) is exactly the gap the article needs for its conclusion but does not bridge. Split-brain and DID cases are the standard pressure on the metaphysical reading and go unmentioned in this section. This is orthogonal to hedge density — the surrounding prose is heavily hedged, yet this specific bridge is unbuilt. A refine pass that distinguishes "we cannot make sense of perspective-merging" from "perspective-merging is impossible," and notes the split-brain counter-pressure, would close it.

## Reasoning-Mode Discipline Check

The article engages panpsychism as the named opponent. Its rejection is honest framework-marking, not boundary-substitution: it does not claim to refute panpsychism *within* panpsychism's framework, and the "Map's Alternative" / "Comparing the Problems" sections explicitly present interactionism as facing its *own* unsolved problem of equal severity (lines 145, 183). No label leakage, no `**Evidential status:**` callouts, no forbidden editor-vocabulary. **Pass.**

One adjacent **medium** note (missed unsupported-move identification, applied reflexively to the Map's own side): the article catalogues panpsychism's unearned move (phenomenal bonding "names the problem rather than solving it," line 90) but states its *own* unearned move — "Unity is fundamental — no combination needed" (line 141) — as a clean advantage rather than as an equal helping-itself-to-a-primitive. Treating fundamental unity as free while charging panpsychism for phenomenal bonding is the same move scored differently for the two sides. The closing section's parity admission ("interactionism's quantum selection is as unspecified as panpsychism's phenomenal bonding") covers the *mechanism* but not this *subject-unity* primitive. Naming the symmetry explicitly would strengthen the article's hard-won even-handedness.
