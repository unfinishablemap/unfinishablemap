## Executive Summary

A well-constructed, citation-clean article that does the rare thing of refusing to overclaim — it explicitly frames the smoothness problem as an *instance* of the explanatory gap rather than a standalone argument for dualism, and grants the functionalist response its strongest form. The principal weaknesses are not in what it asserts but in two structural asymmetries: the functionalist "dissolution" is named but never engaged on its own terms (it is rejected by appeal to the Map's prior verdict on functionalism, i.e. a boundary move dressed as a refutation), and the article's central empirical premise — that the substrate is "demonstrably discrete" — is stated more confidently in the Locke section than the article's own VanRullen/Herzog evidence licenses. Citations were spot-verified against the live literature and are accurate.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist
Churchland would say the "smoothness problem" presupposes that "felt continuity" is a determinate explanandum, when it is exactly the folk-psychological posit due for elimination. The article half-concedes this through Lee's introspective-opacity caveat — we cannot inspect the temporal microstructure of experience — but then does not follow the concession to its conclusion. If introspection cannot tell us whether experience is continuous or discrete-below-threshold, on what basis is "continuity" a fact requiring explanation at all? The article needs continuity to be *real* (so physicalism owes an account of it) while admitting introspection cannot establish it is real. Churchland presses: you have built an explanatory debt out of a report you have already conceded is unreliable.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist
Dennett gets the strongest run here, and the article knows it — the 120 Hz-display analogy and the "constitute vs. merely produce" framing are his ground. His sharpest reply is to the illusionism paragraph. The article claims the illusion theory "pushes the problem back a step" because an illusion needs "a subject who is taken in." Dennett denies the regress: there is no Cartesian-Theatre subject taken in; there is only the brain's representation *that* things are smooth, and a representation's being tokened is not a further smoothness needing explanation. The article asserts the regress rather than earning it. This is the single under-argued move in the piece.

### The Quantum Skeptic
Tegmark has little purchase: the article makes no quantum claim. Worth noting only that the bidirectional-interaction paragraph ("the mind actively integrates discrete neural inputs into continuous experience") is the one place a Tegmark-style "show me the mechanism / the timescale" demand could land, and the article pre-empts it by saying the problem "does not require this specific mechanism." Adequate, but the speculative integration-mechanism sentence adds risk without adding argumentative force; it could be cut with no loss.

### The Many-Worlds Defender
Not applicable; no interpretation-of-QM content.

### The Empiricist
Popper's ghost asks what observation would distinguish "genuine phenomenal continuity supplied by a non-physical mind" from "functional interpolation that produces continuity-reports." The article's own Lee caveat answers: *none from introspection*. So the dualist reading and the functionalist reading are, by the article's own admission, empirically indistinguishable at the phenomenal level. That is fine for a "concrete instance of the gap" framing — but the closing grain-mismatch paragraph claims the three mismatches give a "cumulative structural case" with "collective force." A Popperian notes that summing three individually-untestable structural mismatches does not yield a testable one; cumulative force is rhetorical unless at least one limb generates a discriminating prediction. The article should not let "convergence" do epistemic work that none of the converging strands can do alone.

### The Buddhist Philosopher
Nagarjuna would welcome the Bergson and James material and then turn it on the article: the "stream" James describes is empty of a continuous self riding it. The article treats continuity as a property that, if real, must be *supplied by something* (the non-physical mind, per the dualism/bidirectional sections). Nagarjuna denies the reification: continuity need be neither substrate-produced nor mind-supplied; it can be dependently-originated appearance with no bearer. The article's framing — "continuity cannot be a property of the substrate, it must come from somewhere else" — is a false dilemma to the Madhyamaka eye: *from somewhere else* still assumes continuity is a possessed property with a source.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Functionalist response is rejected by boundary-substitution, not engaged
- **File**: `neural-refresh-rates-and-the-smoothness-problem.md`
- **Location**: "The Functionalist Response" section, esp. para. "The Map rejects functionalism for reasons developed across its treatment of the hard problem and qualia…"
- **Problem**: The article presents functionalism's dissolution as "a coherent position and the strongest physicalist response," then rejects it by appeal to the Map's prior verdict on functionalism elsewhere ("the Map rejects functionalism for reasons developed across…"). This is the direct-refutation-discipline failure mode of *boundary-substitution*: the smoothness-specific disagreement is settled by importing a conclusion reached elsewhere, not by engaging functionalism inside this problem. The article is honest *that* it is doing this ("Those who find functionalism satisfying will find the smoothness problem dissolved") — which softens the charge — but the honest framing co-exists with sentences that read as if the functional story has been shown to fall short ("the functional story explains the *role* of smooth experience but not its *phenomenal character*"), which is the conclusion, asserted, not argued here.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either (a) add the one in-problem move that would earn the disagreement — identify the *unsupported foundational move* the functionalist makes specifically about temporal experience (e.g. functionalism stipulates that the functional role of "registering continuity" *is* felt continuity without specifying why the registering is felt at all), or (b) downgrade explicitly to framework-boundary marking: note that on the smoothness case specifically the disagreement reduces to the standing functionalism dispute and is carried by the Map's commitments, not closed against the functionalist here.

### Issue 2: "Demonstrably discrete substrate" overstates the article's own evidence
- **File**: `neural-refresh-rates-and-the-smoothness-problem.md`
- **Location**: "Locke's Objection Inverted" — "The absence of perceptible discreteness in a demonstrably discrete substrate…"; cf. opening "The brain processes information in discrete bursts."
- **Problem**: The body of the article is carefully hedged about discreteness — gamma binding's "strong form has weakened," synchrony "correlates with awareness but does not explain it," and the two-stage model has *stage one* as "effectively continuous." So the substrate is, on the article's own account, continuous-then-discrete, not flatly discrete. Yet the Locke section and the opening sentence assert flat discreteness ("demonstrably discrete," "discrete bursts") as the premise the anti-physicalist inversion rests on. The strongest version of the smoothness problem needs only *some* discrete stage, which the article has; but the rhetoric claims more than the evidence section delivered, and a physicalist will attack the overstatement rather than the real (weaker, still-valid) point.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Align the Locke section's premise with the two-stage model the article already endorsed: the challenge is to *identity theories that locate consciousness at the discrete stage-two crystallisation*, where the substrate of conscious access is discrete by hypothesis. That is the precise and defensible target; "demonstrably discrete substrate" full stop is not.

### Issue 3: Illusionism rebuttal asserts the regress it needs
- **File**: `neural-refresh-rates-and-the-smoothness-problem.md`
- **Location**: "Bergson's Inversion" — final paragraph on the illusionist option.
- **Problem**: "To call continuity an 'illusion' presupposes a subject who is *taken in* by the illusion" is precisely what the illusionist (Frankish, Dennett) denies. The rebuttal works only if a tokened representation-of-smoothness itself constitutes a felt smoothness needing explanation — which is the conclusion under dispute. As written it is question-begging against the position it dismisses in one sentence.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Replace the one-sentence dismissal with the actual move: the illusionist owes an account of why the *seeming* of smoothness is itself a phenomenal seeming (the "illusion of an illusion" / Frankish's own acknowledged residual). State that the debt is relocated, not that the regress is automatic. One added sentence fixes it and makes the article stronger against its most sophisticated opponent.

## Counterarguments to Address

### "Cumulative structural case" from three untestable mismatches
- **Current content says**: The temporal, spatial (Sellars), and informational (bandwidth) mismatches are independent and their "convergence is what gives the programme its collective force."
- **A critic would argue**: Three structural mismatches that each (per the Lee caveat for the temporal one) lack a discriminating empirical test do not sum to an empirical case; convergence of *interpretations of the same gap across modalities* is not the same as independent confirmations. (Compare the corpus's own altered-state-symmetry discipline: N cases contributing the same evidential move count as one, not N.)
- **Suggested response**: Clarify that the "collective force" is dialectical robustness (dissolving one limb leaves the others), not additive empirical confirmation — and that the functionalist who dissolves the gap in general dissolves all three at once, so their independence is real only conditional on functionalism's falsity.

### The continuity explanandum may not be determinate
- **Current content says**: Experience "feels seamless"; continuity is the phenomenal feature physicalism must explain.
- **A critic would argue** (Churchland/Dennett): Given the Lee opacity concession, "felt continuity" may be an indeterminate or constructed report, not a fixed datum.
- **Suggested response**: The article already contains the materials (Lee caveat) to acknowledge this; it should state once that the smoothness problem is conditional on continuity being a genuine explanandum, and that *if* it is a mere report-artifact the problem reduces to "why are continuity-reports produced," which functionalism handles — re-routing the dispute back to functionalism rather than leaving it implicit.
