## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
Would say the "outcome problem" is a pseudo-problem dressed in physics — that "why *this* outcome" presupposes a folk-psychological "I" who experiences "this" one. The article partly anticipates this by grounding the question in indexical identity (No Many Worlds), but the indexical "fact about which result *I* experience" (line 89) is asserted, not defended here; it leans on `[[tenets]]` rather than arguing the point in-frame. This is a tenet-boundary, honestly a boundary — not a refutation of eliminativism on its own terms. The article does not over-claim it as one, so no discipline violation, but the dependency is real.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
Would press the "distinction without a difference" worry — which the article *itself* raises (line 79: "Whether this 'single act' constitutes a genuine causal contribution or a distinction without a difference is the central question the Map's critics press"). Dennett's strongest move is that an actualisation that leaves Born statistics untouched does no work the formalism needs. The article concedes the vacuity risk (line 75) and does not pretend to have dissolved it. Fair engagement.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
The standard decoherence-timescale objection is **pre-empted** (line 81): post-decoherence framing makes the femtosecond neural decoherence timescale "irrelevant" because consciousness is said to act on the *already-decohered* mixture. This is the article's strongest structural move and it is correctly deployed — it sidesteps Tegmark rather than denying his physics.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
Gets the most direct in-framework engagement (the "Why Not Everett?" section). The article's claim that quantum Darwinism's results are "interpretation-neutral" and so do not *require* Everett is correct and is the legitimate in-frame point. It honestly notes Zurek's own leanings are "close to an Everettian picture" and that this "conflicts directly" with the tenet — marking the boundary rather than claiming to refute MWI. Good discipline. A Deutsch would still say the No-Many-Worlds rejection rests on the indexical-identity intuition the article does not independently establish; the article books this as a tenet commitment, which is honest.

### The Empiricist (Popper's ghost) — **source of the one real finding**
See Critical Issues below. The Falsifiability section asserts both that consciousness's influence "must be statistically invisible at the ensemble level (matching Born-rule predictions)" (line 113) and that "a confirmed deviation from Born statistics... would refute the framework" (line 113). These are consistent only under an **asymmetry the article never states**: the hypothesis *predicts no deviation*, so a deviation can refute it, but a *confirming* observation is structurally inaccessible (the prediction is null). The article would be more honest, not less, to say so plainly.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

Same locus as the eliminativist critique; the article's No-Many-Worlds tenet is where this bites, and the article does not engage the no-self line at all. Not a defect for this article's scope (it is a quantum-foundations piece), but a corpus-level note: the indexical-identity premise is doing heavy lifting across several quantum articles and is nowhere argued *in this one*.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Falsifiability asymmetry (the one substantive finding)
- **Current content says**: "its influence must be statistically invisible at the ensemble level (matching Born-rule predictions), so a confirmed deviation from Born statistics in consciousness-dependent experimental contexts would refute the framework." (line 113)
- **A critic would argue**: If the hypothesis *entails* no ensemble-level deviation, then it makes the same prediction as no-consciousness-involved physics, and the "refutation route" is real but one-directional: the framework can be killed by a deviation it predicts will never occur, while no possible observation can *confirm* it. That is an unusual epistemic posture (refutable-but-unconfirmable) and the article presents it as ordinary falsifiability without naming the asymmetry. The vacuity concern raised at line 75 and the falsifiability claim at line 113 are two faces of the same structural fact, stated 38 lines apart and never connected.
- **Suggested response (light, non-additive or near-neutral)**: Add one clause making the asymmetry explicit — e.g., that the proposal is *refutable but not symmetrically confirmable*, which is exactly why the vacuity worry (line 75) and the falsifiability claim (line 113) are the same coin; the framework's empirical content is entirely on the refutation side. This reconciles the two passages and strengthens the "empirically disciplined, if not straightforwardly falsifiable" framing (line 113) rather than weakening it. The article already owns the limitation at line 117 ("sits at the boundary of empirical accessibility"); this just names *why* precisely.
- **Severity**: Low. The article is already honest about the limitation; this is a clarity/internal-cross-reference improvement, not a substantive gap.
