## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: The "dialectical advantage" claim is in tension with the endorsed measure-problem verdict, and ignores that MWI's decision-theoretic burden is incurred anyway
- **File**: `topics/quantum-immortality-and-the-quantum-suicide-survival-argument.md`
- **Location**: lines 28, 52, 64 (Executive/intro; "The measure/probability problem"; "Relation to Site Perspective")
- **Severity**: MEDIUM (the sharpest genuine defect; the priority-probed question-begging worry is mostly inoculated, but surfaces here in a subtler form)
- **Problem**: The article wants two things that pull against each other.
  - Line 52 calls the measure problem **"the decisive objection"** and endorses that "a measure-weighted agent should assign the runaway-survival outcome a vanishingly small credence, not certainty." I.e. the immortality *expectation does not rationally follow* from MWI once a measure is in play.
  - Line 28 and line 64 nonetheless present immortality as **"a cost MWI carries that collapse avoids"** and **"a genuine dialectical advantage: MWI *has* the immortality problem to solve at all, whereas collapse never generates it."**

  If the measure problem is genuinely decisive, then the rational Everettian *also* avoids the pathological expectation — so the "cost" MWI is said to carry is one its own best theory discharges, and the collapse "advantage" is an advantage in *explanatory route*, not in *outcome*. The article's escape hatch is the conditional on line 64 ("*without a compulsory measure-weighted decision theory*, MWI cannot rule the immortality expectation out"). That is the right move, but it exposes the unaddressed reply: **MWI needs a measure-weighted decision theory (the Born rule) regardless of quantum suicide** — to recover ordinary laboratory statistics. So the very apparatus that blocks immortality is *independently mandatory*, and quantum immortality imposes **no extra explanatory burden** beyond the standard probability problem the companion article already covers. The claim that collapse holds a *distinct* dialectical advantage *here* is therefore weaker than the prose asserts, and a Deutsch–Wallace defender's obvious reply ("the theory that gives me Born statistics gives me mortalism for free") goes unmentioned.
- **Recommendation**: Add one or two sentences at the close of the line-64 paragraph conceding that the burden is not *new* — it is the standard probability problem pointed at the first person — and that the Everettian's route to blocking immortality is the same decision theory they need anyway. Reframe the "advantage" honestly as *"collapse never generates the problem, whereas MWI must lean on a decision-theoretic account it independently owes"* rather than as MWI carrying a standalone extra cost.

### Issue 2: "The Map denies exactly this" overstates the Map's distinctiveness — mainstream Everettian *mortalists* deny branch-relative indexicality too
- **File**: `topics/quantum-immortality-and-the-quantum-suicide-survival-argument.md`
- **Location**: line 62 ("The survival argument has a hidden premise: **branch-relative indexicality** … The Map denies exactly this."); cf. line 54 (Papineau) and line 56 (O'Brien)
- **Severity**: MEDIUM
- **Problem**: The dissolution turns on rejecting "branch-relative indexicality" (each surviving branch carries its own continuing *I*). The article frames this as the thing "**The Map denies exactly this**," implying the denial is proprietary to collapse ontology. But the article's own sources show leading Everettians reject it *from inside MWI*: Papineau (cited line 54) presses whether MWI "even licenses genuine pre-measurement uncertainty," and O'Brien's paper (cited line 56) — as its abstract makes explicit — sets "immortalists" (Tegmark) against **"mortalists" like Sean Carroll, David Papineau, and David Wallace, who argue the MWI has no such implications.** So the premise the Map "denies" is *already contested within Everettianism*, and the mortalist route dissolves the immortality expectation without abandoning MWI. Presenting branch-relative indexicality as a "hidden premise" that only the Map's collapse realism removes is a mild strawman of the naive-MWI position and undersells that the dissolution route is *shared* with mainstream Everettian mortalism. This also blunts the "pressure test of Tenet 4" framing (see Issue 4): the leading Everettians agree the absurd conclusion is not entailed.
- **Recommendation**: In the line-62 paragraph, distinguish *naive* MWI self-location (which does assume branch-relative indexicality) from mortalist Everettianism (Wallace, Carroll, Papineau), which denies it too. Then state the Map's actual distinctive claim precisely: collapse supplies the counterfactual-exclusion / single-actual-successor fact *ontologically*, whereas the Everettian mortalist must recover the same "which successor is me" determinacy *without* a single actual branch — which is the residual explanatory gap the Map is really pointing at. This sharpens rather than weakens the article and removes the "hidden premise" overreach.

### Issue 3: Tegmark quote is authentic but sourced under the wrong work
- **File**: `topics/quantum-immortality-and-the-quantum-suicide-survival-argument.md`
- **Location**: line 42 ("In his summary: 'Most accidents and common causes of death clearly don't satisfy all three criteria ... dying isn't a binary thing where you're either alive or dead.'"); Reference 1 (line 86)
- **Severity**: LOW
- **Problem**: The quoted wording is genuinely Tegmark's (web-verified), but it comes from *Our Mathematical Universe* (2014), not the 1998 "Many Worlds or Many Words?" paper. The paragraph sits entirely under the 1998 paper and the only Tegmark reference (Ref 1) is the 1998 paper, so a reader cannot locate the quote's source and would reasonably attribute it to 1998. Not a fabrication — a mis-location.
- **Recommendation**: Add a reference for Tegmark, M. (2014), *Our Mathematical Universe* (Knopf), and attribute the "dying isn't a binary thing" summary to it, or add a short in-text tag ("in his later popular summary").

### Issue 4: "The sharpest available pressure test of Tenet 4" oversells the risk
- **File**: `topics/quantum-immortality-and-the-quantum-suicide-survival-argument.md`
- **Location**: line 60
- **Severity**: LOW (framing/stylistic)
- **Problem**: Quantum immortality is a *reductio against MWI* — a datum that, if anything, *supports* No-Many-Worlds. Calling it "the sharpest available pressure test of Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds)" that "the framework passes" is slightly inverted: a pressure test is something that could *refute* the tenet, but the article's own analysis shows the case tilts toward collapse. At most it is a test in the sense that mishandling it would embarrass the Map. Minor, but worth softening to avoid the impression that the Map manufactured a threat it then defeats.
- **Recommendation**: Reword to something like "the sharpest illustration of what Tenet 4 buys" or "a stress case where MWI's branch-relative indexicality produces a conclusion almost everyone rejects."

## Counterarguments to Address

### The Deutsch–Wallace mortalist reply
- **Current content says**: Immortality is "a cost MWI carries that collapse avoids" (line 28, 64).
- **A critic would argue**: The Born-rule decision theory MWI needs *anyway* for laboratory statistics is exactly what delivers mortalism; quantum immortality adds no new burden, and Wallace/Carroll/Papineau already reject the expectation without leaving MWI.
- **Suggested response**: Concede the burden is not new (it is the standard probability problem, first-person-directed) and relocate the Map's distinctive claim to the *ontological* supply of counterfactual exclusion, not to "MWI has a problem collapse lacks."
