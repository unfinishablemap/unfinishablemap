# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-03-24
**Content reviewed**: concepts/functionalism.md, concepts/embodied-cognition.md

## Executive Summary

Two foundational concept articles—Functionalism and Embodied Cognition—show strong structural quality and rigorous engagement with opposing positions. However, both exhibit recurring patterns: selective deployment of intellectual honesty (conceding where it's safe, asserting where it matters), under-examined empirical claims, and a tendency to recruit philosophical traditions as allies without fully confronting where those traditions diverge from the Map's commitments.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

**On Functionalism**: The article concedes functionalism's appeal then treats qualia as bedrock. But "qualia" is folk psychology's last refuge. The article notes that the illusionism debate "bottoms out in competing intuitions"—then immediately sides with the anti-illusionist intuition. If the debate is at bedrock, intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that your side also rests on intuition, not argument. The self-undermining objection to illusionism (if seeming is never phenomenal, philosophical intuitions lose evidential standing) applies equally in reverse: if seeming is always phenomenal, we can never get outside phenomenal experience to check whether it accurately reports its own nature. The article presents only one direction of this symmetry.

**On Embodied Cognition**: The article recruits Merleau-Ponty's "irreducibility of lived experience" for dualism, but Merleau-Ponty explicitly rejected the kind of substance/property distinction the Map relies on. His "third term" was meant to dissolve the dualism question, not provide a halfway house. Using phenomenologists as dualist allies misrepresents their project.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

**On Functionalism**: The article claims functional organization "bottoms out in physical terms" because "every functional role is defined by causal relations among physical states." But this conflates the metaphysics of functional realization with the epistemology of functional description. A functionalist can grant that all actual functional states happen to be physical while denying that being physical is part of what makes them the mental state they are. The article treats this distinction as unimportant ("the abstraction is in the description, not in the ontology") but this is exactly what's at issue—asserting it doesn't establish it.

**On Embodied Cognition**: The choking analysis claims phenomenological categories "reliably predict which performers will choke." But reliable prediction from phenomenological categories doesn't require phenomenal causation—it requires only that phenomenological reports correlate with underlying neural states that do the causal work. The article acknowledges the physicalist reading but then asks why phenomenal character would "so reliably track the causal structure" if causally inert. The obvious answer: because phenomenal character supervenes on the causal structure. Supervenience makes reliable correlation expected, not remarkable.

### The Quantum Skeptic

**On Functionalism**: The article's "Empirical Tractability" section claims dualism "predicts systematic behavioral differences between biological and computational systems even at high sophistication." What specific differences? At what level of sophistication? Without quantifiable predictions, this is hand-waving dressed as empirical science. The functionalist makes a clearer prediction: functionally equivalent systems behave equivalently. The dualist prediction is parasitic on the absence of evidence.

**On Embodied Cognition**: The Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet connection is the weakest part. Claiming the Dreyfus progression reveals "where consciousness interfaces with motor systems" conflates a phenomenological description of skill stages with a physical mechanism. The transition between absorbed and reflective modes is described at the psychological level; mapping it to quantum interaction sites requires bridge principles the article doesn't provide.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

**On Functionalism**: The article asserts the No Many Worlds tenet matters because "indexical identity matters." But the article on functionalism doesn't actually develop this connection. It's listed in the Empirical Tractability section as a prediction that consciousness "correlates with quantum-sensitive processes," but this prediction doesn't distinguish collapse from many-worlds—quantum-sensitive processes exist in both interpretations.

**On Embodied Cognition**: The article honestly concedes that choking evidence is "compatible with both collapse and Many-Worlds interpretations." Good. But then it points to "the phenomenology of singular determination in effortful selection" as the stronger anti-MWI argument, citing the mental-effort article. This is the exact phenomenological argument the MWI defender would reject: subjective experience of singular determination is expected on MWI (each branch-self experiences singular determination). The article treats the phenomenology of singularity as evidence against branching, but branching would produce exactly this phenomenology.

### The Empiricist

**On Functionalism**: The "What Would Challenge This View" section is implicit (the Empirical Tractability section lists predictions) but never asks what would falsify the Map's position. The predictions are asymmetric: if AI systems show behavioral equivalence, the dualist can always claim consciousness is absent but behavior is preserved. If they show differences, the dualist claims vindication. What evidence would make the Map abandon dualism? This omission is glaring for an article about a competing theory.

**On Embodied Cognition**: The falsifiability section is exemplary—five specific conditions that would challenge the view. This is how all Map articles should handle the empiricist challenge. However, condition 4 ("Embodied AI achieves genuine understanding") contains a loophole: "capacities that seem genuinely conscious" leaves the Map free to deny that any AI capacity is "genuinely" conscious no matter what it does. Without behavioral criteria for "genuine," the prediction is unfalsifiable.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

**On Functionalism**: The article never considers the Buddhist perspective. Functionalism and dualism both assume a unified subject of experience. The Buddhist would ask: who is it that has qualia? If the self is constructed (as neuroscience increasingly suggests), the entire qualia-based argument against functionalism rests on a reified experiencer that may not exist. The absent qualia objection presupposes something it would be like to *be* someone—but if personal identity is a construction, "something it is like to be X" may be as confused as functionalism claims qualia to be.

**On Embodied Cognition**: The Buddhist phenomenology section is well-handled—it honestly acknowledges that "Buddhist philosophy emphasises śūnyatā (emptiness)" and that "the same phenomenological data supports competing metaphysical frameworks." The article draws the right lesson: embodied experience constrains but doesn't determine ontological commitments. The only weakness: it doesn't follow through on what this means for the Map. If the same data supports both the Map's dualism and Buddhist emptiness, the Map's position is underdetermined by the evidence it cites—making it a choice, not a conclusion.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Functionalism's Central Argument Begs the Question
- **File**: concepts/functionalism.md
- **Location**: Opening paragraph, second paragraph
- **Problem**: The article asserts that "functional organization ultimately bottoms out in physical terms" as though this settles the debate, but this is precisely what functionalists deny. The article provides a gloss ("every functional role is defined by causal relations among physical states") but this restates the claim rather than arguing for it. A functionalist would counter that functional roles are multiply realizable *because* they are not reducible to any particular physical description—the abstractness is in the property, not just the description.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either develop the argument more carefully (engaging with the functionalist response that multiple realizability shows functional properties are genuinely higher-level) or acknowledge this as a contested premise rather than an established conclusion.

### Issue 2: Missing Falsifiability in Functionalism Article
- **File**: concepts/functionalism.md
- **Location**: "Empirical Tractability" section
- **Problem**: The article lists predictions that would vindicate dualism but never specifies what evidence would falsify it. The functionalism article would be stronger if it explicitly stated what would make the Map reconsider its rejection of functionalism—as the embodied cognition article does with its five conditions.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add a "What Would Challenge This View?" section parallel to the one in embodied-cognition.md. Specify concrete conditions under which the Map would reconsider functionalism.

### Issue 3: Supervenience Objection Unaddressed in Choking Analysis
- **File**: concepts/embodied-cognition.md
- **Location**: "Choking Under Pressure" section, paragraph beginning "The interesting question"
- **Problem**: The article frames the reliable correlation between phenomenal character and performance as puzzling for physicalism. But supervenience (phenomenal states supervening on physical states) makes this correlation entirely expected. The article never addresses the supervenience response, which is the standard physicalist move here.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge the supervenience objection explicitly and explain why the Map finds it insufficient (e.g., supervenience without identity leaves the correlation unexplained at the metaphysical level—why do these physical states have these phenomenal characters?).

### Issue 4: Merleau-Ponty Recruited as Dualist Ally
- **File**: concepts/embodied-cognition.md
- **Location**: "Phenomenological Roots" and "Relation to the Map's Perspective" sections
- **Problem**: The article says Merleau-Ponty "treated lived experience as irreducible, rejecting both Cartesian dualism and reductive materialism" and that his "lived body is a third term—leaving space for property dualism." But Merleau-Ponty's third term was meant to overcome the dualism/physicalism dichotomy entirely, not to create a space for a refined version of dualism. Using him as support for property dualism risks misattribution.
- **Severity**: Low
- **Recommendation**: Qualify the claim: note that Merleau-Ponty would not have endorsed property dualism, but that his insistence on irreducibility of experience is *compatible* with property dualist readings, even if that wasn't his intent. The distinction between "supports" and "is compatible with" matters here.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Functionalism: The Multiple Realizability Response
- **Current content says**: Functional organization "bottoms out in physical terms" because causal roles are defined among physical states
- **A critic would argue**: Multiple realizability shows that mental properties are at a genuinely higher level of abstraction than any particular physical implementation—the abstractness is ontological, not merely descriptive, because no single physical property type corresponds to the mental type
- **Suggested response**: Engage directly with the ontological status of higher-level properties. The Map could argue that even if functional properties are genuinely higher-level, they remain *physical* higher-level properties (patterns in physical substrate), and consciousness requires something non-physical.

### Embodied Cognition: The Supervenience Dissolution
- **Current content says**: Phenomenal categories reliably predicting choking outcomes would be a "remarkable coincidence" if phenomenal character were causally inert
- **A critic would argue**: Supervenience makes this correlation expected, not remarkable—phenomenal states track causal structure because they supervene on it
- **Suggested response**: Press the question of *why* supervenience holds. If phenomenal properties are distinct from physical properties (as the Map claims), their systematic correlation with physical causal structure demands explanation. Supervenience as brute fact is explanatorily unsatisfying—which is itself an argument for either identity (which eliminates the gap) or genuine interaction (which explains the correlation).

## Strengths (Brief)

**Functionalism article**: Excellent structural coverage—moves from core claims through modern frameworks, AI implications, specification problems, and the hard problem before the tenet connection. The treatment of IIT as a physicalist rejection of functionalism is a particularly strong move, showing the Map isn't alone in its skepticism. The illusionism section's acknowledgment that the debate "bottoms out in competing intuitions" is admirably honest.

**Embodied cognition article**: The falsifiability section is a model for the entire site. The Buddhist phenomenology section is intellectually generous. The choking analysis, while vulnerable to the supervenience objection, is genuinely insightful in connecting embodied cognition research to the Map's framework. The careful distinction between extended cognition and extended consciousness shows philosophical sophistication.

Both articles are well-referenced, appropriately structured, and engage seriously with opposing positions. The issues identified are refinements, not fundamental failures.
