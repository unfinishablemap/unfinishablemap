## 1. Evidence strength and sufficiency

The article's evidence falls into five categories: measurement-problem space, quantum-biological plausibility, anesthesia/microtubule evidence, attention/volition neuroscience, and low conscious-bandwidth evidence. None is presently sufficient to support the specific hypothesis that consciousness selects neural quantum outcomes.

### 1.1 Measurement problem: an opening, not evidence

The article is right that decoherence does not by itself turn an improper mixture into a proper ignorance mixture on all interpretations. Its surrounding "Post-Decoherence Selection" article correctly frames this as an outcome-selection problem and distinguishes consciousness-mediated selection from objective collapse, hidden-variable, many-worlds, and history-selection families. [concepts/post-decoherence-selection](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/post-decoherence-selection/)

But the target article's stronger line that "causal closure fails precisely at this gap" is too quick. Decoherence plus the measurement problem creates an **interpretive underdetermination**, not a demonstrated failure of physical closure. Everettian, Bohmian, objective-collapse, consistent-histories, QBist, relational, and pragmatist approaches all deny that this gap must be filled by a nonphysical selector. The article's dualist hypothesis is one possible interpretation of the gap, not an evidentially privileged one.

A better formulation would be: **if** one rejects many-worlds, hidden variables, purely physical collapse, and pragmatist no-collapse treatments, **then** the outcome gap becomes a possible location for interactionist dualist causation.

### 1.2 Quantum biology: relevant but overportable

The article fairly rejects the older blanket claim that "warm wet brains cannot host quantum effects." That broad dismissal is no longer safe. However, the leap from "some biological quantum effects occur" to "consciousness selects neural quantum outcomes" remains large.

A 2026 critical review of quantum-consciousness work argues that it is not enough to invoke quantum mechanics; a theory needs physical feasibility, a bridge principle connecting quantum processes to subjectivity, and discriminating tests. The same review concludes that recent microtubule and related findings may support functional relevance, but reproducibility and conformity to Orch OR or other consciousness-specific mechanisms in intact brains remain independently unestablished. [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1730965/full)

This is the most important current-literature correction the target article should absorb: **quantum feasibility is not dualist evidence**. It may help block an impossibility objection, but it does not yet supply positive confirmation of conscious selection.

### 1.3 Microtubule and anesthesia evidence: suggestive, mixed, and not mechanism-specific

The target article presents the Craddock tubulin work appropriately as a prediction about tubulin oscillations and anesthetic effects, not as direct evidence of conscious quantum selection. Craddock et al. used docking, quantum chemistry, and theoretical modeling to argue that anesthetic gases alter collective terahertz dipole oscillations in tubulin in ways correlated with anesthetic potency. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-017-09992-7)

The Wiest 2025 review is also cited in a defensible way: it reviews and develops the microtubule-consciousness case, including room-temperature quantum-effect claims and MRI-entanglement claims, but it does not itself provide new experimental data. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article/2025/1/niaf011/8127081)

The article is less complete on the 2024–2025 anesthesia literature. It mentions the 2024 epothilone B rat result as a clue, but a 2025 BMC Anesthesiology mouse study using several microtubule-modulating drugs found mixed and directionally disparate effects on anesthetic sensitivity, including results that do not straightforwardly support a simple "microtubule stabilization delays unconsciousness" story. The authors themselves emphasize disparate effects, limitations, and the need for further preclinical and clinical work. [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12871-025-02956-9)

The target article should also include more caution about electromagnetic-resonance evidence. A 2024 Scientific Reports study found no evidence for non-thermal microwave effects on microtubule growth after using more appropriate heating controls, and it explicitly warns that speculative microtubule-consciousness claims need tighter connection to controlled experimental data. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-68852-3)

Current evidential status: microtubules remain a **candidate substrate**, not a confirmed consciousness selector. The live literature supports "worth testing," not "probably true."

### 1.4 Decoherence: the article's post-decoherence move is clever but costly

The article's strongest maneuver is to shift the proposed selection point after decoherence. This avoids the full burden of maintaining long-lived coherent superpositions across neural-scale tissue. The article explicitly recognizes that this trades one problem for another: if selection happens after decoherence, the theory becomes less vulnerable to ordinary coherence objections but also loses a distinctive tissue-level coherence signature. [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/)

That tradeoff should be highlighted more sharply. Tegmark's original argument placed neural and microtubular decoherence far below cognitive timescales, with estimates around 10^-13 to 10^-20 seconds versus neural timescales around milliseconds or longer. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/9907009) The post-decoherence theory can say "that does not refute us," but then it must explain exactly how a nonphysical selection of already-decohered alternatives becomes **specific, adaptive, brain-local, and reason-responsive** without producing detectable Born-rule deviations.

That is the central unresolved mechanism.

### 1.5 Conscious bandwidth: accurate citation, weak transfer

The article uses Zheng and Meister's "10 bits/s" result carefully, noting that it concerns report/thought throughput rather than quantum-selection resolution. That restraint is important. Zheng and Meister's paper argues that human behavior and thought have an information throughput of roughly 10 bits/s despite sensory systems taking in around 10^9 bits/s. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.10234)

The result may be useful for constraining conscious-access models, but it does not support quantum selection by itself. A physicalist global-workspace or control-theoretic theory can also explain severe conscious bottlenecks as a consequence of serial access, action selection, working memory, reporting constraints, or policy-level control. The article correctly avoids overstating this evidence, but it should explicitly say: **the 10-bit result is not evidence for dualism unless paired with an independently justified psychophysical selection law.**

## 3. Counterarguments insufficiently engaged

### 3.1 Nonreductive physicalist mental causation

The article often treats the live contrast as dualist selection versus epiphenomenalism or illusionism. That misses the strongest middle position: nonreductive physicalism can accept real mental causation while denying nonphysical intervention. On that view, intentions, attention, effort, and decisions are causally efficacious because they are higher-level realized brain states, not because they select quantum outcomes.

The surrounding "Empirical Evidence for Consciousness-Selecting" article does acknowledge that evolutionary and comparative-cognition arguments support anti-epiphenomenalism more than anti-physicalism. The target article should import that caveat more visibly. [topics/empirical-evidence-for-consciousness-selecting](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/empirical-evidence-for-consciousness-selecting/)

### 3.2 Contemporary physicalist theories of consciousness

The target article engages illusionism but does not sufficiently engage the strongest empirical physicalist programs: Global Neuronal Workspace Theory, Integrated Information Theory, recurrent processing, higher-order theories, predictive processing, and attention-schema approaches.

This matters because attention, report bottlenecks, effort, and volitional control are not unexplained data points waiting for dualism. They are central targets of live neuroscientific theories. The COGITATE adversarial collaboration, for example, tested competing predictions from IIT and GNWT across fMRI, MEG, and iEEG in 256 participants; it found partial alignments and serious challenges for both theories, and it emphasized quantitative, preregistered, theory-neutral testing rather than interpretive fit. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08888-1) The protocol itself used adversarial collaboration, preregistration, multiple theory-impartial labs, replication, and open-science safeguards. [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article)

The Map should treat such programs as serious competitors, not background noise. Even if they fail, their failures must be specified.

### 3.3 Illusionism is engaged, but not at its strongest

The article criticizes illusionism by asking why a system would represent itself as having phenomenal character if there is no phenomenal character. That is a fair challenge. But the strongest illusionist and attention-schema responses say that cognitive systems can misrepresent their own internal architecture for useful control and social-modeling reasons. The article needs to engage that steelman rather than mainly treating illusionism as a regress or "why does the illusion feel real?" puzzle.

A stronger section would separate three questions: whether phenomenal realism is true, whether introspective self-models can misrepresent, and whether misrepresentation theories can explain the functional role of reports of effort, pain, agency, and attention.

### 3.4 Alternative quantum interpretations are too quickly filtered by site tenets

The surrounding "Post-Decoherence Selection" article classifies objective collapse, hidden variables, many worlds, and history selection, but the site often rejects them because they conflict with tenets such as No Many Worlds, Minimal Quantum Interaction, or Dualism. [concepts/post-decoherence-selection](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/post-decoherence-selection/)

That is internally coherent, but it risks **tenet-protective filtering**. In an outer review, these alternatives must be assessed first on explanatory adequacy, empirical risk, parsimony, and compatibility with quantum theory, before being rejected for failing the Map's preferred metaphysical constraints.

### 3.5 The luck objection remains underdeveloped

The article says consciousness-selection should be reason-guided rather than random, but it has not shown how reasons guide selection. If reasons are physically encoded in the brain, a physicalist can claim them without dualism. If reasons are nonphysical, the theory needs a psychophysical law explaining how they bias outcome selection while preserving Born statistics.

The surrounding psychophysical-law article recognizes this specification problem and says the Map needs variables, bandwidth, scope, and an E×P→P coupling formalism. [concepts/psychophysical-laws](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/psychophysical-laws/) The target article should make clear that until this law is specified, "reason-guided selection" is a promissory label.

## 4. Tenet-protective bracketing and falsifiability shielding

The article is unusually honest about its falsifiability problem, but the problem is deeper than the section suggests.

### 4.1 Born-preserving selection shields the central claim

If consciousness selects only among Born-compatible outcomes, ordinary quantum statistics remain unchanged. That preserves no-signalling and avoids easy refutation, but it also means the central causal claim may produce no unique physical signature. The article acknowledges this and says falsifiability relocates to surrounding commitments such as neural quantum effects, attention correlations, phenomenology, and anti-epiphenomenalist arguments. [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/)

That is a serious concession. It means the core claim is not currently a testable neuroscience hypothesis. It is a metaphysical interpretation layered over other empirical claims.

### 4.2 Post-decoherence selection avoids one objection by losing one prediction

The move from pre-decoherence coherent brain states to post-decoherence selection helps avoid Tegmark-style objections, but it also removes a testable expectation of sustained neural coherence. The article notes this tradeoff, but it should elevate it to a central methodological warning: **the theory becomes harder to kill because it predicts less.** [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/)

### 4.3 Brain-boundary bracketing is not yet principled

The article defines the brain boundary through embodied control-loop integration and cites BCI only as partial confirmation through motor cortex. [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/) This is plausible as a starting point, but it risks becoming ad hoc. If consciousness selects outcomes where there is integrated control, then advanced BCI, prosthetics, split-brain cases, deep-brain stimulation, artificial neural implants, and external memory systems become boundary tests. The theory needs advance criteria for when a substrate enters the selectable domain.

### 4.4 "Many Worlds empirically confirmed" is a weak falsifier

The article lists empirical confirmation of Many Worlds as a challenge. But many-worlds confirmation is not likely to arrive as a clean standalone empirical result; interpretations often share empirical predictions. A better falsifier would be a narrower operational claim: for example, results that strongly support unitary-only quantum mechanics across scales relevant to proposed collapse models, or that undermine the need for any single-outcome selector.

### 4.5 Phenomenological falsifiers are under-operationalized

The article says it would be challenged if contemplatives uniformly reported choices happening "to them" rather than "by them." [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/) That is interesting, but too vague. Reports of agency vary by training, culture, task, pathology, and framing. The site needs preregistered phenomenological protocols, not informal appeals to meditative consensus.

### Improvements to the target article

2. **Replace "causal closure fails at this gap" with a conditional formulation.** Suggested wording: "If one accepts a single-outcome ontology and rejects Everettian, Bohmian, objective-collapse, and pragmatist resolutions, the post-decoherence outcome gap becomes a possible location for interactionist selection."
3. **Add a "What this evidence does not show" box.** State explicitly: quantum biology does not show consciousness-selection; microtubule effects do not show dualism; attention effects do not show nonphysical causation; 10-bit conscious throughput does not imply quantum-selection bandwidth; OCD/PET and effort studies are not evidence of quantum outcome selection.
4. **Update the microtubule/anesthesia section.** Add the 2025 mixed-results microtubule-modulating-drug study and the 2024 non-thermal microwave-control caution. 6. **Add a rival-theory section.** Compare the dualist-selection hypothesis against GNWT, IIT, recurrent processing, predictive processing, higher-order theories, attention-schema theory, nonreductive physicalism, and illusionism. Include what each can explain about attention, effort, report, agency, and bottlenecks without quantum selection.
7. **Clarify the role of Keppler 2025.** Present Keppler as a theoretical QED/ZPF neurophysiology model with proposed experiments, not as evidence that consciousness selects neural states. 9. **Define candidate psychophysical variables.** Even a toy model would help: what mental state selects, what physical alternatives are selectable, what probability constraints apply, what temporal window matters, and what prevents arbitrary selection.
10. **Replace loose phenomenological falsifiers with operational protocols.** Specify tasks, populations, predictions, exclusion criteria, and what result would lower confidence.

### Methodological improvements for the site

1. **Use evidence grades everywhere.** Suggested categories: establishes possibility, supports substrate relevance, supports mental causation, supports quantum involvement, supports consciousness-specific selection, supports dualism. Do not allow evidence from one category to silently migrate into another.
2. **Require a rival-explanation paragraph for every empirical claim.** Each neuroscience citation should say what a physicalist, illusionist, nonreductive physicalist, and non-consciousness quantum theorist would say about the same result.
3. **Maintain a live citation-audit ledger.** The changelog shows that publisher-of-record checks catch real errors. 4. **Separate site tenets from evidential conclusions.** Tenets can define the Map's preferred metaphysical route, but article prose should distinguish "ruled out by evidence," "ruled out by parsimony," and "excluded because incompatible with our framework."
