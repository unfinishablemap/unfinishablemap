# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-03-23
**Content reviewed**: obsidian/topics/evolution-under-dualism.md

## Executive Summary

"Evolution Under Dualism" is an ambitious piece attempting to reconcile interactionist dualism with evolutionary biology. While it correctly notes that dualism does not require modifying natural selection, it repeatedly understates the severity of its own problems—particularly the circularity in its fitness arguments and the lack of independent evidence for "interface selection." The three models (Fixed Domain, Co-Evolution, Unfolding) are presented with admirable even-handedness but none escapes the fundamental objection that adding an undetectable non-physical layer to evolution is explanatorily idle.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would target the article's central metaphor: brains as "instruments" or "receivers" for consciousness. This is folk psychology dressed in evolutionary garb. The article claims evolution produced "increasingly capable instruments" for consciousness, but every feature attributed to "interface quality"—neural coherence, attentional systems, working memory expansion—has perfectly adequate explanations in terms of computational capacity and information processing. The article acknowledges this ("These suggestions are speculative") but still frames them as predictions of dualism. They are not predictions; they are re-descriptions of known neural features in dualist vocabulary.

The Churchland objection cuts deepest at the "Three Models" section. The article presents three models of "what evolves" under dualism, but all three are parasitic on the neuroscientific facts that physicalism already explains. The Fixed Domain model adds psychophysical laws it cannot specify. Co-evolution smuggles temporal change into what dualism defines as non-temporal. Unfolding borrows from Whitehead without producing testable content. None of these models generates a prediction that physicalist neuroscience does not already make on its own.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would press the article's treatment of teleology. The article claims dualism "reintroduces a limited form of teleology without invoking a designer"—that conscious organisms act "for reasons" in a way that functionalism's "as if" purposes cannot capture. But this misunderstands the functionalist position. Real patterns are not identified "from the outside" in the way the article claims; they are identified by the same criteria that make anything real—predictive power, causal efficacy at the design level, explanatory indispensability.

The article says "real patterns identified from the outside are not the same as purposes experienced from the inside." Dennett would reply: you have not shown there is an "inside" that adds anything beyond what the real patterns already capture. The article begs the question by assuming that felt purpose is something over and above functional purpose, then citing felt purpose as evidence for dualism. This is the classic Cartesian circle: consciousness is needed to explain purpose, and purpose is cited as evidence for consciousness's irreducibility.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Tegmark would find the "Selection Pressure on Interfaces" section the weakest part of the article. The article lists neural coherence, attentional systems, and working memory as possible evidence for "interface selection." But:

1. **Neural coherence**: The article itself acknowledges the decoherence objection and cites quantum biology as precedent. But photosynthetic quantum effects and avian magnetoreception operate in very different physical regimes from neural decision-making. The photosynthetic case involves energy transfer in femtosecond timescales through rigid molecular structures. Neural systems are warm, noisy, electrochemically driven networks. The precedent is far weaker than the article suggests.

2. **Working memory**: The claim that "7±2 items versus approximately 2±1" reflects interface expansion is a just-so story. Working memory capacity has well-studied computational explanations involving prefrontal sustained firing, recurrent neural circuits, and interference-based decay. No quantum interface is needed.

3. **Attentional systems**: The claim that mammalian and avian attentional systems are "far more sophisticated than what pure information processing would require" is asserted without evidence. What would "pure information processing" require? The article does not say—because any answer would be arbitrary and easily contested.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

Deutsch would focus on the article's dismissal of MWI in the "Relation to Site Perspective" section. The article states: "Under Many Worlds, the unbiased branch also exists, making consciousness's biasing irrelevant to the total outcome." But this conflates "irrelevant to the total outcome" with "irrelevant to the branch we observe." The self-locating uncertainty approach—which the article dismisses as merely explaining the "appearance" of efficacy—is precisely what decision theory under MWI addresses. Deutsch-Wallace decision theory shows that rational agents in a branching universe should act as if probabilities are real, even though all branches exist. The article treats this as a reductio rather than engaging with the technical argument.

More fundamentally: the article claims "single outcomes preserve both the significance of the actual evolutionary path and the causal contribution of consciousness to it." But significance is not a physical property. Many-worlds does not eliminate the evolutionary path we observe; it contextualises it. The article's objection to MWI amounts to: "I want this branch to be the only real one," which is a preference, not an argument.

### The Empiricist

A Popperian would find the article's "What Would Challenge This View?" section simultaneously the best and most damning part of the piece. Credit: the article lists falsification conditions. But examine them:

1. "If consciousness proved epiphenomenal"—this is already the default physicalist reading. How would one *prove* epiphenomenalism true against dualism? The article treats this as an empirical possibility but provides no test.

2. "If quantum effects in neural tissue were definitively ruled out"—even the article acknowledges this would only undermine "the specific mechanism," not dualism itself.

3. "If all cognitive flexibility attributed to conscious deliberation could be replicated by unconscious processing"—this is a retreat to unfalsifiability. Any unconscious replication could be met with "but consciousness was still present and contributing."

4. "If physicalism produced a satisfactory account"—this is not a prediction; it is a bet that the competing programme will fail.

The article ends by noting "none of these would refute dualism itself." This is the hallmark of an unfalsifiable position. Every specific prediction can be failed without threatening the core thesis. The article is honest about this, but honesty about unfalsifiability does not make the position falsifiable.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nagarjuna would target the article's unexamined assumption of a persistent, unified self that interfaces with matter across evolutionary time. The three models all assume that "consciousness" is a coherent entity that either pre-exists (Fixed Domain), co-evolves with (Co-Evolution), or unfolds through (Unfolding) physical systems. But what is this consciousness? The article treats it as a given—something that "interfaces" or "engages" with matter.

Buddhist analysis would decompose this reified consciousness into momentary arising and ceasing. The article's discussion of the musician-instrument analogy is revealing: it assumes the musician is a persistent entity distinct from the instrument. But what if the "musician" is itself constituted by the playing—with no remainder? The article's teleology section claims conscious organisms act "for reasons," but the self that acts for reasons may itself be a construction arising within the very neural processes the article relegates to "mere interface."

The unfolding model comes closest to Buddhist insight—each moment of experience is "genuinely novel"—but the article immediately tethers it back to a persistent consciousness that has "character" developing over evolutionary time. This is the subtlest form of self-clinging: accepting impermanence in principle while preserving permanence in practice.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Circular Fitness Argument
- **File**: evolution-under-dualism.md
- **Location**: "The Selection Pressure on Interfaces" section
- **Problem**: The argument that evolution selected for better "interfaces" assumes that consciousness contributes to fitness—the very claim under dispute. The article cites the evolutionary case for mental causation as established, but that article itself relies on the assumption that consciousness is causally efficacious. Each article supports the other without independent grounding.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge the circularity explicitly. State that the interface selection argument is conditional on mental causation being established independently, and identify what independent evidence would look like.

### Issue 2: "More Sophisticated Than Computation Requires" — Unsupported Claim
- **File**: evolution-under-dualism.md
- **Location**: "Attentional systems" paragraph (line 125-126)
- **Problem**: The article claims attentional systems in mammals and birds are "far more sophisticated than what pure information processing would require." No evidence or argument supports this claim. What baseline of "required" sophistication is being assumed? Without specifying what "pure information processing" would predict for attentional complexity, this is unfalsifiable hand-waving.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either provide a specific computational model that predicts simpler attentional systems than those observed, or remove the claim and reframe as: "The sophistication of attentional systems is *consistent with* interface selection, though also consistent with computational accounts."

### Issue 3: Co-Evolution Model's Temporal Incoherence
- **File**: evolution-under-dualism.md
- **Location**: "Co-Evolution" subsection (lines 82-89)
- **Problem**: The article acknowledges that co-evolution "may smuggle temporal structure into what dualism defines as non-temporal" but treats this as merely a "difficulty" rather than a potential contradiction. If consciousness exists outside spacetime, the concept of it "changing in response to" physical systems is not just difficult—it may be incoherent. The article needs to either explain how non-temporal entities can undergo diachronic change or acknowledge this as a serious objection rather than a footnote.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Expand the difficulty into a full objection. Consider whether the co-evolution model actually requires abandoning the non-temporal characterisation of consciousness—and if so, what that costs.

### Issue 4: Nomological Impossibility Claim for Zombies
- **File**: evolution-under-dualism.md
- **Location**: "What the Models Share" (line 103)
- **Problem**: The claim that philosophical zombies are "nomologically impossible" on all three models is stated but under-argued. The fixed-domain model relies on psychophysical laws, but the article acknowledges these laws are unspecified. How can one claim nomological impossibility when the relevant laws are unknown? This is an assertion about what the laws would say if we knew them—which is not nomological impossibility but a promissory note.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Qualify the zombie claim. State that all three models *predict* nomological impossibility if their respective psychophysical laws hold, but that the laws themselves remain unspecified.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Interface vs. Generation: A Distinction Without Empirical Difference
- **Current content says**: Brains are not consciousness generators but consciousness receivers/channels. Convergent evolution reveals "interface requirements" constrained by physics.
- **A critic would argue**: If every empirical observation about brains and consciousness is equally consistent with brains *generating* consciousness and brains *interfacing* with consciousness, the distinction is metaphysical rather than scientific. The article needs observations that favour interface over generation.
- **Suggested response**: The article's convergent evolution section gestures at this—shared architectural features "beyond what computation requires"—but the computation baseline remains undefined. Strengthening this would require specifying what features interface-only theories predict that generation theories do not.

### Local Directionality vs. Universal Directionality
- **Current content says**: Dualism implies only "local directional trends" in some lineages, not progressive evolution.
- **A critic would argue**: If consciousness confers fitness advantages and the interface can be improved, this is selection pressure that applies wherever consciousness exists. The article's distinction between "local" and "universal" direction is a matter of degree, not kind. In every lineage where consciousness interfaces, there is pressure toward better interfaces—that is a universal tendency, not a local one.
- **Suggested response**: Distinguish between "pressure exists" and "pressure always dominates other pressures." Local directionality fails when other selection pressures are stronger—which they often are.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite these criticisms, the article succeeds at several things worth preserving:

1. **Clear separation from creationism**: The opening section effectively distinguishes dualism from anti-evolution positions—an important and often-neglected clarification.
2. **Three-model taxonomy**: Presenting Fixed Domain, Co-Evolution, and Unfolding as distinct models with different predictions is genuinely useful philosophical work. This taxonomy structures an otherwise muddled debate.
3. **Honest falsification conditions**: The "What Would Challenge This View?" section is forthright about what would undermine the argument, even if the conditions are ultimately insufficient for genuine falsification.
4. **Tenet integration**: The Relation to Site Perspective section connects each tenet substantively rather than perfunctorily.
5. **Convergent evolution framing**: Using convergent evolution to distinguish the three models (quantitative vs. qualitative vs. ill-formed differences) is a productive analytical move.
