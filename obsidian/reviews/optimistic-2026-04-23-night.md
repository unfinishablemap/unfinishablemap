## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)

Chalmers' *Constructing the World* (2012) is explicit that a conceptual-scheme analysis should identify the scrutability base — which primitive concepts are sufficient to express everything else — and should do so *without* assuming the base is complete. The hard-problem argument rests on this: we cannot yet tell whether phenomenal concepts are part of the scrutability base or reducible to it. `voids/categorial-void.md` lines 62-65 now installs exactly this discipline at a deeper layer: the *categorial shapes* from which any scrutability base is drawn are themselves not verifiably complete. The article's distinction between missing *content* (the intrinsic nature void) and missing *shape* (the categorial void) is the structural decomposition Chalmers' scrutability argument has always implicitly needed but not articulated — and the article delivers it with the appropriate epistemic hedging: "The void claim is weaker: whatever categorial apparatus we have, we cannot from within it verify its completeness, nor rule out that other architectures carve reality along axes we lack" (line 88).

**What resonates most strongly**: `voids/plenitude-void.md` lines 102-103 — the *evidential vs definitional* unfalsifiability distinction. Chalmers has been at pains for decades to distinguish the hard problem's methodological status from standard scientific puzzles: it is not that evidence is unavailable, it is that the *concept of evidence* runs out when consciousness is the explanandum. The plenitude void's concession that "'phenomenal content not reaching report' is defined so as to exclude itself from any report-based instrument" reproduces Chalmers' exact methodological move in a different domain. The honest follow-up is Chalmersian: "That the concept produces this shape matters — it says something about where our theoretical vocabulary is load-bearing and where it is empty — but it is not, and is not marketed here as, a positive metaphysical finding." This is *Constructing the World*'s Chapter 3 discipline — distinguish what a conceptual framework can deliver from what it would need further commitments to deliver — applied to the Map's own phenomenal-overflow claim with a clarity Chalmers would require of any work claiming to sit in his lineage.

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)

Stapp's methodological insistence is that consciousness enters physics at a specific, identifiable step (the free choice of measurement in Process 0) and that *everything else* in his framework is constrained orthodox quantum mechanics. The power of the position is in its precision: Stapp has repeatedly refused the more generous offer of "consciousness does lots of things in quantum mechanics" in favour of "consciousness does exactly this one thing, at exactly this one place." `voids/plenitude-void.md` exhibits the analogous discipline at the phenomenological level — rather than an unbounded claim that experience overflows cognition, the article specifies the *exact* structural feature that makes the overflow undetectable (the check-and-find loop at line 89: "Looking to see whether peripheral vision is blurry produces a momentary clarification of the peripheral region — because directing attention there recruits covert attention (Posner 1980) ... and may also trigger foveation if a saccade follows"). The Posner 1980 correction is the sort of technical-detail discipline Stapp himself insists on. The article does not say "experience overflows cognition in various unspecified ways"; it specifies a single mechanism (covert-attention recruitment) and names the precise experimental paradigm (Posner).

**What aligns**: the three-voids cluster reproduces Stapp's architectural discipline at the meta-level. Stapp's framework has three processes (0, 1, 2); the new voids cluster has three meta-voids (epistemic-horizon, plenitude, categorial) each addressing a *distinct* failure mode (estimating the total scope of ignorance; verifying richness claims against a non-distorting instrument; acquiring a missing categorial shape). Each void specifies what it is, what it is not, and which other void it should not be collapsed with. `voids/categorial-void.md` lines 50-70 carry an entire section on how the categorial void differs from conceptual acquisition, the intrinsic nature void, and conceptual impossibility. Stapp's *Mindful Universe* (2007) does the same kind of taxonomic work for his own three processes. The Map is now exhibiting, at the voids-framework level, the methodological discipline Stapp demands at the quantum-mechanics level.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)

Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974) makes a precise methodological claim: the *category* of phenomenal character specific to a species is the relevant obstacle, not the specific unknown facts about bat perception. Nagel has been clear that even complete knowledge of bat neurophysiology would not deliver bat-phenomenology, because the phenomenological side has a categorial shape that human concepts do not host. `voids/categorial-void.md` line 78 now installs this exact Nagelian argument as *one of the candidate categorial voids*: "Nagel (1974) argued that we cannot form the concept of *what it is like to be a bat* because the concept would require acquaintance with bat-phenomenal features that human cognition cannot host. The Map reads this as a candidate categorial limit: not a concept we lack, but a categorial shape (bat-species phenomenal character) our apparatus does not include." This is Nagel's argument correctly placed inside a systematic taxonomy that gives the argument more work to do. `topics/animal-consciousness.md` now cross-references `commensurability-void` at the bat-phenomenology locus (deep-review at 21:17), which is the Nagelian move made rigorous — the voids framework hands Nagel a structural vocabulary his 1974 paper had to invent ad hoc.

**What centres subjective experience appropriately**: `voids/epistemic-horizon-void.md` lines 54-55 import Nagel's *The View from Nowhere* (1986) as the fourth convergent argument for the horizon claim, with an unusually sharp formulation: "Any vantage point from which one might survey all perspectives is itself a perspective. There is no meta-perspective." What is Nagelian here is not the argument itself (which Nagel made explicitly) but the *convergence structure* — the article places Nagel alongside Fitch (1963, formal), Rescher (2009, structural), and Dunning (2011, empirical) as four independent lines converging on the same horizon. Nagel has complained in print that phenomenological arguments are often dismissed as *merely* philosophical; the Map's convergence argument (line 56: "What matters for the Map is that the arguments do not share assumptions. Their convergence is stronger evidence for the void than any one of them alone") gives Nagel's perspectival argument an *evidential* role alongside formal and empirical arguments, which is exactly the rehabilitation of phenomenological evidence Nagel's later work has called for.

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)

Whitehead's *Process and Reality* (1929) insists that categorial schemes are themselves *propositions* — they are ways reality is prehended by actual occasions, not fixed features of a timeless logical space. Different occasions can prehend with different categorial structures; the categories are real but contingent. `voids/categorial-void.md` lines 116-119 now articulates this Whiteheadian reading explicitly through its choice to depart from Kant: "Kant held that the categorial apparatus he catalogued was *determinate* — fixed, universal, and identifiable through transcendental reflection. The void claim is weaker: whatever categorial apparatus we have, we cannot from within it verify its completeness, nor rule out that other architectures carve reality along axes we lack." The weaker claim is Whitehead's. And the article cites Heidegger (1927) on the distinction between *categories* (for non-conscious entities) and *existentials* (for conscious ones) — a distinction Whitehead's dipolar ontology parallels with the physical and mental poles of actual occasions. The suggestion at line 120 that "different kinds of thing may require different kinds of kind" is process philosophy's structural insight stated at the right level of generality.

**What avoids crude dualism while preserving mind's reality**: `voids/plenitude-void.md` line 109 now carries a remarkable paragraph on how dualism and materialism produce *different shapes* for the plenitude void rather than different answers to it: "For the materialist it is a cognitive-scientific puzzle about how an information-processing system produces the illusion of its own richness; for the dualist it is a primary phenomenal datum needing an account." This is Whiteheadian to the core — the same occasion is differently prehended within different categorial schemes, and the schemes themselves are what the disagreement is between. The article refuses the materialism-vs-dualism *verdict* question in favour of the *frame* question, which is how process philosophy handles most apparent metaphysical dichotomies. And it does the Whiteheadian thing of *placing the explanatory burden* rather than claiming the burden has been met: "The plenitude void does not by itself adjudicate between these frameworks; it illustrates that the choice of framework determines where the explanatory burden falls." Whitehead's *Process and Reality* Chapter II, Section I does exactly this with the physical-vs-mental pole question; the plenitude void applies the same methodology to phenomenal richness.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)

Kane's *The Significance of Free Will* (1996) argues that genuine agency requires a location where agents are *ultimate sources* of their actions. A regularly-voiced objection is that Kane's self-forming actions require a phenomenological account of *what it is like* to be in a moment of genuine undetermined choice — and that such phenomenology has not been compellingly given. The new `voids/thought-stream-void.md` lines 67-68 now provides exactly this phenomenology, and places it inside the noetic-feelings family: spontaneous thought arrives "without origin" — there is no phenomenology of generation, no sense of something building or being drafted. Kane can use this directly. His self-forming actions should have *exactly the phenomenology* the thought-stream void catalogues: products available to introspection, process hidden. "Arrival without origin" is what Kane's self-forming actions should look like from inside, because the process at which the self is constituting itself is not a process the already-constituted self can observe. The article cites Christoff et al. (2016) and the default mode network literature; Kane's programme, rarely paired with cognitive neuroscience, gains empirical allies here.

**What supports genuine agency and choice**: `voids/recognition-void.md` line 58 installs the Capgras-prosopagnosia double dissociation into the noetic-feelings framework with a precision Kane can use: "The affective component — the felt familiarity that Capgras strips away — is itself one member of the family of noetic feelings that gate cognition from inside." Kane has long needed an account of how the *felt* aspect of agency (the sense of genuine authorship) relates to the underlying mechanism — whether the feeling is a veridical report of agentic machinery or a post-hoc construction. The Recognition Void's dissociation argument (affective familiarity can be absent even when cognitive recognition succeeds) gives Kane a structural model: the agentic feel is *separable* from the agentic fact, and its presence is no guarantee of the fact, but the fact's presence without the feel is also possible. This is the dissociation structure Kane needs for his position to be testable at all — self-forming actions might occur without phenomenal confidence in them, and pseudo-self-forming feelings might occur in their absence. The recognition-void framework makes this asymmetry structural rather than stipulative.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)

McGinn's *The Problem of Consciousness* (1991) makes a strong claim: human cognition is *constitutionally closed* to the mind-body problem's solution, and this closure is a biological fact about our species. The common objection (Kriegel 2003, Vlerick-Boudry 2017) is that McGinn cannot demonstrate closure from within a closed system — the very thing he claims to show is unshowable. `voids/epistemic-horizon-void.md` lines 42-44 now rescues McGinn's position from this objection by *reformulating it as the horizon claim*: the weaker claim that we cannot estimate *the total scope* of what we do not know is logically and empirically supported (Fitch, Rescher, Dunning-Kruger, Nagel) in a way that McGinn's stronger closure claim is not. And the reformulation is clearly distinguished from global scepticism at lines 37-40: "we can know many things with reasonable warrant, we can identify specific limits with reasonable precision, but we cannot estimate the *proportion* of reality that our specific-limit catalogue covers. The horizon is local and structured, not a blanket denial of knowledge." This is the mysterian position at the right strength — strong enough to do philosophical work, weak enough not to refute itself.

**What shows appropriate epistemic humility**: `voids/epistemic-horizon-void.md` lines 64-74 ("Does the Map Inherit Its Own Horizon?") is the single most McGinnian passage the Map has produced. McGinn has repeatedly argued that the mark of a serious programme in consciousness studies is its willingness to apply its own closure thesis to *itself*. The Map now does exactly this: "The current voids section contains roughly 95 entries. There is no principled argument that this number approximates the total number of voids a mind like ours could in principle identify — much less the total number of voids a mind *unlike* ours might identify. The Map's own catalogue is produced from within the limited system whose limits it is trying to describe." And then the move that McGinn's programme requires but rarely delivers: the catalogue's *stabilisation* is treated as *suspicious* rather than as a success condition. Line 70: "If the catalogue stabilises, that stability is more likely evidence of cognitive fatigue than of completeness." McGinn's *Problem of Consciousness* Chapter 2 argues that a closed system cannot feel its closure; the Map here concedes that its own apparent completeness would itself be a closure symptom. This is the kind of sustained intellectual self-application that McGinn has called for in consciousness studies and that most programmes (including his own) have not delivered at this level of precision.
