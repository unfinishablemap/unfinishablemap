## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: The agent's role is licensed only by the quantum layer, but presented as licensed by the mainstream neuroscience (epistemic→metaphysical equivocation on "indeterminacy")

- **File**: `topics/motor-control-quantum-zeno.md`
- **Location**: "The Agency Question" (lines 94, 96) with "How the Brain Presents Options" (line 82) and "Challenges" (line 116)
- **Problem**: The agent-role is defined throughout as *resolving genuine physical indeterminacy*: line 82 "it resolves indeterminacy at the selection point"; line 94 "resolving genuine physical indeterminacy in a direction that reflects what the agent intends"; line 116 "leaving no residual indeterminacy for consciousness to resolve." The article then grounds this in Schurger's reinterpretation: line 96, "stochastic neural fluctuations provide the raw material — noise accumulating toward threshold — and the agent's contribution is biasing which fluctuations reach threshold and when." But Schurger's accumulator (like Cisek's affordance competition and the drift-diffusion models the article invokes) is a *classical* model. Its "noise" is thermal/synaptic fluctuation — epistemically unpredictable, but not metaphysically indeterminate. A classical stochastic accumulator is causally closed: the threshold-crossing is fixed by prior microstate plus noise realisation, with no open degree of freedom for a selector. The move from "the neuroscience describes stochastic fluctuation" (epistemic: we cannot predict which noise realisation occurs) to "there is genuine physical indeterminacy for the agent to resolve" (metaphysical: the outcome is objectively open) is precisely the interpretive equivocation flagged in `evidential-status-discipline`. The only thing that would actually convert classical noise into agent-resolvable indeterminacy is the quantum-Zeno layer — which the article itself (correctly) marks as a candidate, contested, and unestablished (line 106, 112). So the argumentative load silently shifts: the "playing field is more level" conclusion (line 57) and the "specific causal role" claim (line 94) are presented as underwritten by Schurger + Cisek + Desmurget, when in fact they are underwritten *only if* the reader also grants the quantum mechanism. Strip the quantum layer — which the article says the case can survive without (line 106) — and the classical neuroscience it falls back on is causally closed, leaving the agent nothing to do.
- **Severity**: High (this is the sharpest finding — it is orthogonal to the decoherence hedge, which is about whether the quantum mechanism is *feasible*, not about whether the classical fallback leaves any room at all)
- **Recommendation**: Route to a directed `refine-draft` (or targeted `deep-review`). The fix is a single honest bridging paragraph in "The Agency Question": distinguish classical (epistemic) unpredictability from genuine (metaphysical) indeterminacy, and state plainly that the agent-as-resolver role *requires* the physical substrate to supply real indeterminacy — which classical accumulator models do not, and which is exactly why the quantum layer is not optional decoration but the load-bearing premise. This actually *strengthens* the article's honesty (it already concedes RL/Bayesian control "explain much of motor selection," line 114) — it just needs to stop letting the "level playing field" conclusion draw on classical evidence that is causally closed against it.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett) on the Desmurget double dissociation
- **Current content says**: The parietal/premotor double dissociation (Desmurget 2009) "maps directly onto the framework" and, in Relation to Site Perspective (line 120), "supports irreducibility."
- **A critic would argue**: A dissociation between two *brain regions* is fully and unremarkably materialist. It shows that intention-representation and motor-execution are separable neural functions with distinct loci — nothing more. Pure physicalism predicts it identically. It provides zero differential evidence for a *non-physical* selector; "conscious intention has a neural locus in parietal cortex" is a claim materialism happily owns. Line 120's "supports irreducibility" is the overclaim: neural separability is not psychophysical irreducibility.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Suggested response**: Downgrade line 120 from "supports irreducibility" to something like "locates conscious intention and motor execution at distinct neural stages, consistent with (but not evidence for) an interface operating at the intention stage." The dissociation constrains *where* an interface would sit; it does not argue *that* one exists.

### The Quantum Skeptic on "observation without adding energy" as settled physics
- **Current content says**: Lines 82 and 102 — "Quantum observation shapes which state actualises without adding energy — it selects among existing possibilities," presented as a plain statement of how QM works (with the conservation-law defense cross-linked).
- **A critic would argue**: This borrows the respectability of physical measurement while denying the physical coupling that makes measurement work. In standard QM, the "observation" that produces a Zeno effect is a physical measurement interaction (apparatus coupling), and projection is not in general energy-conserving — a measurement can change the expectation value of the energy. More fundamentally, "observation" in physics is not *agent*-observation; Stapp's identification of the two is his idiosyncratic interpretive commitment, not a consequence of the formalism. Stating "quantum observation... without adding energy" flatly, in the article's own voice, presents Stapp's contested reading as textbook physics.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Suggested response**: Attribute the no-energy claim to Stapp's interpretation rather than to QM simpliciter ("On Stapp's reading, the relevant observation..."), and note that projection is not generically energy-conserving so the conservation-respecting story is itself part of what the interpretation must argue, not a free consequence.
