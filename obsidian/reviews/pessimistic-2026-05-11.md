## Executive Summary

The two restructures today are real improvements (clean direct-refutation-discipline conformance on the indexical/parsimony split; honest acknowledgment that the Hagan-Tegmark calculation is contested rather than settled), but they introduce or fail to close three structural defects: (1) **the relocation of the timing objection** — Tenet 2's lead position now reads as if "consciousness acts after decoherence" *solves* rather than *relocates* the mind-matter interface problem, and a Tegmark-class critic will reply that selecting from an improper mixture without any mechanism is not a smaller intervention than selecting before decoherence, just a less constrained one; (2) **Tenet 2 vs Tenet 5 unresolved parsimony asymmetry** — Tenet 2's *name* is a minimality principle, but Tenet 5 (just made symmetric on this page) holds that minimality is unreliable in domains of incomplete knowledge; the tenets page does not engage the tension its own restructure created; (3) **the falsifiability collapse** — Tenet 2's "Rules out" clause says the interaction is "any interaction that would be empirically detectable under current experimental precision," and the post-decoherence-selection move further insulates the framework by making the timing objection "irrelevant"; the framework is now actively defined to be undetectable, which is a Popperian red flag the page does not address. Plus two label-leakage / hygiene findings (two HTML-commented AI REFINEMENT LOGs containing editor-vocabulary remain in the file despite each one's "should be removed after human review" instruction) and one strawmanning issue (the epiphenomenalist response engages a self-undermining caricature rather than the phenomenal-concept-strategy version Frankish and Dennett actually hold).

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

The whole framing of Tenet 1 ("consciousness is not reducible to physical processes") begs the question. *Of course* it is not reducible *yet*; we have not done the reduction. You point to "the explanatory gap" as if it were a finding rather than a description of where the science currently is — the same intellectual move that vitalists made about life c. 1900. The list of "positive arguments for dualism" you cite (knowledge argument, conceivability, qualia-from-intrinsic-nature) are all *intuition pumps*, each contested for decades, each operating on conceptual material that mature neuroscience will not preserve. The 80 days since this section was last pessimistically reviewed have produced more neuroscience, not less; that direction of travel is itself evidence about which way the reduction is going. And the Tenet 2 restructure today makes this worse, not better: now consciousness is so undetectable that it acts *after* the physics has finished, on outcomes the physics has already determined statistically. That is not minimal interaction — that is interaction without an interaction.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

The Bidirectional Interaction section deploys the standard anti-epiphenomenalist move: "if consciousness were epiphenomenal, our reports about qualia would be causally disconnected from qualia, so the position is self-undermining." But this engages the *weakest* version of the position. The phenomenal-concept strategy — Frankish 2016, my own work, Papineau, Loar — handles exactly this: our reports about phenomenal states are produced by physical-physical causal chains, and those chains track phenomenal states because phenomenal concepts are themselves *physically realised representational states that refer to physical states of the same brain*. The reports are reliable not because qualia cause them but because the physical states that produce the reports *are* the states the reports are about. Your "self-undermining" charge dissolves when the phenomenal-concept strategy is on the table. The fact that the tenets page does not name Frankish or the phenomenal-concept strategy in its Tenet 1 or Tenet 3 sections is a load-bearing omission — the convergent outer reviews from 2026-05-11 flagged this for `arguments/materialism-argument.md` (now P2 task) but the same omission lives at the *tenets* level and needs to be addressed *here*, since this is where the position is foundationally stated. The label "self-undermining" is also slightly question-begging: it presupposes that introspection-grounded reasoning is *legitimate* in a way that already commits you against eliminativism.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Max Tegmark)

The honest move today (presenting Hagan et al. 2002 as "one estimate in a contested literature, not a refutation Tegmark has accepted") is welcome and overdue. But the bigger move — leading Tenet 2 with [[apex/post-decoherence-selection-programme]] as the Map's "most developed position" — does not escape my objection, it *relocates* it. Let me be specific. After decoherence has run to completion, the system is in an improper mixture. The Schlosshauer quote you use ("Decoherence does not tell how and why only one of these outcomes is measured") is accurate. But the move from "decoherence does not solve the measurement problem" to "consciousness selects after decoherence" is not a small step. The Born statistics constrain the *distribution* of outcomes; they do not constrain individual outcomes. So when you say "consciousness biases individual outcomes without altering aggregate distributions" (your apex-page phrasing, which Tenet 2 now points to), you are saying consciousness chooses *between Born-equiprobable alternatives*. But Born-equiprobable alternatives are alternatives the physics has already declared *interchangeable in every measurable respect*. There is no physical fact about which alternative is chosen — that is just what "indeterminate" means. So the "selection" is selecting between things the physics says are not differentiable. Either (a) the selection has empirical consequences, in which case it should alter aggregate distributions and Born statistics should fail, or (b) the selection has no empirical consequences, in which case calling it "selection" is metaphor. The decoherence-timescale objection that Hagan and I have been arguing about for twenty years was at least about *something physical*; the post-decoherence-selection move makes the question *unanswerable*, which is worse. The Tenet 2 restructure today should at minimum acknowledge that the cost of escaping the timing objection is making the mechanism undetectable in principle.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

The split today between indexical (primary) and ontological-multiplicity (subsidiary) is methodologically honest — I will give you that. But the indexical objection itself is confused, and the way the page now leans entirely on it makes the confusion load-bearing rather than incidental. "Why am I *this* branch rather than any of the others?" is the wrong question. There is no "I" that exists prior to a branch and gets assigned to one. *Every* branch has an "I" that asks "why am I this one?" and the answer is the same in each: because you are the one in this branch. The question is no more meaningful than asking "why am I in this moment of time rather than another?" or "why am I this person rather than another?" — both of which we treat as confused indexical questions rather than metaphysical scandals. Sebens-Carroll (2018) and Saunders-Wallace's branch-counting strategies provide an MWI-internal Bayesian account of self-locating uncertainty that the tenets page does not engage by name; the new merged P1 task in todo.md flags Sebens-Carroll for the arguments page, but the *tenet* still bottoms out on "the framework provides no resources to resolve this." That is just not true. The framework provides exactly the resources the framework should provide: a measure over branches that makes Born statistics fall out naturally if you accept self-locating uncertainty. You can reject the resolution, but the tenets page presents it as if the framework had no answer. Demoting ontological-multiplicity to subsidiary while inflating indexical to load-bearing means the *load-bearing* argument is the *weaker* one. The structural integrity of Tenet 4 has gone down today, not up.

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

This is the question I want answered directly. Tenet 2's "Rules out" clause says: "any interaction that would be empirically detectable under current experimental precision." Tenet 2's restructure today moves the Map's most-endorsed mechanism to *after* decoherence, where the apex page (line 78) commits the framework to "respect Born-rule statistics over the long run" and "consciousness biases individual outcomes without altering aggregate distributions." Combine these: the interaction (a) cannot be detected by current experiments, (b) cannot alter aggregate distributions, and (c) cannot violate conservation laws. The Tenet 2 update today, by leading with the post-decoherence path, has further insulated the framework: the timing-gap objection is now "irrelevant" to the most-developed Map position (apex page). What experiment could prove Tenet 2 wrong? The answer, under the current page, is: none. That is the textbook unfalsifiability problem. The honest move — which the page does not make — is to add a sub-section to Tenet 2 acknowledging that the post-decoherence-selection framing trades empirical risk for explanatory completeness, and to identify *some* observational signature the framework would predict (even a future-tech one) that materialism would not. Without such a signature the Tenet is metaphysics in physics drag.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

Tenet 4's indexical objection rests on a robust notion of "I" — "*me* rather than *any other*" — that has to exist independently of branches in order for the question to have content. But this is exactly the substantialist conception of self that the Madhyamaka tradition has been deconstructing for two millennia. The "I" in "why am I *this* one" is not a self-existing thing whose branch-assignment is mysterious; it is an aggregate of conditions that includes the branch. Asking which branch "got" the "I" presupposes a separable "I" that could have got any branch. Many-worlds is awkward for Western substantialism precisely *because* Western substantialism has not yet noticed that the self is conventional. The Map's most foundational tenet — load-bearing argument for rejecting MWI — assumes a metaphysical thesis about personal identity (the indexical thesis is *meaningful*) that is itself a tenet, not a finding. Tenet 5 (parsimony unreliable) should bind here too: how do we know our intuition that "why am I this one" is meaningful is not the same kind of folk-metaphysical reflex that "why does the falling stone want the ground" was? The page does not consider whether the indexical thesis itself is the kind of substantive claim it claims many-worlds *fails to provide resources for*.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Tenet 2: post-decoherence-selection escapes the timing objection
- **Current content says**: "If consciousness acts *after* decoherence — selecting which element of the already-decohered mixture becomes actual — then the decoherence timescale is irrelevant" (apex page, line 52, now pointed to from Tenet 2)
- **A critic would argue**: Tegmark's objection was about *physical* coherence-timescale. The post-decoherence version sidesteps it but creates a new problem: selecting from Born-equiprobable alternatives is selecting between things the physics declares physically indifferent. The "selection" either has detectable consequences (in which case Born statistics should fail and Tenet 2's "Rules out" is violated) or is empirically inert (in which case calling it selection is metaphor). The timing objection has been swapped for an *indifference* objection that is harder, not easier, to answer.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge the trade-off in Tenet 2 itself (see Issue 1). The honest Map position is that the indifference objection points to where the framework's empirical risk has been concentrated; it is not dissolved by the restructure. Reference [[amplification-void]] and the amplification programme as the location where any future empirical signature would appear.

### Tenet 4: indexical objection is the load-bearing argument
- **Current content says**: "Many-worlds raises a question its own framework cannot answer: why am I *this* branch rather than any of the others? ... The framework provides no resources to resolve this." (line 87)
- **A critic would argue**: This is false; the framework does provide resources. Sebens-Carroll (2018) self-locating uncertainty gives a Bayesian account whereby Born statistics fall out of branch measure as the rational credence for an observer with self-locating uncertainty. Saunders-Wallace branch-counting gives an alternative within decision-theoretic MWI. The claim "framework provides no resources" is a factual claim and it is wrong. You may *reject* these resolutions on independent grounds, but the page asserts non-existence where the literature provides multiple candidates.
- **Suggested response**: Soften the load-bearing claim from "framework provides no resources to resolve this" to "framework's resolutions (Sebens-Carroll self-locating uncertainty, Saunders-Wallace branch-counting) require accepting a deflationary account of indexical identity that the Map's commitments do not permit." This changes the disagreement from "MWI cannot answer" to "MWI's answers presuppose what the Map disputes" — a direct-refutation-discipline upgrade from boundary-substitution to in-framework engagement with explicit pointer to the deeper commitment. The merged P1 task already pulls Sebens-Carroll into the arguments page; surfacing the name at the tenet level closes the load-bearing-claim accuracy gap.

## Strengths (Brief)

The restructures today are substantively good moves and should be preserved during the revisions above:

- **The Tenet 4 indexical/parsimony split** is a clean direct-refutation-discipline upgrade — it admits that the ontological-multiplicity objection was leaning on parsimony in a way Tenet 5 doesn't permit, and demotes it explicitly. That is honest and structurally cleaner than the prior version. Issue 2 above flags that Tenet 2 needs the same treatment, but the *form* of the Tenet 4 fix is exemplary.

- **The Tenet 2 Hagan/Tegmark downgrade** ("calculation is contested rather than settled") is a calibration-discipline win. The previous "seven orders of magnitude longer" framing was partisan; the new "estimates ranging across roughly 10⁻¹³ to 10⁻⁴ seconds depending on disputed model parameters; the literature has not converged" is what an honest survey would say.

- **The precedent-vs-licence distinction** on biological quantum coherence (cryptochrome) is exactly the right move. Cryptochrome at microseconds does not by itself license neural-microtubule coherence at milliseconds; calling it precedent rather than licence is the calibrated framing.

- **Tenet 5's "Application to the Map's own arguments"** paragraph is a genuinely useful methodological move that binds parsimony symmetrically. It catches a class of errors the Map was previously vulnerable to. Issue 2 above expands the scope it should bind (Tenet 2 also), but the *principle* is now correctly stated.

- **The Tenet 4 indexical objection itself**, even granting the Many-Worlds Defender's response above, is the strongest available critique of MWI from a position that holds personal identity is meaningful. The Map's commitment to indexical-meaningfulness is itself a tenet-level claim, and once that claim is made, the indexical objection follows. The issue is not that the argument is wrong but that the page should acknowledge what the argument presupposes.

The five Critical Issues and four Counterarguments above are all *upgrades* to a page that is substantively in the right region — they are not requests to undo today's restructures, but to close gaps the restructures created or did not address.
