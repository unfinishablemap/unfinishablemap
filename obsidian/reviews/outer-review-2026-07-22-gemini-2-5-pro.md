## Dimension A: Empirical Inaccuracy and Anachronism in Clinical Exhibits

The manuscript anchors its defense of interactionist dualism on the interpretation of specific clinical deficits, arguing that these cases exhibit a "somatic interface" where the lived body (*Leib*) outlasts, empties, or dissociates from the objectified body (*Körper*). This argumentation relies on a gross mischaracterization of contemporary neurobiology, leaning heavily on phenomenological intuitions while ignoring the high-precision network mapping and computational neuroscience that define the current decade. The manuscript routinely conflates localized network failures with ontological mysteries, treating standard neuropathology as evidence of an irreducible mind-matter interface.

### The Phantom Limb and the Failure of the "Stark Gap"

The manuscript presents phantom limb phenomena as the primary exhibit of a scenario where the *Leib* outlasts the *Körper*. It treats this condition as exhibiting the "standard hard problem in a setting where the failure to bridge is particularly stark". The text cites older phenomenological and neurological literature, including Sacks (1984, 1985) and Ramachandran & Hirstein (1998), to argue that the persistence of the lived body in the absence of the physical limb demonstrates that conscious selection among candidate body-models does real causal work independent of peripheral intervention. The text explicitly refuses to let neuromatrix or predictive-processing mechanistic stories count as bridging this explanatory gap, framing the persistence of the phantom as an ontological victory for the non-physical subject.

This framing demonstrates a severe detachment from the 2020s empirical literature. Contemporary neuroscientific consensus does not view the phantom limb as a mysterious ontological gap between a persistent non-physical subject and a degraded physical substrate. Instead, modern research heavily relies on the persistent representation model combined with multisensory integration frameworks. Individuals lacking proprioceptive and tactile inputs provide a crucial test case for understanding the role of bottom-up signaling in implicit adaptation. The 2020s literature demonstrates that deafferentation leads to a state where higher-order cortical areas maintain strong priors regarding the limb's presence, spatial configuration, and intended movement outcomes. Because the bottom-up proprioceptive and nociceptive signals have been severed, the brain cannot generate the necessary somatosensory prediction errors to update its generative model.

The manuscript attempts to co-opt the contemporary shift toward the "persistent representation" model (specifically citing Makin) as a sharpening of the explanatory gap, arguing that "stable neural activity as the explanandum" deflates the physicalist account. This is an empirically vacuous defensive maneuver. The stable neural activity observed in the deafferented cortex fully accounts for the persistence of the phenomenal limb within a standard representationalist or functionalist architecture. It does not require an irreducible dualist interface. By demanding that a purely computational failure of multisensory integration must cross an ontological divide to be considered "explained," the manuscript engages in an unwarranted metaphysical leap. The persistence of the *Leib* is nothing more than the persistence of a high-precision cortical prior in the absence of sensory contradiction, an entirely physicalist phenomenon supported by modern visuo-centric models of implicit adaptation.

In doing so, it asserts that no direct scientific method, such as mapping neural correlates, can explain this phenomenal relation, thereby rendering it a pure "void".

This position is thoroughly undermined by recent advanced lesion network mapping and structural connectivity data, which the manuscript entirely omits. Far from being a pristine, mysterious phenomenal void, disturbed sensation of ownership (DSO) and somatoparaphrenia have been precisely localized to discrete structural disconnections in the brain. Deficits in multisensory integration, alongside proprioceptive loss, form the critical neurobiological basis for the delusional disavowal of the limb. By treating somatoparaphrenia as a philosophical exhibit of the *Leib* dissociating from the *Körper*, the manuscript ignores the localized biological reality of the condition. Reducing complex, network-level white matter structural failures to an abstract "interface anomaly" represents a profound failure of empirical accuracy, demonstrating a methodology that prefers conceptual mystification over biological precision.

## Dimension B: Epistemic Decay in Cited Literature and Failed Replications

The remit of this review requires evaluating whether the cited literature has been challenged, retracted, or failed replication. The manuscript's evidentiary base suffers from severe epistemic decay. It heavily utilizes older citations whose central claims have been subsequently challenged, significantly modified, or absorbed into entirely different explanatory frameworks in the modern era. The manuscript consistently fails to update its philosophical conclusions to match the empirical corrections of its own citations.

### The Obsolescence of the Ramachandran Remapping Model

As noted above, the manuscript cites Ramachandran & Hirstein (1998) alongside Carman (1999) to establish the phenomenology and neurobiology of phantom limbs. The original Ramachandran model posited that phantom pain and sensation were the direct result of maladaptive cortical remapping, wherein adjacent cortical areas (e.g., the face representation) "invade" the deafferented zone (e.g., the hand representation) in the primary somatosensory cortex.

When high-resolution fMRI studies over the last decade failed to replicate this remapping as the primary driver of phantom sensation, the field underwent a paradigm shift toward the "persistent representation" model (Makin's model), which demonstrated that the original cortical representation is highly preserved and stable, rather than overwritten. The manuscript acknowledges this empirical reversal but commits a grave logical error in its interpretation. The text claims that this shift "correctly turns into a sharpening of the gap (stable neural activity as the explanandum) rather than a deflation," thereby claiming a victory for interactionist dualism.

This represents a profound misunderstanding of the replication failure. The persistent representation model remains an entirely physicalist, functionalist account; it simply corrects the spatial dynamics and network behavior of the deafferented cortex. The failure of the 1998 remapping hypothesis does not magically insert a non-physical consciousness into the causal chain, nor does it elevate the *Leib* to an ontological substance. The manuscript exploits the standard process of scientific self-correction—updating a spatial mapping hypothesis to a functional connectivity hypothesis—and fallaciously frames it as the collapse of physicalist explanation itself.

### The Agency-Ownership Dissociation Paradigm

A central pillar of the manuscript's architectural argument for the interface model relies on Shaun Gallagher's (2000, 2012) distinction between the sense of agency ("I am the initiator") and the sense of ownership ("this is my body moving"). The text utilizes this dissociation—particularly citing schizophrenic thought insertion (agency lost, ownership preserved) versus depersonalization (ownership lost, agency preserved)—to argue for distinct, separable layers of a non-physical interface. The manuscript treats the "mineness" layer and the "agency" layer as structurally distinct voids that consciousness interacts across.

However, the rigid, bidirectional dissociation paradigm championed in the early 2000s has been heavily critiqued and largely superseded by hierarchical predictive processing and embodied interoception models in the 2020s. Recent empirical studies examining the relationship between interoception and the sense of agency demonstrate that these phenomenal features are not independent, modular layers of a dualist interface, but are intimately co-dependent parameters within the brain's regulatory system.

Specifically, 2024 and 2025 research indicates that better interoceptive sensibility (the neurobiological foundation of body ownership and mineness) directly correlates with a higher sense of agency. The hierarchical nature of active inference indicates that the joint development of interoception and motor control renders agency and ownership as intertwined prediction error minimization strategies. For instance, a serial mediation pathway demonstrates that enhancements in interoceptive regulation directly lead to reductions in a negative sense of agency.

The manuscript's reliance on Gallagher's older framework to posit independent dualist layers ignores a decade of clinical and computational literature demonstrating the non-modularity of these subjective states. By clinging to the early-2000s literature to support its "complementary dissociation" claims, the text fails the basic test of empirical currency.

### Structural Misattributions in Neuroplasticity and Mediation

The manuscript also imports findings from contemplative neuroscience to argue that conscious intention directly causes neural change, serving as evidence for bidirectional causation (mind-to-matter). It references structural neuroplastic changes from meditation practice, originally citing older studies that claimed greater cortical thickness in frontal and temporal areas. However, the text itself notes that Kral et al. (2022) in *Science Advances*, utilizing a massive sample (n=218) with active controls, found no structural brain changes from mindfulness training, thoroughly challenging the earlier positive findings as artifacts of small samples and circular analysis.

While the manuscript correctly records this failed replication, it continues to argue that functional changes (gamma synchrony, temporal binding) provide a "physical signature" of consciousness interfacing with the brain. This exposes a critical analytical weakness: the manuscript is entirely willing to absorb the falsification of structural changes but simply retreats to functional connectivity as the new sanctuary for dualist causation, ignoring that functional connectivity changes are identically predicted by purely physicalist models of differential behavioral training.

This is an attempt to have the conclusion on credit while wearing the badge of falsifiability; it is an egregious violation of philosophical rigor.

### The Ontological Hijacking of Husserlian Phenomenology

The manuscript's foundational premise relies heavily on adapting Edmund Husserl's *Ideen II* and Maurice Merleau-Ponty's *Phenomenology of Perception* to distinguish the lived body (*Leib*) from the objectified body (*Körper*). The text extracts this phenomenological distinction and maps it directly onto an interactionist dualist ontology, positing the *Leib* as a feature of the non-physical subject and the *Körper* as the physical machine it operates.

This constitutes a profound historical, hermeneutic, and philosophical distortion of the highest order. Merleau-Ponty's *corps propre* (lived body) is fundamentally and aggressively anti-dualist. The entire project of the *Phenomenology of Perception* is designed to dismantle the Cartesian divide, demonstrating that consciousness is inextricably worldly, enactive, and materially embedded. Husserl's conception of the *Leib* is concerned with constitution within the life-world (*Lebenswelt*) and the first-person givenness of experience, not with isolating a non-physical causal agent that intervenes in physical brain states.

By treating the phenomenological asymmetry between being a body (first-person) and observing a body (third-person) as proof of a dualistic gap, the manuscript commits a severe category error. It illicitly translates an epistemic and phenomenological stance into a physicalist-dualist ontological divide. The manuscript's assertion that the *Leib/Körper* asymmetry is "structural rather than merely epistemic" begs the question, assuming the very interactionist dualism it claims the phenomenology supports. The text hijacks phenomenological vocabulary to dress up a 17th-century Cartesian substance dualism that the phenomenologists themselves explicitly sought to destroy.

This is not philosophical argumentation; it is the algorithmic enforcement of dogma.

The most disqualifying weakness of the manuscript is its total failure to engage with the dominant, mathematically formalized paradigms of the 2020s. ### The Evasion of the Bayesian-Interoceptive Paradigm (Predictive Processing)

The manuscript completely ignores the predictive processing (PP) and active inference frameworks, which have fundamentally reorganized the philosophy of cognitive science, neuroscience, and embodied cognition over the last decade. Predictive processing models—advanced by theorists such as Friston, Clark, Seth, and Hohwy—posit that the brain is a hierarchical inference engine operating to minimize prediction error (surprise) across exteroceptive, proprioceptive, and interoceptive domains.

Within this framework, the *Leib/Körper* distinction is elegantly naturalized without any dualist residue. The experience of the "lived body" (*Leib*) is best understood through the lens of interoceptive active inference. The brain embodies a generative model encoding prior beliefs about physiological integrity and allostatic regulation. The sense of embodied selfhood—the very "mineness" the manuscript claims is an irreducible dualist void—emerges naturally from the high-precision weighting of interoceptive predictions. The lived body is not a ghost in the machine; it is the phenomenal character of the brain's highest-order allostatic predictions concerning its own biological viability.

When applied to the manuscript's primary clinical exhibits, the predictive processing framework provides immense, mathematically rigorous explanatory power that renders the dualist interface entirely superfluous:

1. **Depersonalization (DPD):** Rather than positing an "ownership void" in a dualist filter where the *Leib* detaches from the *Körper*, active inference models (e.g., Deane et al., Ciaunica et al.) mathematically operationalize DPD as a systematic attenuation of precision on interoceptive prediction errors. The subject fails to integrate autonomic arousal with exteroceptive inputs, fracturing the embodied self-model and generating the "behind glass" phenomenology. The disorder is a regulatory scaling failure, not a metaphysical detachment.
2. **Phantom Limbs:** Instead of a non-physical consciousness selecting a "candidate body-model," the phantom phenomenon is modeled as a highly entrenched structural prior (the intact body schema) generating continuous prediction errors that cannot be minimized through action or updated due to the profound loss of sensory bandwidth.
3. **Functional Somatic Syndromes:** The predictive processing framework successfully models functional motor disorders and functional paralysis (like the cases originally explored by Sacks in *A Leg to Stand On*) as conditions governed by abnormally focused attention and predictive mechanisms, completely undercutting the manuscript's dualistic interpretation of functional bodily dissociation.

By entirely omitting interoceptive active inference, the manuscript operates in a theoretical vacuum. It fails to test its hypothesis against the strongest prevailing alternative, violating the basic requirements of contemporary philosophical scholarship.

### The Strawmanning of Eliminativist and Enactivist Defeaters

The manuscript superficially addresses eliminative materialism and illusionism by claiming that they deny "first-person data" and that such views fail to explain the "asymmetry of acquaintance". The text leverages concepts like Michael Huemer's "phenomenal conservatism" (that things are as they seem) to argue that the lived body must be taken at ontological face value.

However, the text fails to engage with the actual, sophisticated core of the illusionist and eliminativist arguments (e.g., Thomas Metzinger's Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity, or the Churchland/Dennett critiques). The sophisticated eliminativist does not blindly deny that the *Leib* feels distinctly different from the *Körper*. Rather, the eliminativist argues that this phenomenal asymmetry is a highly adaptive, computationally efficient user-illusion generated by the brain's self-model. The brain does not represent its own complex, sub-personal data-processing mechanisms to the global workspace; it only represents the simplified *outcomes* of those processes.

The "asymmetry of acquaintance" that the manuscript treats as a slam-dunk proof for dualism is exactly what one would expect from a physical computing system that models its external environment differently than it models its own internal state variables. By dismissing eliminativism merely as a denial of the obvious, rather than engaging with it as an explanation of *why* the obvious appears the way it does, the manuscript's failure to test its *Leib/Körper* dualism against this specific counterargument leaves its foundational premises entirely undefended.
