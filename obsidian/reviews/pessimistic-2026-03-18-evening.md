# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-03-18
**Content reviewed**: topics/non-retrocausal-conscious-selection-models.md, concepts/asymmetric-bandwidth-consciousness.md

## Executive Summary

Two brand-new AI-generated articles published today present reasonably well-structured arguments but contain significant vulnerabilities: the non-retrocausal selection article overstates the strength of contested empirical claims about macroscopic brain quantum effects and quietly treats three very different frameworks as converging when their metaphysical commitments diverge sharply; the asymmetric bandwidth article makes a sophisticated-sounding but ultimately fallacious inference from a throughput asymmetry to dualism, confusing information-theoretic bottlenecks with ontological boundaries.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

**On non-retrocausal selection**: The article presents "consciousness selects among superposed neural states" as though "consciousness" were a well-defined causal agent. But this is precisely the folk-psychological vocabulary that neuroscience is replacing. The article never specifies what consciousness *is* in a way that could be tested — it treats it as a primitive, which is exactly the pre-scientific move eliminativism warns against. "Consciousness modulates collapse" is a sentence with no empirical content until you can say what physical difference the modulation makes and how to measure it.

**On asymmetric bandwidth**: The 10 bits/second figure is a measurement of *cognitive throughput*, not evidence for a non-physical bottleneck. Calling this an "interface" between consciousness and the brain begs the question entirely. Neuroscience explains serial attention bottlenecks through prefrontal gating, global workspace dynamics, and competitive selection — all physical mechanisms. The article dismisses "production models" without engaging with any specific production model's account of serial processing.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

**On non-retrocausal selection**: The article conflates three frameworks (Orch OR, Stapp, Chalmers-McQueen) that have fundamentally different commitments. Penrose-Hameroff invoke new physics (gravitational collapse); Stapp uses orthodox QM but introduces consciousness as an external operator; Chalmers-McQueen graft consciousness onto CSL. Saying they "converge on the same temporal direction" makes them sound like a unified programme when they actually disagree about almost everything except temporal direction. This rhetorical move inflates the apparent weight of evidence.

**On asymmetric bandwidth**: The claim that "production models struggle" with the asymmetry is asserted without engaging with how production models actually handle it. Global workspace theory, for instance, explains exactly why conscious access has a bottleneck: the workspace is a serial broadcast channel selecting among massively parallel unconscious processors. The bottleneck *is* the production mechanism. The article presents a genuine phenomenon but interprets it tendentiously.

### The Quantum Skeptic

**On non-retrocausal selection**: The article's treatment of decoherence is its weakest section. Citing Hagan et al. (2002) for "seven orders of magnitude longer" decoherence times without noting that their revised estimates remain deeply contested is misleading. The 2025 *Frontiers* study claiming 10¹¹ glutamate molecules in coherence domains is presented without noting that this is a single unreplicated study making an extraordinary claim. "The trajectory is toward plausibility rather than away from it" is wishful editorialising disguised as a trend assessment.

The article says Stapp's model "sidesteps the decoherence objection entirely" because each measurement is instantaneous. But this misunderstands the objection: the question is what the state of the system *is* between measurements. If the system has already decohered into a classical mixture, there is no superposition for the Zeno effect to stabilise.

**On asymmetric bandwidth**: No quantum issues here, but the article's suggestion that evolution "cannot widen a channel whose narrow end is non-physical" is a non-explanation dressed as an explanation. It explains the mystery by postulating a bigger mystery.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

**On non-retrocausal selection**: All three frameworks depend on collapse being real and physically meaningful. Under Everettian quantum mechanics, "collapse" is apparent — decoherence produces branching, and all outcomes occur. The article's entire argument structure presupposes what it needs to prove: that collapse is real. It even acknowledges "both require real collapse" without recognising this as a vulnerability. If collapse is not a real physical process — as many physicists now maintain — the entire article addresses a non-problem.

**On asymmetric bandwidth**: The MWI has nothing specific to say about bandwidth asymmetry, but it removes the need for "conscious selection" entirely. In all branches, the brain does what it does; the bandwidth limitation is a feature of neural architecture, not an interface with a non-physical selector.

### The Empiricist

**On non-retrocausal selection**: What experiment distinguishes non-retrocausal conscious selection from ordinary quantum mechanics with objective collapse? If consciousness "biases" collapse, by how much? The article provides no quantitative prediction. "Consciousness modulates collapse in neural systems" generates no testable hypothesis. The Orch OR predictions about anaesthesia-induced collapse changes have not been confirmed. Until one of these frameworks makes a prediction that differs from standard QM — and that prediction is tested — this is metaphysics, not science.

**On asymmetric bandwidth**: The 10 bits/second finding is genuinely interesting empirical science (Zheng & Meister 2025). But the *interpretation* — that this reflects a non-physical interface — is not empirically testable. What observation would distinguish "neural bottleneck at conscious access" from "non-physical interface with limited bandwidth"? If no observation could distinguish them, the dualist interpretation adds no empirical content.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

**On non-retrocausal selection**: The article assumes a unified consciousness that "selects" — a homunculus by another name. But who is doing the selecting? If consciousness selects neural patterns, what selects consciousness's selections? The article addresses the "selection criterion problem" as an open question but does not recognise it as potentially fatal: without a non-arbitrary selection criterion, "consciousness selects" is explanatorily vacuous. The Buddhist perspective questions whether the unified agent presupposed by "selection" exists at all.

**On asymmetric bandwidth**: The article reifies consciousness as an entity with a "channel" that has measurable "bandwidth." This treats consciousness as a thing — a substance with properties — rather than examining whether the concept of a unified conscious agent with fixed throughput survives scrutiny. The asymmetry might instead reveal that the concept of a unified "conscious output" is a construction, not a discovery.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Uncritical Treatment of Contested Empirical Claims
- **File**: topics/non-retrocausal-conscious-selection-models.md
- **Location**: "The Macroscopic Superposition Question" section
- **Problem**: The 2025 *Frontiers* study claiming 10¹¹ glutamate molecules in coherence domains is cited as though it were part of a clear empirical trend. This is an extraordinary and unreplicated claim. The article says "none of this constitutes proof" but the preceding three paragraphs build a cumulative case that gives the opposite impression.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Add explicit caveats about replication status and note that the broader physics community has not accepted these claims. Use language like "if confirmed" more prominently.

### Issue 2: False Convergence of Three Incompatible Frameworks
- **File**: topics/non-retrocausal-conscious-selection-models.md
- **Location**: Opening paragraph and "Three Frameworks" section
- **Problem**: Orch OR, Stapp, and Chalmers-McQueen are presented as a unified "family of models" when they disagree on fundamental questions: Is new physics needed? Is consciousness within or outside the quantum formalism? Does collapse require a threshold? The word "converge" in the opening paragraph creates a misleading impression of scientific consensus.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge explicitly that these frameworks share temporal direction but disagree on almost everything else. Replace "converge" with a more accurate term like "agree on one point."

### Issue 3: Stapp's Zeno Model and Decoherence
- **File**: topics/non-retrocausal-conscious-selection-models.md
- **Location**: "Quantum Zeno Selection" subsection
- **Problem**: The claim that Stapp's model "sidesteps the decoherence objection entirely" is too strong. If decoherence has already converted a superposition into a classical mixture, the Zeno effect cannot stabilise a superposition that no longer exists. The article's reasoning conflates "each measurement is instantaneous" with "decoherence is irrelevant."
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge that Stapp's Zeno approach requires that the measurement frequency exceeds the decoherence rate — which brings the decoherence timescale question back into play.

### Issue 4: Production Models Dismissed Without Engagement
- **File**: concepts/asymmetric-bandwidth-consciousness.md
- **Location**: "It Constrains Interface Models" subsection
- **Problem**: The claim that "production models struggle" is made without engaging with any specific production model. Global workspace theory, higher-order theories, and attention schema theory all predict serial conscious access bottlenecks from physical mechanisms. The article asserts the asymmetry is "unexplained" by production models without showing it has tried to find explanations.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Engage with at least one specific production model's account of the bottleneck (e.g., global workspace theory's serial broadcast architecture) and explain why the Map finds it insufficient.

### Issue 5: Evolutionary Argument is Circular
- **File**: concepts/asymmetric-bandwidth-consciousness.md
- **Location**: "The Evolutionary Puzzle" section
- **Problem**: The argument that evolution cannot widen the outbound channel because "its narrow end is non-physical" presupposes dualism to explain a phenomenon that is then cited as evidence for dualism. This is circular. The article presents the evolutionary puzzle as evidence for the Map's framework, but the "structural explanation" only works if you already accept the framework.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge the circularity explicitly. Present the evolutionary argument as *consistent with* the Map's framework rather than as independent evidence *for* it.

### Issue 6: Epiphenomenalism Argument Overstated
- **File**: concepts/asymmetric-bandwidth-consciousness.md
- **Location**: "It Challenges Epiphenomenalism" subsection
- **Problem**: The argument that epiphenomenal consciousness would produce "zero bits per second" in the outbound channel and therefore no consistent throughput ceiling is flawed. An epiphenomenalist would say the physical brain produces the throughput ceiling and consciousness merely accompanies (without causing) the processing. The ceiling would exist regardless — it's a property of the neural system, not evidence of conscious work.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Rephrase to acknowledge the epiphenomenalist counter: the physical system alone could produce the bottleneck. The argument against epiphenomenalism needs to be about why conscious access specifically — rather than some other neural process — is the bottleneck point.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Non-Retrocausal Selection: The Combination Problem
- **Current content says**: The combination problem is noted as an open question in the "What Non-Retrocausal Selection Does Not Resolve" section.
- **A critic would argue**: This isn't just an open question — it may be a fatal objection. If individual microtubule collapses are the locus of conscious selection, there is no known mechanism for combining thousands of independent selections into a unified decision. The article treats this as a side issue when it may be the central challenge.
- **Suggested response**: Elevate this concern's prominence and note that Orch OR's proposed "orchestration" is precisely an attempt to address this, though its success is debated.

### Asymmetric Bandwidth: The Bottleneck Location
- **Current content says**: The bottleneck is at "conscious access" specifically.
- **A critic would argue**: The bottleneck might not be at conscious access per se. It could be at prefrontal executive control, working memory capacity, or motor planning — all physical systems. Zheng and Meister's paper itself attributes the limit to neural architecture, not to consciousness.
- **Suggested response**: Engage with Zheng and Meister's own interpretation and explain why the Map reads the data differently.

The treatment of the Chalmers-McQueen super-resistance problem is clear and technically accurate. The core distinction between retrocausal and non-retrocausal selection is well-drawn and genuinely useful for readers navigating this space.

**Asymmetric bandwidth article**: The opening paragraph is an exemplary front-loaded summary — the key claim, the key number, and the key interpretation all survive truncation. The Zheng & Meister (2025) citation is a strong empirical anchor. The distinction between "experience delivery" and "volitional selection" as fundamentally different operations (rather than the same operation at different speeds) is a genuinely interesting framing that adds value beyond standard bandwidth discussions.
