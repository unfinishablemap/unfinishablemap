## The Apparent Paradox

The cross-review brief asked whether `atemporal-causation` and `phenomenology-of-memory-and-the-self` make incompatible claims about the past. Surface tension can be stated as follows:

- *Atemporal-causation* says consciousness atemporally selects which transaction completes; the readiness potential preceding awareness is "*part of* what gets selected"; the selection determines "the whole correlated history at once."
- *Phenomenology-of-memory* treats pastness as a phenomenal quale, treats the past self as encountered through autonoetic recall, and rests its No-Many-Worlds inference on "metaphysical singularity of history."

The seeming worry: if consciousness *now* atemporally selects what *was* the case, isn't the past in some sense "constructed" by present consciousness — and doesn't that conflict with memory's phenomenology of *encountering* a determinate past?

## Resolution

The two articles converge once the metaphysical structure is laid out:

1. **Atemporal selection is not present-time construction of past content.** Atemporal causation operates outside temporal sequence; when a transaction completes, the entire correlated history (including its past portions) is determined together. The selecting activity is not located *at* the present moment of remembering.

2. **Once a transaction completes, the past portion is determinate within the modified growing block.** The "Modified Growing Block" section makes this explicit: possibilities have ontological status, transactions complete, the block grows, possibilities become actual, alternatives are eliminated. The past, post-actualisation, is real and singular.

3. **Memory's reconstructive nature is fallible epistemic access, not ontological re-selection.** The phenomenology-of-memory article is careful that memory "is reconstruction, not recovery" *of an existing past*. The reconstruction modifies the *trace*; it does not rewrite *what happened*. The pastness quale presents the remembered event as "having been there," tracking a determinate past, not generating it.

4. **Both articles converge on rejecting Many-Worlds via the singularity argument.** Atemporal selection presupposes one history becomes actual; phenomenology-of-memory's pastness quale presupposes "this past and no other." The metaphysical-singularity-of-history premise does the same anti-MWI work in both articles, from complementary directions.

The atemporal-causation framework *produces* the singular determinate past that the phenomenology-of-memory article *presupposes*. They occupy adjacent rungs of the same ladder — metaphysics of how a singular past is selected, then phenomenology of how it is encountered through memory.
