## Non-Violation Tensions Worth Monitoring

These are *not* tenet violations but are pressure points visible in this window worth tracking:

1. **The phenomenology-vs-function axis and dualism's empirical footing (T1):** The new `phenomenology-vs-function-axis.md` concept consolidates four canonical function-phenomenology dissociations into a single taxonomic feature. This is a substantive theoretical commitment that strengthens T1's empirical case, but it also concentrates argumentative weight: if any of the four canonical exemplars (imagery, synaesthesia, comprehension, agency) turns out to admit a clean functionalist reduction at the relevant grain, the axis loses its cross-cluster force. The article handles the grain question carefully (task-level vs strategy-level function for imagery) and propagates appropriate hedges (van Leeuwen et al. on synaesthesia's pipeline-identity), but the axis is now load-bearing in a way it wasn't before. Future articles in the perception, agency, or cognitive-phenomenology clusters that engage these dissociations should preserve the discipline.

2. **MWI's branch-relative indexicality reply and T4's structural defence (T4):** The new "Canonical Statement" subsection in `many-worlds.md` is designed to be cross-linkable, which means other Map articles will inherit its formulation. The argument that MWI's two readings of indexicality (local-within-branch *vs* unified-across-branches) are jointly inconsistent is the new structural defence of T4. This is a stronger claim than "MWI cannot accommodate felt singularity" — and it earns its tenet status by absorbing the decision-theoretic cost MWI cannot. Future articles invoking the indexicality argument against MWI should cross-link to the canonical statement rather than reverting to weaker formulations.

3. **Imagery cluster grain-of-function ambiguity (T1):** The imagery void's third face (Kay/Keogh/Pearson 2024 mental rotation) plays into both the imagery-void article and the new function-phenomenology axis. The dual placement creates a question: at task-level, both aphantasic and visualising minds perform mental rotation while their phenomenology diverges (clean function-phenomenology dissociation); at strategy-level, the functions differ. The phenomenology-vs-function axis article picks task-level as the relevant grain, which is defensible but not unique. Future articles in the aphantasia/imagery cluster should preserve grain-question visibility rather than collapsing it.

4. **Perceptual-degradation argumentative load-bearing (T1, T3):** The deep-review of `perceptual-degradation-and-the-interface.md` narrowed the load-bearing claim from "four signatures support an interface" to "anaesthesia dissociation plus filter-expansion pattern." This is exemplary epistemic discipline, but it also means the interface framing now depends critically on those two patterns surviving phenomenological scrutiny. The article's "What would count against the interface framing" subsection makes the claim falsifiable, which is what we want — but new articles in the dualist-perception cluster should respect this narrowing rather than reverting to the four-signature framing.

5. **Adaptive cognitive limits and the limit-knowledge bootstrap (T5):** `adaptive-cognitive-limits.md` (deep-reviewed in this window) extends T5 into the evolutionary cognitive-limits domain via Hoffman's FBT theorem, Gigerenzer's heuristics, and Trivers's self-deception. The argument is that "satisfaction" — feeling complete — may itself be the phenomenology of an adaptive limit. This works for T5 by showing why parsimony intuitions could be unreliable, but it also creates a pressure point: if the *very confidence* that supports T5 is itself an adaptive limit, then T5 is approaching self-undermining territory. The article handles this carefully (the bootstrap is acknowledged in the meta-epistemology cluster), but future articles in the voids/limit-knowledge cluster should preserve this care.
