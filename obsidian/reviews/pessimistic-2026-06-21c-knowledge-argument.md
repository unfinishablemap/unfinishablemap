## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
"Mary's Room is folk-psychology theater. 'What it's like to see red' is a placeholder for neural states we haven't reduced yet — the same promissory-note structure that 'caloric' and 'vital force' had before they were eliminated. The article concedes the gap 'shows no sign of closing,' but absence of a current reduction is not evidence of irreducibility; it is the normal state of an immature science." The article partly pre-empts this (it treats the intuition as evidential-but-not-decisive), but it never engages the *eliminativist* move specifically — Dennett's "deny Mary's knowledge" is reply-by-completion, not reply-by-elimination. A Churchland would say the article smuggles in the realism about qualia that is precisely what is at issue.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The article gives Dennett a fair and well-stated hearing (lines 86–91) — better than most treatments. The weakness Dennett would press: line 91 says "even the most detailed physical descriptions leave competent philosophers sensing Mary learns something new" and treats this as "a fact the physicalist owes an account of." Dennett's whole point is that *no one has ever actually been given the complete physical description* — so the persistence of the intuition is evidence about the limits of our imagination under an impoverished stimulus, not about the limits of physical knowledge. The article asserts the burden falls on the physicalist; Dennett would say the burden-shift is itself the question-begging step, and the article does not show why the intuition survives a description no human has ever possessed.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)

### The Empiricist (Popper's ghost)
"What experiment falsifies this?" The article's "What Would Challenge This View?" section (lines 149–159) is its best falsifiability move — but every listed challenge is itself contestable from within ("the explanatory gap shows no sign of closing," "the illusion problem appears as difficult as the hard problem"). The Popperian objection: the falsification conditions are stated, then each is pre-rebutted, so in practice no outcome is allowed to count against the view. Challenge #1 (Mary predicts correctly) is the one genuinely empirical condition, but no operationalisation is offered for what would count as "accurately describing what experiences are like." This is honest metaphysics, but the article slightly oversells it as resilient falsifiability.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
"The argument presupposes a stable Mary who is the same subject before and after — the bearer of the 'new knowledge.' But the self that 'learns' is exactly what should be deconstructed. 'What it's like to see red' reifies a momentary, dependently-arisen event into a *fact Mary now possesses*." The article never addresses the no-self reading, even though it leans on "acquaintance knowledge" as "the strongest grade of indexical knowledge" (line 117) — a move that *deepens* the reification rather than examining it. This is a real gap rather than a critical flaw, but the persona surfaces it consistently.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Jackson self-rejection claim is uncited and likely mis-dated
- **File**: `obsidian/concepts/knowledge-argument.md`
- **Location**: Line 101, "Jackson's Self-Rejection" section — "Frank Jackson, the argument's creator, now rejects it. By 2003 he embraced physicalism, arguing that his original conclusion—epiphenomenalism about qualia—leads to a devastating problem…"
- **Problem**: Two coupled defects. (a) **Date**: the canonical work in which Jackson recants is "Mind and Illusion" (2004); his shift consolidated across the late 1990s. "By 2003" is at best loosely sourced and at worst simply wrong — and it is stated with false precision. (b) **Citation gap**: the References block (lines 193–207) lists only Jackson 1982 and 1986 — i.e. the two papers that *advance* the argument. There is **no entry for any Jackson recantation work** (e.g. "Mind and Illusion," in P. Ludlow et al., eds., *There's Something About Mary*, MIT Press, 2004 — a volume the article already cites at line 202). So the entire self-rejection paragraph, including the specific date and the reconstructed epiphenomenalism reasoning, rests on no cited source. This is exactly the AI-citation-reliability failure mode logged in project memory: a confident factual/biographical claim with fabricated-or-absent provenance on a 100%-AI article.
- **Severity**: High (it is a factual claim about a named living philosopher, stated with a specific date, and entirely uncited).
- **Recommendation**: `refine-draft` pass. Either (i) replace "By 2003" with "By the early-to-mid 2000s" (or cite "Mind and Illusion," 2004) and **add the recantation work to the References** — the 2004 *There's Something About Mary* volume is already half-cited at line 202 and contains Jackson's reply, so this is a low-cost fix; or (ii) soften to "Jackson came to reject the argument over the late 1990s and early 2000s" and drop the false-precision year. Web-verify before editing: the date is the load-bearing detail.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The burden-of-proof shift (Dennett)
- **Current content says** (line 91, 159): the physicalist "owes an account" of why the learning intuition persists even under maximally detailed description; "the burden falls on physicalists to explain it away."
- **A critic would argue**: no human has ever possessed the complete physical description, so the persistence of the intuition is a fact about *us under impoverished input*, not about physical knowledge. Asserting the burden-shift is the contested step, not a neutral observation.
- **Suggested response**: add one sentence acknowledging that the burden-shift itself is what Dennett contests (the article currently states the shift as if settled). The fix is small and would strengthen, not weaken, the section — it already concedes Dennett "exposes a genuine burden-of-proof question" two paragraphs earlier (line 89), so line 91 slightly contradicts the more careful line 89.
