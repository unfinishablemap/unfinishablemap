## Key Sources (New Since 2026-03-14)

### Adlam, E. — "Two Roads to Retrocausality" (Synthese, 2022)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-022-03919-0
- **Type**: Philosophy paper (peer-reviewed)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes two motivations for retrocausality: (a) rescuing locality from Bell's theorem, (b) accepting nonlocality and adding retrocausality because Lorentz invariance demands it
  - Argues that route (a) is poorly motivated—there is no compelling reason to prefer local retrocausal models over nonlocal retrocausal models
  - Route (b) is more natural: if nonlocality is real and there is no preferred reference frame, then retrocausal influences follow as a consequence
  - Concludes that "all-at-once" retrocausality (Wharton-style) is more coherent than dynamical backward-in-time propagation
  - The retrocausality that emerges from nonlocality is not mediated by physical systems traveling backward in time—it is structural
- **Tenet alignment**: Important reframing. The Map's Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds) already rejects MWI, leaving nonlocality or retrocausality as alternatives. Adlam suggests these are not exclusive—accepting nonlocality can lead naturally to retrocausality. This means the Map need not choose between them. The structural (all-at-once) form of retrocausality is more compatible with the Map's growing-block time position than dynamical backward causation.
- **Quote**: "Accepting the existence of nonlocality and insisting on the nonexistence of preferred reference frames leads naturally to the acceptance of a form of retrocausality."

### Ridley, M. — "Many Retrocausal Worlds: A Foundation for Quantum Probability" (European Journal for Philosophy of Science, 2025)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13194-025-00696-8
- **Type**: Philosophy/physics paper (peer-reviewed)
- **Key points**:
  - Introduces the Fixed Point Formulation (FPF)—a fully time-symmetric model within an "all-at-once" approach
  - FPF resolves causal loop paradoxes that plague dynamical retrocausal models
  - Grounds the Born rule by identifying it as a generic pattern in a time-extended wavefunction
  - Uses self-locating probabilities centered in time-extended worlds
  - Despite the "many worlds" in the title, this is structurally different from Everettian MWI—it involves retrocausal constraint satisfaction across single histories
- **Tenet alignment**: Mixed. The title suggests tension with Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds), but the "worlds" here are not Everettian branches—they are time-extended solution histories in a retrocausal framework. The Born rule derivation is valuable since it addresses a gap in other retrocausal models. The causal loop resolution strengthens the viability of retrocausal approaches generally.

### The Tlalpan Interpretation / Quantum Time-Symmetric Interpretation (QTSI) (arXiv, 2025)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.19301
- **Type**: Physics paper (preprint)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes that wavefunction collapse is not primitive but emergent—a spontaneous breaking of time symmetry triggered by amplification and record creation
  - Microscopic quantum laws are time-symmetric; temporal asymmetry is emergent via a phase transition analogous to magnetization in ferromagnets
  - In the "subcritical regime" (without amplification), systems can depend on both past and future conditions—retrocausality without paradoxes
  - Above the critical threshold, amplification breaks time symmetry and collapse appears
  - Testable predictions: (1) sharp threshold for disappearance of interference at critical amplification, (2) anomalously fast decay of reversibility
  - Retrocausal correlations are statistical—no backward signaling, so free will is unaffected
- **Tenet alignment**: Highly promising for the Map. The emergent collapse picture is compatible with the Map's "objective reduction with consciousness modulation" position from the prebiotic collapse discussion. If collapse emerges from time-symmetric physics at a threshold, consciousness could modulate where that threshold is crossed in neural systems—biasing which amplification events occur. This preserves all five tenets while grounding the mechanism in testable physics. The explicit compatibility with free will is a bonus.

### Nature Communications — Test of Physical Significance of Bell Non-Locality (2025)
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-59247-7
- **Type**: Experimental paper (peer-reviewed)
- **Key points**:

  - This means retrocausal models cannot "hedge" with minimal backward influence; the commitment must be wholehearted
- **Tenet alignment**: Important constraint. If the Map adopts retrocausal selection, the retrocausal influence must be fully committed, not a minor perturbation. This actually supports Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction) paradoxically—the interaction is minimal in energy terms but maximal in its temporal scope. ## Updated Major Positions

### Position 6 (New): Emergent Collapse from Time-Symmetric Physics (QTSI/Tlalpan)
- **Proponents**: Authors of QTSI (2025)
- **Core claim**: Collapse is not a fundamental law but emerges from time-symmetric microscopic physics at a critical amplification threshold. Below threshold, retrocausal correlations are present; above threshold, time symmetry breaks and definite outcomes appear.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Grounded in well-understood physics of phase transitions
  - Testable predictions distinguish it from other interpretations
  - Retrocausality is a natural feature of the subcritical regime, not an ad hoc addition
  - No backward signaling—free will is preserved
- **Relation to site tenets**: Very strong fit. The Map's prebiotic collapse resolution already proposes objective reduction with consciousness modulation. QTSI provides a specific mechanism: consciousness could lower or raise the effective amplification threshold in neural systems, determining which superpositions cross the critical point and collapse. This is minimal (Tenet 2), bidirectional (Tenet 3), compatible with single outcomes (Tenet 4), and grounded in physics that is more complex than simple materialism but potentially correct (Tenet 5).

### Position 7 (New): Structural Retrocausality from Nonlocality (Adlam)
- **Proponents**: Adlam (2022)
- **Core claim**: Retrocausality follows naturally from accepting nonlocality without a preferred reference frame. This "structural" retrocausality is not about signals traveling backward in time but about all-at-once constraint satisfaction across spacetime.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Avoids the locality-rescue motivation, which Adlam argues is weak
  - Structural retrocausality is more coherent than dynamical backward causation
  - Compatible with relativistic physics without preferred frames
- **Relation to site tenets**: Resolves a strategic question for the Map. Rather than framing retrocausality as an alternative to nonlocality (Price's "third way"), the Map can frame it as a consequence of nonlocality—a position that is less revisionary and more defensible. This also eases the growing-block tension: structural all-at-once retrocausality is about constraint satisfaction at the growing edge of the block, not about the future pre-existing.

## Key Debates (Updated)

### Must Retrocausal Influence Be Total or Can It Be Partial?
- **Sides**: Nature Communications 2025 result (must be total) vs. models assuming minimal retrocausal bias
- **Core disagreement**: Can consciousness partially bias quantum outcomes retrocausally, or must the retrocausal selection be all-or-nothing?
- **Current state**: The 2025 experimental result suggests partial retrocausal models are excluded—if retrocausality operates, it must fully determine outcomes. For the Map, this strengthens the "selection" framing: consciousness selects which outcome actualizes (total determination among available options) rather than slightly biasing probabilities.

### Is the Fixed Point Formulation Compatible with No Many Worlds?
- **Sides**: Ridley (2025) uses "many retrocausal worlds" terminology; Map's Tenet 4 rejects MWI
- **Core disagreement**: Does the FPF commit to multiple real worlds?
- **Current state**: The FPF "worlds" are time-extended solution histories used to derive the Born rule, not ontologically real parallel branches. The Map can use the mathematical framework while maintaining that only one solution history is actual—the one consciousness selects.

### Does Emergent Collapse (QTSI) Leave Room for Consciousness?
- **Sides**: QTSI provides a purely physical collapse mechanism; the Map requires consciousness to play a causal role
- **Core disagreement**: If collapse emerges from amplification thresholds, is consciousness needed at all?
- **Current state**: Same structure as the Schulman tension identified in 2026-03-14 research. The Map's resolution: QTSI describes the physical mechanism, but consciousness modulates where and when the threshold is crossed in neural systems. The phase-transition framework actually helps—consciousness could alter the effective "temperature" (amplification level) at quantum decision points in the brain.

## Gaps in Research (Updated)

- **Kastner on consciousness**: Still no published work by Kastner explicitly connecting PTI to consciousness as transaction selector. The Map would be constructing an original synthesis.
- **QTSI and neural systems**: The Tlalpan interpretation does not address biological systems. How the amplification threshold maps to neural dynamics is undeveloped.
- **Adlam's nonlocality route and dualism**: Adlam's argument assumes physicalist ontology. Whether structural retrocausality from nonlocality is compatible with dualist consciousness needs philosophical development.
- **Empirical distinguishability**: The QTSI's testable predictions are the most promising lead. Can threshold effects be detected in neural quantum processes?
- **Growing-block compatibility**: Adlam's structural retrocausality and QTSI's emergent time asymmetry both ease this tension but do not resolve it completely. The Map needs a clear position on whether the growing block grows deterministically with retrocausal constraint satisfaction at the edge.
