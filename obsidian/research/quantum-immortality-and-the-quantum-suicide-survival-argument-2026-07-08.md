## Executive Summary

Quantum suicide is a thought experiment in which an observer is placed in front of a quantum-triggered lethal apparatus; on the Many-Worlds Interpretation (MWI), the argument runs, the observer's subjective experience only ever continues in branches where the trigger failed, so they should "expect" to survive indefinitely — **quantum immortality**. The setup originated independently with Hans Moravec (*Mind Children*, 1988) and Bruno Marchal (1988), and was given its canonical formalization by Max Tegmark in 1998. The overwhelming consensus among physicists and philosophers is that this is a **reductio** exposing something wrong with naive MWI self-location, **not** a real prediction: Tegmark himself specified three narrow conditions (quantum-random trigger, near-instantaneous death, virtually certain lethality) that real deaths almost never satisfy, and later said experimenters should expect only normal survival probabilities. The decisive objection is the **measure/probability problem** — surviving branches carry vanishing amplitude, so "you always find yourself alive" illicitly ignores how much of the wavefunction's weight the survivor represents. For the Map this is a direct pressure test of Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds + indexical identity): under real collapse with a determinate fact about which successor "I" am, the immortality expectation simply **dissolves** — death is death, with no surviving-branch self to locate into. The reductio is therefore a cost MWI carries that the Map's collapse-plus-indexical-realism avoids.

## Key Sources

### Tegmark, "The Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics: Many Worlds or Many Words?"
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_suicide_and_immortality (secondary); primary: *Fortschritte der Physik* 46(6–8): 855–862, quant-ph/9709032
- **Type**: Paper (peer-reviewed, with arXiv preprint)
- **Key points**:
  - Formalized "quantum suicide" as a *gedanken* test that would (in principle, first-person only) distinguish MWI from collapse interpretations — a Schrödinger's-cat setup taken from the cat's point of view.
  - Specified **three conditions** required for the argument to bite: (1) the random-number/trigger source must be genuinely quantum, not deterministic/pseudo-random; (2) death must be near-instantaneous — faster than the observer's timescale of awareness; (3) the apparatus must be virtually certain to kill, not merely injure.
  - Later position: experimenters should expect a **normal probability of survival, not immortality**, because the survivor's amplitude/measure in the wavefunction is drastically reduced at each trial. Real deaths are gradual and non-binary and so fail the conditions.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 4 as a *description of MWI's consequences* — Tegmark presents the scenario as what MWI self-location implies. Neutral-to-supportive as a *diagnostic*: it isolates exactly the indexical defect the Map presses against MWI.
- **Quote (Tegmark, via Wikipedia summary)**: "Most accidents and common causes of death clearly don't satisfy all three criteria ... dying isn't a binary thing where you're either alive or dead."

### Moravec, *Mind Children: The Future of Robot and Human Intelligence*
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Moravec (secondary)
- **Type**: Book (Harvard University Press, 1988), ch. "The Doomsday Device," p. 188
- **Key points**:
  - Earliest published statement of the survival argument (sometimes dated 1987 for the underlying idea): from the first-person perspective, one never experiences the branch in which one dies, so a doomsday device is subjectively survivable.
- **Tenet alignment**: Same as Tegmark — a consequence of MWI-style self-location that Tenet 4 rejects.

### Marchal, "Informatique théorique et philosophie de l'esprit"
- **URL**: cited in https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_suicide_and_immortality (refs 9–10)
- **Type**: Conference paper (*Acte du 3ème colloque international Cognition et Connaissance*, 1988; developed in "Mechanism and personal identity," *Proceedings of WOCFAI 91*, 1991)
- **Key points**:
  - Reached a structurally identical conclusion from **computationalist / theoretical-computer-science** premises rather than physics: if consciousness is computable and all computations are realized, subjective experience persists indefinitely across surviving continuations — a comp-theoretic immortality.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 4's indexical-realism; also relevant to the Map's dualism (Tenet 1), since it presupposes a functionalist theory of mind the Map does not grant.

### Lewis, "How Many Lives Has Schrödinger's Cat?"
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/713659799
- **Type**: Paper (*Australasian Journal of Philosophy* 82(1): 3–22, 2004; from a 2001 lecture — Lewis's only work on QM interpretation)
- **Key points**:
  - Even granting branch-survival, Lewis argues the expectation is **horrifying, not consoling**: the "intensity rule" means one should overwhelmingly find oneself in branches that are barely-surviving — maimed, ill, cognitively degraded ("Struldbruggian" immortality) — because injury is vastly more probable at each step than recovery. This is the "quantum torment" worry (later developed by Aranyosi).
  - Turns on how branch **intensities/measures** convert into rational expectations — i.e., the same measure/probability problem.
- **Tenet alignment**: A *reductio-from-within* — accepts MWI self-location and shows it yields absurd/monstrous consequences. Useful to the Map as an internal-cost argument distinct from the Map's own indexical objection.

### O'Brien, "The Costs of Rejecting Quantum Immortality"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-025-05304-z (preprint: https://philsci-archive.pitt.edu/26778/1/The_Costs_of_Rejecting_Quantum_Immortality.pdf)
- **Type**: Paper (*Synthese*, 2025)
- **Key points**:
  - Recent contribution mapping what a committed Everettian has to give up to *avoid* quantum immortality — showing the debate is live and that the escape routes (measure-weighted expectation, particular theories of personal identity/anticipation) each carry theoretical costs.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/diagnostic — documents that MWI's exits from the reductio are not free, reinforcing the Map's "cost MWI carries" framing.

### Papineau, "David Lewis and Schrödinger's Cat"
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/713659793
- **Type**: Paper (*Australasian Journal of Philosophy* 82(1), 2004)
- **Key points**:
  - Papineau vs. Lewis on whether there is genuine **pre-measurement uncertainty** under MWI or only indexical facts to learn; the disagreement is precisely about how (and whether) branch measures license "expectations of experience." Lewis argues there is no genuine pre-measurement uncertainty, only indexical facts — which pressures Papineau's decision-theoretic reconstruction.
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly germane to Tenet 4: the "only indexical facts to learn" horn is close to the Map's claim that MWI must make indexical identity do illicit double-duty.

## Major Positions

### "Quantum immortality is a genuine first-person prediction of MWI"
- **Proponents**: The strong reading attributed to (early) Moravec, Marchal, and sometimes the popular presentation of Tegmark; occasionally defended in fringe/transhumanist writing.
- **Core claim**: Because a subject can never experience their own non-existence, and MWI actualizes a surviving branch for every survivable outcome, the subject's *anticipated* future is always one in which they survive; hence subjective immortality.
- **Key arguments**: First-person indexical continuity ("I only ever find myself where there is an I"); every non-zero-amplitude survival branch is actual under MWI.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly conflicts with **Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds)**. It is the cleanest illustration of the indexical defect the Map attributes to MWI: it treats "which surviving successor I become" as automatically resolved in favor of survival, while providing no fact of the matter about *which* successor is me — the very determinacy the Map insists on.

### "Quantum immortality is a reductio, not a prediction" (mainstream)
- **Proponents**: Tegmark (mature position), most physicists and philosophers of physics; the framing implicit in the measure-problem literature (Gao, Aguirre, O'Brien) and in Lewis's "torment" worry.
- **Core claim**: The argument only goes through under idealized conditions real deaths don't meet, and it ignores measure: surviving branches have vanishing amplitude, so a measure-weighted rational agent should *not* expect immortality. The scenario's absurdity counts *against* naive MWI self-location.
- **Key arguments**: (1) **Three-conditions failure** — real death is gradual, non-binary, rarely quantum-triggered; (2) **Measure/probability problem** — "you always survive" conflates *possibility of a surviving branch* with *rational expectation weighted by amplitude*; (3) **Torment reductio** (Lewis/Aranyosi) — even granting survival, expected quality of that survival is horrific, so the "consoling immortality" reading is self-undermining.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Supports the Map's Tenet-4 case** as a matter of dialectic. The Map does not need to adjudicate the internal MWI measure dispute; it can point out that MWI *has* this problem to solve at all, whereas real collapse never generates the pathological expectation.

### "The Map's dissolution: one-branch-at-a-time indexical realism"
- **Proponents**: The Map (Tenet 4; the death-and-consciousness and probability-problem cluster).
- **Core claim**: Under objective collapse there is a **single actual successor** and a determinate fact about which one is me. When the lethal outcome occurs, there is no surviving-branch self to locate into — death is just death. The quantum-immortality expectation never even forms, because it depends on *branch-relative* indexicality (each branch has "its own" continuing I) that the Map denies.
- **Key arguments**: Collapse supplies the **counterfactual exclusion** MWI lacks (this outcome obtains and the alternatives genuinely do not); indexical identity is a fact about the actual world, not a per-branch bookkeeping device; the Map's reading does not require solving a measure problem because there is only one branch to weight.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Native to **Tenet 4**, drawing on **Tenet 3/2** (bidirectional and minimal quantum interaction give collapse ontological seriousness) and cutting against **Tenet 1**-incompatible functionalist versions (Marchal's comp-immortality).

## Key Debates

### Does MWI actually *imply* quantum immortality?
- **Sides**: "No — measure defeats it" (Gao; the arXiv "Many-Worlds Interpretations Can Not Imply 'Quantum Immortality'" line of argument; mature Tegmark) vs. "It's at least a serious cost that requires a specific decision theory to escape" (O'Brien 2025).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether a measure-weighted account of anticipation is *available and mandatory* within MWI, or whether first-person survival-continuity outruns measure.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The dominant view is that measure blocks the strong prediction, but *why* an agent must reason with measure-weighted expectations (rather than mere branch-existence) is exactly the unresolved probability problem of MWI.

### Would quantum immortality be desirable or horrifying?
- **Sides**: Lewis (2004) and Aranyosi ("quantum torment") argue survival would be dominated by degraded states; others treat immortality neutrally.
- **Core disagreement**: How the intensity/measure rule distributes an immortal subject across barely-surviving vs. flourishing branches.
- **Current state**: Ongoing, but widely read as strengthening the reductio: the "comforting" reading of quantum immortality is self-defeating even on its own terms.

### Is the survivor "the same person"?
- **Sides**: Anyone importing a personal-identity criterion (Parfitian reductionists vs. indexical realists) reaches different verdicts on whether "I" continue.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether branch-successors with equal claim to being "me" leave the identity question determinate.
- **Current state**: This is the hinge the Map presses (see [[personal-identity]], [[indexical-knowledge-and-identity]], [[haecceity]]).
