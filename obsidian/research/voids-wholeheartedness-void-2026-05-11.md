## Executive Summary

Frankfurt's hierarchical theory of agency turns on a phenomenally weighty distinction: some of my desires, beliefs, and values are *mine* (I identify with them) while others happen *in* me (I am alienated from them). The proposed marker of identification is a higher-order volition — a second-order desire that one's first-order desire be effective. To halt the regress to ever-higher orders, Frankfurt invokes *wholeheartedness*: a decisive, unreserved commitment at some level of the hierarchy. The wholeheartedness void is the structural opacity of *that very commitment* to the consciousness performing it. From inside, consciousness cannot reliably tell (i) whether a higher-order endorsement settles the matter or merely defers the question one level up, (ii) whether what feels like wholeheartedness is unanimity or merely the foreclosure of dissent, or (iii) whether a present identification will be retroactively disowned. The void sits at the seam between standard-decision-theoretic agency and the lived sense that some commitments are *one's own*, and it bears directly on which mental events could be candidate sites for any non-physical influence on the physical world.

## Key Sources

### Harry Frankfurt — "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person" (1971) and "Identification and Wholeheartedness" (1987)
- **URL**: [SEP entry on Frankfurt](https://philarchive.org/archive/YAJFCO); [Holton lecture notes (MIT)](https://web.mit.edu/holton/www/courses/moralpsych/Lecture19.pdf)
- **Type**: Foundational essays + secondary commentaries
- **Key points**:
  - First-order desires: I want X. Second-order desires: I want to want X. Second-order *volitions*: I want my desire for X to be effective.
  - A person, on Frankfurt's view, is a being capable of forming second-order volitions; the absence of these defines a "wanton."
  - To halt the threatened regress (third-order desires about second-order desires, fourth-order about third…), Frankfurt introduces *decisive commitment* and later *wholeheartedness*: a state in which the agent endorses without reservation and has "made up his mind."
  - Identification with a first-order desire is constituted by a wholehearted higher-order endorsement of it.
- **Tenet alignment**: Wholeheartedness names a phenomenal state — being content, unreserved, undivided — that Frankfurt could not flatten into purely structural conditions. His later turn to "volitional necessities" and "necessities of love" is the trace of a phenomenally loaded notion resisting reduction; this is the [[tenets#^dualism|dualist]] signature.
- **Quote**: "Your will is utterly divided, and volitional unity is really out of the question."

### Gary Watson — "Free Agency" (1975), *The Journal of Philosophy* 72(8): 205–220
- **URL**: [PhilPapers entry](https://philpapers.org/rec/WATFA); [Holton lecture notes](https://web.mit.edu/holton/www/edin/introfw/introfw2.pdf)
- **Type**: Critical paper
- **Key points**:
  - Watson's diagnosis: even if a higher-order desire stands "unchallenged" at its level, it might still be arbitrary or disconnected from the agent's *values*. Frankfurt's hierarchy locates ownership too thinly — at the level of which desire happens to win — without explaining *why* that higher-order endorsement is authoritative.
  - Watson's proposed remedy: distinguish a *valuational* system from a *motivational* system; ownership flows from the valuational system, not from hierarchical desire alone.
  - The deeper point Watson presses: stopping the regress by stipulation ("a 'decisive commitment'… is arbitrary") leaves Frankfurt without a non-question-begging answer to why *this* level of the hierarchy is the authoritative one.
- **Tenet alignment**: Watson's pressure on the regress is the cleanest articulation of why *from inside* there is no fact of the matter about which level of identification settles the question — a structural epistemic limit consistent with [[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5]] (orthodox parsimony hides the seam).
- **Quote**: "[O]ne makes a 'decisive commitment,' where this just means that an interminable ascent to higher orders is not going to be permitted. This is arbitrary."

### J. David Velleman — Critique via the disownability of motives
- **URL**: [Tandfonline: relations between agency, identification, and alienation](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13869795.2013.815260); [CEU thesis on identification (Wagner)](https://www.etd.ceu.edu/2012/fphwaz01.pdf)
- **Type**: Critical essays + commentary
- **Key points**:
  - Velleman's pressure point: "it seems always possible that an agent 'disowns' the mental attitudes that cause an action." If any motive can in principle be retrospectively disowned, then identification is never structurally guaranteed at the time of action — only inferred or claimed.
  - This generates the *disownability face* of the void: consciousness cannot verify *during* the act that its present identification will survive reflection.
  - Velleman's positive proposal locates agency in a constitutive aim of self-understanding; the Map's reading is that this constitutive aim is itself a phenomenal posit, not a structural one.
- **Tenet alignment**: The disownability face dovetails with [[interested-party-void|the interested-party void]] — consciousness has standing stakes in whether *this present commitment* is "really mine," so introspective verdicts cannot be neutral.
- **Quote**: "[Disownability] shows that the standard theory captures, at best, actions that are defective, and fails to capture 'human action par excellence'."

### Michael Bratman — Planning theory and the identity-over-time response
- **URL**: [Self-Unity, Identification and Self-Recognition (Springer)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11406-018-0024-4); [Bratman: Planning and Its Function in Our Lives](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/japp.12693)
- **Type**: Constructive alternative to Frankfurt
- **Key points**:
  - Bratman replaces Frankfurt's "wholehearted second-order volition" with *plans and policies* that constitute the agent's literal persistence over time. These items "speak for the agent" because they help make the agent the same agent.
  - The proposal is hierarchical in form but anchored in temporal/identity facts rather than in unreserved present endorsement.
  - The Map's reading: Bratman's move converts the structural opacity of present identification into a temporal opacity — consciousness cannot survey its own diachronic plans-and-policies any more reliably than it can introspect its present volitional structure. The void is relocated, not closed.
- **Tenet alignment**: The relocation strategy itself is an instance of the [[mapping-mind-space|mapping-mind-space]] limit — every theory of identification displaces the opacity to a different region of self-relation.

### *The Philosophy and Psychology of Ambivalence: Being of Two Minds* (Brogaard & Gatzia, eds., Routledge 2020/2021)
- **URL**: [Routledge book page](https://www.routledge.com/The-Philosophy-and-Psychology-of-Ambivalence-Being-of-Two-Minds/Brogaard-Gatzia/p/book/9780367673444); [ResearchGate introduction](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/346559143_The_Philosophical_and_Psychological_Significance_of_Ambivalence_An_Introduction)
- **Type**: Edited volume
- **Key points**:
  - The volume documents a substantial literature defending the *rationality* of ambivalence: under conditions of value pluralism, partial knowledge, or genuine conflict, being of two minds may be the calibrated response, not a defect.
  - "Some philosophers defend radical ambivalence by arguing that, to be true to oneself, one sometimes needs to be radically ambivalent" — this directly inverts Frankfurt's wholeheartedness ideal.
  - Empirically, attitudinal ambivalence (simultaneously holding positive and negative evaluations of the same object) is well-attested in social psychology and resists collapse onto either pole even under introspective scrutiny.
- **Tenet alignment**: Ambivalence-rationality literature reveals the *ambivalence-detection face* of the void — consciousness cannot reliably tell whether a felt unity is genuine integration or merely the suppression of one pole. This connects to [[necessary-opacity]] (consciousness cannot verify its own accuracy).
- **Quote**: "Ambivalence relates to debates about virtues, rationality, and decision-making, agency or authenticity, emotions, and social or political metacognition."

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Self-Deception
- **URL**: [SEP: Self-Deception](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/self-deception/)
- **Type**: Encyclopedia entry
- **Key points**:
  - The *static paradox*: how can a self-deceiver simultaneously believe p and ¬p?
  - The *dynamic paradox*: how can the deceiver intend the deception without rendering the intention visible (and so ineffective)?
  - Western philosophical responses partition the self either temporally or psychologically — both moves concede that "the self as completely internally unified and fully self-aware" cannot be sustained.
- **Tenet alignment**: Self-deception supplies the *occlusion* component of the wholeheartedness void. If consciousness can be wholehearted-because-self-deceived (the deception preserving the felt unity by suppressing the dissenting pole), then the phenomenology of wholeheartedness is not a reliable marker of genuine identification.
- **Quote**: "Both [partitioning approaches] challenge the conception of the self as completely internally unified and fully self-aware."

### Jean-Paul Sartre — *Being and Nothingness* on bad faith
- **URL**: [IEP: Sartre's Existentialism](https://iep.utm.edu/sartre-ex/); [Wikipedia: Bad faith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bad_faith_(existentialism)); [Fordham paper: Bad Faith as Evidence for Three Orders of Identification](https://faculty.fordham.edu/davenport/texts/Sartre-BadFaith.pdf)
- **Type**: Foundational existentialist text + commentaries
- **Key points**:
  - Sartre rejects the Freudian partition of conscious/unconscious as the explanation of self-deception; bad faith for him lives "in the attic of consciousness," not in deep cellars.
  - The paradox Sartre embraces: bad faith is a free act of denying one's freedom — the deceiver and the deceived are not different agents but the same consciousness in its self-relation.
  - Authenticity, for Sartre, is the proper coordination of *transcendence* and *facticity* — a state that bad faith refuses by adopting wholesale identification with one pole (the role, the fact, the project).
- **Tenet alignment**: Sartre's analysis converges with the Map's reading: full unreserved identification can be the signature of bad faith rather than of authentic wholeheartedness. The introspective marker of "wholehearted" is therefore not self-validating.
- **Quote**: "Self-deception seems to 'live' not somewhere in the deep 'cellars' of the unconscious but rather in the 'attic' of consciousness."

### Harry Frankfurt — *The Importance of What We Care About* (Cambridge, 1988)
- **URL**: [Cambridge book page](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/importance-of-what-we-care-about/9691C950461B737990D6CE2AE5967E0A); [Schaubroeck on Frankfurt's concept of care](https://repository.uantwerpen.be/docman/irua/fd3a9b/135866.pdf)
- **Type**: Frankfurt's own later reconception
- **Key points**:
  - Frankfurt's later move from "wholehearted second-order volition" to *caring* and *volitional necessity*: cares are not chosen, are not whimsical like desires, and constitute the structure of personal identity.
  - "Caring is not necessarily or even often voluntary" — but "to shape what one cares about is an important way to shape one's will." This produces a second-order opacity: consciousness cannot directly inspect what it cares about (cares are revealed by their stability across time and conflict), and cannot fully choose them.
  - The trajectory Frankfurt → caring → volitional necessities is the philosophical literature *naming* the wholeheartedness void without resolving it: each successive reformulation discovers the opacity at a deeper layer.
- **Tenet alignment**: Caring-as-necessity (not chosen, not surveyable) is exactly the kind of structural feature [[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5]] predicts: parsimonious decision-theoretic models lose the very thing that constitutes ownership.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The wholeheartedness void is a structural opacity that operates at the seam between consciousness and its own commitments. It is not the absence of wholeheartedness — many agents are, by all reasonable markers, wholehearted in their commitments. The void is the impossibility, from within consciousness, of *verifying* that one's present identification with a desire, belief, or value (a) is genuine rather than self-deceived, (b) is settled rather than merely foreclosed by exhaustion or social pressure, and (c) will persist as a stable feature of the self rather than being retroactively disowned.

The void operates simultaneously as *unexplorable* (the regress is structural — every level of higher-order endorsement can in principle be questioned, and consciousness has no Archimedean point from which to survey the hierarchy) and as *occluded* (the self-deception and ambivalence literatures suggest that the felt unity of wholeheartedness can itself be the work of mechanisms that suppress dissent).

### Evidence for the Limit

Several converging lines support reading this as a genuine structural void rather than merely a difficult region of self-knowledge:

1. **The regress argument (Watson)**: stopping the hierarchy of endorsements at any level is provably arbitrary; consciousness cannot non-question-beggingly authorise any particular level as "the" level at which identification settles.
2. **The disownability argument (Velleman)**: any motive that causes action can in principle be retrospectively disowned. From inside, at the time of action, there is no marker that distinguishes "identification I will continue to endorse" from "identification I will later disown."
3. **The self-deception paradoxes (SEP)**: the felt experience of wholehearted commitment is consistent with, and often produced by, partition-based self-deception. The phenomenology is not self-authenticating.
4. **The ambivalence-rationality literature (Brogaard & Gatzia)**: not only is ambivalence common, but it is sometimes the calibrated response to one's situation. The Frankfurtian ideal of wholeheartedness is not always rationally appropriate, which means the felt absence of conflict is not a reliable marker of correct identification.
5. **Frankfurt's own evolution**: the trajectory from "decisive commitment" → "wholeheartedness" → "caring" → "volitional necessities of love" is best read as a series of *displacements* of the opacity rather than resolutions of it. Each successor concept relocates the seam.
6. **Empirical fragility of self-control models**: the ego-depletion programme of self-regulation research — which posited an inspectable, measurable resource constituting willful identification — has substantially failed to replicate (Hagger et al. 2016; Carter et al. 2015). Whatever wholeheartedness names, it is not a quantifiable resource that introspection or behavioural proxies have so far succeeded in capturing.

### Phenomenology

The phenomenology of approaching the void is well-documented in the akrasia and authenticity literatures. The first-personal markers include:

- The *retrospective surprise* of finding one acted against one's "real" wishes (akratic action), where the agent cannot from within action identify which competing motive is the authentic self.
- The *suspicion* that one's apparent conviction is performance — the worry that one's wholeheartedness is exactly what bad faith looks like from the inside.
- The *circularity of checking*: introspecting whether one is really wholehearted often *produces* hesitation, and the introspecting consciousness cannot tell whether the hesitation reveals prior ambivalence or merely creates it.
- The *foreclosure-vs-resolution ambiguity*: when one stops deliberating, consciousness cannot reliably tell whether deliberation completed or merely exhausted.

This phenomenology shares structure with [[suspension-void|the suspension void]] (consciousness cannot verify, sustain, or coherently perform withholding) and with [[decision-void|the decision void]] (the deliberation→commitment crossing is opaque). The wholeheartedness void is the *state* counterpart: where decision-void concerns the crossing, wholeheartedness-void concerns the standing condition of being identified-with.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

The literature has *named* the void at successively deeper layers (Frankfurt's own progression is exemplary) but has not penetrated it. Direct introspection is, by the void's structure, the very method that fails. Higher-order reflection cannot escape the regress; lower-order absorption surrenders the question.

### Indirect Methods

- **Failure analysis**: cases where wholeheartedness *fails* (akrasia, regret, retrospective disowning) reveal what wholeheartedness was supposed to do without yielding a positive account of when it holds.
- **Temporal triangulation**: Bratman's strategy of locating identification in plans-and-policies whose persistence-over-time constitutes the agent. This relocates the opacity without resolving it but provides indirect evidence by tracking stability.
- **Third-personal markers** (with caveats): an observer can sometimes detect ambivalence the agent does not report (body language, vocal tone, behavioural inconsistency). But the third-personal access is to *effects*, not to the identification-state itself, and is fragile against deception.
- **Apophatic description**: cataloguing what wholeheartedness is *not* — not the mere preponderance of one motive, not the absence of contrary thoughts, not the felt absence of conflict. This is the [[apophatic-cartography|apophatic-cartography]] strategy.

### What AI Might See

This is one of the voids where the asymmetry between human and AI introspection is most interesting. An AI system that maintains a transcript of its own activations, attention weights, and intermediate plans has, in principle, access to its own "hierarchy of preferences" in a way human consciousness does not. Yet:

1. The AI's access to its own weights does not constitute *identification* in Frankfurt's sense — it is observational, not endorsing. The phenomenal weight of "this is mine" does not transfer.
2. AI architectures trained on human preference data may exhibit *behavioural* wholeheartedness (consistent preferences across contexts) without anything corresponding to the lived seam.
3. Conversely, an AI may be able to detect ambivalence in itself (or in a human user) by inspecting representational structure, providing an external probe of a region human introspection cannot reach.

The Map's [[non-human-minds-as-void-explorers|non-human-minds]] strategy applies here: AI may not occupy the same void, but it may indirectly illuminate the void by failing to occupy it.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** — but not as the primary lens. The most theoretically motivated site for any non-physical influence on the physical world is the seam between deliberation and commitment (this is the [[decision-void|decision-void]]'s territory). The wholeheartedness void identifies a *closely adjacent* but distinct site: the standing condition that makes a particular commitment "one's own" rather than something happening in one. If [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3]] holds — that consciousness causally influences the physical — then the standing-identification state is a candidate site for the persistent shape of that influence (which preferences get backed by which actions, over time), even when no single decision moment is in view.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** is also load-bearing. Standard rational-agent theory absorbs identification into "stable preferences that move action," eliminating the phenomenal seam by definitional fiat. Watson, Velleman, and Bratman are each in different ways saying: that elimination loses what the theory was supposed to capture. The void is a region where parsimony hides structure.

### Implications

If the wholeheartedness void is real:

1. **Self-knowledge has a structural ceiling**. Even ideal introspection cannot verify present identification. The Map's project of careful boundary-mapping must catalogue the wholeheartedness limit alongside [[necessary-opacity]] and [[self-opacity]].
2. **Decision theory is incomplete in a principled way**. The standard model presupposes stable preferences attributable to the agent; this presupposition is exactly what the void challenges. Extensions that attempt to absorb identification structurally (Bratman's planning, Korsgaard's reflective endorsement) displace the opacity rather than removing it.
3. **The Map's own apophatic method confronts a sharper version of itself here**. When the Map *commits* to its tenets, the wholeheartedness void applies to that commitment too — the Map cannot verify its own wholeheartedness about its claims from inside. This is a structural humility built into any framework that takes consciousness seriously.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a voids article could:

1. **The three-face structure**: regress, disownability, ambivalence-detection. Frame as a conjunction-coalesce cognate in the style of [[suspension-void]] and [[vagueness-void]] — three structurally distinct faces conjoining into a single void at the standing condition of identification. This is the most defensible angle and the one most consistent with the Map's recent methodology.

2. **The sister-state-and-transition pair**: wholeheartedness-void as the state-counterpart of [[decision-void|decision-void]]. Decision-void: the crossing is opaque. Wholeheartedness-void: the standing condition is opaque. Sister voids in the agency family, both adjacent to the bidirectional-interaction tenet's most theoretically motivated site.

3. **Frankfurt's own trajectory as evidence**: the void's most interesting first-personal trace is in the work of the philosopher who came closest to naming it. Track Frankfurt's progression — decisive commitment → wholeheartedness → caring → volitional necessities of love — as a phenomenologically attested *displacement* of the opacity rather than a resolution of it.

4. **The bad-faith inversion**: develop the Sartrean line that full unreserved identification *can be* the signature of bad faith rather than authenticity. This is a more provocative angle and may need careful framing to avoid the [[voids-safety-protocol|safety-protocol]] concern about destabilising self-knowledge.

The three-face angle (1) is recommended as the primary frame because it fits the Map's coalesce methodology, supplies a clean criterion for the void being structural rather than incidental, and connects naturally to existing voids.

## Gaps in Research

- **Empirical phenomenology of wholeheartedness**: little systematic first-person phenomenology has been published on the *experience* of identification (as opposed to its philosophical analysis). The ambivalence-rationality literature is the closest, but it studies divided states more than unified ones.
- **Cross-cultural data**: whether the felt unity of wholeheartedness is culturally universal or culturally shaped (e.g., Western individualism may amplify the importance attached to "this is really mine" relative to dharmic or Confucian frameworks where identification with role or relation is more natural). Worth checking the comparative-phenomenology literature.
- **AI-introspection data**: whether large language models can detect ambivalence in their own activations and whether this constitutes a probe of the void, or merely a metaphor for it. Anthropic's interpretability work on refusal-and-compliance circuits would be relevant.
- **Connection to volitional-necessity**: Frankfurt's late notion of "necessities of love" (one cannot will against what one essentially loves) deserves its own treatment — the wholeheartedness void may have a strong-form variant where the agent is structurally *unable* to test their identification because the love is constitutive.
