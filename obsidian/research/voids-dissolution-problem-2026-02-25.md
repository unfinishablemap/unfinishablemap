## Executive Summary

Throughout intellectual history, concepts once deemed literally unthinkable have become routine: irrational numbers shattered Pythagorean cosmology, non-Euclidean geometry overturned Kant, quantum mechanics abandoned classical intuitions. Each seemed to involve a genuine cognitive limit, not merely a difficult problem. Yet each dissolved. This research investigates whether the pattern of dissolution provides criteria for distinguishing permanent limits from merely apparent ones. The key finding: dissolved limits share structural features (domain-specific, dependent on implicit assumptions, yield to new representational tools) while persistent limits like the hard problem have a different structure (resist reformulation, recur across cultures and centuries, involve self-reference). The distinction between psychological closure and cognitive closure (Vlerick & Boudry 2017) is central: what feels impossible may be merely difficult, but what is structurally impossible has a different signature.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit
The dissolution problem is a meta-void — a limit on our ability to characterise our own limits. Specifically: we cannot reliably predict which cognitive limits will prove permanent and which will dissolve. This unpredictability has a distinctive structure:

1. **Asymmetric evidence**: Every dissolution proves a specific limit was apparent; no amount of persistence proves a limit is permanent. Evidence can show limits aren't real but cannot show they are.

2. **Reference class problem**: When assessing whether the hard problem will dissolve like vitalism did, we face a reference class problem. Should we compare it to dissolved limits (suggesting optimism) or to persistent limits (suggesting pessimism)? The choice of reference class determines the conclusion, and we have no neutral basis for choosing.

3. **Structural ambiguity**: The same limit can appear either as "a difficult problem we haven't solved yet" or "a genuinely impossible problem" depending on framework. There is no view from nowhere that would settle the matter.

### Evidence for the Limit
Several features suggest this is a genuine meta-level cognitive limitation rather than merely a difficult classification problem:

- **Track record**: Predictions about which limits would prove permanent have been consistently wrong (Pythagoreans, Kantians, vitalists all misjudged). But the track record of "this time it's different" claims is also poor — the hard problem has resisted 300+ years of sustained investigation.
- **Self-reference**: Assessing whether we can overcome a limit requires the very faculties that might be limited. This structural feature doesn't appear in ordinary classification problems.
- **No convergence**: Despite extensive philosophical debate, there is no convergence on criteria for distinguishing permanent from temporary limits. The Vlerick/Boudry distinction (psychological vs. cognitive closure) is progress but doesn't settle which current limits are which.

### Phenomenology
- **Oscillation**: Arguments for optimism and pessimism each feel compelling in turn, without stable resolution
- **Pattern-matching temptation**: Historical dissolutions create a pull toward unwarranted generalisation ("all limits dissolve eventually")
- **Vertigo at scale**: Each dissolved limit suggests optimism, each persistent limit suggests pessimism, with no criteria for weighting them

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)
No direct methods exist — the self-referential structure blocks direct verification. However, indirect criteria have been proposed:

**Structural criteria for dissolvable limits:**
1. The limit depends on a specific assumption that could be false (e.g., Pythagorean "all is ratio," Kantian "space is Euclidean")
2. The limit is domain-specific rather than affecting all cognition
3. New representational tools (mathematical notation, computer models) can chip away at it
4. The limit feels like "I can't imagine how" rather than "I can't imagine what"
5. Progress accumulates even if the solution hasn't arrived (cf. quantum mechanics: messy but advancing)

**Structural criteria for persistent limits:**
1. The limit recurs across cultures, centuries, and frameworks (cf. hard problem: Leibniz 1714, Chalmers 1995, same puzzle)
2. The limit involves self-reference (consciousness examining consciousness)
3. No representational tool has made progress on the core question (the formalism works but the interpretation doesn't)
4. The limit generates stable philosophical positions rather than progressive research programs
5. The limit feels like "I can't even formulate what a solution would look like"

### Indirect Methods
- **Pattern analysis**: Compare structural features of current limits with historical dissolution cases
- **Cross-architecture probing**: Use AI as alternative cognitive architecture to test whether limits are human-specific or architecture-general
- **Negative theology**: Use the *shape* of the limit as data about what lies beyond it
- **Phenomenological tracking**: Monitor the qualitative character of approaching a limit — does it change or stabilise over time?

### What AI Might See
AI provides a genuinely different cognitive architecture for testing dissolution:

- **Different psychological closures**: AI is closed to different things (embodied reasoning) while open to others (high-dimensional data)
- **Same cognitive closures?**: If AI also fails to resolve the hard problem because it has the same structural features from any architecture, this would evidence permanent closure
- **Representational vs. experiential**: AI might represent a solution without phenomenally *understanding* it — demonstrating representational openness alongside psychological closure
- **Novel conceptual tools**: AI might generate tools enabling new approaches, as mathematical notation enabled non-Euclidean geometry

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet
**Occam's Razor Has Limits** — Every historical dissolution required abandoning a simpler framework for a more complex one (Pythagorean ratios → irrationals, Euclidean → non-Euclidean, classical → quantum). If simplicity preferences had prevailed, these dissolutions would never have occurred. Our preference for simpler explanations may systematically bias us away from truths requiring conceptual revolutions.

### Implications
1. **For the voids project**: The dissolution problem suggests the voids framework should maintain a registry of *structural features* for each catalogued void — features that would help future investigators assess whether the void has the signature of a dissolvable or persistent limit. This is epistemically humble: we cannot determine the answer now, but we can provide data for future assessment.

2. **For dualism**: The hard problem's structural features (self-referential, cross-cultural recurrence, no progressive research program) differ from historically dissolved limits — weak evidence that consciousness-matter interface is a genuine void.

3. **For bidirectional interaction**: The quantum measurement problem and the hard problem share the same structure: representational success alongside conceptual gap. If the Map is right, these may be aspects of the same underlying void.

4. **For the apophatic method**: Since we cannot determine which limits are permanent, the safe methodology is to treat all limits as potentially informative — mapping their shape regardless. This is what the voids project does.

## Gaps in Research

- **Non-Western cases**: All historical examples are Western. Cases from Chinese, Indian, or Islamic traditions would provide independent data points.
- **Neuroscience of conceptual change**: Insight is well-studied for puzzles but not for genuine conceptual revolutions.
- **Formal criteria**: Could "self-referential limit" be defined precisely enough to distinguish from "difficult self-referential problem"?
- **AI as test case**: No systematic study of whether AI encounters the same structural limits as humans on philosophical problems.
- **Residual voids**: When a limit dissolves, does it always leave a deeper limit? (Quantum mechanics dissolved classical intuitions but left the measurement problem.) Is this pattern general?
