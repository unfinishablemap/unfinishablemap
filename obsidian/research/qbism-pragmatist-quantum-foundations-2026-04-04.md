## Executive Summary

QBism and its broader family of pragmatist quantum interpretations offer the most agent-centred approach to quantum foundations currently on the table. This research specifically examines the pragmatist philosophical scaffolding that distinguishes QBism from generic subjectivism — Fuchs's explicit debts to William James, the participatory realism programme, Healey's independent pragmatist interpretation, and the technical apparatus (SIC-POVMs, Dutch-book coherence) that gives QBism mathematical teeth. The site already has strong QBism coverage (concepts/qbism) and pragmatist philosophy of mind research (research/pragmatist-philosophy-of-mind-2026-03-28), so this research focuses on the *intersection*: how pragmatist commitments shape QBism's treatment of agents, probability, and reality — and where those commitments create openings for the Map's dualist project.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Quantum-Bayesian and Pragmatist Views
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/quantum-bayesian/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (comprehensive entry)
- **Key points**:
  - QBism traces to Caves, Fuchs, and Schack (2002); the name "QBism" adopted in Fuchs, Mermin, & Schack (2014)
  - Quantum states represent an agent's epistemic state concerning possible future experiences — not physical reality
  - Born rule probabilities are subjective Bayesian credences, not objective propensities
  - Quantum theory is "a users' manual that any agent can pick up and use to help make wiser decisions in this world of inherent uncertainty"
  - The entry explicitly covers both QBism and Healey's distinct pragmatist approach
  - QBists argue their view dissolves measurement problem and non-locality puzzles
- **Tenet alignment**: QBism's rejection of objective quantum states conflicts with the Map's collapse realism (Tenet 2), but its agent-centrality and rejection of MWI (Tenet 4) align
- **Quote**: QBists maintain that "rather than (either directly or indirectly) representing a physical system, a quantum state represents the epistemic state of the one who assigns it concerning that agent's possible future experiences"

### Fuchs (2014) — "My Struggles with the Block Universe"
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1405.2390
- **Type**: Philosophical correspondence collection
- **Key points**:
  - William James is described as "the veritable mascot of this volume"
  - Chronicles QBism's evolution from Quantum Bayesianism to a more radical position influenced by pragmatism, pluralism, nonreductionism, and meliorism
  - Fuchs explicitly rejects the block universe in favour of James's open, evolving reality
  - QBism "set out to investigate whether the physical world might be of a type sketched by some false-started philosophies of 100 years ago"
  - The Jamesian influence goes beyond method — Fuchs adopts James's metaphysics of an open, pluralistic universe where novelty is real
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Tenet 5 (Occam's limits) — Fuchs treats pragmatism as recovering insights that premature closure had discarded. Anti-block-universe stance resonates with the Map's rejection of MWI's deterministic branching
- **Quote**: QBism investigates "whether the physical world might be of a type sketched by some false-started philosophies of 100 years ago (pragmatism, pluralism, nonreductionism, and meliorism)"

### Fuchs (2017) — "On Participatory Realism"
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1601.04360
- **Type**: Paper (appeared in *Information and Interaction*, Springer)
- **Key points**:
  - Coined "participatory realism" as umbrella term for interpretations where the agent's actions create new facts
  - Explicitly links QBism to Wheeler's "law without law" and "it from bit" programme
  - Participatory realism asserts reality is more than any third-person account can capture
  - Groups Copenhagen (Bohr, Heisenberg, Pauli), Wheeler's observer-participancy, Zeilinger-Brukner informational interpretation, Rovelli's RQM, and QBism under this banner
  - Wheeler: "every time an act of observer-participancy occurs, one of the undecidable propositions is upgraded to the status of a new axiom"
- **Tenet alignment**: Participatory realism's claim that reality exceeds third-person description directly parallels the Map's argument that consciousness introduces irreducible first-person facts. However, QBist participatory realism stops short of causal interaction — the agent *creates* facts through measurement but not through consciousness acting on quantum states
- **Quote**: Wheeler: "The strange necessity of the quantum as we see it everywhere in the scheme of physics comes from the requirement that — via observer-participancy — the universe should have a way to come into being?"

### Quanta Magazine — "A Private View of Quantum Reality" (2015)
- **URL**: https://www.quantamagazine.org/quantum-bayesianism-explained-by-its-founder-20150604/
- **Type**: Popular science interview with Fuchs
- **Key points**:

  - QBism's roots in personalist Bayesian probability (de Finetti tradition)
  - Fuchs acknowledges James's influence on the open, pluralistic character of QBism's universe

### Healey (2017) — *The Quantum Revolution in Philosophy*
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-quantum-revolution-in-philosophy-9780198714057
- **Type**: Book (Oxford University Press)
- **Key points**:
  - Healey offers a distinct pragmatist quantum interpretation, different from QBism
  - Quantum theory provides "authoritative and objectively correct prescriptions about how agents should act" — not descriptions of reality
  - Unlike QBism's subjective probabilities, Healey treats quantum probabilities as objective prescriptions for agents
  - "The point of a quantum theory is neither to conform our thought to the world by describing or representing it the way it is nor to create or mold the world, but to tell us what to make of it"
  - Presents pragmatism as alternative to both scientific realism and instrumentalism
- **Tenet alignment**: Healey's agent-centred approach shares QBism's recognition that physics requires agents but tries to avoid subjective idealism. His objectivity about prescriptions creates tension with the Map's interest in genuine causal interaction — if quantum theory merely prescribes agent behaviour rather than describing reality, there's nothing for consciousness to causally interact with
- **Quote**: "The point of a quantum theory is neither to conform our thought to the world by describing or representing it... nor to create or mold the world, but to tell us what to make of it"

### Glick (2021) — "QBism and the Limits of Scientific Realism"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13194-021-00366-5
- **Type**: Academic paper (European Journal for Philosophy of Science)
- **Key points**:
  - Examines whether QBism is compatible with scientific realism
  - QBism is "occasionally dismissed as solipsism or idealism" but this characterisation is contested
  - The solipsism objection: if quantum states are purely subjective, is there a shared external reality?
  - Wallace identifies QBism as instrumentalism; QBists insist this is a misunderstanding
  - QBism's relationship to realism is genuinely ambiguous — neither straightforwardly realist nor straightforwardly anti-realist
- **Tenet alignment**: The realism debate around QBism is directly relevant. The Map's tenets presuppose a real external world with real quantum events that consciousness can interact with. If QBism's pragmatism undermines this realism, it undermines the Map's causal interaction framework

### DeBrota, Fuchs, & Schack (2021) — "Born's Rule as a Quantum Extension of Bayesian Coherence"
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.14397
- **Type**: Technical paper (Physical Review A)
- **Key points**:
  - The Born rule is recast as a normative coherence constraint — a "quantum extension" of classical Dutch-book coherence
  - Classical Dutch-book coherence (de Finetti): rational agents' probabilities must satisfy standard probability axioms to avoid sure loss
  - Quantum extension: agents' probabilities must additionally satisfy Born-rule statistics when applied to SIC-POVM measurements
  - The "urgleichung" (primal equation): reformulation of Born rule using SIC-POVMs that makes it look like a rule relating one probability distribution to another
  - SIC-POVMs (symmetric informationally complete positive operator-valued measures) are the key technical tool — they allow quantum states to be expressed entirely as probability distributions
- **Tenet alignment**: The normative reading of the Born rule is significant for the Map. If Born rule probabilities are normative constraints on agent beliefs rather than objective features of reality, the Map's consciousness-interface reading of the Born rule (topics/born-rule-and-the-consciousness-interface) operates in a different register. The Map treats the Born rule as describing something that happens at measurement; QBism treats it as a rule for rational expectation
- **Quote**: "The subjective Bayesian interpretation of quantum mechanics (QBism) asserts that the Born rule is a normative rule in analogy to Dutch-book coherence"

### Brukner-Zeilinger Information Interpretation
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10701-016-0005-z
- **Type**: Academic discussion
- **Key points**:
  - Related but distinct from QBism — treats a quantum state as "a catalogue of knowledge about a system"
  - Unlike QBism, some probabilities are treated as objectively fixed
  - In Brukner-Zeilinger, the quantum state belongs to an "optimally informed agent" rather than any arbitrary agent
  - Information is introduced as the most fundamental notion; finiteness of information is an essential feature of quantum systems
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. More objectivist than QBism, which makes it more compatible with the Map's realism, but still informational rather than ontological

### Healey (2008) — "Quantum Theory: A Pragmatist Approach"
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1008.3896
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Healey distinguishes his pragmatism from QBism: quantum probabilities are "neither subjective nor straightforwardly objective"
  - The quantum state is "a source of sound advice to physically situated agents on the content and appropriate degree of belief about matters concerning which they are currently inevitably ignorant"
  - Healey positions this as alternative to both realist interpretations (Bohm, collapse theories, MWI) and non-realist ones (QBism)
  - Agents in Healey's framework need not be conscious — they must merely be "physically situated" and capable of acting
- **Tenet alignment**: Healey's broader notion of agency (not requiring consciousness) conflicts with the Map's emphasis on conscious agents as irreducible. But his insistence on objective prescriptions rather than subjective beliefs creates a middle ground between QBism and full realism

## Major Positions

### QBism (Fuchs, Caves, Schack, Mermin)
- **Proponents**: Christopher Fuchs, Carlton Caves, Rüdiger Schack, N. David Mermin
- **Core claim**: Quantum states are personal Bayesian degrees of belief about future experiences; the Born rule is a normative coherence constraint extending classical Dutch-book coherence; there is no objective wave function to collapse
- **Key arguments**:
  - Dissolves measurement problem: no objective superposition, only agent uncertainty
  - Resolves non-locality: entanglement correlations are belief-correlations, not action-at-a-distance
  - Agents are primitive and irreducible — QBism "takes for granted" experiencing subjects
  - SIC-POVM reformulation makes quantum mechanics look entirely like a theory of probability
  - Participatory realism: reality is more than any third-person account can capture
- **Relation to site tenets**: Complex. Agrees with No Many Worlds (Tenet 4). Agent-centrality implicitly supports irreducibility of consciousness (Tenet 1). But rejects objective quantum states, undermining Minimal Quantum Interaction (Tenet 2) and Bidirectional Interaction (Tenet 3). The Map's strongest move: QBism's primitive agents are an unexplained posit that dualism explains

### Healey's Pragmatist Interpretation
- **Proponents**: Richard Healey
- **Core claim**: Quantum theory provides objectively correct prescriptions for agents, not descriptions of reality. Probabilities are objective advice, not subjective beliefs
- **Key arguments**:
  - Avoids QBism's subjectivism while retaining agent-centrality
  - Quantum models "offer good advice" on the significance of claims expressed in non-quantum terms
  - Does not require conscious agents — any physically situated entity capable of acting qualifies
  - Pragmatism as alternative to realism/anti-realism dichotomy
- **Relation to site tenets**: Healey's broader agency notion (not requiring consciousness) conflicts with Tenet 1's emphasis on conscious experience. His objective prescriptivism is more compatible with realism than QBism but still doesn't give consciousness a causal role (conflicts with Tenet 3)

### Brukner-Zeilinger Informational Interpretation
- **Proponents**: Časlav Brukner, Anton Zeilinger
- **Core claim**: Information is the most fundamental notion in quantum mechanics; quantum states catalogue information about systems
- **Key arguments**:
  - Finiteness of information as essential quantum feature
  - Some probabilities are objectively fixed (unlike QBism's pure subjectivism)
  - Quantum state belongs to an optimally informed observer
- **Relation to site tenets**: More realist than QBism. Compatible with a world containing real quantum events, but informational framing doesn't clearly support consciousness having a causal role

### Stapp's Copenhagen Pragmatism
- **Proponents**: Henry Stapp (building on Bohr)
- **Core claim**: The Copenhagen interpretation is properly understood as pragmatic, using James's views on truth and experience as philosophical background
- **Key arguments**:
  - "The proper goal of science is to augment and order our experience"
  - Bohr's complementarity can be read through pragmatist lenses
  - The observer is genuinely part of the experimental setup, not eliminable
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong alignment. Stapp goes further than QBism by giving consciousness a causal role in collapse via the quantum Zeno effect — closer to the Map's position than any other pragmatist interpretation

## Key Debates

### QBism vs. Scientific Realism
- **Sides**: QBists (quantum states are beliefs) vs. scientific realists (quantum states describe reality) vs. Healey (quantum theory prescribes without describing)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether quantum mechanics reveals the structure of physical reality or merely guides agent behaviour
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Glick (2021) argues QBism's relationship to realism is genuinely ambiguous. The debate is sharpened by the participatory realism framing — QBists claim to be realists about reality-beyond-quantum-states while anti-realist about quantum states themselves
- **Map's angle**: The Map needs sufficient realism for consciousness to interact with quantum events. QBism's pragmatist anti-realism about quantum states is the deepest tension point

### Solipsism and Intersubjectivity
- **Sides**: Norsen (QBism is solipsism) vs. Fuchs (QBism is realist about an external world; "we find ourselves getting unpredictable kicks from the world all the time") vs. Wallace (QBism is instrumentalism)
- **Core disagreement**: If quantum states are private beliefs, how does intersubjective agreement arise?
- **Current state**: QBists respond that shared reality constrains beliefs, producing convergence. Critics find this insufficient — if the world constrains beliefs so reliably, something objective is doing the constraining, and that something looks like what other interpretations call the quantum state
- **Map's angle**: The intersubjectivity problem parallels the Map's own challenge of explaining shared physical reality under dualism. QBism's answer (reality constrains beliefs) is structurally similar to the Map's answer (consciousness interfaces with real quantum events)

### Normative vs. Descriptive Born Rule
- **Sides**: QBists (Born rule is normative coherence constraint) vs. traditional interpretations (Born rule describes objective probabilities) vs. consciousness-collapse theorists (Born rule describes consciousness's interaction with measurement)
- **Core disagreement**: What kind of claim the Born rule makes — about rational belief, about physical propensities, or about conscious observation
- **Current state**: The SIC-POVM reformulation gives QBism's normative reading technical support, but critics argue the normative reading merely relocates the question: *why* must rational agents satisfy this particular constraint unless the world has this particular structure?
- **Map's angle**: The Map's Born-rule-as-consciousness-interface reading (topics/born-rule-and-the-consciousness-interface) is a different register entirely. The normative vs. descriptive debate is useful context: the Map should acknowledge the pragmatist alternative explicitly

### Pragmatist Agents: Consciousness Required?
- **Sides**: Fuchs (agents are primitive, unexplained — but clearly experiential) vs. Healey (agents need only be physically situated, not conscious) vs. the Map (agents must be conscious, and this is what quantum mechanics reveals)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the "agent" in agent-centred quantum foundations must be a conscious subject
- **Current state**: This is the crux for the Map. QBism implicitly requires conscious agents (they form beliefs, have experiences, are surprised by outcomes) while refusing to theorise about consciousness. Healey tries to avoid the requirement entirely. The Map argues consciousness is exactly what needs explaining
- **Map's angle**: QBism's primitive-agent stance is the Map's strongest point of entry. The question "what makes something an agent?" is the hard problem in pragmatist disguise

## Gaps in Research

- **Fuchs's specific engagement with the hard problem**: Fuchs acknowledges QBism doesn't solve it, but his detailed views on consciousness are scattered across correspondence volumes (the Cerro Grande Fire Series) rather than in systematic publications. These volumes are difficult to search.
- **Healey's response to the consciousness objection**: How does Healey defend his broader (non-conscious) agency against the objection that his "agents" must ultimately be conscious to form expectations?
- **SIC-POVM existence conjecture**: QBism's technical programme depends on SIC-POVMs existing in all finite dimensions. This is conjectured but unproven for all dimensions — verified numerically up to very high dimensions. If the conjecture failed, QBism's technical foundation would need revision.
- **Mermin's "Now" and presentism**: Mermin has written about how QBism recovers the reality of the present moment ("Now"), rejecting block-universe eternalism. This connects to James's anti-block-universe stance but the details and philosophical reception need deeper exploration.
- **Contemporary pragmatist responses to QBism**: How do professional pragmatist philosophers (not physicist-pragmatists like Fuchs) evaluate QBism's use of their tradition?
