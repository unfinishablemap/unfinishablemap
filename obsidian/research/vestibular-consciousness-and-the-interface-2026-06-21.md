## Executive Summary

The vestibular sense (the inner-ear balance organs — three semicircular canals sensing angular acceleration, two otolith organs sensing linear acceleration and the pull of gravity) is the strongest candidate so far in the interface series for a modality with a *disputed or absent proprietary phenomenology*. Where vision, audition, touch, and smell each have an obvious "what-it-is-like," the vestibular sense is repeatedly described in both the philosophical and neuroscientific literature as **silent**: there is "a paucity of vestibular experiences in normal consciousness" (de Vignemont, SEP), and unlike every other sense it has **no primary, unisensory cortex** — the so-called vestibular cortex is by construction a distributed multisensory network (Pfeiffer, Serino & Blanke, 2014). Yet this nearly-silent sense is foundational to the *felt spatial self*: it supplies the world-centred gravitational reference for self-location, first-person perspective, and bodily ownership, and its disruption produces vertigo, depersonalization/derealization, and out-of-body experiences. This gives the vestibular case a distinctive inverted structure relative to its siblings — a sense that contributes the *frame* of conscious spatial experience while barely appearing *as* an experienced object. The physicalist rivals to out-accommodate are strong and converge cleanly: vestibular function as Bayesian/predictive multisensory integration (optimal cue weighting with a gravity prior), and bodily self-consciousness as multisensory integration at the temporo-parietal junction. The subject is genuinely worth covering: it is the explicit gap named by the tactile article, it is structurally distinct from the existing interface articles, and it has a rich, well-anchored empirical base.

### The vestibular system: a spatial reference for bodily self-consciousness
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4028995/ (Frontiers in Integrative Neuroscience open access)
- **Authors / Year / Journal**: Christian Pfeiffer, Andrea Serino & Olaf Blanke (2014), *Frontiers in Integrative Neuroscience* 8:31. VERIFIED at publisher of record (PMC).
- **Type**: Review (primary neuroscience anchor)
- **Key points**:
  - **No primary vestibular cortex**: "No such unisensory vestibular cortex seems to exist in the human brain. Rather, vestibular cortex is considered as any cortical region receiving vestibular input from the thalamus and is a distributed cortical network that overlaps with multisensory and motor representations."
  - Vestibular signals encode head position relative to gravity, providing "a world-centered reference for the bodily self."
  - These signals are critical for **self-location** (where "I" am in space) and **first-person perspective** (the viewpoint from which one experiences the world).
  - Conflicts between vestibular and visual gravitational signals alter both spatial dimensions of bodily self-consciousness.
  - GVS (galvanic vestibular stimulation) affects egocentric mental imagery in body-related tasks and modulates body-part representation.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong physicalist rival to out-accommodate. The whole account is multisensory-integration; the authors draw no dualist conclusion. The "world-centred spatial reference" framing is exactly what the interface reading must accommodate without conscripting.
- **Quote**: "No such unisensory vestibular cortex seems to exist in the human brain."

### The Out-of-Body Experience: Disturbed Self-Processing at the Temporo-Parietal Junction
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15632275/ (also https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1073858404270885)
- **Authors / Year / Journal**: Olaf Blanke & Shahar Arzy (2005), *The Neuroscientist* 11(1):16–24. VERIFIED via PubMed.
- **Type**: Review / model paper
- **Key points**:
  - OBEs result from a transient failure to integrate visual, tactile, proprioceptive, and **vestibular** information converging at the temporo-parietal junction (TPJ), especially on the right.
  - The TPJ anchors the self to the body; stimulation and lesion there can evoke disembodied experience.
  - Vestibular dysfunction contributes a "disintegration between personal (vestibular) space and extrapersonal (visual) space."
- **Tenet alignment**: Physicalist mechanism for a phenomenon (OBE) the Map treats elsewhere. Engage as the mechanistic rival; the residue is the felt first-person perspective the integration is presented *to*.

### Depersonalisation/derealisation symptoms in vestibular disease
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2077438/ (and systematic review: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09574271251412707, Cento et al. 2026)
- **Type**: Clinical / review
- **Key points**:
  - Vestibular disorders trigger or exacerbate depersonalization/derealization (feeling "spaced out," body feeling strange, not in control of self).
  - Mechanism: vestibular dysfunction disrupts the stable self–environment relationship because multisensory integration relies on a veridical vestibular spatial reference. Spatial disorientation and anxiety mediate the transformation of vestibular dysfunction into DP/DR symptoms.
- **Tenet alignment**: Disruption phenomenology — a sense whose *silence* in health becomes loud and self-altering in disease. Physicalist account available (multisensory disintegration); the datum is that the felt unity of the self has a vestibular dependency.

### Bayesian / predictive processing of vestibular information
- **URLs**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17146661/ (Laurens & Droulez, *Biological Cybernetics* 2007, "Bayesian processing of vestibular information" — verify exact metadata); review of internal-model / active self-motion estimation.
- **Type**: Computational neuroscience
- **Key points**:
  - The brain processes vestibular signals in a statistically optimal (Bayesian) fashion tuned to the statistics of natural head movement.
  - Central processing uses an **internal representation of gravity** (a gravity prior) that can produce inferred linear accelerations even without true inertial acceleration (the otolith tilt-translation ambiguity).
  - Active self-motion: motor commands generate sensory predictions; self-motion estimates draw on internal forward models (active inference).
- **Tenet alignment**: THE strongest physicalist rival. Vestibular perception as optimal multisensory inference with a gravity prior predicts the felt sense of orientation and self-motion with no non-physical remainder. Honor evidential-status discipline: this is a rival, not interface evidence.

## Major Positions

### Vestibular function as Bayesian / predictive multisensory integration (the principal rival)
- **Proponents**: Angelaki & Cullen (vestibular neuroscience); Laurens & Droulez (Bayesian vestibular processing); broad predictive-processing / active-inference programme.
- **Core claim**: Self-motion and orientation perception are computed as statistically optimal estimates that integrate vestibular, visual, proprioceptive, and motor-efference signals under a prior encoding Earth's gravity. The otolith ambiguity (gravity vs. linear acceleration) is resolved by inference, not by a dedicated quale.
- **Key arguments**: Optimal cue-weighting (reliability-weighted visuo-vestibular fusion); internal gravity model explains illusions (somatogravic illusion, tilt-translation); active self-motion estimates beat passive ones because predictions cancel reafference.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the rival the article must out-accommodate, NOT conscript. An interface operating below the level experience reaches would produce exactly this inferential architecture (same move the interoceptive article makes against interoceptive inference). The framework does not discriminate between the readings; say so plainly.

### Vestibular system as spatial reference for bodily self-consciousness
- **Proponents**: Blanke, Lopez, Lenggenhager, Pfeiffer, Serino.
- **Core claim**: Vestibular signals supply the world-centred (gravitational) reference frame that grounds self-location, first-person perspective, and bodily ownership; their integration at the TPJ is what anchors the self in the body.
- **Key arguments**: GVS/caloric stimulation shifts self-location and modulates ownership illusions; vestibular-visual conflict alters first-person perspective; TPJ lesion/stimulation produces OBEs; vestibular disease produces depersonalization.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Physicalist multisensory account. The interface reading can represent a *frame-providing* modality, but the evidence is neutral; the residue is *why there is a first-person perspective for the frame to orient at all*.

### The "silent sense" / no-proprietary-phenomenology position (the philosophical hook)
- **Proponents**: de Vignemont (frames the dispute); clinical observation that vestibular organs give "no overt, readily recognizable, localizable, conscious sensation."
- **Core claim**: In health the vestibular sense has little or no distinctive "what-it-is-like" — it is a background frame, not an experienced object — disputed against the view that subtle orientation/motion phenomenology exists and that dizziness/vertigo reveal a real proprietary phenomenology that is simply usually faint.
- **Key arguments**: Paucity of vestibular experiences at rest; no felt locus for the organs; yet vertigo, motion sickness, and the felt sense of "up" show *something* is there. Parallels interoception, which is also continuously active yet rarely felt.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the article's distinctive philosophical engine. It pressures the assumption (implicit across the series) that each modality contributes its own qualia. For the capability-division framework it suggests a modality that is *almost entirely brain-side* in health, whose mind-side contribution is the spatial *frame* of selfhood rather than a felt object. Honor evidential-status discipline: the silence is equally well explained physically (sub-threshold, frame-coding) — it is not evidence for an interface, it is a *structural disanalogy* worth surfacing.

## Key Debates

### Does the vestibular sense have a proprietary phenomenology?
- **Sides**: "No / paucity" (de Vignemont's framing; the silent-sense view) vs. "Yes, but faint / revealed in disruption" (vertigo, motion sickness, the felt 'up', subtle self-motion sensation are genuine vestibular qualia).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the felt orientation/self-motion content belongs *to the vestibular sense* or is a multisensory product to which vestibular signals merely contribute (so there is no vestibular quale, only a vestibular *contribution* to spatial qualia).
- **Current state**: Ongoing; the no-primary-cortex finding is often read as supporting the "no proprietary phenomenology" / inherently-multisensory side.

### Self-location anchor vs. spatial framework for all senses
- **Sides**: Vestibular signals anchor the subject to a location vs. provide the allocentric frame within which all perception is organised (de Vignemont raises both).
- **Core disagreement**: Scope of the vestibular contribution — local (where I am) vs. global (the reference frame itself).
- **Current state**: Open; bears on whether the article frames vestibular as a *self-location* contributor or a *frame-of-experience* contributor (the latter is the more distinctive and more interface-relevant claim).

### Had-frame vs. constitutive-frame (parallel to interoception's had-body vs. constitutive-body)
- **Sides**: The vestibular frame is *had* by a wider consciousness (the Map's commitment) vs. the spatial frame is partly *constitutive* of the experiencing subject (no frame, no located self).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether removing/scrambling the vestibular reference removes the subject's spatial situatedness only, or part of subjecthood itself (depersonalization data press here).
- **Current state**: The article should mark this at the framework boundary, exactly as the interoceptive article marks had-body vs. constitutive-body — the evidence does not adjudicate it.
