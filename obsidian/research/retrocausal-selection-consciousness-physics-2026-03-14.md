# Research: Retrocausal Selection Model for Consciousness-Physics Interaction

**Date**: 2026-03-14
**Search queries used**: "retrocausality consciousness quantum mechanics philosophy", "retrocausal selection model consciousness physics interaction", "Huw Price retrocausality quantum mechanics Stanford Encyclopedia", "delayed choice quantum eraser consciousness retrocausality philosophy of mind", "transactional interpretation quantum mechanics John Cramer retrocausality", "Yakir Aharonov two-state vector formalism retrocausality weak measurements", "Kenneth Wharton retrocausal model quantum mechanics all-at-once", "consciousness selection quantum outcomes retrocausal dualism interactionism", "Costa de Beauregard zigzag causation EPR retrocausality consciousness", "retrocausality free will libertarian agent causation quantum indeterminacy", "Schulman retrocausality special states quantum mechanics future boundary", "Sutherland causally symmetric Bohm model retrocausal hidden variables Bell", "Ruth Kastner possibilist transactional interpretation consciousness quantum mechanics", "Henry Stapp quantum Zeno effect consciousness retrocausal selection mind-body"

## Executive Summary

The retrocausal selection model proposes that consciousness selects which quantum transaction is actualized, operating atemporally rather than at a single forward-in-time moment. This research surveys the major retrocausal frameworks in quantum mechanics—the Transactional Interpretation (Cramer), Possibilist TI (Kastner), Two-State Vector Formalism (Aharonov), all-at-once Lagrangian models (Wharton), Causally Symmetric Bohm model (Sutherland), and Costa de Beauregard's zigzag causation—evaluating each as a candidate mechanism for the Map's consciousness-physics interaction tenets. The retrocausal approach is particularly promising for the Map because it resolves the Libet timing puzzle (consciousness need not precede neural activity if selection is atemporal), provides a Lorentz-invariant account of Bell correlations (avoiding spooky action at a distance), and offers a natural home for consciousness-as-selector within the quantum formalism. Key tensions include the bilking objection, fine-tuning concerns, and the relationship between retrocausality and the growing-block time model already developed on the Map.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Retrocausality in Quantum Mechanics
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-retrocausality/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (peer-reviewed)
- **Key points**:
  - Comprehensive survey of retrocausal approaches: transactional interpretation, TSVF, all-at-once models, causally symmetric Bohm model
  - Core motivation: retrocausality circumvents Bell's theorem by violating measurement independence (the assumption that hidden variables are independent of future measurement settings)
  - The bilking argument—that agents could prevent retrocausal effects—fails in QM because quantum mechanics inherently prevents independent epistemic access to past states (Price 1996)
  - Schulman's "special states" proposal: future boundary conditions cryptically constrain initial states, avoiding macroscopic superpositions without wave function collapse
  - Fine-tuning objection (Wood & Spekkens 2015): retrocausal models must violate faithfulness. Evans (2018) responds that "internal cancelling paths" are physically stable, not conspiratorial
  - No discussion of consciousness in the entry—retrocausality is treated as a purely interpretive question in physics
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction) and Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds). The retrocausal loophole in Bell's theorem supports locality without branching.
- **Quote**: "If there is a backward-directed influence of the chosen measurement setting on the pre-measurement ontic state, it is no longer to be expected that the measurement process is simply uncovering an independently existing definite value."

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Quantum Approaches to Consciousness
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-consciousness/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (peer-reviewed)
- **Key points**:
  - Three main approaches: quantum brain processes, quantum concepts for mental activity, dual-aspect theories
  - Von Neumann/London-Bauer/Wigner lineage: consciousness completes measurement by collapsing the wave function
  - Stapp develops this into a detailed model: mental effort protracts neural assembly lifetimes via quantum Zeno effect; each conscious experience corresponds to a state reduction actualizing a neural correlate
  - Primas distinguishes "tensed (mental) time" from "tenseless (physical) time"—potentially relevant to retrocausal models where consciousness operates outside physical time
  - Tegmark (2000) decoherence objection: tubulin superposition times ~10⁻¹² s; Hagan et al. (2002) dispute, yielding 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁴ s
  - No explicit discussion of retrocausal consciousness models in the entry
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism) via irreducibility of consciousness. Stapp's model directly instantiates Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction).

### Cramer, J.G. — The Transactional Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics (1986, updated 2015/2022)
- **URL**: https://faculty.washington.edu/jcramer/TI/The_Quantum_Handshake_Explored.pdf
- **Type**: Paper / Book (The Quantum Handshake, Springer 2015)
- **Key points**:
  - Quantum events are "transactions"—handshakes between retarded (forward) offer waves ψ and advanced (backward) confirmation waves ψ*
  - Collapse is atemporal: "does not happen at any specific point in time, but occurs along the whole transaction"
  - The Born rule emerges naturally from the amplitude mechanism (ψ × ψ*)
  - Wave functions are real physical waves, not merely knowledge representations
  - The interpretation is explicitly nonlocal yet relativistically invariant
  - Cramer does not invoke consciousness—the transaction completes between emitter and absorber
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds)—transactions select single outcomes. Neutral on consciousness; the mechanism could be adapted for consciousness-as-selector. The atemporal nature of transactions aligns with the Map's interest in consciousness operating outside standard temporal flow.
- **Quote**: "The collapse of the wavefunction does not happen at any specific point in time, but is 'atemporal' and occurs along the whole transaction."

### Kastner, R.E. — The Possibilist Transactional Interpretation (PTI)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/KASTPT
- **Type**: Paper / Book (Cambridge UP, 2012/2022)
- **Key points**:
  - Extends Cramer's TI: offer waves and confirmation waves exist in a "pre-spacetime" (PST) realm of possibilities
  - Actualized transactions establish empirical spatiotemporal events—spacetime itself emerges from transactions
  - Possibilities are ontologically real but sub-empirical
  - PTI resolves consistency problems in original TI by grounding transactions in relativistic quantum field theory
  - The emitter-absorber relationship is genuinely atemporal: neither party is temporally prior in the fundamental ontology
- **Tenet alignment**: Highly relevant. If consciousness selects which possibilities are actualized, PTI provides a natural framework: consciousness operates in the pre-spacetime realm of possibilities, selecting which transactions become actual spacetime events. This fits the Map's Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet—consciousness biases which possibility becomes actual without injecting energy. Aligns with Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds) since only one possibility is actualized.
- **Quote**: "Quantum theory can be regarded as describing a world of possibility that lies beneath our ordinary experienced world of actuality."

### Aharonov, Y., Bergmann, P., & Lebowitz, J. — Two-State Vector Formalism (1964, updated reviews)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0105101
- **Type**: Paper (arXiv review, originally Phys Rev 1964)
- **Key points**:
  - Quantum systems determined by both past (pre-selected) and future (post-selected) boundary conditions
  - The two-state vector ⟨Φ| combined with |Ψ⟩ provides a complete quantum description
  - Weak measurements reveal "weak values" that can lie outside the eigenvalue spectrum—suggesting reality is richer than standard QM assumes
  - Time-symmetric: the formalism treats past and future conditions on equal footing
  - Empirically equivalent to standard QM but offers different ontological picture
  - Aharonov, Cohen et al. (2015) argue retrocausality is compatible with free will: "an already existing future does not exclude free will nor invoke causal paradoxes, as on the quantum level, particles can be thought of as weakly interacting according to their past and future states"
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). The TSVF naturally accommodates consciousness selecting outcomes atemporally: consciousness could function as the post-selection condition that completes the two-state description. Explicitly compatible with free will per Aharonov et al. (2015).

### Wharton, K. — All-at-Once Lagrangian Models (2010+)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-retrocausality/ (Section on Wharton)
- **Type**: Multiple papers
- **Key points**:
  - Treats external measurements as physical constraints on Hamilton's action integral, analogous to boundary conditions
  - Solutions emerge "en bloc" from two-time boundary value problems
  - Measurements play a causal role in bringing about values—not simply revealing pre-existing properties
  - "Probability is a manifestation of our ignorance" about future constraints
  - Contextuality of measured values is "just what one might expect when admitting retrocausal influences"
  - Developed jointly with Price: "Dispelling the Quantum Spooks—a Clue that Einstein Missed?" (2013)
- **Tenet alignment**: Compatible with Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction). The all-at-once framework suggests consciousness need not inject energy—it functions as a constraint on the action integral, selecting among solutions. This is a particularly elegant fit for minimal interaction.

### Price, H. — Time-Symmetry and Retrocausality Arguments
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/PRIDTI-2 ("Does time-symmetry imply retrocausality?")
- **Type**: Philosophy papers
- **Key points**:
  - If fundamental laws are CPT-invariant (time-symmetric), then time-asymmetric causal influences require special justification
  - Time-symmetry implies retrocausality given certain quantum ontological assumptions (Price 2012; Leifer & Pusey 2017)
  - Retrocausality provides a "third option" beyond nonlocality and many-worlds for explaining Bell correlations
  - The bilking argument fails because quantum mechanics inherently blocks the epistemic access needed to exploit retrocausal effects
  - Price is careful to distinguish retrocausality from retro-signaling: no information can be sent backward in time
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds)—retrocausality is presented as the alternative to MWI. Supports Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)—the "simpler" forward-only causal picture may be wrong.

### Sutherland, R.I. — Causally Symmetric Bohm Model (2008+)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0601095
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Extends Bohmian mechanics with a final boundary condition ψ_f alongside initial ψ_i
  - Creates symmetric particle dynamics accounting for Bell correlations via "zigzag" influences (Costa de Beauregard)
  - Removes need for preferred reference frame—Lorentz invariant at the hidden level
  - Each particle described by separate 3D expression rather than 3n-dimensional configuration space
  - Hidden variables depend on future measurements through backward-in-time influences
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds) and provides a hidden-variable framework where consciousness could function as the selector of final boundary conditions.

### Costa de Beauregard, O. — Zigzag Causation (1947+)
- **URL**: https://aeon.co/essays/can-retrocausality-solve-the-puzzle-of-action-at-a-distance
- **Type**: Historical proposal (papers from 1947 onward)
- **Key points**:
  - As de Broglie's student, proposed that EPR correlations result from "zigzag" causal influence through spacetime—influence travels backward from Alice's measurement to the shared origin, then forward to Bob
  - Causal influences follow timelike trajectories—avoiding spacelike action-at-a-distance
  - When Bell's theorem appeared, Costa de Beauregard recognized the deeper significance: the zigzag offers a reconciliation between Bell and Einstein
  - Late in career, connected retrocausality to consciousness: argued for "reciprocal action of mind upon matter" (psychokinesis), though these claims remain highly speculative
- **Tenet alignment**: The zigzag mechanism is appealing for the Map because it preserves locality (influences travel along timelike paths) while explaining nonlocal correlations. Costa de Beauregard's own consciousness connection is too speculative, but the causal architecture is useful.

### Stapp, H.P. — Quantum Zeno Effect and Consciousness
- **URL**: https://www-physics.lbl.gov/~stapp/QID.pdf ("Quantum Interactive Dualism")
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Conscious observation acts as repeated measurement, stabilizing quantum states via quantum Zeno effect
  - Mental effort holds desired neural patterns in place against decoherence
  - Each decision "collapses the fan of potential action templates to a single actual readiness potential"
  - This has an implicitly retrocausal aspect: the Zeno effect operates by repeatedly projecting a state, effectively selecting a trajectory that the system would not have followed under free evolution
  - Stapp's framework is explicitly dualist-interactionist
  - Critics argue quantum Zeno effect cannot survive environmental decoherence (von Neumann entropy theorem); Stapp responds that the effect does survive and could operate in biological brains
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly instantiates Tenet 1 (Dualism), Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction), and Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). Stapp's model is already cited in the Map's tenets page. The retrocausal dimension is implicit rather than explicit in Stapp's formulation.

### Schulman, L.S. — Special States and Future Conditioning
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-retrocausality/ (Section on Schulman)
- **Type**: Papers (multiple)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes that macroscopic superpositions ("grotesque states") are avoided through cryptic constraints from future boundary conditions
  - Initial microscopic states are conditioned by future states—a hidden retrocausal mechanism
  - Maintains pure unitary quantum evolution (no collapse needed)
  - The conditioning is "cryptic"—not apparent to experimenters until future conditions are revealed
  - Provides a retrocausal solution to the measurement problem without invoking consciousness
- **Tenet alignment**: Interesting tension. Schulman avoids consciousness entirely, which conflicts with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). However, the mechanism of future conditioning could be adapted: if consciousness provides the future boundary conditions in neural systems (while physical processes provide them elsewhere), the Map's position is preserved. This connects to the prebiotic collapse resolution already on the Map.

### Aharonov, Y., Cohen, E. et al. — Accommodating Retrocausality with Free Will (2015)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1512.06689
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Directly addresses the concern that retrocausality implies fatalism or eliminates free will
  - Argues that quantum indeterminism masks future states from present access, preserving libertarian free will
  - Particles interact weakly according to past and future states, but the future remains inaccessible to present agents
  - Retrocausality is compatible with an open future because quantum uncertainty prevents deterministic retro-prediction
- **Tenet alignment**: Crucial for the Map. This paper addresses the tension between retrocausality and the libertarian free will position implicit in Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). If retrocausality implied fatalism, it would undermine consciousness-as-selector. Aharonov et al. show this tension is resolvable.

### Chalmers, D. & McQueen, K. — Consciousness and the Collapse of the Wave Function (2021)
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/collapse.pdf
- **Type**: Philosophy paper
- **Key points**:
  - Develops consciousness-collapse interpretation rigorously
  - Identifies "super-resistance" problem: if conscious states cannot superpose, quantum Zeno effect prevents transitions
  - Proposes gradual collapse dynamics rather than instantaneous projection
  - This is primarily a forward-in-time model, but the framework could be adapted to retrocausal selection
  - Chalmers remains skeptical that any new physics resolves the hard problem—quantum theories of consciousness suffer the same explanatory gap
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenets 1 and 3. The Map already has research on non-retrocausal conscious selection (2026-03-07) based on this paper. The retrocausal alternative addresses the super-resistance problem differently: if selection is atemporal, the temporal ordering that creates super-resistance may not apply.

## Major Positions

### Position 1: Transaction Selection — Consciousness Selects Which Transaction Is Actualized (Cramer/Kastner)
- **Proponents**: Framework from Cramer (1986), Kastner (2012); consciousness role suggested by integration with Map tenets
- **Core claim**: Quantum events are handshakes between offer waves (forward) and confirmation waves (backward). Multiple potential transactions compete; consciousness selects which is actualized. In Kastner's PTI, this occurs in a pre-spacetime realm of possibilities.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Transactions are inherently atemporal—resolves Libet timing problem
  - Born rule emerges naturally—consciousness biases within quantum-mechanical probabilities
  - PTI provides ontological home for "where" selection occurs (pre-spacetime realm)
  - Single outcome selected—no branching (supports No Many Worlds tenet)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Most promising framework. Satisfies all five tenets: dualism (consciousness operates in pre-spacetime realm, distinct from physical spacetime), minimal interaction (selection among existing possibilities, no energy injection), bidirectional interaction (consciousness causally selects outcomes), no many worlds (single transaction actualized), Occam's limits (the apparently "simpler" forward-only picture may be wrong).

### Position 2: Two-State Post-Selection — Consciousness as Future Boundary Condition (Aharonov/TSVF)
- **Proponents**: Aharonov, Bergmann, Lebowitz (1964); Vaidman; applied to consciousness by integration
- **Core claim**: Quantum systems are fully described only by both a forward-evolving state from the past and a backward-evolving state from the future. Consciousness could function as the source of the post-selection condition in neural systems.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Time-symmetric treatment of past and future on equal footing
  - Weak values reveal richer ontology than standard eigenvalue measurements
  - Explicitly compatible with free will (Aharonov et al. 2015)
  - Post-selection in laboratory settings is well-established experimentally
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong alignment. Consciousness providing future boundary conditions fits Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction) elegantly. The question of what provides post-selection in non-conscious systems connects to the prebiotic collapse discussion.

### Position 3: Constraint Selection — Consciousness as Action-Integral Constraint (Wharton/Price)
- **Proponents**: Wharton (2010+), Price (2012)
- **Core claim**: Measurements are physical constraints on the action integral, and solutions emerge "en bloc." Consciousness could function as a constraint on the Lagrangian, selecting among solutions to the boundary-value problem.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Most minimal intervention: consciousness is a constraint, not a force
  - Natural fit for "Occam's Razor Has Limits"—the block-universe picture is complex but may be correct
  - Contextuality is expected rather than paradoxical
  - Price's time-symmetry argument makes retrocausality the default assumption if laws are CPT-invariant
- **Relation to site tenets**: Very strong on Tenet 2 (Minimal Interaction)—constraints are the lightest possible intervention. Potential tension with growing-block time model on the Map, since Wharton's framework naturally suggests a block universe where all spacetime exists simultaneously.

### Position 4: Forward-Only Selection — Consciousness Acts at Collapse Moment (Stapp/Chalmers-McQueen)
- **Proponents**: Stapp (2001+), Chalmers & McQueen (2021)
- **Core claim**: Consciousness selects outcomes at the moment of wave function collapse, without needing retrocausality. Stapp's quantum Zeno effect holds desired states in place; Chalmers-McQueen use gradual collapse dynamics.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Avoids metaphysical commitments of retrocausality
  - Stapp's model has detailed neural mechanism
  - Chalmers-McQueen framework is rigorous and identifies specific failure modes
  - Forward-in-time causation is more intuitive
- **Relation to site tenets**: Already well-represented on the Map. Does not resolve Libet timing problem as cleanly as retrocausal models. The Map's existing research note (2026-03-07) covers this position thoroughly.

### Position 5: No Consciousness Role — Retrocausality as Pure Physics (Schulman/mainstream)
- **Proponents**: Schulman, many physicists working on retrocausal models
- **Core claim**: Retrocausality can resolve quantum puzzles without invoking consciousness. Future boundary conditions are physical, not mental.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Parsimony: no need for consciousness to explain quantum phenomena
  - Schulman's cryptic constraints work without observers
  - Most working physicists in this area do not invoke consciousness
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). However, the Map can acknowledge that pure-physics retrocausality is a viable position while maintaining that consciousness provides the relevant boundary conditions in neural systems specifically (as per the prebiotic collapse resolution).

## Key Debates

### Does Atemporal Selection Resolve the Libet Timing Problem?
- **Sides**: Retrocausal theorists (yes) vs. forward-only models (timing problem persists or is addressed differently)
- **Core disagreement**: If consciousness selects outcomes atemporally (via transactions or two-state vectors), the sequence "neural activity → conscious awareness → action" is compatible with genuine conscious causation. In forward-only models, the temporal gap between readiness potential and conscious awareness creates the appearance that consciousness arrives too late to cause anything.
- **Current state**: The Map has already developed this argument in `time-symmetric-selection-mechanism.md`. The retrocausal framework dissolves rather than solves the Libet problem—temporal priority is irrelevant to atemporal selection.

### How Does Wheeler's Delayed-Choice Support This Model?
- **Sides**: Retrocausal interpretation (delayed choice demonstrates genuine backward causation) vs. anti-retrocausal interpretation (no retrocausality needed; information-theoretic or Everettian accounts suffice)
- **Core disagreement**: Wheeler's experiment shows a present measurement determining whether a photon "behaved as" a wave or particle in the past. Retrocausal theorists see this as evidence of backward influence; others argue it shows only that we cannot assign definite properties before measurement.
- **Current state**: Wheeler himself rejected retrocausal readings, favoring "one must abandon a certain type of realism—namely, the idea that the past exists independently of its recording in the present." Sean Carroll and others argue Everettian interpretations explain delayed choice without retrocausality. For the Map, the retrocausal reading supports the transaction-selection model, but the argument is contested.

### What Does Consciousness-as-Transaction-Selector Imply About Time?
- **Sides**: Block universe (Wharton, Price—all of spacetime exists; retrocausality is constraint-propagation) vs. growing block (Map's developing position—the past is fixed, the present is where actualization occurs, the future is open)
- **Core disagreement**: If consciousness selects transactions atemporally, does this require the future to already exist (block universe)? Or can transactions be "completed" at the growing edge of the block, with offer waves extending into an open future?
- **Current state**: This is a genuine tension the Map must address. Kastner's PTI may help: if transactions occur in a pre-spacetime realm, the growing-block picture could describe the actualized spacetime while pre-spacetime remains timeless. The Map's existing growing-block apex article should engage with this.

### Is Retrocausality Compatible with Free Will?
- **Sides**: Aharonov et al. (2015) argue yes—quantum indeterminism masks the future; fatalists argue retrocausality implies a fixed future
- **Core disagreement**: If future boundary conditions constrain present states, doesn't this eliminate freedom? Aharonov et al.'s resolution: particles interact weakly with future states, but quantum uncertainty prevents deterministic access to the future.
- **Current state**: Largely resolved in the literature. The Map's position on libertarian free will (via agent causation and quantum indeterminacy) is compatible with retrocausality per Aharonov et al.

## Gaps in Research

- **Kastner on consciousness specifically**: Kastner's published work does not explicitly address consciousness as the transaction selector. The Map would need to construct this argument by combining PTI's ontology with the Map's tenet commitments. This is an original synthesis, not a report of an existing position.
- **Decoherence interaction**: How do retrocausal models interact with the decoherence objection? If selection is atemporal, does decoherence even apply in the same way? Wharton's all-at-once framework suggests decoherence is "ignorance about future constraints" rather than a physical process that destroys coherence.
- **Neural implementation**: None of the retrocausal frameworks specify how the mechanism would work at the neural level. The Map's existing Stapp and Orch OR material provides some scaffolding, but integrating these with retrocausal selection is underdeveloped.
- **Empirical distinguishability**: Can retrocausal consciousness-selection make predictions that differ from forward-only models? If not, the choice between them is purely philosophical. Weak value experiments (Aharonov) offer some hope for empirical distinction.
- **Growing-block compatibility**: The tension between retrocausal models (which suggest block universe) and the Map's growing-block position needs further philosophical development beyond the sketch provided above.
