## Assess-First Verdict: COVER (distinct from self-stultification)

The subject is worth a dedicated `concepts/` article and is genuinely distinct from
`concepts/self-stultification.md`. The distinction is the axis of the whole note:

- **Self-stultification** is a *property of a position*: a thesis whose truth undercuts the
  rational grounds for holding it (epiphenomenalism, eliminativism). It is a one-place predicate
  on a belief.
- **Reflexive methodology** is the Map's *argumentative move*: take a *rival* framework's own
  criterion of success and run the rival through it, showing that by its own lights the rival
  either fails or is driven to a dualist/deflationary conclusion. It is a two-place relation —
  a method applied *to an opponent* — that can *deploy* self-stultification as one of its tools
  but is not exhausted by it.

The move recurs across the corpus with different engines, which is what makes it a genuine
concept rather than a restatement of one argument:

| Instance (live article) | Rival framework | Its own criterion, turned | Yielded conclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| `concepts/pragmatism.md`, `topics/pragmatisms-path-to-dualism` | Pragmatism ("evaluate ideas by practical consequences") | Evaluation is itself a conscious activity; reduce it to physics and "what works" collapses into "what happens" | Dualism (materialism fails pragmatism's *own* test) |
| `concepts/causal-closure.md` | Causal closure / exclusion argument | If read strong enough to exclude mental causation it presupposes physicalism → circular; self-stultification then bites the epiphenomenalism closure forces | Deflation of the closure argument |
| `concepts/russellian-monism.md` | Russellian monism (structuralism + quiddities) | The very conceivability/knowledge arguments that motivate rejecting physicalism apply *again* to quiddity-structural duplicates (Cutter's instability) | Slide to dualism ("turn back or march on") |
| `concepts/self-stultification.md` (as deployed) | Epiphenomenalism / eliminativism | The belief, if true, undercuts the reasoning that produced it | Consciousness must be causally efficacious |

So the article should be framed as **the Map's method**, cataloguing its instances and situating
each against the named argument-forms in the literature below — NOT as another statement that
epiphenomenalism defeats itself.

## Executive Summary

Philosophy has a rich, named toolkit for arguments that turn a framework against itself: ancient
**self-refutation** (dialectical: you cannot *coherently maintain* the thesis), modern
**self-refutation** (logical: the thesis *entails its own contradictory*), **transcendental
arguments** (X is a necessary condition of a premise the opponent grants, so the opponent is
committed to X), **retorsion / performative (self-)contradiction** (the *act* of asserting the
thesis presupposes what it denies), and **immanent critique** (Hegel/Marx/Frankfurt School:
measure a framework against *its own* avowed norms, not external ones). The Map's move is closest
to two of these: (1) *immanent critique* — it judges rivals by their own standards rather than
importing dualist premises — and (2) *dialectical* self-refutation in Castagnoli's sense (showing
the rival cannot consistently be maintained), rather than the strict logical-entailment kind. It
also draws on **retorsion** where the target's *practice* (evaluating, reasoning, reporting)
presupposes the consciousness it would deflate. Mapping each Map instance to the right form
sharpens the argument and pre-empts the standard objections (Stroud's "belief vs. truth" gap;
tu-quoque as fallacy vs. legitimate retorsion).

## Key Sources

### Transcendental Arguments (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/transcendental-arguments/ (entry by Robert Stern)
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Structure: "X is a necessary condition for the possibility of Y — where then, given that Y is
    the case, it logically follows that X must be the case too." The argument starts from a premise
    the opponent (typically a sceptic) already grants and derives what they deny.
  - **World-directed vs. self-directed**: world-directed arguments claim truths about reality
    (objects/other minds exist); self-directed arguments claim only what we must *believe* or how
    things must *appear*.
  - **Stroud's objection**: a transcendental argument can at most establish what we must *believe*,
    not what is *true* — "it is enough to make language possible if we *believe* that S is true …
    but S needn't actually be true." Anti-realism can close the gap but then looks redundant.
- **Tenet alignment**: Method-neutral. Directly relevant — the Map's pragmatism→dualism move is
  effectively transcendental (consciousness as a necessary condition of evaluation/inquiry), so it
  inherits Stroud's objection and must answer it (the Map's reply: phenomenal experience is a
  *datum*, not merely a belief — the [[hard-problem-of-consciousness]] persists as a condition of
  description, not a description).

### J. L. Mackie, "Self-Refutation — A Formal Analysis"
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/pq/article-abstract/14/56/193/1587734 (verified at publisher)
- **Type**: Paper — *The Philosophical Quarterly*, vol. 14, no. 56 (July 1964), pp. 193–203
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes kinds of self-refutation: **pragmatic** (asserting the thesis is inconsistent
    with the *act* of asserting), **absolute** (the thesis entails its own falsity), **ad hominem**
    (self-refuting relative to the holder's *other* commitments), and **operational**.
  - Gives the Map a precise vocabulary: e.g. the epiphenomenalism argument is closest to *pragmatic
    / operational*; the eliminativism-about-content case is closer to *absolute*.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (analytic taxonomy). Use to type each Map instance precisely.

### Luca Castagnoli, *Ancient Self-Refutation*
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/9780521896313 (Cambridge UP, 2010; ISBN 978-0-521-89631-3)
- **Type**: Book (verified at publisher)
- **Key points**:
  - First book-length treatment; traces self-refutation from Democritus to Augustine (via
    Protagorean relativism, Academic scepticism, the *peritropē*/"table-turning" move).
  - **Central comparative thesis**: *ancient* self-refutation is **dialectical** — it shows the
    opponent "cannot coherently or consistently maintain" the thesis; *modern* self-refutation is
    **logical** — it shows the thesis "logically entails its contradictory and hence is false."
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Load-bearing for framing (used advisedly): the Map's move is mostly
  the *dialectical* kind — it shows a rival cannot be *consistently held* by its own criteria —
  which is a weaker and more defensible claim than "the rival is outright false."
- **Quote**: "aims at showing that someone cannot coherently or consistently maintain some thesis"
  (of the ancient/dialectical variety).

### Retorsion & Performative Contradiction (Apel / Habermas; Wikipedia + Cambridge Habermas Lexicon)
- **URLs**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Performative_contradiction ;
  https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/cambridge-habermas-lexicon/performative-selfcontradiction/E6687FBEE3B506BB5FE184E6D6250E88
- **Type**: Encyclopedia / reference
- **Key points**:
  - **Retorsion**: the argumentative device by which one shows the *act* of asserting a thesis
    presupposes what the thesis denies (the sceptic who asserts "there is no universal claim" makes
    a universal claim). Apel makes it the engine of *Letztbegründung* (ultimate justification);
    Habermas uses it as a debate weapon. First elaboration credited to Hintikka on Descartes' cogito.
  - Crucial contrast: **retorsion is NOT tu quoque**. Tu quoque (attacking the opponent for
    hypocrisy) is a fallacy; retorsion is legitimate because it targets a genuine performative
    inconsistency, not the arguer's character.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral method. The Map's strongest instances are retorsive: reasoning,
  evaluating and reporting are *acts* that presuppose the efficacious consciousness the rival deflates.

### Immanent Critique (Hegel / Marx / Frankfurt School; Wikipedia + Cambridge)
- **URLs**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanent_critique ;
  https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/hegels-dialectic-and-its-criticism (ch. "Determinate Negation and Immanent Critique")
- **Type**: Encyclopedia / scholarly
- **Key points**:
  - Critique that "evaluates reality not with alien principles of rationality but with those
    intrinsic to reality itself" — juxtaposing a framework's professed ideals/norms against its own
    performance. Rooted in Hegel's **determinate negation** (the negation is contentful and yields a
    positive successor position) and taken up by Marx and Adorno.
  - Method advances "drawing in nothing from outside" — each step uses only concepts the target
    already granted.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral method; **strong structural parallel** to the Map's move. The Map's
  self-description in `pragmatism.md` ("materialism about consciousness fails pragmatism's *own*
  test") is textbook immanent critique. Determinate negation also explains why the Map's move is
  *constructive*: turning a rival against itself yields a *positive* dualist successor, not mere
  scepticism.

### Tu Quoque vs. Self-Referential Incoherence (IEP Fallacies; secondary)
- **URLs**: https://iep.utm.edu/fallacy/ ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tu_quoque
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**: Names the failure mode the Map must avoid — a reflexive argument degrades into a
  *tu quoque* fallacy if it merely charges the opponent with hypocrisy rather than exhibiting a
  genuine self-undermining structure. The legitimate version is retorsion / self-referential
  incoherence.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (guardrail). The article should explicitly distinguish the Map's move
  from tu quoque to inoculate against the obvious objection.

## Major Positions (Argument-Forms the Map's Move Instantiates)

### Transcendental argument (Kant → Strawson → Davidson; Stroud as critic)
- **Core claim**: X is a necessary condition of some Y the opponent grants; therefore the opponent
  is committed to X. **Relation to Map**: the pragmatism→dualism and "evaluation presupposes a
  conscious evaluator" arguments are transcendental in form. **Inherited liability**: Stroud's
  belief-vs-truth gap — the Map must argue consciousness is a *datum*, not merely a required belief.

### Self-refutation, dialectical (ancient; Castagnoli) vs. logical (modern; Mackie)
- **Core claim**: the thesis defeats itself, either by being *unmaintainable in consistency*
  (dialectical) or by *entailing its own negation* (logical). **Relation to Map**: most Map
  instances are dialectical (weaker, safer); a few (eliminativism about content) approach the logical
  variety.

### Retorsion / performative self-contradiction (Hintikka, Apel, Habermas)
- **Core claim**: the *speech act* of asserting the thesis presupposes what it denies. **Relation to
  Map**: the epiphenomenalist who *reports* their belief, the eliminativist who *asserts* there are
  no beliefs — the Map's self-stultification deployments are retorsive at root.

### Immanent critique / determinate negation (Hegel, Marx, Adorno)
- **Core claim**: judge a framework by its own avowed norms; a contentful (determinate) negation
  yields a positive successor. **Relation to Map**: the Map's overarching method, and the reason its
  reflexive arguments are *constructive* (they land on dualism) rather than merely sceptical.

## Key Debates

### Does a reflexive argument prove truth or only commitment? (Stroud)
- **Sides**: Stroud (only shows what we must believe) vs. transcendental-argument defenders
  (world-directed versions, or an anti-realism, can bridge the gap).
- **Core disagreement**: whether necessary conditions of *thought/experience* can be conditions of
  *reality*. **State**: unresolved; the Map's best reply routes through the hard problem
  (experience is not a belief that could be merely useful).

### Legitimate retorsion vs. tu quoque fallacy
- **Sides**: those who treat "you do it too" style moves as always fallacious vs. those who separate
  retorsion (genuine performative inconsistency) from ad hominem tu quoque.
- **State**: settled in the literature (retorsion is legitimate; tu quoque is not) — the Map should
  cite the distinction rather than re-litigate it.

### Ancient dialectical vs. modern logical self-refutation (Castagnoli)
- **Core disagreement**: whether self-refutation shows *unmaintainability* or *falsity*. **State**:
  a clarifying distinction, not a live dispute; useful for calibrating how strong the Map's claims
  should be (prefer the dialectical, lower-commitment reading).

   (turn a rival's own success-criterion against it), typify it against the five literature forms
   (transcendental, dialectical self-refutation, logical self-refutation, retorsion, immanent
