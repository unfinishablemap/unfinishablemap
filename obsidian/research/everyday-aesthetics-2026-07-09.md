## Executive Summary

Everyday aesthetics is a subfield, roughly two decades old, that studies the aesthetic dimension of ordinary, non-art, non-scenic experience — the neat/messy/clean/dirty texture of domestic objects, grooming, chores, weather, and routine. Yuriko Saito's *Everyday Aesthetics* (OUP 2007) and Thomas Leddy's *The Extraordinary in the Ordinary* (Broadview 2012) secured its foothold; Sherri Irvin's "The Pervasiveness of the Aesthetic in Ordinary Experience" (BJA 2008) is the field's clearest statement that ordinary experience is "replete with aesthetic character." The field's defining internal debate is the **scope problem**: if the aesthetic is genuinely everywhere, does the category lose discriminating force and collapse into triviality? Responses split between those who welcome an expansive scope (Irvin, Melchionne) and those who worry it dissolves the aesthetic (and want a "major/minor league" gradation or a defamiliarization threshold). Saito additionally develops **moral-aesthetic judgments** — the claim that everyday aesthetic taste has real ethical and world-making consequences. For the Map, the field is a double-edged sword: the same pervasiveness the live article celebrates as evidence for irreducibility is exactly what critics inside everyday aesthetics fear *trivializes* the aesthetic. That symmetry is the article's payload.

## Key Sources

### Aesthetics of the Everyday (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aesthetics-of-everyday/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (canonical survey — the single best entry point)
- **Key points**:
  - Names Saito (2007) and Leddy (2012) as securing "the initial foothold" for the subfield.
  - Frames the scope/trivialization worry explicitly, and catalogues the responses (sliding-scale "major/minor league"; defamiliarization thresholds; intersubjectivity worries about bodily sensation and *sensus communis*).
  - Raises the art-centred vs. break-from-art-centred framework debate.
  - Notes the risk that over-emphasizing *defamiliarization* as a precondition "risks losing the very everyday-ness of everyday experience."
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (survey). Supplies the debate structure the Map must present even-handedly.
- **Quote**: On the sliding-scale response — "Some aesthetic qualities can be considered 'major league' or heavy-weight while other aesthetic qualities are 'minor league' or light-weight, without disqualifying the latter from the realm of the aesthetic altogether."

### Sherri Irvin, "The Pervasiveness of the Aesthetic in Ordinary Experience"
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/bjaesthetics/article-abstract/48/1/29/130483
- **Open-access copy**: https://philarchive.org/rec/IRVTPO
- **Type**: Journal article — *British Journal of Aesthetics*, vol. 48, no. 1 (January 2008), pp. 29–44
- **Key points**:
  - Central thesis: everyday experiences are "replete with aesthetic character," though contemporary aesthetics has neglected this.
  - Directly *against* a Deweyan requirement of unity/closure — argues that experiences which are "simple, lacking in unity or closure, and characterized by limited or fragmented awareness" are NOT thereby disqualified from being aesthetic. (This is important: the Map's live article leans on the *unity/binding* of aesthetic experience as evidence; Irvin represents the opposite view that ordinary aesthetic experience need not be unified. Note the tension for downstream cross-review.)
  - Ends with an ethical turn: aesthetic attention to everyday experience "may provide for lives of greater satisfaction and contribute to our ability to pursue moral aims."
- **Tenet alignment**: Ambiguous / dual-use. Supports pervasiveness (helps the Map's premise) but resists the unity-of-aesthetic-experience line (cuts against one of the Map's five arguments). Present as a genuine complication, not a citation of support.
- **Note**: Title-name resonance — Irvin's title and the live article's section heading ("Pervasiveness of Aesthetic Quality") converge, which is precisely why this is the anchor source to develop.

### Yuriko Saito, *Everyday Aesthetics* (OUP 2007)
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/everyday-aesthetics-9780199278350
- **Type**: Monograph — Oxford University Press, 2007, 273 pp.
- **Key points**:
  - The founding book. Argues Western aesthetics has neglected the everyday because of an "exclusive emphasis on art."
  - Emphasizes the *action-oriented, non-disinterested* nature of everyday aesthetic judgment — a deliberate break from the Kantian disinterestedness at the core of art-centred aesthetics.
  - Examples deliberately mundane: personal grooming, pet choice, garden design, ambient qualities, transience.
  - Introduces "moral-aesthetic judgments of artifacts" (a chapter) — everyday aesthetic taste shapes the world and quality of life.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral on dualism; the anti-disinterestedness and moral-aesthetic strands are orthogonal to Tenet 1.
- **Quote (paraphrase from OUP synopsis)**: everyday aesthetic tastes and judgments "exert a powerful influence on the state of the world and quality of life."

### Yuriko Saito, *Aesthetics of the Familiar: Everyday Life and World-Making* (OUP 2017)
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/book/11785
- **Type**: Monograph — Oxford University Press, 2017, 240 pp.
- **Key points**:
  - Saito's response to a decade of controversy over "the nature, boundary, and status of everyday aesthetics" — i.e., her direct engagement with the scope problem.
  - Argues everyday aesthetics is NOT fully captured by the "defamiliarizing the familiar" account (pushes back on the Leddy-style "extraordinary in the ordinary" framing).
  - Develops "world-making": everyday aesthetic engagement builds our environmental and moral world; we have a "social responsibility" to cultivate aesthetic literacy and guard against "aesthetic manipulation."
  - Structure: "Concepts," "Cases," "Consequences."
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Strengthens the *moral-aesthetic* thread most.

### Thomas Leddy, *The Extraordinary in the Ordinary: The Aesthetics of Everyday Life* (Broadview 2012)
- **URL**: https://broadviewpress.com/product/the-extraordinary-in-the-ordinary/
- **Type**: Monograph — Broadview Press, Peterborough, Ontario, 2012, 275 pp.
- **Key points**:
  - Contra Saito's break-from-art emphasis, Leddy "stresses the close relationship between everyday aesthetics and the aesthetics of art" — the continuity position in the framework debate.
  - Foregrounds neglected everyday aesthetic terms: 'neat,' 'messy,' 'pretty,' 'lovely,' 'cute,' 'pleasant,' 'sparkle,' 'shine.'
  - His governing idea: the everyday becomes aesthetic through a kind of heightened attention/*aura* that reveals the extraordinary latent in the ordinary (the "defamiliarization" strand Saito later resists).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Represents the *continuity-with-art* pole; useful for showing the field is not monolithic.

### Kevin Melchionne, "The Definition of Everyday Aesthetics"
- **URL**: https://digitalcommons.risd.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1290&context=liberalarts_contempaesthetics
- **Type**: Journal article — *Contemporary Aesthetics*, vol. 11 (2013), open access
- **Key points**:
  - Offers a deliberately tight definition to end the boundary controversy: everyday aesthetics concerns the *recurring, ongoing* aesthetic engagements of daily routine and management of the self and home — not one-off extraordinary encounters.
  - Ties everyday aesthetics to *aesthetic well-being* accrued across a life of routine, rather than to peak episodes.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral, and important for the *deflationary* reading — Melchionne's routine/well-being framing is easily given a functionalist gloss (see calibration flag).

### Arto Haapala, "On the Aesthetics of the Everyday: Familiarity, Strangeness, and the Meaning of Place"
- **URL**: (in Andrew Light & Jonathan M. Smith, eds., *The Aesthetics of Everyday Life*, Columbia University Press, 2005)
- **Type**: Book chapter — SEP-sourced, NOT independently verified at publisher (see quarantine list)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that everyday aesthetic value arises paradoxically from *familiarity* and being-at-home, not from strangeness/defamiliarization — a counter-pole to Leddy.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral.

## Major Positions

### Expansive / pervasiveness realism (Irvin; Melchionne)
- **Proponents**: Sherri Irvin, Kevin Melchionne (in his own routine-focused way).
- **Core claim**: The aesthetic genuinely pervades ordinary experience; simple, non-unified, fragmentary experiences still count.
- **Key arguments**: Rejection of the Deweyan unity/closure requirement (Irvin); definition by recurring routine rather than peak episode (Melchionne).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Two-edged. Supports the Map's premise that aesthetic character is everywhere (good for a pervasiveness-based irreducibility argument), but Irvin specifically denies the unity/closure the Map elsewhere treats as evidentially significant. Do not cite as unqualified support.

### Continuity-with-art / defamiliarization (Leddy)
- **Proponents**: Thomas Leddy.
- **Core claim**: Everyday aesthetics is continuous with art aesthetics; the ordinary becomes aesthetic when attention lends it an "aura" — the extraordinary in the ordinary.
- **Key arguments**: Analysis of surface qualities (neat/messy/clean/dirty, sparkle/shine); heightened-attention account.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral. Represents the pole that keeps everyday aesthetics tethered to the traditional (art-centred) apparatus.

### Break-from-art / moral-aesthetic world-making (Saito)
- **Proponents**: Yuriko Saito.
- **Core claim**: Everyday aesthetics must break from art-centred, disinterested models; everyday aesthetic judgment is action-oriented and morally consequential ("world-making").
- **Key arguments**: Anti-disinterestedness; moral-aesthetic judgments of artifacts; social responsibility / aesthetic literacy; resistance to defamiliarization-only accounts.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral on dualism. The moral-aesthetic strand is a distinctive angle the Map could note but should not conflate with irreducibility.

### Familiarity-based (Haapala)
- **Proponents**: Arto Haapala.
- **Core claim**: Everyday aesthetic value comes from familiarity and place-attachment, not strangeness.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral. Counter-pole to Leddy in the internal debate.

## Key Debates

### Debate A — Does everyday aesthetics need the art-centred framework, or break from it?
- **Sides**: Continuity (Leddy — everyday aesthetics closely related to art aesthetics) vs. break-from-art (Saito — Kantian disinterestedness is the wrong model; everyday aesthetic judgment is non-disinterested and action-oriented).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the concepts built for art (disinterested contemplation, the aesthetic object, framing) transfer to chores, weather, and grooming.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; Saito's 2017 book is her considered position resisting both pure art-transfer and pure defamiliarization.

### Debate B — The scope problem (is *everything* aesthetic, and does that trivialize the aesthetic?)
- **Sides**: Expansive (Irvin, Melchionne — welcome broad scope; simple/routine experiences count) vs. containment (critics who fear the category loses discriminating force if messiness, clutter, and mere bodily sensation all count).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether an aesthetic category that applies to *everything* still does explanatory/evaluative work, or whether pervasiveness = dilution.
- **Proposed resolutions**: (i) sliding-scale gradation — "major league" vs "minor league" aesthetic qualities, both genuinely aesthetic; (ii) a defamiliarization/attention threshold to keep the everyday-aesthetic from swallowing everything (with Saito warning this "risks losing the very everyday-ness"); (iii) intersubjectivity constraints to keep bodily sensation from collapsing into "subjective relativism" and losing *sensus communis*.
- **Current state**: Unresolved — this IS the field's defining problem, and it maps exactly onto the Map's payload tension below.

### Debate C — Saito's moral-aesthetic judgments
- **Sides**: Saito (everyday aesthetic taste is inseparable from ethical consequence — beauty standards that harm health, aesthetic manipulation, world-making) vs. those who want to keep aesthetic and moral evaluation distinct.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether aesthetic judgment about the ordinary carries built-in moral weight or whether that conflates two normative domains.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; central to Saito's 2017 development.

## Philosophical Payload (Map angle — carry forward to expand-topic)

The live `aesthetics-and-consciousness.md` asserts that aesthetic quality "suffuses ordinary consciousness," and treats that pervasiveness as underappreciated evidence for [[tenets#^dualism|dualism]] (Tenet 1): aesthetic phenomenal character is a dimension of experience as such, not an exotic special capacity. The everyday-aesthetics literature supplies exactly the pervasiveness data the Map wants — Irvin's "replete with aesthetic character," Saito's mundane examples, Leddy's neat/messy vocabulary.

But the SAME literature contains the counter-move. The field's central worry (the **scope problem**) is that if the aesthetic is genuinely everywhere, the category loses discriminating force — the thing critics call *trivialization*. This produces a genuine, unresolved tension the article should LAY OUT rather than resolve:

- **Strengthening reading**: Pervasiveness broadens the irreducibility case. Aesthetic phenomenal character (felt, evaluative, qualitative) is not confined to galleries and sunsets; it is a constant texture of ordinary consciousness. If even a clean glass or a well-made bed carries irreducible felt-evaluative character, then the explanatory gap is not a boutique problem about rare peak experiences but a pervasive feature of mind — harder for a reductive account to quarantine.
- **Diluting reading**: If everything is aesthetic, "aesthetic" may be doing no discriminating work — and a category that fails to discriminate is a weak lever for any metaphysical argument, including irreducibility. The very ubiquity that looks like strength may signal that "aesthetic quality" is just a loose label for ordinary valenced perception, fully tractable by a functionalist/deflationary account of valence and salience.

## CALIBRATION FLAG (for downstream expand-topic)

Per the lesson recorded as `expand-topic-lead-overclaims-phenomenal-absence`: the expand-topic article must present the irreducibility angle as ONE reading, explicitly framework-relative and explicitly not a proof. Specific guardrails:

1. **Do NOT lead with a bald claim** that pervasive aesthetic quality proves irreducibility or that reduction is impossible. Lead with the phenomenon and the debate; introduce the irreducibility reading as the Map's interpretation among alternatives.
2. **Give the deflationary/functionalist gloss real space.** Melchionne's routine/well-being framing and Irvin's rejection of unity both invite a functionalist reading (aesthetic character = valence + salience + attention, all mechanistically respectable). The pervasiveness could equally support "aesthetic quality is cheap and ubiquitous because it's just ordinary affective perception" — say so.
3. **Flag the internal tension with Irvin.** The live article's five-argument case leans partly on the *unity/binding* of aesthetic experience; Irvin explicitly denies that everyday aesthetic experience needs unity or closure. The article should not cite Irvin as unqualified support without noting she cuts against the unity argument.
4. **Keep "trivialization" as a live risk, not a strawman.** The scope problem is the field's own worry, raised by its own practitioners — present it at full strength.
