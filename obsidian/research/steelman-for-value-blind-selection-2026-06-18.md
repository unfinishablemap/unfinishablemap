## Purpose and Scope

This note assembles, at maximum honest strength, the **rival horn the Map declines**: the positive scientific-and-philosophical case that biological/evolutionary selection and the felt phenomena of wanting and liking can be fully accounted for by **value-blind mechanism** — that "value" is not an irreducible *experiential* property doing causal work, but a functional/dispositional gloss on mechanism (reward-prediction-error, reinforcement, homeostatic regulation, control-theoretic set-points, precision/uncertainty dynamics).

The motivation is a [[evidential-status-discipline|Tenet-5 / Occam's-Limits discipline gap]] flagged in `reviews/optimistic-2026-06-05.md` (§"The positive case for the value-blind horn"): the Map's value-cluster develops the value-*sensitive* horn richly but presents the value-*blind* horn mostly as "the more parsimonious option that explains everything without remainder." The reviewer warned the under-developed side "is at risk of being a strawman by attrition." Steelmanning the parsimonious rival is exactly the discipline of not letting tenet-congeniality do the arguing.

**This note steelmans the rival; it does not endorse it.** Per [[evidential-status-discipline]], the value-blind horn is presented as the strongest version of the view the Map *declines*, with the Map's register-appropriate reasons for declining noted at the end. No citation below was carried in unverified.

## Executive Summary

There is a mature, multi-disciplinary research programme on which "value" is exhaustively mechanism — and on which the felt phenomena adduced for experiential value (wanting, liking, pleasure, dread) are either (a) sub-personal computational quantities, (b) functional/representational states with no irreducible phenomenal residue, or (c) introspective illusions. Four convergent strands give the value-blind horn far more positive content than the Map's current "parsimony" gloss admits:

1. **Reward-prediction-error / reinforcement learning** (Schultz, Dayan, Niv): the brain's reward machinery is well-modelled as a temporal-difference learner whose central quantity — phasic dopaminergic prediction error — is a *teaching signal*, not a felt value. Choice is explained by learned action-values updated by error, with no experiential term.
2. **Wanting/liking handled for the rival** (Berridge himself; Winkielman & Berridge): the affective neuroscientist most cited *for* the value-sensitive side holds that core "liking" is an **objective, unconscious** hedonic reaction distinguishable from conscious pleasure — and that affect can be genuinely unconscious yet behaviour-guiding. This is the value-blind horn's strongest empirical exhibit, and it comes from the value-realist's favourite witness.
3. **Control-theoretic / cybernetic action selection** (Cisek): behaviour is the output of competing affordances biased toward homeostatic set-points; selection among options is a neural competition resolved by biasing inputs, requiring no felt-value currency.
4. **Valence-as-inference** (Joffily & Coricelli; Hesp et al.; Solms & Friston): valence is *defined* as a formal quantity — the time-derivative of variational free energy, or the rate of change of confidence/precision in a generative model. On the deflationary reading this is value reduced to an information-theoretic property of a control system.
5. **Illusionism / functionalism about affect** (Frankish): if phenomenal consciousness is an introspective illusion, then felt value is a *quasi-phenomenal* functional property; there is no extra experiential value to do, or fail to do, causal work.

The honest assessment for the downstream chain: this is enough distinctive, externally-anchored content to support a standalone ~2000-word topic, **and** the existing fork article does NOT already contain it (the parent's value-blind section argues from internal Map parsimony, not from these rival research programmes). Recommendation and a fallback are in the final sections.

## Key Sources (all verified at publisher of record)

### Schultz, Dayan & Montague 1997 — the RPE foundation
- **Citation**: Schultz, W., Dayan, P., & Montague, P. R. (1997). A neural substrate of prediction and reward. *Science*, 275(5306), 1593–1599. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.275.5306.1593
- **Verification**: VERIFIED at PubMed (PMID 9054347) — authors, title, journal, 275(5306):1593-9, DOI confirmed.
- **Value-blind relevance**: Phasic dopamine signals a **reward-prediction error** — the discrepancy between received and predicted reward — formally identified with the temporal-difference error of reinforcement learning. The quantity is a *teaching signal* that updates predictions; it is explicitly NOT a hedonic feeling. This grounds the claim that the brain's core "value" computation is a control-theoretic error term, not an experienced goodness.

### Dayan & Niv 2008 — RL in the brain, critically
- **Citation**: Dayan, P., & Niv, Y. (2008). Reinforcement learning: the good, the bad and the ugly. *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 18(2), 185–196. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.conb.2008.08.003
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — author-hosted PDF (Princeton) confirms authors/title/journal/18:185-196/DOI. (Note: volume issue is 18(2); the PDF header gives vol 18 pp 185–196.)
- **Value-blind relevance**: Reviews how cleanly RL maps onto neural reward processing AND where it strains, while keeping the entire account in computational-learning terms. Useful for a *fair* steelman: it lets the article cite the rival programme's own self-criticism rather than a strawman of it — model-free vs model-based control, the limits of the dopamine-RPE hypothesis — without conceding the value-blind frame.

### Berridge — objective "liking" is unconscious (the rival's strongest exhibit)
- **Citation**: Winkielman, P., & Berridge, K. C. (2004). Unconscious emotion. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 13(3), 120–123. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.0963-7214.2004.00288.x
- **Verification**: VERIFIED at SAGE publisher page — authors, title, journal, 13(3):120–123, DOI confirmed.
- **Value-blind relevance — HANDLE WITH CARE**: The task argument warns that Berridge is usually enlisted FOR the value-sensitive side. The reverse is the sharper point: Berridge & colleagues hold that **core "liking" is an objective, sub-personal reaction that need not be consciously felt**, and that affect can be *genuinely unconscious* yet still bias preference and consumption ("subliminally induced affective reactions still influence people's preference judgments and even the amount of beverage they consume"). They locate basic "liking" in subcortical systems that, on evolutionary grounds, "originated prior to systems for conscious awareness." For the value-blind horn this is decisive: the leading affective neuroscientist's own taxonomy contains a value-relevant, behaviour-guiding hedonic process that is **not** an experience. If felt value can be subtracted while the value-doing-work remains, the experiential residue looks explanatorily idle — which is precisely the rival's thesis.
- **Map-side caveat to preserve for the article**: Berridge separates objective "liking" from subjective conscious pleasure but does NOT thereby deny conscious pleasure exists or matters; he is a physicalist whose constructs are operational. The fork article already records the trap "do not enlist Berridge as a dualist"; the symmetric trap here is "do not enlist Berridge as an eliminativist about felt pleasure." His work supplies the *empirical wedge* (value-relevant processing can be unconscious), not a metaphysical verdict.

### Cisek 2007 — control-theoretic action selection without a value currency
- **Citation**: Cisek, P. (2007). Cortical mechanisms of action selection: the affordance competition hypothesis. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 362(1485), 1585–1599. https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.2007.2054
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — citation cross-confirmed (PubMed PMID 17428779; SciRP reference index gives 362:1585–1599, DOI 10.1098/rstb.2007.2054). Issue number 1485 from the RSTB volume record. (PubMed article page returned a reCAPTCHA to automated fetch; metadata triangulated across PMID listing + SciRP — flag in Gaps.)
- **Value-blind relevance**: The affordance-competition hypothesis models behaviour as simultaneous *specification* of possible actions and *selection* among them, the selection implemented by mutual inhibition among neural populations biased by "decision variables." Crucially, the bias inputs are quantities (expected payoff, cost, urgency) realised as neural activity — a cybernetic competition resolved by control signals, with no step at which a *felt* value is consulted. This is the cleanest contemporary demonstration that genuine, flexible, goal-directed selection-among-options can be a mechanistic competition. It is the direct rival to the Map's "selection needs a valence currency" claim, because it offers a non-valence currency: biasing decision variables.
- **Companion (not separately needed but supports)**: Cisek, P., & Kalaska, J. F. (2010). Neural mechanisms for interacting with a world full of action choices. *Annual Review of Neuroscience*, 33, 269–298. (Title/venue/pages noted from search; verify before citing if used.)

### Joffily & Coricelli 2013 — valence DEFINED as a free-energy derivative
- **Citation**: Joffily, M., & Coricelli, G. (2013). Emotional valence and the free-energy principle. *PLOS Computational Biology*, 9(6), e1003094. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1003094
- **Verification**: VERIFIED at PLOS publisher page — authors, title, journal, 9(6):e1003094, DOI confirmed.
- **Value-blind relevance**: Valence is *operationally defined* as "the negative first time-derivative of free-energy" — positive valence is decreasing free energy (uncertainty), negative valence increasing. Valence is thereby cashed out as an information-theoretic property of a self-organising control system regulating learning rate. On the deflationary reading the article supports, "value" is a quantity a thermostat-like inference machine computes — no experiential primitive required.

### Hesp et al. 2021 — valence as precision/confidence in active inference
- **Citation**: Hesp, C., Smith, R., Parr, T., Allen, M., Friston, K. J., & Ramstead, M. J. D. (2021). Deeply felt affect: the emergence of valence in deep active inference. *Neural Computation*, 33(2), 398–446. https://doi.org/10.1162/neco_a_01341
- **Verification**: VERIFIED at MIT Press publisher page — six authors with initials, title, journal, 33(2):398–446, DOI confirmed.
- **Value-blind relevance**: Valence is "a state of self that is inferred on the basis of fluctuations in the estimated confidence (or precision) an agent has in her generative model" — "affective charge," a higher-order metacognitive estimate of model fitness. Note the *ambiguity for the steelman*: the title "Deeply **Felt** Affect" and the framing "emergence of valence" can read as value-sensitive (affect as a genuine inferred felt state). The value-blind use is the deflationary one — valence is *constituted by* a precision estimate, i.e. value reduces to an inferential bookkeeping quantity. The article must flag that active-inference theorists themselves split on whether this *explains away* felt value or *naturalises* it; cite it for the reductive reading and acknowledge the realist reading exists.

### Frankish 2016 — illusionism: felt value as quasi-phenomenal function
- **Citation**: Frankish, K. (2016). Illusionism as a theory of consciousness. *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 23(11–12), 11–39.
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — PhilPapers + IngentaConnect + author's own site agree on JCS 23(11–12):11–39, 2016. (Humanities journal, no DOI; metadata triangulated across ≥3 indices.)
- **Value-blind relevance**: Illusionism holds phenomenal consciousness is an introspective illusion; experiences have *quasi-phenomenal properties* — "non-phenomenal, physical [functional] propert[ies] that introspection typically misrepresents as phenomenal." Applied to affect: felt goodness/badness is a quasi-phenomenal functional property, not an irreducible experiential value. There is then no "phenomenal value" that could either do selection work or be epiphenomenal to it — the Map's fork dissolves at its root, because one of its premises (value is phenomenal) is rejected. This is the most radical value-blind position and the one most directly opposed to the Map's [[phenomenal-value-realism]].

### Solms & Friston 2018 — BORDERLINE, include with explicit caveat
- **Citation**: Solms, M., & Friston, K. (2018). How and why consciousness arises: some considerations from physics and physiology. *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 25(5–6), 202–238.
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — UCL Discovery eprint + PhilPapers + Semantic Scholar agree on JCS 25(5–6):202–238, 2018. (No DOI; humanities journal.)
- **Why borderline**: This paper *identifies affect with felt experience* ("deviations of internal states from preferred values are experienced as felt affects that constitute the core of consciousness itself") — that is closer to the value-SENSITIVE side. Include it only as the *contrast case* showing that the same free-energy formalism (Friston) is read by some as **naturalising felt value** (Solms/Friston) and by others as **eliminating** it (deflationary readers of Joffily & Coricelli). The steelman article should use this to make the honest point that the free-energy programme is *neutral* between the horns, not automatically a value-blind ally — a calibration the fork article's [[evidential-status-discipline]] demands. DO NOT cite Solms & Friston as a value-blind defender.

## The Value-Blind Position, Assembled at Strength

A single coherent rival emerges. Call it the **mechanism-sufficiency thesis**: every explanatory job we want "value" to do is done by control-theoretic and computational quantities, so positing an irreducible experiential value that *also* does causal work is explanatorily idle (and, by Occam, to be cut).

- **The learning job** is done by reward-prediction error (Schultz; Dayan & Niv): behaviour is shaped by a teaching signal, not a feeling.
- **The motivation job** is done by incentive-salience computation and homeostatic state (Berridge's "wanting"; Winkielman & Berridge's unconscious affect): organisms are pulled toward cues by a mechanism that demonstrably runs without — and prior to — conscious feeling.
- **The selection-among-options job** is done by affordance competition biased by decision variables (Cisek): a non-valence currency suffices for flexible goal-directed choice.
- **The valence job itself** is *re-defined* as a formal property of a self-organising system (Joffily & Coricelli; Hesp et al.): "value" is the rate of uncertainty reduction or the precision of a generative model.
- **The phenomenology job** — "but it FEELS good" — is dissolved by illusionism (Frankish): the felt quality is a quasi-phenomenal functional property misrepresented by introspection, with no extra experiential fact to explain.

The evolutionary clincher the steelman should press: each capacity above is more parsimoniously explained as arising *because it controls behaviour adaptively*, with consciousness (where present) riding on top. Winkielman & Berridge's point that affective systems "originated prior to systems for conscious awareness" is the empirical spearhead — it makes value-blind mechanism the phylogenetic *default* and felt value the late, possibly inert, add-on. This is the strongest form of the rival the Map must out-argue rather than out-assert.

## The Map's Register-Appropriate Reasons for Declining (NOT endorsement)

Stated briefly, to be expanded by the downstream article — and stated *as the Map's reasons*, not as refutations the steelman concedes:

1. **The hard problem is untouched.** RPE, affordance competition, and free-energy valence are all *functional/computational* accounts; by the Map's [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard-problem]] commitment, they explain the *Easy Problems* (discrimination, control, report) and leave *why there is something it is like* to undergo them unaddressed. The mechanism-sufficiency thesis is question-begging against the realist *if* phenomenal value is real — which is exactly the premise illusionism must deny, returning the dispute to the prior question of whether illusionism is true.
2. **Illusionism is the load-bearing premise, and the Map rejects it.** The value-blind horn only fully dissolves the fork if felt value is illusory (Frankish). The Map's [[phenomenal-value-realism]] and [[consciousness-value-connection]] reject this at the framework's foundations; so the steelman's most radical move is unavailable to it *on the Map's commitments*, while its more moderate moves (RPE, Cisek) leave felt value real-but-possibly-epiphenomenal — which is the *internal* tension the fork already tracks, not a defeat.
3. **The formalism is neutral.** Solms & Friston vs the deflationary reading of Joffily & Coricelli show the free-energy programme does not settle the horn; citing it as value-blind would be the same evidential over-reach the Map forbids in the other direction.
4. **Calibration, both ways.** Per [[evidential-status-discipline]], the steelman strengthens the rival to *realistic-possibility* status as a rival research programme — it does NOT downgrade the Map's view, because the dispute turns on the prior, unsettled hard-problem/illusionism question rather than on any datum the steelman owns. The Map holds the value-sensitive (or at least value-real) view on its prior commitments, conceding that mechanism-sufficiency is the rival to beat, not a position already beaten.

## Major Positions

### Position A — Mechanism-sufficiency / value-blind (the steelmanned rival)
- **Proponents (representative, none committed to the Map's framing)**: Schultz (RPE neurophysiology); Dayan, Niv (computational RL); Cisek (control-theoretic action selection); Joffily & Coricelli, Hesp et al. (valence-as-inference, deflationary reading); Frankish (illusionism — the radical wing).
- **Core claim**: "Value" is a functional/dispositional gloss on mechanism; no irreducible experiential value does causal work. Felt wanting/liking are sub-personal computations, unconscious affect, or quasi-phenomenal functional properties.
- **Strongest support**: convergence of independent programmes (learning theory, action-selection neuroscience, free-energy modelling) on mechanistic accounts that need no experiential term; the empirical fact that affective processing can run unconsciously and is phylogenetically prior to consciousness.
- **Relation to tenets**: CONFLICTS with Tenet 1 (Dualism) in its illusionist wing; the RPE/Cisek wing is *neutral on its face* but pressures Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction) by threatening to make the evaluative dimension epiphenomenal to selection.

### Position B — Value-sensitive / phenomenal value realism (the Map's, NOT defended here)
- See [[valence-and-conscious-selection]], [[phenomenal-value-realism]], [[consciousness-value-connection]]. Held on prior commitments; this note exists to ensure Position A is not a strawman by attrition relative to it.

## Key Debates

### Is dopamine RPE a *value* signal or a *teaching* signal?
- **Sides**: RPE orthodoxy (Schultz) — teaching signal updating predictions; incentive-salience (Berridge) — "wanting" assignment. Both are mechanistic; the debate is *internal* to the value-blind camp and the steelman should present it honestly (it shows the rival is a live, self-critical research programme, not a monolith).
- **Bearing on the fork**: either way, the quantity is not a felt value — which is the steelman's point.

### Does the free-energy principle naturalise or eliminate felt value?
- **Sides**: Solms & Friston (naturalise — affect IS felt, constitutive of consciousness) vs deflationary readers of Joffily & Coricelli / Hesp et al. (value = formal quantity).
- **Current state**: unsettled; the formalism underdetermines the metaphysics. Load-bearing for the steelman's honesty.

### Is felt value real at all?
- **Sides**: illusionism (Frankish — no) vs phenomenal realism (the Map — yes).
- **Current state**: the deepest crux; the value-blind horn's most radical form stands or falls here.
