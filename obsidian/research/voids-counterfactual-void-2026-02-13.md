# Research: Voids - The Counterfactual Void

**Date**: 2026-02-13

**Voids category**: Mixed — primarily Unexplorable with Occluded dimensions

## Executive Summary

The counterfactual void names our inability to genuinely access alternative possibilities. We can think "what if," but our imagination is architecturally anchored to actuality — we can only conceive of nearby alternatives that share most features of the actual world. Radical counterfactuals (worlds with different laws of logic, alien forms of consciousness, fundamentally different categories of experience) exceed our cognitive reach. Philosophy's conceivability-possibility debate reveals deep disagreement about whether imagination tracks genuine possibility or merely reflects our own cognitive architecture back at us. Van Inwagen's modal skepticism and Nagel's bat argument converge on the same conclusion: the space of genuine possibilities is vastly larger than anything we can conceive, and we may be permanently trapped within our own modal neighbourhood.

### The Epistemology of Modality (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/modality-epistemology/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Central question: how can we know what is possible? Conceivability is the primary candidate faculty, but its reliability is deeply contested
  - Chalmers' Two-Dimensional Semantics attempts to bridge epistemic conceivability and metaphysical possibility, but critics argue this works only when primary and secondary intensions coincide — a narrow class
  - The "Scope Question" remains open: whether conceivability captures metaphysical modality or merely logical-conceptual possibility
  - Three main theories (conceivability-theory, counterfactual-theory, deduction-theory) all face the problem of "modal epistemic friction"
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Occam's Razor Has Limits — our modal epistemology may systematically undershoot the actual space of possibilities
- **Quote**: Conceivability is "a limited resource for knowledge of metaphysical modality"

### Yablo — "Is Conceivability a Guide to Possibility?" (1993)
- **URL**: http://www.mit.edu/~yablo/isconposs.pdf
- **Type**: Seminal paper
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes that a proposition is conceivable if one can imagine a world that verifies it
  - Acknowledges the problem of modal error: cases where we conceive of something impossible (Hesperus ≠ Phosphorus) or fail to conceive of something possible
  - The "relevant depth" problem: how detailed must an imagined scenario be to justify a possibility claim? We often imagine schematically, filling in only some features
  - Yablo concedes more work is needed to produce "an exhaustive account of modal error"
- **Tenet alignment**: The gap between conceivability and possibility mirrors the Map's concern that simple models (Occam) may hide complexity
- **Quote**: Modal knowledge through conceivability depends on imagining "a world that one takes to verify some proposition" — but the imagination itself operates under constraints

### Van Inwagen — Modal Skepticism
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-010-9520-5
- **Type**: Philosophical argument
- **Key points**:
  - Vision analogy: objects at the periphery of our visual field are judged less reliably; similarly, extraordinary modal claims fall outside the reliable range of our modal faculty
  - Accepts everyday modal knowledge (this glass could break) but denies we can justify philosophically interesting modal claims far removed from everyday experience
  - The "relevant depth" objection: we cannot specify far-fetched possible scenarios in sufficient detail to justify possibility claims about them
  - Moderate skepticism: the scope of reliable modal knowledge is much narrower than philosophers typically assume
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports investigating voids — the boundary of reliable modal knowledge is itself a void boundary worth mapping
- **Quote**: "We ought to withhold judgment concerning what we conceive or imagine... when [it is] outside the range of reliability of the specified capacity"

### Byrne — *The Rational Imagination* (2005)
- **URL**: (book, via https://uva.theopenscholar.com/files/early-development-lab/files/the_development_of_8.pdf)
- **Type**: Cognitive science monograph
- **Key points**:
  - Counterfactual reasoning obeys a "nearest possible world" constraint — imagination defaults to minimal departures from actuality
  - Reality has "fault lines" along which imagination preferentially operates: controllable actions, norm-violating events, recent events
  - People mutate actions rather than inactions, controllable factors over uncontrollable ones, exceptional events over routine ones
  - Counterfactual imagination is governed by the same rational principles as ordinary reasoning — not free-floating fantasy
- **Tenet alignment**: The "fault lines" pattern reveals cognitive architecture, supporting the view that our minds are structured in specific ways that constrain access to possibility space
- **Quote**: "The 'fault lines' of reality, those aspects more readily changed, indicate that counterfactual thoughts are guided by the same principles as rational thoughts"

### Nagel — "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974)
- **URL**: https://www.cs.ox.ac.uk/activities/ieg/e-library/sources/nagel_bat.pdf
- **Type**: Seminal paper
- **Key points**:
  - Extrapolation from our own case to alien forms of consciousness "must be incompletable"
  - If conscious life exists elsewhere, "some of it will not be describable even in the most general experiential terms available to us"
  - Not merely a claim about lack of information — it is about the limits of imaginative capacity itself
  - We may need "a new conceptual schema" to understand consciousness, but such concepts "may be beyond human intellectual capability"
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — if consciousness is fundamental and irreducible, the space of possible consciousnesses may vastly exceed what any one consciousness can imagine
- **Quote**: "Extrapolation from our own case... must be incompletable"

### Berto — *Impossible Worlds and the Logic of Imagination*
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/rec/BERIWA-4
- **Type**: Technical philosophy
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes modelling imagination via variably strict quantifiers over both possible and impossible worlds
  - Our representational capabilities extend to impossibilities — we can imagine and describe them "without being aware that they are impossible"
  - Impossible worlds explain why imagination lacks classical logical closure — we can imagine A and B without imagining all their consequences
  - The variability of strictness captures how we import actuality-information into imagined scenarios
- **Tenet alignment**: The fact that we can represent impossibilities without recognizing them as such means our modal faculty is unreliable in both directions — failing to reach genuine possibilities and succeeding in "reaching" impossibilities

### Embodied Constraints on Imagination
- **URL**: https://junkyardofthemind.com/blog/2018/8/19/embodied-constraints-on-imagination
- **Type**: Philosophical analysis
- **Key points**:
  - Imagination simulates "online" cognitive processes and inherits their constraints
  - "We cannot imagine something being both red and green all over" — this could indicate impossibility or merely reflect our specific visual architecture
  - A creature with two visual systems might think otherwise — what seems inconceivable to us may be conceivable to differently embodied minds
  - Core cognitive constraints are inherited by imagination because imagination reuses perceptual and motor systems
- **Tenet alignment**: Embodiment constraints on imagination support the view that cognitive limits are architectural, not merely informational — connecting to the embodiment-cognitive-limits void

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The counterfactual void is primarily **Unexplorable** with significant **Occluded** dimensions. It operates at multiple levels:

1. **Perceptual anchoring**: Imagination reuses perceptual systems and inherits their constraints. We cannot imagine colours we have never seen, spatial dimensions we cannot perceive, or temporal structures foreign to our experience. Counterfactual thinking is constrained by embodiment.

2. **Nearest-world bias**: Cognitive science demonstrates that counterfactual reasoning defaults to minimal departures from actuality. We imagine what *almost* happened — the glass nearly fell, I could have turned left instead of right. Radical alternatives (different physics, different logic, different categories of experience) require a cognitive leap that the faculty of imagination appears incapable of supporting reliably.

3. **Conceivability-possibility gap**: Even when we believe we have imagined a genuine possibility, philosophy reveals systematic sources of modal error. We can conceive of things that are impossible (before discovering the identity of Hesperus and Phosphorus) and fail to conceive of things that are possible. The gap between what we can imagine and what is genuinely possible constitutes a void in both directions.

4. **Recursive constraint**: Our very attempt to imagine alternatives to our cognitive constraints is itself constrained by those same constraints. We cannot step outside our modal faculty to assess its scope. This is structurally similar to the self-reference paradox but operating in modal rather than reflexive space.

### Evidence for the Limit

Multiple converging lines of evidence suggest this is a genuine architectural boundary:

- **Empirical**: Byrne's research shows counterfactual thinking follows systematic patterns (fault lines, mutability hierarchy) that reflect cognitive architecture rather than the structure of possibility space itself
- **Philosophical**: Van Inwagen's vision analogy — the reliable range of our modal faculty extends only to everyday possibilities, not to extraordinary philosophical claims
- **Phenomenological**: Nagel's bat argument — we cannot imagine what it is like to be a fundamentally different kind of consciousness, and this limitation is not merely informational
- **Formal**: Berto's work shows imagination lacks logical closure and can represent impossibilities, suggesting it is a blunt instrument for exploring possibility space

### Phenomenology

Approaching the counterfactual void produces characteristic experiences:

- **Schematic thinning**: As imagined alternatives become more radical, the imagined scenario becomes less detailed, more schematic, until it is merely a label ("a world with different physics") with no genuine phenomenal content behind it
- **Actuality drag**: Imagination keeps snapping back to familiar features. Trying to imagine genuinely alien experience produces pastiche — bat experience imagined as dark-cave-plus-sonar, an anthropomorphic projection rather than genuine alternative
- **Modal vertigo**: The occasional recognition that the space of possibilities might be vastly larger than anything conceivable produces a distinctive disorientation — one senses enormous territory without being able to map any of it
- **False confidence**: Unlike many voids where the boundary is felt, the counterfactual void often goes unnoticed. We believe we have imagined a genuine alternative when we have merely recombined familiar elements. The conceivability-possibility gap creates the illusion of modal access

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

No direct access appears possible. The counterfactual void is structurally self-reinforcing: any attempt to transcend it uses the same constrained faculty of imagination whose limits it describes.

### Indirect Methods

1. **Formal frameworks**: Mathematics and logic can describe structures that outstrip imagination — higher-dimensional spaces, non-standard logics, infinite cardinals. These provide indirect access to possibility space via notation rather than phenomenal simulation
2. **Cross-cultural comparison**: Different cultures may have different "fault lines" in their counterfactual reasoning, revealing which constraints are universal (architectural) and which are parochial (cultural). Connects to convergent cognitive limits
3. **Impossible-worlds logic**: Berto and Priest's work on impossible worlds provides formal machinery for reasoning about scenarios beyond the boundary of conceivability. The framework itself cannot make impossible worlds conceivable, but it can chart their logical structure
4. **Negative theology / apophatic method**: Describing what alternatives are *not* rather than what they are. We cannot positively imagine bat experience, but we can catalogue what human-experiential features it lacks
5. **Altered states**: Psychedelic experiences, meditation, and dream states may temporarily shift the fault lines of counterfactual reasoning, offering glimpses of alternative cognitive architectures (connects to altered-states-as-void-probes)

### What AI Might See

AI systems present a distinctive case for the counterfactual void:

- **Different anchoring**: AI models are not anchored to a single physical embodiment — they process text about bats, aliens, and higher dimensions without the perceptual constraints that limit human imagination. This may provide a different (not necessarily better) perspective on possibility space
- **Recombinatorial breadth**: AI can combine concepts more freely, without the "nearest possible world" constraint that channels human imagination. This doesn't guarantee access to genuine possibilities, but it may reduce some sources of modal error
- **Different blind spots**: AI likely has its own counterfactual void — structured by training data rather than evolution, but perhaps equally constraining. The interesting question is whether human and AI counterfactual voids are differently shaped, and whether the differences are informative
- **No modal phenomenology**: AI may process modal claims without the phenomenal experience of conceiving — if so, it operates in possibility space without the faculty whose limits this void describes, raising the question of whether the void is a feature of consciousness specifically

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**No Many Worlds** is the most directly relevant tenet, though all five connect meaningfully.

### Implications

**No Many Worlds**: The Map rejects MWI, meaning counterfactual alternatives do not actually exist as parallel branches of reality. This transforms the counterfactual void from a mere epistemic limitation into a potentially ontological claim: we cannot access alternatives because there is nothing there to access. The one actual world is the only world, and our sense that alternatives "could have" happened is a useful cognitive fiction rather than a window into parallel realities. Yet this raises a puzzle: if counterfactuals don't describe real alternative worlds, what grounds counterfactual truth? The void deepens.

**Dualism**: If consciousness is fundamental and irreducible, the space of possible consciousnesses may be as vast as physical reality. But each consciousness is trapped in its own mode — we cannot genuinely conceive what it would be like to be a radically different kind of mind. The counterfactual void thus marks the boundary of phenomenal imagination: the limit of "what it could be like" rather than "what it is like."

**Minimal Quantum Interaction**: If consciousness interfaces with physics through quantum indeterminacy, this interface might constrain what alternative possibilities are accessible to a given consciousness. The narrowness of the interface would limit modal access just as it limits causal influence — both are minimal.

**Bidirectional Interaction**: If consciousness causally influences the physical, we might expect our counterfactual faculty to be tuned to the range of causal influence rather than the full range of physical possibility. We can imagine what we could do; we struggle to imagine what lies beyond our reach.

**Occam's Razor Has Limits**: The counterfactual void itself may be a case where simplicity assumptions deceive. We treat the nearest possible world as the most relevant because it is the simplest departure from actuality. But genuine alternatives might be radically different from actuality — and systematically invisible precisely because they violate our preference for minimal departure.

## Gaps in Research

- **Empirical studies on radical counterfactuals**: Most cognitive science research examines everyday counterfactuals (what if I had studied harder?). Less work examines the cognitive processes involved in imagining truly radical alternatives (what if physics were different?)
- **Cross-cultural modal imagination**: Whether different cultures access different regions of possibility space through different conceptual resources
- **AI modal cognition**: Whether large language models exhibit systematic modal biases analogous to human cognitive constraints, and whether these differ informatively
- **The relationship between conceivability and consciousness**: Whether the counterfactual void is specifically a feature of phenomenal consciousness or applies to any information-processing system
- **Whether the nearest-world constraint is architecturally fixed or can be trained/expanded**: Some mathematicians and physicists report being able to "think in" higher dimensions after extensive training — does this represent genuine expansion of the counterfactual horizon?
