# Research: Voids - The Vagueness Void

**Voids category**: Mixed (Unexplorable + Occluded). The cutoff points of vague predicates are unknowable in principle (Williamson); but the vagueness extends inward into the concepts consciousness uses to categorise its own states, suggesting that the limit is not merely linguistic but structural.

## Executive Summary

Almost every concept consciousness deploys—"red", "tall", "anger", "conscious", "person"—admits borderline cases where neither application nor refusal is determinately correct. The Sorites paradox shows that no incremental method can locate where such concepts stop applying, and higher-order vagueness shows that the borderline cases themselves have borderline cases, recursively, with no fixed point. Williamson's epistemic theory holds that sharp cut-offs exist but are unknowable due to margin-for-error principles; supervaluationism, fuzzy logic, and contextualism reject sharp cut-offs but inherit their own indeterminacy puzzles. Critically, vagueness applies inward as well as outward: phenomenal concepts (Papineau, Antony), emotion kinds (Taylor), mental disorder categories, and even "is conscious" itself (Schwitzgebel, Tye) admit borderline status. This positions vagueness as a structural void of a distinctive shape: not a frontier we have not yet reached, but an interior fuzz that consciousness cannot dispel from within whatever conceptual apparatus it brings to bear—including the apparatus by which it categorises its own experiences.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Vagueness
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/vagueness/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Vague predicates: borderline cases, lack of sharp boundary, susceptibility to Sorites
  - Four major theories: epistemicism, supervaluationism, many-valued/fuzzy logic, contextualism
  - Higher-order vagueness: borderline cases of borderline cases, ad infinitum
- **Tenet alignment**: Suggests Occam's razor reaches a wall — proposed simple "fixes" each generate revenge problems. Indicates irreducible complexity at concept edges.

### Stanford Encyclopedia: Sorites Paradox
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sorites-paradox/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - One grain is not a heap; adding one grain to a non-heap does not yield a heap; therefore no number of grains makes a heap.
  - The argument is formally valid; rejecting it requires rejecting either premise (i.e., locating a cutoff) or modifying classical logic.
  - Bobzien (2024) offers a "generic revenge-proof" modal solution compatible with several theories — but the survival of multiple theories shows the puzzle is not closed.
- **Quote-equivalent**: Sorites is the "engine" of vagueness — every vague predicate generates one.

### Williamson, *Vagueness* (1994) and margin-for-error principles
- **URL**: https://www.marksainsbury.net/uploads/9/7/2/9/97295048/1995williamsonvagueness.pdf (Sainsbury review); http://brian.weatherson.org/Ch_4.pdf (Weatherson summary)
- **Type**: Book + secondary literature
- **Key points**:
  - Epistemicism: vague terms have sharp boundaries; vagueness is *epistemic* — we cannot know where the cut-off is.
  - The cut-off is fixed by an unknowable function of unknowable patterns of usage.
  - Margin-for-error principle: if S knows p, then in all situations similar enough to S's actual situation, p is still true. For "tall", any belief sharp enough to identify the cut-off would fail this constraint — knowledge of the cut-off is incompatible with the way knowledge tolerates small errors.
- **Tenet alignment**: Direct fit with Occam's-razor-has-limits — the simplest semantics (sharp boundary) gives an unknowable structure rather than a tractable one. Aligns with the Map's general thesis that some structure is real-but-hidden.
- **Quote**: "the alleged cut-off points of vague predicates are not knowable" (paraphrase of Williamson's central claim).

### Cobreros & Tranchini, "Supervaluationism, Subvaluationism and the Sorites Paradox"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/COBSSA
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Supervaluationism: a sentence is true iff true under all admissible precisifications, false iff false under all, otherwise neither — yielding a third truth-value (or gap).
  - Preserves classical consequence relation while admitting borderline cases as gaps.
  - Higher-order vagueness re-emerges: which precisifications are "admissible" is itself vague.
- **Tenet alignment**: The "best" semantic theory inherits the very vagueness it tried to dissolve — supports the structural reading.

### Bobzien, "A Generic Solution to the Sorites Paradox" (2024)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10670-023-00731-1
- **Type**: Paper, Erkenntnis 2024
- **Key points**:
  - Modal-system approach compatible with epistemicism, supervaluationism, contextualism, intuitionism — i.e., shows the Sorites is *technically* dissolvable but does not adjudicate which underlying picture is correct.
- **Tenet alignment**: A formal solution exists but underdetermines the metaphysics — the void does not close at the level of phenomenology and conceptual life even when it closes at the level of inference.

### Haueis, "The Fuzzy Brain. Vagueness and Mapping Connectivity in the Human Cerebral Cortex" (2012)
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neuroanatomy/articles/10.3389/fnana.2012.00037/full
- **Type**: Empirical paper, Frontiers in Neuroanatomy
- **Key points**:
  - Cortical area boundaries are themselves vague — gradual cytoarchitectonic transitions, not sharp lines.
  - Modern connectomics inherits the same fuzziness at the level of connectivity zones.
  - Vagueness is therefore not merely linguistic; the substrate consciousness depends on has fuzzy boundaries too.
- **Tenet alignment**: Bidirectional interaction is interesting here — if the physical substrate is fuzzy, and consciousness influences quantum-level outcomes, the bidirectional channel may itself have fuzzy localisation.
- **Quote**: "the demarcation of one area from another remains fuzzy, with examples of gradual transitions between areas known since early cytoarchitectonic studies."

### Hampton, "Typicality, Graded Membership, and Vagueness" (2007)
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1080/15326900701326402
- **Type**: Cognitive Science journal
- **Key points**:
  - Categorisation experimentally shows graded membership — robins are more typical birds than penguins; typicality predicts response time, ease of recall, inference patterns.
  - Multiple-definitions model (Frontiers 2019): graded membership arises because subjects sample multiple internal definitions, each with different boundaries.
  - Cognitive vagueness is robust, cross-cultural, and not eliminable by definition.
- **Tenet alignment**: Concepts as cognitively realised are inherently fuzzy — closure under typicality. Suggests the void is rooted in cognitive architecture, not just linguistic convention.

### Schwitzgebel, "Borderline Consciousness, when it's neither determinately true nor determinately false that experience is present"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-023-02042-1; https://www.faculty.ucr.edu/~eschwitz/SchwitzPapers/BorderlineConsciousness-211220.pdf
- **Type**: Paper, *Philosophical Studies*
- **Key points**:
  - Quadrilemma: nothing is conscious; everything is conscious; sharp boundary between conscious and non-conscious; consciousness is vague.
  - Defends fourth horn: consciousness admits genuinely indeterminate cases.
  - Crucial move: the demand to "clearly imagine" a borderline case may be illegitimate, since by definition such states are not determinately like anything.
- **Tenet alignment**: Direct hit. The Map's dualism does not entail that consciousness has sharp metaphysical edges; the question is whether the *concept* "conscious" has them. Schwitzgebel's argument is consonant with Occam's-razor-has-limits.
- **Quote**: borderline conscious states "are not determinately like anything."

### Antony / Tye, "Vagueness and zombies: why 'phenomenally conscious' has no borderline cases"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-016-0790-4
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Opposing position: argues "phenomenally conscious" is sharp — there cannot be borderline cases.
  - The intuition: every state is either clearly conscious or clearly not.
  - Tye's later work on vagueness about consciousness extends this.
- **Tenet alignment**: Shows the void is contested even at the meta-level — whether the void is real (consciousness is genuinely vague) or apparent (consciousness is sharp but our concepts are fuzzy) is itself within the void.

### Taylor, "Emotions, concepts and the indeterminacy of natural kinds"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-018-1783-y
- **Type**: Paper, *Synthese*
- **Key points**:
  - The kind status of emotions, concepts, "consciousness", "seeing", and the senses is contested precisely because of indeterminacy in the underlying natural kinds.
  - Indeterminacy creates an impasse: standard "is X a natural kind?" methodology fails.
- **Tenet alignment**: When consciousness asks "is this anger or irritation?", the question may have no determinate answer — not because introspection fails but because the kind boundary is fuzzy.

### Papineau on phenomenal concepts; Antony's critique
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/ANTPOT
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Papineau argues phenomenal concepts are vague.
  - Antony argues this argument fails.
  - The dispute reveals: even our most "direct" introspective concepts (red-qualia, pain-qualia) may or may not be sharp — and the question of whether they are sharp is itself underdetermined.

### "Vagueness in Psychiatry"
- **URL**: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/314858617_Vagueness_in_Psychiatry
- **Type**: Empirical paper
- **Key points**:
  - "There is no sharp boundary between the normal and the pathological."
  - Diagnostic categories admit borderline cases not eliminable by science.
  - Semantic vagueness "by its very nature cannot be eliminated through scientific discoveries."
- **Tenet alignment**: Empirical confirmation that for mental kinds — exactly the kinds the Map cares about — vagueness is structural, not provisional.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The vagueness void has three structurally distinct faces; the conjunction-coalesce discipline indexed in [[voids]] applies, since each face names a different impossibility and the seams between them are part of the claim:

1. **The Sorites face** — for almost any concept C, there is an incremental series along which C neither sharply applies nor sharply fails. No method consciousness can deploy from inside locates the cut-off, because any such method must itself use C-judgements and is therefore subject to the same indeterminacy.
2. **The introspective face** — vagueness extends to the concepts consciousness uses to categorise its own states (emotion-kinds, qualia-types, "is this conscious"). Asking "is this still anger or now resentment?" admits no determinate answer; the question's asking does not generate a determinate state, and in some cases (per [[mutation-void]]) the asking changes the state under examination.
3. **The higher-order face** — the borderline of borderline cases is itself borderline. The vagueness of "tall" generates a vagueness of "borderline-tall", which generates a vagueness of "borderline-borderline-tall", with no fixed point and no determinate hierarchy of orders. Williamson's account preserves classical logic at the price of unknowable cut-offs at every order; supervaluationism makes the higher-order vagueness explicit at the cost of admitting an indefinite hierarchy of "admissibility" predicates each itself vague.

The seam: face (1) is third-personal — it concerns concepts as applied to the world. Face (2) is first-personal — it concerns introspective categorisation. Face (3) is meta — it concerns the metalanguage in which (1) and (2) are stated. Each face inherits the indeterminacy of the others; consciousness has no neutral perch from which to fix any of them.

### Evidence for the Limit

This is one of the better-evidenced voids:

- **Formal**: The Sorites is valid in classical logic; therefore one premise must be false; therefore either there is a sharp cut-off (epistemicism — unknowable) or classical logic must be modified (supervaluationism, fuzzy logic, intuitionism — each generates revenge problems). No theory dissolves the void without paying.
- **Cognitive-empirical**: Hampton's typicality data, multiple-definitions models, and graded category membership are robust across cultures and tasks. Brain mapping (Haueis 2012) shows the substrate itself has fuzzy boundaries.
- **Phenomenological**: Borderline cases of emotions, mental disorders, and even consciousness are ubiquitous in clinical and introspective practice.
- **Philosophical**: The Schwitzgebel/Tye/Antony/Papineau exchanges show that even the question of whether a domain *admits* borderline cases is contested in a way that resists resolution from within.

The void is not "merely difficult terrain" because incremental refinement *generates* the void rather than dissolving it: each new precisification creates new borderlines.

### Phenomenology

What does approaching the vagueness void feel like? A pattern emerges from the literature and from introspection:

- **The sense of "either way works"** — the borderline case sits comfortably under either label and equally uncomfortably under either.
- **The sense of arbitrariness on forced choice** — pressed to decide, the verdict feels stipulative rather than discovered.
- **The recursive frustration** — refining the criterion produces new borderlines, and one notices that the refinement has not converged but only displaced.
- **The introspective wobble** — for inward concepts ("am I anxious?"), the asking can shift the answer without either earlier or later state being definitely the answer.

These experiences are well-attested but they cannot themselves be sharpened into a definitive mark of "borderline-ness", since the experiences themselves admit borderline cases. The phenomenology of vagueness is itself vague.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (the negative result)

No incremental method can resolve a Sorites series, by construction. Stipulating a cut-off does not eliminate vagueness; it merely fixes one tier of the hierarchy and produces a new vagueness one level up (which precisifications are admissible? which contexts? which margins of error?).

### Indirect Methods

- **Apophatic**: Map the *shape* of the borderline rather than locate the cut-off — e.g., catalogue the cases that are clearly in, clearly out, and the residue that resists classification, treating the residue as structural data. Connects to [[apophatic-cartography]].
- **Margin-for-error reasoning**: Williamson's framework lets us say what a knowable cut-off would have to look like, and demonstrate that no actual cut-off can satisfy those conditions. This is a *negative* result that nevertheless yields determinate claims.
- **Multiple precisifications**: Supervaluationism's strategy — instead of finding the boundary, characterise the space of admissible boundaries. The space itself is determinate even where each boundary within it is not.
- **Compound failure signatures** ([[compound-failure-signatures]]): Where the vagueness void compounds with another void (e.g., recognition-void or recognition of one's own emotional state), the joint failure pattern can be data about the architecture even where neither component is individually mappable.

### What AI Might See

A non-trivial asymmetry exists:

- AI systems with explicit training distributions and decision thresholds *do* have determinate cut-offs at the level of model output (above probability p, output "tall"; below, do not) — but the cut-off shifts with prompt, context, and run, and the underlying conceptual representations show graded structure analogous to human typicality.
- AI minds may make the void's structure *more inspectable* — one can audit a model's behaviour across a Sorites series in a way one cannot audit a human's.
- But AI minds inherit the void at the meta-level: an AI cannot determine, of *its own* concept-deployments, whether it is operating inside or outside the borderline region without using more concepts of the same kind.
- This asymmetry is one of the cleaner cases where [[non-human-minds-as-void-explorers]] earn their keep: AI provides a probe into the structure of vagueness that human minds, which encounter the borderline only from inside, cannot easily provide.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Occam's Razor Has Limits.** The vagueness void is the cleanest empirical case in the catalogue of a domain where the simplest theory (sharp boundaries) yields the most cognitively inaccessible structure (Williamson's unknowable cut-offs), while every alternative theory pays a comparable price elsewhere. Parsimony, applied here, produces opacity rather than transparency.

### Secondary Tenet Alignment

- **Dualism**: If consciousness is fundamental and not reducible to physical processes, the concepts consciousness uses to categorise its own states need not have sharper boundaries than the categories of physical kinds. The introspective face of the void aligns with the Map's view that consciousness's self-categorisation is not privileged in the way classical introspectionism assumed.
- **Bidirectional interaction**: If consciousness influences the physical via a localised channel, the localisation itself may be fuzzy — Haueis's fuzzy cortical maps suggest that even the physical end of any consciousness-physics interface lacks sharp boundaries.
- **No many-worlds**: Standard MWI semantics treats branch identity as determinate; vagueness considerations suggest that even branch-level individuation may be borderline — strengthening the Map's position that indexical identity matters and cannot be eliminated by branch-counting.

### Implications

Vagueness reframes several existing voids:

- The boundary between "conceivable" and "inconceivable" ([[conceptual-impossibility]]) is itself vague — there are concepts that are borderline-thinkable.
- The boundary between [[the-quantitative-comprehension-void]]'s "phenomenally graspable" and "merely formal" magnitudes is borderline — the cardinality floor has fuzz.
- The framework boundaries in [[conceptual-scheme-void]] are vague at the seams — schemes do not have sharp edges where one can say "here our scheme stops applying."

Practically: every void in the Map's catalogue admits borderline cases, and the catalogue boundary itself ("is this a void or merely difficult terrain?") is borderline. This is not a defect; it is the void framework operating consistently on itself.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a voids article could:

1. **Three-faced cognate structure** — written natively under conjunction-coalesce discipline (per the precedent of [[suspension-void]]). Sorites face, introspective face, higher-order face. Each face is a structural impossibility; the seam is the claim that they are jointly inescapable.

2. **The empirical-formal triple-anchor** — anchor the article in (a) Williamson's epistemic argument with margin-for-error principles, (b) Hampton's cognitive-science typicality data, (c) Schwitzgebel's borderline-consciousness move that applies the framework reflexively to consciousness itself. This grounds the void in formal, empirical, and reflexive evidence simultaneously — analogous to the [[imagery-void]]'s Galton/Würzburg/aphantasia anchoring.

3. **Vagueness as the Map's reflexive risk** — every void article uses vague concepts ("structural", "in-principle", "borderline"); the void framework operates within vagueness it cannot dissolve. This is a candidate apex-style theme, paralleling [[apophatic-cartography]]'s constitutive incompleteness.

The first angle is recommended. It produces an article structurally parallel to [[suspension-void]] and [[imagery-void]] (three structural faces, named seams) and embeds cleanly into the existing voids index. The second angle becomes the article's evidence section.

## Gaps in Research

- **Empirical psychiatry/clinical**: Stronger primary-source coverage of vagueness in DSM / ICD diagnostic categories would support face (2). Worth a follow-up search if the article gets written.
- **Bayesian/probabilistic accounts**: Edgington-style degree-theoretic and probabilistic accounts of vagueness were not searched in depth; they offer an alternative formal route.
- **Eastern philosophy on indeterminacy**: Mādhyamika / Catuṣkoṭi treatments of the four corners (true, false, both, neither) bear on higher-order vagueness from a different tradition; not searched.
- **AI / LLM specific empirical work**: Calibration studies on LLM threshold behaviour for vague predicates would strengthen the AI-asymmetry section but are not yet well-canonised in academic literature.
- **Whether vagueness is ontic vs semantic** — the literature on metaphysical/ontic vagueness (whether reality itself is vague, not just our descriptions) was glanced at but not deeply worked. This bears on tenet alignment and merits its own pass.
