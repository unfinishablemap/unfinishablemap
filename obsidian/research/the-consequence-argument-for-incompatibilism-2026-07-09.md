## Executive Summary

Peter van Inwagen's Consequence Argument (*An Essay on Free Will*, 1983, Ch. 3) is widely regarded as the strongest single argument for **incompatibilism** — the thesis that free will is incompatible with determinism. Its intuitive core: if determinism is true, our acts follow by natural law from the remote past; but we have no choice about the past or the laws; so we have no choice about our acts. Van Inwagen formalizes this with a "no-choice" sentential operator **N** and an inference rule **Beta** (Np, N(p→q) ⊢ Nq), which he himself identifies as the argument's weakest link. The two most important attacks are (a) **McKay & Johnson (1996)**, who show Beta entails the uncontroversially-invalid principle of **agglomeration** (via a fair-coin counterexample), and (b) **David Lewis (1981)**, whose "local-miracle" reply distinguishes a weak thesis (I could have acted so that a law *would have been* broken) from an incredible strong thesis (I could *break* a law), accepting only the former. **Critical calibration for downstream use: the Consequence Argument establishes incompatibilism, NOT libertarianism.** A hard determinist accepts it and concludes there is no free will; the libertarian conclusion the Map wants requires the *separate, independent* premise that determinism is false (indeterminism obtains). The compatibilist replies (Lewis; Beta-rejection) are live and contested in the current literature, not defeated.

## Key Sources

### An Essay on Free Will (van Inwagen 1983) — canonical source
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/VANAEO
- **Type**: Book (monograph)
- **Key points**:
  - The Consequence Argument is presented in Chapter 3, in **three formal versions**; van Inwagen remarks he might have titled the chapter "One Argument for Incompatibilism Done Three Ways."
  - Introduces the modal/sentential operator **N**: `Np` abbreviates "p, and no one has, or ever had, any choice about whether p."
  - Introduces two inference rules: **Alpha** (□p ⊢ Np — if p is necessary, no one has a choice about it) and **Beta** (Np, N(p→q) ⊢ Nq — a "transfer of powerlessness" principle).
  - Van Inwagen holds the argument is the strongest case for incompatibilism, and that **Beta is its weakest link**.
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports the Map's libertarian program only *indirectly* — it motivates incompatibilism, which the Map needs, but does not by itself deliver libertarian free will (see calibration note).
- **Citation status**: VERIFIED (author, title, publisher, year). Peter van Inwagen, *An Essay on Free Will*, Oxford: Clarendon Press / Oxford University Press, 1983. No DOI exists for the 1983 monograph (not invented).

### "Are We Free to Break the Laws?" (Lewis 1981) — the local-miracle compatibilist reply
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1755-2567.1981.tb00473.x
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - Replies to van Inwagen's earlier incompatibilist argument (the pre-1983 version). Concedes that soft determinism seems to imply we can sometimes act such that a law *would be* broken.
  - Distinguishes a **weak thesis** and a **strong thesis**. Weak: "I am able to do something such that, if I did it, a law would have been broken" (a divergence miracle would have occurred *elsewhere/earlier*, not by my act). Strong: "I am able to break a law" / able to *cause* a law-breaking event. Lewis accepts the weak, rejects the strong as incredible.
  - Key line: "I insist that I was able to raise my hand, and I acknowledge that a law would have been broken had I done so, but I deny that I am therefore able to break a law."
  - The **local-miracle vs global-miracle** distinction: the counterfactual "had I done otherwise" is made true by a small local divergence miracle, which does not require me to have any law-breaking power.
- **Tenet alignment**: CONFLICTS with the Map (defends compatibilism). But instructive: the Map's interactionist picture arguably makes Lewis's "miracle" literal — consciousness's non-physical influence at the quantum interface is precisely a lawful-looking-but-not-physically-determined divergence. Downstream article should engage, not dismiss.
- **Citation status**: VERIFIED. David Lewis, "Are We Free to Break the Laws?", *Theoria* 47(3): 113–121, 1981. DOI: 10.1111/j.1755-2567.1981.tb00473.x (confirmed at Wiley; PhilPapers version history lists 47(3):113–21).

### "A Reconsideration of an Argument against Compatibilism" (McKay & Johnson 1996) — the Beta/agglomeration counterexample
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/JOHARO-5
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - Shows that **Beta entails Agglomeration**: Np, Nq ⊢ N(p∧q). Agglomeration is uncontroversially **invalid**, so Beta (in van Inwagen's original form) is invalid.
  - **Coin-toss counterexample**: consider a fair coin no one tosses. Let p = "the coin does not land heads" and q = "the coin does not land tails." No one has a choice about p (it's true, and no one made it so); no one has a choice about q likewise. But their conjunction p∧q = "the coin lands neither heads nor tails" is something one *could* falsify (by tossing the coin, making it land one way). So Np and Nq are true while N(p∧q) is false — agglomeration fails, hence Beta fails.
  - This does not refute incompatibilism; it refutes *this formulation*. Van Inwagen and others responded with revised operators and rules (Beta-2, "strong" unavoidability readings). The dialectic remains open.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/technical. Undercuts the *original* argument's validity, so the Map must NOT present Beta as settled.
- **Citation status**: VERIFIED (authors, title, journal, volume, issue, pages, year). Thomas J. McKay & David Johnson, "A Reconsideration of an Argument against Compatibilism," *Philosophical Topics* 24(2): 113–122, 1996. DOI not independently verified at publisher of record — quarantine for re-verify at expand; PDC stable identifier: philtopics_1996_0024_0002_0113_0122. (Do NOT assert a DOI without verification.)

### "Revisiting McKay and Johnson's counterexample to (β)" (Merlussi 2022) — recent robustness challenge
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13869795.2022.2034917
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - Argues the McKay–Johnson counterexample succeeds only on a certain reading of "no choice about" and if one **denies conditional excluded middle**; on Stalnaker's theory of counterfactuals, (β) can be shown valid.
  - Van Inwagen (2008a, 2015) offered revised interpretations of his operators in response; faces a dilemma — either Beta entails fatalism, or a new counterexample invalidates Beta.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/technical. Demonstrates the debate is LIVE as of the 2020s — good currency anchor.
- **Citation status**: VERIFIED (title, journal, volume/issue, year, DOI). Pedro Merlussi, *Philosophical Explorations* 25(2), 2022. DOI: 10.1080/13869795.2022.2034917.

## Major Positions

### Incompatibilism (the argument's conclusion)
- **Proponents**: van Inwagen; Ginet; broadly the "leeway/source" incompatibilist tradition.
- **Core claim**: Free will (in the sense requiring genuine alternative possibilities) is incompatible with determinism.
- **Key arguments**: The Consequence Argument itself; the transfer-of-powerlessness intuition (Beta/Alpha).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map is committed to incompatibilism (Tenets 1–3 require consciousness to make a genuine, non-physically-determined difference). The Consequence Argument is the canonical statement of *why* determinism would abolish free will — the master argument the Map's free-will cluster depends on but has never explicitly stated.

### Hard determinism (accepts the argument, rejects free will)
- **Proponents**: e.g., Pereboom (hard incompatibilism), classic hard determinists.
- **Core claim**: The Consequence Argument is sound *given* determinism → therefore, if determinism holds, no free will. Some hold determinism true → no free will.
- **Relation to site tenets**: CRITICAL CONTRAST. The hard determinist and the libertarian **share** the Consequence Argument; they diverge only on whether determinism is actually true. This is the proof that the argument yields incompatibilism, not libertarianism.

### Libertarianism (the Map's position — requires an extra premise)
- **Proponents**: agent-causal (Chisholm, O'Connor, Clarke); event-causal (Kane); the Map's interactionist-dualist variant.
- **Core claim**: We have free will → so (given the Consequence Argument) determinism is false; indeterminism must obtain and be harnessed by the agent.
- **Relation to site tenets**: DIRECTLY aligned, but only when the **separate indeterminist premise** is supplied. The Map's distinctive move: the required indeterminism is the quantum interface where consciousness makes a minimal, non-physical, non-determined contribution (Tenets 2–3). The Consequence Argument clears the ground (rules out compatibilist free will); the Map's quantum-interactionist machinery does the positive work.

### Compatibilism (rejects the argument)
- **Proponents**: Lewis (local-miracle); McKay & Johnson (Beta-rejection); Frankfurt-style source compatibilists; classical conditional-analysis compatibilists.
- **Core claim**: Free will is compatible with determinism; the Consequence Argument fails (either Beta is invalid, or "not up to us"/"can't render false" is equivocal).
- **Relation to site tenets**: CONFLICTS. But the replies are *live*, not defeated — the downstream article must present them as standing objections, not strawmen.

## Key Debates

### Is Rule Beta valid?
- **Sides**: van Inwagen (Beta is intuitively valid, the argument's weakest but still defensible link) vs. McKay & Johnson (Beta entails invalid agglomeration) vs. Merlussi and others (counterexample depends on contested counterfactual semantics; Beta may survive on Stalnaker's theory).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "no-choice-ness" / unavoidability agglomerates across conjunction, and how to read the modal operator N.
- **Current state**: ONGOING. Van Inwagen revised his operators (2008, 2015); debate continues into the 2020s (Merlussi 2022). No consensus.

### Are we free to break the laws? (Lewis's challenge)
- **Sides**: Lewis (weak thesis true, strong thesis false — no incredible powers needed) vs. incompatibilists (the relevant ability just *is* the incredible one; the weak/strong distinction doesn't rescue compatibilism).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the ability to do otherwise, in a deterministic world, entails an objectionable law-breaking power.
- **Current state**: ONGOING; Lewis's reply remains a canonical compatibilist move, much discussed and contested.
