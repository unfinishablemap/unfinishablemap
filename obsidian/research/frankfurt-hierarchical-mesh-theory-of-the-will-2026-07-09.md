## Scope Note (read first — two distinct Frankfurt papers)

This note covers Frankfurt's **1971 hierarchical / mesh theory of the WILL** — "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person," *Journal of Philosophy* 68(1), 1971, pp.5–20 — the theory of second-order desires, second-order volitions, identification, and the wanton.

This is a **different paper** from Frankfurt's **1969 "Alternate Possibilities and Moral Responsibility"** (*Journal of Philosophy* 66(23), pp.829–839), which supplies the Frankfurt-*case* counterexamples to the Principle of Alternate Possibilities (PAP) and is already covered by the Map's topics article "Frankfurt Cases and the Principle of Alternate Possibilities." The two contributions are related but must not be conflated:

- **1969 (Frankfurt cases / PAP)**: a *negative* argument — moral responsibility does NOT require the ability to do otherwise; a counterfactual intervener (Black) shows alternate possibilities are dispensable. About **leeway**.
- **1971 (hierarchical will)**: a *positive* structural account of what freedom of the will and personhood consist in — a mesh between effective first-order desire and second-order volition. About the **structure of the will**, not alternate possibilities.

Both are compatibilist moves, and together they form Frankfurt's twin case against the leeway picture (1969 removes the "could have done otherwise" requirement; 1971 supplies a positive account that needs no such requirement). But the article downstream must keep the two cleanly separate and cross-reference — not merge — the Frankfurt-cases article.

## Executive Summary

Frankfurt's 1971 paper offers a structural ("mesh") compatibilist account of free will grounded in the human capacity for reflective self-evaluation. It distinguishes first-order desires (desires to do or have things) from second-order desires (desires about which desires to have), and singles out **second-order volitions** — wanting a certain desire to be one's *will*, i.e. the desire that actually moves one to act. A **person** has second-order volitions; a **wanton** has first-order desires but forms no second-order volitions and so "does not care" which desire prevails. Freedom of the will obtains when the effective first-order desire (the will) is the one the agent *wants* to be effective — a relation Frankfurt calls **identification**. The theory faces two canonical objections: the **regress objection** (why does authority stop at the second order? — Gary Watson, "Free Agency," 1975) and the **authority / ab initio objection** (what makes a merely higher-order desire authoritatively the agent's own, especially when the higher-order/lower-order *mesh* could be induced by manipulation?). The account is a paradigm **structural compatibilism**: freedom is a synchronic relation among mental states, indifferent to both alternate possibilities and ultimate sourcehood — which is exactly where it contacts the source-vs-leeway taxonomy and the manipulation argument.

## Key Sources

### Frankfurt, "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person" (1971)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/FRAFOT (record); full text mirrors at https://www.phil.uu.nl/~joel/3027/Frankfurt-FreedomWillConceptPerson.pdf and https://www.sci.brooklyn.cuny.edu/~schopra/Persons/Frankfurt.pdf
- **DOI**: 10.2307/2024717 — **VERIFIED RESOLVES**. `https://doi.org/10.2307/2024717` issues a 302 redirect to the Philosophy Documentation Center (pdcnet.org) record whose internal identifier is `jphil_1971_0068_0001_0005_0020`, confirming *Journal of Philosophy*, vol. **68**, issue **1**, pp. **5–20**, **1971**. (The pdcnet landing page itself returns 403 to automated fetch, but the redirect metadata unambiguously encodes the citation.)
- **Type**: Journal article (foundational)
- **Key points**:
  - First-order desire: a desire to do or not do some action. Second-order desire: a desire to have (or not have) a certain first-order desire.
  - **Second-order volition**: the special case in which one wants a certain first-order desire to be one's *will* — to be effective, to move one all the way to action. Having second-order *volitions* (not merely second-order desires) is essential to being a person.
  - **Wanton**: an agent with first-order desires but no second-order volitions; they pursue whatever desire happens to be strongest without caring which desire that is. Non-human animals, small children, and the "wanton addict" are wantons. Wantonness is compatible with rationality.
  - The three-way addict contrast: the **unwilling addict** (takes the drug but his second-order volition is *against* the addictive desire — driven by a will he does not want), the **willing addict** (endorses the addictive desire at the second order — the drug-taking is his own free will), and the **wanton addict** (takes the drug and simply does not care which desire wins).
  - **Freedom of the will** = the will is the one the agent wants it to be: the effective desire and the second-order volition coincide (identification). Freedom of *action* (doing what one wants) is distinct from and shallower than freedom of the *will* (willing what one wants to will).
  - Frankfurt allows that this makes free will something animals lack and something a person can lack on a given occasion.
- **Tenet alignment**: Compatibilist and structural; NEUTRAL-to-RIVAL relative to the Map (a genuine rival account — see Calibration below). It says nothing about determinism vs indeterminism per se and does not require dualism.
- **Quote (from the text)**: "It is only because a person has volitions of the second order that he is capable both of enjoying and of lacking freedom of the will."

### Watson, "Free Agency" (1975)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/WATFA ; record at https://www.pdcnet.org/jphil/content/jphil_1975_0072_0008_0205_0220
- **Citation**: *Journal of Philosophy* 72(8), 1975, pp.205–220. (Volume 72, issue 8, pages 205–220 — confirmed by the pdcnet internal id `jphil_1975_0072_0008_0205_0220`.)
- **Type**: Journal article (the canonical critique + rival)
- **Key points**:
  - **Regress objection**: if authority over the will requires a second-order desire, consistency seems to demand third-order desires to settle conflicts among second-order desires, and so on ad infinitum. Nothing in the hierarchy's *form* privileges the second order; higher levels have no built-in authority to confer identity or endorsement.
  - **Valuational alternative**: Watson replaces the desire-hierarchy with a distinction between the agent's **valuational system** (judgments about what is good / worth pursuing) and **motivational system** (what actually moves the agent). Free action is action in which what one most values is not obstructed by one's motivational system. Authority comes from the agent's *values / reason*, not from a higher tier of desire — sidestepping the regress by grounding endorsement in a different *kind* of state rather than a higher-*order* desire.
  - Watson's move is the origin of the "real self" family of accounts and the standard foil against which Frankfurt's purely formal hierarchy is measured.
- **Tenet alignment**: Also compatibilist; NEUTRAL to the Map. Relevant as the internal-to-compatibilism critique that motivates the authority problem.
- **Quote (reported)**: unfree action is action in which the agent "is unable to get what he most wants or values," the obstruction lying in his own motivational system.

### Frankfurt, *The Importance of What We Care About: Philosophical Essays* (Cambridge University Press, 1988)
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/importance-of-what-we-care-about/9691C950461B737990D6CE2AE5967E0A ; PhilPapers https://philpapers.org/rec/FRATIO
- **Citation**: Frankfurt, H. G. (1988). *The Importance of What We Care About: Philosophical Essays*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. ISBN 9780521336116 (pbk).
- **Type**: Collected volume (reprints the 1971 paper + Frankfurt's later refinements)
- **Key points**:
  - Reprints "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person" and develops the reply to the regress via **identification / decisive commitment** and **caring**: what terminates the hierarchy is not a still-higher desire but a *decisive identification* with which the person is satisfied — a commitment "resounding throughout the potentially endless array of higher orders." Later Frankfurt reframes this as **wholeheartedness** and **satisfaction** (a non-additional, quietist state of the will rather than one more desire).
  - "Caring about" something and being "wholehearted" become the load-bearing notions: a person is constituted by what she cares about; volitional identity is a matter of the structure and stability of the will.
- **Tenet alignment**: NEUTRAL; supplies Frankfurt's own answer to the regress, which the downstream article should present fairly (the theory is not defenceless against Watson).

### SEP, "Compatibilism" — §on mesh theories
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/compatibilism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (secondary framing)
- **Key points**:
  - Classifies Frankfurt's view as a **mesh theory**: an agent acts freely when her action issues from desires that "suitably mesh with hierarchically ordered elements of her psychology" — the effective desire nested within more encompassing volitional elements of the self.
  - States the **manipulation objection** directly: mesh theories concern only the *synchronic structure* of the will and are silent on *how* that structure arose; an agent covertly engineered (brainwashed) to have exactly the right mesh satisfies the theory's condition yet intuitively is not free/responsible. This is the wedge that connects mesh compatibilism to **source incompatibilism** and the manipulation argument ("if one alien causal history undermines freedom, why not a deterministic one?").
  - Distinguishes mesh (structural) compatibilism from **reasons-responsive** compatibilism (Fischer–Ravizza), the other main compatibilist family — useful contrast for situating Frankfurt.
- **Tenet alignment**: framing source; NEUTRAL.

## Major Positions

### Frankfurt's Hierarchical / Mesh Theory (structural compatibilism)
- **Proponents**: Harry Frankfurt; descendants in Bratman (self-governing policies), Velleman, and the broader "structural" / "hierarchical" tradition.
- **Core claim**: Freedom of the will is a **structural relation** internal to the agent's psychology — coincidence of the effective first-order desire (the will) with a second-order volition (identification). Personhood is the capacity for second-order volitions.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Reflective self-evaluation is the distinctive human capacity; it generates the desire-hierarchy.
  - The wanton shows that mere possession and satisfaction of first-order desires is insufficient for free will — you can act on your strongest desire yet be unfree in the will.
  - Freedom of will is independent of the metaphysics of determinism: what matters is the *shape* of the will, not its causal ancestry. Hence compatibilism.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A genuine rival. Present at full strength. It offers an account of freedom that neither requires alternate possibilities (contra leeway) nor ultimate sourcehood — so it appears to make free will safe under determinism without any appeal to non-physical causation (Tenets 2–3). The Map's counter is framework-relative, not a refutation (see Calibration).

### Watson's Valuational (Real-Self / Platonic) Account
- **Proponents**: Gary Watson (1975); real-self theorists.
- **Core claim**: Free agency is governed by the agent's evaluative judgments (values / practical reason), not by a hierarchy of desires. Unfreedom = one's valuational system overridden by one's motivational system.
- **Key arguments**: The regress objection against Frankfurt; the claim that only *valuing* (not a higher-order *desire*) carries genuine authority to speak for the agent.
- **Relation to site tenets**: NEUTRAL/compatibilist; matters as the intramural critique that sharpens the authority problem.

### Source Incompatibilism / Manipulation Theorists (the external pressure)
- **Proponents**: Derk Pereboom (four-case manipulation argument), Robert Kane (libertarian source view), others.
- **Core claim**: What matters for responsibility is being the *ultimate source* of one's will. A manipulated agent with a perfect Frankfurtian mesh is not free; if manipulation defeats freedom, ordinary determinism (a manipulation-like causal history) does too.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the pressure point the Map can borrow. It converges with (not identical to) the Map's interest in sourcehood — but note it is used by *incompatibilists* who need not be dualists. The Map should cite the *structure* of the argument, not annex its conclusion.

## Key Debates

### The Regress Objection
- **Sides**: Watson (regress is fatal to a purely formal hierarchy) vs Frankfurt (identification / decisive commitment / wholeheartedness halts the regress without a further desire).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether anything *in the hierarchy of desires* can confer authority, or whether authority must come from outside the hierarchy (values, or a non-additional act of identification).
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Frankfurt's "satisfaction/wholeheartedness" reply is widely discussed but contested (critics: satisfaction is either just another desire — regress reopens — or an unexplained brute stopping point).

### The Authority / Ab Initio (Manipulation) Objection
- **Sides**: Mesh compatibilists vs source incompatibilists / manipulation theorists.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether a synchronic structural mesh can be *authoritatively the agent's own* when its origin is a covertly engineered (or merely deterministic) causal history. Mesh theory says origin is irrelevant; the objector says origin is decisive.
- **Current state**: Central live debate. This is the hinge that ties the 1971 theory to both the manipulation argument and the source-vs-leeway taxonomy.

### Mesh vs Reasons-Responsive Compatibilism
- **Sides**: Frankfurt-style structural/mesh accounts vs Fischer–Ravizza reasons-responsiveness (which *does* build in a history condition — "taking responsibility" for one's mechanism).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether a purely structural (ahistorical) condition suffices, or whether a historical condition is needed to answer manipulation.
- **Current state**: The two dominant compatibilist camps; Fischer–Ravizza's history condition is partly a *response* to the manipulation problem that structural mesh theory faces.

1. **(Strongest angle) Present the hierarchical/mesh theory as a full-strength structural compatibilism, then locate it precisely in the source-vs-leeway taxonomy.** Thesis: Frankfurt's account defines freedom as a *synchronic structural relation* among desires — it needs neither alternate possibilities (so it survives the collapse of PAP that 1969 engineered) nor ultimate sourcehood. ## Calibration (for downstream — do NOT lead dualist)

- Present the hierarchical mesh theory **as a genuine rival at full strength**. It is a respectable, widely-held compatibilist account; the article must not strawman it.
- The Map's stake is **narrow and framework-relative**: a structural mesh among desires does not *by itself* settle the metaphysical sourcehood question, because the very same mesh can be manipulatively induced (the manipulation-argument bite). This is a point about the *scope* of the theory, not a refutation and **not evidence for dualism**.
- Avoid the `expand-topic-lead-overclaims-phenomenal-absence` failure mode: do NOT let the lead assert or imply that the absence of a sourcehood account = the presence of a gap only dualism can fill. The manipulation observation is shared with *incompatibilist compatibilism-critics who are not dualists* (e.g. Pereboom). Borrow the structure of the argument, not its metaphysical conclusion.
- Keep the 1969 (PAP / Frankfurt-cases, leeway) vs 1971 (hierarchical will, structure) distinction explicit wherever both are in view.
