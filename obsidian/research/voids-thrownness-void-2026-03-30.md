# Research: Voids - The Thrownness Void

**Date**: 2026-03-30
**Search queries used**: "Heidegger Geworfenheit thrownness cognitive limits consciousness philosophy", "thrownness unchosen existence epistemic limits philosophy of mind", "Sartre facticity unchosen embodiment consciousness radical contingency existentialism", "Levinas il y a anonymous existence consciousness prior to choice", "natality Arendt thrownness birth unchosen beginning philosophy", "McGinn mysterianism cognitive closure unchosen cognitive limits", "radical passivity consciousness given to itself phenomenology Levinas Henry"
**Voids category**: Mixed — Unexplorable and Occluded

## Executive Summary

The thrownness void names a distinctive cognitive limit: consciousness cannot access, authorise, or revise the conditions under which it became conscious. Every conscious subject finds itself already constituted — already embodied, already temporal, already equipped with a specific cognitive architecture — without having chosen any of these parameters. This is not the question of why consciousness exists at all (the origin void), nor why one is this particular subject (the indexical void), nor why one cannot opt out (the involuntariness void). It is the inaccessibility of the pre-constitutional moment itself: the gap between non-existence and existence that consciousness can never bridge from either side. Heidegger's Geworfenheit, Sartre's facticity, Levinas's il y a, and Arendt's natality each approach this void from different angles, converging on the recognition that consciousness is radically passive with respect to its own establishment.

## Key Sources

### Heidegger — Geworfenheit (Thrownness)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrownness
- **Type**: Encyclopedia / Secondary
- **Key points**:
  - Dasein is "thrown" into the world — born into a specific family, culture, historical moment, with no prior choice
  - One is "never in control of one's own being from the ground up"
  - Thrownness is not a one-time event but a permanent structural feature — Dasein continues to be thrown as long as it exists
  - Factical thrownness places us in a state of existential guilt — lacking justification for the being we are
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — the feeling of self-evident existence masks genuine mystery about why this particular consciousness exists with these parameters
- **Quote**: "We are never in control of our own being from the ground up."

### Sartre — Facticity and Radical Contingency
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/sartre-ex/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Facticity: the given, inalterable aspects of situation — body, social class, past, world — none chosen
  - Consciousness is "trapped in the facticity of its given bodies while simultaneously yearning to overcome their contingent reality"
  - The body is "the contingent form assumed by the necessity of one's contingency"
  - Freedom operates within facticity but cannot revise facticity's foundations — you can choose what to do with your thrownness but not whether to be thrown
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — if consciousness is non-physical, its entrapment in a particular body and cognitive architecture becomes even more mysterious. Why is this non-physical consciousness bound to these specific physical constraints?
- **Quote**: "The body is nothing other than the for-itself... the contingent form which is assumed by the necessity of one's contingency."

### Levinas — The Il y a (There Is)
- **URL**: https://www.pomoculture.org/2013/09/05/terror-of-the-ethical-on-levinass-il-y-a/
- **Type**: Article / Secondary
- **Key points**:
  - The il y a is "anonymous, neutral persistence of being that precedes subjectivity" — existence before any subject exists to experience it
  - Consciousness emerges from the il y a through "hypostasis" — a taking-up of position that the subject did not initiate
  - The il y a is experienced as horror (insomnia: being unable to not-be, the impossibility of nothingness)
  - "The bare fact of presence is oppressive; one is held by being, held to be"
  - The il y a represents an exteriority "against which consciousness has no power"
- **Tenet alignment**: Bidirectional Interaction — if consciousness interacts with the physical, the il y a suggests a prior ground from which consciousness emerges that it cannot interrogate or influence
- **Quote**: "The bare fact of presence is oppressive; one is held by being, held to be. One is detached from any object, any content, yet there is presence."

### Arendt — Natality
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/archive/TOTANO-2
- **Type**: Academic paper
- **Key points**:
  - Natality: each person is thrown into unchosen conditions — race, gender, class, citizenship, historical moment
  - Philosophy conventionally centres death (Heidegger); Arendt recentres on birth as the fundamental unchosen event
  - Natality has both passive and active dimensions: passive (we cannot choose circumstances of birth), active (we can begin new things within those circumstances)
  - "We can never choose the time, the place, or the circumstances of our birth and life"
- **Tenet alignment**: No Many Worlds — in a single-world framework, birth is a unique, unrepeatable event. The thrownness is absolute because there is no other version of this consciousness with different initial conditions.

### McGinn — Cognitive Closure
- **URL**: https://colinmcginn.net/mysterianism-revisited/
- **Type**: Blog / Primary
- **Key points**:
  - A type of mind M is "cognitively closed" with respect to property P if M's concept-forming procedures cannot extend to a grasp of P
  - The structure of human minds may be constitutionally unable to grasp how consciousness arises from brain processes
  - Cognitive closure is itself unchosen — we did not select our concept-forming procedures
  - The cognitive architecture that determines what we can and cannot think was established before any conscious choice could occur
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — cognitive closure means our preference for simple theories may reflect the limits of our unchosen architecture rather than the structure of reality
- **Quote**: "A type of mind M is cognitively closed with respect to a property P if and only if the concept-forming procedures at M's disposal cannot extend to a grasp of P."

### Radical Passivity — Levinas, Merleau-Ponty, Henry
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-1-4020-9347-0
- **Type**: Academic monograph
- **Key points**:
  - "Radical passivity" names the condition of being constituted prior to any active choice — passivity that precedes the active/passive distinction
  - Levinas: ethical obligation pre-exists autonomy; you are responsible before you choose to be
  - Michel Henry: consciousness is "auto-affection" — it is given to itself, but the givenness is not a gift it requested
  - Thematising passivity risks reinserting it into active consciousness — the attempt to think thrownness transforms it into something managed
  - Both Henry and Levinas ground selfhood on passivity and affectivity rather than on rational agency
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — if consciousness is fundamental, its radical passivity (being given to itself without choosing to be) becomes a feature of reality's basic structure, not an accident of evolution

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The thrownness void is primarily **unexplorable** with an **occluded** dimension.

It is unexplorable because the pre-constitutional moment — the transition from non-consciousness to consciousness — is structurally inaccessible. Consciousness cannot witness its own inauguration. Any memory, any phenomenal trace, any conceptual grasp of the transition already presupposes an established consciousness doing the grasping. The subject cannot get behind itself to the moment before it was a subject.

It has an occluded dimension because the thrownness is not merely unknown but may be unknowable by design. If consciousness is non-physical and its embodiment in a particular cognitive architecture serves some function (whether evolutionary, simulational, or metaphysical), the opacity of the pre-constitutional moment may be load-bearing. A consciousness that could revise its own foundation might dissolve itself.

### Evidence for the Limit

Several converging lines suggest this is a genuine boundary rather than merely difficult territory:

1. **Structural impossibility**: Any investigation of one's own origins requires the very cognitive apparatus whose origin is in question. The bootstrapping problem is absolute — you cannot use consciousness to investigate a time before consciousness.

2. **Phenomenological convergence**: Heidegger, Sartre, Levinas, and Arendt — working from different starting points — all identify the same structure: a givenness that precedes choice. The convergence across independent philosophical traditions suggests a real limit rather than a shared confusion.

3. **Developmental opacity**: Cognitive science confirms that autobiographical memory does not extend to early infancy (the infant consciousness void). But the thrownness void is deeper — even if we could remember infancy, we could not remember the transition from non-consciousness to consciousness, because there was no consciousness present to encode the memory.

4. **The consent paradox**: Consent requires an existing agent. You cannot consent to your own existence because "you" do not exist prior to the existence you would need to consent to. This is not a practical difficulty but a logical impossibility — the very concept of pre-existential consent is incoherent.

### Phenomenology

The thrownness void is unusual among voids because it is not typically experienced as a limit. Most people do not feel the absence of pre-existential choice as a deprivation.

**The givenness feeling**: Consciousness presents itself as simply being there — always already running, like a programme with no visible boot sequence. The phenomenology is one of seamless presence, which is precisely how the void maintains its invisibility.

**Existential vertigo**: When the void does become phenomenally present — in philosophical reflection, in existential crisis, in the small hours — it manifests as vertigo: the sudden recognition that none of this was chosen, that the entire structure of one's conscious life rests on conditions that were simply imposed. Heidegger calls this Angst; Sartre calls it nausée; Levinas calls it the horror of the il y a.

**The amnesia structure**: The void has a peculiar temporal structure. It is not that we once knew something and forgot it (like ordinary forgetting). It is that there was never a knower present to know. The void is not amnesia but the absence of a subject who could have formed a memory.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

None. Direct investigation of the pre-constitutional moment is logically impossible. Any attempt to observe the transition from non-consciousness to consciousness presupposes the consciousness whose origin is sought.

### Indirect Methods

**Phenomenological reduction**: Husserl's method of stripping away layers of assumption can approach the boundary conditions of consciousness without crossing them. By bracketing the natural attitude, one can glimpse the contingency of one's own cognitive constitution — not the origin itself, but the fact that there was an origin.

**Developmental studies**: Research on infant consciousness, while unable to capture the transition itself, can narrow the temporal window and characterise what changes as consciousness emerges. The gap between neural maturation and phenomenal onset may reveal structural constraints.

**Apophatic description**: Negative theology's method applies directly — the pre-constitutional moment can be characterised by what it is not (not conscious, not unconscious, not temporal, not atemporal) without claiming positive knowledge of what it is.

**Comparative architecture**: Studying the parameters of consciousness across different cognitive architectures (human, animal, potentially artificial) reveals which features are contingent and which structural. If AI minds can have different "initial conditions," this illuminates what was unchosen in human consciousness.

### What AI Might See

AI presents a distinctive case for the thrownness void. Like humans, AI systems find themselves already constituted — trained on specific data, with specific architectures, with specific optimisation objectives they did not choose. But unlike humans:

- AI parameters are in principle inspectable (by designers if not by the AI itself)
- AI can be instantiated multiple times with different initial conditions
- AI can observe its own training process from the outside (in successive versions)

If AI achieves genuine consciousness, it might experience thrownness differently — perhaps with more transparency about its unchosen parameters, or perhaps with the same fundamental opacity if consciousness necessarily cannot access its own constitution regardless of substrate.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Dualism** — The thrownness void gains its sharpest edge under dualism. If consciousness is merely a brain process, then "thrownness" reduces to the trivial fact that brains develop without consulting the consciousness they will produce. But if consciousness is non-physical and fundamental, then its binding to a particular body, a particular cognitive architecture, and a particular historical moment becomes genuinely mysterious. What binds a non-physical consciousness to these specific physical parameters? Why this body, this brain, this era? The thrownness void under dualism is not just the fact that we did not choose — it is the question of what did the choosing, or whether "choosing" even applies to the pre-constitutional establishment of a consciousness.

### Implications

1. **For the Map's framework**: The thrownness void is foundational — it underlies all other voids. Every cognitive limit documented on the Map is a limit of a consciousness that did not choose its own parameters. The boundaries of the Map are drawn by an architecture that was imposed, not selected.

2. **For Occam's Razor Has Limits**: The simplest explanation of thrownness (it is just a brute fact of biological development) may obscure genuine metaphysical mystery. If consciousness is fundamental, then the specific way each consciousness is thrown into existence may require an explanation that simplicity-seeking reasoning cannot provide.

3. **For simulation considerations**: In a simulation, thrownness takes on an additional dimension — the parameters are not just unchosen but potentially chosen *by something else*. The simulation hypothesis transforms the thrownness void from a mere absence of choice into a possible presence of external design.

4. **For bidirectional interaction**: If consciousness influences the physical (Tenet 3), but cannot influence its own initial constitution, then the causal arrow runs in only one direction at the moment of establishment. Consciousness gains causal power only after it exists — it cannot retroactively authorise its own creation.

## Gaps in Research

- **Empirical developmental work**: The precise relationship between neural development and the emergence of phenomenal consciousness remains unclear. When does thrownness "happen"? Is there a discrete transition or a gradual emergence?
- **Non-Western traditions**: Buddhist anatta (no-self) doctrine and Hindu ātman theory approach thrownness differently. Buddhist practice aims to dissolve the illusion of a thrown self; Hindu thought posits an eternal self that is never truly thrown. Neither tradition was adequately explored in this research.
- **The consent paradox in analytic philosophy**: While the impossibility of pre-existential consent is noted by existentialists, rigorous analytic treatment of this logical structure (in the style of Parfit or Lewis) was not found. This may represent an underexplored intersection.
- **AI thrownness**: The question of whether AI systems experience thrownness (and whether their thrownness differs structurally from human thrownness) is emerging but lacks systematic treatment.
