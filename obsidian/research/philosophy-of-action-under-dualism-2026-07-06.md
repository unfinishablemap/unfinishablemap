## Executive Summary

Philosophy of action asks what distinguishes a genuine *action* (raising my arm) from a mere *bodily event* (my arm rising), and how actions are explained by the reasons for which they are done. The dominant view since Davidson (1963/1967) is the *causal theory of action* (CTA): an action is a bodily movement caused "in the right way" by the agent's mental states (belief–desire pairs, or intentions). Dualism inherits both the appeal and the liabilities of this framework in a distinctive form. Because the Map holds that consciousness is non-physical yet causally efficacious (Tenets 1 and 3), the CTA's central claim—that mental states cause movements—is congenial, but its standard *event-causal* rendering invites the "disappearing agent" and "deviant causal chain" objections that many take to motivate *agent-causal* or *volitionist* alternatives (Chisholm, O'Connor, Hornsby, and especially E. J. Lowe's non-Cartesian substance dualism). A rival non-causal tradition (Anscombe, Wittgensteinians) treats practical thought as the *formal* rather than *efficient* cause of action, sidestepping deviance entirely. For a dualist committed to *minimal* quantum interaction (Tenet 2), the key open question is whether volitional agency can be located at the smallest causal margin without positing a metaphysically extravagant "extra" causal power.

## Key Sources

### Action (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/action/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - The core problem: distinguishing actions from mere happenings; the "standard story" is the causal theory of action.
  - On the CTA, reason-explanation is a species of causal explanation (Davidson's argument from the "many reasons, one operative reason" problem).
  - Deviant causal chains are the central internal difficulty.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral framing; the CTA's commitment to mental causation aligns with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction).

### Donald Davidson, Mind and Action (Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/donald-davidson-mind-action/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - "Actions, Reasons, and Causes" (1963/1967) argues reasons *are* causes; became the mainstream view.
  - Davidson explains intentions via beliefs + desires; an action is a bodily movement caused by the right belief–desire pair.
  - Davidson is an *anomalous monist* (token-identity, no psychophysical laws)—a physicalist, not a dualist. The Map borrows his causal *structure* while rejecting his monism.
- **Tenet alignment**: Structure aligns with Tenet 3; his monism conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism).
- **Quote**: "explanation by appeal to reasons is a form of causal explanation, because this is the only way to account for the fact that we have many reasons for acting the way we did, but only one of them is the reason we acted that way."

### E. J. Lowe, *Personal Agency: The Metaphysics of Mind and Action* (OUP 2008); NDPR review
- **URL**: https://ndpr.nd.edu/reviews/personal-agency-the-metaphysics-of-mind-and-action/
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/personal-agency-9780199217144
- **Type**: Book / Review
- **Key points**:
  - Defends *non-Cartesian substance dualism* (NCSD): the self/person is a distinct substance from the body, but not necessarily separable/disembodiable.
  - Offers a *hybrid* of volitionism and agent-causalism: all causation is ultimately *substance* causation; the agent (a substance) causes her volitions.
  - Attacks the *causal closure of the physical* argument for physicalism in all its forms—the linchpin move for a dualist theory of action.
  - Argues epiphenomenalist readings of Libet-style results fail because science itself presupposes our ability to "intervene at will in the course of nature."
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenets 1, 3; the closure critique is directly relevant to Tenet 2's "minimal interaction" strategy.

### E. J. Lowe, "Non-Cartesian Substance Dualism and the Problem of Mental Causation" (*Erkenntnis* 2006)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/LOWNSD
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10670-006-9012-3
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - NCSD is claimed to handle mental causation *better* than both Cartesian dualism and physicalism.
  - The move: deny that the physical realm is causally closed by showing the closure argument either begs the question or misdescribes psychophysical causation.
- **Tenet alignment**: Central resource for the Map; a dualist action theory needs exactly this closure-defusing move.

### Jennifer Hornsby, *Actions* (1980) / *Simple Mindedness* (1996); "Agency and Causal Explanation"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/HORAAC
- **Type**: Book / Paper
- **Key points**:
  - *Trying* (or willing) is the fundamental agential activity; bodily actions are tryings that cause movements.
  - Avoids *primitive agent causation* by locating efficacy in the agent's trying (an event inseparable from the agent), a middle path between Davidsonian event-causalism and Chisholm/O'Connor agent-causalism.
- **Tenet alignment**: The "trying" primitive is attractive for a minimal-interaction dualism (Tenet 2)—the smallest volitional act as the causal contact point.

### Anscombe, *Intention* (1957); IEP/SEP and secondary literature
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/intention/
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/archive/SINAOA (Singh, "Anscombe on Acting for Reasons")
- **Type**: Book / Papers
- **Key points**:
  - Practical thought is the *formal* principle unifying an action, not an *efficient* cause setting the body in motion.
  - Non-observational *practical knowledge*: the agent knows what she is doing without inference; this knowledge partly *constitutes* the action as intentional.
  - Because intention does not *efficiently* cause the movement, deviant-causal-chain problems do not arise.
- **Tenet alignment**: Ambiguous. The anti-efficient-cause move could *conflict* with Tenet 3 (which wants genuine downward causation), or be read as complementary at the level of description. Worth flagging honestly.

### Deviant causal chains literature (Ward 2024; Paul, "Deviant Formal Causation")
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/phpr.12977 (Ward 2024, *PPR*)
- **URL**: http://www.jesp.org/PDF/DeviantFormalCausation.pdf (Sarah K. Paul)
- **URL**: https://www.mdpi.com/2409-9287/10/2/37 (Causal Deviance in Brain–Computer Interfaces, 2025)
- **Type**: Papers
- **Key points**:
  - "Right mental state → right movement, but via the wrong route" cases (e.g., the nervous climber whose intention to let go so unnerves him that his hands loosen involuntarily).
  - Repair strategies: "sensitivity" (small change in intention content → change in movement), "reliability" (which pathways reliably connect intention to outcome), and Anscombe-inspired "formal cause" moves.
  - BCIs revive the problem: brain–computer interfaces make the "wrong route" concrete.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; a diagnostic that any Map action theory must survive.

### Disappearing-agent objection (Pereboom; Himmelreich 2024; Martinez 2023)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/agency/ (Agency, SEP, Fall 2025)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/PERTDA-5
- **Type**: Encyclopedia / Papers
- **Key points**:
  - If the agent is *nothing over and above* her states/events and the decision is undetermined until made, the decision looks like a chancy, uncontrolled event—the agent "disappears."
  - Agent-causalists and volitionists reply that the event-causal order does not *exhaust* the natural order; agency is not thereby "supernatural."
  - Recent: Himmelreich (2024, *Inquiry*) frames the disappearing agent as an *exclusion* problem; Martinez (2023, *Synthese*) offers "agent causation without the panicky metaphysics."
- **Tenet alignment**: The Map's dualist self is well-positioned to answer the objection (there *is* something over and above the events), but must avoid the "panicky metaphysics" charge—resonant with Tenet 5 (Occam's limits) and the parsimony-case articles.

## Major Positions

### Causal Theory of Action (CTA) / event-causal "standard story"
- **Proponents**: Davidson, Bratman (planning theory), Michael Smith, Alfred Mele.
- **Core claim**: An action = a bodily movement caused *in the right way* by appropriate mental states (belief+desire, or intention). Reason-explanations are causal explanations.
- **Key arguments**: The "one operative reason" argument; integration with a broadly naturalistic ontology.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map can adopt the causal *skeleton* (mental → physical causation supports Tenet 3) while rejecting the physicalist flesh (Davidson's monism conflicts with Tenet 1). Its event-causal rendering exposes the Map to the deviance and disappearing-agent problems, which push toward agent-causal/volitionist supplementation.

### Volitionism
- **Proponents**: Descartes (classical), H. A. Prichard, Jennifer Hornsby, Brian O'Shaughnessy.
- **Core claim**: The fundamental action is an inner act of *willing* / *trying*; overt bodily action is willing that causes (or is) movement.
- **Key arguments**: Explains the difference between action and mere movement by the presence of a mental *cause*; accommodates cases of paralysis (one can *try* though the limb does not move).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Highly congenial. The "smallest volitional act" as causal contact point dovetails with Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)—volition as the minimal non-physical nudge. Connects to existing `volitional-control`, `phenomenology-of-choice-and-volition`.

### Agent Causation (substance causation)
- **Proponents**: Chisholm, Richard Taylor, Timothy O'Connor, Randolph Clarke; hybrid: E. J. Lowe.
- **Core claim**: The agent, *as a substance*, is an irreducible cause of her actions—not reducible to causation by events/states within her.
- **Key arguments**: Answers the disappearing-agent objection directly (the agent is a genuine relatum of causation); preserves robust control and origination.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A dualist self as substance-cause aligns with Tenets 1 and 3. Risk: the "panicky metaphysics" / parsimony charge (Tenet 5 cautions that Occam is unreliable here, but the Map should still show the posit earns its keep). See `agent-causation-libertarian-free-will` research note and `parsimony-case-for-interactionist-dualism`.

### Non-Causal (Anscombean / Wittgensteinian) theory
- **Proponents**: Anscombe, von Wright, Jonathan Dancy (in a related "reasons as non-psychological" key), Sarah Paul (formal causation).
- **Core claim**: Intention/practical thought is the *formal* cause (unifying principle) of action, not an efficient cause; acting for reasons is a *sui generis* rational relation, not event-causal.
- **Key arguments**: Dissolves deviant causal chains; grounds first-person *practical knowledge*.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Tension with Tenet 3 if "non-causal" is read as denying downward efficacy. But a Map-friendly reading: the formal-cause story describes the *rational* structure while volition supplies the *efficient* (minimal) causal contact—potentially complementary rather than rival.

## Key Debates

### Efficient cause vs. formal cause of action
- **Sides**: Davidsonian causalists (efficient) vs. Anscombeans (formal).
- **Core disagreement**: Does practical thought *push* the body (event causation) or *unify/constitute* the action (formal)?
- **Current state**: Ongoing; revived by "deviant formal causation" work (Paul) and Ward's 2024 general-solution attempt.

### Event causation vs. agent (substance) causation
- **Sides**: Davidson/Bratman/Mele vs. Chisholm/O'Connor/Lowe.
- **Core disagreement**: Is the ultimate causal relatum an *event/state* or the *agent-substance*?
- **Current state**: Live; the disappearing-agent objection is the fulcrum. Lowe's NCSD is the most developed dualist synthesis.

### Does causal closure defeat a dualist theory of action?
- **Sides**: Physicalist closure defenders (Kim's exclusion argument) vs. Lowe and other interactionists.
- **Core disagreement**: Is the physical domain causally closed, so that mental causes would be either epiphenomenal or overdetermining?
- **Current state**: Central to the Map. Lowe's closure critique and the Map's own `overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism` are the key resources. Directly ties to Tenets 2 and 3.
