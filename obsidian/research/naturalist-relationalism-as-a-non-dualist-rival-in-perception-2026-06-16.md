## Executive Summary

Naturalist relationalism (a.k.a. naive realism, the relational view, Brewer's Object View) holds that veridical perceptual experience *fundamentally consists* in a conscious relation of acquaintance between a subject and mind-independent objects, such that those objects and their features are literal *constituents* of the experience and *constitute its phenomenal character*. It is naturalist (no appeal to non-physical mind), anti-representationalist (phenomenal character is not fixed by representational content), and presentational (the world is presented, not depicted). Its signature liability is the **problem of perception** (arguments from illusion and hallucination); its signature defence is **disjunctivism** (perception and hallucination are not the same fundamental mental kind). For the Map, relationalism is the sharpest *non-dualist rival* in perception because it secures phenomenal transparency and direct world-contact *without* any inner experience-bearer — precisely the explanandum the Map locates on the phenomenal side of the interface. The Map's leverage is not that relationalism is incoherent (it is not) but that it relocates qualitative character into a perceiver–world *relation* while leaving unexplained why that relation is *felt from the inside at all*.

## Major Positions

### Naive Realism / Relationalism (the view itself)
- **Proponents**: M.G.F. Martin (1995 onward — coined the modern usage), John Campbell (2002, *Reference and Consciousness*), Bill Brewer (2011, *Perception and Its Objects* — the "Object View"), William Fish (2009), Mike Martin, Matthew Nudds. New-wave defenders: Heather Logue, Craig French, Thomas Raleigh, Roberta Locatelli, Ian Phillips, Giulia Martina.
- **Core claim (two theses, per Logue/French)**:
  - *Relational Claim*: perceptual experience fundamentally consists in the subject bearing a perceptual (acquaintance) relation to mind-independent objects.
  - *Constitutive Claim*: the phenomenal character of veridical experience is *(partly) constituted by* those mind-independent objects and their properties — not merely caused by them or represented.
- **Key arguments**:
  - *Phenomenological transparency*: introspecting an experience, we find only the worldly scene, never an inner medium. Relationalism takes this at face value: there is no inner item to find because the experience is *of* the world directly.
  - *Openness / presentation*: perception "presents" the world as simply there, "blankly and starkly," in a way representation (which can misfire while leaving content intact) allegedly cannot capture.
  - *Brewer's Inconsistent Triad*: (I) physical objects are mind-independent; (II) physical objects are the direct objects of perception; (III) the direct objects of perception are mind-dependent. The triad is inconsistent; relationalism rejects (III), where the sense-datum tradition rejected (II).
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Sharp non-dualist rival.** Relationalism is wholly naturalist — it posits no non-physical mind and no inner experiential domain. It *agrees with the Map's phenomenology* (transparency, direct world-presentation) but draws the *opposite metaphysical conclusion*: phenomenal character lives in the perceiver–world relation, not in an irreducible experiential domain on the subject's side. It therefore **conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism)** by design, and is the cleanest illustration that the transparency datum the Map leans on does *not* entail dualism. CO-OPTATION FIREWALL: do **not** conscript relationalists toward the Map; engage them as rivals who would reject the Map's interface conclusion.

### Disjunctivism (the relationalist's defence against hallucination)
- **Proponents**: J.M. Hinton (1960s–70s, originator), Paul Snowdon, John McDowell, M.G.F. Martin (negative epistemic disjunctivism), William Fish.
- **Core claim**: veridical perception and a subjectively indistinguishable hallucination are *not the same fundamental kind* of mental state. Rejecting the "Common Kind Claim" lets the relationalist keep mind-independent objects as constituents of veridical perception (since they obviously cannot constitute a hallucination, where no object is present).
- **Martin's negative/epistemic version**: a (causally matching) hallucination has *no positive phenomenal nature* beyond "being not introspectively knowably distinct from a veridical perception of such-and-such." This avoids the **screening-off problem**: if hallucination had a positive phenomenal character of its own, that character — being shareable with the matching perception — would render the world-constituent relation explanatorily idle.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral-to-rival machinery. Disjunctivism is the structural move that makes relationalism viable; the Map can note that the *cost* of saving direct contact is a deflationary, almost contentless account of hallucination — a place where the relationalist's phenomenology of failure cases looks strained, which is the Map's opening.

### The Map's own position (for contrast)
- **Hybrid indirect realism** (`dualist-perception.md` L125): "the phenomenal interface is itself constitutive — neither private theatre nor unmediated contact, but the experiential face of an interaction across the divide." The Map keeps a subject-side phenomenal medium; relationalism denies there is one. This is the precise axis of disagreement.

## Key Debates

### The Problem of Perception (the relationalist's burden)
- **Sides**: relationalists (deny perceptual error reaches the constitutive relation) vs. representationalists / sense-datum theorists.
- **Core disagreement**: do illusion and hallucination show that something *other than* the mind-independent object is the direct object of awareness?
- **Argument from illusion** (white wall looks yellow under yellow light): the "Phenomenal Principle" says something *actually* yellow must be present to awareness; since the wall isn't yellow, the direct object isn't the wall. Relationalist reply (Brewer): the object has **objective "looks"** — *visually relevant similarities* to paradigm yellow things under those conditions — so it "looks yellow" without any inner yellow item. Illusion is a fact about the object-in-conditions, not an inner medium.
- **Argument from hallucination**: a total hallucination can be indistinguishable from veridical perception, yet has no object. If the same account covers both (Common Kind Claim), the object is never the direct constituent. Relationalist reply: **disjunctivism** (see above).
- **Current state**: ongoing and fractious; no consensus. The **causal argument** (matching brain states should yield a common mental element) remains the most pressing objection to disjunctivism.

### Byrne & Green, "Whither Naïve Realism?" I & II (2023, *Philosophical Perspectives* 37:49–68; II forthcoming)
- **The challenge**: illusions are the *best proving ground* for naive realism vs. representationalism. On naive realism, illusions "never involve perceptual error." Byrne & Green assess the two leading relationalist treatments of illusion — **William Fish's** and **Bill Brewer's objective-looks** account — and argue *both fail*; Part II sketches a representational alternative.
- **Why it matters for the Map**: this is a *naturalist-internal* attack (representationalism is also non-dualist), so it equips the Map to show that relationalism's handling of error is contested *even among its naturalist neighbours* — without the Map having to import dualist premises to press the point.

### Ian Phillips, "Naïve Realism and the Science of Illusion" (2016, *Philosophical Topics* 44(2))
- **Thesis**: naive realism is *not* refuted by, and can be made continuous with, the science of illusion. Careful reflection on an empirically grounded model of illusion yields "unconventional ways of thinking about familiar illusions" compatible with naive realism. On the Müller-Lyer, Brewer/Phillips read the diagram as having *visually relevant similarities* to unequal lines that "jump out" / capture attention — the *perceptually natural* similarities in context — rather than as an inner misrepresentation.
- **Map use**: Phillips is the relationalist who most directly tries to answer the "out of touch with vision science" complaint. Engaging *his* version (not a strawman) is what the ChatGPT outer review demanded. The Map's counter: even granting Phillips's reconception, the *felt character* of the illusion (its compellingness from the inside) is left untheorised.

### Giulia Martina, "Lessons from Blur" (2024, *Erkenntnis* 89(8):3229–3246)
- **Thesis**: a *relationalist pluralism* about blur. Martina rejects two implicit assumptions in the blur debate — that we owe a *unified* explanation of blurry experience and that blur must be explained the *same way* across cases. Blur recruits several distinct factors; some of its character is fixed by the *relation* between perceiver and scene under poor viewing conditions, not by represented content.
- **Map use** (already partly engaged in `perceptual-failure-and-the-interface.md` L67): the relationalist can grant that blur degrades phenomenal content yet deny it exposes a *subject-owned phenomenal medium* — "the haziness is a fact about the perceptual relation under poor viewing, not a property of an inner experience-bearer." This is the cleanest single example of relationalism doing the Map's explanatory work *without* the Map's ontology, and is exactly why it is a sharp rival.

### Must naive realists be relationalists? (Stephan, et al.)
- A live sub-debate: the *Constitutive* and *Relational* claims may come apart; some argue naive realism survives without full relationalism. The concept page should note relationalism and naive realism are *near-synonyms in current usage* but not strictly identical, to avoid the corpus conflating them.

**Recommended structure for `concepts/naturalist-relationalism.md`:**

1. **What relationalism claims** — the Relational + Constitutive theses; naive realism / Object View as near-synonyms; presentation and openness; transparency taken at face value. Front-load: *relationalism secures direct world-contact with no inner medium and no non-physical mind.*
2. **Why it is the strongest non-dualist rival** — it *shares the Map's phenomenology* (transparency, directness, qualitative richness located "out there") but draws the opposite metaphysical lesson. State plainly that it threatens the inference "transparency ⇒ phenomenal interface."
3. **How it handles the problem of perception** — objective looks (illusion) + disjunctivism (hallucination), engaging Brewer's and Phillips's *actual* moves (Müller-Lyer, science of illusion) and Martina on blur. This is where the article must avoid the direct-realism caricature the ChatGPT review warned against.
4. **Where it strains (the Map's leverage)** — (a) it *relocates* qualitative character into a relation but does not dissolve it: why is the relation *felt from the inside*? (b) the screening-off / negative-disjunctivist account of hallucination is deflationary to the point of contentlessness; (c) the causal argument from matching brain states; (d) Byrne & Green's naturalist-internal verdict that the illusion treatments fail. The Map presses (a) as the residual *hard-problem* of the relation.
5. **Relation to Site Perspective** — Tenet 1 (Dualism): relationalism is the cleanest demonstration that the transparency datum underdetermines dualism; the Map's reply is the *felt-from-inside* residue, not a charge of incoherence. Tenet 5 (Occam): relationalism is simpler (no extra ontological domain) — note that simplicity does not settle the matter under incomplete knowledge of how a relation could be *experienced*.
