## Executive Summary

The standard parsimony objection to dualism claims that physicalism is simpler because it posits fewer substance types. This research finds that multiple philosophers have challenged this framing, arguing that (1) ontological parsimony is only one dimension of simplicity, (2) physicalism accumulates hidden explanatory costs that offset its apparent ontological economy, and (3) the parsimony argument relies on contested meta-metaphysical assumptions about whether simplicity tracks truth in this domain. Several thinkers—Zanotti, Hubbard, Anderson, Lowe, Swinburne, and Chalmers—have developed distinct arguments that interactionist or property dualism may actually be the more parsimonious position once all explanatory burdens are counted.

## Key Sources

### "Physicalism and the Burden of Parsimony" — Giacomo Zanotti (2021)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-021-03281-7
- **Type**: Journal article (Synthese, vol. 199, pp. 11109–11132)
- **Key points**:
  - Challenges the widespread assumption that parsimony favors physicalism over dualism
  - Makes explicit the meta-metaphysical assumptions physicalists rely on when leveraging parsimony
  - Argues the most promising grounding for parsimony's epistemic value is "modesty" (epistemic risk of a theory)
  - Concludes the modesty rationale does not ultimately favor physicalism over dualism
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)
- **Quote**: Physicalists "take for granted that parsimony has an epistemic value in metaphysics" — but this is "rather controversial"

### "Parsimony and the Mind" — J. Hubbard
- **URL**: https://www.newdualism.org/papers/J.Hubbard/simple.shtml
- **Type**: Paper (New Dualism Archive)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes three types of parsimony: ontological, epistemological, and linguistic
  - Argues dualism is more ontologically complex but more parsimonious overall
  - Eliminating mental terms creates enormous linguistic and epistemological complexity
  - Physicalism's ontological simplicity is purchased at the cost of linguistic and explanatory unwieldiness
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 5; supports the argument that apparent simplicity masks hidden complexity
- **Quote**: "Adopting the physicalist's simpler ontology is not worth being forced to talk in a language devoid of mental terms"

### "Can Ockham's Razor Cut Through the Mind-Body Problem?" — Christopher J. Anderson
- **URL**: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=895730
- **Type**: Journal article (Journal of Theoretical and Philosophical Psychology)
- **Key points**:
  - Critiques Churchland's "Raze Dualism" argument (that materialism is superior because simpler)
  - Shows the argument is undermined by assumptions that do not withstand scrutiny
  - Argues Ockham's razor is problematic in this application
  - Notes idealism is equally parsimonious with physicalism (one substance type), undermining the razor's usefulness
  - Concludes the case for dualism should be reopened on grounds other than parsimony
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 5; demonstrates parsimony cuts in multiple directions simultaneously

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Dualism" entry (Howard Robinson)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/dualism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia article
- **Key points**:
  - The simplicity argument is "probably the simplest and also the most common form of argument against dualism"
  - Dualists respond that standard arguments for dualism don't proceed by inference to the best explanation, so parsimony is irrelevant to their force
  - Modal arguments (conceivability, knowledge argument) establish dualism independently of explanatory considerations
  - "Enemies of dualism must show that modal independence arguments fail, not appeal blithely to ontological parsimony"
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism) and Tenet 5
- **Quote**: "This response would only be correct if the only basis for admitting non-physical mental events stemmed from their role in explaining purely physical events"

### E.J. Lowe — Non-Cartesian Substance Dualism
- **URL**: https://www.newdualism.org/papers/E.Lowe/Lowe-BPG2012-ch2.pdf
- **Type**: Book chapter / paper
- **Key points**:
  - Develops a four-category ontology balancing explanatory power and ontological parsimony
  - The self is a "simple substance" — its simplicity explains the unity of consciousness
  - Non-Cartesian substance dualism avoids Cartesian difficulties while retaining dualist explanatory power
  - Better equipped than either Cartesian dualism or standard physicalism to explain mental causation
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenets 1, 3 (Bidirectional Interaction); the simplicity of the self as a positive parsimony argument for dualism

### Richard Swinburne — Simplicity of the Soul
- **URL**: https://www3.nd.edu/~jspeaks/courses/2006-7/20208/swinburne-dualism.html (lecture notes on Swinburne)
- **URL**: http://www.newdualism.org/papers/R.Swinburne/Dualism-Intact1996.htm
- **Type**: Arguments from multiple works
- **Key points**:
  - The simplest hypothesis is more likely to be true (simplicity as an a priori epistemic principle)
  - The soul is a simple substance with no parts — this simplicity itself is a parsimony argument
  - Modal argument: it is conceivable that a conscious person continues to exist without their body; this requires a non-bodily part (soul)
  - Simple things cannot cease to exist by decomposition (no parts to separate)
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1; provides a positive simplicity argument for dualism rather than merely defending against parsimony objections

### David Chalmers — Consciousness and Simplicity
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/zombies/ (related entry)
- **Type**: Multiple works (The Conscious Mind, 1996; various papers)
- **Key points**:
  - Zombie conceivability demonstrates consciousness is logically separable from physical structure
  - Chalmers prefers dualist interpretations of consciousness-collapse models "because we already have good reason to believe that consciousness is a fundamental property"
  - The ontological parsimony of physicalism is offset by its failure to explain consciousness at all
  - A theory that explains nothing about its target phenomenon (experience) cannot claim the virtue of simplicity
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenets 1, 5; the zombie argument shows physicalism's "simplicity" comes at the cost of leaving the explanandum entirely unaddressed

### Ole Koksvik — "In Defence of Interactionism"
- **URL**: https://www.newdualism.org/papers/O.Koksvik/indefenceofinteractionism.htm
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Defends interactionist dualism against the causal closure objection
  - The minimal interactionist position: mind sometimes brings about physical events that would not otherwise have occurred
  - Causal overdetermination (physical and mental causes both sufficient) is rejected — it doesn't capture the intuition that the mental makes a difference
  - Interactionism is simpler than epiphenomenalism because it doesn't require explaining away the universal appearance of mental causation
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction); simplicity argument against epiphenomenalism

## Major Positions

### The Standard Physicalist Parsimony Claim
- **Proponents**: J.J.C. Smart, Paul Churchland, physicalists broadly
- **Core claim**: Physicalism posits one substance type; dualism posits two. By Ockham's razor, prefer the simpler ontology.
- **Key arguments**: Ontological parsimony is a theoretical virtue; the simplest theory that explains the data should be preferred; mental events can be identified with or reduced to brain events.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly conflicts with Tenets 1 and 5. The Map argues this application of parsimony is illegitimate because (a) physicalism does not actually explain consciousness, and (b) simplicity is unreliable with incomplete knowledge.

### The Hidden Complexity Response
- **Proponents**: Zanotti, Hubbard, Chalmers (implicitly)
- **Core claim**: Physicalism's apparent ontological simplicity masks enormous explanatory complexity. Once you account for the full explanatory burden, dualism may be simpler overall.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Physicalism must explain consciousness as brute emergence (unexplained), as eliminable (consciousness doesn't exist), or as identical with brain states (explanatory gap). Each option carries hidden costs.
  - Brute emergence is itself a massive theoretical cost — a fundamental, unexplained law connecting physical arrangements to experience.
  - Eliminativism about consciousness (Dennett, Frankish) requires an error theory explaining why everyone believes they are conscious when they aren't — an enormous explanatory burden.
  - Identity theory faces the explanatory gap: no amount of physical description entails phenomenal character.
  - Linguistic parsimony: eliminating mental vocabulary creates unwieldy descriptions of what mental terms capture naturally.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly supports Tenet 5 and implicitly Tenet 1. This is the core argument the Map should develop.

### The Modesty/Epistemic Risk Response
- **Proponents**: Zanotti (2021)
- **Core claim**: The best justification for parsimony as a theoretical virtue is "modesty" — preferring theories with lower epistemic risk. But dualism and physicalism carry comparable epistemic risks in different directions.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Physicalism risks being wrong about consciousness existing (if eliminativist) or about its nature (if reductionist)
  - Dualism risks being wrong about a second substance/property type
  - Neither risk profile is clearly preferable on modesty grounds
- **Relation to site tenets**: Supports Tenet 5; neutralizes the parsimony argument without requiring dualism to be simpler

### The Positive Simplicity of Dualism
- **Proponents**: Swinburne, Lowe, Chalmers
- **Core claim**: Dualism is actually simpler than physicalism in important respects.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The soul/self is a simple substance (Swinburne, Lowe) — no parts, no decomposition
  - Dualism respects the apparent structure of reality (physical and mental as genuinely different) rather than forcing a reduction
  - Interactionism is simpler than epiphenomenalism because it doesn't require explaining away the universal appearance of mental causation (Koksvik)
  - Property dualism adds only one new fundamental category (phenomenal properties) while physicalism must add brute psychophysical laws, error theories, or unexplained emergence
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly supports Tenets 1, 3, and 5

### The Irrelevance Response
- **Proponents**: Robinson (SEP), Anderson
- **Core claim**: Parsimony is simply irrelevant to the dualism debate because dualism isn't established by inference to the best explanation.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Ockham's razor applies when choosing between theories that explain the same data equally well
  - Dualism is established by modal arguments (conceivability, knowledge argument), not by explanatory inference
  - If zombies are conceivable, physicalism is false regardless of its simplicity
  - Idealism is equally parsimonious with physicalism (one substance), so parsimony alone cannot decide between positions
- **Relation to site tenets**: Supports Tenet 5; shows parsimony is a category error in this debate

## Key Debates

### Is Ontological Parsimony the Right Metric?
- **Sides**: Physicalists (yes — fewer substance types is better) vs. dualists (no — total explanatory burden matters)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether counting substance types captures the relevant notion of simplicity, or whether explanatory adequacy, linguistic economy, and theoretical elegance also matter
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Zanotti (2021) and Hubbard have made strong cases that ontological parsimony is only one dimension. No consensus.

### Does Physicalism Actually Explain Consciousness?
- **Sides**: Physicalists (yes, or will eventually) vs. dualists and mysterians (no, and possibly can't)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the explanatory gap is epistemic (we don't yet understand how, but it's physical) or ontological (consciousness genuinely isn't physical)
- **Current state**: The hard problem remains unsolved after 30+ years. Physicalists have shifted from confident reductionism to more modest positions (illusionism, type-B physicalism). This shift is itself evidence that the parsimony advantage is less clear than originally claimed.

### Can Parsimony Adjudicate Metaphysical Disputes?
- **Sides**: Naturalists (yes — parsimony is a theoretical virtue in metaphysics as in science) vs. skeptics (no — parsimony's epistemic value in metaphysics is ungrounded)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the success of parsimony in empirical science transfers to metaphysics
- **Current state**: Zanotti (2021) argues the transfer is problematic. The SEP entry on simplicity notes the justification for parsimony remains philosophically contested.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, an article could:

1. **"The Hidden Costs of Physicalism"** — Argue that physicalism's apparent simplicity conceals massive explanatory debts: brute emergence, error theories about consciousness, the explanatory gap as an IOU. Interactionist dualism, by contrast, takes consciousness at face value and adds only a minimal interaction mechanism. Tally the full costs on both sides. This aligns directly with Tenets 1, 3, and 5.

2. **"Parsimony Cuts Both Ways"** — Survey the multiple dimensions of parsimony (ontological, linguistic, explanatory) and show that physicalism wins on only one dimension while losing on others. Incorporate Hubbard's linguistic parsimony argument, Zanotti's modesty argument, and the Map's existing material on parsimony epistemology. Aligns with Tenet 5.

3. **"Why Interactionism Is Simpler Than Epiphenomenalism"** — A narrower article arguing that among dualist positions, interactionism is actually the most parsimonious because it doesn't require explaining away the universal appearance of mental causation. Epiphenomenalism adds a massive coincidence (consciousness tracks behavior perfectly but doesn't cause it). Aligns with Tenet 3.

**Recommended approach for the Map**: Option 1 or 2. The Map already has strong material on parsimony epistemology (Tenet 5, existing articles). What's missing is the *positive* case — that interactionist dualism is actually simpler once you count all the costs. This would fill the gap identified in the optimistic review.

- **Empirical evidence on parsimony's reliability**: Limited empirical work on whether parsimony actually tracks truth in domains beyond empirical science
