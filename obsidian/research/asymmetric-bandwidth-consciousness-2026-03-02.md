# Research: Asymmetric Bandwidth of Consciousness

**Date**: 2026-03-02
**Search queries used**: "asymmetric bandwidth consciousness philosophy perception action", "bandwidth of consciousness information processing bottleneck", "consciousness input output asymmetry sensory richness vs motor control", "Nørretranders user illusion consciousness bandwidth bits per second", "Caltech 10 bits per second consciousness Zheng Meister", "Block overflow consciousness rich phenomenology sparse access", "Mandik control consciousness motor awareness action phenomenology bandwidth", "global workspace theory consciousness bottleneck Baars broadcasting", "Stapp quantum Zeno effect consciousness attention neural selection", "consciousness causal role downward causation bandwidth information dualism"

## Executive Summary

Consciousness exhibits a striking asymmetry: the inbound bandwidth required to generate conscious experience is enormous (a rich, vivid, smoothly moving 3D world with colour, sound, emotion), while the outbound bandwidth—consciousness's measurable influence on behaviour—is vanishingly small (~10 bits/second). This asymmetry has deep implications for the Map's interactionist dualism. A landmark 2024 Caltech study (Zheng & Meister) quantified the output bottleneck across dozens of cognitive tasks, confirming a universal ~10 bits/s ceiling on conscious throughput. Meanwhile, sensory systems deliver ~1 billion bits/s to the brain. The 100-million-fold gap between sensory input and conscious output remains unexplained by neuroscience and constitutes a genuine paradox. For the Map's framework, this asymmetry suggests consciousness operates at a narrow interface—consistent with the Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet—where it selects among pre-computed options rather than performing the computation itself.

## Key Sources

### Zheng & Meister (2024) — "The Unbearable Slowness of Being"
- **URL**: https://www.cell.com/neuron/abstract/S0896-6273(24)00808-0
- **Type**: Research paper (Neuron, December 2024)
- **Key points**:
  - Human conscious throughput is ~10 bits/s across all measured tasks (typing, gaming, speech, Rubik's cube solving, memory sports)
  - Sensory systems process ~10^9 bits/s (1 gigabit/s)
  - The "sifting number"—the ratio of sensory input to behavioural output—is ~10^8
  - Proposes an "outer brain" (high-bandwidth sensory/motor processing) vs "inner brain" (low-bandwidth serial conscious processing) distinction
  - Individual neurons can transmit far more than 10 bits/s, so the bottleneck isn't at the cellular level
  - Suggests the serial architecture may be inherited from primitive organisms navigating chemical gradients
  - Actual lifetime information accumulation (~4 GB) is far below theoretical brain storage capacity (~50 TB)
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports Dualism—the bottleneck is inexplicable if consciousness is merely neural computation, since the neural hardware has vastly more capacity. Supports Minimal Quantum Interaction—a narrow interface is exactly what you'd expect if consciousness selects among options at quantum indeterminacies rather than performing computation
- **Quote**: "The information throughput of a human being is about 10 bits/s... This stark contrast remains unexplained and resolving this paradox should teach us something fundamental about brain function."

### Nørretranders (1998) — "The User Illusion"
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_illusion (concept page)
- **Type**: Book (popular science / philosophy)
- **Key points**:
  - Estimated sensory input at ~11 million bits/s (10 million for vision, 1 million for touch, rest distributed among other senses)
  - Estimated conscious processing at ~16-20 bits/s
  - Proposed the "user illusion" metaphor: consciousness is like a computer's desktop interface—a simplified representation, not the underlying computation
  - The subconscious brain's purpose is to reduce information flow to a rate consciousness can handle
- **Tenet alignment**: The "user illusion" metaphor is compatible with interactionist dualism if reframed—consciousness isn't merely a simplified display but an interface where a non-physical mind interacts with the physical brain. The massive data reduction from 11 million to 20 bits/s suggests the brain prepares options for consciousness to select among
- **Quote**: "The whole point of the subconscious parts of the brain is to reduce the information flow into and out of the brain down to a rate which our feeble 20 bits/sec consciousness can handle."

### Cohen, Dennett & Kanwisher (2016) — "What is the Bandwidth of Perceptual Experience?"
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4898652/
- **Type**: Research paper (Trends in Cognitive Sciences)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues perception is both richer than narrow attentional studies suggest and sparser than subjective experience implies
  - The visual system exploits scene redundancy through "ensemble statistics"—representing groups of items as summary statistics rather than individual objects
  - With statistical structure, observers can access ~24 items; without it, only ~4.5
  - Scene recognition can occur from global statistics alone, without detailed object analysis
  - Proposes this resolves the phenomenal overflow debate: apparent perceptual richness comes from efficient statistical summaries, not conscious overflow
- **Tenet alignment**: Partially neutral. If ensemble statistics explain apparent richness, the inbound bandwidth may be lower than it seems. However, even ensemble representations require substantial computation to generate the subjective experience of a coherent visual scene—the hard problem remains
- **Quote**: "Items that are attended to and foveated are perceived at higher resolution, while items that are unattended or in the periphery are primarily perceived as ensemble approximations."

### Block (2011) — "Perceptual Consciousness Overflows Cognitive Access"
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/rec/BLOPCO-2
- **Type**: Journal article (Trends in Cognitive Sciences)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues phenomenal consciousness (P-consciousness) is richer than access consciousness (A-consciousness)
  - Uses iconic memory evidence: observers are conscious of more items than they can report
  - The "overflow" thesis: we are consciously aware of more than we can cognitively access
  - Opponents (sparse view) argue consciousness is limited to what we can report
  - The debate remains unresolved but has major implications for understanding consciousness's bandwidth
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports Dualism. If phenomenal consciousness overflows cognitive access, this means there is more to experience than functional/computational access can capture—consistent with the irreducibility claim. The overflow itself may represent the inbound bandwidth (rich experience) that far exceeds the outbound bandwidth (what we can report or act on)

### Mandik (2010) — "Control Consciousness"
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/archive/MANCC
- **Type**: Journal article (Topics in Cognitive Science)
- **Key points**:
  - Identifies a bandwidth asymmetry between sensory and motor phenomenology
  - Vision bandwidth: 10^6 to 4.32 × 10^7 bits/s; motor output bandwidth: vastly lower
  - Control consciousness (the feeling of being in control) is informationally sparse compared to sensory consciousness
  - Introspecting motor control introduces a "doubling of tasks" that makes control phenomenology even harder to notice
  - Argues motor commands directly contribute to control awareness (not just sensory feedback from actions)
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports the asymmetry thesis. The inbound side (sensory phenomenology) is rich; the outbound side (control phenomenology) is sparse. For interactionist dualism, this suggests consciousness receives vast information passively but can only influence the physical world through a narrow channel—consistent with Minimal Quantum Interaction

### Baars (1988, updated) — Global Workspace Theory
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_workspace_theory
- **Type**: Theoretical framework
- **Key points**:
  - Consciousness acts as a broadcasting bottleneck: information becomes conscious when widely broadcast across the brain
  - The "global workspace" has very limited capacity compared to the vast number of unconscious processes
  - Compensating advantage: consciousness creates access to essentially any part of the nervous system
  - Neural architecture confirms a "bow-tie" structure with a central core in parietal/prefrontal areas forming a structural bottleneck
  - The bottleneck is functional: it routes information between specialized processors
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral to supportive. GWT is typically presented within a physicalist framework, but the architectural insight—a narrow conscious bottleneck mediating between vast unconscious systems—is compatible with interactionist dualism. The bottleneck could be precisely where non-physical consciousness interfaces with physical neural computation

### Stapp (2006) — Quantum Interactive Dualism
- **URL**: https://www-physics.lbl.gov/~stapp/QID.pdf
- **Type**: Journal article (Zygon)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes consciousness selects among quantum possibilities via the quantum Zeno effect
  - Mental attention acts as repeated observation, holding desired neural patterns stable
  - Focuses on narrow ion channels in synaptic walls as the interface where consciousness takes control
  - The mechanism explains how low-bandwidth conscious attention can influence high-bandwidth neural processing: not by computing, but by selecting/stabilising
  - Criticised: simulations suggest quantum Zeno effect breaks down at timescales longer than brain decoherence times
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports all five tenets. Stapp's framework is explicitly interactionist dualist, proposes minimal quantum interaction at specific neural sites, rejects many-worlds, and offers a mechanism where consciousness needs only low bandwidth (repeated observation) to causally influence the physical world

### Libet (1983) — Readiness Potential and Conscious Timing
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6640273/
- **Type**: Experimental research (Brain)
- **Key points**:
  - The readiness potential (RP) precedes conscious awareness of intention by ~350ms
  - Suggests unconscious neural processes initiate voluntary actions before consciousness "decides"
  - Libet argued consciousness retains a "veto" power—it cannot initiate but can abort
  - The timing asymmetry is consistent with consciousness operating as a selector/filter rather than an initiator
  - Significant criticisms: the experiment measured trivial decisions (when to move), not genuine deliberation
- **Tenet alignment**: Partially supports the bandwidth asymmetry. The brain prepares options (high bandwidth), and consciousness selects or vetoes (low bandwidth). The veto interpretation is consistent with Bidirectional Interaction—consciousness causally intervenes, but narrowly. The low bandwidth of conscious intervention (~1 bit: veto or permit) is the extreme case of the asymmetry

## Major Positions

### The "Inner/Outer Brain" Framework (Zheng & Meister)
- **Proponents**: Jieyu Zheng, Markus Meister (Caltech)
- **Core claim**: The brain operates in two distinct modes—an "outer brain" for high-dimensional parallel sensory/motor processing and an "inner brain" for serialised, low-dimensional conscious thought
- **Key arguments**: Cross-domain evidence shows ~10 bits/s ceiling regardless of task type; the bottleneck isn't motor (it persists in purely cognitive tasks); individual neurons have far more capacity; evolutionary inheritance from serial-processing primitive organisms may explain the architecture
- **Relation to site tenets**: The inner/outer distinction maps naturally onto the physical/non-physical divide. If the "inner brain" is where consciousness interfaces with neural hardware, its low bandwidth is exactly what Minimal Quantum Interaction predicts. The outer brain is purely physical computation; the inner brain is the interface

### Phenomenal Overflow (Block)
- **Proponents**: Ned Block (NYU)
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal consciousness is richer than access consciousness—we experience more than we can report or act upon
- **Key arguments**: Iconic memory experiments show awareness exceeds reportability; Sperling's partial report paradigm; Sligte's fragile visual short-term memory results
- **Relation to site tenets**: Supports Dualism by suggesting phenomenal experience has features that outstrip functional/computational accounts. The overflow is the inbound bandwidth—vast conscious experience that cannot be fully captured by the outbound channel of report and action

### Sparse Perception / Cognitive Access View
- **Proponents**: Daniel Dennett, Michael Cohen, Nancy Kanwisher; Jesse Prinz; others
- **Core claim**: Consciousness is limited to what passes through the cognitive access bottleneck; apparent richness is an illusion created by ensemble statistics and rapid updating
- **Key arguments**: Change blindness, inattentional blindness, ensemble perception research
- **Relation to site tenets**: If correct, reduces the inbound bandwidth significantly—but doesn't eliminate the asymmetry. Even sparse conscious perception requires generating subjective experience, which the hard problem shows is not explained by physical processes alone

### Global Workspace / Broadcasting (Baars, Dehaene)
- **Proponents**: Bernard Baars, Stanislas Dehaene, Jean-Pierre Changeux
- **Core claim**: Consciousness is the global broadcasting of information to distributed brain regions; its capacity is inherently limited by the architecture of the workspace
- **Key arguments**: Neural evidence for ignition/broadcasting; the bow-tie cortical architecture; the capacity limits match attentional limits
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral framework—describes the physical architecture of the bottleneck without addressing why broadcasting generates subjective experience. Compatible with interactionist dualism if the workspace is reinterpreted as the interface between physical brain and non-physical consciousness

### Quantum Selection / Zeno Effect (Stapp)
- **Proponents**: Henry Stapp (Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory)
- **Core claim**: Consciousness selects among quantum possibilities via the quantum Zeno effect, holding desired neural states stable through repeated attentional observation
- **Key arguments**: Orthodox quantum mechanics permits consciousness to fill the causal gaps left by physical indeterminacy; the Zeno effect provides a mechanism requiring only repeated low-bandwidth observation
- **Relation to site tenets**: The most directly aligned framework. Explains why consciousness has low outbound bandwidth (selection, not computation), why it interfaces at the quantum level (minimal interaction), and why it is causally efficacious (bidirectional)

## Key Debates

### Debate 1: Is Perceptual Consciousness Rich or Sparse?
- **Sides**: Block (rich/overflow) vs Dennett, Cohen & Kanwisher (sparse/ensemble)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether phenomenal experience exceeds cognitive access, or whether apparent richness is constructed from efficient statistical representations
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Recent ensemble perception research favours the sparse view empirically, but the philosophical question—whether ensemble representations are themselves phenomenally conscious—remains open
- **Relevance to asymmetry**: If rich, the inbound bandwidth is truly vast, making the asymmetry more dramatic. If sparse, the asymmetry is reduced but not eliminated

### Debate 2: Why Is Conscious Throughput So Low?
- **Sides**: Evolutionary constraint (inherited serial processing from primitive organisms) vs Functional necessity (consciousness must be serial for coherent decision-making) vs Interface limitation (the mind-brain interface has inherently limited bandwidth)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the bottleneck is a contingent feature of brain evolution, a necessary feature of conscious function, or evidence of a fundamentally different substrate
- **Current state**: Zheng & Meister favour the evolutionary explanation but acknowledge it's speculative. No consensus exists

### Debate 3: Does Consciousness Initiate or Select?
- **Sides**: Libet-inspired veto view (consciousness selects/vetoes among unconsciously prepared options) vs Traditional agency view (consciousness initiates voluntary actions) vs Epiphenomenalist view (consciousness does neither—it merely accompanies)
- **Core disagreement**: The causal role of consciousness in action
- **Current state**: Libet's original experiments are heavily criticised methodologically, but the broader question—whether consciousness computes or selects—remains vital. The selection view is more compatible with low outbound bandwidth

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, an article could:

1. **"The Bandwidth Asymmetry" (primary angle)** — Frame the asymmetry as evidence for the interface model of consciousness. The inbound channel (sensory experience) has enormous bandwidth; the outbound channel (volitional influence) has ~10 bits/s. This is exactly what interactionist dualism with minimal quantum interaction predicts: consciousness receives vast information from the physical world but influences it only through a narrow quantum-level interface. The brain computes options; consciousness selects. This aligns with Dualism (the bottleneck is unexplained materialistically), Minimal Quantum Interaction (narrow outbound channel), and Bidirectional Interaction (selection is genuine causation, not epiphenomenon).

2. **"The Consciousness Bottleneck as Evidence Against Epiphenomenalism"** — If consciousness were merely an epiphenomenal byproduct, why would it exhibit a consistent throughput ceiling? Epiphenomena don't have capacity limits—they're not doing work. The very existence of a measurable bandwidth suggests consciousness is performing a function: selection under constraint. The ~10 bits/s figure is not randomness; it's consistent, task-independent, and irreducible. Something is doing exactly 10 bits/s worth of work.

3. **"Inner Brain, Outer Brain, and the Interface"** — Use Zheng & Meister's inner/outer brain distinction as a naturalistic framework that maps onto the Map's interface model. The outer brain is physical computation (unconscious, parallel, high-bandwidth). The inner brain is where consciousness meets matter (serial, low-bandwidth, selection-based). The unexplained gap between them is the hard problem in information-theoretic form.

## Gaps in Research

- **No direct measurement of inbound bandwidth**: The "richness" of conscious experience is inferred from subjective reports and phenomenological argument, not information-theoretically quantified. The overflow debate (Block vs Dennett) turns on this unresolved question
- **Evolutionary explanation is speculative**: Zheng & Meister's suggestion that serial processing was inherited from primitive organisms is a hypothesis without direct evidence
- **Quantum decoherence challenges**: Stapp's quantum Zeno mechanism, which most directly supports the Map's framework, faces serious criticisms from decoherence timescale calculations. The Map already addresses this (Hagan et al. corrections, biological quantum effects) but should engage with the specific bandwidth implications
- **No philosophical treatment of the asymmetry per se**: While individual components (10 bits/s output, rich phenomenology, the hard problem) are extensively discussed, the asymmetry between inbound and outbound bandwidth as a unified philosophical datum has received little dedicated attention. This is a genuine gap and an opportunity for the Map
- **Connection to the binding problem**: The enormous bandwidth required to produce unified conscious experience (binding disparate sensory inputs into a coherent scene) versus the minimal bandwidth of conscious output—this connection is underexplored in the literature
