## Framing note (why this is a distinct article, not a third intersubjectivity piece)

The Map already treats intersubjectivity *as content* in two live articles:
`consciousness-and-intersubjectivity` (is consciousness constitutively intersubjective or solitary?)
and `consciousness-and-social-understanding` (does genuine social understanding require phenomenal
consciousness?). To avoid coalesce-worthy overlap, the downstream article this note feeds must take
the **epistemic/evidential** angle, not the metaphysical-content angle:

> **The core question**: What kind of evidence about consciousness does genuine, reciprocal,
> real-time *engagement with* another mind provide that neither third-person observation of a
> passive subject nor first-person introspective report can provide?

This reframes second-person neuroscience from a subfield of social cognition into a candidate
**third evidential channel** alongside the two the philosophy of mind usually recognizes
(third-person measurement; first-person report). The distinctive claim to investigate is *epistemic*:
that reciprocity itself is a source of evidence, not merely a richer stimulus condition.

## Executive Summary

"Second-person neuroscience" (Schilbach et al. 2013, a *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* target article)
argues that the neural and cognitive processes underlying real, reciprocal social interaction differ
in kind from those recruited when a solitary subject merely *observes* social stimuli — and that
because standard neuroscience isolates the subject, these interactive processes are the "dark matter"
of the field: dominant by mass, invisible to the usual instruments. The empirical program built around
this claim (dual-EEG / dual-fMRI *hyperscanning*, interactive vs. spectatorial paradigms) and its
developmental and phenomenological cousins (Reddy's second-person infancy science, Trevarthen's primary
intersubjectivity, Levinson's "interaction engine", Fuchs & De Jaegher's participatory sense-making)
together make a strong case that **being addressed** and **being responded to** disclose facets of
another mind that spectating cannot reach. For the Map this is valuable precisely as a *methodological*
result: it marks a genuine evidential channel with its own signature (contingency, reciprocity,
being-a-"you"), while — critically — it is **not** positive proof of dualism. It is a channel; the
question is what the channel can and cannot license.

## Key Sources

### Schilbach et al. (2013), "Toward a second-person neuroscience" (target article)
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/behavioral-and-brain-sciences/article/abs/further-steps-toward-a-secondperson-neuroscience/D40C82F6900B866E51984595E000CF3A (journal); https://philpapers.org/rec/SCHTAS-15 (record)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed target article (with open peer commentary), *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*
- **Citation (VERIFIED title + venue)**: Schilbach, L., Timmermans, B., Reddy, V., Costall, A., Bente, G., Schlicht, T., & Vogeley, K. (2013). Toward a second-person neuroscience. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 36(4), 393–414. DOI: 10.1017/S0140525X12000660
- **Key points**:
  - The neural mechanisms of real social *encounters* are the "dark matter" of social neuroscience — mostly unstudied because paradigms isolate the subject.
  - Central empirical wager: social cognition is *different in kind* during interaction than during observation (the "interactor vs. observer" or "online vs. offline" distinction), not merely quantitatively richer.
  - Two candidate constituents of the second-person stance: emotional **engagement** and **reciprocity** — you are treated as, and treat the other as, a "you."
  - Methodological upshot: study minds while they are *coupled*, using genuinely interactive designs, not spectator tasks.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-supportive of the Map's stance on the limits of third-person science (Tenet 5). Does not by itself argue for or against dualism. Supplies a methodological distinction the Map can exploit without inheriting the authors' (broadly enactivist/non-dualist) metaphysics.
- **Quote (paraphrase from abstract)**: the neural underpinnings of social interaction represent the "dark matter" of social neuroscience.

### De Jaegher, Di Paolo & Gallagher (2010), "Can social interaction constitute social cognition?"
- **URL**: https://www.cell.com/trends/cognitive-sciences/abstract/S1364-6613(10)00146-4 ; https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20674467/
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed opinion/review, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*
- **Citation (VERIFIED title + venue)**: De Jaegher, H., Di Paolo, E., & Gallagher, S. (2010). Can social interaction constitute social cognition? *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 14(10), 441–447. DOI: 10.1016/j.tics.2010.06.009
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes three explanatory roles interaction could play: **contextual**, **enabling**, and **constitutive**. This taxonomy is the crux of the evidential question — is interaction just a better *condition* for observing individual cognition, or is it partly *what the cognition is*?
  - Operational definition of social interaction (co-regulated coupling of two autonomous agents that retains its own dynamics).
- **Tenet alignment**: The *constitutive* reading pushes toward an anti-individualist, enactivist metaphysics that sits in tension with the Map's **Dualism** and ontological individuality of consciousness. The Map should adopt the *taxonomy as a tool* while resisting the constitutive metaphysics — a clean example of taking method without ontology.
- **Why it matters for the article**: gives the precise conceptual vocabulary (contextual/enabling/constitutive) needed to state carefully what a second-person *evidential* channel does and does not commit us to.

### Fuchs & De Jaegher (2009), "Enactive intersubjectivity: Participatory sense-making and mutual incorporation"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11097-009-9136-4
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed article, *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences*
- **Citation (VERIFIED title + venue)**: Fuchs, T., & De Jaegher, H. (2009). Enactive intersubjectivity: Participatory sense-making and mutual incorporation. *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences*, 8(4), 465–486. DOI: 10.1007/s11097-009-9136-4
- **Key points**:
  - Social understanding is an ongoing, dynamical process of **participatory sense-making** — meaning generated *between* agents in real-time coordination, not decoded from behavioral output.
  - Phenomenological gloss: "mutual incorporation" — the lived bodies of both participants form a common intercorporality; understanding happens *in* the coupling.
  - Explicit critique of the "third-person paradigm of social cognition as passive observation."
- **Tenet alignment**: Phenomenologically rich, metaphysically enactivist. Same posture as above: harvest the phenomenology of the second-person stance (what reciprocal engagement *feels like* and *reveals*) without endorsing the deflation of individual consciousness.
- **Quote (paraphrase)**: research on the "social brain" has favored a third-person paradigm of social cognition as passive observation of others' behavior.

### Reddy (2008), *How Infants Know Minds*
- **URL**: https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674046078 (ISBN 9780674026667)
- **Type**: Monograph, Harvard University Press
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes a **second-person solution** to the problem of other minds: *we know other minds if we can respond to them* — knowing-by-engagement rather than knowing-by-inference.
  - Developmental evidence: pre-verbal infants tease, joke, show self-consciousness, and track others' attention/expectations *within* face-to-face engagement, long before any "theory of mind."
  - Argues primary intersubjectivity is not merely a precursor to mentalizing but *the* primary and continuing mode of grasping others in second-person interaction.
- **Tenet alignment**: Supportive of the epistemic claim (engagement discloses minds) while agnostic on ontology. Useful as evidence that the second-person channel is developmentally *prior* — it is not a late, theory-laden add-on.
- **Quote (from reviews)**: "we know other minds if we can respond to them."

### Levinson (2006), "On the human 'interaction engine'"
- **URL**: https://pure.mpg.de/rest/items/item_3171367_3/component/file_3186123/content ; https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rstb.2021.0092 (2021 comparative reappraisal)
- **Type**: Book chapter (in Enfield & Levinson, eds., *Roots of Human Sociality*, Berg, pp. 39–69)
- **Key points**:
  - Posits an evolved "interaction engine": a package of pre-linguistic pragmatic competencies — joint attention, rapid nested turn-taking (~200 ms gaps), repair, recognition of communicative intention — that scaffolds language.
  - Turn-taking as a signature of genuine reciprocity: the ~200 ms response gap implies predictive coupling that only exists *inside* live interaction.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Supplies a concrete, measurable behavioral marker of the second-person channel (contingent timing) that a spectatorial paradigm cannot exhibit. Useful for making "reciprocity" operational rather than merely evocative.

### Trevarthen (1979) — primary intersubjectivity (background/classic)
- **Type**: Foundational developmental-psychology concept (cited throughout the above)
- **Key point**: By ~6–8 weeks infants engage in reciprocal, affectively attuned face-to-face "protoconversation" — evidence of an innate readiness for second-person engagement prior to conceptual mind-reading.
- **Note**: Cite as historical grounding; verify exact 1979 chapter details at publisher of record before using in-article (classic reference, metadata varies across secondary sources).

### Hyperscanning empirical literature (dual-EEG / dual-fMRI)
- **URLs**: review — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6186988/ ; systematic review of spoken-communication hyperscanning — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0149763420305650
- **Type**: Empirical methods program
- **Key points**:
  - Hyperscanning records two (or more) brains simultaneously, enabling measurement of **inter-brain synchrony/coupling** — a quantity that *only exists across a dyad* and is therefore literally invisible to single-subject neuroscience.
  - Role- and context-dependence: beta-band motor-area modulation depends on interaction-vs-observation and actor-vs-observer role; shared-attention periods show distinct centroparietal signatures.
  - Caveats (important for evidential-status discipline): inter-brain synchrony can arise from **common input** (both partners see the same thing) or shared bodily rhythm rather than from genuine mind-to-mind coupling; establishing that synchrony is *evidence of shared consciousness* rather than correlated stimulation is an open, contested inferential step.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral evidence base. Its very existence supports the methodological point (there are dyad-level observables); its interpretive pitfalls support the Map's evidential-status discipline (a channel, not a proof).

### Fuchs (2013), "The phenomenology and development of social perspectives"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11097-012-9267-x
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed article, *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences* (DOI: 10.1007/s11097-012-9267-x)
- **Key point**: Systematizes the first/second/third-person *perspectives* and argues neutral observation is a scientific abstraction layered on top of a prior engaged stance. Directly useful for the article's tripartite evidential framing.

## Major Positions

### Position A: "Interactive turn" / second-person primacy (Schilbach, Reddy, Fuchs, De Jaegher, Di Paolo, Gallagher)
- **Core claim**: Reciprocal interaction is epistemically (and for some, constitutively) primary; observation and inference are derivative, late, or abstracted modes.
- **Key arguments**: dark-matter argument (interactive processes are dominant but unstudied); developmental priority (Reddy/Trevarthen); phenomenology of the engaged stance; dyad-level observables (hyperscanning).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The *epistemic* half is congenial and directly supports **Tenet 5** (third-person science has limits). The strong *constitutive/enactivist* half conflicts with **Dualism** and the Map's ontological individuality of consciousness. The article should split these: endorse the evidential channel, quarantine the anti-individualist metaphysics.

### Position B: Third-person / theory-theory orthodoxy (implicit foil)
- **Core claim**: Understanding other minds is inference to hidden mental states from observed behavior; the interaction adds no evidence in principle that a good enough observer + theory couldn't extract.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map partly agrees this is *inadequate* (with the interactive-turn critics) but for its *own* reason: the residual privacy of phenomenal consciousness (Dualism) means no third-person channel — however rich — closes the gap. This is the Map's distinctive move: use second-person neuroscience to widen the evidence base *and* to dramatize that even the widened base does not deliver another's qualia.

### Position C (the Map's likely synthesis): Second-person as a genuine-but-bounded evidential channel
- **Core claim**: Engagement provides real evidence unavailable to spectating — contingency you can only detect by *being the one responded to*, reciprocal timing, the felt address of being a "you." This channel is developmentally primary and neurally measurable at the dyad level. **But** it is evidence about *coupling and responsiveness*, not a window into another's phenomenal interior; it neither closes the other-minds gap nor proves dualism. It refines *what third-person science can and cannot reach* (Tenet 5) and gives phenomenal-authority a social dimension without dissolving individual consciousness.

## Key Debates

### Debate 1: Contextual vs. enabling vs. constitutive (De Jaegher et al. 2010)
- **Sides**: enactivists (interaction is at least partly *constitutive* of cognition) vs. cognitivists (interaction is a *context* or *enabler* for individual cognition).
- **Core disagreement**: whether the dyadic dynamics are part of the *cognition itself* or just favorable conditions for it.
- **Map's stake**: The Map can accept "enabling" (interaction unlocks evidence and capacities) while rejecting "constitutive of consciousness." This is the load-bearing distinction for keeping the evidential channel without the enactivist ontology.

### Debate 2: What does inter-brain synchrony actually evidence?
- **Sides**: coupling-is-meaningful vs. synchrony-is-artifact (common input / shared rhythm).
- **Current state**: unresolved; a live methodological caution. Essential for the article's evidential-status discipline.

### Debate 3: Is the second-person channel *sui generis* or reducible?
- **Sides**: Reddy/Fuchs (irreducible second-person mode) vs. those who see it as first-person + third-person combined.
- **Map's stake**: The interesting article claim is that reciprocity supplies evidence *neither* introspection *nor* observation supplies — the contingency signal of being-responded-to. Worth stating carefully and hedging.
