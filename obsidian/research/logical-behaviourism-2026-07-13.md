## Executive Summary

Logical (analytical) behaviourism is a **semantic thesis about the meaning of mental terms**: statements about beliefs, desires, sensations and the rest are either translatable into statements about behaviour and behavioural dispositions, or else — where they resist such translation — the product of a logical confusion. It is the historical **first term** in the materialist lineage the Map otherwise covers page by page (`type-identity-theory` → `functionalism` → `eliminative-materialism`, with `biological-naturalism` as a later off-shoot), and it supplied that lineage's two most durable weapons against dualism: Ryle's **"category-mistake"** and the **"Ghost in the Machine"**. It is also the lineage's **first casualty**: by the early 1960s it had been defeated *from within materialism* by Putnam's super-Spartan case, the circularity/holism-of-dispositions objection (Chisholm, Geach), and the residual felt-character ("what-it's-like") the translation leaves out — the same three objections a dualist can turn against any behavioural reduction. This note is research substrate for a future `concepts/logical-behaviourism.md`; it does not re-derive the successor pages, only fixes the missing predecessor and the reply the Map owes to Ryle.

## The Core Distinction (do not conflate)

- **Logical / analytical / philosophical behaviourism** (Ryle, Hempel, Carnap in his 1930s physicalist phase): a claim about the **meaning** of mental vocabulary. To attribute a mental state *is* to attribute a pattern of actual and hypothetical behaviour; "inner" mental-state talk is either shorthand for dispositions or a category mistake. This is the note's subject.
- **Methodological / psychological behaviourism** (Watson, Skinner): a **research methodology** — psychology should study only observable behaviour and stimulus-response laws, bracketing the inner as unscientific. A stance about scientific practice, not about meaning.

The Stanford Encyclopedia states the philosophical thesis directly: "Analytical or logical behaviorism is a theory within philosophy about the meaning or semantics of mental terms or concepts" (verbatim, SEP "Behaviorism"). A page can be a strict logical behaviourist and reject Skinner's psychology, and vice versa; the two share only a suspicion of the inner.

## Key Sources

### Ryle, *The Concept of Mind* (1949) — primary source, verified
- **URL**: publisher London: Hutchinson, 1949; Univ. of Chicago Press reprint 2002; Penguin. Chapter 1 "Descartes' Myth" read in full via Purdue course PDF (web.ics.purdue.edu/~drkelly/RyleDescartesMyth1949.pdf), pp. 11-24.
- **Type**: Book (primary)
- **Key points**:
  - Names the Cartesian picture "the official doctrine" and, in his own words, "the dogma of the Ghost in the Machine".
  - Diagnoses the doctrine as a **category-mistake**: mind and body are treated as terms of the *same* logical type (two "things", two "collateral histories"), when mental-conduct concepts belong to a different logical category — dispositions, capacities, and performances rather than occult inner episodes.
  - Positive account: to have a mind is to be *disposed to behave* intelligently; "knowing how" (a skill, a competence) is not reducible to "knowing that" (possession of inner propositions). The mind is "not hidden — it is expressed."
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with Tenet 1. Ryle's whole project is to dissolve the very inner phenomenal realm the Map holds is irreducible. This is the argument the Map must answer, not endorse.
- **Verbatim quotes (confirmed at primary source, "Descartes' Myth", §2 "The Absurdity of the Official Doctrine")**:
  - "I shall often speak of it, with deliberate abusiveness, as 'the dogma of the Ghost in the Machine.' I hope to prove that it is entirely false, and false not in detail but in principle."
  - "It is one big mistake and a mistake of a special kind. It is, namely, a category-mistake. It represents the facts of mental life as if they belonged to one logical type or category (or range of types or categories), when they actually belong to another."
  - Note Ryle's own hyphenation: **category-mistake** (not "category mistake"). His illustration is the visitor shown Oxford's colleges, libraries, and playing fields who then asks where "the University" is — expecting an entity *over and above* the colleges, as the dualist expects a mind over and above intelligent conduct.

### Hempel, "The Logical Analysis of Psychology" (1935 / 1949 trans.)
- **URL**: PhilPapers rec HEMTLA; SEP "Behaviorism".
- **Type**: Paper. Originally "Analyse logique de la psychologie", *Revue de Synthèse* 10 (1935); English translation in Feigl & Sellars (eds.), *Readings in Philosophical Analysis* (1949), and later in Block (ed.), *Readings in Philosophy of Psychology*, vol. 1 (1980).
- **Key points**: The logical-positivist / verificationist route into behaviourism. A psychological statement is meaningful only insofar as it is testable, and its test conditions are describable in physical-thing / behavioural language; so its meaning is exhausted by translatable behavioural consequences.
- **Verbatim (via SEP)**: on this view "all psychological statements that are meaningful … are translatable into statements that do not involve psychological concepts."
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with Tenet 1 (denies any meaningful surplus beyond the behaviourally testable).
- **Important scholarly caveat (calibration)**: The received textbook picture — Hempel and Carnap as strict logical behaviourists — is **contested**. Scholarship (e.g. the "Laying a Legend to Rest" line, Springer 2016) argues both admitted reference to internal neurophysiological states and treated the psychology-to-physical links as *synthetic and a posteriori*, not analytic. Hempel himself later (1966/1980) recanted the reductive reading. The future article should present Ryle as the paradigm analytical behaviourist and flag Hempel/Carnap as the verificationist *route in* while noting that ascribing the full analytic-translation thesis to them oversimplifies.

### Carnap, "Psychology in Physical Language" (1932/33)
- **URL**: originally "Psychologie in physikalischer Sprache", *Erkenntnis* 3 (1932/33); English in Ayer (ed.), *Logical Positivism* (1959).
- **Type**: Paper. Physicalism as the background program: every meaningful sentence is translatable into the intersubjective "physical language." Supplies the semantic backdrop against which behavioural translation of the mental looked mandatory.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with Tenet 1. Same caveat as Hempel about the strength of the reductive reading.

### Putnam, "Brains and Behavior" (1963) — the defeat
- **URL**: SEP "Functionalism"; IEP "Functionalism". In R. J. Butler (ed.), *Analytical Philosophy: Second Series* (Oxford: Blackwell, 1963); reprinted in Putnam, *Mind, Language and Reality: Philosophical Papers* vol. 2 (Cambridge Univ. Press, 1975).
- **Type**: Paper.
- **Key points**: The **super-Spartan** thought experiment. Imagine a community of warriors who so thoroughly suppress pain that they exhibit *no* pain behaviour and *no disposition* to it — yet still feel pain (they will, in level voices, admit it). If pain were *analytically* a behavioural disposition, super-Spartans could not feel pain; they plainly could. Conclusion: the pain–behaviour relation is **causal, not constitutive**; logical behaviourism is false. (A complementary case is the perfect actor, who has the pain behaviour with no pain.)
- **Tenet alignment for the Map**: a materialist's argument, but its structure — *the felt state can come apart from its behaviour* — is exactly the wedge Tenet 1 drives. Attribute the super-Spartan case to **Putnam alone**.

## Major Positions

### Logical / Analytical Behaviourism (the position itself)
- **Proponents**: Ryle (paradigm); Hempel and Carnap (verificationist route, with the caveat above); Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* is a frequent fellow-traveller though he resisted the label.
- **Core claim**: Mental-state terms are definable in / translatable into statements about behaviour and behavioural **dispositions**; there is no inner theatre the terms name over and above the dispositions.
- **Key arguments**: (a) the category-mistake diagnosis of dualism; (b) the verificationist demand that meaning reduce to testable (behavioural) conditions; (c) the anti-private-language / other-minds motivation — if meaning were inner and private, mental talk could not be publicly learned or corrected, yet it plainly is.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The **direct antagonist** of Tenet 1. Its category-mistake charge is still the most-cited objection to interactionist dualism.

### Type-Identity Theory (the successor — cross-reference, do NOT re-derive)
- **Proponents**: U. T. Place, "Is Consciousness a Brain Process?" (*British Journal of Psychology*, 1956); J. J. C. Smart, "Sensations and Brain Processes" (*Philosophical Review*, 1959).
- **Core claim**: Mental states are *identical* to brain states (a contingent, a posteriori identity), not behavioural dispositions. Explicitly framed by Place/Smart as escaping behaviourism's failure to accommodate the *occurrent, felt* character of sensation.
- **Relation**: The Map already covers this at `concepts/type-identity-theory.md`. The future behaviourism page should hand off to it as "the successor that kept materialism but abandoned the semantic-translation strategy."

## Key Debates

### Did logical behaviourism ever really claim analytic translation?
- **Sides**: Textbook history (Ryle as behaviourist, Hempel/Carnap as analytic reductionists) vs revisionist scholarship ("legend": Carnap/Hempel allowed inner states and synthetic links).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the position was ever held in the strong form its critics attacked, or is partly a retrospective straw target.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; the strong analytic-translation thesis is a real position (Ryle, early Hempel) but ascribing it wholesale to the positivists is disputed. The page should hedge accordingly.

### What defeated it (the three objections)
- **Sides**: Behaviourists vs the emerging identity theorists and functionalists (Putnam, Chisholm, Geach) of the late 1950s–early 1960s.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether mental terms *mean* behaviour.
- **Current state**: **Resolved against behaviourism.** Three objections, each attributed to its single author/pair:
  1. **Super-Spartan / perfect-actor (Putnam, 1963)**: felt state and behaviour dissociate in both directions — the relation is causal, not analytic.
  2. **Circularity / holism of dispositions (Chisholm, *Perceiving*, 1957; Geach, *Mental Acts*, 1957)**: you cannot specify the behavioural disposition constituting a *belief* without invoking the subject's *desires*, and vice versa; the translation never bottoms out in pure behaviour. SEP "Functionalism" notes this circularity is exactly what pushed the field to functionalism (a mental state defined by relations to inputs, outputs, *and other mental states*).
  3. **Absent / residual qualia (the "what-it's-like" residue)**: even a complete dispositional profile leaves out the felt character of a sensation. (This is the general phenomenal-residue objection later sharpened by Nagel 1974 and Block 1978 against functionalism; against behaviourism it is best stated as the point that dispositions omit the occurrent feel — attribute the *sharpened* versions to Nagel/Block, not to a behaviourism-era author.)

## Relation to Site Perspective (Tenet 1)

Logical behaviourism is the **original, foundational anti-dualist argument**, and Ryle's category-mistake charge remains the single most-cited objection to interactionism: the "Ghost in the Machine" is precisely the Map's central posit — an irreducible, causally-efficacious mind — reframed as a logical confusion. The future article's job is to answer it, not dodge it.

**The Map's reply, in one line**: the category-mistake charge only bites if mental terms are **exhaustively** dispositional/behavioural — and the very objections that killed behaviourism *from within materialism* show they are not. The super-Spartan (Putnam) and the qualia-residue point demonstrate a surplus — the felt, occurrent "what-it's-like" — that no behavioural translation captures. That residue is Tenet 1's phenomenal consciousness. So the dualist gets Ryle's own executioners for free: objections a fortiori available against any *behavioural* reduction of experience.

A sharper framing worth developing in the article: **Ryle does not so much refute the ghost as decline to look for it.** His method dissolves the inner theatre by redescribing mind-talk as disposition-talk; it never confronts the datum — that there is something it is like to be in pain — that motivated the inner in the first place. Dispositionalism changes the subject. The category-mistake accusation, turned around, is that treating the felt quality of experience as *nothing but* a disposition to behave is itself a category error in the opposite direction: mistaking a report of what-it's-like for a summary of behavioural tendencies.

Care to keep for the article: the Map should **concede** what behaviourism got right — mind is not a second *substance-thing* sitting behind the colleges, and much mental vocabulary genuinely is dispositional/relational (Ryle's positive story about skills and "knowing how" survives). The Map's dualism is a claim about the irreducibility of *phenomenal character*, not a defence of a Cartesian inner mechanism. Framing the reply this way keeps it honest and avoids attacking the strawman Ryle himself warned against.
