## Executive Summary

The Simulation Theory of Memory (STM) holds that remembering is a species of imagining: to recall a past episode is to *construct* (simulate) a representation of it rather than to retrieve a stored record. The view has two intertwined lineages. In cognitive neuroscience, Schacter and Addis's **constructive episodic simulation hypothesis** (2007) and Hassabis and Maguire's **scene construction** account argue that remembering the past and imagining the future recruit a shared "core network" (hippocampus, medial temporal lobe, default mode network), and that the constructive, recombinatorial nature of memory exists precisely *because* its real function is prospective — to simulate possible futures. In philosophy, Kourken Michaelian's **(post)causal simulationism** (2016) radicalises this: he finds "no intrinsic difference between remembering and imagining" and concludes that to remember is to imagine the past, dropping the requirement of a causal connection (a memory trace) to the original experience. STM is the leading rival to the older **causal theory of memory** (Martin & Deutscher; defended today by Bernecker, Robins) in the active "continuism vs. discontinuism" debate. For the Map, STM is doubly relevant: it deflates the storage/retrieval metaphor the Map's memory articles already reject, but its eliminative reading — that memory is "just" a physical recombination engine — directly opposes the Map's dual-domain framing of episodic memory (physical trace + conscious autonoesis).

## Key Sources

### Constructive Episodic Simulation of the Future and the Past (Addis, Schacter et al.)
- **URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0028393208004223
- **Type**: Paper (Neuropsychologia, 2009)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinct but overlapping subsystems of a single core brain network mediate imagining and remembering.
  - Constructing an event (drawing details together) recruits common regions; elaborating it shows differences.
  - Empirical anchor for the claim that past and future thinking are neurally continuous.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-conflicting — the shared-substrate finding is compatible with the Map's filter/interface reading but is usually presented as evidence *for* a purely physical constructive mechanism.

### Episodic Simulation of Future Events: Concepts, Data, and Applications (Schacter, Addis, Buckner)
- **URL**: https://www.prospectivepsych.org/sites/default/files/pictures/Schacter-et-al_Episodic-simulation-of-future-events-2008.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (Annals NYAS, 2008)
- **Key points**:
  - States the functional argument: memory is "constructive and prospective rather than archival."
  - Because the *components* of episodic memories are stored independently, they can be flexibly extracted and recombined to simulate novel (especially future) events.
  - The constructive flexibility that produces memory errors is the price of a system optimised for future simulation (the "adaptive constructive processes" thesis).
- **Quote**: "The constituent elements of episodic memories are stored independently, [which] facilitates mental simulation, as details are easily extractable from different episodic memories and can be recombined to construct simulations of hypothetical events."

### The cognitive neuroscience of constructive memory: remembering the past and imagining the future (Schacter & Addis)
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2429996/
- **Type**: Paper (Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B, 2007 — the founding statement)
- **Key points**: Original articulation of the constructive episodic simulation hypothesis; ties memory distortion to the adaptive demands of future simulation.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral on metaphysics; conflicts only when read eliminatively.

### Patients with hippocampal amnesia cannot imagine new experiences (Hassabis, Kumaran, Vann, Maguire)
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2666708/ (related: scene construction work)
- **Type**: Paper (PNAS, 2007) — the scene-construction evidence
- **Key points**:
  - Five patients with hippocampal damage, asked to imagine fictitious scenarios and future episodes, were significantly impaired on *both*; their descriptions lacked spatial coherence and were fragmented.
  - Supports a *common* hippocampal "scene construction" function across remembering, fictitious imagining, and future thinking.
- **Quote**: Patients' imagined experiences were "lacking in spatial coherence" — taken as evidence that the same constructive machinery underwrites memory and imagination.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; the dependence of imagination on memory machinery is the empirical core both sides accept.

### Mental Time Travel: Episodic Memory and Our Knowledge of the Personal Past (Michaelian)
- **URL**: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262551151/mental-time-travel/
- **Type**: Book (MIT Press, 2016) — the canonical philosophical statement of STM
- **Key points**:
  - Memory is a form of *mental time travel*, continuous with future-oriented imagination.
  - "Finding no intrinsic difference between remembering and imagining, he argues that to remember is to imagine the past." Drops the causal-connection (trace) requirement of the causal theory.
  - Investigates the *reliability* of simulational memory via the adaptivity of constructive processes and metacognitive monitoring.
  - Sketches an evolutionary account distinguishing human episodic memory from animal "episodic-like" memory.
- **Quote**: To remember is "to imagine the past."
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts (eliminative reading) — Michaelian's naturalistic framework treats remembering as a wholly physical/computational reconstructive process with no role for an irreducible conscious contribution.

### Against discontinuism: Mental time travel and our knowledge of past and future events (Michaelian)
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/rec/MICMTT
- **Type**: Paper — Michaelian's defence of continuism
- **Key points**: Rebuts metaphysical discontinuism (alleged causal/intentional/phenomenological breaks between memory and future MTT) and epistemological discontinuism (epistemic openness of the future, directness of past knowledge, immunity to error through misidentification).

### Confabulating, Misremembering, Relearning: The Simulation Theory of Memory and Unsuccessful Remembering (Michaelian)
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2016.01857/full
- **Type**: Paper (Frontiers in Psychology, 2016)
- **Key points**: Michaelian's reply to the confabulation objection; concedes Bernecker's positive causalist account is "partly successful" while arguing simulationism survives the attack.

### The Simulation Theory of Memory and the phenomenology of remembering (Rivadulla-Duró)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11097-022-09881-z
- **Type**: Paper (Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences, 2022) — a key recent critique
- **Key points**:
  - STM must explain how we distinguish genuine memory from free imagination; it appeals to a distinctive "feeling of remembering."
  - Two objections to how that feeling is "engineered" in the theory.
  - Conclusion: STM "cannot simultaneously defend the simulational character of episodic memory and ground our ability to discriminate between memories and imaginings."
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns (as ammunition) with the Map — the discrimination gap is where a non-physical / phenomenological contribution could do explanatory work the simulationist mechanism cannot.

### Memory (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/memory/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**: Frames the causal-vs-simulation contrast in terms of how each characterises *successful* remembering versus misremembering and confabulation; standard reference for the whole debate.

### Radicalizing simulationism: Remembering as imagining the (nonpersonal) past
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09515089.2022.2082934
- **Type**: Paper (Philosophical Psychology, 2022)
- **Key points**: Pushes simulationism further — remembering need not even target one's *personal* past; the imaginative process is the same regardless. A "radical simulationist" wing.

## Major Positions

### Constructive Episodic Simulation Hypothesis (cognitive neuroscience)
- **Proponents**: Daniel Schacter, Donna Rose Addis, Randy Buckner.
- **Core claim**: Episodic memory and episodic future thinking are products of one constructive system; memory's function is prospective simulation, and its constructive (error-prone) character is an adaptation, not a bug.
- **Key arguments**: shared core-network fMRI activation; correlated past/future deficits in amnesia; the independent-storage-of-components argument for recombinatorial flexibility.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Empirically neutral and largely compatible with the Map's rejection of storage/retrieval. Conflicts only when its proponents assume the constructive *physical* mechanism is the *whole* story of remembering (no residue for consciousness). The Map can adopt the neuroscience while denying the eliminative gloss.

### Simulationism / Post-Causal Simulation Theory (philosophy)
- **Proponents**: Kourken Michaelian; Denis Perrin; André Sant'Anna (continuist/simulationist wing).
- **Core claim**: There is no intrinsic, kind-level difference between remembering and imagining; remembering is imagining (the past), and a causal trace linking memory to the original experience is *not* required for genuine memory.
- **Key arguments**: continuity of neural substrate; constructive memory data; reliability secured by adaptive constructive processes + metacognition rather than by trace-preservation; parity of memory and future MTT (continuism).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts in its standard naturalistic form — treats remembering as fully reducible to physical reconstructive computation, contra **Tenet 1 (dualism)**. But its *deflation of the trace/storage metaphor* is convergent with the Map's existing position that episodic memory is reconstructive re-experiencing.

### Causal Theory of Memory (the discontinuist rival)
- **Proponents**: C. B. Martin & Max Deutscher (1966, origin); Sven Bernecker; Sarah Robins (contemporary defenders).
- **Core claim**: Genuine remembering requires an appropriate *causal connection* — a retained memory trace — between the original experience and the present representation. This is what distinguishes remembering from merely imagining or confabulating, even when the content is accurate.
- **Key arguments**: the **confabulation objection** — simulationism "collapses the processing distinction between memory errors and successful remembering" (Robins); without a trace requirement, an accurate lucky guess about the past would count as a memory. Robins's taxonomy distinguishes errors by (1) accuracy of representation and (2) involvement of retained information.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral-to-aligned in structure — its insistence on a real, retained connection to the past, and that successful remembering is a distinct achievement, leaves more conceptual room for the Map's view that something beyond raw reconstruction (a conscious contribution) marks genuine remembering. But the causal theory is itself physicalist about the trace.

### Phenomenological Critique (the discrimination problem)
- **Proponents**: Andrea Rivadulla-Duró; broadly, those who emphasise the "feeling of pastness" / metacognitive feeling of remembering (Perrin, Sant'Anna also work this seam from inside simulationism).
- **Core claim**: STM cannot, from purely simulational resources, explain how the subject *tells apart* a memory from an imagining; the felt quality of remembering is left underexplained.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns as a wedge — the "feeling of remembering" / autonoesis is exactly where the Map locates an irreducible phenomenal contribution.

## Key Debates

### Continuism vs. Discontinuism
- **Sides**: Continuists (Michaelian, Perrin, Sant'Anna) hold that, temporal orientation aside, episodic memory and future-oriented mental time travel are qualitatively continuous. Discontinuists hold there are further qualitative metaphysical or epistemological differences.
- **Core disagreement**: whether memory of the past and imagination of the future are the *same kind* of mental act differing only in time-direction.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and central; Michaelian's "Against discontinuism" is the focal defence.

### Causalism vs. Simulationism
- **Sides**: Causalists (Bernecker, Robins) require a memory trace; simulationists (Michaelian) do not.
- **Core disagreement**: what makes a present mental state a *memory* rather than an imagining — a causal/trace connection to the past, or just the right kind of (reliable, internally generated) construction.
- **Current state**: Active. The **confabulation objection** is the live pressure point; some propose **explanatory contextualism** (Erkenntnis 2022) as a diagnosis dissolving the dispute. Hybrid/middle positions are emerging.

### The Reliability Question
- **Sides**: If remembering is constructing, why trust it? Simulationists ground reliability in the *adaptivity* of constructive processes plus metacognitive monitoring; critics argue this under-determines the memory/imagination distinction and over-trusts construction.
- **Current state**: Open; central to STM's epistemology.
