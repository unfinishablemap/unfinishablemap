## Executive Summary

Dual-aspect traditions form a broad family of positions claiming that the mental and physical are two aspects of a single underlying reality rather than two substances or one substance with epiphenomenal properties. The tradition runs from Spinoza through Fechner, James, Russell, Pauli-Jung, and into contemporary work by Atmanspacher, Chalmers, Velmans, and Benovsky. For the Map, the critical question is how its bi-aspectual ontology—where structure (wavefunction) and actuality (consciousness) are two genuine aspects of reality—differs from these traditions. The key differentiator: most dual-aspect monisms deny genuine causal interaction between aspects, treating them as complementary descriptions of one neutral substance. The Map's tenets require genuine dualism with bidirectional causal interaction at quantum indeterminacies, making its position structurally distinct from classical dual-aspect monism while sharing the bi-aspectual vocabulary.

- **Type**: Encyclopedia

- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-aspect_theory
- **Type**: Encyclopedia

  - The mental and physical are two aspects of the same substance
  - Not to be confused with mind-body dualism
  - Originated with Spinoza; renewed by Fechner, James, Russell
  - Modern variants include Pauli-Jung complementarity, Velmans' reflexive monism, Atmanspacher's deep structure framework
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction — classical double-aspect theory denies causal interaction between aspects (they are merely different descriptions)

### The Pauli-Jung Conjecture and Its Relatives (Atmanspacher, Open Philosophy 2020)
- **URL**: https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/opphil-2020-0138/html
- **Type**: Academic paper
- **Key points**:
  - Formally augments the Pauli-Jung dual-aspect framework
  - Mental and physical aspects arise from an underlying "unus mundus" (one world) via an "epistemic split"
  - Aspects stand in complementarity (quantum physics sense): mutually exclusive yet jointly necessary
  - "Induced correlations" allow changes in consciousness to indirectly affect the physical via the unconscious and archetypal structures

- **Tenet alignment**: Partially aligns — the complementarity framework respects irreducibility (Dualism tenet) but the "induced correlations" mechanism is weaker than genuine bidirectional causal interaction. The unus mundus is monist, not dualist.
- **Quote**: "It would be most satisfactory if physis and psyche could be conceived as complementary aspects of the same reality" (Pauli, 1952)

### Dual-Aspect Monism à la Pauli and Jung (Atmanspacher, Information Philosopher)
- **URL**: https://www.informationphilosopher.com/presentations/Milan/papers/Dual-aspect-Atmanspacher.pdf
- **Type**: Academic paper
- **Key points**:
  - Dual-aspect approaches consider mental and physical as aspects of an "undivided reality"
  - The distinction between mind and matter results from an "epistemic split"
  - Mind and matter emerge from a holistic domain (unus mundus or implicate order)
  - Correlations between aspects can be "prearranged" (synchronicity) or "induced" (by changes in consciousness)
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction — "induced correlations" is not the same as causal interaction; it operates through the neutral substrate, not directly between aspects

### Russellian Monism (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/russellian-monism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Physics describes only structural/dispositional properties; quiddities (intrinsic natures) ground consciousness
  - Three core theses: structuralism about physics, realism about quiddities, quidditism about consciousness
  - Panpsychist variant: quiddities are themselves phenomenal
  - Panprotopsychist variant: quiddities are proto-phenomenal, giving rise to consciousness at higher levels
  - Avoids epiphenomenalism: quiddities play indispensable roles in physical causation
- **Tenet alignment**: Partially aligns — shares the insight that physics describes only structure. Conflicts with Dualism tenet because it is ultimately monist (one kind of stuff with two faces). Conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction because consciousness doesn't causally interact with the physical; it *is* the intrinsic nature of the physical.
- **Quote**: "On Russellian monism, the properties that constitute consciousness—quiddities and structural properties—play indispensable roles in physical causation."

### Spinoza's Theory of Attributes (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/spinoza-attributes/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Substance has infinite attributes; we know two: Thought and Extension
  - Attributes are "explanatorily self-contained" with a conceptual barrier between them
  - Physical changes must be explained by physical items; ideas by other ideas
  - No causal interaction between attributes — this is constitutive of the framework
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction. Spinoza's parallelism is the defining feature the Map must reject. Also conflicts with Dualism: Spinoza is explicitly monist (one substance, two attributes).
- **Quote**: "Spinoza holds Thought and Extension to be explanatorily self-contained, with a conceptual barrier between them."

## Major Positions

### Spinozist Dual-Aspect Monism
- **Proponents**: Baruch Spinoza, Gustav Fechner, contemporary Spinozists
- **Core claim**: One substance (God/Nature) with infinite attributes, of which we know Thought and Extension. Mental and physical are parallel, non-interacting attributes of the same substance.
- **Key arguments**: Elegantly dissolves the interaction problem by denying interaction. Preserves irreducibility of mental to physical. Grounded in Spinoza's rationalist metaphysics.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts with Dualism** (one substance, not two or more ontological categories), **conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction** (parallelism denies causal interaction between aspects). The Map's view differs from Spinoza specifically because it insists that consciousness *causally acts on* the physical at quantum indeterminacies, which Spinoza's framework categorically forbids.

### Pauli-Jung Complementarity
- **Proponents**: Wolfgang Pauli, Carl Jung, Harald Atmanspacher, Dean Rickles
- **Core claim**: Mental and physical aspects emerge from an underlying psychophysically neutral reality (unus mundus) through an "epistemic split." The aspects are complementary in the quantum-mechanical sense—mutually exclusive descriptions that are jointly necessary.
- **Key arguments**: Draws on Bohr's complementarity principle. Explains synchronicity as "prearranged correlations" between aspects at the level of the unus mundus. "Induced correlations" allow consciousness changes to indirectly affect physical domain through the neutral substrate.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Partially aligns with Dualism** (mental and physical are genuinely irreducible to each other), **partially aligns with Minimal Quantum Interaction** (quantum complementarity provides a formal bridge), but **conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction** (induced correlations via a neutral substrate is not direct causal interaction). The Map is more ontologically committed: it claims genuine causal interaction, not merely correlational coupling through a deeper layer.

### Russellian Monism
- **Proponents**: Bertrand Russell, David Chalmers, Philip Goff, Torin Alter, Sam Coleman
- **Core claim**: Physics describes only the structural and dispositional properties of matter. The intrinsic nature of matter (quiddities) may be phenomenal or proto-phenomenal. Consciousness is what matter is "from the inside."
- **Key arguments**: Physics tells us what matter *does*, not what matter *is*. The "structural dynamics" argument: physical theory is purely structural; something must realize that structure; consciousness is the best candidate for the realizer.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Partially aligns with Dualism** in spirit (consciousness is not reducible to physical structure), but **technically conflicts** because Russellian monism is ultimately monist—there's one kind of stuff with two faces, not two ontological categories. **Conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction** because consciousness doesn't interact with physics from outside; it *is* the intrinsic nature of physical entities. The Map's "partial but complete physics" claim (physics describes all structure but not all reality) is close to Chalmers' "structural dynamics" argument, but the Map goes further by insisting on genuine causal interaction at collapse.

### Neutral Monism
- **Proponents**: Ernst Mach, William James, Bertrand Russell (early), contemporary followers
- **Core claim**: The basic stuff of reality is neither mental nor physical. Mental and physical are different arrangements or organizations of this neutral stuff.
- **Key arguments**: Dissolves the mind-body problem by denying both substances. Compatible with empiricism (James's "pure experience"). Avoids the interaction problem entirely.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts with Dualism** (no genuine dualism; one neutral substance), **conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction** (no interaction because no distinct domains to interact). The Map requires ontological commitment that neutral monism explicitly avoids.

### Reflexive Monism (Velmans)
- **Proponents**: Max Velmans
- **Core claim**: Conscious experiences and brain processes are complementary first-person and third-person appearances of a single underlying reality. Neither is reducible to the other.
- **Key arguments**: Phenomenological observation that our experience of the external world *is* the physical world as experienced. Bridges materialist/dualist divide through epistemological rather than ontological analysis.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Epistemologically useful** (the first-person/third-person distinction maps onto the Map's structure/actuality distinction), but **ontologically insufficient** — reflexive monism is still monist and does not provide the causal interaction the Map requires.

### Benovsky's Dual-Aspect Pan-Proto-Psychism
- **Proponents**: Jiri Benovsky
- **Core claim**: Everything has two aspects—physical and proto-mental. Proto-mental aspects at the fundamental level are not experiential; full consciousness emerges through combination. The combination problem is addressed via eliminativism about the self.
- **Key arguments**: Dual-aspect monism has "all the advantages of property dualism and then some." Avoids interaction problem. The combination problem (how micro-mental aspects combine into macro-consciousness) is reframed through the concept of "mineness."
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts with Dualism** (monist foundation), **conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction** (no causal interaction between aspects). Le Bihan's rejoinder questions whether "aspects" do genuine explanatory work — a concern the Map should note.

## Key Debates

### The Interaction Problem: Avoided or Dissolved?
- **Sides**: Dual-aspect monists (Spinoza, Atmanspacher) claim to dissolve the interaction problem by denying interaction. Interactionist dualists (the Map's position) accept the problem and propose a mechanism (quantum collapse).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether dissolving the interaction problem by denying interaction is a genuine solution or an evasion. Dual-aspect monists argue the problem doesn't arise if there's only one substance. Interactionist dualists argue that the phenomenology of mental causation (I decide to raise my arm, and it rises) demands a causal story.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The interaction problem remains the primary motivation for dual-aspect approaches, and the primary thing the Map must argue against when positioning itself.

### Causal Closure and Dual-Aspect Monism
- **Sides**: Physicalists argue causal closure rules out mental causation. Dual-aspect monists argue their view sidesteps the issue. The Map argues causal closure fails at quantum indeterminacies.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the physical world is causally closed. Benovsky notes that dual-aspect monism "can satisfy the desideratum concerning the irreducibility of qualia that motivates dualism" while avoiding mental causation problems. But critics (physicalists) note that "each time a neutral event is caused by mental properties, there is a conflict with the physical closure principle."
- **Current state**: The Map's position is distinctive here: it accepts that causal closure fails specifically at quantum indeterminacy, which is neither the dual-aspect move (deny interaction) nor the physicalist move (deny mental causation).

### What Are "Aspects"?
- **Sides**: Benovsky treats aspects as genuine ontological features. Le Bihan challenges whether "aspects" can do real explanatory work or are merely a relabeling of the problem.
- **Core disagreement**: Is "X has a mental aspect and a physical aspect" explanatorily richer than "X is both mental and physical"? If not, dual-aspect language is vacuous.
- **Current state**: This is a live concern for any bi-aspectual vocabulary, including the Map's. The Map should ensure its use of "structure" and "actuality" as two aspects carries genuine explanatory weight rather than merely restating the problem.

### Russellian Monism vs. Interactionist Dualism
- **Sides**: Russellian monists (Chalmers, Goff) claim consciousness is the intrinsic nature of matter. Interactionist dualists claim consciousness is ontologically distinct from matter and causally acts on it.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "physics describes only structure" implies that consciousness *is* the intrinsic nature of matter (Russellian monism) or that physics is genuinely incomplete and consciousness is a separate domain that interacts with physics at collapse (the Map's position). The Map's "partial but complete" physics—physics is structurally complete but ontologically incomplete—is close to but distinct from Russellian structuralism.
- **Current state**: This is the most important positioning question for the Map. The Russellian monist can ask: if physics describes all structure, what causal work is left for consciousness to do? The Map's answer: consciousness selects among physically undetermined outcomes at collapse. This goes beyond Russellian monism, which doesn't require a mechanism of selection.

## Gaps in Research

- **Fechner's psychophysics**: Fechner's dual-aspect position deserves more detail — he may be closer to the Map than Spinoza because he took phenomenal experience seriously as a domain
- **Nagel's teleological naturalism**: Mind and Cosmos (2012) argues physics is incomplete without consciousness; his position may align more closely with the Map than any dual-aspect monism does, but the research didn't find detailed treatment of whether Nagel endorses causal interaction
- **Bohm's implicate order**: Mentioned in connection with Pauli-Jung but not researched independently; Bohm's framework could be a closer precursor to the Map's view than Spinoza's
- **Empirical predictions**: None of the dual-aspect traditions generate testable predictions that distinguish them from each other or from the Map's position — this is a weakness shared by the entire family
- **Le Bihan's critique of "aspects"**: The concern that "aspect" vocabulary is explanatorily vacuous needs deeper engagement, especially as the Map adopts bi-aspectual language
