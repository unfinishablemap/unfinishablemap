## Executive Summary

Libertarianism about free will is not a single theory but a genus with three species, all sharing two commitments (determinism is false; free will is real) and dividing over what role indeterminism plays and where it sits. The standard taxonomy — canonised in the Stanford Encyclopedia's "Incompatibilist (Nondeterministic) Theories of Free Will" — sorts libertarians by which kind of indeterminism grounds freedom: **event-causal** (free actions are nondeterministically caused by agent-involving mental events — desires, beliefs, reasons — with no extra agent-as-substance cause), **agent-causal** (the agent, an irreducible substance, is a cause over and above any event), and **non-causal** (free actions are basic and uncaused). The event-causal wing (Kane, Ekstrom, Mele, Balaguer) is the naturalistically respectable mainstream but is the primary target of the **luck / rollback / disappearing-agent** objection: if only prior events cause the choice and the outcome was undetermined, nothing about the agent settles which way it went, so the difference looks like chance. Agent-causal views answer luck by making the agent the settler, but pay the price of the **intelligibility objection** to substance causation. This note's distinctive contribution to the Map is the **taxonomy itself** and specifically the **event-causal wing (Kane's self-forming actions), which currently has no home page** — complementing, not duplicating, the Map's existing agent-causation and luck-objection concept pages.

## The Core Division

All libertarians agree on two things: (1) determinism is false, and (2) some agents sometimes act freely and are morally responsible. They divide on a third question: **what is indeterminism's role, and what is the ultimate source of the free action?** The three answers map to the three species below. A useful frame: event-causal libertarianism tries to secure free will from indeterminism *without* invoking agent-causation; agent-causal libertarianism holds that indeterminism alone is not enough and an irreducible agent-cause is required; non-causal libertarianism holds that free action needs no positive causal story at all.

## Major Positions

### (a) Event-Causal Libertarianism
- **Proponents**: Robert Kane, Laura Ekstrom, Alfred Mele (as a defended-but-not-endorsed model), Mark Balaguer, Christopher Franklin.
- **Core claim**: A free action is nondeterministically caused by agent-involving mental *events* (the agent's having certain desires, beliefs, reasons, intentions). These accounts differ from event-causal *compatibilist* accounts only by adding that the causation is indeterministic. Crucially, the agent is **not** an additional cause over and above these events; "the agent's freely bringing about a choice is reducible to states and events involving the agent." This is the reductive, broadly naturalist wing.
- **Kane's self-forming actions (SFAs)**: Kane's model (developed in *The Significance of Free Will*, OUP 1996) locates the indeterminism at "self-forming actions" — the difficult, character-shaping moments when an agent is *torn* between competing visions of what to do or become (e.g., the moral course vs. ambition; present desire vs. long-term goal). In these **torn decisions** the agent makes competing *efforts of will simultaneously*, and it is undetermined which effort succeeds. Because the indeterminism is located inside the agent's own effort, whichever way it resolves, the outcome is something the agent willed and rationally endorsed — Kane calls this **plural voluntary control**: the agent is such that, whichever option is chosen, the choice will be rational, voluntary, and the agent's own doing (able to be endorsed either way). SFAs need not be frequent: most actions flow deterministically from a character that was itself forged by earlier SFAs, so responsibility for ordinary actions traces back to the undetermined self-forming ones.
- **Ekstrom's coherentist account**: Ekstrom (*Free Will: A Philosophical Study*, Westview Press, 2000) defends an indeterminist event-causal view built on a *coherence* theory of self-determined action, modelled on Keith Lehrer's coherence theory of knowledge. Preferences are "authorized" as the agent's own when they cohere appropriately with the agent's evaluative system; free action requires indeterministic causation among events and appeals to agent causation only in a reducible sense. She later refines this ("Toward a plausible event-causal indeterminist account of free will").
- **Balaguer's naturalist version**: Balaguer argues that whether we have libertarian free will reduces to an *open scientific question* about the causal histories of certain neural events — his "torn decisions" do not require Kane-style competing efforts of will. He offers a naturalistic defence of libertarianism.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the Map's principal *rival within libertarianism*. It agrees with the Map that determinism is false and free will real (compatible with Tenet 3 bidirectional interaction in spirit) but denies that any non-physical agent-cause is needed — indeterminism among physical/mental events is supposed to do the work. The Map holds this wing succumbs to the luck objection (below): if only events cause the choice, the undetermined outcome is not settled by the agent and looks like chance. This is a *framework-relative* disagreement, not a proof against event-causal views.

### (b) Agent-Causal Libertarianism
- **Proponents**: Roderick Chisholm, Timothy O'Connor, Randolph Clarke (as expositor/critic; his own verdict is guarded), E.J. Lowe (non-Cartesian version), and historically Thomas Reid and Richard Taylor.
- **Core claim**: A free action must be caused by the **agent as a substance** — not reducible to causation by prior events or mental states. The agent is an "uncaused cause" or *immanent* cause (Chisholm's term) of the decision. Chisholm's founding statement is "Human Freedom and the Self" (1964), distinguishing **transeunt** (event) causation from **immanent** (agent) causation.
- **O'Connor**: *Persons and Causes: The Metaphysics of Free Will* (OUP, 2000) gives the leading modern defence: free actions are caused by intentions which are produced not by prior states/events but by the agent herself. O'Connor engages Reid, Chisholm, and Taylor and grounds agent-causal power in an emergent capacity of persons.
- **E.J. Lowe's non-Cartesian version**: Lowe's Non-Cartesian Substance Dualism holds the self/person is distinct from the organic body but not necessarily separable (no commitment to disembodied existence). His action theory is a *hybrid* of volitionism and agent-causalism; he argues NCSD explains mental causation better than Cartesian dualism or physicalism and is consistent with neuroscience. Directly relevant to the Map because it is a substance-dualist agent-causal position that avoids classical Cartesian liabilities.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is where the Map locates its own view (see "The Map's Location" below). It aligns with Tenet 1 (dualism) and Tenet 3 (bidirectional interaction): the agent is a genuine cause, not a link in a purely physical chain.

### (c) Non-Causal Libertarianism
- **Proponents**: Carl Ginet, Hugh McCann, Stewart Goetz (and adjacent: Thomas Pink; E.J. Lowe is sometimes grouped here for the non-causal element of his hybrid).
- **Core claim**: Free actions need not be caused by anything — not by events, not by an agent-substance. Freedom is a *basic*, uncaused feature of action. Ginet grounds it in an "actish phenomenal quality" — the intrinsic way it seems to the agent that she is making the action happen. McCann stresses intrinsic intentionality and spontaneity as irreducible. Causal language applied to agency is, on the strongest versions, metaphorical.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Least congenial to the Map. By denying that the agent *causes* the action, non-causal libertarianism cannot support Tenet 3's bidirectional *causal* influence of consciousness on the physical. The Map needs the mental to make a genuine causal difference at the quantum interface; a non-causal account gives it no causal purchase.

### Hybrid note
Kane's mature view is sometimes described as a **hybrid**: agent-involving events (reasons, efforts) are necessary but insufficient, leaving a role for the agent. But Kane resists positing an agent-*substance* cause; he wants the "extra factor" without agent-causation, which is precisely what critics say cannot be had — the luck objection reappears at exactly this seam.

## Key Debates

### The Luck / Rollback / Disappearing-Agent Objection (bears heaviest on event-causal)
- **The objection**: If, right at the moment of choice, it was genuinely undetermined which way the agent would choose, then imagine "rolling back" the universe to that instant and replaying it. In some replays she chooses A, in others B, with the *same* prior events. What accounts for the difference between the replays? Nothing about the agent — the prior events were identical. So the difference is a matter of **luck / chance**, and luck does not ground responsibility. **Pereboom's "disappearing agent" formulation** (intended as a general objection to *all* event-causal libertarianism) sharpens this: with the causal role of the antecedent events already given and yet the outcome undetermined, *nothing settles* whether the decision occurs — so the agent does not settle it. The agent "disappears" from the moment of settling.
- **Why it bites event-causal hardest**: On the event-causal picture there is, by design, no cause beyond the events; so when the events leave the outcome open, there is nothing left to do the settling. The theory's own reductive commitment is what leaves the gap.
- **Kane's replies**: (i) *Indeterminate efforts*: Kane argues that in a genuinely indeterminate effort of will, "exact sameness" across rollback worlds is not well-defined, so the thought experiment illicitly assumes a determinacy the model denies. (ii) *Plural rationality*: whichever way the torn decision resolves, it issues from the agent's own effort and is rationally endorsable, so it is not mere chance but a willed self-formation. Critics (Clarke, Mele) reply that this shows the outcome is *voluntary* either way but not that the agent *controls which* — the "which" is still unsettled, and that residue is the luck.
- **Does agent-causation escape?**: The luck objection *redivivus* argues it does not fully: even if the agent-substance causes the decision, one can still ask why the agent caused A rather than B at that instant, and the answer again looks arbitrary. Agent-causalists reply that the agent's *exercising* its causal power just *is* the settling — the difference is not luck but the exercise of free will itself — though this needs independent defence.
- **Cross-references (by plain name)**: see the Map's existing concept page **luck-objection** (which develops the Map's consciousness-selection reply in full) and the **manipulation-argument** discussion; this note supplies the *taxonomic* context (which libertarian species the objection targets and why), not a re-derivation.

### The Intelligibility Objection (bears heaviest on agent-causal)
- **The objection**: Can causation *by a substance* (rather than by an event) even be made coherent? The orthodox view is that causation relates events; an enduring substance is not the right kind of relatum, and a "timeless" agent-cause seems mysterious (Taylor himself called it "strange"). Critics ask how an unchanging agent produces a change *at a particular time* without some triggering event — and if there is a triggering event, event-causation is back.
- **Replies**: Neo-Aristotelian *substance causation* as ontologically fundamental (Lowe 2008; O'Connor 2014; Jacobs & O'Connor 2013; Steward 2012) and *causal pluralism* (events cause things only as changes *in* substances, so substance-causation is not exceptional). The Map's interface model is a concrete proposal for *where* and *how* an agent-cause makes contact with the physical (the quantum interface), which is a distinctive move most agent-causalists leave metaphysically schematic.

### The Control / Reason-Explanation Objection (bears heaviest on non-causal)
- Non-causal views struggle to explain (i) how the agent exercises *active control* — an event with the right "actish" feel could be induced by direct brain stimulation, with no genuine agency — and (ii) how an action is done *for a reason*, since a reason-explanation looks causal. This is why non-causal libertarianism is the least populated wing.

## The Map's Location Within the Taxonomy (note for downstream — do not over-claim in the article)

- The Map is broadly **agent-causal / interface-based**. Its distinctive claim is that a conscious selection at the quantum interface is *the agent's own* settling of an otherwise-undetermined outcome — not a chance fluctuation. This is precisely the Map's answer to the luck objection: what event-causal libertarianism leaves unsettled (and therefore lucky), the Map has settled by the conscious agent via the interface (Tenet 2, minimal quantum interaction; Tenet 3, bidirectional interaction).
- **Event-causal libertarianism is therefore the RIVAL**: it is the attempt to get free will out of indeterminism *without* agent-causation. The Map's considered view is that this attempt succumbs to luck — the undetermined event is not settled by anyone — and that agent-causation (concretely, the interface) is needed to close the gap.
- **Calibration (important)**: The three-way taxonomy is *shared-ground* philosophy of action — it is not itself evidence for dualism, and the article must not frame it as such (this is the expand-topic-lead-overclaims-phenomenal-absence failure mode: do not let a neutral survey lead with a dualist conclusion). The Map's location within the taxonomy is *one position among rivals*; its claim that event-causal libertarianism fails on luck is a **framework-relative argument, not a proof**. The article should present all three species fairly (including the strongest event-causal replies) before locating the Map.
