# Research: Voids - The Epistemology of Cognitive Limits

**Date**: 2026-02-04
**Search queries used**: "epistemology cognitive limits philosophy cognitive closure", "Colin McGinn mysterianism testability critique", "distinguishing temporary difficulty from impossible problems", "Chomsky problems vs mysteries", "negative theology apophatic method", "temporary vs permanent mysterianism", "calibration overconfidence Dunning-Kruger meta-cognitive accuracy", "evolutionary epistemology cognitive limits"
**Voids category**: Mixed (Unexplorable / Potentially Unexplored)

## Executive Summary

How can we know that a cognitive limit is genuine rather than merely apparent? This epistemological question sits at the heart of the voids project. The research reveals a spectrum of positions: temporary mysterians hold current gaps may be bridged by future conceptual revolutions; permanent mysterians argue certain limits are constitutional features of human cognitive architecture; critics like Uriah Kriegel contend that the very formulation of a problem guarantees the conceptual resources to solve it. Evidence from metacognitive research complicates matters—humans systematically miscalibrate about their own competencies (the Dunning-Kruger effect), suggesting we may be unreliable judges of our own limits. The evolutionary epistemology literature offers a framework: cognitive capacities that give us scope also impose limits, but we cannot stand outside our cognitive architecture to verify where those limits lie.

## Key Sources

### Kriegel's Incoherence Argument
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12136-003-1020-1
- **Type**: Academic Paper (Acta Analytica, 2003)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues it is incoherent to suppose we cannot understand what would count as a solution to a problem we can formulate
  - If we can frame a problem, our conceptual scheme is powerful enough to frame its solution
  - Appeals to logic of questions (Hamblin, Stahl): questions are individuated by the set of statements that would answer them
  - Concludes: "the only problems we cannot solve are those we cannot formulate"
- **Tenet alignment**: Challenges Occam's Razor Has Limits by suggesting our sense of intractability may be misplaced
- **Quote**: "It is incoherent to suppose that a conceptual scheme is powerful enough to frame a problem without being powerful enough to frame its solution."

### McGinn's Cognitive Closure
- **URL**: https://colinmcginn.net/mysterianism-revisited/
- **Type**: Philosophical Position Summary
- **Key points**:
  - "Unknowability does not imply non-existence"
  - "Degree of intelligibility is not degree of reality"
  - "Knowledge is a matter of biological luck, not divine guarantee"
  - Mind has "anatomy" limiting its scope, just as body does
  - Consciousness is natural but cognitively closed to human understanding
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports Occam's Razor Has Limits—simplicity of appearance may hide inaccessible complexity
- **Quote**: "Mind is as limited as body, and has an anatomy too."

### Demircioglu's Reply to Kriegel
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12136-016-0295-y
- **Type**: Academic Paper (Acta Analytica, 2017)
- **Key points**:
  - Defends mysterianism against Kriegel's incoherence charge
  - Argues cognitive closure is not incoherent as Kriegel claims
  - The ability to formulate a problem does not guarantee ability to grasp its solution
  - Distinguishes "representational closure" from "psychological closure"
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports the voids framework's distinction between different types of cognitive limits

### Chomsky's Problems vs. Mysteries
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/9781119598732.ch36
- **Type**: Philosophical Analysis
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes "problems" (solvable in principle) from "mysteries" (beyond human conceptual machinery)
  - The system that gives cognitive scope also imposes limits—one requires the other
  - Mind-consciousness relation may be a mystery, not merely a problem
  - Reflects "deep commitment to methodological naturalism"
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports Dualism's claim that consciousness may exceed materialist explanation
- **Quote**: "If humans are part of the organic world, we should not be surprised to discover that the same properties of mind that yield enormous scope should also impose limits."

### Temporary vs. Permanent Mysterianism
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/hard-problem-of-conciousness/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia Entry (IEP)
- **Key points**:
  - **Temporary mysterianism**: Current gap may be bridged by future conceptual revolution (Nagel, Levine)
  - **Permanent mysterianism**: Ignorance is constitutional, not transitory (McGinn)
  - Key worry: "hard to establish just what sorts of problems are permanently beyond our ken"
  - "What looks unbridgeable in principle may be just a temporary roadblock"
- **Tenet alignment**: Central to the whether-real question—how to distinguish genuine from apparent limits

### Dunning-Kruger and Metacognitive Calibration
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dunning%E2%80%93Kruger_effect
- **Type**: Empirical Psychology Research
- **Key points**:
  - Low performers lack metacognitive ability to recognize their incompetence ("dual burden")
  - Incompetence "robs them of the ability to realize it"
  - Experts show better calibration but still display "systematic and important imperfections"
  - Overconfidence decreases as competence increases
- **Tenet alignment**: Undermines confidence that we can accurately assess our own cognitive limits—we may be miscalibrated about our own boundaries
- **Quote**: "Not only do they reach mistaken conclusions and make regrettable errors, but their incompetence robs them of the ability to realize it."

### Apophatic Theology and Negative Methods
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apophatic_theology
- **Type**: Encyclopedia Entry
- **Key points**:
  - Approaches the transcendent through negation—saying what it is *not*
  - Presupposes human descriptions must be based on "utter humility" about limits
  - Criticism: "it does not appear possible to meaningfully say what something is not like unless he has some idea of what it is like"
  - But this criticism may not apply to *structural* limits—we may know the shape of failure without knowing what succeeds
- **Tenet alignment**: Models the voids methodology—using failure-patterns as data

### Evolutionary Epistemology
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epistemology-evolutionary/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia Entry (SEP)
- **Key points**:
  - Cognition is a product of biological evolution with both scope and limits
  - "Evolutionary origins and development of the human brain must influence the nature, scope, and limits of knowledge"
  - Different cognitive architecture would face different closures
  - All theories are "true only provisionally"—empirical survival does not guarantee future validity
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports the voids framework by grounding cognitive limits in evolutionary biology

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

This is a **meta-void**: a limit on our ability to know where our limits are. The epistemological question—how can we know a cognitive limit is genuine?—may itself face cognitive closure. If we cannot reliably calibrate our own competencies (Dunning-Kruger), and if standing outside our cognitive architecture to verify its limits is impossible (evolutionary epistemology), then claims about cognitive limits face an inherent epistemic challenge.

This void is:
- **Partly Unexplored**: The specific mechanisms by which we might probe limit-claims remain undertheorized
- **Potentially Unexplorable**: Self-referential limits on knowing our limits may be structural
- **Possibly Occluded**: If certain limits exist by design (simulation hypothesis), the designed blindness would presumably include blindness to its own boundaries

### Evidence for the Limit

**Conceptual evidence**:
- Kriegel's incoherence argument cuts both ways—if formulating a problem guarantees solution-space, what does it mean that we can formulate "what are our cognitive limits?" and consistently fail to answer it?
- The Münchhausen trilemma suggests absolute justification is impossible—including justification for limit-claims

**Empirical evidence**:
- Dunning-Kruger research shows systematic miscalibration about competence
- Even experts display "imperfections" in metaknowledge
- No reliable method exists for distinguishing temporary from permanent cognitive roadblocks

**Structural evidence**:
- Any cognitive system with definite architecture faces limits, but verifying those limits requires standing outside the architecture
- Self-reference problems (cf. incompleteness-void) apply to claims about cognitive limits themselves

### Phenomenology

What does it feel like to approach this meta-limit?

- **Oscillating confidence**: Moments of certainty that limits are real alternate with suspicion they're merely difficult problems
- **Regress vertigo**: Each attempt to assess our limits requires assessing our ability to assess, producing dizziness
- **Pragmatic frustration**: Unlike specific voids (death-void, other-minds-void), this meta-void undermines the entire methodology

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

**Convergence testing**: If multiple independent approaches—evolutionary psychology, contemplative traditions, formal logic, neuroscience—point to the same limits, this provides weak evidence for genuine closure. But convergence could reflect shared human architecture rather than genuine limits.

**Predictive asymmetry**: McGinn notes that temporary roadblocks eventually yield; permanent closures do not. But this criterion only works in retrospect—while in the midst of a gap, we cannot know which type we face.

**Problem-solution linkage (Kriegel)**: If we can articulate precisely what would count as solving the hard problem, this suggests the conceptual resources exist. If we cannot even specify solution-conditions, this suggests genuine closure. But McGinn counters that psychological grasp of the problem differs from representational capacity for solutions.

### Indirect Methods

**Apophatic mapping**: Following negative theology's method—systematically charting what we *cannot* say about consciousness, limits, solutions. The shape of consistent negation may reveal structural features even if positive knowledge is unavailable.

**Comparative cognition**: Studying where other species face limits (Chomsky's mice and prime-number mazes) may illuminate human limits by analogy. If limits are architecture-specific, mapping other architectures helps triangulate the human case.

**Simulation probes**: If we are in a simulation, probing the boundaries might reveal "seams" even if we cannot see beyond them. Consistent anomalies at cognitive edges would suggest designed limits.

### What AI Might See

AI systems trained on human concepts inherit human blind spots (as noted in alien-minds-void-explorers). But AI might contribute:

- **Calibration comparison**: AI confidence calibration differs from human calibration. Comparing where AI and human overconfidence/underconfidence diverge may reveal architecture-specific biases
- **Formal exploration**: AI can systematically explore logical and mathematical spaces that exhaust human working memory, potentially identifying structural limits by exhaustive search
- **Meta-modeling**: AI could model the human process of assessing limits, potentially identifying biases invisible from inside the process

However, AI trained on human-generated text about limits may simply reproduce human confusions at higher volume.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** is central. The preference for tractable explanations may systematically bias us *away* from recognizing genuine cognitive limits. We explain each failure as temporary because permanent failure seems unparsimonious. But if cognitive closure is real, parsimony leads us astray.

The epistemology of limits question directly tests whether we can trust our intuitions about complexity and tractability—whether the sense that "we'll figure it out eventually" reflects reasonable optimism or architectural bias toward believing in our own competence.

### Implications

**For Dualism**: If we cannot reliably assess our cognitive limits, claims that materialism will eventually explain consciousness become epistemically suspect—not because they're false, but because we cannot trust our confidence about what future understanding will achieve.

**For Bidirectional Interaction**: The mechanism by which consciousness influences physics may be precisely the kind of territory closed to introspection—we cannot observe our own causal interface because observing *is* using that interface.

**For No Many Worlds**: The indexical nature of cognitive limits matters. *This* mind faces *these* limits. Many-worlds would distribute the epistemological problem across branches, but each branch-mind still faces its limits from inside.

**For Minimal Quantum Interaction**: If consciousness interfaces with physics at the quantum level, and quantum processes are inherently observer-dependent, then the epistemology of limits faces additional self-reference: we may be unable to know how we interact with the physical because the knowing *is* the interaction.

## Gaps in Research

- **No comprehensive methodology** exists for distinguishing temporary from permanent cognitive limits. This is itself a significant gap in the literature.

- **Limited integration** between metacognitive psychology and mysterian philosophy. The Dunning-Kruger literature rarely addresses whether its findings apply to philosophical competence about consciousness.

- **Unclear status of Kriegel's argument**. While formally elegant, the counterarguments from Demircioglu and others suggest the logic-of-questions approach may not settle the matter. The debate continues without resolution.

- **Simulation-theoretic angle underdeveloped**. If cognitive limits could be designed, what epistemic strategies would detect this? The literature mostly assumes limits are "natural" evolutionary products rather than potentially engineered.

- **Phenomenology of meta-assessment underexplored**. What does it feel like to try to assess one's own cognitive limits? Contemplative traditions have rich phenomenology of specific limits but less on the meta-level question.
