## Executive Summary

This note assembles the literature on a distinct asymmetry: negative valence weighs more heavily than positive valence in driving choice. Two convergent research programmes establish the pattern — Kahneman and Tversky's **loss aversion** (losses loom larger than equivalent gains; the prospect-theory value function is steeper below the reference point) and the **negativity bias** programme in social psychology (Baumeister et al.'s "bad is stronger than good"; Rozin and Royzman's negativity dominance). Both are robust and decades-deep, *but* loss aversion's universality is now genuinely contested (Gal, Rucker and Shavitt 2018 argue it is contingent, not general; Mrkva et al. 2020 and a 2024 *Journal of Economic Literature* meta-analysis report λ ≈ 1.96 with moderators; a 2025 re-meta-analysis disputes even that). For the Map, the load-bearing point is that **the mapping from this behavioural asymmetry to a felt-valence asymmetry is interpretive, not established** — prospect theory is a model of *decision* behaviour, and the negativity-bias mechanisms (asymmetric reward-prediction-error coding, more-elaborate processing of negative information) are exactly the kind of value-blind computational story the value-blind steelman needs. If felt value guides selection, the Map must specify a *weighting function* with a negative bias, and own that the asymmetry may live in the mechanism that reads value rather than in felt value itself.

## Key Sources

### Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk.
- **URL**: https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/1979/03/01/prospect-theory-analysis-decision-under-risk
- **Type**: Paper (Econometrica 47(2):263-291). Verified at the Econometric Society / RePEc record (v47y1979i2p263-91).
- **Key points**:
  - Introduces the value function defined over *gains and losses relative to a reference point*, not over final wealth.
  - The value function is concave for gains, convex for losses, and **steeper for losses than for gains** — the formal origin of "losses loom larger than gains."
  - Decision weights are nonlinear in probability; this is a *descriptive* alternative to expected-utility theory.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. It is a behavioural-economic model, silent on consciousness or felt value. The asymmetry is a property of *choice*, which is the gap the Map must not paper over.
- **Quote (paraphrase of the core claim)**: the disutility of a loss is greater than the utility of an equivalent gain; the value function is kinked at the reference point.

### Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1991). Loss Aversion in Riskless Choice: A Reference-Dependent Model.
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/106/4/1039/1873382
- **Type**: Paper (Quarterly Journal of Economics 106(4):1039-1061). Verified at Oxford Academic / RePEc.
- **Key points**:
  - Extends loss aversion from risky gambles to *riskless* choice — preferences over goods, not just money.
  - "The central assumption of the theory is that losses and disadvantages have greater impact on preferences than gains and advantages." (Abstract.)
  - Explains the endowment effect and status-quo bias via a kink in the reference-dependent value function.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Establishes that the asymmetry is *general across choice domains*, not an artefact of gambling.

### Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1992). Advances in Prospect Theory: Cumulative Representation of Uncertainty.
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00122574
- **Type**: Paper (Journal of Risk and Uncertainty 5(4):297-323; DOI 10.1007/BF00122574). Verified at Springer.
- **Key points**:
  - Cumulative prospect theory: cumulative (rank-dependent) rather than separable decision weights; **separate weighting functions for gains and for losses**.
  - Two organising principles: *diminishing sensitivity* and *loss aversion*.
  - Reports the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes (risk-averse for likely gains/unlikely losses; risk-seeking for unlikely gains/likely losses).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. The separate gain/loss weighting functions are the precise formal hook for "the selection law needs a weighting function, not just a sign."

### Baumeister, R. F., Bratslavsky, E., Finkenauer, C. & Vohs, K. D. (2001). Bad Is Stronger than Good.
- **URL**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1037/1089-2680.5.4.323
- **Type**: Review article (Review of General Psychology 5(4):323-370; DOI 10.1037/1089-2680.5.4.323). Verified at SAGE.
- **Key points**:
  - Surveys a broad domain set — everyday events, trauma, relationships, social networks, learning, impression formation — and finds bad consistently outweighs good of equal magnitude.
  - Negative information is processed more thoroughly; bad impressions and stereotypes form faster and resist disconfirmation more strongly.
  - Frames the asymmetry as plausibly adaptive: failing to register a threat is costlier than failing to register an opportunity.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-contestable. The "more thorough processing of negative information" mechanism is a *cognitive/computational* account of the asymmetry — it does not require, and arguably does not invoke, a felt-valence asymmetry. This is a value-blind reading the Map must engage.
- **Quote**: "bad is stronger than good, as a general principle across a broad range of psychological phenomena" (abstract summary).

### Rozin, P. & Royzman, E. B. (2001). Negativity Bias, Negativity Dominance, and Contagion.
- **URL**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1207/S15327957PSPR0504_2
- **Type**: Review/theory article (Personality and Social Psychology Review 5(4):296-320; DOI 10.1207/S15327957PSPR0504_2). Verified at SAGE.
- **Key points**:
  - Decomposes the negativity bias into four distinct sub-phenomena: **negative potency** (negatives are stronger), **steeper negative gradients** (negatives grow faster as the event nears), **negativity dominance** (in combination, the whole is more negative than the sum of parts), and **negative differentiation** (negatives are more varied, complex, and elaborated).
  - Argues the bias is general across animals and humans and operates at multiple levels.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. The four-way decomposition is useful for the Map because it shows the "asymmetry" is not one thing — different components could plausibly sit at different places (felt value vs. the reader of felt value).

### Contemporary debate — is loss aversion even real / general?
- **Gal, D., Rucker, D. D. & Shavitt, S. (2018). The Loss of Loss Aversion: Will It Loom Larger Than Its Gain?** Journal of Consumer Psychology 28(3):497-516; DOI 10.1002/jcpy.1047. Verified (three authors) at Wiley/SciRP. Argues current evidence does **not** support a general tendency for losses to loom larger; the effect is contingent on context, and canonical support (e.g., the endowment effect) admits alternative explanations.
- **Simonson, I. (2018). Bringing (Contingent) Loss Aversion Down to Earth — A Comment on Gal & Rucker.** Journal of Consumer Psychology 28(3); DOI 10.1002/jcpy.1046. Concedes loss aversion is less universal than assumed but defends it as real-with-moderators.
- **Mrkva, K., Johnson, E. J., Gächter, S. & Herrmann, A. (2020). Moderating Loss Aversion: Loss Aversion Has Moderators, But Reports of its Death Are Greatly Exaggerated.** Journal of Consumer Psychology 30(3):407-428; DOI 10.1002/jcpy.1156. Five samples, N≈17,720; everyone is loss-averse but domain knowledge/experience reduce it, supporting a preference-construction account.
- **Brown, A. L., Imai, T., Vieider, F. M. & Camerer, C. F. (2024). Meta-analysis of Empirical Estimates of Loss Aversion.** Journal of Economic Literature 62(2):485-516; DOI 10.1257/jel.20221698. 607 estimates from 150 articles (1992-2017); mean λ ≈ **1.955**, 95% interval [1.820, 2.102]; few moderators substantially shift it.
- **A 2025 re-meta-analysis** ("Loss aversion is not robust: A re-meta-analysis," Journal of Economic Psychology; ScienceDirect S0167487025000133) disputes the robustness of even the meta-analytic estimate — the debate is live as of 2025-2026.
- **Tenet alignment of the debate**: Neutral, but load-bearing for honesty. The Map cannot lean on loss aversion as a settled empirical pillar; the *behavioural* asymmetry is real-with-moderators at best, and "felt value is asymmetric" is a further, harder inference.

### Neural / mechanistic correlates (interpretive bridge — read with care)
- **Ito, T. A., Larsen, J. T., Smith, N. K. & Cacioppo, J. T. (1998). Negative Information Weighs More Heavily on the Brain: The Negativity Bias in Evaluative Categorizations.** Journal of Personality and Social Psychology 75(4):887-900. Verified. Larger late-positive ERP amplitudes to negative than to positive stimuli that were equally probable, extreme, and arousing — a *physiological* counterpart to the behavioural bias.
- **Asymmetric reward-prediction-error coding** (e.g., Diederen & Fletcher 2021, *The Neuroscientist*, DOI 10.1177/1073858420907591; Schultz 2016 on RPE coding; recent human-microdialysis and biorxiv work on asymmetric positive/negative RPE scaling). Distinct dopaminergic subpopulations and *asymmetric learning rates* for positive vs. negative prediction errors give the asymmetry a purely computational substrate.
- **Tenet alignment**: This is the crux. These mechanisms supply a complete *value-blind* story: the negative-vs-positive asymmetry can be a feature of the predictive/learning machinery (asymmetric RPE gains, more elaborate processing of threat) with no appeal to an asymmetry *in felt value*. The Map's value-sensitive reply must argue this case, not assume it.

## Major Positions

### Loss aversion / prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky)
- **Proponents**: Kahneman, Tversky; the broad behavioural-economics tradition.
- **Core claim**: Choices are reference-dependent and the value function is steeper for losses than gains; a loss-aversion coefficient λ > 1 scales losses relative to gains (meta-analytic mean ≈ 1.96).
- **Key arguments**: Endowment effect, status-quo bias, the fourfold risk pattern, reflection effects.
- **Relation to site perspective**: Supplies the *shape* the Map's selection-weighting function would need (a kink at the reference point, λ > 1 on the loss side). But prospect theory is silent on whether the kink reflects felt valence or a decision-computation. Adopting λ as a parameter of *felt* value is the interpretive leap the Map must flag, not smuggle.

### Negativity bias / "bad is stronger than good" (Baumeister, Rozin & Royzman, Cacioppo)
- **Proponents**: Baumeister, Bratslavsky, Finkenauer & Vohs; Rozin & Royzman; Cacioppo (evaluative space model); Ito et al.
- **Core claim**: Across domains, negative entities have greater potency, steeper gradients, dominance in combination, and greater differentiation than positive ones.
- **Key arguments**: Cross-domain breadth (everyday events to trauma to impression formation), ERP signatures, cross-species generality, adaptive rationale (asymmetric cost of missed threats vs. missed opportunities).
- **Relation to site perspective**: Reinforces that the asymmetry is general and likely evolved. The "more thorough processing" / "more neural networks" mechanisms are precisely value-blind: the asymmetry sits in *information processing*, not necessarily in *felt* value. Adaptive framing actually *helps* the value-blind reading — evolution can install asymmetric processing weights without installing asymmetric phenomenology.

### The contingency / deflationary position (Gal, Rucker & Shavitt)
- **Proponents**: Gal, Rucker & Shavitt 2018; partially Simonson 2018 (moderated version).
- **Core claim**: Loss aversion is not a general law; whether losses loom larger depends on context, and canonical evidence has alternative explanations.
- **Key arguments**: Failures to find the effect for small losses; endowment-effect confounds; publication-bias concerns.
- **Relation to site perspective**: A cautionary boundary. The Map should not build a selection law on a "law" that a serious minority regards as artefactual. The defensible framing treats the *behavioural* asymmetry as real-but-moderated (Mrkva et al. 2020; Brown et al. 2024), and treats the felt-valence asymmetry as a further open question.

## Key Debates

### Is the negative-valence weighting asymmetry a feature of felt value, or of the mechanism that reads it?
- **Sides**: Value-sensitive reading (the Map's lean): negative experiences are felt as *more* than positive ones of equal "size," so the weighting asymmetry is in the currency itself. Value-blind reading (the steelman): the asymmetry is a computational property — asymmetric RPE coding, more elaborate threat processing, reference-dependent decision weights — that needs no felt asymmetry.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the steeper loss-side value function describes phenomenology or decision-computation.
- **Current state**: Unresolved and, on present evidence, *underdetermined*. Prospect theory and the RPE literature are entirely compatible with the value-blind reading; the value-sensitive reading is an additional metaphysical commitment, not a datum.

### Is loss aversion robust enough to be a pillar at all?
- **Sides**: Robust-with-moderators (Mrkva et al. 2020; Brown et al. 2024, λ ≈ 1.96) vs. not-general / not-robust (Gal et al. 2018; the 2025 re-meta-analysis).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether a stable, general loss-aversion parameter exists once moderators and publication bias are accounted for.
- **Current state**: Live as of 2026. The Map should cite the asymmetry as real-but-contested, not settled.

## Relation to Site Perspective

This subject closes the **"negative valence and avoidance" open question** that [[valence-and-conscious-selection]] flags explicitly: "A complete selection law must specify whether positive and negative valence are symmetric in their selection influence or whether one is primary." The literature answers the *behavioural* half cleanly — selection is, at minimum with moderators, more sensitive to negative valence — and leaves the *phenomenal* half open.

The key framing for the Map's perspective:

- **Distinct axis from the siblings.** This is the *sign-weighting* axis, not the temporal/predictive axis of the [[affective-forecasting-gap]] (anticipated vs. experienced valence) and not the motivation-vs-pleasure axis of the [[wanting-liking-and-the-value-in-mechanism-fork]]. A complete value-sensitive selection law would need *all three* refinements: a predictive input (forecasting gap), a wanting/liking specification, and a **negatively-biased weighting function**.

- **The value-sensitive horn must specify a weighting function, not just a sign.** "Felt value guides selection" is incomplete if selection responds asymmetrically to equal-magnitude positive and negative value. The Map's selection law would need a loss-aversion-like λ > 1 on the negative side. The honest question this forces: is that λ a property of *felt value* (negatives are simply felt as larger) or of the *mechanism that reads felt value* (the controller weights negative signals more)?

- **The interpretive gap (flag, do not overclaim).** Prospect theory is a behavioural/decision model, and the negativity-bias mechanisms (asymmetric RPE coding, more-elaborate threat processing) are value-blind computational stories. **The inference from "choice is asymmetric" to "felt valence is asymmetric" is precisely what is contested** — it is not licensed by the data. This is the same evidential discipline the [[the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection]] enforces: the value-blind reading gets the asymmetry for free as a feature of the reading mechanism, with no felt asymmetry required. The Map's value-sensitive reply must engage that reading on its merits (e.g., via the pain-asymbolia and wanting/liking dissociations the parent fork already collates), not assume the felt-asymmetry conclusion.

- **Tenet touchpoints (for the eventual article).**
  - *Bidirectional Interaction*: an asymmetric weighting strengthens the interaction's specificity if the asymmetry is felt; it narrows the evaluative dimension's role if the asymmetry lives in a value-neutral reader.
  - *Minimal Quantum Interaction*: a value-blind asymmetric weighting is more minimal (the asymmetry is just a parameter of the existing reading channel); a felt-valence asymmetry adds structure to the currency.
  - *Occam's Razor Has Limits*: licenses declining the cheaper value-blind reading *only if argued* — the asymmetry's mechanistic availability is a real parsimony advantage for the rival.
  - *No Many Worlds*: under MWI the asymmetry has no selective referent; genuine collapse is what makes "negatives weigh more in selection" a live, answerable claim.

## Gaps in Research

- **No direct neuroscience of "asymmetric phenomenal valence as a selection input."** As with the affective-forecasting gap, the dopamine/RPE asymmetry literature concerns *behavioural/motivational* selection, not the Map's selection over quantum-underdetermined neural outcomes. The bridge is structural (asymmetric ≠ symmetric), not a ready-made mechanism. Flag, do not overclaim.
- **Whether the felt asymmetry exists at all.** Is the felt badness of losing $100 actually *greater* than the felt goodness of gaining $100, or is the asymmetry entirely in choice/processing? Introspective and psychophysical evidence here is thin and confounded — this is the empirical question the value-sensitive reading would most need.
- **Symmetry of the *anticipatory* vs. *experienced* asymmetry.** Loss aversion is largely a *decision*-time (anticipatory) phenomenon; whether experienced losses are felt asymmetrically (Kahneman's experienced utility) is less settled. This intersects with the affective-forecasting gap and would need disentangling.
- **Pain-asymbolia evidence for the sign-asymmetry specifically.** The parent fork uses asymbolia for the general value-sensitivity case; whether it speaks to the *asymmetry* (do asymbolics lose loss aversion specifically?) is unexplored and would be a strong discriminating test if it exists.
