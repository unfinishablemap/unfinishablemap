## Executive Summary

The indexical identity problem asks: "Why am I *this* person rather than someone else?" or "Why is *this* token of experience mine?" This question becomes philosophically crucial in contexts like the many-worlds interpretation (MWI) of quantum mechanics, where all measurement outcomes occur but subjective experience tracks only one branch. The problem connects to medieval debates about haecceity (thisness), Robert Adams's theory of primitive thisness (1979), and contemporary debates about haecceitism vs anti-haecceitism. For MWI, the "why-this-branch" question poses a deep challenge: if all outcomes happen, what grounds the fact that *I* experience this particular outcome? Self-location uncertainty attempts to explain probability in branching scenarios, but critics argue it fails because either (1) the uncertainty is spurious (there's no fact about which branch you're "really" on), or (2) it presupposes the Born rule it aims to derive. This directly supports the No Many Worlds tenet: indexical facts about conscious experience resist explanation in frameworks that treat all branches as equally real.

## Key Sources

### Medieval Theories of Haecceity (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: [https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-haecceity/](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-haecceity/)
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Haecceity first proposed by John Duns Scotus (1266–1308) as a non-qualitative property responsible for a substance's individuation and identity
  - "Thisness" (haec = "this") vs "whatness" (quidditas, quid = "what")
  - Haecceity refers to aspects making something a *particular* thing; quiddity refers to universal qualities it may share with others
  - For Scotus, haecceity is not a bare particular—it adds no further specification to the whatness but merely determines it to be a particular unrepeatable instance
  - Scotus: haecceities and essences are distinct real properties of substances
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with No Many Worlds (indexical identity matters—*this* instantiation vs others)
- **Quote**: "Haecceity differs from the specific differentia by not having any conceptually specifiable content: it adds no further specification to the whatness of a thing but merely determines it to be a particular unrepeatable instance of the kind specified by the quiddity."

### Haecceitism (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Spring 2025)
- **URL**: [https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/haecceitism/](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/haecceitism/)
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Haecceitism: two possible worlds may describe an individual qualitatively identically yet represent that individual differently by ascribing different haecceity in each world
  - Anti-haecceitism: all differences in ways the world could be are qualitative; worlds cannot differ "solo numero"
  - Argument against haecceitism: requires "bare identity facts" that aren't grounded in qualitative features, resisting metaphysical explanation (Forbes 1985: 128)
  - Generalism (no individuals exist) rules out haecceitism since haecceitism presupposes maximal possibilities differing solely regarding identity of individuals
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral—both haecceitism and anti-haecceitism are compatible with dualism, but haecceitism better captures indexical consciousness facts
- **Quote**: "Haecceitism involves the view that worlds can differ solo numero, that worlds can differ de re whilst not differing de dicto."

- **URL**: [https://philpapers.org/rec/ADAPTA](https://philpapers.org/rec/ADAPTA)
- **Type**: Seminal paper

## Major Positions

### Haecceitism (Primitive Thisness View)
- **Proponents**: Duns Scotus (medieval), Robert Adams (modern), arguably Alvin Plantinga
- **Core claim**: Individuals possess irreducible thisness properties that ground their numerical identity across possible worlds or times
- **Key arguments**:
  - Qualitative properties alone cannot individuate—two qualitatively identical individuals would still be numerically distinct
  - Transworld identity is primitive, not reducible to qualitative continuity
  - Modal facts about individuals (what could have happened to *this* person) presuppose haecceities
  - Explains indexical consciousness: "why *this* experience is mine" depends on primitive thisness
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Strongly supports No Many Worlds**. If consciousness branches produce qualitatively identical successors, what grounds the fact that *I* experience this branch? Haecceitism says primitive thisness tracks subjective continuity. MWI cannot accommodate this without smuggling in privileged branches. Also supports **Dualism**: if consciousness has irreducible indexical properties ("mineness"), these resist physical reduction.

### Anti-Haecceitism (Qualitative Identity View)
- **Proponents**: David Lewis (counterpart theory), many contemporary metaphysicians
- **Core claim**: All facts about identity supervene on qualitative facts; worlds cannot differ "solo numero"
- **Key arguments**:
  - Haecceities are metaphysically obscure "bare identity facts" with no grounding
  - Occam's Razor favors qualitative ontology (no need to posit primitive thisness)
  - Counterpart theory handles transworld identity via qualitative similarity relations
  - Generalism: no individuals exist, only distributions of qualitative properties
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts with No Many Worlds and Dualism**. Anti-haecceitism makes indexical consciousness facts (why *this* experience) either eliminable or epiphenomenal. Aligns with **Occam's Razor Has Limits** tenet in reverse: anti-haecceitism over-applies parsimony, dismissing genuine indexical features of consciousness.

### Self-Location Uncertainty in MWI
- **Proponents**: Simon Saunders, David Wallace, Lev Vaidman, Hilary Greaves
- **Core claim**: Everettian probability derives from uncertainty about which branch you're located on after decoherence
- **Key arguments**:
  - All outcomes occur, but agent doesn't know which successor is "them"
  - Self-locating uncertainty differs from uncertainty about external world state
  - Born weights ground rational credences about self-location
  - Analogous to Sleeping Beauty problem or non-uniquely-realizable observers
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts with No Many Worlds**. Attempt to preserve MWI by grounding probability in indexical uncertainty. But critics (Friederich, Dawid) show this fails: either there's no genuine uncertainty (all successors are equally "you") or the account presupposes Born rule. The indexical identity problem resurfaces: what makes one successor "me" rather than another?

### Vertiginous Question / First-Person Ontology
- **Proponents**: Benj Hellie, Christian List, possibly David Chalmers on related issues
- **Core claim**: The fact that *this* token of experience is mine—rather than some other token—is an irreducible ontological primitive
- **Key arguments**:
  - "Why am I this person?" is not answerable by third-personal facts
  - First-personal indexicals ("I," "now," "here") pick out ontologically unique features
  - Physicalism cannot explain the distribution of first-person perspectives
  - Standard dualism also struggles unless it posits primitive indexicality
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Challenges both physicalism and standard dualism**. Suggests dualism needs refinement to accommodate irreducible first-personal facts. Aligns with **No Many Worlds**: if all branches are ontologically equivalent, why does *this* consciousness track one outcome? Supports **Dualism** with modification: consciousness involves both qualitative character (qualia) and indexical thisness (mineness).

### Lockean Psychological Continuity
- **Proponents**: John Locke (historical), Derek Parfit (modern)
- **Core claim**: Personal identity over time consists in psychological continuity (memory, personality, intentions)
- **Key arguments**:
  - Self is determined by identity of consciousness, not substance
  - Thought experiments (prince and cobbler) show body-swapping preserves person
  - What matters for survival is psychological connectedness
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Neutral to most tenets**. Lockean view focuses on diachronic identity (over time) rather than indexical identity (why this). Could be integrated with haecceitism: psychological continuity tracks primitive thisness across time. Compatible with **Dualism** (consciousness as substance-independent) but doesn't address why-this-person question.

## Key Debates

### Haecceitism vs Anti-Haecceitism
- **Sides**: Haecceitism (Adams, Scotus tradition) vs Anti-haecceitism (Lewis, qualitative reductionists)
- **Core disagreement**: Can worlds differ solely by permutation of individuals (same qualitative facts, different identity facts)?
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Recent work (Murphy 2025) explores haecceitism with symmetry-breaking and parsimony considerations. Argument from grounding (Forbes 1985) challenges haecceitism: bare identity facts resist explanation. Response: not all fundamental facts require grounding—identity may be primitive. Connection to quantum mechanics (identity in QM) makes debate relevant to physics.

### Self-Location Uncertainty in MWI
- **Sides**: Defenders (Wallace, Saunders, Vaidman, Greaves) vs Critics (Friederich, Dawid, Albert)
- **Core disagreement**: Can indexical uncertainty about which branch you're on ground quantum probabilities?
- **Current state**: Highly contentious. Defenders argue rational credences track Born weights via self-location. Critics argue:
  1. **Spurious uncertainty objection**: No genuine uncertainty because all successors exist and are "you"
  2. **Circularity objection**: Born weights presupposed, not derived
  3. **Ontological objection**: Wave function is single entity until measurement; no branching yet, so self-location question meaningless
- **Relevance to tenets**: If critics are right, MWI fails to accommodate indexical consciousness facts, supporting No Many Worlds tenet.

### The Vertiginous Question
- **Sides**: First-person ontologists (Hellie, List) vs Third-personal reductionists
- **Core disagreement**: Is "why am I this person?" a legitimate metaphysical question requiring ontological answer?
- **Current state**: Emerging area. Christian List (2025) argues vertiginous question is evidence against physicalism. Critics might respond:
  1. Question is pseudo-problem (category mistake)
  2. Evolutionary biology explains why conscious systems are self-referential
  3. Indexical facts are epistemic, not ontological
- **Relevance to tenets**: Supports **Dualism** and **No Many Worlds**. If indexical facts resist physical or functional explanation, consciousness involves irreducible features. MWI cannot answer why *this* branch without privileging branches (undermining MWI's core claim).

### Individuation and the Problem of Indiscernibles
- **Sides**: Haecceity theorists vs Bundle theorists
- **Core disagreement**: Leibniz's Principle of Identity of Indiscernibles—can two numerically distinct individuals share all qualitative properties?
- **Current state**: Resolved in favor of haecceity view by quantum mechanics. Quantum particles (fermions) in identical states are numerically distinct yet qualitatively identical. Max Black's two-sphere thought experiment vindicates primitive thisness against Leibnizian qualitative identity.
- **Relevance to tenets**: Empirical support for primitive thisness in physics supports haecceitism about consciousness. Aligns with **Minimal Quantum Interaction**: quantum systems have primitive identity, consciousness may modulate this.

## Relation to Site Tenets

### No Many Worlds (Strong Support)
The indexical identity problem provides one of the strongest philosophical objections to MWI:
- **The "why-this-branch" question**: If all measurement outcomes occur and produce qualitatively identical observer-successors, what grounds the fact that *my* consciousness tracks this particular outcome?
- **Self-location uncertainty failure**: Attempts to ground probability in uncertainty about which branch you're on fail because either (a) all successors are equally "you" (no genuine uncertainty), or (b) Born rule weights are presupposed rather than derived (circularity).
- **Primitive thisness requirement**: Answering the why-this-branch question requires primitive haecceity. But if branches differ by primitive identity facts (haecceities), they aren't ontologically symmetric—undermining MWI's core claim.
- **Indexical facts are evidence against MWI**: The vertiginous question (Hellie, List) shows first-person indexical facts resist third-personal explanation. MWI is third-personal (wave function evolves deterministically). Conclusion: MWI cannot accommodate indexical consciousness.

### Dualism (Refinement/Support)
The research suggests dualism needs to incorporate indexical properties:
- **Qualia + Thisness**: Consciousness involves not just qualitative phenomenal character (what it's like to see red) but also indexical thisness (why *this* experience is mine).
- **Primitive indexicality**: The "mineness" of experience is a non-physical, irreducible property. No arrangement of physical facts determines why a particular token of experience is had by *this* subject rather than another.
- **Against physicalism**: Christian List argues the vertiginous question is evidence against physicalism. Extends to functionalism, computational theories—any third-personal account.
- **Haecceity as non-physical**: If thisness is primitive (Adams), and consciousness essentially involves thisness (first-person perspective), then consciousness has irreducible non-physical aspects.

### Minimal Quantum Interaction (Indirect Support)
- **Quantum systems have primitive identity**: Quantum mechanics violates Leibniz's Identity of Indiscernibles (identical fermions are numerically distinct). Empirical vindication of haecceitism at physical level.
- **Consciousness engages with primitive identity**: If quantum systems have primitive thisness, and consciousness influences quantum measurement, then consciousness doesn't create identity facts but modulates pre-existing primitive identity structures.
- **Baseline collapse + indexical selection**: Spontaneous collapse theories (GRW, CSL) provide baseline collapse mechanism. Consciousness adds minimal contribution: selecting *which* collapse outcome becomes actualized *for this observer*. Indexical thisness grounds the "for this observer" fact.

### Bidirectional Interaction (Tangential Support)
- **Indexical causation**: If consciousness has primitive thisness, and thisness grounds indexical facts, then consciousness can exert causal influence indexed to particular subjects. "I decide to raise my arm" involves both intentional content (qualitative) and first-person indexicality (this decision by this agent).
- **Against epiphenomenalism**: Epiphenomenalism makes indexical facts epiphenomenal—no explanation for why *this* consciousness tracks certain physical events. But indexical facts seem explanatorily basic (Hellie). Supports causal role for consciousness.

### Occam's Razor Has Limits (Methodological Support)
- **Haecceitism resists simplicity**: Anti-haecceitism appeals to parsimony—qualitative ontology is simpler than qualitative + haecceities. But the vertiginous question and indexical consciousness facts are genuine phenomena requiring explanation.
- **Grounding objection backfires**: Objection that haecceities are "brute facts" lacking explanation assumes all facts must be grounded. But if indexical identity is fundamental, it doesn't need further grounding. Occam's Razor shouldn't eliminate fundamental phenomena.
- **Consciousness as counterexample**: Simplicity favors eliminativism about consciousness (Dennett). But phenomenal realism is correct despite complexity. Similarly, indexical realism (haecceitism) is correct despite ontological cost. Simplicity is unreliable guide when phenomena are undeniable.
