## Executive Summary

Thermoception (felt warmth and cold, and the affective comfort/discomfort gradient of temperature) was flagged by `optimistic-2026-06-21c` and the tactile article as the last genuine structural gap in the modality survey, alongside vestibular (now written). This note researches the topic **assess-first**, because thermoception overlaps the existing tactile and interoceptive treatments more than vestibular did: temperature already appears in the tactile article as a submodality bound into the felt object, and "thermal" is one of the named convergent channels of the interoceptive article ("cardiac, respiratory, gastric, thermal, nociceptive"), where "the ache of cold" already serves as an example of valenced-and-owned visceral feeling.

**Verdict: WRITE (standalone, target `topics/`), with a tightly-scoped distinctive angle.** Two candidate distinctives were tested against the literature and both survive verification:

1. **Homeostatically-indexed valence.** Temperature is the modality where affective valence is least separable from the sensation *and* is indexed to body state: the *same* thermal stimulus flips valence with the body's thermoregulatory need (Craig's "a glass of cool water is pleasant if you are overheated but aversive if you are chilled"). This is a stronger and *different* claim than the tactile article's constitutive-valence point (C-tactile affective touch, pain): affective touch carries a fixed hedonic tone; thermal valence is *state-dependent*, computed against a homeostatic set-point. The thermal grill illusion (Craig & Bushnell 1994) is the sharp datum that thermal and pain integrate centrally.
2. **The exteroception/interoception boundary case.** Skin thermoreceptors report *both* ambient/environmental temperature (exteroceptive) *and* contribute to core homeostatic/thermoregulatory state (interoceptive). Crucianelli & Ehrsson (2023) build the case that skin is the dual organ and that thermoception is exactly where the exteroceptive/interoceptive line blurs. This makes thermoception the *boundary* case for the apex's exteroceptive-vs-interoceptive asymmetry — neither a clean distance sense nor a clean visceral one.

Neither distinctive is fully carried by the existing articles. The tactile article treats temperature as a bound feature and covers fixed-valence affective touch; the interoceptive article names thermal as a channel but does not develop state-dependent valence or the skin-as-dual-organ boundary point. The standalone load-bearing content is the *homeostatic indexing of thermal valence* and the *boundary status* of the modality — both of which sharpen, rather than repeat, the apex.

Per evidential-status-discipline, every account below is a **physicalist rival to out-accommodate**, not interface evidence: TRP-channel transduction (Julius/Patapoutian Nobel), Craig's homeostatic-emotion reconceptualization, and predictive/active-inference thermoregulation all explain the data with no non-physical remainder.

## Key Sources

### Craig, A. D. (2002) — "How do you feel? Interoception: the sense of the physiological condition of the body"
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn894 (publisher page gated; metadata confirmed via Blomqvist 2023 review and prior publisher-verification in `interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface`)
- **Citation**: *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 3(8), 655–666.
- **Type**: Review
- **Key points**:
  - Reconceptualizes temperature from a discriminative cutaneous (exteroceptive) sense into an interoceptive/homeostatic signal: temperature is a "feeling from the body" carried by lamina I spinothalamic neurons to the posterior insula, re-represented to the anterior insula.
  - Thermal sensation generates an affective state of thermal comfort/discomfort whose valence depends on thermoregulatory need.
  - Already cited and publisher-verified in the interoceptive article (channel-convergence onto insula). This note reuses the *temperature-specific* portion.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. A convergent insular homeostatic integrator is what a physicalist expects; cited as the framing rival, not as support.
- **Note**: Already in the corpus's verified-citation pool. Safe to reuse.

### Craig, A. D. & Bushnell, M. C. (1994) — "The Thermal Grill Illusion: Unmasking the Burn of Cold Pain"
- **URL**: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.8023144 (publisher gated, HTTP 403); metadata confirmed via IASP Pain Research Forum page.
- **Citation**: *Science*, 265(5169), 252–255. PMID 8023144. **Volume is 265, not 256** (Blomqvist 2023 review prints "256" — that is a typo; IASP and the DOI confirm 265).
- **Type**: Primary research (electrophysiology + psychophysics)
- **Key points**:
  - Interlaced warm and cool bars (neither painful alone) produce a strong, often painful *heat* sensation (Thunberg's 1896 illusion).
  - Central-disinhibition / "unmasking" model: warm stimuli suppress cool-sensitive lamina I neural activity, releasing inhibition on polymodal nociceptors; predicts (and psychophysics confirms) a quantitative correspondence between grill-evoked pain and cold-evoked pain.
  - Demonstrates that thermal and pain channels integrate centrally — the affective/nociceptive character of cold is not read off the periphery.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. A clean physicalist integration story; the *datum* (thermal sensation is centrally constructed, not a transparent readout of skin temperature) is what the interface reading accommodates.
- **Quote**: "In Thunberg's thermal grill illusion, first demonstrated in 1896, a sensation of strong, often painful heat is elicited by touching interlaced warm and cool bars to the skin."

### Blomqvist, A. (2023) — "Pain and temperature, and human awareness: The legacy of Bud Craig"
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10732649/ (open access, PMC — publisher of record)
- **Citation**: *Temperature (Austin)*, 10(4), 395–401. DOI: 10.1080/23328940.2023.2265946
- **Type**: Review / obituary-retrospective
- **Key points**:
  - States Craig's core reframe directly: "while we normally regard temperature sensation as a discriminative cutaneous sensory capacity, the valence of the feeling depends on the body's thermoregulatory needs."
  - The state-dependent valence example: "a glass of cool water is pleasant if you are overheated but aversive if you are chilled."
  - Confirms the lamina I → VMpo thalamus → insular cortex pathway and the anterior-insula integration role.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. The most quotable secondary source for the *homeostatic-indexing-of-valence* distinctive (a). Open-access, fully verified.
- **Use**: Primary support for distinctive (a). Cite the Craig primary works *via* this review for the valence claims, with the primaries themselves for the pathway and the illusion.

### Crucianelli, L. & Ehrsson, H. H. (2023) — "The Role of the Skin in Interoception: A Neglected Organ?"
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9902974/ (open access, PMC)
- **Citation**: *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 18(1), 224–238. DOI: 10.1177/17456916221094509 (published online 2022-08-15; journal issue 2023).
- **Type**: Review / position
- **Key points**:
  - "the skin... is a sensory organ extensively and directly exposed not only to the inside of the body but also to the external environment" — the dual-organ thesis.
  - "interoceptive responses to thermal stimuli can refer both to internally generated stimuli but also in response to the application of thermosensory stimuli on the skin."
  - "one of the reasons why skin signals might have been overlooked so far is the fact that they provide both interoceptive and exteroceptive sensory information."
  - Argues thermoception is precisely where the exteroception/interoception distinction blurs, and that careful experiments are needed to isolate the interoceptive component.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Primary support for distinctive (b) — the boundary-case status. The boundary is a *structural* fact the apex's asymmetry must locate, not interface evidence.

### Vriens, Owsianik, Voets, Droogmans & Nilius / Julius & Patapoutian — TRP-channel thermosensory transduction
- **URLs**:
  - The Role of TRP Channels in Thermosensation (NCBI Bookshelf): https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK5244/
  - PubMed 21204494: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21204494/
  - TRP ion channels in thermosensation, thermoregulation and metabolism (PMC): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4843888/
- **Type**: Reviews / book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - TRPV1: capsaicin/noxious-heat receptor, threshold ~43 °C (≥42 °C noxious).
  - TRPM8: menthol/cold receptor, innocuous cooling (<26–28 °C) to noxious cold (<16 °C); TRPM8-null mice lose cold sensing up to ~15 °C and show cold-avoidance deficits.
  - TRPV2/V3/V4 as warm/heat thermosensors; TRPA1 in noxious cold.
  - David Julius shared the 2021 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the molecular identification of temperature receptors (TRPV1, TRPM8); Ardem Patapoutian shared it primarily for Piezo mechanotransduction (touch) but also contributed to TRPM8/TRPA1.
- **Tenet alignment**: The lead physicalist **rival to out-accommodate**. Molecular transduction is a complete, well-characterized peripheral mechanism. Cite a *specific* TRP review (recommend the Nobel-anchored summary + one named review) at publisher of record before article-write; the book/PubMed metadata above is verified but pick a single citable primary review for the article.

### Predictive / active-inference thermoregulation (Barrett & Simmons; allostatic-interoception programme)
- **URLs**:
  - Interoceptive predictions in the brain (Barrett & Simmons 2015, PMC): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4731102/
  - Simulating homeostatic/allostatic/goal-directed interoceptive control using Active Inference (bioRxiv): https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2021.02.16.431365
- **Type**: Review (Barrett & Simmons, already in corpus) + preprint

- **Tenet alignment**: The second physicalist **rival**. Barrett & Simmons (2015) is already publisher-verified in the interoceptive article — reuse it. Predicts the affective, anticipatory, set-point character of thermal feeling with no non-physical remainder.

## Major Positions

### Craig's homeostatic-emotion / interoceptive account of temperature
- **Proponents**: A. D. (Bud) Craig; downstream, Blomqvist's retrospective.
- **Core claim**: Temperature is not an exteroceptive perception of an object's property but an interoceptive *feeling* about the body's thermoregulatory condition; its felt valence (comfort/discomfort) is indexed to homeostatic need.
- **Key arguments**: Lamina I spinothalamic pathway shared with pain; insular convergence; state-dependence of thermal pleasantness; thermal grill illusion as central integration of thermal and nociceptive channels.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The strongest *framing* rival. It already concedes everything the Map would want to say about thermal valence being constitutive — and explains it physically. The Map's residue is the usual one: it explains why thermal predictions become available to the system, not why there is a subject the comfort/discomfort is *felt by*. This is an argumentative residue (the ownership/explanatory-gap point), not a place the thermal data favour the interface.

### TRP-channel molecular transduction
- **Proponents**: David Julius, Ardem Patapoutian (2021 Nobel); Nilius, Voets, Vriens.
- **Core claim**: Thermal sensation begins in temperature-gated ion channels (TRPV1 heat, TRPM8 cold, TRPV3/V4 warm, TRPA1 noxious cold) with discrete activation thresholds.
- **Key arguments**: Knockout phenotypes (TRPM8-null mice lose cold sensing), defined thresholds matched to psychophysical categories.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The lead peripheral physicalist rival. The Map *adopts* this as mechanism; it forces nothing dualist. Its role in the article is to establish how complete the physical account of transduction is, so the interface claim is correctly located *above* transduction (at the felt/owned level), not at the receptor.

### Predictive / active-inference thermoregulation
- **Proponents**: Barrett & Simmons; Seth; the allostatic-interoception programme.
- **Core claim**: Thermal feeling is anticipatory inference; visceromotor predictions act as homeostatic set-points minimized by sweating/shivering.
- **Key arguments**: Anticipatory regulation precedes sensed dysregulation; thermoceptive prediction errors localize to anterior insula.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The second physicalist rival to out-accommodate; structurally identical to the interoceptive article's treatment of interoceptive inference. The interface reading accommodates a predictive thermal loop without strain — but so does the physicalist, and the framework does not discriminate on this evidence.

## Key Debates

### Is thermoception exteroceptive or interoceptive?
- **Sides**: Classical somatosensory view (a cutaneous discriminative sense, alongside touch) vs. Craig/Crucianelli & Ehrsson (an interoceptive/homeostatic sense, or a dual-mode boundary sense).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether temperature reports a property of the *world* or a condition of the *body* — and whether the skin is best modelled as an outward- or inward-facing organ.
- **Current state**: Live and converging on "both." Crucianelli & Ehrsson explicitly frame thermoception as the case that resists the clean dichotomy. This *is* the distinctive (b) hook: thermoception is the apex's boundary case.

### Does thermal sensation have neutral content with valence appended, or is valence constitutive and state-indexed?
- **Sides**: A neutral-temperature-percept-plus-hedonic-judgement view vs. Craig's constitutive, homeostatically-indexed valence.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the same skin temperature can be neutrally perceived independent of body state.
- **Current state**: The cool-water example and the thermal grill weigh toward constitutive, state-dependent valence. This is distinctive (a) and the cleanest contrast with the tactile article's *fixed*-valence affective touch.

## Potential Article Angles

The recommended article is the thermal companion in the modality-interface series, **scoped tightly to its two non-redundant distinctives** so it sharpens the apex rather than repeating tactile/interoceptive.

1. **Recommended — "the modality where valence is homeostatically indexed, and the modality that straddles the extero/intero boundary."** Lead with state-dependent thermal valence (Craig, Blomqvist; the cool-water case; the thermal grill) as a *different* constitutive-valence datum from affective touch (fixed tone) — bearing on `evaluative-phenomenal-character`. Then develop the boundary status (Crucianelli & Ehrsson): skin thermoreceptors report both ambient and homeostatic state, making thermoception the case that tests whether the apex's exteroceptive/interoceptive asymmetry is a clean dichotomy or a gradient. Out-accommodate the TRP-transduction, Craig-homeostatic-emotion, and predictive-thermoregulation rivals. Calibrate as accommodation-not-proof throughout, exactly as tactile/vestibular/interoceptive do.
2. **Alternative (rejected) — fold-in into tactile or interoceptive.** A "Temperature" section in the tactile article (extending the affective-touch discussion) or in the interoceptive article (extending the channel list) would capture *some* of distinctive (a) but neither host article has room for the boundary argument (b) without distorting its own thesis. The boundary argument is genuinely apex-facing and needs the standalone frame. If a future editor judges distinctive (a) alone too thin, the minimal fold-in target is the interoceptive article's mind-side section; but on the evidence here, (a)+(b) together clear the standalone bar that vestibular cleared.
