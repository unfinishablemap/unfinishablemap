## Executive Summary

The metaphysics of composition—the question of when parts form wholes—intersects with consciousness in multiple philosophically fertile ways. The Special Composition Question (SCQ), introduced by van Inwagen, asks under what conditions material objects compose a further object. The most prominent answers (universalism, nihilism, and restricted composition) each have distinct implications for the ontology of conscious beings. Strikingly, several leading answers to the SCQ—van Inwagen's organicism, Merricks' eliminativism about non-conscious composites, and McQueen & Tsuchiya's IIT-based restriction—place consciousness at the centre of composition itself, making it the criterion for when genuine wholes exist. This convergence supports the Map's dualist tenets by showing that consciousness resists reduction to mereological parts and may be the very thing that distinguishes genuine composite objects from mere aggregates.

- **URL**: https://books.google.com/books/about/Material_Beings.html?id=mE38_qz5BUAC
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes organicism: composition occurs if and only if the activity of simples constitutes a life
  - Only living organisms are genuine composite objects; tables, rocks, and stars do not strictly exist
  - Consciousness enters indirectly—organisms (the only composites) include all conscious beings
- **Tenet alignment**: Partially aligns with Tenet 1—grants consciousness a special ontological status within the physical world, though van Inwagen himself is a materialist about minds
- **Quote**: "For any xs, there is a y composed of those xs if and only if the collective activity of those xs constitutes a life"

### Merricks, *Objects and Persons* (2001)
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/book/9701
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Only conscious organisms exist as composite objects; all other macroscopic objects are eliminated
  - Eliminates composites on causal grounds—inanimate composites are causally redundant given their parts
  - Conscious beings escape elimination because consciousness does not globally supervene on microscopic physical properties—conscious organisms have causal powers their parts lack
  - The non-supervenience of consciousness is the criterion distinguishing genuine composites from eliminable aggregates
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism)—consciousness is irreducible and grants unique ontological status; aligns with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)—conscious beings have causal powers over and above their parts
- **Quote**: "Consciousness, instantiated by human persons, does not even globally supervene on microscopic physical properties"

### McQueen & Tsuchiya, "When Do Parts Form Wholes?" (2023)
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/nc/article/2023/1/niad013/7189628
- **Type**: Journal article (Neuroscience of Consciousness)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes Integrated Information Theory (IIT) as the restriction on mereological composition
  - Composition occurs when integrated information (Φ) is maximised—the whole constrains past/future states more than its parts do
  - Avoids the vagueness and counterexample problems plaguing other restricted composition proposals
  - Links consciousness directly to the metaphysics of composition: the same measure (Φ) that purportedly measures consciousness also determines when genuine wholes exist
- **Tenet alignment**: Mixed—IIT is a physicalist theory that identifies consciousness with integrated information, conflicting with Tenet 1. However, the move of making consciousness the criterion for composition aligns with the Map's emphasis on consciousness as ontologically fundamental
- **Quote**: "Composition happens when integrated information is maximized"

### Chalmers, "The Combination Problem for Panpsychism" (2017)
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/combination.pdf
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Systematises the combination problem into three dimensions: subject combination, quality combination, and structural combination
  - The subject-summing problem is the hardest: how do distinct conscious subjects compose a single unified subject?
  - Explores phenomenal bonding, co-consciousness, and other proposed solutions, finding all inadequate
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1 and the Map's thesis of phenomenal non-compositionality—consciousness resists composition
- **Quote**: "Little conscious subjects of experience with their micro-experiences coming together to form a big conscious subject with its own experiences... this is very difficult to make sense of"

### Panpsychism (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/panpsychism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - The combination problem is "generally agreed, both by its proponents and opponents" to be panpsychism's hardest problem
  - Surveys proposed solutions: phenomenal bonding (Goff, Miller), co-consciousness, continuist panpsychism (Roelofs)
  - The subject-summing problem is the paradigmatic form of the combination problem
- **Tenet alignment**: The Map rejects panpsychism but leverages the combination problem as evidence for phenomenal non-compositionality and dualism

### Hasker, *Emergent Dualism*
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/HASTCF-2
- **Type**: Book chapter / philosophical position
- **Key points**:
  - The soul is an emergent substance generated by brain functioning but immaterial and possessing its own causal powers
  - Unity-of-consciousness argument: materialism implies the "individual" is a series of distinct individuals due to continual physical change
  - The emergent soul is spatially located and not simple in the Cartesian sense, but has no material parts
  - Distinct from substance dualism: the soul depends on the body for its generation
- **Tenet alignment**: Partially aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism) and Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)—the soul is immaterial and causally efficacious. Tension with the Map's openness between substance and property dualism—Hasker commits to a specific emergent substance view

### Cotnoir & Baxter, *Composition as Identity* (2014)
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/composition-as-identity-9780199669615
- **Type**: Edited volume
- **Key points**:
  - Explores the thesis that a whole is nothing over and above its parts—composition is identity
  - If composition is identity, then the whole has no properties that are not ultimately properties of the parts
  - This creates severe problems for consciousness: if a conscious organism is identical to its parts collectively, phenomenal properties must somehow be properties of the parts
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with the Map's dualism—composition as identity pushes toward reductionism. The Map's position requires that conscious wholes are genuinely something *over and above* their physical parts

### Gasparov, "Substance Dualism and the Unity of Consciousness"

## Major Positions

### Mereological Universalism
- **Proponents**: Lewis, Sider, van Cleve
- **Core claim**: Any collection of objects composes a further object. Composition is unrestricted.
- **Key arguments**: Avoids arbitrariness about which collections compose; avoids vagueness problems (composition cannot be vague if existence cannot be vague)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral on consciousness per se, but creates a bloated ontology including "scattered" objects (your nose plus the Eiffel Tower). Does not privilege conscious beings. The Map's emphasis on consciousness as ontologically special is orthogonal to universalism.

### Mereological Nihilism
- **Proponents**: Dorr, Rosen, (partially) Merricks
- **Core claim**: Composition never occurs. Only mereological simples exist.
- **Key arguments**: Eliminates all puzzles about vagueness in composition; there are only simples "arranged statuewise" or "arranged tablewise"
- **Relation to site tenets**: Deeply problematic for the Map. If no composite objects exist, then conscious organisms do not exist as genuine entities. Undermines Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction), which requires conscious agents as genuine causal powers. Merricks partially escapes this by exempting conscious beings from elimination.

### Van Inwagen's Organicism
- **Proponents**: Van Inwagen, (arguably) Olson
- **Core claim**: Composition occurs if and only if the parts constitute a life. Only organisms are composite objects.
- **Key arguments**: Provides a principled restriction on composition; organisms are natural kinds with clear boundaries; avoids arbitrary composition
- **Relation to site tenets**: Life-based criterion is suggestive but does not go far enough for the Map—the Map would prefer consciousness rather than mere life as the criterion. Van Inwagen's own physicalism about minds limits alignment with Tenet 1.

### Merricks' Consciousness-Based Eliminativism
- **Proponents**: Merricks
- **Core claim**: Only conscious organisms exist as composite objects because only they have irreducible causal powers. All inanimate composites are eliminated.
- **Key arguments**: Causal redundancy principle—if a composite's causal powers are entirely explained by its parts, it doesn't genuinely exist. Consciousness breaks this redundancy because it does not supervene on microscopic properties.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongest alignment with the Map. Consciousness is the criterion for genuine composition. Non-supervenience of consciousness echoes Tenet 1. Causal irreducibility echoes Tenet 3. The Map could adopt this framework while substituting dualism for Merricks' physicalism.

### IIT-Based Restricted Composition (McQueen & Tsuchiya)
- **Proponents**: McQueen, Tsuchiya
- **Core claim**: Composition occurs when integrated information (Φ) is maximised—when the whole constrains its own states more than its parts do individually.
- **Key arguments**: Provides a precise, non-vague criterion; avoids counterexamples that plague other restrictions; connects consciousness to the most fundamental metaphysical question about which objects exist
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicting. IIT is physicalist (consciousness *is* integrated information), which conflicts with Tenet 1. However, the structural insight—that consciousness is what makes genuine wholes—can be extracted from the physicalist framework and redeployed in a dualist one.

### Composition as Identity (CAI)
- **Proponents**: Baxter, Lewis (weak version), Wallace
- **Core claim**: A whole is literally identical to its parts taken collectively. Composition is a kind of identity.
- **Key arguments**: "A whole is nothing over and above its parts" taken literally; avoids ontological inflation
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with the Map. If a conscious organism is identical to its parts, consciousness must reduce to properties of parts. The Map requires that conscious wholes are genuinely something over and above physical parts.

### Emergent Dualism (Hasker)
- **Proponents**: Hasker, O'Connor (related)
- **Core claim**: Consciousness is an emergent substance—immaterial, causally efficacious, generated by but not reducible to brain activity.
- **Key arguments**: Unity-of-consciousness argument; changing physical parts cannot ground a persistent conscious subject; emergence of a genuinely new substance
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns with Tenets 1 and 3. The emergent soul is non-physical and causally active. Tension: Hasker commits to a specific mechanism of emergence from the physical, which may not satisfy the Map's openness to various dualist frameworks.

## Key Debates

### The Special Composition Question and Consciousness
- **Sides**: Van Inwagen (life criterion) vs. Merricks (consciousness criterion) vs. universalists (no criterion needed) vs. nihilists (composition never occurs)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether consciousness plays any role in determining which objects exist as genuine composites
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The trend of connecting consciousness to composition has intensified with McQueen & Tsuchiya's 2023 IIT paper, but no consensus.

### The Combination Problem vs. Non-Compositionality
- **Sides**: Panpsychists (consciousness composes bottom-up) vs. dualists (consciousness is non-compositional) vs. emergentists (consciousness emerges from but is not composed of parts)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether phenomenal properties can be assembled from constituent experiential parts
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The combination problem remains panpsychism's deepest challenge. The Map's existing articles on [[panpsychisms-combination-problem]] and [[phenomenal-non-compositionality]] already stake a position: consciousness resists composition in all directions.

### Composition as Identity and the Status of Wholes
- **Sides**: CAI proponents (wholes are nothing over and above parts) vs. opponents (wholes are genuinely novel entities)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether composites have any ontological reality beyond their parts
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The 2014 Cotnoir & Baxter volume catalysed the debate. Consciousness is a test case: if any whole is genuinely more than its parts, a conscious organism is the strongest candidate.

### Unity of Consciousness as Evidence for Dualism
- **Sides**: Dualists (unity proves irreducibility) vs. physicalists (unity is functionally grounded) vs. eliminativists (unity is an illusion)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the unified character of experience implies a non-physical bearer
- **Relation to composition**: Unity of consciousness is the phenomenal analogue of mereological unity—what makes a subject a single whole rather than a collection of parts
