## Executive Summary

Type-identity theory (a.k.a. the identity theory, central-state materialism, mind-brain
identity theory) is the reductive-materialist thesis that mental state-**types** are
**identical to** physical (brain) state-types — the textbook example being "pain is C-fibre
firing." It was the first fully-developed reductive materialism of the analytic 20th century,
introduced by U.T. Place (1956) and Herbert Feigl (1958) and given its canonical defence by
J.J.C. Smart (1959). The theory claims an *a posteriori*, contingent-looking identity
(mirroring "water is H2O" or "heat is molecular motion") rather than a mere correlation,
motivated partly by Occam-style parsimony and by Feigl's complaint that dualist
mind-body correlations are "nomological danglers." It was displaced from dominance within a
decade by Hilary Putnam's **multiple realizability** argument (1967), which drove the field
toward functionalism, and pressed further by Saul Kripke's modal/rigid-designator argument.
Type-identity survives today mainly in narrow, species-specific, or "second-order property"
forms. For the Map, type-identity is the paradigm reductive materialism that Tenet 1
(dualism) denies: the theory **asserts** the identity rather than explaining it, re-labelling
the explanatory gap rather than closing it.

## Key Sources

### SEP — "The Mind/Brain Identity Theory" (Smart, J.J.C.)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mind-identity/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **Status**: WEB-VERIFIED (full entry fetched)
- **Key points**:
  - Origin with Place (1956) and Feigl (1958); Smart (1959) the canonical proponent, converted
    after discussions with Place at Adelaide and unable to refute him.
  - **Topic-neutral analysis**: Smart analyses mental reports in language "neutral" between
    physicalism and dualism — e.g. an orange after-image report becomes "there is something
    going on which is like what is going on when I have my eyes open, am awake, and there is an
    orange illuminated in good light in front of me." This blocks the inference to
    non-physical properties while allowing the state to be identical to a brain process.
  - **Nomological danglers**: Feigl's term for the dualist's mind-physical correlation laws —
    they would "dangle" from the nomological net of physical science as implausible
    excrescences.
  - **Type vs token**: type-identity = each mental *type* is a brain-process *type*
    universally; token-identity = each particular mental event is some particular physical
    event, but the same type may be differently realized across individuals/species.
  - **Multiple realizability**: functionalists observed identical functional roles could have
    different physical realizations (silicon vs carbon), challenging strict type-identity;
    some defend type-identity via second-order "having some physical property that plays role
    R" moves.
  - **Kripke's modal objection**: identities like "heat = molecular motion" are necessary if
    true, yet mind-brain identity looks contingent; the usual "it merely appears contingent"
    move (available for heat via the felt-heat/heat distinction) is harder to run for pain,
    since pain's appearance just is pain.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (this is the reductive materialism dualism
  denies); the entry is the neutral scholarly anchor for the whole cluster.

### Smart, J.J.C. — "Sensations and Brain Processes"
- **URL**: https://www.pdcnet.org/phr/content/phr_1959_0068_0002_0141_0156 (Philosophy
  Documentation Center) / PhilPapers rec SMASAB
- **Type**: Journal paper — *The Philosophical Review* 68(2): 141–156, 1959.
- **Status**: CITATION WEB-VERIFIED (PDC lists volume 68, issue 2, pp. 141–156, 1959;
  concordant with PhilPapers).
- **Key points**:
  - The canonical defence. Sensations are "nothing over and above" brain processes; the
    identity is contingent and known *a posteriori*.
  - Develops the **topic-neutral** translation of experience-reports (see SEP summary above).
  - Answers eight objections; parsimony (a broadly Occamist consideration) is a stated
    motivation — a dualism of "irreducible psychic" properties multiplies fundamental
    kinds needlessly.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1; the parsimony motive is exactly the move
  Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits) is designed to resist.

### Putnam, Hilary — "Psychological Predicates" / "The Nature of Mental States"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/PUTPP-2
- **Type**: Book chapter — originally "Psychological Predicates," in W.H. Capitan & D.D.
  Merrill (eds.), *Art, Mind, and Religion*, Univ. of Pittsburgh Press, 1967, pp. 37–48;
  reprinted (retitled) as "The Nature of Mental States" in Putnam, *Mind, Language and
  Reality: Philosophical Papers Vol. 2*, Cambridge Univ. Press, 1975, pp. 429–440.
- **Status**: CITATION WEB-VERIFIED (original venue, editors, publisher, pages, and the
  retitling/reprint all confirmed via PhilPapers version history).
- **Key points**:
  - The **multiple realizability** argument: it is "overwhelmingly probable" that a given
    psychological kind (e.g. pain) is realized by different physical states in different
    creatures (mammal, mollusc, hypothetical Martian/silicon being); so a mental type cannot
    be identical to any single brain-state type.
  - This is the argument credited with ending type-identity's dominance and launching
    functionalism.
- **Tenet alignment**: Anti-type-identity but still materialist — the Map treats functionalism
  as a *different* rival, not an ally.

## Major Positions

### Type-Identity Theory (central-state materialism)
- **Proponents**: Place, Feigl, Smart; later defenders in narrow/species-specific forms
  (e.g. arguments that "pain-in-humans" = a specific human neural type), and "modest" or
  "revised" identity theorists.
- **Core claim**: Mental state-**types** are numerically identical to physical (brain)
  state-types. Not correlation, not causation — identity, in the mode of "water is H2O."
- **Key arguments**: contingent *a posteriori* identity (Place's "is" of composition);
  topic-neutral analysis blocks anti-physicalist inferences (Smart); parsimony / elimination
  of nomological danglers (Feigl, Smart).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The paradigm Tenet-1 rival. Its weakness, from the Map's
  angle, is that the identity is **posited, not explained** — asserting "pain = C-fibre
  firing" re-describes the correlation the explanatory gap is about; it does not show *why*
  that firing feels like anything.

### Token-Identity Theory
- **Core claim**: Every particular mental event is identical to some particular physical
  event, but mental *types* need not map onto physical *types*.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Weaker than type-identity; compatible with functionalism and
  non-reductive physicalism. Still conflicts with Tenet 1 (it remains a physicalism about
  the mental), but it is not the specific thesis the Map most directly targets.

### Functionalism (successor)
- **Core claim**: Mental types are defined by causal/functional role, realizable in any
  suitable substrate — the position multiple realizability pushed the field toward.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A distinct rival with its own Map page; note the contrast for
  the article but do not merge.

## Key Debates

### Multiple Realizability (Putnam vs type-identity)
- **Sides**: Putnam (and later functionalists) vs Place/Smart-style type-identity.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether a mental type can be one physical type. If pain is realized
  by different physical states across species/substrates, type-identity fails.
- **Current state**: Widely taken as decisive against *global* type-identity; contested for
  *narrow/species-specific* type-identity (Bechtel & Mundale and others have argued the
  multiple-realizability case is overstated once brain-mapping grain is fixed). Live but
  no longer field-defining.

### Kripke's Modal Argument
- **Sides**: Kripke vs identity theorists.
- **Core disagreement**: Identity statements between rigid designators are necessary if true;
  "pain = C-fibre firing" seems contingent (we can conceive pain without C-fibres and vice
  versa). For "heat = molecular motion" the apparent contingency is explained away via the
  felt-heat/heat distinction — but pain has no appearance/reality gap to exploit, since
  pain's appearance is pain. So the identity theorist owes an explanation the heat case does
  not need. (Kripke, *Naming and Necessity*; lectures 1970, published 1972 in Davidson &
  Harman eds., *Semantics of Natural Language*; book edn. Harvard Univ. Press, 1980 — dates
  from standard reference knowledge, primary text NOT re-fetched this session.)
- **Current state**: Ongoing; overlaps the conceivability/zombie and knowledge-argument
  literatures.

### Explanatory-Gap / Knowledge-Argument Pressure
- **Sides**: Levine (explanatory gap), Jackson (knowledge argument / Mary), Nagel (bat) vs
  reductive identity.
- **Core disagreement**: Even if the identity holds, it leaves *why* the physical type feels
  the way it does unexplained; Mary learns something new on seeing red though she knew all
  physical facts.
- **Current state**: Central to contemporary anti-physicalism; the Map's own motivation lives
  here. (Names cited from standard reference knowledge; primary texts not re-verified this
  session — downstream expand-topic should verify Levine 1983, Jackson 1982/1986, Nagel 1974
  if quoted.)

## Map-Relevance (Tenet 1 focus)

- **Type-identity is the paradigm reductive materialism the Map rejects.** The Map's dualism
  (Tenet 1: consciousness is not reducible to physical processes) is, in the sharpest terms,
  precisely the **denial of the type-type identity**. Where the identity theorist says
  "the mental type just *is* the physical type," Tenet 1 says the two come apart.
- **The identity is asserted, not explained.** The Map's core critical move: naming
  "pain = C-fibre firing" as an identity re-labels the correlation rather than closing the
  explanatory gap. It tells us *that* the two co-occur (which dualist interactionism also
  predicts) but not *why* the physical process is accompanied by experience. Smart's own
  topic-neutral translation arguably concedes this — it deliberately drains the phenomenal
  content from the report so the identity can go through, which is where the Map says the
  hard problem was smuggled out rather than solved.
- **Parsimony pressure meets Tenet 5.** Smart/Feigl motivate the identity partly by
  Occam-style economy and dislike of "danglers." Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits) is the
  Map's direct reply: with incomplete knowledge of consciousness, simplicity is an
  unreliable guide, and eliminating the "dangler" by fiat is not the same as showing it does
  not exist.
- **Do NOT conflate with the Map's own substrate-sensitivity.** The Map *also* holds that
  substrate matters — but for **interface / interaction reasons** (which physical systems can
  couple to consciousness, per Tenets 2–3 on minimal, bidirectional quantum interaction),
  **not** because the mental type *is* the physical type. This is an important contrast to
  draw explicitly in the article: agreeing that "the brain matters" is not agreeing with
  type-identity. The Map's substrate-sensitivity is dualist-interactionist, not identity-
  reductive; conflating the two would misrepresent both.
- **Multiple realizability is a double-edged tool.** It refutes type-identity but does so *in
  service of functionalism*, another physicalism. The Map should present it as an internal
  materialist crisis, not as evidence for dualism — the Map's objection to type-identity is
  the explanatory gap, which functionalism inherits.
