## Executive Summary

Content externalism is the thesis that the content of at least some mental and linguistic states is partly constituted by facts external to the subject — the physical environment (Putnam's Twin Earth) and the social/linguistic community (Burge's arthritis case). Its slogan, "meanings just ain't in the head," directly threatens purely internalist physical accounts of content-fixing: two intrinsic physical duplicates can differ in what their thoughts are about. For the Map, the load-bearing move is the **phenomenal-vs-intentional split**: externalism is strongest for *intentional/referential* content (what a thought is about) and weakest — arguably reversible — for *phenomenal* content (what an experience is like). The Map can therefore hold a hybrid: externalist about wide intentional content while remaining internalist about phenomenal character, which is exactly what the phenomenal-constitution stance needs. A secondary payoff is dialectical: externalism is a *materialist-camp* result (Putnam, Burge, Dretske are naturalists) that nonetheless shows intrinsic physical state underdetermines content — a pressure point the Map can cite rather than re-derive per article.

## Key Sources

### Putnam, "The Meaning of 'Meaning'" (1975)
- **URL**: https://conservancy.umn.edu/items/7bfe66c3-bfb2-4be8-953a-58c997ce90bd (University Digital Conservancy, Univ. of Minnesota Press)
- **Type**: Book chapter (verified at publisher)
- **Full citation**: Putnam, H. (1975). "The Meaning of 'Meaning'." In *Language, Mind and Knowledge*, ed. Keith Gunderson. Minnesota Studies in the Philosophy of Science, vol. 7. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, pp. 131–193. Reprinted in Putnam, *Mind, Language and Reality: Philosophical Papers, vol. 2* (Cambridge University Press, 1975).
- **Key points**:
  - Twin Earth: an atom-for-atom duplicate world where the watery stuff is XYZ, not H2O. Oscar and Twin-Oscar are intrinsic physical/psychological duplicates, yet Oscar's "water" refers to H2O and Twin-Oscar's refers to XYZ. Reference is fixed by the actual substance the term is causally embedded with, not by anything in the head.
  - The "division of linguistic labour": ordinary speakers defer to experts for the extension of natural-kind terms; the individual need not know the identifying criteria.
  - Meaning has an indexical, environment-involving component — natural-kind terms rigidly designate the actual stuff.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-supportive of Tenet 1. Putnam is a naturalist here, but the result (intrinsic physical state underdetermines aboutness) is a wedge the Map can use against internalist physicalism about content.
- **Quote** (verbatim, verified against the primary-source PDF, not any Map page): "Cut the pie any way you like, 'meanings' just ain't in the head!"

### Burge, "Individualism and the Mental" (1979)
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1475-4975.1979.tb00374.x (Wiley, DOI 10.1111/j.1475-4975.1979.tb00374.x)
- **Type**: Journal article (verified at publisher)
- **Full citation**: Burge, T. (1979). "Individualism and the Mental." *Midwest Studies in Philosophy* 4:73–121.
- **Key points**:
  - The "arthritis" thought experiment: a patient believes he has "arthritis in his thigh" (a linguistic error, since arthritis is by definition a joint disease). Hold the patient's intrinsic state fixed but vary the *community's* usage of "arthritis" so that in the counterfactual community the word applies to thigh ailments too. Then the patient's belief contents differ — social externalism, generalising Putnam beyond natural-kind terms to ordinary concepts.
  - Anti-individualism: the individuation of a subject's mental states depends constitutively on relations to a linguistic/social community, not just on the individual's intrinsic constitution.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-supportive of Tenet 1; strengthens the case that content is not fixed by intrinsic physical facts, but Burge himself is a materialist and does not draw dualist conclusions.

### Burge, "Individualism and Psychology" (1986)
- **URL**: https://philosophy.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/Burge-1986-Individualism-and-Psychology.pdf (author's institutional copy) / https://www.pdcnet.org/phr/content/phr_1986_0095_0001_0003_0045
- **Type**: Journal article (verified at publisher)
- **Full citation**: Burge, T. (1986). "Individualism and Psychology." *The Philosophical Review* 95(1):3–45.
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that scientific psychology (esp. Marrian vision science) is itself anti-individualist: it type-individuates perceptual states by their environmental causes, so wide content is not merely folk-psychological.
  - Undercuts the reply that "real" cognitive science can retreat to narrow content — the externalist individuation is already baked into empirical theories of mind.
- **Relevance**: This is the citation to use when an article claims externalism is confined to intentional/folk content; Burge extends it into the representational states cognitive science posits.

### Dretske, *Naturalizing the Mind* (1995)
- **URL**: https://direct.mit.edu/books/monograph/4689 / https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262540896/naturalizing-the-mind/
- **Type**: Book, Jean Nicod Lectures (verified at MIT Press)
- **Full citation**: Dretske, F. (1995). *Naturalizing the Mind*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.
- **Key points**:
  - The Representational Thesis: all mental facts are representational facts; phenomenal qualities of experience are the *external* properties the experience represents objects as having. Representations are "in the head," but what makes them representations lies outside.
  - This is the boldest extension of externalism to the phenomenal: qualia go wide.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with the Map's phenomenal-constitution stance. Dretske is the position the Map must resist on phenomenal content while it may accept externalism about intentional content.

### Dretske, "Phenomenal Externalism, or If Meanings Ain't in the Head, Where Are Qualia?" (1996)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/DREPEO (JSTOR DOI 10.2307/1522899)
- **Type**: Journal article (verified; venue confirmed)
- **Full citation**: Dretske, F. (1996). "Phenomenal Externalism, or If Meanings Ain't in the Head, Where Are Qualia?" *Philosophical Issues* 7:143–158. (Replies in the same volume by John Biro, Jaegwon Kim, and Paul Horwich.)
- **Key points**:
  - Explicitly asks whether the Twin-Earth style argument transfers from meaning to qualia; Dretske says yes — the qualities distinguishing experiences need not be in the head.
  - Kim's reply ("Dretske's qualia externalism") is the pivot the Map can lean on: extending externalism to phenomenal character is far more contentious than extending it to reference.
- **Relevance**: This paper *is* the phenomenal-vs-intentional externalism debate in miniature; it names the exact fault line the Map's hybrid exploits.

### Farkas, *The Subject's Point of View* (2008)
- **URL**: https://ndpr.nd.edu/reviews/the-subject-s-point-of-view/ (Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews) / https://philarchive.org/rec/FARPIW ("Phenomenal Intentionality without Compromise")
- **Type**: Book, Oxford University Press (verified via NDPR review + SEP)
- **Full citation**: Farkas, K. (2008). *The Subject's Point of View*. Oxford: Oxford University Press.
- **Key points**:
  - Defends an uncompromising internalism grounded in first-person access ("Cartesian" theory of mind): the defining features of mental states are phenomenally available to the subject.
  - "Phenomenal intentionality": intrinsic, narrow, phenomenally-constituted directedness is all a theory of intentionality needs. Externalism is charged with making mental states opaque to introspection and violating first-person authority.
- **Tenet alignment**: Supportive of the Map's internalist read of phenomenal content; but note it is *more* internalist than the Map needs — Farkas would narrow intentional content too, whereas the Map's hybrid can concede wide intentional content.

### SEP, "Externalism About the Mind"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/content-externalism/ (Fall 2025 edition)
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (verified)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes *wide/broad* content (susceptible to Putnam/Burge arguments) from *narrow* content (immune, supervenes on intrinsic state).
  - Phenomenal intentionality theory (Loar, Horgan/Tienson, Kriegel): phenomenal intentionality is basic and narrow; other intentionality is derivative. Carruthers, Rey, and Chalmers hold experiences have narrow content and phenomenal character reduces to narrow content; Block and Deutsch deny phenomenal character supervenes on content at all.
  - Records the standard worry that constructions of narrow content have been "systematically criticized" (cites Yli-Vakkuri and Hawthorne 2018) — flagged here as a live objection the Map should not paper over.

## Major Positions

### Semantic / Content Externalism (Putnam, Burge, Davidson)
- **Core claim**: The individuation of (at least some) intentional content depends on relations to the physical environment and/or social community; intrinsic duplicates can differ in content.
- **Two strands**: *physical externalism* (Putnam — natural-kind terms; the environment) and *social externalism* (Burge — the linguistic community; generalises to non-natural-kind concepts).
- **Relation to Map**: The Map can *accept* this for intentional/referential content. It coheres with the corpus's existing lean — the functionalism article already uses semantic externalism against narrow functional role, and the pragmatism route reads Putnam's later trajectory as a retreat from internalist functionalism. A dedicated page would let the content cluster (intentionality, teleosemantics, hard problem of content, the naturalisation-failure-for-content) cite externalism as a positioned thesis instead of re-deriving it.

### Phenomenal Externalism (Dretske, Lycan, Tye)
- **Core claim**: Externalism extends to phenomenal character — qualia are, or are fixed by, wide representational content.
- **Relation to Map**: **Conflicts with the phenomenal-constitution stance (Tenet 1).** This is the position the Map must *resist*. The Map's hybrid says: externalism is a claim about aboutness, and aboutness is not the same as felt character. Twin-Oscar's XYZ-thoughts differ in reference but the *what-it-is-likeness* of his experience can be held fixed. So the very duplicate-invariance externalists exploit for reference cuts the other way for phenomenal character.

### Internalism / Phenomenal Intentionality (Farkas, Loar, Horgan and Tienson, Kriegel; narrow-content defenders Chalmers, Carruthers)
- **Core claim**: The mental essentially involves first-person-accessible, narrow, phenomenally-grounded content; wide content is derivative or dispensable for the explanatory core.
- **Relation to Map**: Supportive of the internalist half of the hybrid — but stronger than the Map requires. The Map need not deny wide intentional content exists; it only needs phenomenal character to be internally constituted. This is a friendlier, more defensible position than blanket internalism, and it sidesteps the Yli-Vakkuri/Hawthorne pressure on narrow *intentional* content by not resting the phenomenal claim on a constructed narrow-content notion.

## Key Debates

### Does externalism transfer from meaning to qualia?
- **Sides**: Dretske/Lycan/Tye (yes, phenomenal externalism) vs. Kim/Block/Farkas/Chalmers (no, or highly contested).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "what a state represents" exhausts "what it is like" to be in that state. Externalist representationalism says yes; the Map (with the anti-representationalists here) says the felt character is not identical to wide representational content.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and unresolved; this is precisely the seam the Map's hybrid occupies. The Map's distinctive claim: reference/aboutness can go wide, felt character stays narrow.

### Wide content and mental causation (anti-individualism → causation interface)
- **Sides**: Externalists (content is wide) vs. those who fear wide content is causally inert (the "causal exclusion" worry — if content is individuated by distal/historical relations, how does it do local causal work?).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether externally-individuated content can be causally relevant to behaviour, given that causation seems to be a local matter.
- **Relation to Map**: Directly relevant to the Map's content-specificity-of-mental-causation and status-of-content concerns. If externalist content is causally relevant, then content-in-virtue-of-environment is doing causal work — a wide analogue of the interaction the Map already posits. If the exclusion worry bites, externalism becomes another site where content's causal purchase is under threat, reinforcing the Map's claim that content-fixing resists tidy physical reduction. Either horn is usable; a dedicated page should present both without forcing.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a `concepts/` article ("content-externalism-twin-earth" or "content-externalism") could:

1. **Present the hybrid directly** (recommended): externalist about intentional/referential content, internalist about phenomenal content. Lead with the split as the Map's thesis, use Twin Earth only as much as needed (LLM-first: assume the reader knows the thought experiment), and tie explicitly to Tenet 1 via the phenomenal-constitution stance. Show phenomenal externalism (Dretske) as the position resisted and phenomenal-intentionality internalism (Farkas et al.) as the friendly-but-stronger neighbour.
2. **Frame it as a materialist-camp concession** (concession-convergence angle): externalism is a naturalist result showing intrinsic physical state underdetermines content — one more independent retreat from tidy internalist physicalism, parallel to the functionalism article's existing use of Putnam.
3. **Center the mental-causation interface**: use anti-individualism + the causal-exclusion worry to sharpen where content's causal purchase comes from, connecting to content-specificity-of-mental-causation and status-of-content.
