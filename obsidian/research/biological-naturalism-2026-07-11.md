## Executive Summary

John Searle's **biological naturalism** is the position that consciousness is (a) *caused by* neurobiological processes in the brain, (b) *realized in* the brain as a higher-level system feature, (c) *ontologically irreducible* because it has a first-person (subjective) mode of existence, yet (d) *not* a distinct substance or property in the dualist sense and *not* eliminable or reducible in the physicalist sense. Searle's central move is to claim the traditional mind-body problem *dissolves* once we abandon the assumption that "mental" and "physical" name mutually exclusive categories: consciousness is a biological feature of the brain the way digestion is a feature of the stomach. The signature technical distinction is *causal reducibility without ontological reducibility* — brain processes fully cause consciousness (nothing else is going on causally), but consciousness cannot be redefined out of existence because a third-person redescription always leaves out the very first-person feature we were trying to explain. This is a **non-dualist rival** to the Map that sits *adjacent* to the Map's property-dualist neighbours: many critics (Chalmers, Corcoran, Nagel) argue biological naturalism *collapses into* property dualism in all but name, and it inherits the causal-exclusion tension (how does an irreducible higher-level feature do causal work without either overdetermining or being epiphenomenal?). Biological naturalism is also the philosophical engine behind Searle's Chinese Room argument and his rejection of Strong AI: only systems with the right *causal powers* (biological brains, or something causally equivalent) can produce consciousness. Searle died in 2025; this remains one of the most-discussed "irreducible-but-not-dualist" positions in philosophy of mind.

## Major Positions

### Biological Naturalism (Searle's own view)
- **Proponents**: John Searle (chiefly); sympathisers among naturalist philosophers who want to take consciousness seriously without dualism.
- **Core claim**: Consciousness is a higher-level biological feature of the brain — *caused by* lower-level neuronal processes and *realized in* the brain as a system feature — that is *causally reducible* but *ontologically irreducible* to those processes, because it has an ineliminable first-person mode of existence. The mind-body problem is a pseudo-problem generated by the false Cartesian dichotomy of "mental" vs "physical."
- **Key arguments**:
  1. **The caused-but-irreducible structure.** Lower-level neurobiology (synaptic transmission, neuronal firing) causes consciousness the way molecular motion causes liquidity. Consciousness is a *macro* feature of the *micro* system, not an extra thing added on.
  2. **Causal vs ontological reduction.** Standard scientific reductions (heat = mean kinetic energy) *redefine* the phenomenon in terms of its cause and discard the surface appearance as merely subjective. Searle says we *could* do this for consciousness too — but we *choose not to*, because the whole point of studying consciousness *is* the first-person appearance. The appearance *is* the reality here, so there is nothing to redescribe away. Hence consciousness is causally reducible but ontologically irreducible — and the irreducibility is a "trivial consequence" of how we define reduction, not a deep metaphysical fact.
  3. **Why consciousness differs from liquidity/solidity.** This is the crux critics press on. Liquidity is *ontologically reducible* — once you have the molecular story, "liquid" adds nothing. Consciousness is not: even with the full neural story, the first-person subjective ontology remains. Searle grants the disanalogy and locates it in the first-person/third-person ontology distinction, insisting this still does not make consciousness *non-physical*.
  4. **Rejection of both rivals.** Against *identity theory / reductive physicalism*: consciousness cannot be ontologically reduced without losing its subject matter. Against *property dualism and substance dualism*: consciousness is not an extra non-physical property or substance floating alongside the neural — it is just the brain doing something biological.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the position the Map most needs to engage carefully, because it is the strongest *non-dualist* attempt to keep subjectivity real and irreducible. It rejects Tenet 1's substance/realm framing outright, yet shares the Map's refusal to reduce or eliminate consciousness. The Map's charge (below) is that the position is *unstable* — it either becomes property dualism or leaves the hard problem untouched.

### Property Dualism (the position critics say Searle really holds)
- **Proponents (of the charge)**: David Chalmers, Kevin Corcoran, Thomas Nagel, and others argue Searle's view *is* property dualism.
- **Core claim (of biological naturalism as read by critics)**: Mental properties have a first-person ontology; physical properties have a third-person ontology; these are two irreducibly different kinds of property instantiated by the brain. That *is* property dualism, whatever Searle calls it.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Chalmers: if the phenomenal feature is conceptually distinct from and not entailed by the neural realizer (zombies/inverts are conceivable), then it is a further, non-physically-necessitated property — property dualism, and the hard problem is untouched.
  - Corcoran / others: Searle's admission of an "irreducible first-person ontology" makes his disavowal of property dualism *merely verbal*; strip the Cartesian baggage and epiphenomenalist property dualism is exactly what remains.
- **Relation to site tenets**: If the critics are right, biological naturalism is *dualism-in-denial*, and the Map's position is that an *honest, explicit* dualism (property or substance) is more coherent than a denied one.

### Searle's self-defense (why he says he is not a dualist)
- **Core claim**: Property dualism (as Searle characterises it) treats mental and physical as *two mutually exclusive metaphysical categories*, importing the Cartesian apparatus. Searle rejects the categories themselves: consciousness's first-person ontology is a *biological* fact about how one physical system (the brain) exists, not evidence of a second realm. "First-person ontology" describes a *mode of existence within nature*, not a non-physical property.
- **Assessment**: Fair to present this as a genuine dialectical move, not a dodge. Whether "a physical feature with an irreducible first-person ontology" is *distinct from* "a non-physical property" is precisely the contested point — do NOT bald-assert that Searle loses.

## Key Debates

### Does biological naturalism collapse into property dualism?
- **Sides**: Searle (no — the mental/physical dichotomy is false, so the "two kinds of property" framing does not apply) vs Chalmers, Corcoran, Nagel and many commentators (yes — an irreducible first-person ontology *is* a distinct property).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether affirming an ontologically irreducible subjective feature commits one to a distinct *property* over and above the physical, or whether "irreducible first-person mode of existence" can be a purely *biological* description of one physical system.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and, arguably, the defining objection to the view. Widely judged the most serious pressure point; no consensus that Searle escapes it.

### The causal-exclusion / overdetermination tension (Kim)
- **Sides**: Jaegwon Kim (biological naturalism faces the exclusion problem) vs Searle (the trilemma does not arise for him).
- **Core disagreement**: If consciousness is a higher-level feature *and* does genuine causal work, then either (i) the neural cause and the conscious cause *overdetermine* behaviour, or (ii) consciousness is *epiphenomenal*, or (iii) consciousness *reduces* to the neural. Kim argues Searle can only escape by accepting mind-brain *identity* (mental state = brain state), which Searle rejects.
- **Searle's response**: There is "nothing there causally except the neurobiology" — consciousness is *causally reducible*, so there is no second cause competing with the neural one, hence no overdetermination and no epiphenomenalism; but because the *ontologies* differ, consciousness is not ontologically reduced/identical either. Critics reply this either quietly concedes identity of the causal work (Kim's point) or leaves the higher-level feature doing no distinctive causal work after all.
- **Current state**: Unresolved; a live seam where the Map can press. The Map's *interactionist* dualism (Tenets 2-3) takes the opposite bet — that consciousness *does* genuine, minimal, bidirectional causal work — so this debate maps directly onto the Map's own causal-closure commitments.

### Biological naturalism and Strong AI (the Chinese Room)
- **Sides**: Searle (only systems with the right biological *causal powers* — brains, or something causally equivalent — can be conscious; running the right program is neither sufficient nor constitutive) vs computationalists/functionalists (the right functional organisation suffices, substrate-independently).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether consciousness is *substrate-neutral* (computation) or depends on specific *causal powers* of biological matter.
- **Current state**: Enduring. Biological naturalism is the *reason* Searle rejects Strong AI: because consciousness is a caused biological feature, syntax (program) is not sufficient for semantics/consciousness; you need whatever causal powers brains have. Fodor and Haugeland counter that biological specificity (hydrocarbon chemistry) is irrelevant — environmental causal connections, not biochemistry, fix intentionality.
