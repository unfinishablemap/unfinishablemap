## Executive Summary

Enactivism is a family of positions in philosophy of mind and cognitive science holding that cognition arises through dynamic interaction between an organism and its environment, rather than through internal computation or representation. It draws on autopoiesis (self-organizing living systems), phenomenology (Merleau-Ponty, Husserl), and Buddhist philosophy. Enactivism challenges interactionist dualism primarily by denying the mind-body separation that dualism presupposes—arguing that cognition is constituted by embodied activity, not something that happens in a separate mental realm and then causally influences the body. However, enactivism faces its own significant problems with consciousness, and its phenomenological commitments actually share important ground with dualism's insistence on the irreducibility of experience.

### "Enactivism is Not Interactionism" (De Jaegher, Di Paolo, Gallagher, 2013)
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3539800/
- **Type**: Paper (Frontiers in Human Neuroscience)
- **Key points**:
  - Clarifies that enactivism's emphasis on interaction is not the same as "interactionism" in the dualist sense
  - Enactivism's interactions are between organisms and environments, not between mind and body as separate substances
  - The Interactive Brain Hypothesis: brain processes are functionally shaped by social interactions
  - Participatory sense-making relies on coordination breakdowns and recoveries
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly opposes Tenet 3's notion of mind-body causal interaction; reframes "interaction" in purely embodied terms

### Thompson, "Mind in Life" (2007)
- **URL**: https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674057517
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Deep continuity thesis: where there is life, there is mind
  - Experience is irreducible—but not because of metaphysically peculiar properties (contra property dualism)
  - Irreducibility stems from the "ineliminable transcendental character" of lived experience
  - Neurophenomenology as method: combining first-person phenomenological reports with third-person neuroscience
  - Self-organizing features of mind are enriched versions of self-organizing features of life
- **Tenet alignment**: Shares Tenet 1's commitment to irreducibility of experience but rejects the metaphysical framework; directly opposes Tenet 2 (quantum interaction) by locating the mind-life connection in autopoiesis rather than quantum mechanics
- **Quote**: "Experience is irreducible not because it possesses metaphysically peculiar 'properties'... It's irreducible because of its ineliminable transcendental character"

  - Introduced the term "enaction" to cognitive science
  - Cognition is "the enactment of a world and a mind on the basis of a history of the variety of actions that a being in the world performs"
  - Drew on Buddhist philosophy and phenomenology

- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 by rejecting the pre-given mind that dualism requires; the Buddhist influence introduces non-self doctrines incompatible with dualist conceptions of a persisting mental substance

### Hutto & Myin - "Radicalizing Enactivism" (2013)
- **URL**: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262534642/radicalizing-enactivism/
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Radical enactivism (REC): basic minds are contentless—no representations, no content
  - The "Hard Problem of Content": naturalizing intentional content is as intractable as the hard problem of consciousness
  - Only linguistically sophisticated cognition involves representational content
  - Two-front battle: ontological (mental representations don't exist) and methodological (non-representationalist explanations are possible)
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with dualist accounts of intentionality (Tenet 1 concept links); however, the Hard Problem of Content parallels dualism's Hard Problem of Consciousness—both identify fundamental explanatory gaps in physicalist/naturalist frameworks

### "Never Mind the Gap" - Neurophenomenology and the Hard Problem

### O'Regan & Noe - Sensorimotor Contingency Theory (2001)
- **URL**: http://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Sensorimotor_theory_of_consciousness
- **Type**: Theory/Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Seeing is a way of acting—exploring the environment through sensorimotor contingencies
  - Experience arises from mastering the laws governing how sensory stimulation changes with movement
  - Perception and action are on equal footing
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 3 by making consciousness an activity rather than a cause; however, the theory struggles to explain why mastering sensorimotor contingencies should produce subjective experience at all (the hard problem resurfaces)

### Merleau-Ponty - Phenomenology of Perception
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/merleau-ponty/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia entry on foundational philosopher
- **Key points**:
  - The "lived body" (corps vecu) is neither pure object nor pure subject
  - "We do not have bodies; we are bodies"
  - Mind is an "accomplishment of structural integration" conditioned by matter and life
  - Challenges Cartesian dualism while preserving the primacy of experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Opposes substance dualism directly; however, Merleau-Ponty's insistence on the primacy of lived experience and his rejection of reductionism shares ground with Tenet 1's commitment to irreducibility

## Major Positions

### Autopoietic Enactivism (Varela, Thompson, Di Paolo)
- **Proponents**: Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, Ezequiel Di Paolo, Humberto Maturana
- **Core claim**: Cognition is a biological phenomenon rooted in the self-organizing (autopoietic) dynamics of living systems. There is deep continuity between life and mind—all living things engage in "sense-making."
- **Key arguments**:
  - Autopoietic systems are self-producing and self-maintaining, creating their own boundaries
  - This self-organization gives rise to a perspective or "point of view" on the world
  - Consciousness is an enriched form of the same organizational principles found in all life
  - Neurophenomenology bridges first-person experience and third-person neuroscience
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly challenges Tenet 1 by grounding mind in biological organization rather than a separate ontological category. Challenges Tenet 2 by locating the mind-life interface in autopoiesis rather than quantum mechanics. However, Thompson's claim that experience is "irreducible" due to its "transcendental character" inadvertently supports the dualist insight that consciousness cannot be eliminated or reduced.

### Sensorimotor Enactivism (O'Regan, Noe)
- **Proponents**: J. Kevin O'Regan, Alva Noe
- **Core claim**: Perceptual experience is constituted by the exercise of sensorimotor knowledge—practical understanding of how sensory stimulation changes with bodily movement.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Seeing is not a passive receipt of information but an active exploration
  - Qualia are explained by the structure of sensorimotor contingencies, not internal representations
  - The "feel" of red vs. blue reflects different patterns of sensorimotor interaction
- **Relation to site tenets**: Attempts to dissolve qualia (central to Tenet 1's arguments) into patterns of activity. But the "explanatory gap" persists: why should exercising sensorimotor knowledge produce subjective experience? This is the same gap dualism identifies.

### Radical Enactivism (Hutto, Myin)
- **Proponents**: Daniel Hutto, Erik Myin
- **Core claim**: Basic minds are contentless—they involve no mental representations. Only language-involving cognition has representational content.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The "Hard Problem of Content": naturalizing intentional content is as intractable as naturalizing consciousness
  - Representationalist explanations are not just wrong but unnecessary for basic cognition
  - Embodied, interactive explanations can replace representational ones
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Hard Problem of Content is structurally parallel to dualism's Hard Problem of Consciousness—both identify fundamental limits in physicalist explanation. However, radical enactivism's solution (eliminate content from basic minds) is the opposite of dualism's (posit a non-physical domain).

## Key Debates

### Enactivism vs. Interactionist Dualism: The Core Challenge
- **Sides**: Enactivists argue that mind-body interaction presupposes a false separation; dualists argue that the separation is real and causally bridged
- **Core disagreement**: Whether mind and body are genuinely distinct (dualism) or whether the distinction is a philosophical error that should be dissolved (enactivism)
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Enactivism has not provided a satisfying account of why experience is subjective; dualism has not provided a satisfying account of how a non-physical mind causally interacts with the physical brain. Both face explanatory gaps.

### Does Enactivism Really Dissolve the Hard Problem?
- **Sides**: Enactivists (Thompson, Varela) claim it dissolves the problem by reframing it; critics (various) argue it merely relocates the problem
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the deep continuity of life and mind eliminates the explanatory gap or merely describes a correlation that itself demands explanation
- **Current state**: Critics appear to have the stronger position. Even sympathetic commentators note that neurophenomenology "assumes rather than dissolves the hard problem." Thompson's claim of irreducibility based on "transcendental character" is a restatement of the problem, not a solution.

### Is Enactivism Coherent as a Unified Position?
- **Sides**: Proponents see the three strands (autopoietic, sensorimotor, radical) as complementary; critics argue they are in tension
- **Core disagreement**: Whether enactivism's simultaneous deflationary claims (dissolving problems) and revisionist claims (reframing the metaphysics of mind) are compatible
- **Current state**: The "Putting down the revolt" critique (2022) argues persuasively that enactivism's theoretical stance is "inherently unstable" due to blending deflationism and revisionism. Internal debates between the strands continue.

### Embodied Cognition and Property Dualism
- **Sides**: Embodied cognition proponents who accept irreducibility of experience vs. those who aim for full naturalization
- **Core disagreement**: Whether embodied cognition is compatible with property dualism
- **Current state**: Surprisingly open. The phenomenological tradition embedded in enactivism supports irreducibility of experience, which is the core commitment of property dualism. Some commentators argue embodied cognition is "still dualist" in this sense.

1. **Enactivism's challenge to the causal interface** — Focus on how enactivism rejects the very framework of mind-body causal interaction. Argue that while enactivism correctly identifies problems with naive Cartesian dualism, it fails to account for the specific features of consciousness (qualia, unity of experience, intentionality) that motivate interactionist dualism. The Map's position: dissolving the mind-body problem is not the same as solving it, and enactivism's inability to address the hard problem shows that dissolution is premature. 2. **Strange bedfellows: enactivism and dualism on irreducibility** — Explore the surprising convergence between Thompson's insistence on the irreducibility of experience and dualism's core commitment. Both reject eliminativism and reductive physicalism. Both insist that first-person experience is a fundamental datum. The difference is metaphysical: dualism posits a non-physical aspect of reality, while enactivism tries to locate irreducibility in biological organization. Argue that enactivism's irreducibility claim is unstable without metaphysical grounding—it either collapses into property dualism or reduces to a methodological preference. Connect to Tenet 1 and the existing [[embodied-cognition]] article.

3. **The hard problem as litmus test** — Use enactivism's struggle with the hard problem as evidence that any position acknowledging the reality of consciousness must eventually face the question of whether consciousness is reducible. Enactivism's failure to dissolve the hard problem (as critics argue) actually supports the dualist framing. The deep continuity thesis describes a correlation between life and mind but does not explain *why* living systems have subjective experience—which is precisely the gap that interactionist dualism's non-physical consciousness fills.
