## Executive Summary

Parsimony arguments are the most commonly invoked reason to prefer physicalism over dualism in consciousness science. This research finds that these arguments face severe and multiply-independent challenges. At minimum four distinct lines of critique undermine parsimony's authority in the consciousness domain: (1) philosophical analyses showing parsimony lacks universal epistemic warrant (Huemer, Sober), (2) the multidimensionality of simplicity yielding contradictory verdicts (ontological vs syntactic vs explanatory), (3) historical cases where parsimony led science astray (Mach on atoms, geocentrism, caloric theory), and (4) the specific failure condition that parsimony requires equal explanatory adequacy as a precondition — which physicalism and dualism do not share regarding consciousness. This research extends the existing research note (2026-02-06) with deeper coverage of Anderson's critique of Churchland, Lycan's surprising concessions, Masi's historical analysis, and the IIT case study.

## Key Sources

### Huemer, M. (2009). "When Is Parsimony a Virtue?"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/archive/HUEWIP.pdf
- **Type**: Journal paper (*The Philosophical Quarterly* 59(235): 216–236)
- **Key points**:
  - Reviews four contemporary accounts of why parsimony works in empirical science (likelihoodist, curve-fitting, prior probability, pragmatic)
  - Tests whether each account extends to philosophical cases — specifically physicalism vs dualism and nominalism vs realism
  - Concludes: "none of the accounts extends naturally to either of these philosophical cases"
  - In typical philosophical contexts, ontological parsimony has no evidential value
  - The assumption that the world is intrinsically more likely to be simple is questionable — complexity may be more likely
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)
- **Quote**: Parsimony's epistemic value does not extend naturally to metaphysical theory choice

### Anderson, C. J. (2002). "Can Ockham's Razor Cut Through the Mind-Body Problem?"
- **URL**: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=895730
- **Type**: Journal paper (*Journal of Theoretical and Philosophical Psychology*)
- **Key points**:
  - Critiques Churchland's (1988) "Raze Dualism" argument — the claim that materialism is superior because it is simpler
  - Shows the argument rests on three assumptions that don't withstand scrutiny:
    1. That Ockham's razor is a reliable principle (contested — see Huemer)
    2. That materialism has empirical superiority over dualism (unresolved)
    3. That materialism is the only parsimonious alternative (idealism is equally parsimonious)
  - The "Raze Dualism" argument has the form: materialism and dualism explain the same data; materialism is simpler; therefore prefer materialism. But the premises are each contestable.
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports Tenet 5; the critique of Churchland is specifically useful for the Map's arguments against materialism
- **Quote**: "The argument is undermined by assumptions which do not withstand scrutiny"

### Lycan, W. G. (2009). "Giving Dualism its Due"
- **URL**: https://www.newdualism.org/papers/W.Lycan/Du.htm
- **Type**: Journal paper (*Australasian Journal of Philosophy* 87(4))
- **Key points**:
  - Lycan is a committed materialist of 40+ years who argues that no convincing case has been made *against* substance dualism
  - On parsimony specifically: "there is no evidence against dualism; it only offends parsimony"
  - Calls parsimony "a very posterior reason" — it should only break ties when all else is equal
  - Acknowledges standard objections to dualism (interaction problem, neural dependence, parsimony) are "not very convincing"
  - Significant because Lycan concedes these points from *within* the materialist camp
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports Tenet 1 (Dualism) and Tenet 5. A hostile witness admitting the prosecution's case is weak.
- **Quote**: "There is no evidence against the view that physical events are overdetermined by physical and nonphysical causes; it only offends parsimony"

### Masi, M. (2023+). "When Occam's Razor Cuts Too Deep" / "The Obsession with Occam's Razor"
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/archive/MASTOW-2
- **Type**: PhilArchive paper (multiple versions/titles)
- **Key points**:
  - Comprehensive historical analysis of cases where Occam's razor obstructed scientific progress
  - Material monists invoke Occam to dismiss anything immaterial: "We don't need to posit anything immaterial to explain consciousness and mind"
  - But dualists appeal to the same principle defending the opposite thesis — "this attachment to parsimony does not have its roots in genuine conviction but rather in the desire to appease physicalist colleagues"
  - Historical examples from physics and evolutionary biology where strict parsimony led away from truth
  - Science repeatedly shows future data support more complex theories than existing data
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports Tenet 5; the historical examples strengthen the case that parsimony is unreliable when knowledge is incomplete
- **Quote**: "If one misses [the] complexity and does not know all the underlying laws, processes, and variables that determine a phenomenon, invoking uncritically Occam's razor will almost certainly lead to an oversimplification"

### Hubbard, J. (1995). "Parsimony and the Mind"
- **URL**: https://www.newdualism.org/papers/J.Hubbard/simple.shtml
- **Type**: Senior thesis (Macalester College)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues the principle of parsimony is not applicable to the mind-body problem
  - No clear and agreed-upon definition of "parsimony" or "simplicity" exists
  - The justification for parsimony's worth as a guiding principle is itself questionable
  - Reductionist materialism is more parsimonious than dualism *only under one definition of parsimony*
  - Defends dualism: we should acknowledge both mental states and physical matter rather than reducing one to the other
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports Tenet 5; argues the key premise of the parsimony argument (that parsimony is well-defined and justified) fails
- **Quote**: "There is no clear and agreed-upon definition of 'parsimony' or 'simplicity'"

### Smart, J. J. C. (1959). "Sensations and Brain Processes" (and retraction)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mind-identity/ (discussed in SEP entry)
- **Type**: Journal paper (*Philosophical Review* 68(2))
- **Key points**:
  - Smart championed Occam's Razor as basis for preferring mind-brain identity theory over dualism
  - Was "severely criticized" for this use of the razor
  - Ultimately retracted his advocacy of parsimony in this context
  - Key criticism: Occam's Razor is only warranted when things are *equal explanatorily* — but physicalism and dualism are not equal with respect to consciousness
  - Smart's retraction is historically significant: the most prominent philosopher to use parsimony in the mind-body debate abandoned the argument
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports Tenet 5; Smart's retraction is evidence that the parsimony argument was recognized as problematic even by its strongest proponent

### SEP Entry: "Simplicity"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/simplicity/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia entry (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes ontological simplicity (parsimony — number of entity kinds) from syntactic simplicity (elegance — number of laws/principles)
  - These dimensions can conflict: reducing ontological commitments often inflates theoretical complexity
  - Baker (2003) provides analysis of quantitative vs qualitative parsimony
  - No universal justification for simplicity as a guide to truth has been established
  - Sober's key insight: parsimony's legitimacy depends on "subject matter specific (and a posteriori) considerations"
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral resource; provides the conceptual framework for understanding why "simplicity" is not one thing

### Chalmers, D. J. (1995/1996). "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" / *The Conscious Mind*
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/facing.html
- **Type**: Foundational paper/book
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes "naturalistic dualism": consciousness as a fundamental property alongside charge, spin, etc., with new fundamental psychophysical laws
  - Acknowledges simplicity is a relevant criterion in theory construction — but consciousness requires expanding the fundamental ontology
  - Analogy: "Just as Maxwell sacrificed a simple mechanistic worldview by postulating electromagnetic fields in order to explain certain natural phenomena, we need to sacrifice a simple physicalistic worldview in order to explain consciousness"
  - The simplest theory consistent with all the data (including phenomenal data) may require dualism
  - Nature does not always favor the simplest theory: general relativity over Newtonian gravity, quantum mechanics over classical determinism
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports Tenets 1 (Dualism) and 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)
- **Quote**: "Where we have new fundamental properties, we also have new fundamental laws"

### IIT and the Parsimony Paradox (Tononi, Koch)
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/integrated-information-theory-of-consciousness/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia/multiple sources
- **Key points**:
  - IIT illustrates the tension: it claims methodological parsimony (identifying consciousness with Φ) but leads to panpsychism — ontological extravagance
  - Tononi "openly stands by panpsychism insofar as it follows from IIT"
  - The theory's parsimony at the level of principles produces complexity at the level of implications
  - Searle's critique: panpsychism "does not get up to the level of being false. It is strictly speaking meaningless"
  - Shows how parsimony at one level (axiom count) can generate anti-parsimony at another (entity attribution)
- **Tenet alignment**: Mixed. IIT's panpsychism conflicts with Tenet 1 (dualism is not panpsychism) but IIT's problems illustrate Tenet 5's point about parsimony's limits

## Major Positions

### The Parsimony Case for Physicalism
- **Proponents**: Smart (1959, later retracted), Churchland (1988), Lewis, most analytic materialists
- **Core claim**: Physicalism posits one substance; dualism posits two. By Occam's Razor, prefer the simpler ontology.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Fewer entity types = simpler theory = more likely true
  - Growing neuroscience correlations support physical explanation
  - No positive evidence for non-physical substance
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenets 1 and 5. The Map argues this is the *weakest* argument for physicalism yet the most commonly cited.

### The Critique of Parsimony in Consciousness
- **Proponents**: Huemer, Sober, Anderson, Lycan (concessions), Masi, Chalmers (implicit), Zanotti
- **Core claim**: Parsimony lacks epistemic warrant in metaphysical theory choice generally, and in the consciousness debate specifically.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. **Precondition failure**: Parsimony is a tie-breaker; physicalism and dualism do not tie on explanatory adequacy (physicalism leaves the hard problem untouched)
  2. **Multidimensionality**: "Simpler" depends on which dimension — ontological, syntactic, or explanatory — and these yield contradictory verdicts
  3. **No transfer**: Scientific justifications for parsimony (curve-fitting, likelihood) do not extend to metaphysics (Huemer 2009)
  4. **Historical unreliability**: Nature has repeatedly proven more complex than parsimonious theories predicted
  5. **Idealism parity**: Idealism is as parsimonious as physicalism (one substance), so parsimony cannot uniquely favor physicalism (Anderson 2002)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly supports Tenets 1 and 5

### Swinburne's Reversal: Dualism as Simpler
- **Proponents**: Richard Swinburne
- **Core claim**: In certain respects, dualism is *simpler* than physicalism because it posits a straightforward correlation (mind ↔ brain) rather than requiring elaborate theoretical machinery to explain consciousness away
- **Key arguments**:
  - Physicalism must explain why and how consciousness arises from physical processes — this generates enormous theoretical complexity (functionalism, higher-order theories, illusionism, etc.)
  - Dualism takes consciousness at face value; physicalism requires explaining it away
  - The proliferation of incompatible physicalist theories of consciousness is itself evidence of explanatory strain
- **Relation to site tenets**: Supports Tenets 1 and 5; this "reversal" argument is underexplored in existing Map content

## Key Debates

### The Equal Explanatory Power Precondition
- **Sides**: Physicalists (parsimony applies because both sides explain the data) vs critics (parsimony requires a tie, but there is no tie)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether physicalism and dualism explain consciousness equally well. If physicalism leaves the hard problem untouched, parsimony cannot be applied as a tie-breaker.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The hard problem's persistence after 30+ years of sustained effort suggests physicalism does *not* explain consciousness equally well.

### Quantitative vs Qualitative Parsimony
- **Sides**: Those who count entity types (qualitative — physicalism wins) vs those who count theoretical principles/mechanisms (syntactic — verdict unclear) vs those who count unexplained brute facts (explanatory — dualism may win)
- **Core disagreement**: Which dimension of simplicity is epistemically relevant for the mind-body problem
- **Current state**: No resolution. Baker (2003) and the SEP entry note the multidimensionality but offer no principled way to weight the dimensions.

### Scientific vs Metaphysical Parsimony
- **Sides**: Those who treat parsimony as domain-general (applies everywhere) vs those who treat it as domain-specific (Sober, Huemer)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the success of simplicity in empirical science (curve-fitting, model selection) transfers to metaphysical disputes where no differential predictions exist
- **Current state**: The domain-specific view has the stronger philosophical support. No published defense of domain-general parsimony has successfully addressed Huemer's critique.
