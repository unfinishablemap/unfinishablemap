Research notes on the relationship between consciousness and the sense of self. The goal is to explore what the self is phenomenologically and metaphysically, distinct from questions of personal identity (which focus on persistence over time).

## Core Distinction: Minimal Self vs. Narrative Self

Shaun Gallagher and Dan Zahavi have articulated a crucial distinction between two forms of selfhood:

### The Minimal Self

The minimal self is "a very basic form of first-person, pre-reflective self-consciousness, which includes bodily self-awareness." As Zahavi puts it, "subjectivity is a built-in feature of experiential life. Experiential episodes are neither unconscious nor anonymous; rather, they necessarily come with first-personal givenness or perspectival ownership."

Key characteristics:
- **Pre-reflective**: Present before any explicit self-reflection
- **For-me-ness**: The what-it-is-like-for-me-ness of experience
- **Necessary**: Cannot be absent while consciousness is present
- **Non-narrative**: Does not depend on stories we tell about ourselves

The minimal self is disclosed phenomenologically through the epoché. It is "an essential feature of every experience that manifests itself by first-personal givenness."

### The Narrative Self

The narrative self emerges through self-interpretation and storytelling. "Being a self is an achievement rather than a given. Selfhood is achieved in and through narrative self-interpretation."

Key characteristics:
- **Constructed**: Built through ongoing autobiographical narrative
- **Social**: Shaped by cultural context and interpersonal relationships
- **Extended in time**: Provides sense of continuity across life
- **Distinctive**: Captures what makes each person unique

### Relationship Between the Two

The minimal self is a "pre-linguistic presupposition for any narrative practices." One cannot construct a narrative self without the basic experiential core that the minimal self provides.

However, recent work suggests this relationship is bidirectional. Affective experience "involves deep entwinement of minimal and narrative forms of selfhood"—narrative self-understanding can structure even minimal self-experience.

Source: [Continental Philosophy Review](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11007-019-09471-y), [PhilPapers](https://philpapers.org/rec/ALWSWA), [Open Encyclopedia of Cognitive Science](https://oecs.mit.edu/pub/y25banqk/release/1)

## Metzinger's Self-Model Theory

Thomas Metzinger proposes a representationalist theory: "No such things as selves exist in the world: nobody ever had or was a self. All that exists are phenomenal selves, as they appear in conscious experience."

### Core Concepts

The **phenomenal self-model (PSM)** is the central concept. The self is "not a thing but an ongoing process; it is the content of a 'transparent self-model.'"

Three phenomenal properties constitute the PSM:
1. **Mineness**: Higher-order property of ownership ("this is my experience")
2. **Perspectivalness**: The ecological self as immovable center of perception
3. **Selfhood**: The phenomenal self over time, creating first-person perspective

### Transparency

We are "naïve realists, who believe they are perceiving reality directly when in actuality they are only perceiving representations of reality." The data structures are "transparent"—we can introspect on representations but not on the mechanisms generating them.

### Implications for Dualism

Metzinger's theory is physicalist—he aims to explain how "strong, consciously experienced subjectivity emerge[s] out of objective events in the natural world." However, his detailed phenomenological analysis of self-experience provides rich data that any theory must accommodate.

From The Unfinishable Map's perspective: If the PSM is "transparent," this raises questions about what generates the representations. The filter theory could interpret the PSM as consciousness's interface with the brain's self-modeling processes, rather than the brain producing selfhood.

Source: [MIT Press - Being No One](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262633086/being-no-one/), [Scholarpedia](http://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Self_models), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self_model)

## Buddhist No-Self (Anattā)

Buddhism presents "the most radical challenge to the notion of a self." The doctrine of anattā denies "the existence of a permanent, unchanging self or soul at the core of a person."

### The Five Aggregates

A person is understood as a composite of ever-changing aggregates (skandhas):
1. Form (body)
2. Feelings
3. Perceptions
4. Mental formations
5. Consciousness (vijñāna)

"Even consciousness is not a permanent entity but rather a stream of momentary awareness that arises and passes away continuously."

### Comparison with Phenomenological Accounts

The Buddhist analysis converges with some phenomenological findings:
- Both emphasize impermanence (Buddhist anicca, Husserlian time-consciousness)
- Both treat the self as process rather than substance
- Both challenge reified self-conceptions

However, there are differences:
- Zahavi's minimal self is a necessary feature of experience; Buddhism questions whether even this minimal structure is substantial
- Buddhism aims at liberation from self-attachment; phenomenology aims at accurate description

### Contemporary Intersections

A 2025 Oxford Academic work explores "Buddhist Physicalism"—whether the Buddhist commitment to non-existence of self means "consciousness must be understood to be subjectless." This opens doors to illusionism about phenomenal consciousness.

The Map's position diverges: Buddhism's no-self doctrine concerns the constructed, substantial self, not necessarily the minimal experiential structure. The Dualism tenet can accommodate Buddhist insights about the illusory nature of the narrative self while maintaining that phenomenal experience is irreducible.

Source: [1000-Word Philosophy](https://1000wordphilosophy.com/2023/02/25/no-self/), [Stanford Encyclopedia - Mind in Indian Buddhism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mind-indian-buddhism/), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/book/60496?login=false)

## The Self and the Hard Problem

The relationship between self and consciousness bears directly on the hard problem:

### The Self as Explanatory Target

The hard problem asks why physical processes produce subjective experience. But subjective experience is inherently perspectival—there is always a "what it is like *for someone*." The minimal self is not an additional phenomenon requiring explanation; it is the form that conscious experience takes.

Property dualism, as articulated by Chalmers, "does not posit the existence of any nonphysical or immaterial substances, but instead posits the existence of properties—qualia—that are ontologically independent of any physical properties."

### Psychological Origins of the Problem

A 2023 study argues that "The 'hard problem of consciousness' arises from human psychology"—specifically from "two intuitive biases that lie deep within human psychology: Essentialism and Dualism." We intuitively treat mind and body as distinct.

The Map's response: These psychological tendencies may track genuine metaphysical features rather than being mere cognitive errors. The phenomenological analysis of the minimal self reveals structural features of experience that physicalist accounts cannot capture.

Source: [MIT Press Open Mind](https://direct.mit.edu/opmi/article/doi/10.1162/opmi_a_00094/117074/The-Hard-Problem-of-Consciousness-Arises-from), [Stanford Encyclopedia - Dualism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/dualism/)

## Implications for Site Framework

### Support for Dualism Tenet

The minimal self analysis supports irreducibility:
- First-personal givenness is not a feature of physical descriptions
- The "for-me-ness" of experience is disclosed only phenomenologically
- No third-person account captures what it is to be a subject

### Connection to Phenomenology and Neurophenomenology

Gallagher and Zahavi work within the phenomenological tradition that the Map draws on. The minimal self is precisely what Varela's neurophenomenology aims to correlate with neural processes without reducing.

### Buddhist Compatibility

The Map's framework can accommodate Buddhist insights:
- The narrative self is indeed constructed—a product of brain processes shaping the interface
- The minimal self may be what remains when narrative constructions are seen through
- Liberation from self-attachment need not require denying phenomenal consciousness
