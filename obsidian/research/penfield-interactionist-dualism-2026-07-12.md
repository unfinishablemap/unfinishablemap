## Executive Summary

Wilder Penfield (1891–1976), founder of the Montreal Neurological Institute, performed direct electrical stimulation of the cortex in conscious epilepsy patients across thousands of awake craniotomies. Two clinical regularities anchor his late-career philosophy: (1) stimulation reliably evokes movements, sensations, and vivid experiential "flashbacks," yet patients consistently *disown* the evoked movement ("I didn't do that — you did"), sharply distinguishing it from action they own; and (2) no stimulation site ever produced a decision, a belief, or an act of will — only sub-components of behaviour, never agency itself. In *The Mystery of the Mind* (Princeton University Press, 1975) Penfield reported that these findings reversed his early physicalist expectation and led him to an interactionist dualism: the mind is a distinct "element" that acts through, but is not identical to, the brain's "highest mechanism," analogized as a programmer directing a computer. This makes Penfield an unusually *empirically grounded* interactionist — a cousin to the Eccles mechanism already heavily threaded in the corpus, and congenial to Map Tenets 1 (dualism) and 3 (bidirectional mind→brain action). But the inference is **suggestive, not decisive**: the modern comparator/efference-copy model of the sense of agency gives the disowned-movement datum a fully physicalist reading (evoked movement lacks the predictive efference copy that normally generates ownership), so Penfield's evidence *underdetermines* dualism versus the comparator account. Treat as framework-relative (evidential-status discipline).

## Major Positions

### Penfield's late interactionist dualism
- **Proponent**: Wilder Penfield (reversing his own earlier materialism).
- **Core claim**: The mind is a distinct essence/element that acts *through* the brain's highest mechanism without being identical to it; mind programs the brain-computer. Free will / decision is not localizable to any stimulable cortical site.
- **Key arguments** (empirical, from the clinical series):
  1. **Agency/ownership asymmetry.** Stimulation of motor cortex produces movement the patient *disowns* ("I didn't do that — you did it"), categorically unlike willed action the patient owns. UNVERIFIED verbatim; robustly attested in paraphrase across sources.
  2. **No cortical centre for the will.** Across a very large series, stimulation evoked movements, sensations, and memories — never a decision. There is (per Penfield) no site where an electrode makes the patient *decide*. UNVERIFIED verbatim.
  3. **Compulsion of body, never of belief.** Stimulation can make a patient turn the head or vocalize, but cannot make the patient *believe* or *decide*. Penfield reports never stimulating reasoning, abstract thought, or the intellect. UNVERIFIED verbatim.
  - Inference: the mind's characteristic operations (deciding, believing, reasoning) are *absent from the map of stimulable functions*, which Penfield reads as evidence they are not products of the highest brain-mechanism alone.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong prima facie fit with **Tenet 1** (consciousness/will not reducible to physical mechanism) and **Tenet 3** (mind exercises downward, bidirectional causal influence — the "programmer" directs the "computer"). Penfield is valuable to the Map precisely because his is a *clinical*, first-person-surgical case for the will's irreducibility rather than an armchair one.

### The comparator-model physicalist reading (rival)
- **Proponents**: mainstream cognitive neuroscience of agency (Frith, Blakemore, Wolpert; Synofzik et al.).
- **Core claim**: The disowned-evoked-movement asymmetry is exactly what a purely physical forward/comparator model predicts. Ownership tracks the presence of a matching efference copy; electrode-evoked movement bypasses the motor-command pathway, so no efference copy is generated, so the movement is (correctly) not self-attributed.
- **Why it bites**: It converts Penfield's headline datum from "metaphysical proof of a non-physical will" into "an empirical fact about the comparator/prediction machinery." Absence of a *stimulable* will-site is likewise consistent with the will being a *distributed, prediction-dependent process* rather than a non-physical element.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A rival monist reading; the Map should present it and concede that Penfield's evidence does **not** discriminate dualism from this account. (Evidential-status discipline.)

### Companion: the Eccles interactionist mechanism (already in corpus)
- **Contrast**: Eccles (with Popper, and later the psychon/dendron quantum-probability-field proposal) offered a *mechanism* for mind→brain action at the synapse; Penfield offered *clinical evidence* of the will's non-localizability but explicitly declined to propose a mechanism. They are complementary neuroscientist-dualists — Eccles the mechanism side, Penfield the phenomenological/clinical side. The SEP lineage sentence names them together.
- **Map relevance**: The Map already threads Eccles heavily (interface-problem, psychophysical-control-law, amplification-mechanisms clusters). Penfield supplies an *independent, non-quantum* clinical buttress for the same Tenet-1/3 commitments — an empirical foothold that does not ride on the contested quantum-interface machinery. ## Key Debates

### Does non-stimulability of the will show it is non-physical?
- **Sides**: Penfield (yes — the will is not a product of the stimulable brain) vs. comparator-model physicalism (no — non-stimulability and disownership follow from prediction-based agency machinery).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the *absence of the will from the stimulation map* is metaphysically diagnostic or merely reflects the architecture of a physical agency system (distributed, prediction-dependent, not point-stimulable).
- **Current state**: Ongoing / underdetermined. The clinical facts are not seriously contested; their interpretation is. The Map's honest position: the evidence is *congenial* to Tenets 1/3 but does not by itself defeat the physicalist reading.

### How much does the "programmer / computer" metaphor commit one to?
- **Sides**: Read as strong substance dualism vs. read as a heuristic for a levels/control distinction.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether Penfield's own framing is a metaphysical thesis or a placeholder for an unexplained interaction he deferred to future science.
- **Current state**: Penfield himself framed the book as a *negative* argument and left the interaction mechanism open — so over-reading the metaphor is a live risk for any article.

## Map-Relevance (Tenets 1 + 3)

- **Why it belongs on the Map**: Penfield is a rare *empirically grounded* interactionist — a neurosurgeon whose case for the irreducibility of the will and for mind→brain (bidirectional) action rests on a very large clinical series, not on conceivability arguments. That is directly on-tenet for Tenets 1 and 3, and it diversifies the Map's interactionist evidence base beyond the quantum-interface / Eccles line.
