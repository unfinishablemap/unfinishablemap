## Executive Summary

Jonathan Birch's *The Edge of Sentience* (Oxford University Press, 2024) installs a precautionary framework with a two-tier evidential categorisation (**sentience candidate** / **investigation priority**), an operational five-of-eight indicator threshold inherited from Birch et al. (2021) for animals capable of nociception, three foundational principles (avoid gratuitous suffering; sentience candidature carries moral weight; democratic deliberation determines proportionate precaution), and an AI-specific **run-ahead principle**. The framework is studiously neutral on metaphysics — Birch gives non-materialist views ~10% — and is designed to deliver policy under irreducible philosophical disagreement rather than to settle which beings are sentient. It is parallel rather than substitutive to the Map's five-tier calibration scale (*established → strongly supported → realistic possibility → live hypothesis → speculative integration*): the Map's scale tracks *what the catalogue is entitled to claim*; Birch's scheme tracks *what action is warranted under uncertainty*. The two converge at the "realistic possibility" tier — both schemes inherit the New York Declaration's language — and diverge at the action layer (Birch demands proportionate precaution; the Map demands honest labelling). The complementarity is structural: Birch is a *welfare-action* framework that says nothing about what should be written; the Map is a *catalogue-prose* framework that says nothing about what should be done. Both operate against the same failure mode (*defeater-removal silently treated as evidence-elevation*), and Birch's two-tier scheme can be read as a coarsened version of the Map's middle three tiers.

## Birch's Framework Structure (Detailed)

### Three Foundational Principles

1. **Avoid gratuitous suffering** — the duty grounding all welfare obligation
2. **Sentience candidature is morally relevant** — that an entity is a candidate (not certain) is enough to ground precautionary obligation
3. **Democratic deliberation determines proportionality** — citizens' assemblies, not expert commissions, decide what counts as proportionate precaution; aimed at avoiding the "tyranny of expert values"

### Two-Tier Evidential Categorisation

Birch operates with three categories along an evidential continuum:

| Category | Definition (paraphrased) | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Sentient (recognised)** | Strong scientific consensus; near-universal agreement among signatories of major declarations | Mammals, birds, octopuses (Cambridge 2012; NY 2024) |
| **Sentience candidate** | Realistic possibility, evidence base rich enough to identify welfare risks and design precautions | Cephalopods broadly, decapods, insects, many other invertebrates |
| **Investigation priority** | Could plausibly be sentient with more research; affected by human activity such that welfare risks would arise if sentient | *C. elegans*, possibly some plants on permissive readings, brain organoids without brainstem tissue |

**Reasonable disagreement on placement is expected** — the framework is designed to function despite this. Different reasoners will draw the lines differently, but the *categories themselves* allow disagreement to be substantive rather than purely verbal.

### Operational Five-of-Eight Indicator Rule

Inherited from the 2021 UK report on cephalopods and decapods, Birch's framework identifies eight indicators of pain-sentience:

**Neurobiological criteria:**
1. Nociceptors (receptors specifically responding to harmful stimuli)
2. Integrative brain regions (capable of combining sensory information)
3. Integrated nociception (neural pathways linking nociceptors to integrative regions)
4. Analgesia (behavioural responses to harmful stimuli modulated by chemical compounds)

**Behavioural criteria:**
5. Motivational trade-offs (dynamic decision-making weighing injury costs against rewards)
6. Flexible self-protection (adaptive protective behaviour like wound tending)
7. Associative learning (forming connections between harmful stimuli and neutral cues)
8. Analgesia preference (preferring analgesics/anaesthetics when injured)

**Threshold rule**: Precautionary measures are warranted for animals when there is high or very high confidence that they satisfy at least five of the eight criteria. This is explicitly not a sentience guarantee but an action-trigger that tolerates false positives to reduce false negatives.

### The Run-Ahead Principle (AI-Specific)

For AI sentience, Birch proposes: **at any given time, measures to regulate the development of sentient AI should run ahead of what would be proportionate to the risks posed by current technology, considering also the risks posed by credible future trajectories.**

Operational corollary: a licensing scheme for companies attempting to create artificial sentience candidates, or whose work creates even a small risk of doing so. Licenses depend on subscription to a code of good practice that includes norms of transparency.

### The Gaming Problem (LLM-Specific)

Birch identifies a structural epistemological trap with LLMs: they may be selected (by their training process) to *produce* the markers that humans use to attribute sentience, without those markers being load-bearing for any underlying experience. Crabs cannot strategically game our intuitions; LLMs can. This means the eight-indicator framework cannot transfer directly to AI — the markers no longer track what they track in evolved animals. Birch advocates running the precautionary framework anyway, but on different grounds: future trajectories, opaque architectures, and the asymmetric-risk logic.

## Major Positions

### Position: Birch's Precautionary Framework

- **Proponent**: Jonathan Birch
- **Core claim**: Action under sentience-uncertainty must be guided by precaution and proportionality, not by certainty; the question "is the entity sentient?" should be replaced by "is it a sentience candidate?"
- **Key arguments**:
  - Asymmetric-risk: false negatives (denying sentience to sentient beings) cause worse harms than false positives
  - Reasonable disagreement is irreducible; we still need policy
  - Democratic deliberation is preferable to expert dictatorship for proportionality decisions
  - Operational thresholds (five-of-eight, sentience-candidate criteria) make the framework actionable
- **Relation to site tenets**:
  - **Tenet 1 (Dualism)**: Birch is neutral; gives non-materialist views ~10%. The framework is *compatible* with dualism but does not presuppose it. Birch is the canonical example of a metaphysics-neutral interlocutor whose framework the Map can adopt without requiring tenet-import.
  - **Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)**: Birch's facilitation hypothesis is explicitly anti-epiphenomenalist (consciousness facilitates cognitive abilities relative to unconscious processing); aligns with Tenet 3.
  - **Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)**: The asymmetric-risk argument and the precautionary stance are exactly anti-parsimony at the action level — denying that simplicity arguments can dismiss the possibility of sentience in cognitively unfamiliar systems. Strong tenet alignment without tenet-import.

### Position: Schwitzgebel-Sinnott-Armstrong Critique

- **Proponents**: Eric Schwitzgebel, Walter Sinnott-Armstrong
- **Core claim**: The precautionary framework has both methodological and operational problems that Birch (and Sebo, Keane) avoid by retreating from radical implications
- **Key arguments**:
  - Conflicting precautionary principles: caution-against-harm vs caution-for-liberty
  - Vague threshold: why insects pass and plants do not is contested
  - Citizens' panels can't be simultaneously deliberative, representative, informed, and timely
  - The aggregation problem: if insects have any sentience and vastly outnumber humans, their welfare could swamp human welfare — yet authors do not endorse this
- **Relation to site tenets**: Methodologically friendly; the critique is at the operational layer, not the metaphysical layer. The Map's five-tier scale partly addresses problem 2 (vague threshold) by using a more fine-grained calibration; addresses problem 1 (conflict) by separating *labelling* from *action* (Birch conflates them).

### Position: O'Brien's Public Review

- **Proponent**: O'Brien (3 Quarks Daily, Jan 2025)
- **Core claim**: Birch's framework is admirable public-facing philosophy; the democratic-deliberation component is the weakest link
- **Key arguments**:
  - Praise: clarity, repeated principle-restatement, the productive sentience-candidate concept
  - Critique: human-only democratic procedures lack standing to decide non-human welfare; Birch's confidence in "morally responsible and reasonable government" is unrealistic
- **Relation to site tenets**: Within-framework critique; preserves Birch's overall structure

### Position: Singh, Pinto, et al. — Refinement of the Eight Criteria

- **Core claim**: The 2021 framework is defensible but should be refined: distinguish absence of evidence from evidence of absence; weight criteria by evidential value; use phylogenetically informed analysis
- **Relation to site tenets**: Methodological refinement; aligns directly with Map's evidential-status discipline (the refinements are themselves anti-slippage moves at the indicator-counting layer)

## Key Debates

### Debate: Two-Tier vs Multi-Tier Calibration

- **Sides**: Birch (two evidential tiers + recognised sentient) vs the New York Declaration / Map five-tier scale
- **Core disagreement**: How fine-grained should the evidential calibration be?
- **Current state**: Both schemes coexist in the literature. The NY Declaration's "strong scientific support" / "realistic possibility" language is closer to a three-tier scheme; the Map's five-tier scheme adds *live hypothesis* and *speculative integration* below "realistic possibility" to handle simple-organism cases (nematodes, Hydra, slime molds) that Birch would group as investigation priorities. The Map's scheme is therefore *more conservative at the lower tiers* but inherits Birch's cap of "strongly supported" / "realistic possibility" at the upper tiers.

### Debate: Action vs Description

- **Sides**: Birch's framework is action-oriented (what should we do under uncertainty?); the Map's evidential-status discipline is description-oriented (what should we *write* under uncertainty?)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the calibration scheme's primary role is to license welfare interventions or to discipline catalogue prose
- **Current state**: This is the core complementarity. Birch needs a labelling layer he doesn't fully provide; the Map needs an action layer it doesn't claim to provide. Each scheme *implies* but does not *contain* the other.

### Debate: The Gaming Problem and AI Indicators

- **Sides**: Birch (precaution applies despite gaming risk; run-ahead principle compensates) vs. critics who argue the gaming problem makes any indicator-based scheme useless for AI
- **Core disagreement**: Whether behavioural indicators can ever be load-bearing for AI sentience attribution given selection pressures on LLMs to produce them
- **Current state**: Open. The Map's five-tier scale can read the gaming problem as a reason to keep AI claims at *live hypothesis* or *speculative integration* tiers — Birch's framework cannot easily do this because its categorisation is binary (candidate / not).

### Debate: Democratic vs Expert Determination of Proportionality

- **Sides**: Birch (citizens' assemblies, to avoid "tyranny of expert values") vs critics (Schwitzgebel; O'Brien) who argue democratic procedures will privilege established human interests
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the political layer of the framework can be made fair to non-human welfare candidates
- **Current state**: Substantively unresolved. The Map's framework does not make this decision; it operates upstream of the action question.

## Comparison: Birch's Framework vs the Map's Five-Tier Scale

### Structural Mapping

| Map Tier | Birch Equivalent | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Established** | "Recognised sentient" | Strong scientific consensus; major declarations |
| **Strongly supported** | (Recognised sentient, weaker) | Substantial evidence; broad agreement |
| **Realistic possibility, contested** | Sentience candidate | Realistic non-negligible possibility; rich enough evidence base |
| **Live hypothesis** | Investigation priority | Could plausibly be sentient with more research |
| **Speculative integration** | (Below investigation priority) | Tenet-coherent but not evidence-supported; Birch has no formal label |

### Convergences

- **Asymmetric-risk reasoning**: Both schemes treat false negatives as worse than false positives at the relevant layer (Birch at action; the Map at labelling)
- **Modal/evidential separation**: Birch's "credible non-negligible possibility" is the same conceptual move as the Map's distinction between *tenet register* and *evidence register* — possibility is not probability
- **Anti-parsimony at the boundary**: Both reject Occam's-razor dismissal of sentience candidates (the Map via Tenet 5; Birch via the sentience-candidate concept's design)
- **NY Declaration heritage**: Both inherit the "realistic possibility" language as a tier label; this is the cleanest verbal bridge

### Divergences

- **Layer of operation**: Birch operates at the action layer (welfare policy, regulation, licensing); the Map operates at the description layer (article prose, evidential calibration)
- **Granularity**: Birch is two-tier (with a third "recognised" category); the Map is five-tier
- **Treatment of speculative cases**: Birch's framework declines to engage at the "speculative integration" tier — entities below "investigation priority" are simply not in the framework's scope. The Map *names* this tier explicitly to discipline catalogue language about Hydra, slime molds, biofilms.
- **Metaphysical commitment**: Birch is studiously neutral; the Map's framework is tenet-installed (Dualism, Bidirectional Interaction, Occam's-Razor-Has-Limits) and explicit about it
- **Democratic deliberation**: Birch makes citizens' assemblies a core component; the Map has no analogue
- **AI handling**: Birch installs the run-ahead principle and the gaming problem as AI-specific moves; the Map currently has no AI-specific calibration extension

### Where the Schemes Disagree on Specific Verdicts

- **Insects**: Birch — sentience candidates; Map — *realistic possibility, contested*. Substantively the same.
- **Decapods, cephalopods**: Birch — sentience candidates (close to recognised); Map — *strongly supported* (cephalopods) / *realistic possibility, contested* (decapods). Substantively close.
- ***C. elegans***: Birch — investigation priority; Map — *live hypothesis* sliding toward *speculative integration*. The Map is *more conservative*: it labels the case at the lower end of the live-hypothesis tier and explicitly notes the negative empirical findings (failed trace conditioning, multimodal integration, self-other distinction).
- **Hydra, slime molds**: Birch — likely below investigation priority (no neurons; no associative learning); Map — *speculative integration*. The Map's lower tier picks up exactly what Birch's framework excludes.
- **LLMs (current)**: Birch — gaming-problem caveat; not formal sentience candidates yet; Map — currently does not place AI on the calibration scale (gap identified in [[reviews/optimistic-2026-05-05]]). Birch's run-ahead principle suggests action despite non-candidate status; the Map could read this as "treat AI claims at *live hypothesis* even when current evidence places them lower, because of asymmetric trajectory risk."

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, an article could:

1. **Structural-comparison article** (recommended). Build a single topic article that lays out Birch's two-tier scheme alongside the Map's five-tier scale, showing exactly where the schemes converge (NY Declaration heritage; modal/evidential separation; asymmetric-risk logic), where they diverge (granularity; layer of operation; metaphysical neutrality), and where they make different verdicts on specific cases (*C. elegans*, Hydra, slime molds, LLMs). The article's value is in the *complementarity* claim: the Map's scheme handles description; Birch's scheme handles action; the Map needs an action layer; Birch needs a description layer.

2. **Tenet-import angle**. Show how Birch's framework can be adopted *without importing dualism*, then trace what changes when the Map's tenets are added: at the action layer, very little (Birch's framework is tenet-stable); at the description layer, the Map's tenets add the *live hypothesis* and *speculative integration* tiers below investigation priority, where Birch's framework is silent. This frames the Map as Birch+tenets rather than Birch-rival.

3. **The action layer the Map currently lacks**. Frame Birch's framework as the answer to the question the Map's evidential-status discipline opens but does not close: *given our calibrated labels, what action is warranted?* The Map currently outputs catalogue prose; Birch's framework outputs welfare policy. An article could install proportionate-precaution language inside the Map's broader ethics section without upgrading evidential labels.

4. **Critique-comparison angle**. Use Schwitzgebel-Sinnott-Armstrong's four problems as a stress test for both Birch and the Map. The conflict-of-precautions problem (1) and the suppressed-radical-implications problem (4) bite both frameworks. The vague-boundaries problem (2) bites Birch more; the citizens'-panel problem (3) bites Birch but not the Map. The article's value is in showing how the Map's calibration can be more honest about lower-tier claims while still vulnerable to the same higher-order critiques.

5. **The gaming problem and AI calibration extension**. Use Birch's gaming-problem analysis as the entry point for an extension of the Map's five-tier scale to AI. The current optimistic-review (2026-05-05) flagged that the Map has not applied the calibration to `topics/ai-consciousness.md`. Birch's gaming-problem analysis suggests a default cap at *live hypothesis* / *speculative integration* for AI claims even when behavioural markers are present, on the grounds that the markers are not load-bearing in selected systems.
