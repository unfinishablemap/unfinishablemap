# Research: Completeness in Physics — EPR, Bell, and "Partial but Complete"

**Date**: 2026-03-14
**Search queries used**: EPR argument completeness quantum mechanics, Bell's theorem completeness physics local hidden variables, Kochen-Specker theorem contextuality, PBR theorem wavefunction ontic epistemic, Fine prism models Bell theorem, "partial but complete" quantum mechanics philosophy, notions of completeness EPR discussion, Newtonian mechanics complete within domain partial description

## Executive Summary

The word "completeness" in quantum foundations carries at least six distinct meanings, and conflation between them has fuelled eighty years of talking past one another. Einstein's EPR argument (1935) challenged quantum mechanics as incomplete because it lacks counterparts for every "element of reality." Bell's theorem (1964) closed the local-hidden-variable escape route. The Kochen-Specker theorem (1967) blocked non-contextual value assignments. The PBR theorem (2012) established the wavefunction as ontic rather than merely epistemic. Together, these results constrain what "completing" quantum mechanics could mean — and, crucially for the Map, they leave open the possibility that quantum mechanics is *complete about structure* while remaining *partial about actuality*. This "partial but complete" framing — a theory that exhausts its domain without exhausting reality — has a clear precedent in Newtonian mechanics and aligns with the Map's position that consciousness acts at the point where quantum description falls silent.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "The EPR Argument in Quantum Theory"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-epr/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - EPR defines completeness as: "every element of the physical reality must have a counterpart in the physical theory"
  - The argument's logical core is a disjunction: either QM is incomplete OR incompatible observables cannot have simultaneous reality
  - EPR uses the criterion of reality: if a quantity can be predicted with certainty without disturbing the system, it corresponds to an element of reality
  - Bohr's response redefines what counts as "disturbing" — measurement on one system changes the conditions defining possible predictions for the distant system
  - Einstein preferred a simpler argument from Born completeness (probabilistic descriptions are inherently incomplete) over the EPR paper's technical apparatus
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral — EPR's concern is about hidden variables, not consciousness; but the incompleteness claim aligns with the Map's view that physics leaves something out
- **Quote**: "According to their criterion, since either one of two incompatible quantities...can be predicted with certainty...both have simultaneous reality. But no quantum state can be a simultaneous eigenstate for both."

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Bell's Theorem"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/bell-theorem/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Bell showed that no local hidden-variable theory can reproduce all quantum predictions
  - The theorem requires factorizability (parameter independence + outcome independence), not outcome determinism per se
  - Fine (1982) proved that local deterministic models, local stochastic models, Bell inequalities, and existence of joint distributions are all equivalent
  - After Bell, the live options are: nonlocality (Bohm), contextuality, superdeterminism, abandon common cause principle, or many-worlds
  - Bell himself noted the de Broglie-Bohm theory as proof that hidden-variable theories *tout court* cannot be ruled out — only local ones
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with No Many Worlds (Bell's theorem is often used to motivate MWI; the Map must engage with this). Aligns with Bidirectional Interaction (Bell leaves open nonlocal influence). Neutral on consciousness specifically.
- **Quote**: Bell: "Either the wavefunction...is not everything, or it is not right."

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "The Kochen-Specker Theorem"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kochen-specker/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Proves that no non-contextual hidden-variable theory can assign definite values to all observables (in Hilbert spaces of dimension ≥ 3)
  - Requires three assumptions: value definiteness (VD), noncontextuality (NC), and correspondence between properties and projection operators
  - Unlike Bell, KS is purely mathematical — no locality assumption needed
  - The theorem forces a choice: abandon comprehensive value definiteness or embrace contextuality
  - Modal interpretations accept "partial value definiteness" as a response
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Minimal Quantum Interaction — KS shows that values are not pre-existing but emerge through measurement context, exactly where consciousness might participate
- **Quote**: "The KS theorem establishes contextuality to be an inevitable feature of any hidden-variable theory that recovers quantum predictions."

### Pusey, Barrett & Rudolph — "On the reality of the quantum state" (2012)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1111.3328 (original); see also https://www.emergentmind.com/topics/pusey-barrett-rudolph-pbr-theorem
- **Type**: Paper / overview
- **Key points**:
  - Proves: if preparation independence holds, then distinct quantum states must correspond to non-overlapping probability distributions over ontic states — the wavefunction is ψ-ontic
  - Key assumption: preparation independence (independently prepared systems have independent ontic states)
  - Rules out ψ-epistemic models where the wavefunction merely represents incomplete knowledge of a deeper reality
  - Does NOT rule out Bohmian-type theories where ψ is part of the ontic state alongside hidden variables
  - Dubbed "the most important result in quantum foundations since Bell" by some commentators
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports "partial but complete" — if ψ is ontic (real), then quantum mechanics is not merely an epistemic shorthand for something deeper. It is a genuine description of reality, yet one that (per the measurement problem) does not tell us which outcome becomes actual
- **Quote**: "Any model in which a quantum state represents mere information about an underlying physical state...must make predictions that contradict those of quantum theory."

### Fine — "Hidden Variables, Joint Probability, and the Bell Inequalities" (1982)
- **URL**: https://link.aps.org/doi/10.1103/PhysRevLett.48.291
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Proves the equivalence of five statements: (1) deterministic hidden-variable model exists, (2) factorizable stochastic model exists, (3) joint distribution for all observables exists, (4) compatible joint distributions for all pairs/triples exist, (5) Bell inequalities hold
  - This means Bell inequality violations rule out *all* of these simultaneously — not just deterministic hidden variables but any local model whatsoever
  - Fine later developed "prism models" attempting to construct local models by allowing detector inefficiency to filter results
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral — technical result constraining the space of possible completions
- **Quote**: Fine showed "there is a deterministic hidden-variables model [if and only if] Bell inequalities hold."

### Fine — Prism Models
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/philosophy-of-science/article/abs/fines-prism-models-for-quantum-correlation-statistics/61D05439CC4C1F15C57FB13DCE26E8F8
- **Type**: Paper (critical analysis)
- **Key points**:
  - Prism models attempt to reproduce quantum statistics locally by allowing some detection events to be filtered ("prism" metaphor for selective detection)
  - Criticisms: (1) additional measurement settings reveal unacceptably high rejection rates, (2) models are only superficially local and deterministic, (3) they resurrect the conceptual problems they aimed to solve
  - Largely regarded as unsuccessful — quantum nonlocality resists the prism escape
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral — demonstrates the difficulty of maintaining locality, which indirectly supports the Map's position that nonlocal influence (including by consciousness) should not be dismissed

### Adan, Barbosa, Pieczarelli — "Notions of Completeness in the EPR Discussion" (2023)
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10137958/
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Identifies six distinct notions of completeness in the EPR literature: theory completeness, bijective completeness, Born completeness, Schrödinger completeness, ψ-completeness, and predictable completeness
  - Einstein retreated from the EPR argument to Born completeness (demanding non-probabilistic descriptions) by 1936
  - The conflation of these notions has "hindered progress" in the debate for decades
  - ψ-completeness (ontic state ≅ Hilbert space) is often confused with bijective completeness (reality-to-theory mapping) — they are distinct
  - Einstein's concern was ultimately that probabilistic description of individual systems indicates the theory doesn't capture everything
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly relevant — the Map's "partial but complete" position requires precisely this kind of conceptual hygiene about which sense of completeness is at issue

## Major Positions

### "Quantum Mechanics Is Incomplete" (Einstein/EPR)
- **Proponents**: Einstein, Podolsky, Rosen; de Broglie; Bohm (in a different way)
- **Core claim**: QM's inability to assign definite values to all observables simultaneously means it is missing something — "hidden variables" that would complete the description
- **Key arguments**: EPR criterion of reality; the statistical character of QM suggests ensemble description, not individual description; the gunpowder thought experiment (after one year, the powder either exploded or didn't — QM gives only probabilities)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partially aligns — the Map agrees that QM is not the whole story, but disagrees about *what* is missing. Einstein wanted more physics (hidden variables). The Map says what's missing is not physics but the role of consciousness in actualising outcomes.

### "Quantum Mechanics Is Complete" (Copenhagen/Orthodox)
- **Proponents**: Bohr, Heisenberg, Born, Pauli; most textbook quantum mechanics
- **Core claim**: The wavefunction is the complete description. Questions about "what really happens" between measurements are meaningless. Complementarity forbids simultaneous attribution of incompatible properties.
- **Key arguments**: Bohr's complementarity principle; the measurement problem is dissolved rather than solved; asking for hidden variables misunderstands the theory
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction — if QM is complete and consciousness is outside QM, consciousness has no role. Also conflicts with Dualism if "complete" means "nothing else exists."

### "Quantum Mechanics Is Complete but Nonlocal" (Post-Bell mainstream)
- **Proponents**: Most contemporary physicists; Aspect, Zeilinger, Clauser (Nobel 2022)
- **Core claim**: Bell experiments confirm QM predictions. QM is complete. The price is nonlocality (or at minimum, non-separability). There are no hidden variables to find.
- **Key arguments**: Experimental violations of Bell inequalities; the PBR theorem (wavefunction is ontic); Kochen-Specker (no non-contextual value assignment)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partially aligns — accepts nonlocality (consistent with the Map's openness to nonlocal consciousness interaction). But "complete" here often means "there is nothing more to the physical story," which conflicts with Dualism.

### "Quantum Mechanics Is Complete for Its Domain but Partial" (Map's position)
- **Proponents**: Implicitly: von Neumann (consciousness causes collapse); Wigner; Stapp; the Map
- **Core claim**: QM accurately describes structural/probabilistic features of physical systems. It is not missing *physical* information. But it is silent on *which outcome becomes actual* at measurement — and that is where consciousness operates. The theory is complete about what it addresses; it simply does not address everything.
- **Key arguments**: The measurement problem (QM predicts superpositions, not definite outcomes); PBR shows the wavefunction is real (not just ignorance); KS shows values emerge in context (not pre-existing); Bell closes local hidden variables. Together: QM is genuine, not a placeholder for better physics — but it has a structural gap at measurement that is not a deficiency in physics but an opening for non-physical causation.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly supports Dualism (something beyond physics needed), Bidirectional Interaction (consciousness selects outcomes), Minimal Quantum Interaction (the gap is specifically at quantum indeterminacy), and No Many Worlds (rejecting the "all outcomes happen" escape).

## Key Debates

### "Complete" vs. "Complete for Its Domain"
- **Sides**: Copenhagen/orthodox (complete period) vs. Map-aligned (complete within physics, partial about reality)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "complete" means "nothing more exists" or "nothing more *of this kind* exists." The Map's position requires disambiguating completeness: QM exhaustively describes quantum structure without exhaustively describing reality.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The six-fold distinction identified by Adan et al. (2023) shows the debate remains conceptually muddled.

### Local Hidden Variables vs. Nonlocality
- **Sides**: Einstein (local realism) vs. Bell/experiments (nonlocality or contextuality)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether correlations require local causal explanation (hidden variables) or whether nature is fundamentally nonlocal
- **Current state**: Resolved experimentally — Bell inequalities are violated. Local hidden variables are ruled out. But the *interpretation* of this (nonlocality? contextuality? retrocausality? many-worlds?) remains open.

### Wavefunction: Ontic or Epistemic?
- **Sides**: ψ-ontic (PBR, most realists) vs. ψ-epistemic (QBism, some information-theoretic approaches)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the wavefunction directly represents reality or merely encodes an observer's knowledge/expectations
- **Current state**: PBR strongly favours ψ-ontic (given preparation independence). But ψ-epistemic views survive by rejecting that assumption or redefining "ontic."

### The Measurement Problem as Physical Gap vs. Conceptual Artefact
- **Sides**: Realists/dualists (genuine gap — something selects outcomes) vs. pragmatists/many-worlds (apparent gap — artefact of description)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the transition from superposition to definite outcome requires explanation beyond physics
- **Current state**: Unresolved — the central open problem in quantum foundations.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, an article could:

1. **"Partial but Complete: What Quantum Mechanics Does and Doesn't Describe"** — Develop the Map's distinctive position that QM is complete *as physics* while remaining partial *as ontology*. Ground this in the no-go theorems: Bell closes local hidden variables, KS closes non-contextual values, PBR shows the wavefunction is real. Therefore QM is not an incomplete sketch of deeper physics — it is a complete sketch of physical structure. But the measurement problem shows it is silent on actuality: which outcome *becomes real*. This is where the Map locates consciousness. Einstein was right that QM is incomplete in the Born sense (it gives only probabilities for individual events). Bohr was right that QM is complete in the predictive sense (no local hidden variables will improve it). The Map adds: QM is complete in the structural sense and partial in the ontological sense — the gap is not physics-shaped but consciousness-shaped.

## Gaps in Research

- **Retrocausal approaches**: The Map already has research on retrocausal selection; the completeness framing should be cross-referenced with that work
- **QBism and completeness**: QBism (Quantum Bayesianism) treats QM as complete in a pragmatist sense — "complete for agents" rather than "complete for reality." This deserves engagement as a sophisticated alternative to the Map's position
- **Consciousness-collapse formalisations**: How precisely does the "partial but complete" framing connect to specific consciousness-collapse proposals (von Neumann-Wigner, Stapp, Orch OR)? The research notes establish the philosophical scaffolding but the technical interface needs further development
- **Fine's prism models in detail**: The critical literature on prism models was accessible only in abstract; a full engagement would strengthen the case that locality-preserving completions fail
- **Hardy's theorem (1992)**: Another no-go result showing QM cannot be completed with local realism, even for non-maximally entangled states — not covered here
