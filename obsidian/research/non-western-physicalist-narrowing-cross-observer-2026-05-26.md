## Executive Summary

This research tested the specific empirical claim at [[concepts/concession-convergence]] §"Three Discriminating Tests" (line 106): that no non-Western tradition has produced a *physicalist theory of consciousness that narrows toward type-specificity-respecting specifications independently of the Western analytic literature*, and that this is therefore a **gap** (no available evidence) rather than a failed test.

**The gap is confirmed, and the confirmation is now better-grounded than the article's current one-sentence treatment.** Across four candidate traditions surveyed — contemporary Chinese philosophy of mind, contemporary Japanese naturalized phenomenology, Buddhist Abhidharma, and Indian Cārvāka/Lokāyata materialism — **none satisfies the conjunction the discriminator requires**: (physicalist) AND (narrows toward type-specificity under its own internal logic) AND (independent of the Western physicalist literature). Each candidate fails on at least one conjunct, and the *way* each fails is informative:

- **Chinese philosophy of mind**: indigenous tradition (qi-naturalism, heart-mind, Buddhist consciousness-only) is **not reductive physicalism** and does not narrow; contemporary analytic work by Chinese philosophers **is in active dialogue with the Western literature** (transmission channel open → not independent).
- **Japanese naturalized phenomenology** (Taguchi/CHAIN, Yuasa lineage): explicitly **non-reductive / enactivist / phenomenological**, and openly **engages Western phenomenology and Western analytic theories (IIT, Husserl, enactivism)** — neither physicalist-narrowing nor independent.
- **Abhidharma citta-caitta**: a **non-physicalist** taxonomy — citta and caitta are dharma-categories *irreducible to* rūpa (the physical). It types phenomenal characters but is not a physicalist narrowing.
- **Cārvāka/Lokāyata**: the one genuinely indigenous, pre-contact **physicalist** tradition — but it is a **broad emergentism that does not narrow** toward type-specificity. Crucially, the type-specificity pressure in the Indian debate came from its **Nyāya opponents'** "absent in parts" objection, *not* from Cārvāka's own internal logic — the opposite of the concession-convergence dynamic.

**A confirmed gap is a legitimate finding, not a failure.** No convergence is manufactured here. The honest upshot is that the cross-observer discriminator remains *unbuilt* (absence of a qualifying theory), and the Cārvāka case in fact supplies a mild *disconfirming nuance* worth recording (see "Article Angles").

## Major Candidate Traditions

### 1. Contemporary Chinese Philosophy of Mind

**Indigenous tradition is not reductive physicalism and does not narrow.** The classical and neo-Confucian materials center on *xin* 心 (heart-mind), *qi* 氣 (cosmic/material force), Daoist holism, and Buddhist consciousness-only (*Weishi*/Yogācāra). The Stanford Encyclopedia "Mind (Heart-Mind) in Chinese Philosophy" entry and the Diversity Reading List blueprint (Wei) both describe a holistic heart-mind that is "the centre of cognition but also of emotion, understanding and intuition" — a functional-holist picture, not a type-identity physicalism.

- **Qi-naturalism is explicitly distinguished from reductive materialism.** A ResearchGate paper "Chinese Qi-Naturalism and Liberal Naturalism" (summarised via SEP) states qi-cosmology "has a naturalistic spirit ... and yet it has not been accepted into the naturalist camp for the lack of any working reduction between qi-terminology and contemporary physical terms," and is "a genuine alternative to substance dualism, differing from reductive materialism." So the indigenous "naturalist" strand is *non-reductive by construction* — it does not narrow toward type-specific physical requirements; it never makes the reductive identity claim that would generate the narrowing pressure.
- **Some scholars read the classical tradition as dualist or idealist, not physicalist.** Chen Xunwu (2017, *Asian Philosophy*) interprets Confucians as substance dualists (mind and body as distinct substances); Wang Yangming's "mind is the Principle" is read by several scholars as verging on subjective idealism (Liu, 2024, *Asian Journal of Philosophy*). These are the opposite of physicalist-narrowing.
- **Contemporary analytic work by Chinese philosophers is in dialogue with the West.** Hengwei Li's *Consciousness: Metaphysics, the First-Person Method, and Contemporary Theories* (Zhejiang University Press, 2020) and the multi-author *The Challenges of Cognitive Science to Contemporary Philosophy* (Science Press, 2020) engage contemporary (Western-framed) consciousness theory directly. Slingerland (2013) and Raphals (2023) are comparative projects explicitly informed by Western cognitive science. **This is the transmission-channel-open case: where Chinese philosophers do narrow toward physical specificity, they do so within the shared Chalmers-framed analytic conversation — which is exactly what the independence criterion disqualifies.**

**Verdict**: Fails the conjunction. Indigenous strand = non-reductive (no narrowing); contemporary analytic strand = not independent (open transmission channel). Matches the article's current claim ("active dialogue ... transmission channel open").

### 2. Contemporary Japanese Naturalized Phenomenology

**Non-reductive / phenomenological, and openly engaged with Western thought — neither physicalist-narrowing nor independent.**

- **Kyoto School (Nishida, Tanabe, Nishitani)**: founded on "pure experience" / "absolute nothingness" — an explicitly *anti-reductive*, experience-first metaphysics developed in dialogue with Husserl and Bergson. Not physicalist.
- **Yasuo Yuasa (1925–2005)**: theory of the body that *re-evaluates* Descartes' dualism through Eastern non-dualistic thinking, emphasising no clear mind/body distinction, and explicitly aligns with Merleau-Ponty. Non-reductive embodiment, not physicalist narrowing.
- **Shigeru Taguchi (Hokkaido, director of CHAIN — Center for Human Nature, AI, and Neuroscience)** is the strongest contemporary candidate for "naturalized phenomenology" and the clearest test case. His position is **non-reductive**: he critiques approaches that identify "life and mind ... with physically deterministic phenomena" and argues "meaning as such could make a difference for an agent's behavior." His framework is **enactivist-phenomenological** (collaboration with Tom Froese; autopoiesis, precarious existence), and he applies category theory to *assess* IIT while *explicitly declining to reduce consciousness to physical structure*. He engages **both** Western phenomenology (Husserl, temporality, the "primal I") **and** Western analytic theory (IIT). So: not physicalist-narrowing, and deeply in dialogue with the West on both flanks.

**Verdict**: Fails the conjunction. The tradition sits at the phenomenology interface (as the article says), is non-reductive, and is in dialogue with Western phenomenology *and* analytic philosophy of mind.

### 3. Buddhist Abhidharma (citta-caitta analysis)

**A non-physicalist taxonomy: mind-dharmas are irreducible to physical dharmas.** The SEP "Abhidharma" entry confirms the Sarvāstivāda/Theravāda systems sort dharmas into categories that keep consciousness (*citta*, 1 dharma), mental factors (*caitta*/*cetasika*, ~46–52 dharmas), and **materiality (*rūpa*, ~11–28 dharmas) as distinct, coordinate categories**. The mental and the material "occur in perfect synchronicity" (parallel coordination) but the mental is *not reduced to or derived from* the physical.

- Abhidharma *does* type phenomenal/mental characters with great precision (52 cetasikas, 89 cittas) — so it is a *taxonomic narrowing of the mental*. But that is a taxonomy of mental dharmas in their own right, **not a specification of which physical configurations yield consciousness**. The narrowing the concession-convergence pattern tracks is a *physicalist* narrowing (which physical/architectural features are required); Abhidharma's narrowing is a phenomenological-taxonomic one within an irreducibly mental category.
- The motivating project is anti-*ātman* (no enduring self), not mind-from-matter reduction. Yogācāra develops this further into *cittamātra* (mind-only) idealism (ālayavijñāna) — the opposite ontological direction from physicalism.

**Verdict**: Fails the "physicalist" conjunct. It is a phenomenological-taxonomic project on irreducibly mental categories. Matches the article's claim ("types phenomenal characters but is not physicalist in the contemporary sense").

### 4. Cārvāka / Lokāyata (Indian materialism) — the new, important case

**The one genuinely indigenous, pre-Western-contact physicalist tradition — but it is a broad emergentism that does NOT narrow, and the narrowing pressure came from its opponents.** This case is not currently named in the article and is the most evidentially significant addition.

- **Genuinely physicalist and genuinely independent.** Cārvāka (IEP "Lokayata/Carvaka — Indian Materialism"; Wikipedia "Charvaka") holds the four elements (earth, water, fire, air) are the only reality and that **consciousness emerges when they combine into a body** — "though no element contains consciousness within itself, their combination produces consciousness," analogised to red colour arising from betel + areca nut + lime, or "matter secretes mind as the liver secretes bile." Adamson & Ganeri compare it to Mill-style British emergentism. This is a real, indigenous, ancient (pre-contact) physicalist-emergentist theory of consciousness — so the *existence* clause that defeats Chinese/Japanese/Abhidharma cases is satisfied here.
- **But it does NOT narrow toward type-specificity.** The surviving doctrine (only fragments of the *Bṛhaspati Sūtra* remain) is a *broad* "body is an inalienable factor" emergentism. It specifies no particular physical/architectural configuration as uniquely consciousness-bearing; it does not retreat from absent-qualia-style counter-examples by adding specificity. There is no internally-driven ratchet toward type-specific physical requirements.
- **Critically: the type-specificity pressure was EXTERNAL, from opponents — the reverse of concession-convergence.** The "absent in parts" objection (if consciousness is absent in each element, how can it emerge from their combination?) was pressed by **Nyāya** (via the *anvaya-vyatireka* method) and Buddhists *against* Cārvāka, to argue for a distinct self/soul. In the concession-convergence pattern, the physicalist theory narrows under *its own* internal logic when it confronts the hard problem. In the Indian debate, Cārvāka largely *held its broad emergentism* and the narrowing/anti-physicalist conclusion was drawn by its **dualist/substance-self opponents**. So Cārvāka is, if anything, a mild *counter-example to directionality*: an independent physicalism that did *not* ratchet toward type-specificity from within.

**Verdict**: Satisfies "physicalist" and "independent," **fails "narrows toward type-specificity (internally driven)."** This is the most informative result of the survey: the genuinely independent non-Western physicalism we can find did *not* reproduce the narrowing move — which is a (weak, fragmentary-source-limited) data point *against* the universality of the narrowing, not for it.

## Independence Audit (the load-bearing question)

The discriminator requires a non-Western theory that is **(a) physicalist**, **(b) narrows toward type-specificity under its own internal logic**, AND **(c) reached (b) without being in dialogue with the Western physicalist literature**. Scoring each candidate:

| Tradition | (a) Physicalist? | (b) Narrows (internally)? | (c) Independent of Western lit? | Qualifies? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chinese qi-naturalism / heart-mind | No (non-reductive) | No | Yes (indigenous) | No — fails (a),(b) |
| Contemporary Chinese analytic PoM | Sometimes | Sometimes | **No** (open channel) | No — fails (c) |
| Japanese naturalized phenomenology (Taguchi/CHAIN) | No (non-reductive) | No | No (dialogue w/ West) | No — fails (a),(c) |
| Abhidharma citta-caitta | No (mental irreducible to rūpa) | Taxonomic, but of *mental* dharmas | Yes (indigenous) | No — fails (a) |
| Cārvāka/Lokāyata | **Yes** | **No** (broad emergentism; narrowing came from *opponents*) | **Yes** | No — fails (b) |

**No candidate fills all three cells.** The gap the article reports is real. The new information is *why* it is structured the way it is, and that the closest indigenous physicalism (Cārvāka) actively *did not* narrow.

## Honest Evidential Status

- This is a **confirmed gap**, consistent with [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]]: the cross-observer discriminator remains **absent (unbuilt)**, not negatively tested. The article's current sentence is accurate; this research substantiates it with named sources and adds the Cārvāka case.
- **No convergence has been manufactured.** If anything, the Cārvāka finding is a small *tension* for the "directionality" feature (line 88) of the concession-convergence argument: an independent physicalism that confronted opponents' absent-qualia-style pressure and *did not* ratchet toward type-specificity from within. Evidential honesty requires flagging this rather than burying it.
- **Source limitation (declared)**: Cārvāka's primary texts survive only as fragments (*Bṛhaspati Sūtra*); its doctrine is largely reconstructed from opponents' rebuttals. So "Cārvāka did not narrow" is itself uncertain — the *absence* of a narrowing move in a fragmentary, opponent-mediated record is weak evidence. It should be reported as a *suggestive* nuance, not a firm disconfirmation.
- **Contemporary Chinese analytic PoM** is a moving target; this survey did not exhaustively canvass Chinese-language journals (*Frontiers of Philosophy in China* surfaced but was not deeply mined). A future deeper pass (ideally with Chinese-language sources) could check whether any contemporary Chinese physicalist has narrowed *without* citing the Western literature — but the structural expectation is that any such work post-dates and engages the global analytic conversation (channel open), so independence is unlikely to be recoverable even if narrowing is found.

## Potential Article Angles (for a later expand/refine — NOT done here)

1. **Tighten line 106 with named sources and the Cārvāka case.** Replace the thin one-sentence treatment with: the indigenous traditions are non-reductive (qi-naturalism) / idealist (Yogācāra) / phenomenological (Kyoto, Taguchi/CHAIN) and so do not narrow; contemporary analytic work is dialogue-dependent (channel open); and Cārvāka — the one independent indigenous physicalism — was a *broad* emergentism that did not narrow, with the type-specificity pressure supplied externally by Nyāya. This *strengthens* the article's honesty without changing the gap verdict.
2. **Surface the Cārvāka nuance against "directionality."** The directionality feature (line 88) claims the ratchet only turns toward specificity. Cārvāka is a fragmentary but real case where an independent physicalism faced the absent-qualia-style "absent in parts" objection and the narrowing conclusion was drawn by *dualist opponents*, not by the physicalists. Worth a sentence acknowledging this as a (source-limited) caveat — consistent with [[evidential-status-discipline]] and [[common-cause-null]].
3. **Reframe the cross-observer score's "weak because absent."** The audit (line 117) currently says cross-tradition convergence is "weak because the discriminator is absent rather than negative." This research lets the article say something sharper: the discriminator is absent *for narrowing-physicalisms specifically*, and where an independent physicalism does exist (Cārvāka) it did *not* narrow — so the cell is not merely empty, it has one suggestive contrary data point. This should *lower*, not raise, confidence that non-Western physicalism would converge — and the article should say so plainly rather than leaving the gap as neutrally "to be filled."

## Gaps in This Research

- No Chinese-language primary sources consulted (English-language and translated secondary literature only). A native-language pass might find contemporary Chinese physicalists not visible in English search.
- Cārvāka doctrine is reconstructed from fragments and hostile sources; the "did not narrow" reading is therefore tentative.
- Korean philosophy of mind and contemporary Indian analytic philosophy (e.g., the Nyāya-influenced analytic tradition, J. Ganeri's "cosmopolitan" PoM) were not surveyed; Ganeri in particular works explicitly *across* traditions and would likely count as dialogue-dependent, but a dedicated check was out of scope.
- Did not assess whether any contemporary IIT/GWT-style formal theory has been independently re-derived in a non-Western institutional setting without citation to the originals (structurally improbable but not checked).
