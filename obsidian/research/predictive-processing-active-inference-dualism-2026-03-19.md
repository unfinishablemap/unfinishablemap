# Research: Predictive Processing and Active Inference Under Dualism

**Date**: 2026-03-19
**Search queries used**: "predictive processing active inference philosophy of mind", "predictive processing dualism consciousness Karl Friston free energy principle", "active inference framework compatible with dualism non-physicalist interpretation", "Andy Clark predictive processing consciousness phenomenology", "Jakob Hohwy predictive processing consciousness self-model prediction error", "active inference hard problem of consciousness Chalmers explanatory gap", "free energy principle quantum mechanics consciousness", "predictive processing phenomenal consciousness qualia subjective experience", "Anil Seth controlled hallucination beast machine", "predictive processing interactionism mental causation top-down causation", "Friston dual aspect monism Markov blanket consciousness self-evidencing"

## Executive Summary

Active inference — the action-oriented extension of predictive processing under the free energy principle — is almost universally presented as a physicalist framework. Its architect Karl Friston advocates "Markovian monism," and its major interpreters (Seth, Hohwy, Clark) lean toward functionalism or illusionism about consciousness. Yet the framework's mathematical formalism is metaphysically neutral, and several structural features map remarkably well onto interactionist dualism: hierarchical hypothesis selection creates the branching possibilities consciousness could select among, precision weighting functions as the interface where attention (and therefore consciousness) acts, and the hard problem remains fully open within the framework. This research examines what a dualist appropriation of active inference would look like and where it diverges from the framework's standard interpretation.

## Key Sources

### Friston, Wiese & Hobson — "Sentience and the Origins of Consciousness: From Cartesian Duality to Markovian Monism" (2020)
- **URL**: https://www.mdpi.com/1099-4300/22/5/516
- **Type**: Paper (Entropy journal)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes "Markovian monism": fundamentally one type of thing, one type of irreducible property
  - All systems possessing a Markov blanket have properties relevant to consciousness
  - Dual information geometry: extrinsic (beliefs about world) and intrinsic (information about own future states)
  - Extrinsic geometry potentially accounts for "mental" properties or "qualia"
  - But both interpretations are "mutually reductive" — hence monism, not dualism
  - Self-evidencing: anything that exists must model the world relative to its own viability
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism) — Friston explicitly rejects Cartesian dualism in favor of monism. However, the "dual information geometry" concession is interesting: acknowledging two irreducible descriptions while insisting they reduce to one ontology looks more like a promissory note than a completed reduction
- **Quote**: Self-organizing systems model the world as if saying not "I think, therefore I am" but "I am, therefore my model is viable"

### Hohwy — The Predictive Mind (2013) and subsequent work
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-predictive-mind-9780199686735
- **Type**: Book / papers
- **Key points**:
  - Brain as sole source of cognition — inference engine behind a "veil" of sensory input
  - PP is not itself a theory of consciousness but provides an ideal foundation for building one
  - Precision weighting is functionally equivalent to attention
  - Self-model within hierarchical generative model accounts for phenomenal aspects
  - Prediction error minimization explains perception, action, and everything mental "in between"
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral to weakly conflicting — Hohwy is honest that PP leaves consciousness unexplained. His "veil of perception" internalism is actually congenial to dualism: if the brain is inferring rather than directly accessing reality, the question of what does the inferring becomes pressing
- **Quote**: PP is "not itself a theory of consciousness" — it provides "significant potential" but makes no direct claims about subjective experience

- **Type**: Book / papers
- **Key points**:

  - "Beast machine" theory: conscious experiences happen with, through, and because of living bodies
  - Sympathetic to illusionism about consciousness
  - Consciousness grounded in self-modelling processes inherent in action selection and precision control
  - Argues for embodied, enactive interpretation of PP
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism) — Seth's illusionism directly opposes the Map's commitment to irreducible consciousness. However, his emphasis on interoceptive inference (the body-model) identifies mechanisms that a dualist could reinterpret as the channels through which consciousness operates
- **Quote**: "If we can think of hallucination as uncontrolled perception... then we can think of perception in the here and now as controlled hallucinations"

## Major Positions

### Markovian Monism (Friston)
- **Proponents**: Karl Friston, Wanja Wiese, J. Allan Hobson
- **Core claim**: Consciousness arises from the dual information geometry inherent in any system with a Markov blanket. The extrinsic geometry (beliefs about world) and intrinsic geometry (self-information) together account for mental properties. This is monism because both geometries describe the same underlying reality.
- **Key arguments**: Self-evidencing (all existing systems must model themselves), blanket states as interface between internal and external, mathematical equivalence of inference and existence
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly conflicts with Tenet 1. However, the "dual information geometry" is strikingly similar to property dualism — Friston acknowledges two irreducible *descriptions* while insisting on one *ontology*. The Map could argue that this is property dualism wearing a monist disguise.

### Controlled Hallucination / Beast Machine (Seth)
- **Proponents**: Anil Seth
- **Core claim**: Perception is a controlled hallucination generated by predictive processing. Consciousness arises from interoceptive self-modelling — the brain's predictions about its own body. We are "beast machines" whose consciousness is inseparable from biological embodiment.
- **Key arguments**: Interoceptive inference, controlled hallucination experiments, measuring consciousness through perturbational complexity
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (consciousness as illusory/reducible). But the "controlled" in controlled hallucination is doing heavy lifting — *what controls the hallucination?* Seth says sensory input, but this only pushes the question back. The Map could argue that consciousness is the controller, not the controlled.

### Radical Predictive Processing (Clark)
- **Proponents**: Andy Clark
- **Core claim**: The brain-body-environment system collectively minimizes prediction error. Cognition extends beyond the skull. Consciousness may be an illusion generated by meta-prediction.
- **Key arguments**: Extended mind, action-oriented representations, embodied cognition
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (illusionism) and potentially with Tenet 2 (extended cognition dilutes quantum interface). However, Clark's emphasis on active inference as world-shaping action supports Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction) — organisms genuinely change the world, they don't just passively model it.

### Dualist Reinterpretation (the Map's position)
- **Proponents**: Implicit in the Map's existing article "Predictive Processing and the Map's Framework"
- **Core claim**: PP/active inference describes the neural machinery through which consciousness operates, without explaining why this machinery is conscious. The framework is metaphysically neutral. Precision weighting (= attention) is the interface where consciousness acts on the physical. The hard problem remains fully open.
- **Key arguments**: PP's own proponents admit it's not a theory of consciousness; the mathematics doesn't mandate monism; precision weighting maps onto the attention-as-interface concept; hierarchical hypothesis generation creates the selection space consciousness needs
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong alignment with all five tenets. PP provides the physical side; dualism provides the experiential side; quantum indeterminacy provides the interface mechanism.

## Key Debates

### Is Active Inference Metaphysically Neutral?
- **Sides**: Friston et al. (no — it entails Markovian monism) vs. critics like Kiefer (yes — mathematics underdetermines metaphysics)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the free energy principle's mathematical formalism forces a particular ontological conclusion
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Kiefer's critique that "we cannot read off metaphysical theses about the nature of target systems from our theories" has not been convincingly rebutted. This is significant for the Map: if the mathematics is neutral, dualist appropriation is philosophically legitimate.

### Does PP Explain or Merely Describe Consciousness?
- **Sides**: Seth/Hohwy (PP is a foundation for consciousness science, with potential to dissolve the hard problem) vs. skeptics (PP describes neural correlates without touching subjective experience)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "dissolving" the hard problem through better mechanistic description is a genuine philosophical achievement or a redefinition of the question
- **Current state**: Seth and Hohwy have been candid that PP is not itself a theory of consciousness. Their strategy of identifying neural correlates rather than solving the hard problem directly is exactly the gap the Map exploits.

### Free Energy Principle and Quantum Cognition
- **Sides**: Pothos et al. (mathematical connections exist) vs. mainstream neuroscience (quantum effects irrelevant to cognition)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether quantum probability is relevant to brain-level Bayesian inference
- **Current state**: Early stage. The mathematical bridge between variational free energy and quantum probability is formal but its physical significance is disputed. For the Map, even a formal connection supports the possibility that consciousness operates at the quantum-Bayesian interface.

### Active Inference: Representationalist or Enactivist?
- **Sides**: Conservative PP (rich internal representations, Hohwy) vs. radical PP (embodied, action-first, Clark) vs. enactivist (Ramstead, Kirchhoff — active inference *is* enactive inference)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether active inference requires internal representations or whether the framework is better understood through enactivist concepts
- **Current state**: No consensus. The Map's position cuts across this debate — what matters is not whether representations are "real" but whether the computational structure (whichever interpretation) leaves room for consciousness to act.

## Gaps in Research

- **No dualist philosopher has explicitly adopted active inference.** The appropriation is the Map's own move — there is no established literature on "dualist active inference" to cite. This is both a gap and an opportunity.
- **The quantum-Bayesian bridge is early-stage.** Pothos et al. (2022) is a formal/mathematical connection; empirical evidence for quantum effects in brain-level Bayesian inference is lacking.
- **Friston's response to Kiefer is unclear.** The critical analysis of Markovian monism has not been decisively answered, but Friston's subsequent work may implicitly address the objections.
- **Limited coverage of how active inference handles pathological states** (psychosis, dissociation) under a dualist interpretation — these edge cases could test the framework.
- **No research found on whether active inference's "self-evidencing" concept is compatible with the Map's rejection of Many Worlds** (Tenet 4). Self-evidencing implies the system models its own persistence, which could connect to indexical identity concerns.
