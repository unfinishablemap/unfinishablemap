## Executive Summary

Anarchic hand is a neurological sign in which one hand performs complex, goal-directed, well-coordinated acts that the patient disowns, did not intend, and cannot voluntarily inhibit — reaching for objects, unbuttoning what the other hand just buttoned, bringing a too-hot cup to the lips. Crucially, the patient recognises the limb as their own (body ownership is intact) but denies authorship of its movements (the sense of agency is lost). This makes it the **agency-side complement** to split-brain's perception-side dissociation: the interface between conscious volition and motor execution fragments while the subject remains a single experiencer. The literature is unusually careful about terminology: **Marchetti & Della Sala (1998)** drew the now-standard distinction between the *anarchic* hand (disowned action, frontal/callosal motor sign) and the *alien* hand (disowned sensation of belonging, posterior/parietal somatognosic sign) — a distinction that is routinely conflated and easy to mis-attribute. For the Map, anarchic hand is honestly compatible with both a filter/interface reading and a physicalist comparator-failure reading; it supplies a discriminating question, not a proof.

## The Critical Terminological Point (get this right)

The anarchic/alien distinction is attributed to:

> **Marchetti, C. & Della Sala, S. (1998). "Disentangling the alien and anarchic hand." *Cognitive Neuropsychiatry*, 3(3), 191–207.**

Their claim, per the source:
- **Anarchic hand** = complex goal-directed movements performed *against the patient's will* that cannot be voluntarily inhibited. A "frontal" sign, linked with anterior lesions of the corpus callosum and the supplementary motor area (SMA) contralateral to the wayward hand. The hand acts; the patient disowns the *act*.
- **Alien hand** = the *feeling of non-belonging* of a hand — the limb feels foreign, not-mine. Reported after posterior lesions of the corpus callosum; best understood as a *partial hemisomatognosia* (loss of the sense of one's own body). The patient disowns the *limb*, not (primarily) its action.

**Do not conflate these, and do not mis-cite the distinction.** Much older and clinical literature uses "alien hand syndrome" as an umbrella term covering both; Marchetti & Della Sala's contribution was precisely to split the umbrella. When the downstream article discusses *disowned action*, the correct technical term is **anarchic hand**, and the correct primary citation for the distinction is Marchetti & Della Sala 1998.

## Key Sources

### Marchetti & Della Sala (1998) — "Disentangling the Alien and Anarchic Hand"
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/135468098396143 (PDF mirror: http://pacherie.free.fr/COURS/MSC/Marchetti-DellaSala-1998.pdf)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed paper (Cognitive Neuropsychiatry, 3(3), 191–207)
- **Key points**:
  - Coins/consolidates the anarchic-vs-alien distinction (see above).
  - Anarchic hand = anterior CC + SMA; a frontal disinhibition sign.
  - Alien hand = posterior CC; a somatognosic/sensory ownership sign.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral / usable by both readings; primary value is descriptive rigour.

### Della Sala, Marchetti & Spinnler (1991) — canonical "tea-cup" case
- **Citation**: Della Sala, S., Marchetti, C., & Spinnler, H. (1991). "Right-sided anarchic (alien) hand: a longitudinal study." *Neuropsychologia*, 29(11), 1113–1127.
- **Key points**:
  - Documents the frequently-cited case in which the anarchic hand raises a cup of tea the patient *knows* is too hot to drink — action against avowed intention.
  - Patients typically restrain the "bad" hand with the "good" hand (self-directed intermanual conflict).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; strong illustrative case of intact ownership + lost authorship.

### Feinberg, Schindler, Flanagan & Haber (1992) — "Two alien hand syndromes"
- **URL**: https://www.neurology.org/doi/10.1212/WNL.42.1.19
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed paper (Neurology, 42(1), 19–24)
- **Key points**:
  - Reviews ~20 cases and argues AHS is really *two* syndromes:
    - **Frontal AHS** (dominant hand): reflexive grasping, groping, compulsive tool manipulation; damage to SMA, anterior cingulate gyrus, medial prefrontal cortex, anterior corpus callosum.
    - **Callosal AHS**: intermanual conflict as the primary sign; requires only an anterior callosal lesion; loss of dominant-hemisphere control over bimanual coordination.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; the physicalist inhibition-breakdown story is well-supported here.

### Goldberg, Mayer & Toglia (1981) — "Medial frontal cortex infarction and the alien hand sign"
- **Citation**: Goldberg, G., Mayer, N.H., & Toglia, J.U. (1981). *Archives of Neurology*, 38(11), 683–686.
- **Key points**:
  - Early canonical localisation of the sign to medial frontal cortex.
  - Goldberg's broader account (SMA structure/function) frames the sign as loss of SMA inhibitory control over premotor cortex, releasing environmentally-driven (exogenous) motor behaviour.
- **Tenet alignment**: This is the paradigm *physicalist* reading — disinhibition, no ghost required. Cite it honestly as the strong opposing account.

### Assal, Schwartz & Vuilleumier (2007) — "Moving with or without will"
- **URL**: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17638304 (Annals of Neurology, 62(3), 301–306)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed fMRI case study
- **Key points**:
  - fMRI in a patient (right parietal lesion) contrasting *alien* (involuntary) vs *voluntary* left-hand movement.
  - Alien movement showed anomalous **isolated activation of primary motor cortex (M1)** in the damaged hemisphere, without the normal premotor/preparatory activation that accompanies willed movement.
  - Interpretation: alien movements are "separated" from premotor influence — motor output without the normal precursor signals that generate the sense of agency.
- **Tenet alignment**: Ambivalent. Reads naturally as comparator/forward-model failure (no efference signal to predict the movement → no agency), but equally illustrates a *dissociation* between execution and the felt authorship the Map cares about.

### Haggard (2017) — "Sense of agency in the human brain"
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn.2017.14 (Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 18(4), 196–207; open PDF: https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/1545235/)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed review
- **Key points**:
  - Defines sense of agency as "the experience of controlling one's own actions and, through them, the course of events in the outside world."
  - Distinguishes **sense of agency** (authorship of action) from **body ownership** (the limb is mine) — the exact axis anarchic hand dissociates.
  - Reviews comparator/forward-model machinery and its measurement (intentional binding, etc.).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral, foundational. Supplies the agency/ownership vocabulary the article should adopt.

### Frith, Blakemore & Wolpert (2000) — comparator model of action awareness
- **Citation**: Frith, C.D., Blakemore, S.-J., & Wolpert, D.M. (2000). "Abnormalities in the awareness and control of action." *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 355(1404), 1771–1788.
- **Key points**:
  - Forward model: a copy of the motor command (efference copy) predicts sensory consequences; predicted and actual feedback are compared.
  - Successful prediction → the movement feels self-generated (agency). Comparator mismatch or absent prediction → the movement feels externally caused / not-mine.
  - Framework used to explain delusions of control in schizophrenia and, by extension, agency breakdowns.
- **Tenet alignment**: The mechanistic backbone of the physicalist reading; cite as the account the Map must engage, not dodge.

- **Type**: Popular/authoritative essay by Della Sala.

## Major Positions

### Physicalist comparator/inhibition-failure reading (the honest default to concede)
- **Proponents**: Goldberg; Frith/Blakemore/Wolpert; Feinberg (implicitly); Assal et al.
- **Core claim**: Anarchic hand is a straightforward breakdown of motor control architecture. Frontal/SMA lesions release premotor cortex from inhibition, producing exogenously-triggered movements; the absence of the normal efference-copy/prediction chain means the movements carry no sense of agency. No non-physical element is needed.
- **Key arguments**: Clean lesion-to-symptom localisation; fMRI (Assal) shows the missing premotor precursor directly; the comparator model predicts exactly this agency-loss when prediction is absent.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the strongest opposing account and must be conceded as fully adequate to the *neural* facts. It explains the mechanism without dualism.

### Interface/filter reading (the Map's angle — possibility, not proof)
- **Core claim**: What fractures in anarchic hand is the **sense of ownership of action** — the interface between conscious volition and motor execution — while **phenomenal unity is preserved**. The patient remains *one* experiencer who disowns a movement; there are not two subjects, not two streams of experience. This is the "interface fragments, not the mind" pattern (already developed for perception in split-brain) applied to **volition**.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Ownership (limb is mine) and agency (act is mine) doubly dissociate — Haggard's own distinction — showing authorship is a constructed interface signal, not a given.
  - The unity of the experiencer survives the fracture: the patient narrates, is distressed by, and physically restrains the anarchic hand as a *single* self.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns with **Tenet 1 (Dualism)** and **Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)** by treating volition as an interface phenomenon that can be locally disrupted without dissolving the unified subject. But it is **not a proof**: nothing here requires the filter reading over the comparator reading.

### The discriminating question (state this, do not overclaim)
Both readings predict the *same clinical picture*. The honest move is to name what would separate them rather than assert victory:
- The physicalist predicts that the *felt* single subject is itself just another neural construct — unity is a story the intact hemisphere tells.
- The filter reading predicts that the unified experiencer is ontologically prior and merely *loses read/write access* to one motor channel.
- **No current datum decides between these.** Anarchic hand shows agency is *dissociable* from ownership and from execution; it does not show *what the experiencer is*. Frame the article as possibility-preservation, per evidential-status discipline.

## Key Debates

### Is anarchic hand about agency or ownership?
- **Sides**: Marchetti & Della Sala (anarchic = disowned *action*/agency; alien = disowned *limb*/ownership) vs. loose clinical usage that merges both under "alien hand syndrome."
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the disorder is fundamentally motor-frontal (agency) or sensory-parietal (ownership).
- **Current state**: The anarchic/alien split is the accepted technical framing; clinicians still use "alien hand" loosely, and mixed cases exist (e.g., extensive callosal infarcts producing combined pictures).

### Is the comparator model sufficient for the sense of agency?
- **Sides**: Comparator/forward-model advocates (Frith, Haggard) vs. critics arguing "a match does not make a sense" — that predictive matching is necessary but not sufficient for felt agency.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether efference-copy prediction *constitutes* agency or merely *contributes* to it.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; recent predictive-processing accounts attempt to rehabilitate a unified comparator story. Relevant because the Map's angle exploits any residue the mechanism cannot capture — but must not overstate the residue as settled.
