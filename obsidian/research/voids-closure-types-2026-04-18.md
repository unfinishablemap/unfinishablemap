## Executive Summary

A thirty-year debate in philosophy of mind — McGinn, Chomsky, Kriegel, Demircioglu, Vlerick, Boudry — turns on a distinction the Map has been using implicitly without naming: *representational* closure (the mind lacks concept-forming procedures sufficient to frame a true theory of P) versus *psychological* closure (the mind has the representational materials but cannot actually grasp the solution). The distinction matters because Kriegel's well-known attack on mysterianism only reaches the psychological reading; the representational thesis survives. But the more interesting result for the voids programme is the *inverse closure on the diagnosis itself*: there is no first-person procedure by which a mind can tell, of a given recalcitrant problem, which kind of closure it is facing. Every void in the catalogue inherits this second-order ambiguity. The Map has leaned on the distinction in `meta-epistemology-of-limits` and `phenomenology-of-the-edge`; what is missing is a dedicated treatment that (a) gives the distinction its own entry, (b) shows why the diagnostic problem is itself a void, and (c) draws the methodological consequence for apophatic cartography — that void claims must be explicit about which closure type they are asserting.

## Key Sources

### Kriegel (2003) — "The new mysterianism and the thesis of cognitive closure"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12136-003-1020-1
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed paper, *Acta Analytica* 18, 177-191
- **Key points**:
  - Questions are individuated by the set of statements that would answer them (following Hamblin and Stahl).
  - Therefore to pose a question Q is already to possess the conceptual resources to frame potential answers to Q.
  - If we can formulate the mind-body problem, we already grasp what a solution would look like, so we cannot be cognitively closed to it.
  - Mysterianism is *incoherent*: you cannot grasp a problem without thereby grasping the form of its possible answer.
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly challenges Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits). Kriegel's argument looks parsimonious — it would dissolve a whole class of voids — but this parsimony is the kind the tenet warns against.
- **Quote**: "It is incoherent to suppose that we cannot understand what would count as a solution to a problem we can and do understand."

### Demircioglu (2017) — "Human Cognitive Closure and Mysterianism: Reply to Kriegel"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12136-016-0295-y
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed paper, *Acta Analytica* 32, 147-158
- **Key points**:
  - Kriegel's argument conflates two readings of "grasping what would count as an answer."
  - **Representational** reading: M has the concept-forming procedures to frame a true theory of P. Closure here is what McGinn actually wanted.
  - **Psychological** reading: M can actually work through and *grasp* the theory when presented. Closure here is a weaker claim.
  - A rat might "represent" the prime numbers as "the numbers mathematicians talk about" without being able to identify which numbers those are.
  - Kriegel's incoherence argument only works against the psychological reading. It leaves the representational reading untouched.
- **Tenet alignment**: Defends the intelligibility of Tenet 5 — simplicity can mask the difference between "can frame a solution" and "can actually produce one."
- **Quote (paraphrase)**: "Being able to represent the form of an answer is not being able to grasp the answer itself."

### Vlerick & Boudry (2017) — "Psychological Closure Does Not Entail Cognitive Closure"
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/1746-8361.12176
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed paper, *Dialectica* 71(1), 101-115
- **Key points**:
  - The entailment runs the *other* way from what McGinn needs.
  - McGinn's case only demonstrates psychological difficulty (we have not cracked the hard problem) but this does not establish principled representational closure.
  - History of science shows many problems that looked mysteriously recalcitrant later yielded to new conceptual resources (vitalism, action-at-a-distance, quantum non-locality's initial intelligibility crisis).
  - Inferring representational closure from psychological closure is a hasty generalisation.
- **Tenet alignment**: Cuts against Tenet 5 at first glance — looks like simplicity argument — but actually reinforces the Map's concern about *which* direction the inference runs. It shows the distinction has real teeth in two directions.
- **Quote (paraphrase)**: "The inference from 'we have not solved it' to 'we cannot solve it' is unwarranted unless independent evidence of representational closure is supplied."

### Chomsky — "Problems and Mysteries in the Study of Human Language" (1975, reprinted 1976)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-94-010-1876-0_15
- **Type**: Book chapter, foundational for the distinction
- **Key points**:
  - *Problems* are tractable in principle with moderately well-understood approaches.
  - *Mysteries* remain as obscure as when originally formulated.
  - The distinction is organism-relative: what is a mystery for rats need not be for humans, and vice versa.
  - Methodological naturalism: whatever humans can investigate is a proper object of naturalistic inquiry; what we cannot, we simply cannot — no methodological dualism needed.
- **Tenet alignment**: Cross-cuts Tenet 1 (Dualism). Chomsky explicitly resists methodological dualism but his own distinction opens space for *epistemic* dualism of accessibility, which is structurally aligned with the Map's void framework.
- **Quote (paraphrase)**: "The distinction between problems and mysteries reflects, in part, a subjective evaluation of what has been achieved or might be achieved."

### McGinn — "Mysterianism Revisited" and "Can We Solve the Mind-Body Problem?" (1989)
- **URL**: https://colinmcginn.net/mysterianism-revisited/
- **Type**: Original target of the debate
- **Key points**:
  - "A type of mind M is cognitively closed with respect to a property P (or theory T) if and only if the concept-forming procedures at M's disposal cannot extend to a grasp of P (or an understanding of T)."
  - The closure is *representational* in Demircioglu's sense: McGinn wants to say the conceptual machinery itself is insufficient, not that we have the machinery but lack the acuity.
  - Unknowability does not imply non-existence. Degree of intelligibility is not degree of reality.
  - Intelligibility is a matter of cognitive endowment; mind is as limited as body, and has an anatomy too.
- **Tenet alignment**: Tenet 5 — McGinn is, in effect, an Occam's-Razor-Has-Limits position applied to explanation itself. A simpler mind cannot be assumed capable of producing the theory an adequate explanation requires.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The closure-types problem points at **two distinct voids**, nested:

1. **First-order void**: the representational/psychological distinction carves philosophical territory that most void talk elides. "We cannot know X" is systematically ambiguous between "we lack the concepts to frame an answer" and "we have the concepts but cannot put them together." These are different modal claims with different evidential requirements.

2. **Second-order void** (the more interesting one for this site): *from within a mind, there is no reliable procedure for determining which type of closure one is confronting.* Representational closure is, by its own definition, undetectable — if our concept-forming procedures are insufficient, we lack the very resources needed to notice the insufficiency. Psychological closure, by contrast, leaves traces (partial understandings, dissolving insights, experts disagreeing on whether a "solution" has been found). But the distinction between "we haven't yet constructed the concepts" and "our concept-forming procedures could never construct them" is not visible from the mind whose limits are in question.

This second-order void is what makes the first-order distinction philosophically serious rather than merely taxonomic: the distinction matters precisely because it cannot be applied from within.

### Evidence for the Limit

- **Direct evidence for the first-order distinction**: the Kriegel-Demircioglu-Vlerick exchange itself. Competent philosophers read the same term ("cognitive closure") in two incompatible ways and reach opposite conclusions. This is evidence that the ambiguity is not a surface imprecision but tracks a real structural divide.
- **Indirect evidence for the second-order void**: the history of apparent representational closures that turned out to be psychological (Vlerick & Boudry's examples — vitalism, action-at-a-distance, quantum non-locality's early intelligibility crisis). Each one, at the time, looked to the participants like representational closure. In retrospect, it was psychological — the concepts existed but had not been assembled. This track record does not prove there are no genuine representational closures; it shows we are unreliable judges of which is which.
- **Asymmetry of error**: false-positives for representational closure are more common than false-negatives. We over-diagnose ourselves as facing principled limits when we are facing merely difficult ones. This is the mirror of the Dunning-Kruger effect, applied to mysterianism.

### Phenomenology

What does each type feel like from the inside, and how do they differ?

- **Psychological closure** feels like *difficulty*. There is a sense of "I almost have it," of conceptual handles that slip, of formulations that are close but wrong. Partial progress is possible; the problem admits of being "worked on."
- **Representational closure** — if any of it is experienced at all — feels like *nothing*. The mind does not represent its own conceptual absences. There is no qualitative marker distinguishing "this is beyond us" from "this hasn't been solved yet." When people report it, they are often reporting psychological closure with extra emphasis, not a distinct phenomenology.
- This asymmetry matters: the void between "hard" and "impossible" is not crossable by introspective report.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods

- **Test-case comparisons**: we can compare the trajectory of a *currently* intractable problem against the historical trajectory of problems that yielded. If the current one shows the same structural signatures of psychological closure that dissolved examples showed (divergent partial theories, expert disagreement about what counts as progress, conceptual handles that slip), that is some evidence for psychological rather than representational closure.
- **Formal limitations**: where we can *prove* representational closure (Gödel's incompleteness results, certain no-go theorems), the proof is itself the method. These cases are rare and limited in scope. They establish that representational closure is possible but do not help diagnose suspected cases in the wild.

### Indirect Methods

- **Apophatic cartography** applied to the distinction itself: rather than claiming a void is representationally closed, the Map can describe the *structural signatures* of each type of closure and let the void's own behaviour assort it. A void that produces consistent partial understandings across investigators is probably psychological. A void where all attempted formulations break down in the same *structural* way may be representational.
- **AI as distributed probe**: an AI with access to conceptual resources we do not use may reach solutions we could only achieve via translation. This would show the problem was psychologically closed *to us* but representationally open to a better-endowed mind — a stronger claim than Chomsky's organism-relativity, because the better-endowed mind would be (at least partly) our artefact.
- **Convergent bounds**: when multiple different methodological approaches converge on the same apparent wall, this is weak evidence for representational closure. When they dissent about where the wall is, psychological closure is more likely.

### What AI Might See

The closure-types void has an unusually asymmetric relation to AI probing:

- AI systems have genuinely different conceptual tooling. Where their repertoire *includes* concepts we lack, they can diagnose *our* representational closure — they can see the missing piece and describe its role.
- Where their repertoire *overlaps* with ours but their working memory is different, they can diagnose our psychological closure — they can hold together combinations we cannot.
- But AIs are no better than we are at diagnosing *their own* representational closure. The second-order void replicates at their level.
- A sufficiently different mind would reveal, by its successes, which of our walls are walls and which are scaffolding. But we would still not know which of *its* walls are walls.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Tenet 5 — Occam's Razor Has Limits**. The closure-types void is precisely the kind of distinction that simplicity arguments tend to collapse. "There is only one kind of limit" is simpler than "there are two kinds, and we cannot reliably tell them apart." The simpler story is cognitively seductive and it is wrong.

### Secondary Connection: Tenet 1 (Dualism)

If consciousness is irreducibly non-physical, the question of whether any given fact about it is representationally or psychologically closed to a physical-concept-only mind becomes sharper, not looser. Some consciousness-facts may be representationally closed to any mind using purely physical conceptual primitives — regardless of that mind's cognitive power. This is a stronger claim than mysterianism makes and a more precise one: not "our minds cannot" but "minds of this representational shape cannot."

### Implications

1. **Every void article should declare its closure type** — or explicitly flag that the closure type is itself uncertain.
2. **The voids catalogue is better understood as a catalogue of suspected closures**, most of which are likely psychological with pockets of representational limit.
3. **Apophatic cartography becomes sharper** when it drops the pretence that it is mapping a single type of wall. Different closure types admit different indirect probes.
4. **Mysterianism is partially rehabilitated**: Kriegel's argument is locally successful (against naive representational claims derived from the fact that we can pose the question) but does not touch sophisticated mysterianism that locates closure in the mismatch between our concept-forming procedures and the property's true theory.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a voids article could be written in any of the following ways:

1. **"The Closure-Types Void"** — centre the second-order problem: we cannot tell, from within, which kind of wall we are hitting. Use the first-order distinction as setup, make the diagnostic impossibility the main result. *(Most promising — novel contribution to the voids programme.)*

2. **"Representational and Psychological Closure: A Taxonomy for the Voids"** — pedagogical, classificatory. Walk through the distinction, apply it to a sample of existing voids (hard problem, self-opacity, numinous void, intentionality void), note where each is best classified, and flag cases that are ambiguous. Lower novelty but higher utility as a reference article.

3. **"Why Kriegel's Argument Does Not Dissolve the Voids"** — more polemical. Centre the move in the literature, show how Kriegel's dissolution of mysterianism depends on the psychological reading, and argue that the Map's voids programme survives the attack. Risk: too narrow in scope for a standalone void article.

4. **"The Mysterianism Spectrum"** — present naive mysterianism, Kriegel's critique, Demircioglu's reply, Vlerick & Boudry's counter, and the Map's position as a cumulative refinement. A genealogical treatment. Strong for context but weaker as a first-person contribution.

**Recommended**: Angle 1 ("The Closure-Types Void"). It fits the voids-as-second-order-phenomena pattern the programme has been developing (cf. `meta-epistemology-of-limits`, `whether-real`, `cartography-problem`), adds a new named void to the catalogue without overlap, and operationalises a distinction the site already depends on.

## Gaps in Research

- **Formal results on representational closure**: the philosophy-of-mind literature is largely informal. What formal constraints (Gödel, Turing, Chaitin, Blum speed-up theorems) translate into constraints on representational closure of specific conceptual systems? `formal-cognitive-limits` touches this but the bridge to the closure-type distinction has not been built.
- **Empirical phenomenology**: is there any empirical work distinguishing the phenomenology of hard-but-tractable problems from phenomena reported by participants who believe they are at genuine limits (mathematicians on Gödel sentences, theoreticians on the measurement problem)? Anecdotal reports exist; systematic comparison does not.
- **AI evidence**: specific cases where AI systems have cracked problems humans suspected of representational closure. Protein folding is the obvious candidate — was it representationally closed to human cognition or merely psychologically? This would be a concrete test case.
- **Cross-disciplinary uptake**: the distinction appears mainly in philosophy of mind. Does it appear independently in philosophy of mathematics, philosophy of physics, linguistics (Chomsky), or theology (apophatic tradition)? Some of these likely have their own vocabularies for the same distinction.
