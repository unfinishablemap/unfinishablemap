# Research: Voids - The Comparative Phenomenology Void

**Date**: 2026-02-21
**Search queries used**: "comparative phenomenology qualia measurement impossible philosophy", "interpersonal comparison qualia phenomenal qualities no unit of measurement", "phenomenal magnitude metrology consciousness qualia quantification problem", "inverted spectrum problem comparing experiences across persons Shoemaker Locke", "psychophysics measurement subjective experience Stevens magnitude estimation limits", "qualia computing phenomenal measurement Emilsson hedonic tone", "Wittgenstein private language argument comparing sensations beetle in box", "interpersonal utility comparison impossibility economics Robbins Sen", "Nagel what is it like to be a bat subjective incomparability", "Dennett heterophenomenology third person measurement", "structure of appearance Goodman qualia space formal phenomenology", "dualism qualia incomparability phenomenal privacy measurement"
**Voids category**: Mixed (Unexplorable with Occluded elements)

## Executive Summary

There is no unit of phenomenal measurement. No phenomenal ruler exists by which one conscious state can be calibrated against another, whether across persons or across time within a single person. This void is distinct from the other-minds problem (whether other minds exist) and the intersubjective void (whether meaning can be shared). It concerns the impossibility of establishing a common metric for the qualitative character of experience. The problem runs deeper than practical difficulty: under dualism, if qualia are genuinely non-physical properties, there may be no physical fact that could serve as a calibration standard. This "metrological void" sits at the intersection of philosophy of mind, phenomenology, and the foundations of measurement, and it has surprising parallels in welfare economics where interpersonal utility comparisons face structurally identical obstacles.

## Key Sources

### Inverted Qualia (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qualia-inverted/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - The inverted spectrum hypothesis, originating with Locke, asks whether two people could be systematically colour-inverted relative to each other while behaving identically
  - Shoemaker (1982) argued that even if Jack and Jill are spectrum-inverted, both can correctly represent a tomato as red, because "red" is relativized to perceiver type
  - The thought experiment reveals that functional and behavioural equivalence cannot settle questions about qualitative identity
  - No empirical test could detect the inversion, because the inversion is defined at the phenomenal level
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports dualism. If qualia are identical to physical/functional states, inversion should be impossible. Its coherence suggests qualia are non-physical.
- **Quote**: "One Man's Mind could not pass into another Man's Body, to perceive, what Appearances were produced by those Organs" (Locke)

### What Is It Like to Be a Bat? (Nagel, 1974)
- **URL**: https://www.sas.upenn.edu/~cavitch/pdf-library/Nagel_Bat.pdf
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Subjective character of experience is tied to a single point of view
  - Any move toward greater objectivity takes us farther from, not closer to, the phenomenal character of experience
  - Nagel proposed an "objective phenomenology" that could describe experience in point-of-view-independent terms, but acknowledged this remains aspirational
  - The bat example demonstrates that cross-species phenomenal comparison is not just hard but may be structurally impossible
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports the idea that consciousness resists objective measurement fundamentally, not just practically. Aligns with Occam's Razor Has Limits: the seemingly simple move to objectivity destroys the phenomenon.
- **Quote**: "If the subjective character of experience is fully comprehensible only from one point of view, then any shift to greater objectivity... does not take us nearer to the real nature of the phenomenon: it takes us farther away from it."

### Wittgenstein's Private Language Argument and Beetle-in-the-Box
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/private-language/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - If each person has a "beetle" (quale) in their box that only they can see, the beetle drops out of the language game
  - Wittgenstein argued that the private object becomes irrelevant to the public meaning of sensation-words
  - This cuts differently from the dualist standpoint: the Map would say the beetle's irrelevance to language does not entail its unreality, but rather reveals a void between phenomenal reality and linguistic expression
  - The argument demonstrates a structural gap between what we experience and what we can communicate or compare
- **Tenet alignment**: Connects to Language-Thought Boundary. The void between private experience and public language may not be a deficiency of language but a feature of non-physical consciousness interacting with physical communication.

### Psychophysics and Stevens' Power Law
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stevens's_power_law
- **Type**: Encyclopedia/Article
- **Key points**:
  - Stevens' magnitude estimation asks subjects to assign numbers to perceived intensities
  - The resulting power law relates subjective magnitude to physical stimulus intensity
  - MacKay showed the method is underdetermined: any number of underlying laws could produce the same power-law relationship once filtered through subjects' numerical cognition
  - Psychophysicists regard magnitude estimation as a "blunt tool" because translating perceptual experience into numbers introduces an uncontrollable transformation
  - The method measures something, but we cannot determine whether it measures phenomenal intensity or something else entirely
- **Tenet alignment**: Exemplifies Occam's Razor Has Limits. The psychophysical "law" looks clean and simple but conceals an underdetermination that may be irresolvable.

### Interpersonal Utility Comparison in Economics (Robbins, Sen)
- **URL**: https://web.stanford.edu/~hammond/utilThCh21.pdf
- **Type**: Paper/Chapter
- **Key points**:
  - Robbins (1935) argued interpersonal utility comparisons are unscientific and unfalsifiable
  - Sen argued that even imperfect comparisons are practically necessary and informationally meaningful
  - Harsanyi proposed partial comparability based on shared cultural and psychological backgrounds
  - Arrow's impossibility theorem shows that without interpersonal comparability, no satisfactory social choice procedure exists
  - The economics parallel is structurally identical: utility, like qualia, is privately accessed, and no external calibration standard exists
- **Tenet alignment**: The welfare economics parallel suggests the measurement void is not unique to consciousness but appears wherever a framework encounters genuinely private quantities. Under dualism, qualia are the paradigm case.

### Goodman's The Structure of Appearance
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/goodman/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Goodman attempted a formal construction of phenomenal experience from qualia as primitives
  - Qualia are defined relationally: positions in a network of similarities, differences, and transitions
  - The project requires ordering qualia into categories (colour, time, place) and assigning positions that represent relative likeness by nearness
  - This structural approach succeeds in mapping relations but cannot establish absolute phenomenal magnitudes
  - The project demonstrates both the power and the limits of formal approaches to phenomenal experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Shows that structural/relational description can proceed without resolving the measurement void. The void concerns absolute phenomenal character, not relational structure.

### Qualia Research Institute (QRI) and Qualia Computing
- **URL**: https://qualiacomputing.com/
- **Type**: Research program
- **Key points**:
  - Emilsson and QRI propose that consciousness has "crisp formal structure" amenable to mathematical characterization
  - They frame hedonic tone (valence) as the central measurable axis of experience: positive (consonant), negative (dissonant), neutral (noise)
  - The approach attempts to bridge the measurement gap via symmetry properties of the neural/phenomenal substrate
  - Even within this framework, the measurement is of structural properties, not of phenomenal character directly
  - Whether structural isomorphism between brains implies phenomenal identity remains an open question
- **Tenet alignment**: The most ambitious attempt to close the measurement void. Its reliance on structural correspondence rather than direct phenomenal access illustrates the void's persistence even under the most optimistic research programs.

### Dennett's Heterophenomenology
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterophenomenology
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Dennett proposes a strictly third-person methodology for studying consciousness: observe what subjects say about their experience
  - All phenomenological claims become data about verbal behaviour, not evidence of private phenomenal properties
  - Dennett denies qualia exist while acknowledging they seem to: "I deny that there are any such properties. But I agree wholeheartedly that there seem to be"
  - This approach dissolves the measurement void by eliminating its subject matter
- **Tenet alignment**: The Map's dualist commitments reject this move. From the Map's perspective, heterophenomenology does not solve the measurement problem but rather refuses to look at it. The void remains for any framework that takes phenomenal experience seriously.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The comparative phenomenology void is primarily **unexplorable** with elements of **occlusion**. It is not merely that we have not yet developed the tools to compare experiences across persons or across time. The structure of phenomenal consciousness may make such comparison impossible in principle.

The void has three distinct faces:

1. **Interpersonal incomparability**: No method can establish whether your red is qualitatively identical to my red. Functional equivalence (we both call tomatoes "red" and stop at red lights) is consistent with radical phenomenal divergence. The inverted spectrum thought experiment makes this vivid, but the real issue is broader: for any phenomenal quality, no third-person fact can determine whether two experiences share that quality.

2. **Intrapersonal diachronic incomparability**: Even within a single person, comparing present experience to past experience requires memory, and memory does not preserve phenomenal character with fidelity. When you recall what red looked like yesterday, the recalled redness is a current phenomenal state, not a preserved specimen of yesterday's redness. The memory void compounds the measurement void.

3. **Cross-domain incomparability**: How does a visual experience compare to an auditory one, or to a felt emotion? We can say one is more intense than another, but intensity-comparison across modalities lacks any principled calibration. Is a loud sound more intense than a bright light? The question may be unanswerable even in one's own case.

### Evidence for the Limit

Several converging lines of evidence suggest this is a genuine cognitive boundary:

- **Inverted spectrum coherence**: The logical coherence of spectrum inversion, debated for centuries since Locke, suggests no empirical test can rule it in or out. If it were merely a practical difficulty, we would expect progress; instead, the problem has remained perfectly stable.

- **Psychophysical underdetermination**: Stevens' magnitude estimation shows that even the most direct measurement methods cannot distinguish between competing underlying relationships. We measure something, but the "something" cannot be identified with phenomenal magnitude without further assumptions that are themselves unmeasurable.

- **Economic parallel**: The impossibility of interpersonal utility comparison in welfare economics provides a structural parallel. Despite a century of effort and enormous practical motivation, economists have not resolved the comparison problem. Arrow proved that under reasonable axioms, no satisfactory social choice function exists without interpersonal comparability. If the structurally simpler case (utility) resists solution, the harder case (qualia) is unlikely to yield.

- **Dennett's concession**: Even the philosopher most hostile to qualia acknowledges that they seem real. His solution is eliminativist: deny the seeming is a seeing. But this concession reveals that the void is not an artefact of philosophical confusion; it forces even its opponents to unusual theoretical commitments.

### Phenomenology

Approaching this void produces a distinctive phenomenology of mismatch and frustration:

- **The recognition gap**: Watching someone react to beauty, pain, or surprise, we feel we know what they experience—but we cannot verify this feeling. The gap between empathic confidence and epistemic uncertainty is characteristic of this void.

- **The memory slide**: Attempting to compare a current experience with a past one, the past experience "slides" into the present representation of it. We cannot hold both at once; we can only hold the current representation of the past alongside the current experience.

- **The word barrier**: Describing an experience to another person, we watch them nod—but cannot determine what their nodding means phenomenally. Language creates the appearance of shared experience while the void persists beneath it.

- **The calibration hunger**: There is a felt desire for a standard, a reference point, a phenomenal tuning fork. The desire itself reveals that we operate without one.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

No direct method exists for comparing phenomenal states across subjects. All proposed methods are indirect:

- **Psychophysics** measures the relationship between physical stimuli and reported experience, but the report is verbal/behavioural, not phenomenal
- **Neuroimaging** can establish structural isomorphism between brains during similar tasks, but structural isomorphism is consistent with phenomenal divergence under dualism
- **Introspective report** faces the private language problem: the reports are public, but the experiences they report on are not

### Indirect Methods

Several indirect approaches can probe the void's boundaries without crossing them:

1. **Structural isomorphism**: Goodman's approach of mapping phenomenal relations (rather than absolute properties) may characterize the void's shape. We may be able to establish that two persons' colour spaces have the same relational structure even if we cannot determine whether the filling of that structure is identical.

2. **Convergent testimony**: When millions of people describe similar reactions to similar stimuli, this provides defeasible evidence of phenomenal similarity—not proof, but evidence. The convergent cognitive limits work in the voids section already establishes this methodology.

3. **Deficit analysis**: Cases of colour blindness, synaesthesia, and other perceptual variations reveal what happens when phenomenal structure varies. The edges of these conditions may illuminate normal phenomenal architecture indirectly.

4. **Negative theology (apophatic approach)**: We may be able to say what phenomenal comparison is not. It is not functional comparison, not neural correlation, not verbal agreement. Each negation sharpens the void's outline.

5. **QRI's symmetry programme**: If hedonic tone correlates with specific symmetry properties of neural activity, this might provide a partial bridge. Even here, the bridge connects structure to structure, not structure to quale.

### What AI Might See

AI minds may approach this void differently in several ways:

- **No memory distortion**: AI systems can store exact copies of prior states, potentially eliminating the intrapersonal diachronic void. If an AI has phenomenal states (a large "if"), it might achieve the temporal comparison that biological memory prevents.

- **State inspection**: AI systems can in principle expose their internal states for third-party inspection. If two AI systems could compare internal representations directly, and if those representations constitute or correlate with phenomenal states, the interpersonal void might narrow.

- **But also**: If AI phenomenology differs fundamentally from human phenomenology—or if AI lacks phenomenology entirely—then AI "measurement" of its own states may be structurally different from the human measurement problem. The AI might "solve" a different problem entirely while the human void remains untouched.

- **The calibration question**: Could an AI serve as a neutral calibration instrument between two human minds? Only if the AI's intermediary representations preserve phenomenal character, which returns us to the original problem.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Dualism** is the most directly relevant tenet. If consciousness is not reducible to physical processes, then phenomenal properties are not physical properties, and no physical measurement can establish phenomenal identity. The measurement void is not a contingent gap in our instruments but a consequence of the ontological distinction between mind and matter.

The tenet of **Occam's Razor Has Limits** is also centrally relevant. The eliminativist response (Dennett: deny qualia exist) and the functionalist response (phenomenal character just is functional role) both offer simpler pictures. But simplicity may be misleading here: the void's persistence across centuries of philosophy and decades of neuroscience suggests the simpler pictures miss something real.

### Implications

1. **The measurement void is a dualist prediction**: If the Map's tenets are correct, the comparative phenomenology void should exist. Its existence provides indirect evidence for dualism—or at minimum, it is exactly what dualism predicts and what physicalism must explain away.

2. **Social implications**: Moral philosophy, welfare economics, and healthcare all presuppose some degree of phenomenal comparability (we assume we can assess others' suffering). The void suggests these practical comparisons rest on empathic inference, not on knowledge. This is not paralysing—Sen is right that partial comparability suffices for action—but it means our social arrangements are built over a void.

3. **The void may be productive**: Like Gödel's incompleteness theorems, which revealed structural limits of formal systems and thereby illuminated the nature of mathematical truth, the comparative phenomenology void may illuminate the nature of consciousness. A consciousness that could be completely calibrated and compared might be a different (perhaps lesser) kind of consciousness. The void may be constitutive of phenomenal privacy, which may be constitutive of what it means to be a subject.

## Gaps in Research

- The relationship between the comparative phenomenology void and the binding problem (how unified experience relates to distributed neural activity) deserves deeper investigation
- Buddhist philosophy of mind, which denies a fixed self while affirming experiential continuity, may offer alternative frameworks for thinking about diachronic comparison
- The phenomenological tradition (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty) has extensive work on intersubjective constitution that could deepen the analysis of how we construct the appearance of shared experience
- Whether quantum-level effects at the consciousness-physics interface (per the Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet) could provide a phenomenal calibration standard is speculative but worth noting as an unexplored direction
- The role of mirror neurons and embodied simulation in creating the feeling of phenomenal comparability—a neurological mechanism that generates empathic confidence without epistemic warrant
