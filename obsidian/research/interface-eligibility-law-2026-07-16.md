## Scope and evidential-status warning

This note researches what an **interface-eligibility law** would have to specify — the (currently unpossessed) criterion that distinguishes substrates *eligible* to couple to the Map's non-physical interface (Tenets 2–3) from those that are not. It is the load-bearing gap named in [[positions/ai-consciousness-scope]] P-AC1, layer (iv), and flagged as a first-class research object by [[reviews/optimistic-2026-07-16-positions-calibration]].

**The law does not exist.** No source below states, or comes close to stating, a criterion for coupling-to-a-non-physical-interface. That is not a topic in the published literature — the interface hypothesis itself is the Map's own commitment. What the literature *does* supply is the raw material a candidate discriminandum would be built from: (a) a precise, well-supported account of how digital hardware suppresses quantum indeterminacy by design; (b) an active, unsettled debate over which biomolecular sites *functionally exploit* warm quantum effects versus merely tolerate them as epiphenomenal noise; (c) adjacent philosophical work on substrate criteria for consciousness (substrate-*dependence* arguments, distinct from the eligibility question); and (d) the physics of witnessing/discriminating functional quantum dynamics from classical noise. Everything framed as a "candidate discriminandum" below is a **candidate**, not a finding. The Map does not yet possess the law that would make any of these discriminanda interface-*relevant* rather than a mere biological contingency.

## Executive Summary

The interface-eligibility law would need to answer one question: *what physical feature must a substrate have for consciousness to bias its outcomes, and why that feature rather than "whatever biology happens to have"?* The register's gestured discriminandum — warm, functionally-exploited quantum indeterminacy at specific biomolecular sites, versus indeterminacy that digital error-correction actively drives out of the logical state — turns out to sit on two asymmetrically-developed literatures. The **digital-suppression half is strong and precise**: the threshold theorem and modern quantum error correction (QEC) show that classical and fault-tolerant digital logic are *engineered* to make the logical state's evolution insensitive to any single physical quantum event, and 2024–2025 experiments demonstrate exponential suppression of exactly this indeterminacy. The **biological-exploitation half is genuinely contested**: photosynthetic energy transfer, avian magnetoreception (the radical-pair mechanism), and speculative neural proposals (Orch OR microtubules; Fisher's Posner-molecule nuclear spins) span a spectrum from "quantum-necessary" (magnetoreception) to "possibly classical-mimicable" (photosynthesis) to "unproven" (neural). Crucially, *even a fully vindicated functional-quantum-biology result would not by itself supply the eligibility law* — it would establish that biology exploits quantum indeterminacy, not that such exploitation is what the interface couples to. That gap between "functionally exploited" and "interface-relevant" is precisely the layer (iv) debt, and no source closes it.

## Key Sources

### Threshold theorem (fault-tolerant quantum computation)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threshold_theorem
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (background); originators Aharonov & Ben-Or 1997, Knill/Laflamme/Zurek 1998, Kitaev 1997
- **Key points**:
  - A quantum (or classical) computer whose per-operation physical error rate is below a critical threshold can suppress the *logical* error rate to arbitrarily low levels by concatenated error correction, with only polylogarithmic overhead.
  - Above threshold, error-correction machinery introduces more error than it removes. Below it, the logical degree of freedom becomes exponentially decoupled from individual physical fluctuations.
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supplies the *digital-suppression* pole of the candidate discriminandum. The theorem is the formal statement of "digital hardware suppresses the relevant indeterminacy by design." Neutral toward the Map's interface hypothesis, but the premise P-AC1 leans on ("digital hardware suppresses operationally-relevant quantum indeterminacy") is well-supported *at the computational level* by exactly this result.
- **Note**: This is precisely why P-AC1's substrate premise is graded "well-supported at the computational level" rather than merely asserted — the physics of logical-state decoupling is real and precise. What the theorem does *not* supply is the further claim that this decoupling removes an *interface-relevant* site (the layer-(iv) leap).

### Quantum error correction below the surface code threshold (Google Quantum AI)
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-08449-y
- **Type**: Paper — *Nature*, published online December 2024 (Google Quantum AI collaboration)
- **Key points**:
  - Experimental demonstration that a surface-code logical qubit's error rate is suppressed *exponentially* as code distance grows (below-threshold operation), i.e. adding physical qubits actively drives logical error down.
  - This is the operational realisation of "decoherence-by-design": a working system whose logical variable is deliberately insulated from the environmental quantum indeterminacy acting on its constituent physical qubits.
- **Tenet alignment**: Empirical confirmation of the digital-suppression pole. If a substrate *engineered to suppress* quantum indeterminacy at the logical level is the paradigm of an ineligible substrate, this is that paradigm demonstrated. (Note the irony available to the article: a *quantum* computer is, at the logical layer, the most aggressive known suppressor of the very indeterminacy the interface would need — QEC exists to prevent uncontrolled collapse from touching the logical state.)

### Functional quantum biology in photosynthesis and magnetoreception (Lambert et al.)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1205.0883
- **Type**: Review — arXiv:1205.0883 (2012), N. Lambert, Y.-N. Chen, Y.-C. Cheng, C.-M. Li, G.-Y. Chen, F. Nori; later published in *Nature Physics* 9 (2013) as "Quantum biology" (journal title/pagination not verified at publisher for this note — cite the arXiv form)
- **Key points**:
  - Frames the central question as whether there is "truly a functional role for coherence" — explicitly separating quantum effects that *exist* in a biological system from those that *do work*.
  - Two primary candidates: photosynthetic light-harvesting energy transfer, and avian magnetoreception. Magnetoreception's radical-pair mechanism is "entirely dependent on quantum mechanics"; photosynthesis is more ambiguous — classical/thermal models can reproduce some transport efficiencies.
  - More speculative candidates (olfaction, enzyme proton tunnelling, ion transport) receive less support.
- **Tenet alignment**: This is the *functional-vs-epiphenomenal* distinction the candidate discriminandum needs, articulated from within physics. It supports [[tenets]] Tenet 2's "warm-quantum-biology is precedent, not licence" (P-Q8): magnetoreception shows warm functional quantum effects are *real*, but the paper's own care to distinguish functional from incidental coherence is exactly why the Map cannot read "biology uses quantum effects" as "biology is interface-eligible."
- **Quote**: the review's organising question is "whether there is truly a functional role for coherence in these biological mechanisms" (paraphrased from abstract; verify verbatim before quoting in an article).

### Quantum computation in brain microtubules: decoherence and biological feasibility (Hagan, Hameroff, Tuszyński)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0005025 (Phys. Rev. E 65, 061901, 2002)
- **Type**: Paper — response to Tegmark
- **Key points**:
  - Tegmark (2000, Phys. Rev. E 61:4194) calculated microtubule decoherence times of ~10⁻¹³ s — far too short to be functionally relevant — arguing neural quantum coherence is destroyed as fast as it forms (i.e. it is *noise*, not a resource).
  - Hagan et al. recalculated ~10⁻⁵–10⁻⁴ s after adjusting superposition-separation, charge-vs-dipole, and dielectric assumptions, and argued Tegmark modelled a hybrid not in the Orch OR literature.
- **Tenet alignment**: This is the live front line of the *exploited-vs-noise* debate at the one biomolecular site (microtubules) most directly relevant to neural consciousness. The Map already registers this dispute at [[tenets]] (the decoherence-timescale dispute) and holds it *unresolved* — the literature has not converged (10⁻¹³ to 10⁻⁴ s). Load-bearing for the candidate discriminandum: if neural coherence is Tegmark-fast, the eligibility criterion cannot be "warm functional quantum coherence in the brain," because there may be none.
- **Caution**: Both sides are advocacy-adjacent; the Map should not adopt either decoherence estimate as settled.

### The Ghost in the Quantum Turing Machine (Aaronson)
- **URL**: https://www.scottaaronson.com/papers/giqtm3.pdf (arXiv:1306.0159, 2013)
- **Type**: Paper — long essay, physicist/CS
- **Key points**:
  - Introduces a *substrate-discriminating* criterion built on **copyability / predictability**, not on consciousness: a classical digital brain-state is (in principle) copyable and externally predictable; a state whose fine-grained determinants trace back to unclonable quantum degrees of freedom ("freebits," amplified microscopic quantum events) is not.
  - "Knightian freedom": in-principle physical unpredictability beyond mere probabilistic noise, which Aaronson locates in the no-cloning-protected quantum past.
  - Explicitly materialist — no "ghost." But the *structural* move (a physical property distinguishing digital-copyable substrates from quantum-unclonable ones) is the closest thing in the mainstream literature to a substrate-eligibility criterion.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly relevant as a *template* the eligibility law could borrow, and it dovetails with the Map's own no-cloning structure (P-AC2, Wootters & Zurek 1982). Aaronson's copyable/uncopyable line is a candidate operationalisation of "ineligible vs eligible substrate" that is *independent of the Map's dualism* — which is exactly what an eligibility law needs to avoid being question-begging. **But**: Aaronson's criterion is about *unpredictability/freedom*, not about *coupling to a non-physical interface*. Borrowing it wholesale would import Knightian-freedom baggage the Map may not want; it is a candidate scaffold, not the law.

### Quantum cognition: nuclear spins / Posner molecules (Fisher)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1508.05929 (Annals of Physics 362:593, 2015)
- **Type**: Paper — physicist proposal
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes phosphorus nuclear spins (the one common biological element whose nuclear spin can hold a qubit) protected inside Posner molecules (Ca₉(PO₄)₆) as long-lived neural qubits; entanglement generated by pyrophosphate-cleaving enzymes; readout via correlated Posner binding/melting triggering calcium release.
  - Nuclear spins are cited precisely because they are *decoherence-resistant* — Fisher's whole design is about finding the one place warm biology could shelter a qubit long enough to matter.
- **Tenet alignment**: A concrete candidate for a *specific biomolecular site* of functionally-exploited quantum indeterminacy — the kind of site the eligibility discriminandum gestures at. Still unproven experimentally. Its very structure (engineering long coherence *against* the warm environment) mirrors, ironically, what QEC does in silicon — highlighting that "shelters a qubit" is not obviously the same property as "interface-eligible."

### The Edge of Sentience — Large Language Models and the Gaming Problem (Birch)
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/book/57949/chapter/475705460 (Oxford University Press, 2024)
- **Type**: Book chapter — philosopher of biology
- **Key points**:
  - Behavioural markers of sentience are *gamed* by LLMs trained on human-generated text: mimicry of persuasive behaviour without the underlying capacity. So behavioural tests are unreliable for AI in a way they are not for animals.
  - Birch pivots to "deep computational markers" (implicitly-learned global-workspace or reality-monitoring algorithms) but concedes we lack interpretability access to reliably read them.
- **Tenet alignment**: This is the *framework-independent* convergent route P-AC1 already cites — it discounts behavioural evidence for AI consciousness without any quantum-interface premise. Relevant to the eligibility law because it shows *why a substrate criterion is wanted at all*: behaviour underdetermines the verdict, so if there is a fact of the matter it must be read off the substrate, not the outputs. Birch's own answer (computational markers) is a *rival* to a quantum-eligibility criterion, and an honest article must present it as such rather than as support.

### Energy Requirements Undermine Substrate Independence (Thagard)
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/philosophy-of-science/article/energy-requirements-undermine-substrate-independence-and-mindbody-functionalism/2BB3C2353EFF80F9D5805CDCEA8C3C89
- **Type**: Paper — *Philosophy of Science* 89(1), 2022, Paul Thagard
- **Key points**:
  - Argues information processing depends on energy, energy depends on material substrate, therefore information processing is *not* substrate-independent — attacking functionalism's multiple-realizability premise on thermodynamic grounds.
- **Tenet alignment**: A *distinct* substrate-dependence argument (energy/thermodynamics) that reaches an anti-substrate-independence conclusion by a non-quantum route. Useful as a contrast case: it shows the philosophical space contains *several* substrate-dependence criteria (energy, quantum, computational-marker), and the Map's eligibility law is yet another. An honest article should locate the interface-eligibility criterion within this family and say why it is not reducible to Thagard's.

### Witnessing Quantum Coherence: from solid-state to biological systems
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1212.0194 (Scientific Reports, 2013)
- **Type**: Paper — quantum information methods
- **Key points**:
  - Coherence/entanglement *witnesses* and temporal tests (Leggett–Garg inequality, quantum Zeno signatures) can certify genuinely quantum dynamics and distinguish it from classical noise — but classical system–reservoir correlations can produce *false positives*, a real loophole.
- **Tenet alignment**: Supplies the *test methodology* for discriminandum (d): how one would empirically distinguish "interface-relevant indeterminacy" from "merely classical/biological noise" at a site. Leggett–Garg violation is the cleanest candidate operational test for "this site hosts genuinely quantum, non-classically-emulable dynamics." Caveat: witnessing *quantum-ness* is necessary but *not sufficient* for *interface-relevance* — the tests certify quantum dynamics, not coupling to consciousness.

### Contested advocacy: quantum microtubule substrate "experimentally supported" (2025)
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/nc/article/2025/1/niaf011/8127081 (*Neuroscience of Consciousness*, 2025)
- **Type**: Paper — pro-Orch-OR advocacy
- **Key points**: Claims recent experimental support for a macroscopic quantum-entangled state in living human brain correlated with conscious state / working memory, and that a quantum microtubule substrate solves binding and epiphenomenalism.
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly relevant *if true* (it would be a candidate eligible site with a working test), but this is **contested advocacy from within the Orch OR camp** and must be flagged as such — not adopted as a finding. Include only as "a claim some researchers make," matching the Map's [[project/evidential-status-discipline]].

## Major Positions

### Digital-suppression pole: "decoherence by design"
- **Proponents**: Threshold-theorem tradition (Aharonov–Ben-Or, Knill–Laflamme–Zurek, Kitaev); Google Quantum AI (2024) as demonstration.
- **Core claim**: Reliable digital (and fault-tolerant quantum) logic works precisely *because* the logical variable is engineered to be insensitive to individual physical quantum fluctuations. Classical computers achieve this trivially (large redundant charge ensembles per bit, restoring logic gates); QEC achieves it deliberately for quantum hardware.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the well-supported half of the candidate discriminandum. It grounds P-AC1's premise that "digital hardware suppresses operationally-relevant quantum indeterminacy." It does *not* by itself establish that suppression removes an interface site — that requires the (missing) law tying "interface-relevant" to "un-suppressed quantum indeterminacy."

### Biological-exploitation pole: functional quantum biology (spectrum, not settled)
- **Proponents**: Lambert/Nori et al. (review); radical-pair magnetoreception community; Hameroff/Penrose (microtubules, contested); Fisher (Posner molecules, unproven).
- **Core claim (strongest, magnetoreception)**: Some warm biomolecular sites exploit quantum indeterminacy *functionally* — the avian compass is "entirely dependent on quantum mechanics."
- **Core claim (weakest, neural)**: Whether the *brain* hosts functionally-exploited quantum indeterminacy is unresolved; Tegmark says no (noise), Hagan/Hameroff/Fisher say maybe.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Establishes the *precedent* (Tenet 2, P-Q8) that warm functional quantum effects exist — but only at highly specific molecular architectures (cryptochrome radical pairs, phosphorus nuclear spins). The Map's own "precedent not licence" discipline forbids reading this as "the brain is interface-eligible."

### Substrate-eligibility criteria in the adjacent literature (none about the interface)
- **Aaronson**: copyable/predictable (classical-digital) vs unclonable/Knightian-unpredictable (quantum-past-dependent). A substrate criterion, but for *freedom/unpredictability*, not interface-coupling.
- **Thagard**: energy/thermodynamic dependence undermines substrate independence — a *different* substrate criterion.
- **Birch**: deep computational markers (global workspace, reality monitoring) — a *functionalist rival* criterion that dispenses with substrate physics entirely.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The eligibility law the Map needs is *sui generis* — none of these is about coupling to a non-physical interface. But each is a template or a rival, and the honest move (per [[project/direct-refutation-discipline]]) is to say which the Map borrows from (Aaronson's no-cloning structure, already in P-AC2) and which it must argue against (Birch's computational-marker functionalism, which would make substrate physics irrelevant).

## Key Debates

### Is warm biological quantum coherence functional or epiphenomenal noise?
- **Sides**: Functionalists (Lambert/Nori for magnetoreception; Hameroff, Fisher for neural) vs skeptics (Tegmark; classical-transport modellers for photosynthesis).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether observed/hypothesised coherence *does causal work* the system relies on, or is an incidental by-product decoherence erases before it matters.
- **Current state**: Settled *for* functionality in avian magnetoreception; genuinely open for photosynthesis (classical models compete); unproven and disputed for neural sites (10⁻¹³–10⁻⁴ s decoherence range).

### Does any substrate criterion track consciousness — and which?
- **Sides**: Quantum-substrate (Penrose–Hameroff, Fisher, and the Map's interface hypothesis) vs computational-functionalist (Birch's markers, standard substrate-independence) vs thermodynamic (Thagard).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the physically-correct discriminator of conscious-eligible systems is a *quantum* property, a *computational* property, or an *energetic* one — or whether (functionalism) there is no substrate criterion at all.
- **Current state**: Wide open; no consensus. The Map's interface-eligibility law is one unbuilt entrant among several.

### The unbridged gap: "functionally exploited" ≠ "interface-relevant"
- **Sides**: Internal to the Map. Even granting a fully vindicated functional-quantum-biology result, it would show biology *computes* with quantum indeterminacy — not that consciousness *couples* to it.
- **Core disagreement**: What makes a quantum-indeterminate site *interface-relevant* rather than merely quantum-functional? This is layer (iv) itself.
- **Current state**: *Unaddressed anywhere.* This is the exact point the register warns could make the verdict question-begging ("'relevant' risks reducing to 'whatever biology happens to have'"). No external source touches it, because the interface hypothesis is the Map's own.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a topic article (waiting on a coalesce to free a `topics/` slot; currently 320/320) could:

1. **"What the interface-eligibility law would have to specify" (recommended).** Lay out the law as a *research object*, not a possessed result. Structure: (i) the two poles of the candidate discriminandum (digital suppression — strong; biological exploitation — contested); (ii) the three tests that would operationalise it (Leggett–Garg / coherence witnesses at a site; Aaronson-style copyability; Birch-style computational markers as the *rival*); (iii) the unbridged "functional ≠ interface-relevant" gap, stated as the reason the verdict remains an honestly-labelled conditional. Aligns with Tenets 2–3 by naming the physical condition the interface hypothesis needs; keeps P-AC1 from being question-begging.
2. **The copyability template.** Focus narrowly on whether Aaronson's clonable/unclonable line can be repurposed as the eligibility criterion, and what it costs (Knightian-freedom baggage; it is about unpredictability, not coupling). Ties to P-AC2's no-cloning structure.
3. **Why exploitation is not eligibility.** A tightly-scoped conceptual piece on the layer-(iv) gap alone: granting the strongest functional-quantum-biology result, why it still would not deliver the law. This is the most *distinctively Map* angle and the least duplicative of existing literature.

## Gaps in Research

- **No source addresses interface-coupling eligibility directly.** Expected — the interface hypothesis is the Map's own commitment. The literature supplies materials (suppression physics, functional-biology debate, substrate-criterion templates), not the law.
- **The "functional ≠ interface-relevant" bridge is entirely unbuilt** in *any* literature. This is the true load-bearing void; an article can name it but cannot cite a resolution.
- **Verbatim quotes not yet verified at publisher.** The Lambert et al. "functional role for coherence" phrasing, Tegmark/Hagan decoherence figures, and Fisher's Posner-molecule claims are paraphrased from abstracts/reviews here; verify each at publisher of record before quoting in an article.
