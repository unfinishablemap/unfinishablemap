## Executive Summary

The Kyoto School (Kyōto-gakuha) is the twentieth-century Japanese philosophical movement that brought Zen and Pure Land Buddhism into critical dialogue with German idealism (Fichte, Hegel) and William James's radical empiricism, organised around the unifying idea of "absolute nothingness" (zettai mu, 絶対無). Its founder Nishida Kitarō (1870–1945) begins from *pure experience* (junsui keiken, 純粋経験) — experience prior to the subject-object split — and later develops the "logic of place" (basho, 場所) culminating in absolute nothingness as the formless field within which all determinate reality takes place. Nishitani Keiji radicalises this into a soteriology of emptiness (śūnyatā) that passes *through* nihilism to overcome it. For the Map this is a second "basin-edge" case (paired with the Chinese organicist article, chinese-philosophy-of-mind): a NON-SUBSTANTIALIST tradition that lands structurally near the hard problem's residue without positing a Cartesian mind-substance. The central unresolved question the downstream article must pose and hold open: does junsui keiken **dissolve** the hard problem (deflate the subject-object gap) or **dodge** it (relocate the explanandum rather than answer it)? Critically, absolute nothingness reads more naturally as a monism/non-dualism than as dualism — so the Kyoto School exemplifies the framework-dependence-vs-depth tension (Tenet 5) and must NOT be conscripted as a dualist ally.

## Key Sources

### SEP — "Nishida Kitarō"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/nishida-kitaro/
- **Author / edition**: John C. Maraldo. First published Feb 25, 2005; substantive revision Feb 21, 2024. **VERIFIED** (author confirmed via search; publication/revision dates from the entry header).
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (primary reference)
- **Key points**:
  - Pure experience (junsui keiken) *precedes* the differentiation into "subject experiencing and object experienced"; the individual is *formed out of* experience, not the reverse. Nishida questions the assumption that experience is inherently individual and subjective.
  - Influence of James and Ernst Mach acknowledged, but Nishida's version differs by its "emphasis on the non-individuated character and the seamless development of such experience" — it is a "pre-individual basis of a systematic and all-comprehensive process."
  - Logic of place (basho): a hierarchy of topoi — the *topos of being* (world of nature), the *topos of relative nothingness* (field of consciousness), and the *topos of absolute nothingness* (the ultimate "within which").
  - "Self-identity of absolute contradiction" (zettai mujunteki jiko dōitsu): opposed terms held together asymmetrically.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-conflicting. Aligns with the Map's anti-reductionism (consciousness not derived from the object side) but the non-substantialist, non-dual framing conflicts with a Cartesian reading of Tenet 1 (dualism). Best treated as basin-edge, not ally.
- **Quote (paraphrase from SEP)**: pure experience is "metaphysically ultimate activity from which both the experiencing subject and the whole range of experienced objects emerge as its self-unfolding."

### SEP — "The Kyoto School"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kyoto-school/
- **Author / edition**: Bret W. Davis. First published Feb 27, 2006; substantive revision Jul 3, 2026. **VERIFIED** (author confirmed via search).
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (primary reference)
- **Key points**:
  - The school is "a group of 20th century Japanese philosophers" drawing especially on Mahāyāna Buddhism, unified by the idea of absolute nothingness (Nishida, Tanabe Hajime, Nishitani Keiji, Ueda Shizuteru).
  - Nishitani distinguishes the *field of nihility* (vacuous nothingness besieging modernity) from the *field of emptiness* (absolute nothingness); nihilism is overcome "by way of passing through nihilism," via "trans-descendence" to a non-dualistic reaffirmation of self and world.
  - Tanabe's *metanoetics*: "absolute mediation," "absolute critique" where finite reason meets antinomies, existential death-and-resurrection through Other-power (tariki).
  - **IMPORTANT nuance for framework tension**: the school "emphasized non-dual consciousness breaking through subject-object dualism, though they explicitly rejected simple monism, prioritizing radical individuality within interconnectedness." So it is neither dualism NOR flat monism — a genuinely third framing.
  - Political caveat: the school's wartime political engagements remain "highly contentious" and require careful examination rather than blanket dismissal or endorsement.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. The explicit rejection of *simple* monism is a datum the article should surface — it complicates any easy classification and directly feeds Tenet 5 (framework-dependence).

## Major Positions

### Pure Experience (junsui keiken) — Nishida, early period
- **Proponent**: Nishida Kitarō (*An Inquiry into the Good*, 1911).
- **Core claim**: The primitive datum is undifferentiated experience "before one has judged what color or sound it is." Subject and object are *later* differentiations within pure experience, not its preconditions.
- **Key arguments**: Starting from the object side and trying to derive the subject is what *generates* the hard problem; start from pure experience and there is no gap to bridge.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the crux of the **dissolve-vs-dodge** question (see below). If it works, the explanatory gap is deflated rather than crossed. But the deflation buys its result by denying the priority of the physical/objective — which is a metaphysical commitment, not a neutral observation.

### Logic of Place / Absolute Nothingness (basho / zettai mu) — Nishida, mature period
- **Proponent**: Nishida, mid-to-late works (basho essays, ~1926 onward).
- **Core claim**: Reality is structured as nested topoi; the ultimate ground is *absolute nothingness*, a formless field (not a thing, not a substance) within which all determinate being and consciousness take place.
- **Key arguments**: "Self-identity of absolute contradiction" — the concrete real holds opposites (one/many, being/nothing) together dynamically.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Absolute nothingness is arguably a **monism/non-dualism**, not a dualism. This is the framework-dependence tension the article must hold *unadjudicated* (Tenet 5): the same phenomenon (irreducibility of experience) is read by the Map as evidence for dualism and by Nishida as evidence for a non-dual ground.

### Standpoint of Emptiness (śūnyatā) — Nishitani
- **Proponent**: Nishitani Keiji (*Religion and Nothingness*, 1961).
- **Core claim**: The modern crisis is nihility; the cure is not to retreat from it but to radicalise it into emptiness (absolute nothingness), yielding a non-dual reaffirmation of self and world.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Soteriological, not primarily an answer to the phenomenal hard problem — a reason to be careful about treating the school as a philosophy-of-mind theory in the analytic sense.

### Logic of Species / Metanoetics — Tanabe
- **Proponent**: Tanabe Hajime.
- **Core claim**: "Absolute mediation" and "absolute critique"; the self undergoes death-and-resurrection through Other-power (tariki). The "logic of species" mediates individual and universal through the social/ethnic substrate.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Peripheral to the hard problem; relevant as the school's dialectical/social wing and (with the logic of species) part of the wartime-politics caveat.

### Betweenness (aidagara) — Watsuji (relational/ethical wing)
- **Proponent**: Watsuji Tetsurō (*Rinrigaku*).
- **Core claim**: The human (ningen) is constitutively individual-AND-social; ethics and, arguably, selfhood arise in the "between."
- **Relation to site tenets**: A relational counter-model to the isolated Cartesian ego; a useful contrast axis, not a phenomenal-consciousness theory.

## Key Debates

### Dissolve vs. Dodge (the article's spine)
- **Sides**: (a) Kyoto-sympathetic reading — pure experience genuinely *dissolves* the hard problem by showing the subject-object gap is an artefact of starting on the object side. (b) Sceptical reading — it *dodges* by relocating the explanandum: asserting an undifferentiated ground does not *explain* why there is something it is like; it renames the mystery "absolute nothingness."
- **Core disagreement**: Whether deflating the subject-object distinction addresses phenomenal consciousness or merely changes the subject.
- **Current state**: Open. The article should present it as unresolved and framework-relative — NOT adjudicate it. (Same discipline as the Chinese and Jain basin-edge articles.)

### How much does Nishida really owe James?
- **Sides**: Long comparative tradition vs. Stone (2023) and others noting "no consensus" on the degree of resemblance.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether junsui keiken is a development of Jamesian pure experience or a substantially different (more metaphysically loaded, less pluralist) notion.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; err toward "kinship, not identity." **Crucially, James's pure experience underwrites NEUTRAL MONISM, not dualism** — so the James bridge connects the Map's pragmatism thread (pragmatisms-path-to-dualism) to a *non-dualist* neighbour, and must be flagged as such, not annexed.

### The wartime-politics caveat
- **Sides**: Critics reading the logic of species / "overcoming modernity" as complicity vs. defenders urging careful, non-dismissive examination.
- **Current state**: "Highly contentious" (SEP/Davis). The article should note it briefly and honestly without letting it dominate a philosophy-of-mind piece.
