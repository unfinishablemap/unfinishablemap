## Executive Summary

The aesthetics of nature is a subfield of philosophical aesthetics, largely dormant through mid-twentieth-century analytic philosophy (which had effectively equated aesthetics with philosophy of art), and revived by Ronald Hepburn's 1966 essay. The central modern debate pits **cognitivism** — Allen Carlson's view that appreciating nature *correctly* requires natural-scientific and ecological knowledge, paralleling how appreciating art requires art history — against **non-cognitivist** alternatives: Arnold Berleant's aesthetics of *engagement* (multisensory immersion, dissolving the subject-object distance of disinterested contemplation), Noël Carroll's emotional *arousal* model ("being moved by nature"), and Emily Brady's *imagination*-based account. Carlson also defends **positive aesthetics**: pristine nature, appreciated under scientific understanding, is always aesthetically good and never ugly — a striking contrast with art criticism, which routinely delivers negative verdicts. For the Map, the field's live nerve is the **observer-dependence** of aesthetic value, which the sunset-with-no-observer intuition sharpens: is natural beauty a phenomenal fact arising in a conscious encounter (Tenet-1-adjacent), or a property of the object independent of any perceiver?

### Ronald W. Hepburn, "Contemporary Aesthetics and the Neglect of Natural Beauty"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/WILBAP-3 (host volume); essay reprinted widely
- **Type**: Book chapter — the founding essay of the field (1966)
- **Venue (VERIFIED)**: in Bernard Williams & Alan Montefiore (eds.), *British Analytical Philosophy*, London: **Routledge & Kegan Paul**, 1966, **pp. 285–310** (volume: 346 pp.). - **Key points**: Mid-century analytic aesthetics had effectively reduced all aesthetics to philosophy of art, ignoring nature. - **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/foundational; establishes the art-vs-nature disanalogy the whole debate rests on.

## Major Positions

### Scientific Cognitivism / Natural Environmental Model (Carlson)
- **Proponents**: Allen Carlson; Glenn Parsons; Holmes Rolston III (a related "science-informed" line).
- **Core claim**: There is a *correct* way to appreciate nature aesthetically, and it is framed by natural science (geology, ecology, evolutionary biology) the way correct appreciation of a painting is framed by art history. Knowledge is not decoration; it fixes the appropriate object and categories of appreciation.
- **Key arguments**: (1) Analogy to art — categories of appreciation are knowledge-relative (Kendall Walton's "categories of art" adapted to nature). (2) Objectivity — cognitivism secures genuine appreciation vs. mere pleasant reaction. (3) It underwrites positive aesthetics: science reveals order, function, and adaptive fit, which read as aesthetically good.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partly **observer-independent** — aesthetic merit tracks mind-independent scientific facts about the object. This is the principal RIVAL to the Map's phenomenal-value-realism reading and must be given full weight (calibration flag below).

### Positive Aesthetics of Nature (Carlson)
- **Core claim**: Insofar as nature is pristine (untouched by humans), it is *always* aesthetically good and never ugly — there are no negative aesthetic verdicts on wild nature, in sharp contrast with art criticism.
- **Key arguments**: Scientific understanding brings order, regularity, and pattern to *any* natural environment; once seen under the right categories, all of pristine nature comes out beautiful. Critics (e.g., Malcolm Budd, Glenn Parsons in later work, Stan Godlovitch) question whether "all nature is beautiful" is defensible or merely stipulative.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral-to-tension. If beauty is always present in the object regardless of the encounter, that pulls toward observer-*independence*; but the thesis is really about the *deliverances of a knowledgeable observer*, so it can be read either way — good material for the calibrated presentation.

### Aesthetics of Engagement (Berleant)
- **Proponents**: Arnold Berleant.
- **Core claim**: Reject the disinterested, distanced, subject-object model inherited from Kant. Aesthetic experience of environment is *immersive* and continuous — the appreciator is *within* the object, engaged multisensorially, with no contemplative gap.
- **Key arguments**: Environments have no frame and no fixed viewpoint; you move through them; perception is bodily and total. Disinterestedness is an artifact of the art-gallery paradigm.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Foregrounds the *lived conscious encounter* — congenial to observer-dependence, though Berleant's project is phenomenological rather than dualist.

### Arousal / Emotion Model (Carroll)
- **Core claim**: Being emotionally moved by nature — awe, excitement, homeyness — is a legitimate aesthetic mode that cognitivism wrongly excludes. No science required.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly observer-dependent (aesthetic value shows up *as* a felt phenomenal state). Most Tenet-1-congenial rival, but framed as anti-cognitivist, not metaphysical.

### Imagination Model (Brady; Hepburn)
- **Core claim**: Imagination (and emotion, and meaning) is central to appreciating nature well; Brady adds a normative constraint of "imagining well" so the account is not anything-goes subjectivism.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Observer-dependent but disciplined — appreciation is an activity of the conscious subject, constrained by the object. A moderate ally of the Map's reading.

### Pluralist / Skeptical Middle (Budd)
- **Core claim**: Nature can be appreciated "as nature" without any one mandatory model; resist both strong cognitivism and pure engagement.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral; the deflationary/pluralist option the Map must acknowledge to avoid over-claiming.

## Key Debates

### Cognitivism vs. non-cognitivism
- **Sides**: Carlson (science-framed, objective, correct/incorrect appreciation) vs. Berleant (engagement), Carroll (arousal), Brady (imagination).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether appropriate nature-appreciation requires conceptual/scientific knowledge, or whether felt immersion, emotion, and imagination are sufficient (even primary).
- **Current state**: Ongoing; no consensus. Widely treated as *the* organizing axis of the field.

### Is "all pristine nature beautiful" (positive aesthetics) defensible?
- **Sides**: Carlson (yes, under NEM) vs. Budd, Godlovitch, later Parsons (no, or only in a weakened form).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether positive aesthetics is a substantive discovery or a stipulation built into the choice of scientific categories.
- **Current state**: Contested; a live target for critique.

### Sunset-with-no-observer: does natural beauty require a perceiver?
- **Sides**: object-realist (beauty is in the sunset whether or not anyone sees it) vs. response-dependent / phenomenal (beauty is realized only in a conscious encounter).
- **Core disagreement**: The ontological locus of aesthetic value. This is where the field touches the Map's core interests.
- **Current state**: Under-theorized within environmental aesthetics specifically; the standard literature is more concerned with the cognitivism axis. The Map can make a genuine contribution by connecting the two — but see the calibration flag.
