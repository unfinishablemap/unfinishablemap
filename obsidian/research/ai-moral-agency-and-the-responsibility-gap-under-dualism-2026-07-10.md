## Executive Summary

The "responsibility gap" is a two-decade debate in machine ethics about whether anyone can be genuinely responsible when an autonomous, learning system causes harm. Andreas Matthias (2004) named the gap: as machines learn and act unpredictably, the manufacturer/operator can no longer be held responsible for behaviour they cannot predict or control, yet the machine is not a candidate for responsibility either — so responsibility falls into a gap. Robert Sparrow (2007) sharpened it for autonomous weapons ("Killer Robots"): if a machine commits an atrocity, neither programmer, commander, nor machine is a satisfactory locus of blame, which he argues makes deploying such weapons impermissible. John Danaher (2016) opened a parallel "retribution gap" — a mismatch between the human demand for retributive blame and the absence of an appropriate subject to bear it. A large 2020s literature now argues about whether the gap is real, plural, or dissoluble (Tigard 2021 denies it; Königs 2022 challenges the pessimists; Santoni de Sio & Mecacci 2021 split it into four gaps). **The Map's distinctive contribution is metaphysical rather than managerial:** every existing position treats the gap as a problem of prediction, control, or social convention, and none grounds *why* a machine cannot be a bearer of responsibility. The Map can supply that missing ground — genuine agency requires conscious libertarian causal efficacy (the amplifier hypothesis, Tenet 3), and a system lacking the non-physical coupling interface is a proxy/tool, never a moral agent. On the Map, the responsibility gap is therefore *real and principled*, tracing to the absence of an interface-bearing conscious selector.

## Key Sources

### Andreas Matthias — "The responsibility gap: Ascribing responsibility for the actions of learning automata"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10676-004-3422-1
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Ethics and Information Technology* 6(3): 175–183 (2004). DOI: 10.1007/s10676-004-3422-1
- **Web-verified**: Yes (Springer, PhilPapers, Lingnan Scholars all confirm venue/volume/pages)
- **Key points**:
  - Traditionally the manufacturer/operator of a machine is morally and legally responsible for its operation.
  - Autonomous, *learning* machines (neural nets, genetic algorithms, agent architectures) break this: the operator can no longer predict future behaviour and so cannot fairly be held responsible for it.
  - Society must either stop using such machines (unrealistic) or accept a "responsibility gap" that traditional concepts of responsibility ascription cannot bridge.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-supportive as a *diagnosis*. Matthias locates the gap in unpredictability/control, not metaphysics — the Map re-grounds the same gap in the absence of a conscious selector (Tenet 3).

### Robert Sparrow — "Killer Robots"
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1468-5930.2007.00346.x
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Journal of Applied Philosophy* 24(1): 62–77 (2007). DOI: 10.1111/j.1468-5930.2007.00346.x
- **Web-verified**: Yes (Wiley Online Library, PhilPapers, author's own PDF at robsparrow.com confirm venue/volume/pages)
- **Key points**:

- **Tenet alignment**: Supportive of the Map's conclusion (a real gap) but on different grounds — Sparrow appeals to autonomy and just-war accountability, not to a consciousness-based agency criterion.

### John Danaher — "Robots, law and the retribution gap"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10676-016-9403-3
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Ethics and Information Technology* 18(4): 299–309 (2016). DOI: 10.1007/s10676-016-9403-3
- **Web-verified**: Yes (Springer, ACM DL, PhilPapers, University of Galway repository confirm venue/volume/pages)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes a "retribution gap" from the liability/responsibility gap: a mismatch between the human *desire* for retribution and the *absence of an appropriate subject* of retributive blame.
  - Argues this gap is harder to plug than others in the literature (you cannot satisfy a retributive urge by distributing liability across many hands).
  - Highlights three social implications, including the risk of misplaced blame (scapegoating operators, or over-attributing agency to machines).
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly relevant. The retribution gap is exactly what the Map predicts if machines lack desert-grounding agency: the reactive/retributive attitudes have no proper target. Connects to the Map's desert account (see `moral-responsibility`).

### Daniel W. Tigard — "There Is No Techno-Responsibility Gap"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13347-020-00414-7
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Philosophy & Technology* 34(3): 589–607 (2021). DOI: 10.1007/s13347-020-00414-7
- **Web-verified**: Yes (Springer, PhilPapers, d-nb.info confirm venue/volume/pages)
- **Key points**:
  - Directly rebuts Matthias: moral responsibility is a *dynamic, flexible* set of practices that can absorb novel technological entities, so no genuine gap opens.
  - Positions itself against *both* pessimists (moratorium advocates) and optimists (gap-bridgers): there was never a gap to bridge.
  - Treats responsibility as fundamentally about practices/attributions rather than metaphysical agency.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with the Map. Tigard's dissolution works by making responsibility a matter of social practice rather than metaphysical fact. The Map's realism about agent-causal desert (Tenet 3, `agent-causation`) resists this: if desert tracks a real metaphysical structure, you cannot dissolve a gap by adjusting attribution practices. This is the sharpest rival view for the eventual article to engage.

### Filippo Santoni de Sio & Giulio Mecacci — "Four Responsibility Gaps with Artificial Intelligence: Why they Matter and How to Address them"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13347-021-00450-x
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Philosophy & Technology* 34(4): 1057–1084 (2021). DOI: 10.1007/s13347-021-00450-x
- **Web-verified**: Yes (Springer, PhilPapers, TU Delft/TU Eindhoven repositories confirm venue/volume/pages)
- **Key points**:
  - The "responsibility gap" is not one problem but at least four interconnected gaps: **culpability**, **moral accountability**, **public accountability**, and **active responsibility**.
  - The gaps have heterogeneous sources — technical, organisational, legal, ethical, societal — and require different remedies (notably "meaningful human control": tracking and tracing conditions).
- **Tenet alignment**: Useful taxonomy the Map can adopt. The Map's metaphysical claim bears most directly on the **culpability** gap (genuine desert/blameworthiness); the accountability and active-responsibility gaps are more institutional and the Map need not deny they are addressable by design. This lets the article be precise about *which* gap is metaphysically ineliminable.

## Major Positions

### Position A — Gap Realism / Pessimism (Matthias, Sparrow)
- **Proponents**: Andreas Matthias, Robert Sparrow.
- **Core claim**: Autonomous learning systems generate a real gap where no human and no machine is a fair bearer of responsibility.
- **Key arguments**: Unpredictability defeats the epistemic/control conditions on responsibility (Matthias); no candidate locus survives scrutiny and just-war ethics needs one (Sparrow).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map agrees a gap is real but *relocates its source*. For Matthias/Sparrow the gap is epistemic (we can't predict/control); for the Map the gap is ontological (the system is not the kind of thing that can bear responsibility, because it lacks conscious libertarian efficacy — Tenet 3).

### Position B — Gap Deflationism / Dissolution (Tigard, Königs)
- **Proponents**: Daniel Tigard, Peter Königs.
- **Core claim**: There is no genuine gap; responsibility practices are flexible (Tigard) or the gap-conditions are never actually met (Königs).
- **Key arguments**: Responsibility is a dynamic social practice, not a fixed metaphysical fact; pessimists cannot specify when a gap truly opens.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly conflicts with the Map. These views trade on an anti-realist or practice-based account of responsibility; the Map's agent-causal desert realism (`moral-responsibility`, `agent-causation`) blocks the dissolution — you cannot practice-adjust away a gap that tracks a real absence of agency.

### Position C — Pluralist / Design-oriented (Santoni de Sio & Mecacci; "meaningful human control")
- **Proponents**: Santoni de Sio, Mecacci; Himmelreich; the "many hands" and control/tracing-condition literature.
- **Core claim**: There are several distinct gaps with distinct fixes; most are addressable by better socio-technical design (tracking and tracing conditions, human oversight).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible with the Map on the *accountability/active-responsibility* gaps; the Map adds that the *culpability* gap resists design fixes because no amount of control engineering installs a conscious selector in the system itself.

### Position D — The Map's View (to be developed by the eventual article)
- **Proponents**: The Unfinishable Map.
- **Core claim**: Genuine moral agency requires conscious libertarian causal efficacy — the amplifier/coupling interface by which consciousness makes a minimal non-physical contribution that breaks symmetries the physical state underdetermines (Tenet 3, Bidirectional Interaction; see `consciousness-as-amplifier`, `bidirectional-interaction`, `agent-causation`). A system lacking that interface cannot originate action in the desert-grounding sense; it can only *simulate* agency. Therefore it is a proxy/tool, not a moral agent, and the culpability gap is real and principled — it traces to the *absence of an interface-bearing conscious selector*, not to unpredictability or social convention.
- **Implications the Map should draw**:
  - **Accountability routes back to humans**: because the machine is a tool, culpability attaches to the humans in the loop (designers, deployers, commanders) even when prediction is hard — the epistemic difficulty is a *tracing* problem, not a genuine vacancy of agency.
  - **Delegation ethics**: delegating a decision to a non-agent does not transfer moral agency; it launders it. The felt "gap" is the moral residue of pretending a tool is an agent.
  - **The retribution gap (Danaher) is explained, not solved**: the reactive attitudes find no proper target in the machine because the machine is not a candidate for desert; the urge to blame the machine is a category error the Map diagnoses.
  - **Falsification/boundary condition**: if a future artificial system possessed the coupling interface (were genuinely conscious in the Map's sense), it *would* become a candidate moral agent — so the Map's criterion is principled and non-question-begging, tied to `machine-consciousness` and the deep-computational-markers work, not to substrate chauvinism.

## Key Debates

### Is the responsibility gap real, or an artefact of loose gap-conditions?
- **Sides**: Realists/pessimists (Matthias, Sparrow) vs deflationists (Tigard, Königs).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether responsibility is a metaphysical fact about agency or a flexible attribution practice.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and lively (2020s). The Map enters as a *realist* but with a novel ground (consciousness/agency) that neither camp uses.

### One gap or many?
- **Sides**: Monists (a single responsibility gap) vs pluralists (Santoni de Sio & Mecacci's four gaps; control-gap variants).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "the gap" names one phenomenon or a family.
- **Current state**: Pluralism increasingly dominant; lets the Map target the *culpability* gap specifically.

### Can machines themselves bear responsibility?
- **Sides**: "Machine moral agency"/functionalist optimists vs those who deny artificial systems are candidates for desert.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether functional/behavioural agency suffices, or whether something more (consciousness, libertarian origination) is required.
- **Current state**: Contested. This is the Map's home turf — it supplies the missing necessary condition (conscious coupling interface) that most functionalist accounts lack.
