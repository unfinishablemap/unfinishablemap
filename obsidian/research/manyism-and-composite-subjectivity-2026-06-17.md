## Executive Summary

"Manyism" is Luke Roelofs' name for the thesis that **many overlapping, slightly different conscious subjects exist in one place, wherever we thought there was just one** (Roelofs' own phrasing, lukeroelofs.com/mental-combination). It is the load-bearing move in his book-length defence of *constitutive panpsychism* against the combination problem (Roelofs, *Combining Minds: How to Think about Composite Subjectivity*, Oxford University Press, 2019). The strategy: instead of explaining how micro-subjects fuse into one new macro-subject, Roelofs allows that a single token experience can belong to more than one subject at once ("experience-sharing"), so composite subjects and their micro-parts coexist rather than one replacing the other. He has since defended the multiplication of subjects this implies directly ("No Such Thing as Too Many Minds," *Australasian Journal of Philosophy* 102(1), 2024, 131–146) and explored its ethical fallout (Roelofs & Sebo, "Overlapping minds and the hedonic calculus," *Philosophical Studies* 181, 2024, 1487–1506). For the Map, manyism matters as the **strongest contemporary attempt to make constitutive panpsychism work** — i.e., as the most credible monist rival to the Map's interactionist dualism on the unity/combination terrain, NOT as evidence for dualism (see [[evidential-status-discipline]]).

- **Type**: Book (monograph)
- **Verified metadata (publisher of record, OUP + Google Books)**: Luke Roelofs, *Combining Minds: How to Think about Composite Subjectivity*, Oxford University Press, 2019 (Jan 28 2019), Philosophy of Mind Series, 272 pp. Hardback ISBN 9780190859053; another printing/eBook ISBN 9780190859060 / 0190859067.
- **Key points**:
  - First book-length defence of *constitutive* panpsychism against *all* aspects of the combination problem.
  - Constitutive panpsychism: human consciousness is built up out of basic consciousness the same way human bodies are built up out of physical matter.
  - Conclusion: "composite subjectivity, in various forms, may be much more common than we think."
  - Relates mental combination to panpsychism, functionalism, and Neo-Lockean personal identity; applies results to split brains, collective intentionality, and the combination problem.
- **Tenet alignment**: CONFLICTS with Tenet 1 (Dualism) — it is a monist (Russellian/constitutive-panpsychist) program designed to make consciousness compositional, the very thing the Map's [[combination-problem]] argues fails. Engaged as a rival, not endorsed.

### "No Such Thing as Too Many Minds" (article — the canonical statement of manyism)
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00048402.2022.2084758 ; record https://philpapers.org/rec/ROENST
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Verified metadata**: Luke Roelofs, "No Such Thing as Too Many Minds," *Australasian Journal of Philosophy* 102(1), 2024, 131–146. DOI 10.1080/00048402.2022.2084758 (online-first 2022).
- **Key points**:
  - Names and defends "manyism" explicitly: "there's nothing wrong with 'manyism' — the idea that many overlapping, slightly different, conscious subjects exist in one place."
  - Within one human there may be anywhere from two (person + brain/body) to trillions of minds (via the Problem of the Many).
  - Considers metaphysical, phenomenological, and moral arguments that this multiplication is *uniquely* absurd for minds (vs. physical objects) and argues each fails: "many overlapping conscious minds is no more problematic than many overlapping physical objects."
  - Conditional on phenomenal sharing being possible.
- **Tenet alignment**: CONFLICTS with Tenet 1; relevant to Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds / indexical identity) because manyism multiplies *subjects* without multiplying worlds — a different multiplication the Map should not conflate with MWI.

### "Overlapping minds and the hedonic calculus" (article — ethical downstream)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-024-02167-x ; record https://philpapers.org/rec/ROEOMA
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Verified metadata**: Luke Roelofs & Jeff Sebo, "Overlapping minds and the hedonic calculus," *Philosophical Studies* 181, 2024, 1487–1506. DOI 10.1007/s11098-024-02167-x.
- **Key points**:
  - Neurotechnology may soon let two subjects share a single token mental state (e.g., one feeling of pleasure).
  - If subjects share few states, count the value twice; if they share many, count it once — different cases pull opposite ways.
  - Shows manyism / experience-sharing is not idle metaphysics: it generates a live problem for utilitarian aggregation.
- **Tenet alignment**: NEUTRAL on the dualism question; useful as evidence the position is taken seriously and has testable-ish stakes.

### "Can Subjects Be Proper Parts of Subjects? The De-Combination Problem"
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/rati.12166 ; record https://philarchive.org/rec/MILCSB-2
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Verified metadata**: Gregory Miller, "Can Subjects Be Proper Parts of Subjects? The De-Combination Problem," *Ratio* 31(2), 2018, 137–154. DOI 10.1111/rati.12166. (Already cited correctly in the corpus.)
- **Key points**:
  - Cosmopsychism (one cosmic mind) faces a "de-combination problem" equivalent to the combination problem: both concern subjects being proper parts of other subjects.
  - The combination and de-combination problems are the SAME impossibility approached from opposite ends — which is exactly the hinge the Map's [[mereology-of-mind]] apex already uses.
  - Proposes two escape routes: (i) distinguish absolute vs. relative phenomenal unity; (ii) modify the essential nature of subjects.
- **Tenet alignment**: NEUTRAL — a problem-statement both monist camps inherit; the Map cites it to show neither combination nor de-combination is intelligible.

- **Key points**: Argues the unity of consciousness can hold *between* distinct subjects, not only within one. - **Tenet alignment**: CONFLICTS with the Map's [[unity-of-consciousness]] reading (unity as primitive, not a between-subjects achievement). The direct counter-thesis worth naming.

## Major Positions

### Manyism (Roelofs) — the focal position
- **Proponent**: Luke Roelofs.
- **Core claim**: Wherever we assumed one conscious subject, there are really many overlapping, slightly different subjects that *share* token experiences; macro-subjects and their micro-subject parts coexist.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Experience-sharing dissolves subject-summing: you don't need micro-experiences to *vanish into* a new macro-experience; the same experience can be had by part and whole (the "mass combines" analogy).
  - Anti-combination intuitions (privacy of experience, unity requiring a single owner, unimaginability) all rest on assumptions that fail under scrutiny.
  - The multiplication of subjects ("too many minds") is no worse for minds than the already-tolerated multiplication of overlapping physical objects.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A serious MONIST rival to Tenet 1. The Map's reply (already present in [[consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition]]) is calibrated: combinability is *contested rather than secured*; the monist "helps itself to combinability without having earned it by the field's own standards." Manyism does not refute the Map; the Map does not refute manyism. Frame strictly per [[evidential-status-discipline]] — manyism's difficulties are NOT positive evidence for dualism.

### Phenomenal Bonding / Co-consciousness (Goff; Dainton; Miller)
- **Proponents**: Philip Goff (phenomenal bonding); Barry Dainton, Gregory Miller (co-consciousness).
- **Core claim**: A primitive relation between micro-subjects constitutes the macro-subject.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Already covered in [[panpsychisms-combination-problem]] (the "names the problem without explaining it" objection). A rival *to* manyism within the panpsychist family — useful contrast: manyism keeps the parts as subjects; bonding tries to forge one new subject.

### Fusionism / Layered Emergentism (Mørch; Lewtas)
- **Proponents**: Hedda Hassel Mørch (fusionism); Patrick Lewtas (layered emergentism).
- **Core claim**: Micro-subjects fuse into (or are superseded by) a new macro-subject; the old subjects cease.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map reads fusionism as "relabelling emergence." Direct foil to manyism (manyism's parts *persist*; fusionism's do not).

### Eliminating Subjects (Annaka Harris) / Cosmopsychism + De-combination (Albahari; Miller)
- **Proponents**: Annaka Harris (no subjects); Miri Albahari, Goff (cosmopsychism).
- **Core claim**: Either drop subjects entirely, or start from one cosmic subject and face the de-combination problem instead.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Already in the corpus. The de-combination problem is the Map's [[mereology-of-mind]] hinge; manyism is the opposite bet (more subjects, not one).

## Key Debates

### Does experience-sharing actually dissolve subject-summing, or rename it?
- **Sides**: Roelofs (it dissolves — sharing is intelligible, witnessed in perceptual co-presentation) vs. critics including the Map's calibrated line (combinability is asserted, not earned; "intelligible" remains contested).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether a single token experience genuinely *can* belong to two subjects, or whether "sharing" just smuggles in the unexplained unity the combination problem targets.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Schwitzgebel (NDPR 2021) grants the rigour, withholds assent.

### Is the multiplication of minds ("too many minds") a reductio or a tolerable cost?
- **Sides**: Roelofs (tolerable — symmetric with overlapping physical objects) vs. those who treat trillions-of-minds-per-skull as absurd.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether minds are relevantly different from physical objects with respect to the Problem of the Many.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; Roelofs & Sebo (2024) push it into ethics (aggregation/double-counting).

### Manyism vs. the Map's "unity as primitive"
- **Sides**: Roelofs (between-subjects unity is real and explains composition) vs. the Map (unity is intrinsic to consciousness, not built from or shared across parts).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether phenomenal unity is an achievement (compositional/shared) or a primitive.
- **Current state**: This is the cleanest point of contact between manyism and the Map; worth foregrounding in any new page.
