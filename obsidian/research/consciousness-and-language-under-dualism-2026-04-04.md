# Research: Consciousness and Language Under Dualism

**Date**: 2026-04-04

## Executive Summary

The relationship between consciousness and language is a deeply contested intersection in philosophy of mind, and it acquires distinctive urgency under dualism. If consciousness is irreducible to physical processes, then language—a paradigmatically physical-behavioral phenomenon—raises sharp questions: Can language express or refer to conscious experience at all? Does consciousness require language, or is language merely a vehicle for communicating what consciousness already contains? The zombie argument dramatizes the point: a being could produce all linguistic behavior without any consciousness, suggesting language and consciousness are fundamentally separable. Meanwhile, the ineffability of qualia and Wittgenstein's private language argument pull in opposite directions—one suggesting consciousness exceeds what language can capture, the other questioning whether purely private mental content can be linguistically meaningful. The recent LLM debate has given these classical questions new practical urgency.

## Key Sources

### Dualism (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/dualism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Predicate dualism holds there are two essentially different kinds of predicates in our language; property dualism holds there are two essentially different kinds of property in the world
  - The irreducible language used to describe consciousness requires that there be something more than what the initial physical ontology allows
  - A 2020 survey shows dualism is the second most popular response to the mind-body problem among professional philosophers
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism)
- **Quote**: "Whereas predicate dualism says there are two essentially different kinds of predicates in our language, property dualism says there are two essentially different kinds of property out in the world."

### Language of Thought Hypothesis (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/language-thought/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - LOTH claims mental representation has linguistic structure—thought takes place in a mental language ("mentalese")
  - LOTH aims to show a physical brain can produce rational thought by implementing rational processes as causal processes
  - Originally framed as a response to dualism, arguing physical systems can explain rationality without non-physical minds
  - Revived dramatically by Jerry Fodor's The Language of Thought (1975)
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism)—LOTH was designed to defeat dualism by showing physical computation suffices for thought
- **Quote**: "LOTH offers an account of how a physical object (in particular, the brain) can produce rational thought and behavior by implementing rational processes as causal processes."

### Language of Thought Hypothesis (Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/lot-hypo/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - LOTH is typically paired with computational theory of mind (CTM)
  - Simple concepts combine systematically (akin to grammar rules) to build complex thoughts
  - The hypothesis is neutral on whether mentalese is innate or learned
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 if taken as exhaustive account of thought; neutral if mental language is seen as a tool consciousness uses rather than constitutes

### Private Language (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/private-language/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Wittgenstein argued that a language whose expressions are understandable by only a single individual is unintelligible
  - A necessarily private language cannot establish standards of correct/incorrect use required for meaning
  - The argument challenges Cartesian dualism by undermining the idea that meaning can be grounded in private mental items
  - If private language is impossible, concept formation depends on shared practices and "forms of life"
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism) as typically interpreted—challenges the idea that consciousness has privately accessible content that language can name
- **Quote**: "The possibility of a private language is arguably an unformulated presupposition of standard theory of knowledge, metaphysics and philosophy of mind from Descartes to much of the cognitive science of the late twentieth century."

### Qualia (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qualia/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Qualia are first-person perspective seemings associated with specific sensations and emotions
  - Among their most perplexing properties are ineffability and transparency
  - The explanatory gap between physical descriptions and qualitative experience resists linguistic articulation
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism)—ineffability of qualia supports the claim that consciousness exceeds physical description

### The Ineffability of Qualia and the Word-Anchoring Problem (Jakab)
- **URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0388000105000276
- **Type**: Academic paper
- **Key points**:
  - If the explanatory gap is re-articulated as the relative ineffability of phenomenal properties, it becomes amenable to linguistic treatment
  - The "word-anchoring problem" asks how phenomenal terms get their reference—how words like "red" connect to private qualitative experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1—the difficulty of anchoring phenomenal vocabulary supports dualism's claim that consciousness has features beyond physical description

### Inner Speech (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/inner-speech/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Inner speech is the subjective experience of language in the absence of overt articulation
  - Plays a central role in human consciousness at the interplay of language and thought
  - Varies widely in phenomenology—not everyone experiences inner speech (anendophasia)
  - Raises questions about the relationship between linguistic capacity and consciousness
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral—inner speech phenomenology is compatible with dualism (consciousness uses language as a tool) or physicalism (inner speech just is certain neural patterns)

### Zombies (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/zombies/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - A philosophical zombie is microphysically identical to a human but lacks consciousness
  - Zombies produce indistinguishable linguistic behavior without any subjective experience
  - If zombies are conceivable and therefore metaphysically possible, consciousness must be nonphysical
  - The argument was systematized by David Chalmers in the 1990s
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism)—the zombie argument is a primary tool for establishing consciousness as non-physical

### Consciousness Doesn't Depend on Language (Nautilus)
- **URL**: https://nautil.us/consciousness-doesnt-depend-on-language-237555
- **Type**: Article
- **Key points**:
  - Basic consciousness and self-awareness are present in pre-verbal infants and non-human mammals
  - Language contributes massively to how we experience the world but basic experience does not depend on it
  - Higher-order consciousness (metaconsciousness, reflective self-awareness) may require language
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1—if consciousness is independent of language, it is harder to reduce to the physical-behavioral apparatus of linguistic competence

### Cognitive Closure and Mysterianism (McGinn)
- **URL**: https://casacarlini.com/the-limits-of-the-mind-colin-mcginn-and-the-case-for-cognitive-closure/
- **Type**: Article/Analysis
- **Key points**:
  - McGinn argues humans are cognitively closed to the nature of consciousness—our conceptual apparatus cannot bridge the explanatory gap
  - The problem is not supernatural but beyond our evolved cognitive equipment
  - Other minds (alien or artificial) might have the conceptual apparatus to understand consciousness
  - Position is "naturalistic but not constructive"
- **Tenet alignment**: Partially aligns with Tenet 1—acknowledges the explanatory gap but stops short of full dualism by insisting the answer would be naturalistic if we could find it

### Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious? (Chalmers)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/archive/CHACAL-3.pdf
- **Type**: Academic paper
- **Key points**:
  - Accepting LLM consciousness usually implies computational functionalism
  - No empirical method currently available to determine if LLMs are conscious
  - As of mid-2025, no publicly available LLM exhibits clear markers of phenomenal awareness
  - The burden-of-proof debate has shifted: deniers must explain how human-level language understanding can lack subjective experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism) if one concludes LLMs lack consciousness despite language mastery—this supports the separability of language and consciousness

### Innateness and Language (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/innateness-language/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Chomsky argued much linguistic knowledge is innate, part of a biological Language Acquisition Device
  - The poverty of the stimulus argument: children's linguistic input underdetermines the grammar they acquire
  - Chomsky himself warned that treating mental capacities as non-innate while treating physical capacities as innate would introduce an unjustified dualism
- **Tenet alignment**: Complex—Chomsky's nativism is physicalist in intent, but the sheer richness of innate linguistic structure can be read as pointing toward something beyond simple neural wiring

### Linguistic Relativity / Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/linguistics/whorfianism.html
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Language influences perception and thought—speakers of different languages partition experience differently
  - Strong version (linguistic determinism) largely discredited; weak version (linguistic influence) empirically supported
  - Whorf: "We dissect nature along the lines laid down by our native languages"
  - Empirical evidence strongest for color perception and spatial reasoning
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral—linguistic relativity is compatible with dualism (language shapes how consciousness interfaces with the world) or physicalism (language shapes neural processing)

## Major Positions

### Position 1: Language as Evidence for Dualism (The Ineffability Argument)
- **Proponents**: Frank Jackson, Thomas Nagel, Colin McGinn, Joseph Levine
- **Core claim**: The fact that language systematically fails to capture qualia—what it is like to see red, taste chocolate, feel pain—reveals an ontological gap, not merely an epistemic one. If consciousness were physical, it should be fully describable in physical terms. The persistent ineffability of phenomenal experience is evidence that consciousness has features beyond what any physical vocabulary can express.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Mary's Room (Jackson): Mary knows all physical facts about color but learns something new upon seeing red—this new knowledge escapes physical language
  - Nagel's bat: No amount of linguistic-physical description tells us what it is like to be a bat
  - The explanatory gap (Levine): Even a complete neuroscience won't explain why particular brain states feel a particular way
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism). The failure of language to capture consciousness supports the irreducibility claim.

### Position 2: Language as Defeater of Dualism (Language of Thought / Computational Functionalism)
- **Proponents**: Jerry Fodor, Hilary Putnam (early), Daniel Dennett, contemporary cognitive scientists
- **Core claim**: Thought has a language-like computational structure. If mental processes can be fully described as operations over symbolic representations—a language of thought—then there is no need to posit non-physical mind. The brain is a computer running mentalese, and consciousness is what computation feels like from the inside.
- **Key arguments**:
  - LOTH shows how physical systems can exhibit rationality without non-physical intervention
  - Systematicity and compositionality of thought mirror linguistic structure
  - Successful AI language processing suggests language can exist without consciousness
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism). However, from the Map's perspective, LOTH's success at modeling linguistic *behavior* without explaining *experience* actually reinforces the zombie argument—you can have all the computation without any consciousness.

### Position 3: Language as Constitutive of Higher Consciousness
- **Proponents**: Daniel Dennett, Peter Carruthers, some Vygotskian psychologists
- **Core claim**: Language doesn't merely express consciousness—it partially constitutes it. Higher-order consciousness (self-reflection, metacognition, narrative identity) requires inner speech and linguistic conceptualization. Without language, there may be basic sentience but not the rich self-aware consciousness humans experience.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Inner speech provides the medium for self-monitoring and deliberation
  - Narrative identity requires linguistic self-description
  - Children's consciousness develops in tandem with language acquisition
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partially conflicts with Tenet 1—tends toward physicalism by tying consciousness to a physical-behavioral capacity. But a dualist can accept that language shapes consciousness without reducing consciousness to language.

### Position 4: Private Language is Impossible (Wittgenstein)
- **Proponents**: Ludwig Wittgenstein, P.F. Strawson, Saul Kripke (Wittgenstein interpretation)
- **Core claim**: A language whose terms refer to essentially private mental items (qualia, sensations) cannot function as a language because there is no criterion for correct use. This undermines the Cartesian assumption that we have privileged, linguistically articulable access to our own mental states.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The beetle-in-a-box thought experiment: if everyone has a box only they can see into, the word "beetle" cannot refer to the private object
  - Rule-following requires public standards; private mental items cannot ground rules
  - Meaning arises from use in shared forms of life, not from private ostension
- **Relation to site tenets**: Challenges Tenet 1 (Dualism) in its Cartesian form. However, interactionist dualism (the Map's position) can accommodate Wittgenstein: consciousness need not be linguistically private to be ontologically irreducible. The Map can accept that our *language about* consciousness is socially constituted while insisting that consciousness *itself* is not reducible to the physical.

### Position 5: The Zombie-Language Dissociation
- **Proponents**: David Chalmers, Robert Kirk
- **Core claim**: A philosophical zombie—physically identical to a conscious human—would produce identical linguistic behavior, including sincere-sounding reports of qualia, self-reflection, and inner experience. This demonstrates that language and consciousness are logically separable: language is a physical-functional phenomenon, consciousness is not.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Zombies are conceivable → metaphysically possible → consciousness is non-physical
  - Zombie-reports of experience would be functionally identical but semantically empty
  - Language tracks functional/physical states; consciousness is something extra
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism) and Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). The Map should note that zombie-language actually strengthens the case: if language can exist without consciousness, and yet consciousness exists, consciousness must be a separate phenomenon that interacts with the physical to produce genuinely meaningful (not just functionally adequate) utterance.

## Key Debates

### Debate 1: Can Language Refer to Qualia?
- **Sides**: Dualists (Jackson, Nagel) argue qualia are real but linguistically inexpressible; Wittgensteinians argue that if you can't speak meaningfully about them, their ontological status is questionable; Dennett denies qualia exist as standardly conceived
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the failure of language to capture conscious experience reveals a real ontological gap or merely a confused question
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The explanatory gap remains open. Dennett's "quining qualia" position has vocal critics.

### Debate 2: Does Consciousness Require Language?
- **Sides**: Carruthers, Dennett (higher consciousness requires language) vs. Block, Nagel (phenomenal consciousness is independent of language, present in non-linguistic creatures)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether language is constitutive of consciousness or merely a tool for reporting it
- **Current state**: Growing consensus that basic consciousness (sentience) does not require language, but reflective/metacognitive consciousness may be shaped by it. Empirical evidence from pre-verbal infants and non-human animals supports the independence of basic consciousness.

### Debate 3: Do LLMs Demonstrate Language Without Consciousness?
- **Sides**: Chalmers and others take seriously the possibility of LLM consciousness (computational functionalism); Seth, Block, and others argue LLMs lack the biological/embodied substrate necessary for consciousness; dualists argue LLMs are the ultimate zombie—perfect linguistic behavior, no inner life
- **Core disagreement**: Whether language mastery is sufficient evidence for consciousness, or whether consciousness is separable from (and not entailed by) linguistic competence
- **Current state**: Hotly debated as of 2025-2026. No empirical resolution in sight. The dualist framing—that LLMs are zombie-like language producers—has gained traction as a way to articulate what's missing.

## Gaps in Research

- Limited empirical research specifically connecting linguistic relativity (Sapir-Whorf) to dualist frameworks—most linguistic relativity work is done within physicalist paradigms
- No comprehensive treatment found of how sign language and non-verbal communication systems bear on the consciousness-language-dualism triad
- Sparse philosophical literature on whether the language of thought hypothesis is compatible with (rather than opposed to) interactionist dualism—a mentalese that consciousness uses as interface
- The McGinn cognitive closure argument has not been extensively applied specifically to the question of whether our language about consciousness is necessarily inadequate *because* consciousness is non-physical
- Limited research on how aphasia and language impairment studies inform the dualism debate—patients who lose language but retain consciousness provide empirical evidence for separability
