## Executive Summary

Buddhist philosophy offers a distinctive approach to meaning that differs markedly from both Western nihilism and Western dualism. Rather than locating meaning in a cosmic purpose or eternal soul, Buddhism diagnoses the *desire* for such meaning as itself a source of suffering. The path to liberation (nirvana) involves recognizing the impermanence of all phenomena, including the self, and cultivating compassion and wisdom. This creates both tensions and surprising convergences with the project's tenets—particularly around consciousness, the nature of self, and the hard problem.

## Major Positions

### The Four Noble Truths Framework
- **Proponents**: All Buddhist schools (foundational teaching)
- **Core claim**: Life involves suffering (dukkha); suffering arises from craving (tanha); suffering can cease; the Eightfold Path leads to cessation
- **Key arguments**:
  - Impermanence (anicca) ensures nothing satisfies permanently
  - Craving for permanence, pleasure, and self creates suffering
  - Liberation is achievable through ethical conduct, meditation, and wisdom
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral framework—compatible with dualist metaphysics though not requiring it

### Anattā (No-Self) Doctrine
- **Proponents**: All Buddhist schools (with varying interpretations)
- **Core claim**: No permanent, unchanging self or soul exists; what we call "self" is a constantly changing process
- **Key arguments**:
  - The five aggregates (body, feelings, perceptions, mental formations, consciousness) are all impermanent
  - Belief in a permanent self causes attachment and suffering
  - Liberation comes from recognizing non-self
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Tension with Dualism tenet**. the project commits to consciousness as irreducible; Buddhism denies any permanent conscious self. However, Buddhism doesn't deny consciousness exists—it denies consciousness is a *permanent substance*. This may be compatible with property dualism while conflicting with substance dualism.

### Yogācāra (Mind-Only) School
- **Proponents**: Vasubandhu, Asaṅga (4th-5th century CE)
- **Core claim**: External objects are mental constructions; only consciousness is ultimately real
- **Key arguments**:
  - The "storehouse consciousness" (ālaya-vijñāna) underlies experience
  - What appears as external world is mental projection
  - Liberation comes from recognizing the mind-only nature of experience
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Partial alignment**—takes consciousness seriously as fundamental, but differs from interactionist dualism by denying external physical reality rather than affirming mind-matter interaction

### Madhyamaka (Middle Way) School
- **Proponents**: Nāgārjuna (2nd century CE)
- **Core claim**: All phenomena are "empty" (śūnyatā) of inherent existence, including both self and external objects
- **Key arguments**:
  - Neither eternalism nor nihilism is correct
  - Everything arises dependently (pratītyasamutpāda)
  - Emptiness is not nothingness but lack of independent essence
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Complex relationship**—the rejection of "inherent existence" might seem to conflict with dualism, but some interpret emptiness as compatible with conventional distinctions between mind and matter

## Key Debates

### Is Buddhism Compatible with Dualism?

**Sides**:
- Some scholars argue Buddhism is anti-dualist, rejecting any substantial distinction between mind and matter
- Others argue Buddhism is compatible with property dualism—it denies a permanent self but accepts consciousness as irreducible to physical processes

**Core disagreement**: Whether anattā (no-self) rules out all forms of dualism or only substance dualism

**Current state**: Ongoing scholarly debate. Key insight: Buddhism denies *ātman* (eternal soul) but this may not preclude all dualist positions about consciousness

### Buddhist Views on the Hard Problem

**Sides**:
- Some argue Buddhism dissolves the hard problem by rejecting the subject/object distinction
- Others argue Buddhism acknowledges the phenomenal character of experience while locating liberation in transformation rather than explanation

**Core disagreement**: Whether Buddhist meditation practices and liberation imply a position on the metaphysics of consciousness

**Current state**: Under-explored in Western philosophy of mind; potential for productive dialogue

### Ethics: Rule-Based vs. Virtue-Based

**Sides**:
- Some interpret Buddhist ethics as deontological (the precepts as rules)
- Others interpret it as consequentialist (focused on karmic outcomes)
- A third view sees it as virtue ethics (cultivating wholesome character)

**Core disagreement**: The nature of Buddhist moral reasoning and its relationship to liberation

**Current state**: Most contemporary scholars favor virtue ethics interpretation, noting that enlightened beings transcend rules while acting compassionately

## Comparison with Site Tenets

### Dualism Tenet
**Alignment**: Mixed
- Buddhism takes consciousness seriously and rejects eliminativism—it doesn't say consciousness is an illusion
- Buddhism denies a permanent, unchanging self (potential conflict with substance dualism)
- Yogācāra school treats consciousness as fundamental (partial alignment)
- Madhyamaka rejects inherent existence of both mind and matter (potential conflict)

**Key question**: Can the project's dualism be formulated as "consciousness is irreducible" without requiring "consciousness is a permanent substance"? If so, Buddhism and site tenets may be more compatible than they first appear.

### Minimal Quantum Interaction / Bidirectional Interaction
**Alignment**: Not directly addressed
- Buddhism doesn't engage with quantum mechanics (pre-modern tradition)
- Buddhist emphasis on mental causation (karma) implies mind affects physical outcomes
- Some contemporary Buddhists (e.g., Dalai Lama in dialogue with physicists) express interest in quantum consciousness theories

**Potential bridge**: Both Buddhism and the project reject epiphenomenalism—both hold that mental states have causal consequences

### No Many Worlds
**Alignment**: Neutral
- Buddhism doesn't address quantum mechanics interpretations
- Buddhist rejection of eternalism might align with rejection of MWI's infinite proliferation
- Buddhist emphasis on *this* life and *this* moment could support indexical concerns

### Occam's Razor Has Limits
**Alignment**: Partial
- Buddhist metaphysics (dependent origination, emptiness, rebirth) is not "simpler" than materialism
- Buddhism might agree that simplicity doesn't determine truth
- However, Buddhism might also question whether the complexity of dualist metaphysics is necessary for liberation
