**Date**: 2026-01-23

## Executive Summary

The energy conservation objection to interactionist dualism—that non-physical consciousness cannot influence physical matter without violating conservation of energy—has been pressed since Leibniz (1690s). Contemporary physicists like Sean Carroll argue that the Standard Model provides no room for "soul particles" or "spirit forces." However, recent work by J. Brian Pitts, Robin Collins, and others demonstrates that this objection rests on misunderstandings of how conservation laws actually work in physics. Conservation is *local* and *conditional*—it fails where external influences act. The quantum indeterminacy response provides the most promising avenue: consciousness might bias which of the quantum-permitted outcomes becomes actual without injecting energy, since all outcomes are already present in the probability distribution. The Map should develop this "selection not injection" framework: consciousness determines *which* outcome among equiprobable alternatives actualizes, rather than adding new energy or force to the physical domain.

## Key Sources

### [Cucu & Pitts - How Dualists Should (Not) Respond to the Objection from Energy Conservation (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.13643)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.13643
- **Type**: Academic paper (arXiv preprint)
- **Key points**:
  - Surveys 14 dualist responses to the energy conservation objection
  - Most responses fail because they ignore the mathematical foundations of physics (Lagrangian mechanics, Noether's theorems)
  - The key insight: conservation laws are *conditional* upon symmetries (Noether's theorem). If something breaks temporal symmetry (like a mind acting on matter), conservation fails *there and then* while holding elsewhere
  - "The most defensible approach treats conservation as conditional upon symmetries and simply accepts energy non-conservation as an aspect of interactionist dualism"
  - The real objection should come from empirical neuroscience, not a priori physics arguments
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong—supports the Map's position that physics alone doesn't refute dualism
- **Quote**: "Expecting conservation to hold in the brain (without looking) simply assumes the falsehood of Cartesianism. Hence Leibniz's objection begs the question."

### [Pitts - Conservation Laws and the Philosophy of Mind: Opening the Black Box, Finding a Mirror (2022)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9038821/)
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9038821/
- **Type**: Academic paper (Philosophia)
- **Key points**:
  - Conservation laws are *local*, not global. Energy is conserved at each spatial point due to local continuity equations, not as a cosmic sum
  - Conservation can fail in brains while holding in "refrigerators and stars"
  - Conditionality: energy conservation holds *if* there are no external influences. If immaterial minds act on bodies, they constitute external influences making violation inevitable where minds act
  - By Noether's converse theorem: conservation laws *entail* symmetries. Violation of symmetry logically entails violation of conservation
  - The traditional objection begs the question by assuming conservation holds in brains without empirical investigation
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong—directly supports Bidirectional Interaction tenet
- **Quote**: "Energy conservation holds not primarily for the whole universe, but in every place separately."

## Major Positions

### Position 1: The Conservation Objection (Leibniz/Carroll)

- **Proponents**: Leibniz (1690s), Sean Carroll (contemporary), Jaegwon Kim, Dennett, Flanagan, many mainstream philosophers
- **Core claim**: If non-physical mind could move matter, energy would be created; if matter acted on non-physical mind, energy would disappear. Either way, energy conservation is violated.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The laws of physics underlying everyday life are "completely understood" (Carroll)
  - There's no room in the Standard Model for "soul particles" or "spirit forces"
  - Any interaction would require modifying the Dirac equation with new terms
  - Existing experiments would have detected any new particles/forces interacting with electrons
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct challenge to Bidirectional Interaction. Must be addressed.

### Position 2: Locality and Conditionality Response (Pitts/Cucu)

- **Proponents**: J. Brian Pitts, Alin Cucu
- **Core claim**: The conservation objection begs the question. Conservation laws are local and conditional—they fail where external influences act.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Conservation is *local*: energy is conserved at each spatial point, not as a cosmic sum. Can fail in brains while holding in stars.
  - Conservation is *conditional* upon symmetries (Noether's theorem). Time-varying external forces violate energy conservation at the locations where they act.
  - If immaterial minds affect bodies, they constitute external influences—conservation violations are *expected*, not problematic
  - By Noether's converse: conservation entails symmetries; violated symmetry entails violated conservation
  - The real test is empirical neuroscience, not a priori physics
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly supports Bidirectional Interaction. Allows local energy non-conservation at neural sites.

### Position 3: Quantum Indeterminacy / Selection Not Injection

- **Proponents**: Henry Stapp, Friedrich Beck, John Eccles, Robin Collins
- **Core claim**: Consciousness biases *which* of the quantum-permitted outcomes becomes actual, without injecting energy. Selection operates within the probability space physics already provides.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Quantum mechanics is *not* causally closed—outcomes are genuinely indeterminate until measurement
  - The Born rule gives probabilities; something must select among equiprobable outcomes
  - Consciousness could be the selector without adding energy (all outcomes are energetically equivalent)
  - Stapp's quantum Zeno: attention as repeated "observation" prolongs desired neural states
  - Beck/Eccles: exocytosis probability is <1; consciousness biases vesicular emission
  - Quantum entanglement shows interaction without energy transfer is physically possible
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong support for Minimal Quantum Interaction—matches the "smallest possible non-physical influence" framing

### Position 4: Information vs. Energy Framework

- **Proponents**: Information philosopher tradition, David Chalmers (partially), some dual-aspect monists
- **Core claim**: Information is physical but immaterial—neither matter nor energy. Consciousness acts through information without energy exchange.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Information needs matter for embodiment and energy for communication, but isn't reducible to either
  - Mental causation operates through information constraints on physical processes
  - Local entropy decrease (information creation) is thermodynamically compensated elsewhere
  - The mind as "pure information" controls atomic-molecular components through biological structures
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible with Bidirectional Interaction if information transfer doesn't require energy

### Position 5: Accept Non-Conservation (Deflationary Response)

- **Proponents**: Some dualists acknowledge energy non-conservation as acceptable
- **Core claim**: If dualism is true, energy conservation may simply be false at the locations where minds act. This isn't problematic—conservation was only an empirical hypothesis, not a metaphysical necessity.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Conservation is an empirical finding, not an a priori truth
  - Local violations in brains are consistent with conservation holding elsewhere
  - The burden is on neuroscience to demonstrate conservation holds in neural systems—this hasn't been done
  - Dualism trivially predicts non-conservation where minds act; this is a feature, not a bug
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible but less elegant than the quantum selection response

## Key Debates

### Debate 1: Is Physics Causally Closed?

- **Sides**:
  - Yes (Carroll, mainstream physicalists): The Core Theory is complete; electrons respond only to local quantum fields
  - No (Pitts, Collins, quantum consciousness theorists): Quantum indeterminacy, entanglement, GR complications show physics is not closed
- **Core disagreement**: Whether quantum indeterminacy is "merely epistemic" or provides genuine room for mental causation
- **Current state**: No consensus. Carroll dismisses quantum wiggle room as "expressing ignorance"; Stapp argues it's ontological and exploitable

### Debate 2: Does Selection Require Energy?

- **Sides**:
  - Yes: Any physical effect requires a physical cause with associated energy transfer
  - No: Selecting among equiprobable outcomes doesn't inject energy—all outcomes were already present in the wavefunction
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the act of "choosing" or "biasing" among quantum possibilities is an energy-requiring physical process
- **Current state**: The Map should develop the "selection not injection" framework—consciousness picks which already-possible outcome actualizes, without adding anything to the physical domain

### Debate 3: What Would Count as Evidence?

- **Sides**:
  - Physics-first: Detailed mechanism required before taking dualism seriously
  - Philosophy-first: The hard problem shows physicalism fails; mechanism can come later
- **Core disagreement**: Burden of proof—does dualism need to specify the interaction mechanism, or does physicalism's failure to explain consciousness shift the burden?
- **Current state**: Carroll demands "What particles? What forces?" but Pitts argues this is question-begging. The Map's position: mechanism (quantum Zeno, synaptic tunneling) is speculative but compatible with physics; the philosophical case for dualism is strong independent of mechanism details

### Debate 4: What About the Standard Model Completeness?

- **Sides**:
  - Carroll: QFT is complete for everyday energies; no room for new interactions
  - Dualist response: Standard Model applies to closed systems; minds are external influences creating non-closed conditions
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "completeness" means "no external influences possible" or merely "complete for closed systems"
- **Current state**: Pitts's locality/conditionality argument directly addresses this—completeness holds *given* no external influences; minds provide external influence
