# Research: Decoherence and Macroscopic Superposition

**Date**: 2026-01-14
**Search queries used**: "quantum decoherence macroscopic superposition Schrödinger cat 2025", "decoherence time biological systems neural microtubules warm wet", "Stanford Encyclopedia Philosophy quantum decoherence", "quantum biology photosynthesis warm wet coherence evidence 2024 2025", "Tegmark decoherence brain consciousness critique response Hameroff", "hot cat states quantum superposition thermal noise high temperature Science Advances 2025"

## Executive Summary

Decoherence—the loss of quantum coherence through environmental interaction—was long thought to definitively rule out macroscopic quantum superpositions and quantum effects in biological systems. However, recent experimental advances (2024-2025) have significantly complicated this picture. "Hot" Schrödinger cat states have been created at temperatures 60 times higher than ground state, quantum coherence has been observed in photosynthesis and potentially in neural microtubules, and matter-wave interference has been demonstrated in nanoparticles with over 7,000 atoms. The Tegmark vs. Hameroff debate about brain decoherence remains unresolved, but the decoherence objection is weaker than previously assumed. Crucially, decoherence does *not* solve the measurement problem—it merely selects preferred bases without explaining definite outcomes.

## Major Positions

### The Decoherence Objection (Tegmark et al.)
- **Proponents**: Max Tegmark, Christof Koch, Klaus Hepp
- **Core claim**: Brain is too warm, wet, and noisy for quantum coherence; decoherence occurs in femtoseconds (10⁻¹³ to 10⁻²⁰ seconds), far too fast for neural processes (milliseconds)
- **Key arguments**:
  - Calculated microtubule superpositions would decohere in <10⁻¹² seconds
  - Neural processes operate 10⁹ times slower
  - No demonstrated mechanism for maintaining coherence in warm biological tissue
- **Relation to site tenets**: If correct, challenges Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet's physical plausibility

### The Orch OR Response (Hameroff, Penrose)
- **Proponents**: Stuart Hameroff, Roger Penrose, Jack Tuszynski
- **Core claim**: Tegmark's calculation was based on wrong model (24 nm separation instead of Orch OR's smaller values); corrected calculations give coherence times 7 orders of magnitude longer
- **Key arguments**:
  - Tegmark assumed thermal equilibrium (death, not life)
  - Hydrophobic microtubule interior provides protection
  - Debye layer of counterions screens thermal fluctuations
  - Actin gel ordering further protects coherence
  - Metabolic energy could order water and enhance protection
  - Quantum error correction may operate
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly supports Minimal Quantum Interaction if correct

### Quantum Biology Evidence (Engel, Lambert, et al.)
- **Proponents**: Graham Fleming, Greg Engel, Alexandra Olaya-Castro
- **Core claim**: Quantum effects demonstrably operate in warm biological systems—photosynthesis, bird navigation, enzyme catalysis
- **Key arguments**:
  - FMO complex shows quantum coherence in energy transfer (~99% efficiency)
  - Cryptochrome radical pairs in bird magnetoreception
  - These are warm, wet systems yet exploit quantum effects
- **Relation to site tenets**: Weakens "warm biology excludes quantum" objection

### Stapp's Quantum Zeno Approach
- **Proponents**: Henry Stapp
- **Core claim**: Decoherence objection doesn't apply to quantum Zeno mechanism—rapid repeated "observation" can maintain states without sustained coherence between observations
- **Key arguments**:
  - Uses orthodox quantum mechanics, no new physics
  - Mental attention acts like repeated measurement
  - Doesn't require coherence to survive milliseconds—just repeated rapid observations
- **Relation to site tenets**: Potentially sidesteps decoherence objection entirely

## Key Debates

### Does Decoherence Solve the Measurement Problem?
- **Sides**: Physicists who think decoherence dissolves quantum weirdness vs. philosophers who argue it relocates rather than solves the problem
- **Core disagreement**: Whether environmental entanglement selecting preferred bases constitutes an explanation of definite outcomes
- **Current state**: SEP is clear: decoherence does NOT solve measurement problem alone. Still requires interpretation (Everett, collapse, pilot-wave). This is important for the Map—decoherence doesn't eliminate the need for collapse or consciousness's role.

### Tegmark vs. Hameroff on Brain Coherence Times
- **Sides**: Tegmark (femtosecond decoherence) vs. Hameroff group (10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁴ seconds after corrections)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether Tegmark's model accurately represents Orch OR's claims; whether living systems have protective mechanisms
- **Current state**: Unresolved empirically. Recent experiments (superradiance in tryptophan, hot cat states) suggest decoherence may be more manageable than Tegmark assumed, but direct brain measurements remain elusive.

### Schrödinger's Cat: Impossible or Merely Difficult?
- **Sides**: Those who see cat as reductio ad absurdum vs. those pushing macroscopic superposition experimentally
- **Core disagreement**: Where the quantum-classical boundary lies (if anywhere)
- **Current state**: Boundary keeps being pushed. Nanoparticles with 7,000+ atoms now shown in superposition. Hot cat states at 1.8K. No principled boundary found—only practical limits.

## Key Insight for Site

The original todo asked: "Schrödinger's cat was warm and macroscopic yet in a superposed state. Explore coherence time, what decoherence really means in this context, and whether neural firing patterns could exhibit similar superposition behavior despite being 'warm and wet.'"

**Findings:**
1. Schrödinger's cat was a thought experiment, not a claim that cats *can* be superposed. In practice, macroscopic objects decohere almost instantly (quadrillions of years to delocalize a 10kg box by 1µm—and that's without decoherence events).
2. BUT: The boundary keeps moving. 7,000-atom nanoparticles, hot cat states at 1.8K, photosynthetic complexes—all show quantum effects where classical intuition said "impossible."
3. Neural coherence times remain disputed. Tegmark: femtoseconds. Hameroff: microseconds to 100 microseconds after corrections. Neither confirmed experimentally in living brains.
4. the Map's position is defensible: decoherence is a challenge, not a refutation. New experiments (hot cats, quantum biology) suggest biology may have tools to manage decoherence that weren't anticipated. The Zeno mechanism may sidestep the objection entirely.
