## Executive Summary

Phenomenal Conservatism (PC) is Michael Huemer's foundationalist epistemological principle: if it seems to S that P, then, in the absence of defeaters, S thereby has at least some (prima facie, defeasible) justification for believing P. First developed in *Skepticism and the Veil of Perception* (Rowman & Littlefield, 2001) and given its canonical general statement in "Compassionate Phenomenal Conservatism" (*Philosophy and Phenomenological Research*, 2007), PC treats *appearances of every kind*—perceptual, introspective, intuitive, memorial, moral—as a uniform source of prima facie justification. Its signature argument is the **self-defeat argument**: anyone who denies PC must still rely on how things seem to them in order to hold and argue for that very denial, so denying PC is self-undermining. The principal objections are the **crazy/rogue-seemings** problem (PC is too liberal), the **cognitive-penetration / bad-basis** problem (irrational or manipulated seemings would inherit justificatory force they do not deserve), and the **metajustification demand** (that trusting seemings itself needs independent warrant). PC is the epistemological engine behind the Map's willingness to count first-person phenomenal seemings as genuine evidence for dualist conclusions.

## Major Positions

### Phenomenal Conservatism (Huemer)
- **Proponents**: Michael Huemer (2001, 2007, 2013); sympathetic development by Chris Tucker, Matthew McGrath.
- **Core claim**: Every seeming/appearance confers *some* prima facie, defeasible justification on its content, in the absence of defeaters, regardless of the seeming's source-type.
- **Key arguments**:
  - **Self-defeat argument** (the flagship): premises are roughly (1) doxastic justification requires the belief be based on a source of propositional justification, and (2) our beliefs are in fact typically based on how things seem; so denying that seemings justify would leave the denier's own belief unjustified — the denial saws off the branch it sits on.
  - **Anti-skeptical / internalist motivation**: PC blocks perceptual skepticism without smuggling in premises the skeptic can reject.
  - **Simplicity / no-privileging**: a single principle unifies perceptual, introspective, intuitive, memorial, and moral justification, avoiding ad hoc source-by-source epistemologies.
- **Relation to site tenets**: PC is the epistemological grounding for the Map's Tenet-5 phenomenological-evidence methodology. It answers the prior question "why do first-person seemings count as evidence at all?" so that the hard problem's intuitive force and the felt reality of agency can be treated as prima facie evidence rather than dismissed.

### Dogmatism (Pryor) — the adjacent view
- **Proponents**: James Pryor.
- **Core claim**: Perceptual experience with content P immediately (non-inferentially) justifies belief that P. Narrower than PC — restricted to *perceptual* experience rather than all seemings.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Useful contrast: PC's generality (covering introspection/intuition) is precisely what the Map needs, since phenomenal evidence is introspective, not perceptual. The concept page should mark this PC-vs-dogmatism boundary.

### Critics / deniers
- **Proponents**: Peter Markie, Laurence BonJour, Michael Tooley, Susanna Siegel, Clayton Littlejohn, Jack Lyons, Luca Moretti.
- **Core claim**: PC is too liberal, or requires a metajustification it cannot supply, or wrongly ignores the causal history (basis) of a seeming.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map does not need PC in full generality — only for robust, persistent, cross-traditional introspective seemings, which are the paradigm case least vulnerable to the crazy-seemings and cognitive-penetration objections (this is already the move made in phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence).

## Key Debates

### The self-defeat argument — does denying PC really refute itself?
- **Sides**: Huemer (yes) vs. critics.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether an epistemology can consistently reject PC while accounting for its own justified acceptance. Huemer says no; critics argue there are non-self-defeating reasons to reject PC. John DePoe has defended the self-defeat argument; there is a published rebuttal ("Defeating the self-defeat argument for phenomenal conservatism") contending the argument overreaches.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. This is the single most important argument for the Map to present, since it is the argument the Map already deploys (via self-stultification) to defend taking phenomenal reports seriously.

### Crazy / rogue seemings — is PC too permissive?
- **Sides**: Markie, Tooley, Littlejohn (PC over-justifies) vs. Huemer.
- **Core disagreement**: A seeming produced by wishful thinking, prejudice, or a crystal-ball gazer's conviction would, on PC, still confer some justification. Critics call this a reductio. Huemer replies that genuine *seemings* are distinguishable from mere inclinations/wishes/guesses (they have a distinctive phenomenal "pushiness" or forcefulness), and that marginal seemings are readily defeated by background knowledge, so only *some* defeasible justification is granted.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; turns on whether "seeming" can be independently characterized.

### Cognitive penetration / the "bad basis" — does a seeming's causal history matter?
- **Sides**: Siegel (2013, and *The Rationality of Perception* 2017), Lyons (2011) — etiology can "downgrade" a seeming's justificatory power — vs. Huemer, who holds justification depends on the seeming's present content, not its provenance.
- **Core disagreement**: If an irrational background belief *causes* an experience, does the resulting seeming still justify? For the Map's purposes: the seeming that *pain hurts* is not plausibly penetrated by theory; the seeming that qualitative character is *irreducible* is more exposed. This is exactly the layer where the Map already concedes its case is weakest.
- **Current state**: Active; the most philosophically live objection.

### The metajustification demand
- **Sides**: BonJour (2004) — trusting seemings requires independent evidence they are reliable — vs. Huemer, who argues the demand either leads to a vicious regress (every source would need a meta-source) or is question-begging, and that PC is a legitimate candidate for the foundational role because its rivals covertly rely on it.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; connects directly to the foundationalist regress problem.
