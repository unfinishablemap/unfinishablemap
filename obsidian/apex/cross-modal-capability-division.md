The Unfinishable Map has worked the same division across five senses one modality at a time: in each case the brain extracts and organises candidate content without consciousness, and conscious experience contributes phenomenal unity, ownership, and flexible deployment. Vision states it first; hearing, smell, touch, and proprioception each restate it in their own register. Read side by side, these five treatments invite a structural question none of them answers from inside itself: *does the division recur across the senses, and if it does, what recurs?* The answer is more interesting than a simple "yes." The brain-side machinery does not recur—it is irreducibly modality-specific, with no shared substrate across V1, the cochlea, and the olfactory receptor code. The conscious/unconscious boundary, by contrast, carries a *common supramodal signature* that decodes across vision, hearing, and touch. That asymmetry—modality-specific brain side, supramodal mind side—is the genuinely new content. It is also, on the Map's own discipline, the place where a physicalist rival accounts for the same data, so the asymmetry sharpens the question rather than settling it.

## Five Statements of One Division

The division was stated first for vision, the most data-rich sense and the sharpest test case. [[capability-division-in-vision|Capability division in vision]] assigns to the brain feature extraction (orientation, colour, motion in V1), action guidance (the dorsal stream's reaching and grasping, intact in patient DF), affective tagging, and blindsight discrimination—all demonstrably proceeding without phenomenal awareness. It assigns to consciousness perceptual organisation that tracks awareness, detection and spontaneous report, perceptual *ownership* (information available not merely to the system but *to someone*), and flexible deployment for open-ended reasoning. The two-streams dissociation gives this a rough neuroanatomical map: recognition (ventral) leans on consciousness, action-guidance (dorsal) largely does not.

Each subsequent modality restates the division while removing one of vision's conveniences, and the removals are the point. [[auditory-consciousness-and-the-interface|Auditory consciousness]] strips away the spatial scaffolding: sounds from many sources arrive superimposed in a single pressure wave, so auditory scene analysis (Bregman, 1990) must bind by frequency proximity, harmonicity, and common onset rather than by shared location. The brain-side capabilities deliver organised candidate streams; the experienced unity of *hearing one sound* is read as the mind-side contribution. Because the grouping cannot be read off a spatial layout, the auditory case shows the division is not an artefact of vision's same-place heuristic.

[[chemosensory-consciousness-and-the-interface|Chemosensory consciousness]] removes the scaffolding entirely. A smell corresponds to no single molecular property—mirror-image carvone enantiomers smell of spearmint and caraway—because olfaction uses a combinatorial receptor code rather than dedicated lines. Odour quality space has no smooth dimensional ordering to anchor an identity claim, and flavour binds gustation, retronasal olfaction, and oral somatosensation with no common spatial frame at all. [[tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface|Tactile consciousness]] adds further registers the distance senses cannot: perception that constitutively requires movement (active haptics has no passive equivalent), a perceiver who is simultaneously perceived (the touching hand is also touched), affect woven into contact through dedicated C-tactile afferents, and a felt body boundary the rubber-hand illusion can move. [[dual-domain-capabilities-in-proprioception-and-spatial-imagination|Proprioception and spatial imagination]] take the same architecture inward, dissociating the non-conscious body schema from the felt body image (Sacks's patient Christina), and dissociating spatial computation from phenomenal imagery across the aphantasia spectrum.

Each article gestures at the others—the auditory case "generalises that division beyond the visual modality," touch "reads the experienced unity of feeling one object as the mind-side contribution"—but none establishes the recurrence as a claim with its own evidence. That is the gap this synthesis fills, and filling it well requires being precise about *what* recurs.

## The Brain Side Does Not Recur

The first half of the structural finding is a negative one, and it is easy to miss because the division *labels* recur even though the *machinery* does not. There is no shared brain-side substrate across the senses. Vision begins with retinotopic feature maps in V1 and the ventral/dorsal split. Hearing begins with cochlear frequency decomposition and tonotopic maps, and its hardest computation—separating concurrent sources—has no spatial map to work from. Smell begins with roughly four hundred receptor types whose combinatorial activation pattern, not any single line, encodes an odour (Malnic et al., 1999), and the represented space is high-dimensional and categorical rather than retinotopic. Touch begins with several mechanoreceptor populations plus a parallel affective fibre class, and its discriminative content often arrives only through self-generated movement.

These are not variations on one mechanism. A deflationary account that explained away the explanatory gap in vision by appeal to V1 feature integration would have nothing to say about the combinatorial olfactory code or about why a still finger has no roughness-quale. The proprioception article makes this "substrate divergence" point explicit for memory, proprioception, and spatial imagination: because the capability-specific machinery cannot be shared, a reduction that worked at one site would not automatically transfer to the others. The same holds across the exteroceptive senses. The brain side is a family of unrelated solutions to unrelated transduction problems.

This matters for the recurrence claim's honesty. If someone says "the same division recurs across the senses," the natural reading is that *one thing* recurs—and on the brain side, nothing does. What recurs on the brain side is only the *form* of the labour (extraction and organisation of candidate content), realised by machinery that shares no substrate. The genuine cross-modal commonality, if there is one, has to live on the other side of the division.

## The Mind-Side Boundary Carries a Common Signature

The second half of the finding is positive, and it is where the external evidence enters—evidence no per-modality article uses. Sanchez and colleagues recorded magnetoencephalography while participants received near-threshold tactile, visual, and auditory stimuli, then decoded conscious from unconscious trials *across* modalities. Training a classifier on one sense and testing on another, they found that "supramodal spatiotemporal neural activity patterns predicting conscious perception of the feeble stimulation" generalise across senses (Sanchez et al., 2020). The pattern is late (post-stimulus) and, strikingly, includes activity in primary sensory regions belonging to *other* modalities—visual-cortex activity helping predict whether a near-threshold sound was consciously perceived.

This is the external grounding the recurrence claim needs. Without it, "the conscious/unconscious boundary recurs across the senses" would be a bare inductive generalisation over the Map's own articles. With it, there is a measured, modality-general signature of the transition into awareness. The conscious/unconscious boundary—the very threshold the per-modality articles each locate within their sense (perceptual organisation in vision, scene analysis in hearing, the limbic-tinged percept in smell)—appears to have a common neural signature regardless of which sense feeds it. The mind side is where the cross-modal commonality lives, exactly opposite to the modality-specific brain side.

So the structural finding is an asymmetry: **modality-specific brain machinery, supramodal mind-side boundary.** That asymmetry is what generalising across the senses establishes—not the recurrence of a single divided mechanism, but the recurrence of a *boundary* fed by unrelated machinery. It is a more disciplined and more interesting claim than "the division recurs," and it is the apex-grain content the five articles cannot voice from inside their own modality.

## The Inward Case That Inverts Both Terms

The asymmetry has so far been read off five exteroceptive and proprioceptive senses—the senses that point outward, or that locate the body in space. Turning the survey fully inward, to interoception (the body's sense of its own physiological condition: cardiac, respiratory, gastric, thermal, nociceptive signal), supplies the case that tests whether the asymmetry is a law of perception or a regularity of the *distance* senses. It is the latter. [[interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface|Interoception]] inherits the capability division—most visceral regulation runs below threshold, a narrow band crosses into awareness—but it inverts the apex's headline asymmetry on *both* of its terms. The inversion does not weaken the finding; it locates its scope, and the scope is the discovery.

**The brain side converges.** The negative half of the asymmetry—nothing recurs on the brain side, because V1, the cochlea, and the olfactory receptor code are unrelated transducers feeding unrelated cortices—holds because the distance senses *have* separate transducers feeding separate cortices. Interoception's afferent channels are equally heterogeneous (cardiac, respiratory, gastric, thermal, nociceptive lines with distinct peripheral physiology), yet they converge on a *shared* cortical integrator. Craig identifies the insula, re-represented posterior-to-anterior, as the visceral-integration site across these channels, with the anterior insula as the cortical image of the felt bodily self (Craig, 2002). Critchley and colleagues supplied direct evidence that this convergence carries the portion reaching awareness: right anterior insular activity predicts heartbeat-detection accuracy, and grey-matter volume there correlates with both objective accuracy and subjective visceral awareness (Critchley et al., 2004). So "the brain side does not recur" is exposed as an exteroceptive regularity, not a structural necessity of perception. When a single integrator serves many channels, the brain side converges; the modality-specificity was contingent on the distance senses' separate transducers all along.

**The mind side changes type.** The positive half of the asymmetry was that a supramodal access *boundary* recurs while the mind-side *contribution* (phenomenal unity, ownership) varies. Interoception is the limiting case at the vivid-ownership end. Visceral states are barely presented as detached perceptual objects: a felt heartbeat, a wave of nausea, the ache of cold arrive already valenced and already owned. The mind-side term does not merely recur weakly here—it changes type, from the phenomenal unity of a perceived object to the affective ground of subjecthood itself. Three predictive-processing literatures converge on this reframing: Seth's interoceptive-inference account treats felt emotion as active inference on visceral causes, with the embodied self as the high-precision portion (Seth, 2013); Barrett and Simmons's Embodied Predictive Interoception Coding model unifies allostatic regulation and felt experience in one predictive architecture (Barrett & Simmons, 2015); Park and Tallon-Baudry propose continuous visceral monitoring as a "neural subjective frame" from which first-person experience is generated (Park & Tallon-Baudry, 2014). On all three, interoception is the felt substrate *of* subjecthood, not content delivered to it. The mind-side "ownership" term the apex foregrounds is not faint here, as it is in smell; it is total.

The sharpened statement is therefore: the asymmetry—modality-specific brain machinery, supramodal mind-side boundary—holds for the distance and proprioceptive senses; interoception inverts it, the brain side converging and the mind side becoming affect-and-ownership. The recurrence claim was never universal, and saying so precisely is stronger than asserting it everywhere. Interoception is the revealing exception that marks the edge of the regularity, and the apex's structural finding is the more honest for carrying its own scope condition.

The outward/inward partition this scope condition rests on is itself not a clean line. [[thermal-consciousness-and-the-interface|Thermoception]] is the boundary case: skin thermoreceptors report at once the temperature of the world (exteroceptive) and the body's own thermoregulatory state (interoceptive), carried by a single lamina I pathway to the insula (Crucianelli & Ehrsson, 2023; Craig, 2002). A modality genuinely both at once straddles the middle of the outward/inward axis rather than falling on either end, so the asymmetry runs as a gradient—exteroceptive at one pole, interoception-inverted at the other, thermal on the line. Per the [[common-cause-null|common-cause null]] this straddling eighth source does not strengthen the asymmetry claim, only locate its edge.

This inward inversion is, by the Map's [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]], no more probative than the supramodal signature it parallels. A convergent insular integrator is exactly what a physicalist expects if the body needs one homeostatic hub—there is metabolic economy in routing visceral state to a common cortex—and interoceptive inference predicts the affective, self-constituting character of visceral experience with no non-physical remainder. The convergent substrate and the predictive construction are what *both* pictures expect; the brain-side fact is neutral between the readings, and the mind-side reframing favours only *some* self-constituting account, of which the leading one is physicalist. The inversion changes what the apex's terms mean inward without changing the register in which they are held. And because interoception converges on a single insular hub, that hub is maximally a common cause: on the [[common-cause-null|common-cause null]] the interoceptive evidence is one datum about the inward limit, not a stack of independent confirmations of the interface.

## The Silent Frame: The Asymmetry at Its Brain-Side Extreme

Interoception inverts the asymmetry; the vestibular sense pushes it to its limit. Where the inward case turns *both* terms over, [[vestibular-consciousness-and-the-interface|the vestibular sense]] leaves the asymmetry pointing the same way and drives each term to an extreme—maximal brain-side labour, minimal felt object—making it the case that tests whether the mind-side term is one contribution or a family with more than one member.

The brain-side extreme is structural. Vision, audition, and somatosensation each begin in a dedicated unisensory primary cortex; the vestibular sense has none. Pfeiffer, Serino, and Blanke put it directly: "While for vision, audition, and somatosensation specific unisensory primary cortices have been identified, no such unisensory vestibular cortex seems to exist in the human brain" (Pfeiffer, Serino & Blanke, 2014). The signal is multisensory by construction—woven into visual, somatosensory, proprioceptive, and motor representations from the moment it reaches cortex, with no stage at which it is processed *as itself*. The negative half of the apex's finding reached its sharpest exteroceptive form in olfaction's combinatorial code, a substrate maximally unlike a retinotopic map. Vestibular cortex is more radical: not an unrelated substrate but no proprietary substrate at all, a sense whose whole brain-side existence is as a frame for the others.

The mind-side extreme matches it. The Stanford Encyclopedia notes "a paucity of vestibular experiences in normal consciousness" besides dizziness (de Vignemont, 2024): in health the sense is nearly silent, with no proprietary quale answering to the otoliths' report of gravity. Yet its contribution is not faint, only *non-objectual*—it supplies the felt spatial frame of the self, self-location and first-person perspective, anchoring the subject to gravity's allocentric axis. That the silent frame is load-bearing shows in its failure: vestibular disturbance triggers depersonalization (Cento et al., 2026) and, at the limit, the out-of-body experience, where Blanke and Arzy model self-location detaching from the body as vestibular, visual, tactile, and proprioceptive signals fail to integrate at the temporo-parietal junction (Blanke & Arzy, 2005). Vestibular input even moves bodily ownership: galvanic and caloric stimulation modulate illusory hand ownership (Lopez, Lenggenhager & Blanke, 2010), the very ownership the touch article reads as the mind-side contribution at its most vivid.

This is why the case is load-bearing and not a seventh restatement. The disanalogy section already concedes that ownership is vivid where the body is in the loop and faint in smell; the vestibular sense shows the gradient has *two* axes. Smell is faint-object and faint-frame; vestibular is faint-object yet foundational-frame. So the mind-side term is not a single scale from smell to touch but a family of distinct contributions—object-unity (touch, hearing), owned affect (interoception), and the situatedness of the viewpoint (vestibular). The viewpoint-frame is a member the object-centred senses never isolate, because in them it is always fused with a felt object; naming it turns the apex's "differently-weighted family" from a hedge into a structure.

The case is held, like every other, as consonant-not-probative, and the common-cause cap bites hardest here. Laurens and Droulez model vestibular perception as Bayesian estimation against a gravity prior, with no quale doing explanatory work (Laurens & Droulez, 2007), and Blanke synthesises self-location, perspective, and ownership as products of multisensory integration with vestibular signals one weighted input among many (Blanke, 2012)—a sense that is almost all sub-threshold frame-coding is exactly what such inference predicts. Because the vestibular signal *is* multisensory integration, with no separate channel to converge from beyond the temporo-parietal hub it shares with vision, touch, and proprioception, it cannot count as independent confirmation. On the [[common-cause-null|common-cause null]] it is the survey's most thoroughly shared-upstream datum: not a seventh converging line but a deeper sounding of the architecture the other lines already tap. It extends what the asymmetry *means* without adding to how strongly it is confirmed.

## The Rival That Predicts the Same Datum

A supramodal signature of conscious access is precisely what the strongest physicalist theory of consciousness already predicts—and naming that rival honestly is required by the Map's [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]], which forbids treating a shared explanandum as proof of the interface. The Global Neuronal Workspace hypothesis holds that conscious access is a single, modality-general mechanism: a sudden, late, non-linear "ignition" with frontoparietal involvement that amplifies a representation and broadcasts it to many cognitive modules—report, memory, reasoning (Mashour, Roelfsema, Changeux & Dehaene, 2020; see [[global-workspace-theory|global workspace theory]]). On this account the modality-specific cortex does the feature extraction and the modality-general workspace does the making-available. The Sanchez supramodal signature reads, on the workspace view, as the broadcast itself: the late, cross-modally decodable, other-modality-recruiting pattern is what global ignition looks like in MEG.

The labour split is the same one the Map draws. The workspace assigns extraction to modality-specific cortex and flexible availability to a modality-general mechanism—which is exactly the brain-side/mind-side division, with the second term read as a neural broadcast rather than a mind-side contribution. The supramodal signature is therefore a *shared explanandum*, not a confirmation of the interface. Both theories expect a common cross-modal signature of conscious access; the datum does not discriminate between them. The Map must out-accommodate the workspace, not enlist Sanchez against it, and the per-modality articles already model this restraint—each engages enactivism and predictive processing as rivals to out-accommodate rather than allies to conscript.

Where the Map claims the workspace under-describes the phenomena is the *ownership* term. The vision article distinguishes information available *to the system* from information available *to someone*; global broadcast explains availability-to-the-system—why content becomes reportable, memorable, reasoned-over—but not why there is a subject the content is available to. That gap is the Map's value-add, and it is also, candidly, where direct cross-modal evidence is thinnest: there are supramodal-access experiments but, as the research underlying this synthesis notes, no direct cross-modal *ownership* experiments. The ownership claim is a place the workspace looks incomplete, not a place the Map has independent evidence the workspace lacks.

## Where the Division Does Not Cleanly Recur

The disanalogies carry at least as much weight as the convergence. Three of them constrain the recurrence claim directly.

**Ownership is uneven across the senses.** The mind-side term the Map foregrounds—experience owned by a subject—is vivid in touch and proprioception and comparatively thin in smell. Touch stages the owner explicitly: the touching hand is also a touched object, and the rubber-hand illusion shows the felt body boundary to be a movable construction. Proprioception's whole dual-domain structure turns on the felt body image that Christina lost while retaining motor function. Smell, by contrast, presents quality and affect with little sense of an owned perceptual *object* at all; there is a live reading on which olfactory states are closer to affective or interoceptive states than to detached representations. So "ownership is the mind-side contribution" does not recur uniformly—it is strong where the body is in the loop and faint where it is not. The inward case treated above is one limit: in [[interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface|interoception]] the mind-side term is ownership at its most total, and the brain side converges rather than staying modality-specific—which is why the asymmetry is exteroceptive, not universal. The [[vestibular-consciousness-and-the-interface|vestibular sense]] treated above marks an orthogonal limit that shows the gradient runs on more than one axis—a faint felt object alongside a foundational frame. The recurrence is of a *family* of mind-side contributions whose members are differently weighted by modality, not of one contribution present in equal measure everywhere.

**Binding and awareness are doubly dissociable.** The Map reads experienced unity—of a heard stream, a felt object, a flavour—as the mind-side contribution across modalities. But integration and awareness come apart in both directions: some cross-modal integration occurs without awareness, and awareness can occur without integration (Deroy, Chen & Spence, 2014). Multisensory integration is therefore not a reliable marker of consciousness, and the dependence that does hold is task-specific—awareness of a cue is necessary for *some* multisensory bindings to influence behaviour, not all. The honest form of the Map's claim is narrow: *phenomenal* unity tracks awareness, not that all binding requires it.

**The conscious boundary is internally graded.** Even within a single modality the transition into awareness is not a clean cut. In vision, local contour completion can proceed without awareness while global gestalt completion appears to require it, and some perceptual integration leaves a neural signature indistinguishable from the conscious case (Fahrenfort et al., 2017). The boundary marks where integration reliably *tracks* awareness, not an on/off switch. A supramodal signature of the transition is therefore compatible with the transition being graded and partly modality-bound within each sense—the common signature may be a common downstream broadcast *of* heterogeneous, locally graded events rather than a single upstream mechanism. The recurrence is of a graded boundary with a shared late signature, not of a uniform cut.

These disanalogies do not refute the structural finding; they calibrate it. What recurs is an asymmetry—modality-specific machinery, a supramodal access signature—whose mind-side term is a differently-weighted family rather than a single uniform contribution, whose binding claim must be stated as *phenomenal unity tracks awareness*, and whose boundary is graded rather than sharp. The proprioception article also flags the corresponding count discipline for the inward cases: memory, proprioception, and spatial imagination share a single upstream (the explanatory gap each presupposes), so they are one cross-substrate pattern recurring at several sites, not several independent confirmations. The same discipline applies here. The cross-modal convergence is one pattern with shared upstream architecture, and on the Map's [[common-cause-null|common-cause null]] that shared upstream caps how much independent confirmation the convergence can supply.

## Synthesis

Laying the five modality treatments beside one external literature yields a claim none of them states and none could state alone. The capability division does recur across the senses—but what recurs is an asymmetry, not a single mechanism. On the brain side, nothing is shared: V1 feature maps, cochlear frequency analysis, the combinatorial olfactory code, and mechanoreceptor populations are unrelated solutions to unrelated transduction problems, so only the *form* of the brain-side labour recurs, never its substrate. On the mind side, the conscious/unconscious boundary carries a common supramodal signature that decodes across vision, hearing, and touch and even recruits the sensory cortex of irrelevant modalities. The cross-modal commonality lives entirely on the side opposite the machinery.

That asymmetry comes with its own scope condition, and naming it is part of the finding. It holds for the distance and proprioceptive senses; turning the survey inward to interoception inverts both terms—the brain side converges on a single insular integrator, the mind side becomes affect and ownership outright. So the asymmetry is an *exteroceptive* regularity with a revealing inward exception, not a universal law—with thermoception marking the line itself, a single modality reporting both world and body at once. The disciplined claim is sharper for carrying that boundary: the modality-specific brain side recurs over the senses with separate transducers, and the inward case where the transducers feed a common hub is exactly where it stops recurring. The vestibular sense marks the other edge—not an inversion but the asymmetry at its limit, a modality with no unisensory cortex and almost no felt object that nonetheless supplies the spatial frame of the subject—and so the mind-side term resolves into a family rather than a single contribution.

That asymmetry is consonant with the interface reading without being probative of it, because the Global Neuronal Workspace predicts the very same supramodal signature and reads it as a neural broadcast. The signature is a shared explanandum; the Map's distinctive wager is on the ownership term the broadcast under-describes—a wager strong on argument and thin on direct cross-modal evidence. And the disanalogies set the ceiling: ownership is uneven across the senses, binding and awareness doubly dissociate, and the conscious boundary is graded rather than sharp. The disciplined statement is therefore: *the form of the brain-side division recurs over modality-specific machinery; the mind-side access boundary recurs as a supramodal signature; the mind-side contribution is a differently-weighted family rather than a single uniform thing; and the whole convergence is one pattern with shared upstream architecture, consistent with the interface reading and equally consistent with its strongest rival.* That is more than any one sense shows, and less than a proof—which is exactly the register the Map's perception articles have held throughout.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: The recurrence of the division across senses with no shared brain-side substrate is what makes it structural rather than a vision artefact—the division is not an accident of how one sense happens to be wired. The Map reads the brain-side/mind-side asymmetry as the operational shape of an ontological distinction. But the supramodal signature does not *establish* that distinction; the workspace reads the same datum physicalistically, and per [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]] this synthesis holds the asymmetry as consonant-not-probative.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: The mind-side term across modalities is not a passive screen. Active haptics requires the perceiver's movement to deliver content; auditory and chemosensory attention foreground one stream or one flavour; spatial imagination shapes physical action. The Map interprets these as the mind side participating in perception, consonant with bidirectional interaction—an interpretive alignment the enactivist and predictive-processing rival re-describe in wholly physical terms.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]**: The asymmetry's mind side—a common access boundary fed by unrelated machinery—is the kind of signature that sits below the resolution at which current neuroscience can localise a mechanism, which is why the boundary "resists precise drawing" in every modality article. No cross-modal datum establishes the quantum interface; the dependence runs from the framework to the reading of the data, not the reverse.

The Map does not claim the senses jointly settle dualism. It claims that generalising the capability division across them yields a precise and previously unstated structural fact—modality-specific machinery, supramodal access boundary—which any adequate theory of perception must accommodate, and which the interface reading accommodates without strain while its strongest rival accommodates equally well.
