# Competency Without Felt Experience

A single *Escherichia coli* cell senses its surroundings, holds a few seconds of memory, decides whether things are improving, and swims accordingly—with no neurons. A sponge coordinates a whole-body contraction with no neurons and no synapses. A Venus flytrap counts. The immune system learns, remembers, and tells self from non-self across a lifetime. Half a billion neurons in the gut wall run digestion when the brain is disconnected. Frog cells freed from their embryo self-assemble into novel organisms that navigate and self-replicate. And at the far edge, a dish of cultured human cortical neurons—real synapses, real oscillations—learns to play Pong. Every one of these systems performs work that earns, without strain, the full vocabulary of cognition: sensing, memory, valence, adaptive decision, goal-pursuit, even a functional "self."

On the Map's framework, none of it is evidence that anything is felt. That is the thread this synthesis pulls tight. Taken one at a time, each case is a curiosity a materialist and a dualist can both absorb. Taken together—as a ladder running from prokaryotes through neuron-less animals, plants, the immune system, and the enteric nervous system, up to engineered neural tissue—they make a cumulative argument no single rung makes alone: that **cognitive-grade competency and phenomenal presence are separate axes**, and that the boundary of consciousness, wherever it lies, is not read off the sophistication of a system's behaviour. The Map draws two structural conclusions from that. First, the consciousness-physical interface is tied to specific neural machinery rather than to functional competence in general (Tenet 3). Second, minimality forbids positing an interface where there is no neural substrate to host one (Tenet 2). The argument is abductive and defeasible throughout; the discipline that keeps it honest is the subject of the last third of this piece.

## The Ladder and Its Two Axes

The cases sort onto two orthogonal axes, and seeing the geometry is half the synthesis. The first axis descends the biological tree, stripping away neural substrate rung by rung.

- **Bacterial chemotaxis** is the floor: sensing, memory, and adaptive decision compressed into one cell, with no neurons and no multicellularity beneath it. Everything above adds machinery to a competence already fully present here.
- **Neuron-less animals**—sponges and placozoans—coordinate whole-organism behaviour with zero neurons and zero synapses, running on paracrine chemistry and ciliary coupling. They sit below the cnidarian nerve net: multicellular, but neurally empty.
- **Plants** occupy the multicellular non-neural rung, performing whole-body electrical signalling and time-windowed counting with no neurons at all.
- **The immune system** learns and discriminates self from non-self through clonal selection, a mechanism as Darwinian as natural selection—again with no neurons.
- **The enteric nervous system** has neurons in abundance—hundreds of millions—but runs its reflex circuits semi-autonomously, outside the brain, and outside any felt agency.
- **Basal and bioelectric cognition**, Michael Levin's programme, supplies engineering-grade agency: rewritable body plans stored in voltage, xenobots that navigate and self-replicate—systems whose principal investigator pointedly declines to claim they feel.

The second axis is not about *less* substrate but *differently placed* substrate, and it holds two edge cases that keep the argument from collapsing into "no neurons, no mind."

- **Brain organoids** are the mirror image of the sponge floor: real cortical neurons, real synapses, emergent oscillations—but no body to act with, no world the activity is about, no biography. Substrate without behaviour, where neuron-less animals are behaviour without substrate.
- **Fish** are orthogonal to both. They are full vertebrates with nervous systems, nociceptors, and flexible behaviour. What varies at the fish rung is not the presence of neural machinery but its *architecture*: the teleost pallium is everted and non-laminated, lacking the neocortical homologue that in mammals houses the pain network.

Between them these axes span the space: from behaviour with no substrate, through substrate with no world, to substrate with the "wrong" architecture. The adjacent survey of [[consciousness-in-simple-organisms|simple organisms]]—nematodes, *Hydra*, slime moulds—fills the eukaryotic rungs between bacteria and the animals. The claim of this piece is that every point in that space returns the same verdict, and that the verdict is more informative for being reached from so many directions at once.

## The Shared Move: Competency Is Not Experience

Each source article performs the same operation, which its authors and the Map both name the **sub-personal-competence reading**: treat the agency, memory, and goal-directedness as genuine *function*, and treat the behavioural markers of that function as *defeasible*—decisive nowhere on their own. The move is a decoupling. What a system can do and whether there is something it is like to be that system come apart.

The decoupling gets its cleanest demonstrations where the machinery is fully specified. The *E. coli* run-and-tumble loop is one of the most completely characterised signalling pathways in biology: receptor binding, phosphotransfer to the flagellar motor, and a methylation feedback that stores the recent-concentration baseline. Every word in the cognitive vocabulary—"sense," "decide," "remember"—names a real dynamical feature of that loop, and the loop is complete without remainder. The sponge sneeze and the placozoan feeding pause are likewise explained end to end by purinergic and glutamatergic chemistry, leaving no residue a controller, still less an experiencer, needs to fill. When the mechanism is this complete, adding felt experience on top adds an entity the account did not need.

Two disciplines keep the move from overreaching in the other direction. The first is that a *parts-list is not a machine, and a machine is not a mind*. The demosponge genome carries a nearly complete set of post-synaptic protein orthologues—the scaffold for a synapse—in an animal with no synapses; reading experience off a molecular toolkit would be exactly the over-inference to avoid. The second is that removing a defeater is not supplying evidence. This matters most for the loaded words. The immune system has a self in the functional sense—a maintained capacity to track and defend its molecular identity—and, on the Map's framework, no self in the phenomenal sense. Alfred Tauber's warning that immunology's "self" is a theory-laden metaphor does not embarrass the Map; it sharpens the very distinction the Map insists on, between a functional self that genuinely learns and a felt self that the functional facts never deliver.

A single caution runs under all of it, and the piece states it once here rather than at every rung: the absence of felt experience is never claimed as *proven*. It is what the Map's neural-interface commitment *licenses withholding*. These competencies give no purchase on the phenomenal question either way, because the same behaviour is generated in full without the substrate any candidate theory of experience would require. The hard problem is left exactly where it stood.

## The Discipline Cuts Both Ways

Here is the synthesis no individual rung voices, because it is visible only when the fish case is set beside the sub-personal ones. The same decoupling does *opposite* dialectical work at different points on the ladder.

At the sub-personal rungs, the decoupling blocks an **inflationary** inference. Impressive, goal-directed behaviour tempts the step "therefore probably conscious"—the engine of most over-attribution. Bacteria, sponges, plants, and xenobots break that step: they supply the behaviour and, on the Map's reading, host no experiencer. So behavioural richness cannot by itself be positive evidence for consciousness, because it is present in full where even sympathetic experts decline to assert feeling.

At the fish rung, the identical discipline blocks a **deflationary** inference. The skeptics' argument runs: felt pain in humans localises to specific cortical networks; fish lack a homologue; therefore fish do not feel. The move from the first two premises to the conclusion quietly assumes that felt pain *is constituted by* the cortical architecture that correlates with it—a reductive identity, or in a subtler version a claim of functional necessity. Grant the identity and architectural absence guarantees phenomenal absence. The Map declines the identity, and with it the entailment. "Fish lack the neocortex" stops settling "fish lack felt pain." Notably, the Map here defends *openness* rather than withholding: it does not assert fish feel, but it refuses to convert missing architecture into a proof of absence, and treats the rich behavioural markers—nociceptors, opioid modulation, analgesia-reversible trade-offs—as suggestive though defeasible.

The two moves look opposed—one refuses to read experience *in*, the other refuses to read it *out*—but they share a single root. Both are refusals to let a third-person fact settle the first-person question. At the sub-personal rungs the tempting fact is *functional richness*; at the fish rung it is *architectural absence*. Neither closes the explanatory gap, because the gap is precisely the place where third-person description runs out. The ladder is therefore not a machine for lowering credence in animal minds. It is a demonstration that the phenomenal question is invariant under third-person evidence of either sign—and that invariance is the deepest thing the cluster shows.

## Answering the Rivals

A cumulative argument earns its keep by meeting the strongest positions that read the same evidence differently. The cluster's rivals sort by the direction of their over-reach.

**Reading experience into competence.** The sharpest is Reber and Baluška's **Cellular Basis of Consciousness**: sentience and life are coterminous, the excitable membrane of a single cell already feels, and consciousness reaches back to the origin of life. CBC makes exactly the inference the Map denies—from adaptive, membrane-based sensing to feeling. The Map answers at the framework level rather than by winning the empirical dispute about whether bacterial "decision" is proto-choice or protein-level noise. It grants CBC's genuine premise—excitable membrane biology really does run continuously from bacteria to neurons—while denying that continuity of *biology* carries continuity of *phenomenality*. CBC posits feeling precisely where the Map's commitments license its absence; that is a framework-relative disagreement, honestly labelled, not a demonstration either way. William Bechtel and Leonardo Bich's argument that *eating is cognition*, grounded in chemical coordination rather than neurons, meets the same reply: the Map grants the competence freely and withholds only the experience, and the neuron-less animals are where that wedge goes in most cleanly.

**Keeping competence and consciousness apart.** Several rivals are better read as allies, and the Map leans on their restraint as evidence. Pamela Lyon's **biogenic cognition** treats cognition as continuous with life while keeping consciousness explicitly separable—a claim about function, not feeling—which the Map can largely adopt, holding only the one line that functional cognition does not entail phenomenal experience. Levin and Dennett's "cognition all the way down" applies the intentional stance below the whole organism as an instrumentalist licence for agency-talk that deliberately does not smuggle in phenomenality. Irun Cohen's cognitive immune "self" is expressly computational. In each case the working scientist's own care—Levin bracketing xenobot sentience, Cohen keeping cognition functional—is the restraint the Map treats as data. Where a reader might slide from "the system cognises" to "the system experiences," these authors decline the step, and their declining is part of the cumulative case.

**Reading experience out on structural grounds.** The fish skeptics—James Rose, Brian Key's "structure determines function"—err in the opposite direction from CBC, and are answered by the opposite move. "Structure determines *function*" is unobjectionable engineering; "structure determines *feeling*" is a metaphysical identity claim wearing a bioengineering principle's clothes. The Map targets only the second. That Key's article drew more than forty commentaries, most rejecting the conclusion, records that the cortical-necessity premise is contested inside mainstream comparative science, not only from a dualist vantage.

The unifying diagnosis is that both over-reaches—CBC's and Key's—commit the same error in mirror image: they let a third-person fact (functional continuity in one case, architectural absence in the other) stand in for a verdict on experience. The cluster's discipline is to refuse both.

## What the Cumulative Pattern Can and Cannot Carry

The whole ladder pointing one way is suggestive; treating it as more than suggestive would repeat, at the apex level, the exact over-inference the cluster warns against. Three calibrations set the ceiling.

First, the convergence is partly **framework-generated**. The rungs are not fully independent witnesses: each reaches its verdict by applying the same underlying commitment—experience is tied to a neural interface—so their agreement is in part the framework consulting itself. This is the [[common-cause-null]] applied to the Map's own synthesis. The abductive support is real but discounted, in the same way the [[apex/taxonomy-of-voids|taxonomy of voids]] discounts its own convergence for a selection effect. What survives the discount is modest and worth stating precisely: the cluster shows that behavioural and functional markers, however rich, *do not settle* the phenomenal question—a claim about the limits of a kind of evidence, which even a committed materialist can grant.

Second, each marker is **defeasible and substrate-sensitive**, decisive nowhere on its own. Weighting toward phenomenal markers over bare competency markers cannot mean discarding behavioural evidence altogether, because there are no non-behavioural third-person markers of consciousness to fall back on—that very absence *is* the explanatory gap. This is why the Map's practice across the ladder stays graded rather than eliminative: the fish rung can still treat its markers as suggestive, and edge-of-sentience frameworks can still grade credence by behavioural criteria, without contradicting the sub-personal rungs.

Third, the verdict is **framework-relative, not a proof of absence**. At no rung has the Map shown that nothing is felt. It has shown that the phenomenal question is not answerable from the competence, in either direction. The brain-organoid edge makes this vivid from the positive side: the neural machinery is really present, and its presence still settles nothing, because oscillation and closed-loop learning are competence signals and none of them is an experiencer signal. The Map lands near the precautionary conclusions of the organoid-ethics literature, but for its own reason—physical-interface uncertainty, not complexity-scaled probability.

## Synthesis

What does the ladder say that no rung says alone? Three things.

That the **decoupling is universal across the tree of life**, not a quirk of one hard case. A single article about bacteria, or plants, or the immune system, can always be met with "but that one is too simple to feel." The cluster removes the escape by running the same result from the prokaryotic floor to engineered human cortex, and from behaviour-without-substrate to substrate-without-world. Wherever competence is found, experience is not thereby found.

That the **discipline is bidirectional**, and this is its non-obvious payload. The same refusal to let third-person facts settle the phenomenal question blocks inflation at the sub-personal rungs and deflation at the fish rung. A framework that only ever lowered credence in non-human minds would be a machine for denial; a framework that only ever raised it would be credulous. The Map's is neither, because its instrument is a symmetric one—the explanatory gap cuts both the "therefore conscious" and the "therefore not" inferences.

And that the pattern furnishes **calibrated abductive support for the interface being neural-specific**, not proof. If consciousness were constituted by functional organisation, systems realising that organisation—bacteria, immune networks, xenobots, organoids—would be candidates for experience, and they are the systems almost no one counts. That the interface tracks specific neural machinery rather than sophistication in general is what the pattern is consistent with and points toward, once the framework-generated share of the convergence is subtracted out. The Map claims that ceiling and not one inch above it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The cluster bears on four of the Map's [[tenets|five tenets]].

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)]]** is where the payload lands. The tenet frames consciousness as interfacing with the physical world at some localisable point, which immediately raises the question of where that interface bottoms out. Each sub-personal rung answers the negative half: there is no neural substrate here for an interface to occupy, so if adaptive, goal-directed behaviour is nonetheless present—and it plainly is—such behaviour cannot by itself mark the interface. The immune system does the cognitive-grade work and is not where mind gets in. That supports the positive claim: the interface is tied to particular neural machinery, not to functional competence in general.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)]]** cuts hardest at the floor. Minimality counsels the smallest non-physical footprint the record demands. Positing an experiential interface where the biochemistry is complete and there is no neural substrate at all would be the maximal posit, not the minimal one. The principle is *no interface where no substrate*—and, at the organoid edge, no interface merely because neuron count or oscillatory complexity crosses a threshold.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Tenet 1 (Dualism)]]** is what the decoupling illustrates. If consciousness were reducible to functional organisation, competence and experience could not come apart as cleanly as they do here. Their coming apart is consistent with dualism—indeed it is what dualism leads one to expect—though on its own it does not decide between dualism and a functionalism that locates experience in a *specific* functional organisation. The cluster narrows the field without closing it.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)]]** guards both flanks. It blocks the deflationist from wielding parsimony as a proof that nothing is felt, and blocks the inflationist from reading experience off behavioural sophistication. Simplicity is an unreliable guide when the phenomenal layer is, by hypothesis, invisible to the third-person evidence parsimony weighs. The honest verdict the cluster protects is the non-simple one: functional competence that is real, and felt experience that its presence never establishes.
