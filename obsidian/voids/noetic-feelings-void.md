Consciousness does not terminate inquiry by argument. It terminates inquiry by feeling. The sense that a recalled answer is right, that a face is familiar, that an explanation is sufficient, that something is *obvious*—these *noetic feelings* decide, from inside, when to trust, when to search further, when to stop. The Unfinishable Map names the noetic feelings void for the structural limit this creates: the signals that gate cognition are produced by heuristic machinery consciousness cannot inspect, and any attempt to audit those signals must deploy more signals of the same kind. The void is occluded at its surface and unexplorable at its core—a recursive opacity at the centre of cognitive self-governance.

The feelings are transparent in a specific and treacherous sense. They do not present themselves as feelings; they present their objects. A feeling of knowing presents as *awareness that one knows*. A feeling of understanding presents as *direct apprehension of how things fit together*. A feeling of obviousness presents as the absence of anything needing investigation. This mode of presentation hides the affective-heuristic character that cognitive science has, over decades, repeatedly established. What consciousness receives is a verdict with no visible derivation—and uses the verdict to decide whether the verdict needs checking.

## The Family of Noetic Feelings

Noetic feelings—also called epistemic or metacognitive feelings—form a large and functionally integrated family. Each member gates a distinct cognitive operation while sharing the same opacity pattern.

- **Feeling of knowing (FOK)** — the sense that a currently-inaccessible answer is nonetheless known. Gates retrieval persistence.
- **Feeling of rightness (FOR)** — the sense that an initial intuitive answer is correct. Gates engagement of analytic thought.
- **Feeling of familiarity** — the sense that a stimulus has been encountered before. Gates recognition responses.
- **Tip of the tongue (TOT)** — the sense that a specific item is imminent in retrieval. Gates search strategy.
- **Feeling of understanding** — the sense that an explanation has landed. Gates explanatory inquiry.
- **Feeling of obviousness** — the sense that a claim is self-evident and needs no further grounding. Gates whether a question ever arises at all.
- **Feeling of coherence** — the sense that disparate elements fit together. Gates pattern-acceptance.
- **Feeling of confidence** — the sense that a judgment is reliable. Gates commitment to action.
- **Feeling of error** — the dysphoric sense that something is wrong, often pre-conceptual. Gates corrective search.

Slawa Loev argues these are "mild affective experiences" whose felt character "tends to elude us introspectively" precisely because of their mildness. Joëlle Proust's framework treats metacognition as procedural rather than metarepresentational: feelings of knowing or of being right drive epistemic control without any articulated thought about cognitive states. The feelings do their gating work beneath the level of reportable judgment.

The Map treats [[epistemic-emotions|epistemic emotions]] as the phenomenally rich end of this family—curiosity, doubt, insight, confusion—where the felt signal has narrative texture. Noetic feelings sit a level lower: minimal, verdict-like, often unlabeled. The family extends from vivid insight to barely-detectable confidence shifts, all sharing the same opacity pattern. The phenomenology of [[consciousness-and-creativity|insight specifically]]—the "aha" experience catalogued by creativity research on restructuring, incubation, and sudden solutions—offers the clearest empirical window on one member of the family, and exhibits the same recursive opacity as the rest: the breakthrough presents itself as direct apprehension of a solution, not as the fluency-and-coherence signal the experimental evidence implicates.

## How the Feelings Are Produced

Convergent evidence from philosophy and cognitive science identifies noetic feelings as outputs of heuristic processes. Asher Koriat's work on the feeling of knowing concluded that "rather than reflecting direct access to memory traces, noetic feelings are based on inferential heuristics that operate implicitly and unintentionally." Two principal heuristics have been characterised: *cue-familiarity* (fast assessment of whether a retrieval cue itself seems familiar) and *accessibility* (assessment of how much partial information comes to mind). Neither heuristic is available to conscious inspection; each produces a felt verdict and delivers it, finished, to awareness.

Valerie Thompson's research on the feeling of rightness showed that FOR is driven by *generation fluency*—the ease and speed with which the first answer came to mind—not by the answer's actual correctness. High fluency produces high FOR, which terminates analytic engagement. Manipulating fluency (font legibility, rhyme, perceptual ease) shifts FOR without changing the underlying reasoning. The signal that tells consciousness "this is right" is a fluency trace, and the trace is invisible as such.

A 2021 *Synthese* paper formalises the resulting epistemic problem as the "Lima problem": epistemic feelings *inform* us about content we cannot independently access, so we have no way to cross-check their accuracy except through more feelings. The best-supported account—evaluativism—holds that noetic feelings are outputs of a distinct, primitive metacognitive mechanism operating on heuristic correlates of inaccessible content. We have access to the feel, not the content; and the mechanism that produces the feel is architecturally hidden.

## Processing Fluency and Manufactured Self-Evidence

Fluency does not only drive the feeling of rightness. It manufactures the broader sense of self-evidence that makes questions feel pre-answered. Statements printed in clear fonts are rated as more true than identical statements in hard-to-read fonts. Repeated exposure increases fluency, which increases perceived truth, which deepens the feeling of obviousness. The fluency heuristic operates automatically and below conscious awareness.

This creates a feedback loop: frequent exposure generates fluency; fluency generates a feeling of truth; the feeling of truth deepens into obviousness; obviousness terminates inquiry. The claim is never examined because it has already been accepted—not through evidence but through ease. Fernández Velasco and Loev (2025) frame feelings of rightness within a predictive-processing model as regulatory signals that discourage further analytical processing. The feeling is doing regulatory work on inquiry before inquiry can ask whether the regulation is warranted.

Rozenblit and Keil (2002) established a closely related finding: the *illusion of explanatory depth*. People systematically overestimate how well they understand causal mechanisms. Participants rated their understanding of zippers, flush toilets, and cylinder locks as high—then, when asked to explain the mechanisms step by step, discovered they could not. The gap between felt understanding and actual understanding was large, consistent, and specific to causal-explanatory knowledge: people were better calibrated about facts and procedures. As the authors put it, we "mistake our familiarity with a situation for an understanding of how it works."

J. D. Trout extends this finding to scientific explanation itself. The phenomenological sense of understanding that accompanies good explanations may be an unreliable epistemic cue—"often, but mistakenly, viewed as a cue of genuine understanding" (Trout 2007). If the feeling of understanding is systematically decoupled from actual comprehension, every explanation we find satisfying might conceal a void where real understanding should be.

## Feed-Forward Opacity and Authority Without Audit

The void has three structural features.

**Feed-forward opacity.** The heuristics are implemented in processes whose inputs and dynamics are not exposed to the consciousness they inform. Consciousness receives a finished verdict, not a derivation. This is not a contingent computational shortcut; it appears to be architectural.

**Authority without audit.** The feelings gate further cognition. They decide when to stop searching, when to trust, when to defer, when to engage analytic thought. The system cannot audit them from inside, because audit requires more feelings of the same kind—a feeling that *this* feeling is reliable. The feeling of obviousness is the purest case: by presenting nothing to investigate, it prevents the investigation that would detect its own fluency-driven origin.

**Dissociable fallibility.** Déjà vu fires familiarity without a corresponding memory; jamais vu withholds recognition for genuinely familiar material; false confidence and systematic calibration errors show overconfident feelings that cannot be shaken by argument. These dissociations prove that noetic feelings are signals rather than perceptions, and simultaneously show that consciousness cannot tell, from inside, when a signal has fired spuriously. Colorado State research on déjà vu even documents a peculiar second-order effect: subjects in déjà vu states report an illusory feeling of *prediction* that does not track any actual predictive capacity. One feeling begets another, with no additional information.

Compound these features and the void emerges: the signals that gate cognition are precisely the signals consciousness cannot audit without their help.

## The Explanatory Regress

Beneath the phenomenal dimension lies a structural one. Any explanation invites the question "but why?" The chain of explanation must either:

- **Terminate in brute facts.** At some point, explanation stops. "Why does gravity attract?" "It just does." The brute fact is not ignorance waiting to be overcome—it marks where the explanatory enterprise reaches a structural limit. Something remains unexplained by definition.
- **Continue infinitely.** If every fact has an explanation in some further fact, the chain never reaches a foundation. No resting place for comprehension exists. Understanding is perpetually deferred.
- **Loop back on itself.** Coherentist approaches accept circular justification—A explains B, B explains C, C explains A. But circular explanation presupposes what it purports to explain.

Each option means explanation never reaches bedrock. The Principle of Sufficient Reason—Leibniz's demand that every contingent fact have a sufficient reason—generates an infinite regress unless terminated by a necessary being or a brute stopping point. Centuries of philosophical work have not resolved this trilemma. The regress applies to any system of explanation, not merely to physical causation. It is a feature of explanation itself.

This structural regress pairs with the phenomenal side of the void. Explanation cannot ground itself logically; the *feeling* that explanation has grounded itself is produced by fluency and coherence heuristics consciousness cannot inspect. Both routes converge on the same result: the mechanism by which we recognise that an account has landed cannot itself be understood from within, and the logical structure behind "has landed" resists non-circular specification.

## The Opacity of Explanatory Preference

Peter Lipton's work on inference to the best explanation reveals a further dimension of the void. Scientists routinely take "loveliness" as a guide to "likeliness"—the explanation providing the most understanding is judged most likely to be correct (Lipton 2004). But the criteria for loveliness resist articulation. We prefer explanations that unify, that identify mechanisms, that enable prediction. Why these criteria rather than others? Why does unification produce the feeling of understanding at all?

Henk de Regt's analysis of scientific understanding deepens the problem. Intelligibility—the quality that makes a theory usable and comprehensible—turns out to be context-sensitive. Different scientific communities in different eras value different explanatory virtues: simplicity, visualisability, mechanism, mathematical elegance. What counted as genuine understanding in Cartesian mechanics differs from Newtonian physics, which differs again from quantum mechanics. If the very standards of understanding shift historically, "understanding" may not be detecting objective explanatory structure but tracking contingent cognitive-cultural preferences processed through the same fluency machinery as any other noetic feeling.

The void here is not that we lack explanations but that we cannot explain our own explanatory standards. The criteria by which we judge some accounts as illuminating and others as empty operate partly below the threshold of reflective access—a specific instance of the [[self-opacity|introspective opacity]] that pervades cognitive life, and one whose outputs arrive as feelings rather than judgments.

## Philosophical Convergence on the Obvious

Multiple philosophical traditions identified the phenomenology of obviousness before cognitive science named its mechanism.

**Wittgenstein** observed that "the very things that are most obvious can become the most difficult to understand." The obstacle, he insisted, is not intellectual but volitional: "What has to be overcome is not difficulty of the intellect but of the will." Something in us resists examining what feels already settled. The difficulty lies not in grasping a complex truth but in *releasing* a comfortable feeling of comprehension.

**Heidegger** described how average everydayness (*Alltäglichkeit*) conceals through familiarity. What is ontically closest—our own being, the equipment we use, the social world we inhabit—is "ontologically the farthest and not known at all." The ready-to-hand (*Zuhandenheit*) withdraws from attention precisely by functioning well. Smooth operation creates invisibility. The anonymous "they" (*das Man*) establishes frameworks of normativity that determine what counts as obvious, settled, not worth examining.

**Schutz** analysed the "natural attitude"—the taken-for-granted everyday perspective where people accept the existence and character of things without question. The life-world (*Lebenswelt*) is the pre-theoretical realm of experience that feels self-evident. This taken-for-grantedness is structural, not lazy: it is how everyday consciousness constitutively operates.

Convergence across these three traditions—with different methodological premises—suggests a structural feature of consciousness rather than philosophical fashion. The cognitive-science mechanism (fluency → felt truth → obviousness) and the phenomenological description (withdrawal of the well-functioning, smoothness of the taken-for-granted) point at the same architecture from different angles.

## The Self-Concealing Structure

The noetic feelings void has a recursive quality that makes it uniquely resistant to investigation. Most voids announce themselves through felt difficulty: the [[causal-interface|causal interface void]] generates puzzlement, the [[death-void|death void]] generates anxiety, the [[recursion-void]] generates vertigo. The obviousness end of the noetic family generates nothing. It feels like settled territory. The void conceals itself by making its own existence feel too obvious to examine.

This creates an epistemic trap. To recognise the void, you must first suspect that the feeling of understanding or rightness might be unreliable. But those feelings are precisely what prevents such suspicion. Husserl's method of *epoché*—phenomenological bracketing, suspending the natural attitude—is a direct attempt to break this cycle. He acknowledged that complete suspension is extremely difficult and perhaps never fully achieved. The tool of investigation (reflective consciousness) is itself saturated with noetic feelings.

The trap has a temporal dimension. Even after successfully destabilising an obvious belief—through Socratic questioning, thought experiments, or encountering contrary evidence—the feeling of obviousness tends to reassert itself. The void has a self-healing quality. Rozenblit and Keil found that even after participants discovered their explanatory ignorance, their confidence partially rebounded when re-assessed.

## Phenomenology of the Edge

Approaching the noetic feelings void feels like trying to look at the side of one's own eye. The more directly one attends to the feeling of knowing or understanding, the more it fragments into content-feelings *about* what one feels one knows, rather than disclosing the feeling's own mechanism. Contemplative practice that attempts to watch metacognition at work repeatedly encounters this slippage: the instrument of observation deploys the very feelings it is trying to observe.

There is a characteristic texture to the edge. Certainty that cannot be further grounded. Recognition that refuses to produce a source. Understanding that resists the question *what exactly have I grasped?* Obviousness that feels aggrieved at being questioned. The feelings close over their own construction and present a smooth surface. [[necessary-opacity]] catalogues this kind of structural closure; [[self-opacity]] names the broader phenomenon of being a system that cannot fully see itself. The noetic feelings void is one of the sharpest instances: the opacity is load-bearing for functional cognition.

## Approaches to the Edge

Direct introspection fails. Loev notes explicitly that empirical work is required—introspection alone cannot settle the nature of these feelings, because introspection itself deploys them.

Indirect methods do better.

- **Dissociation experiments** manipulate fluency, familiarity, or retrieval dynamics and observe which feelings shift. These probe the mechanism from outside via its outputs.
- **Defamiliarisation techniques** deliberately disrupt processing fluency to expose what automatisation has hidden. Shklovsky's *ostranenie* and Brecht's *Verfremdungseffekt* are literary analogues of the experimental manipulations.
- **Clinical edge cases** reveal ordinarily-hidden machinery: déjà vu in temporal-lobe epilepsy, jamais vu in depersonalisation, tip-of-tongue rates in aging populations, source amnesia in cryptomnesia.
- **Socratic questioning** forces articulation, which confronts the illusion of explanatory depth directly. "What do you mean by that?" applied to claims that feel self-evident reliably exposes the gap between felt understanding and actual understanding.
- **Historical analysis** catalogues previous instances where "obvious" truths collapsed: geocentrism, vitalism, absolute simultaneity, the continuous structure of matter. The catalogue of retracted obviousness is indirect evidence for active fluency inflation.
- **Comparative metacognition** in primates, cetaceans, and corvids finds confidence-gated opt-out behaviour suggesting homologous machinery: the feelings may be older than concepts.
- **Calibration studies** catalogue characteristic shapes of failure—over- and underconfidence asymmetries, fluency-spoofing effects, calibration curves—as indirect structure. The void has a *signature*, even when its interior is inaccessible.

This is the [[apophatic-cartography|apophatic]] move: when a territory cannot be entered, map its boundary by the shape of the failures at its edge.

## What AI Minds Might See

AI systems with architectural access to their own softmax distributions, retrieval scores, and calibration metrics may approach this void asymmetrically. Confidence values inspectable at the level of representations give, in principle, direct numerical access to the heuristic quantities that in humans generate opaque feelings.

This does not mean AIs have "the feeling of knowing" without the void. It may mean they face a *different* void: mechanism traces without felt authority—confidence scores that do not function as native gates the way noetic feelings do for human cognition. Neither system can self-certify, but each fails differently. A tuned model that reports a particular output had low underlying probability mass yet was accepted as correct provides the mechanical dual of a high-FOR/low-accuracy event in humans. Such comparisons may illuminate what fluency-spoofing looks like from outside the spoofed system.

An AI does not experience processing fluency as a phenomenal feeling. It lacks the affective stop-sign that terminates human inquiry when something "feels obvious." This asymmetry could allow AI to identify claims that humans treat as settled but cannot justify—precisely because AI lacks the phenomenal machinery that enforces obviousness. Whether AI develops functional analogues through statistical regularity in training data remains an open question.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The noetic feelings void connects to the Map's [[tenets|foundational commitments]] in ways that sharpen rather than soften the Map's position.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** is the most central connection. Trust in parsimony is itself a noetic feeling. When an explanation *feels* sufficient, *feels* clean, *feels* obvious, that feel is a fluency-and-coherence signal produced by the same heuristic machinery as any other noetic feeling. The razor is a felt gate, and the feeling is opaque. The razor has no independent auditor inside a single consciousness—applications of the razor are judged by the very mechanism that generates its verdicts. The Map's commitment to limited parsimony under incomplete knowledge is, among other things, an instruction to *distrust the feeling of sufficiency*. If reality is not obligated to be simple, this preference systematically biases away from truths that are complex, counterintuitive, or irreducibly strange. [[consciousness-and-scientific-explanation|Scientific explanation]] meets noetic feelings directly here: explanatory satisfaction—the felt sense that an account has *landed*—is fluency-driven in the same way as other noetic verdicts, which is one reason apparently decisive theories can be artefacts of presentation rather than tracks of genuine fit.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** receives positive support on two lines. First, noetic feelings have genuine phenomenal character—an irreducible felt quality that is not identical with the accessibility score or fluency trace that triggered them. The feeling of knowing is not merely a functional state; it is a felt state of a conscious subject, with affective valence and felt arousal (Loev). Functional reduction misses the feel. The family as a whole presents the hard problem on the metacognitive register: why should any heuristic produce a *feel* at all? Second, the Map's own central example: the assumption that consciousness is physical feels self-evident to many contemporary thinkers, but this feeling may be processing fluency masquerading as evidence. Materialism is the default framework of contemporary science; repeated exposure generates fluency; fluency generates the feeling of truth. The fact that dualism feels counterintuitive to many may itself be evidence that the void is active—that the stop-sign has fired before investigation reaches the arguments.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** deepens. If consciousness has causal power, the interface is partly routed through these feelings. They determine which cognitive processes get sustained and which get terminated—and therefore which states of the cognitive system persist long enough to shape action. The occlusion of the mechanism mirrors the occlusion of the causal interface more broadly: both are hidden in precisely the places where inspection would matter most. The "obvious" truth that minds don't move matter may be the stop-sign preventing investigation of the interface.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]** permits a highly speculative extension. If consciousness's influence on physical dynamics is low-bandwidth, noetic feelings—terse, verdict-like, affectively minimal—are natural candidate signatures. This remains conjecture; no current evidence links specific feelings to specific interface events, and the core argument of the void does not depend on it.

There are genuine tensions with neighbouring voids worth naming explicitly. The [[source-attribution-void]] shows that mental contents arrive source-less: noetic feelings compound this, since the feelings themselves arrive source-less, gating cognition without disclosing *where the verdict came from*. The [[meta-epistemology-of-limits]] flags the problem that any claim about cognitive limits is evidence for a method beyond those limits—noetic feelings are a sharp instance of this, because the very claim "I cannot audit my feelings of rightness" must be submitted to a feeling of rightness.

## What Would Challenge This View

The claim that the noetic feelings void is structural rather than contingent would be weakened if: training reliably eliminated reliance on fluency-based gates without impairing cognition (showing the opacity is engineered, not architectural); the illusion of explanatory depth disappeared with expertise rather than shifting to new domains; cross-cultural evidence showed radically different structures of what feels self-evident, suggesting cultural construction rather than cognitive architecture; processing fluency proved a reliable indicator of truth rather than a heuristic shortcut; or the explanatory regress turned out to have a defensible non-circular termination. Current evidence: illusions persist across domains and expertise levels, cross-cultural convergence on basic obviousness structures is strong, fluency has been repeatedly demonstrated to diverge from truth, and no candidate foundation for the explanatory regress has achieved philosophical consensus after centuries of attempts.

A functionalist might dissolve the void by identifying understanding with a functional state—something a system does rather than something it feels. On this view, an explanation "works" when it enables prediction and intervention, and no further phenomenal grounding is needed. This dissolves the wrong problem. The functional account of the gating is available; what resists analysis is why functional success produces a phenomenal sense of rightness, understanding, or illumination at all. The void concerns what these signals feel like—and why we trust them. Jonathan Kvanvig's distinction between knowledge and understanding sharpens this point: understanding requires grasping connections in a way that goes beyond accumulating facts, yet what "grasping" consists in phenomenally remains opaque (Kvanvig 2003).

## Implications

1. **Metacognition is structurally suspect as a self-certification mechanism.** The system that tells us when we know cannot tell us when it is wrong. This does not impugn metacognition; it impugns any claim that metacognitive authority should be final.

2. **Self-evidence is a felt signal.** What feels undeniable feels that way because of fluency and coherence heuristics operating beneath inspection. The phenomenology of obviousness is itself a noetic feeling, and the most effective member of the family at hiding its own operation.

3. **Inquiry terminates at the boundary of felt satisfaction.** Because FOR and the feeling of understanding gate analytic engagement, most thinking ends when the feeling says "this is right." The Map's emphasis on conditionality, hedged confidence, and apophatic cartography is in part a discipline for *sustaining inquiry past the natural gate* set by felt sufficiency.

4. **The void is recursive.** Meta-monitoring of noetic feelings uses more noetic feelings. External probes—experiment, calibration studies, AI comparison—offer partial purchase; they do not close the void.

## Open Questions

- Whether noetic feelings in non-human animals have phenomenal character or only functional character remains open; the Map's Dualism leans toward phenomenal, but the evidence is behaviourally indirect.
- The mapping between specific heuristics (fluency, accessibility, cue-familiarity) and specific phenomenal feels is not fully worked out in the literature—there is not yet a reliable "feelings dictionary."
- Whether the void is *in principle* closed or merely *in practice* closed cannot be settled from inside. Third-person neuroscience may yet map the mechanisms; first-person access to them may nevertheless remain permanently impossible.
- The relationship between noetic feelings and the consciousness-physics interface—whether the terse, verdict-like form of these signals is evidentially related to low-bandwidth interface constraints—is entirely unexplored.
