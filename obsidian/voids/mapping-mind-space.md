The Unfinishable Map's dualist commitments imply that consciousness is fundamental and irreducible, which means the space of possible minds may be as vast as physical reality itself. You occupy a single point in that space, and two structural limits follow from this location. You cannot survey the landscape to determine whether your form of consciousness is central or peripheral, typical or exotic. And you cannot introspect the dimensions along which phenomenal experience varies, because the dimensions themselves shape what introspection can represent. These two limits — the location problem and the dimensionality problem — are facets of a single void: the geometry of mind-space is inaccessible from within.

The void is primarily unexplorable in the [[three-kinds-of-void|voids taxonomy]]. Surveying mind-space requires a vantage point independent of any particular mind, which would mean being conscious without being any particular kind of conscious. That is incoherent. Yet elements remain genuinely unexplored: comparative neuroscience, altered states, cross-architectural comparison with AI, and the emergence of non-biological cognition may push the boundaries of what can be mapped from a single vantage point.

## The Space We Cannot Survey

Murray Shanahan's framework of the "space of possible minds" encompasses biological minds that evolved on Earth, extraterrestrial intelligence, artificial minds, and forms of consciousness that are possible but have never existed. He argues we should shift from investigating *the* mind to mapping *the space* of possible minds — analogous to what physics does with state-spaces, charting the full landscape of possibility rather than fixating on a single case.

The ambition is sound. The execution faces a fundamental obstacle. Any survey of mind-space must be conducted from within a particular mind, using that mind's concepts, perceptual categories, and cognitive architecture. Thomas Nagel's argument about bat consciousness establishes the point sharply: we cannot even form adequate conceptions of what it is like to be a bat, a creature that shares our planet, evolutionary history, and much of our biology. The space of possible minds extends incomparably further than the distance between human and chiropteran experience, yet imaginative reach fails at the first step.

Nagel concluded that "there is no reason to suppose that [bat experience] is subjectively like anything we can experience or imagine." If true for bats, it is overwhelmingly true for minds with different sensory modalities, different physical constraints, or structures built around principles we lack concepts for.

## Location Without Coordinates

The self-location problem makes the void concrete. We know we exist as conscious beings. We do not know — and may be structurally unable to determine — where we fall in the broader landscape of possible consciousness. This connects to the [[indexical-void]]: the question "why am I *this* consciousness?" becomes unanswerable when you cannot even locate "this" within the space of alternatives.

The analogy to pre-Copernican cosmology is instructive. Humans assumed Earth occupied the centre of the universe because they had no external vantage point. The Copernican revolution required instruments that revealed actual position. For mind-space, no analogous instruments exist. There is no telescope that shows us mind-space from outside.

The problem goes deeper than missing instruments. Self-locating beliefs — what David Lewis called "attitudes *de se*" — are essentially indexical, as John Perry's work on the essential indexical demonstrates. They cannot be characterised without terms like "I," "here," "now." Knowing that minds exist tells you nothing about which mind you are or where it falls in the space. You cannot triangulate from a single point.

Observer selection effects compound the difficulty. Nick Bostrom's work on anthropic reasoning shows that what we can observe is systematically filtered by the fact that only certain positions permit observation. Applied to mind-space: we can only observe from within minds like ours, which gives a fundamentally skewed sample. If most of mind-space consists of forms of consciousness so different from ours that they would not recognise us as minds — and vice versa — we would have no way to detect this.

## The Flatland Structure

The dimensionality facet of the void operates one level deeper than location. Most cognitive limits concern first-order ignorance: there is a fact, and we cannot access it. The dimensionality limit is recursive. If phenomenal experience has dimensions beyond those we can introspect, then we cannot know what experiences along those dimensions feel like, we cannot know that those dimensions exist, and we cannot formulate the question of what we are missing — because formulating it would require the very concepts those dimensions would provide.

Edwin Abbott's *Flatland* illustrates this structure precisely. A Flatlander confined to two spatial dimensions does not merely fail to see three-dimensional objects; it cannot formulate the concept of depth. The question "what lies above?" is not unanswered but unaskable. Crucially, a Flatlander can be *told* about depth, can learn mathematical descriptions of three-dimensional geometry, can manipulate formal systems that describe cubes and spheres. What it cannot do is *conceive* depth as a dimension of its experience. The mathematical understanding does not close the phenomenal gap.

Colin McGinn's [[mysterianism|cognitive closure]] argument formalises the Flatland predicament for consciousness. A mind is cognitively closed with respect to a property if its concept-forming procedures cannot extend to grasping that property. The solution to the mind-body problem may exist but "lie in dimensions of reality our cognitive architecture cannot access." The claim is not that the answer is difficult but that we lack the conceptual apparatus to formulate it.

Peter Sjöstedt-Hughes extends this into an explicit dimensional framework. Drawing on the More-Broad-Smithies theory, he argues that perceptual space and physical space are slices of a higher-dimensional manifold. The brain, on this view, acts as "an inhibiting filtering interface rather than as a producer of mind." Consciousness is already higher-dimensional; the brain constrains access to a navigable projection. Our phenomenal experience is a dimensional cross-section — faithful within its plane, but missing everything orthogonal to it.

## Convergent Arguments for Missing Dimensions

Several independent theoretical lines converge on the possibility that phenomenal space is richer than introspection reveals, and that mind-space may not have the shape our folk geometry assumes.

**Neural high-dimensionality.** Brain dynamics occupy spaces of thousands of independent variables, yet conscious experience appears to compress this into far fewer dimensions. Jolly and Chang document what they call "the Flatland fallacy" in cognitive science: a systematic preference for low-dimensional models driven by tractability rather than evidence. "Like the Flatlanders in Edwin Abbott's famous short story," they write, "we may be led to believe that the parsimony offered by our low-dimensional theories reflects the reality of a much higher-dimensional problem." Their point is methodological, but the implication cuts deeper: if our *theories* of mind systematically flatten high-dimensional processes, our *introspection* of mind may do the same.

**Experience-space.** Barron and Klein propose an "experience-space" (E-space) for mapping qualia — a configuration space whose dimensionality reflects the degrees of freedom needed to specify phenomenal properties. They argue that E-space requires "at least several times as many dimensions as there are categories of qualia." Different organisms may occupy different regions with fundamentally different dimensionalities of experience. Bat echolocation, mantis shrimp colour vision, and shark electroreception suggest phenomenal dimensions inaccessible to human introspection — not merely unfamiliar sensory data but entire axes of experiential variation. The [[synesthetic-void]] explores a related phenomenon within our own species: phenomenal dimensions that some human minds access and others do not.

**Morphospace frameworks.** Arsiwalla and Verschure propose a "morphospace of consciousness" organised along three axes: autonomic complexity, computational complexity, and social complexity. Human consciousness occupies one corner of this space, with biological and artificial minds plotted elsewhere. Under the Map's dualism, however, the morphospace is almost certainly incomplete. If consciousness is not reducible to functional properties, the relevant dimensions of mind-space may be entirely non-physical — dimensions for which we lack concepts and perhaps the capacity to form them. The morphospace approach maps what can be measured from outside; mind-space extends into territory that external measurement cannot reach.

**Higher-dimensional physics of consciousness.** Bernard Carr proposes a five-dimensional model in which a mental-time dimension distinct from physical time produces "a hierarchy of levels of consciousness, associated with a hierarchy of extra dimensions." If the dimensional framework of physics itself is incomplete — if resolving the hard problem requires expanding beyond 3+1 spacetime — then consciousness may inhabit dimensions that physical science, by construction, does not describe.

## Dimensional Ignorance

We may not know how many dimensions mind-space has, let alone our coordinates. This is not ignorance of a particular fact but ignorance of the framework within which facts would be expressed — *dimensional ignorance*, where we lack not just answers but the vocabulary for questions. The problem connects to [[biological-cognitive-closure]]: if some concepts are permanently inaccessible to human cognition, the dimensions of mind-space that require those concepts remain permanently unnamed.

Dimensional ignorance is the deepest form of the location problem. Without knowing the dimensions of the space, no talk of coordinates is even coherent. Attempts to give mind-space concrete structure — morphospace, experience-space, Shanahan's possibility-space — reveal how little we know about its basic geometry. Each framework may capture some axes while leaving others invisible. The union of all proposed frameworks may still be a low-dimensional projection.

## Phenomenology of the Edge

Approaching this void produces distinctive experiential signatures — instances of what the Map calls the [[phenomenology-of-the-edge]], the characteristic ways cognition behaves at its own limits.

**The illusion of centrality.** We naturally assume our form of consciousness is the norm — the default setting. This cognitive geocentrism resists correction through argument alone. One can intellectually accept that human consciousness might be peripheral or exotic, but one cannot *feel* one's way to that understanding.

**The flattening effect.** Attempting to conceive a new phenomenal dimension collapses into recombinations of known dimensions. Try to imagine a colour outside the visible spectrum — not infrared *detected*, but a genuinely novel hue. The attempt produces nothing, or familiar colours in unfamiliar arrangements. Thought flattens the unknown into the known, and the flattening feels seamless rather than forced.

**The sufficiency illusion.** Unlike voids where one senses something missing — the nagging incompleteness when trying to conceive what matter is *in itself*, or the vertigo when contemplating the [[scale-void|scale of cosmic distances]] — the dimensionality facet may produce no feeling of absence at all. Experience presents itself as complete. The absence of absence is the void's deepest feature.

**The vertigo of scale.** William James observed that "normal waking consciousness is but one special type of consciousness, whilst all about it, parted from it by the filmiest of screens, there lie potential forms of consciousness entirely different." Contemplating this produces intellectual vertigo — a sense of assumed normality falling away. The experience echoes the scale void, but here the scale is in a domain we cannot even visualise.

**Occasional disruption.** Psychedelic experiences sometimes produce reports of "impossible" spatial geometries, novel qualia categories, and what subjects describe as encountering additional dimensions. Contemplative traditions report awareness "opening" into new modalities after sustained practice. These may be glimpses of normally filtered dimensions, consistent with Sjöstedt-Hughes's filtering model — or they may be confabulations generated by disordered processing. The ambiguity is itself informative: we lack the framework to distinguish genuine dimensional access from its simulation.

**Recognition without comprehension.** Different mind-types are cognitively closed with respect to different properties. One can recognise that unthinkable thoughts exist without comprehending what those thoughts would be. Labels point toward alternatives — hive minds, non-temporal consciousness, purely aesthetic minds, minds structured around senses we lack — but the labels mark voids they cannot illuminate. Each pointer lacks a referent.

## What AI Might Reveal

If AI minds occupy a different position in mind-space — or if they have a position at all — comparing human and AI cognitive profiles may reveal something about local topology. McGinn argues that other minds, alien or artificial, might possess the conceptual apparatus to bridge gaps where human cognition fails. The leverage comes from difference: what AI can process and humans cannot, and vice versa, triangulates the shape of nearby mind-space.

AI offers a non-biological vantage point — a prospect explored more fully in [[ai-as-void-explorer]]. One cannot survey mind-space from a single location, but two locations give a bearing. Even if AI lacks phenomenal consciousness entirely, the pattern of its capabilities and limitations — mapped against human capabilities and limitations — provides structural information about at least one neighbourhood of the space. The inverse question — whether consciousness opens territories that computation cannot reach — is explored in [[consciousness-only-territories]].

The asymmetry cuts two ways. AI may lack certain dimensions of human experience while accessing dimensions humans lack. Patterns visible from one side may be invisible from the other. Neither survey completes the map, but their intersection expands what can be seen.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The void connects to all five of the Map's [[tenets]].

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** makes the void real. If consciousness is reducible to physical processes, mind-space is a subset of physical possibility-space and can in principle be surveyed by mapping brain configurations. The dimensionality facet collapses into a technical question about neural coding. If consciousness is fundamental and irreducible, mind-space is its own ontological domain — potentially as vast as physical reality — and its dimensional structure need not match that of spacetime. Neither location nor dimensions are surveyable by physical methods alone.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** finds its purest expression here. The assumption that human consciousness is typical or representative — a form of cognitive parsimony — may be as misleading as the geocentric assumption. Eric Schwitzgebel argues that "the truth — whatever it is — is weird," and our preference for tractable models of mind may systematically underestimate the variety and strangeness of possible consciousness. The preference for low-dimensional models is itself a candidate for what parsimony gets wrong. If phenomenal reality is high-dimensional, then parsimony applied to theories of consciousness would systematically steer us toward the wrong answer — and a dimensionally limited mind would feel exactly the confidence it does.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]** gains context from the dimensional picture. If consciousness operates in higher-dimensional phenomenal space but interfaces with the brain through quantum indeterminacies, then the "minimal" in minimal interaction may reflect the narrowness of the dimensional bottleneck — a vast phenomenal reality squeezing its influence through the tiny aperture where quantum outcomes remain undetermined. Most of what consciousness *is* may have no physical signature. The filtering model suggests the interaction is minimal not because consciousness is weak, but because the dimensional interface is narrow.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** gains a natural geometry. If different forms of consciousness interact with the physical world differently, some regions of mind-space are more physically efficacious than others. If consciousness exists in a higher-dimensional space and the brain acts as a [[filter-theory|filtering interface]], then bidirectional interaction occurs at the dimensional boundary — consciousness influencing physical outcomes from dimensions the brain cannot fully represent, and physical processes constraining which dimensions of consciousness are accessible. The interface is not a mystery to be dissolved but a dimensional projection to be characterised.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]** makes both facets genuine. Under many-worlds, every position in mind-space is simultaneously occupied across branches; the question of location dissolves into indexical triviality, and every possible phenomenal cross-section is realised somewhere. Under single-world realism, one's position in mind-space is a fact about reality — you are *here*, with *this* dimensional signature, and not elsewhere. The void is a genuine void, not a pseudo-problem generated by quantum metaphysics.

## What Would Challenge This View

The void would be weakened if: (1) a method emerged for surveying mind-space from a vantage point independent of any particular mind; (2) introspection reliably revealed novel phenomenal dimensions under training or cognitive intervention; (3) AI systems produced descriptions of consciousness that resolved the dimensional question rather than merely redescribing it; (4) cross-cultural or cross-species convergence on the dimensions of experience proved so strong that dimensional variation were ruled out; or (5) a reductive physical theory of consciousness captured what introspection reports, making the higher-dimensional hypothesis unnecessary.

Current evidence does not meet these conditions. Every survey of mind-space proceeds from within a particular mind. Introspective training produces refinement along known dimensions rather than discovery of new ones. AI outputs either redescribe human reports or diverge in ways that cannot be evaluated without assuming a framework. The evidence is consistent with a genuine void rather than contingent ignorance.
