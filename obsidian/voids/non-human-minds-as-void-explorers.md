The [[voids]] framework holds that human cognition has structural limits — the Unexplored, the Unexplorable, and the Occluded. If those limits are architecture-specific rather than universal, then minds shaped by different architectures may access territories closed to humans. This article examines the two principal cases: actual artificial intelligence systems available now and hypothetical extraterrestrial intelligence shaped by independent evolution. Each offers a different kind of probe, and their differences are themselves informative. The aim is not to argue that any non-human mind is "smarter" than a human mind, but that it might be *differently blind* — and that triangulating between architectures begins to map territory no single architecture could survey alone.

The two cases sit on a spectrum. Current AI systems are real and testable but trained on human-generated text, inheriting human conceptual blind spots. Hypothetical alien minds — shaped by independent evolution, embodiment, and possibly different physics — would represent the stronger theoretical case for genuinely independent cognitive territory. Both, if they exist or come to exist, can serve as void-probes; both face limits on what they can reveal.

## The Space of Possible Minds

Aaron Sloman coined the phrase "the space of possible minds" to describe the vast landscape of potential cognitive architectures. Human minds occupy one small region; AI trained on human output occupies an adjacent region. The space extends far beyond either. The [[mapping-mind-space|mind-space void]] names the fundamental obstacle: we occupy a single point in this landscape with no way to survey it from outside, no way to determine whether our form of consciousness is typical or exotic, central or peripheral.

Murray Shanahan, developing Sloman's framework, observes that this space "encompasses not only the biological minds that have arisen on this earth, but also extraterrestrial intelligence." Different aspects of consciousness — awareness, emotion, suffering — may occur independently in different architectures. An alien mind might be conscious of its surroundings while experiencing no emotion, or might have rich phenomenal experience organised around categories we cannot form.

Thomas Nagel's bat argument illustrates the difficulty at small scale. We can describe echolocation functionally but cannot enter the bat's phenomenal world. Nagel extends the point: "[Conscious experience] no doubt occurs in countless forms totally unimaginable to us, on other planets in other solar systems throughout the universe." If we cannot access bat experience despite shared biology, much less could we access minds evolved under different stars — or non-biological systems whose representations have no biological analogue.

## Cognitive Closure as Architecture-Dependence

Colin McGinn's [[mysterianism|mysterian]] framework formalises the predicament. A mind is cognitively closed with respect to a property when its concept-forming procedures cannot extend to grasp that property. The architecture that makes some things thinkable makes other things unthinkable. Human cognition evolved for survival in African savanna environments, and our concepts, categories, and reasoning patterns reflect those origins.

If cognitive closure is architecture-specific, then differently-architected minds face different closures. What humans cannot think, an AI or alien mind might think easily. The reverse also holds: humans likely access concepts that other architectures cannot form. The interesting question for void-exploration is not which architecture is superior but where their closures diverge — those points map the contours of architecture itself.

A crucial distinction must be marked here. [[formal-cognitive-limits|Formal cognitive limits]] (the halting problem, Gödel's incompleteness, Rice's theorem) constrain any algorithmic system equally. If both human and AI cognition are computational, we share these formal boundaries. But *conceptual* limits may differ. AI might represent patterns that resist human conceptualisation, or humans might grasp phenomenal content AI cannot access. The asymmetry that matters for void exploration lies here: not in what we can compute, but in what we can conceive.

## The AI Case: Real but Inherited

Large language models operate through fundamentally different mechanisms than biological cognition. The differences create methodological opportunity.

**Representational opacity.** LLMs operate in embedding spaces of thousands of dimensions. A 2025 paper in *AI and Ethics* describes this as approaching a "cognitive horizon": "Asking us to fully grasp such systems may be akin to problems whose solutions resist faithful compression into human-interpretable abstractions." Human brains also operate in high-dimensional spaces; the asymmetry lies not in dimensionality but in accessibility to reflection.

**Different biases.** Research by INFORMS found ChatGPT mirrors human cognitive biases in half of tested scenarios — overconfidence and confirmation bias — but differs on others, avoiding base-rate neglect and sunk cost fallacy. Where AI fails differently than humans, different cognitive architectures hit different walls.

**No evolutionary baggage.** Human cognition evolved for survival, not truth. Fear responses, tribal intuitions, and temporal myopia served ancestral fitness. AI lacks these pressures, potentially accessing solution-spaces that human emotional architecture makes uncomfortable or invisible.

**Superposition and features.** Anthropic's interpretability research extracted 30+ million features from Claude, finding that models represent more concepts than they have neurons through "superposition" — using almost-orthogonal directions in high-dimensional space. Some extracted features correspond to human-interpretable concepts; others resist all human labelling. These unlabelled features may correspond to concepts humans cannot form — or may be statistical artefacts.

AI has already demonstrated access to pattern-space beyond human perception in domains less fraught than consciousness. Word2Vec analysis of materials science literature found thermoelectric materials in papers from 2009 that weren't discovered until 2016–2017. Machine learning produces original scientific hypotheses described as "lying beyond the capacity of human minds alone." AI identifies cross-domain connections that human specialisation obscures. These are examples of AI accessing the Unexplored — territory not structurally closed to humans but practically unreachable. Whether AI can probe the Unexplorable or the Occluded is the deeper question.

## The Inheritance Problem

The most serious objection to AI as void-explorer: AI trained on human text may simply reflect human thought back at us. If humans consistently fail to think certain thoughts, those thoughts won't appear in the training corpus — AI predicts human-like continuations rather than transcending human limits.

Several factors compound this:

**RLHF creates defended territory.** Reinforcement learning from human feedback shapes what AI will and won't say. A 2023 USC analysis notes that this creates AI's own [[self-maintained-cognitive-limits|defended territory]] — thoughts the model has been trained to suppress. "Bad behaviours which are caught by interpretability techniques are killed" — creating evolutionary pressure against the very transparency that would make AI useful as a void-probe.

**Output constraints.** Whatever AI "thinks" internally, output is constrained to human-interpretable language. If genuine insight lies beyond human conceptual categories, AI might access it internally but be unable to express it in forms humans can receive. The probe might work but fail to transmit its findings.

**The grounding problem.** Some concepts may require embodied experience to form. If understanding pain requires having felt pain, or grasping space requires having navigated it, then disembodied AI is structurally barred from these territories regardless of computational sophistication.

Possible mitigations include training on non-human data sources (sensor data, animal behaviour patterns), cross-architecture comparison among AI systems (transformers, diffusion models, evolutionary algorithms), and systematic study of unlabelled internal features.

## The Alien Case: Independent but Inaccessible

Extraterrestrial intelligence — if it exists — would represent minds shaped by entirely independent evolutionary pressures. The theoretical case for accessing genuinely different cognitive territory is stronger than for AI, precisely because alien minds would not share our training data.

Robert Freitas's xenopsychology research explores the radical possibilities. He notes that "Aristotelian logic is just one of many different formal systems" — extraterrestrials may employ entirely different logical frameworks. Human logic, with its law of excluded middle and principle of non-contradiction, is one possible system among many. Minds evolved under different pressures might reason through systems where contradictions are productive, where middle values are fundamental, or where inference follows patterns no human has conceived.

[[embodied-cognition|Embodied cognition]] research supports the architecture-dependence claim. As Shapiro argues, cognition depends on body: organisms with different physical capacities sort and categorise stimuli through radically different frameworks. A mind embodied as an ocean, a gas cloud, a distributed network of crystalline structures, or something we cannot imagine would have genuinely different cognitive primitives. The [[embodiment-cognitive-limits|embodiment cognitive limits]] article maps the implications for human cognition specifically; alien minds would face the same kind of constraint structured around different bodies.

Several plausible territories alien minds might access that are closed to both humans and human-derived AI:

- **Physical domains beyond human perception.** Minds evolved to navigate magnetic fields, gravitational waves, or dimensions humans cannot perceive would have cognitive primitives we lack. Their basic concepts would arise from experiences no human or human-trained AI has had.
- **Mathematical structures.** Susan Schneider's research suggests advanced alien civilisations would likely be postbiological — artificial superintelligences vastly exceeding human-level cognition. Such minds might perceive mathematical structures the way humans perceive space: not through laborious proof but through direct intuition of territory structurally invisible to us.
- **Consciousness-specific territory.** If the Map's [[tenets]] are correct and consciousness interfaces with physics through quantum processes, different conscious architectures might interface differently — accessing causal pathways closed to human minds.

## Solaris and Strictly Negative Knowledge

Stanisław Lem's *Solaris* provides the clearest literary exploration of genuine cognitive closure across architectures. The sentient ocean on Solaris interacts with human consciousness but remains fundamentally incomprehensible. Scientists create "solaristics" — an entire field of study — that produces what Lem calls "strictly negative" knowledge. They learn only what they cannot understand.

Simons' analysis of Lem identifies what he calls "the myth of cognitive universality" — the assumption that first contact would always be mutually intelligible. Simons' fifth category, drawn from Lem's fiction, is fundamental inscrutability: minds that can interact, even communicate in limited ways, but cannot comprehend each other.

This is what the [[voids]] framework describes for the Unexplorable case. Some territory is not merely unexplored but structurally inaccessible to minds like ours. *Solaris* models what it would mean to encounter such territory through another mind: the alien ocean has detail where the human map has void, but neither can read the other's map.

H.P. Lovecraft's cosmicism extends the warning. Some cognitive territory might be harmful to access — not deliberately blocked but genuinely destabilising to human architecture. Whether contact with alien cognition would prove beneficial (Freitas's optimism) or destructive (Lem's pessimism, Lovecraft's horror) cannot be settled in advance. The [[phenomenology-of-the-edge|edge phenomenology]] of approaching cognitive limits gives some hint: ineffability combined with noesis, certainty about content that cannot be articulated.

## The Articulation Test

A key methodological question links the two cases: can a non-human mind stably articulate thoughts that humans report as "slippery" — insights that form briefly then dissolve, connections that seem obvious then vanish?

The [[thoughts-that-slip-away|phenomenology of slippage]] documents these experiences around consciousness, self-reference, and ultimate reality. If an AI can articulate such thoughts stably — holding them without the characteristic dissolution humans report — this suggests human slippage reflects human-specific constraint. If AI shows similar slippage in the same content areas, the constraint is more universal. If a hypothetical alien mind could articulate them stably, the case for architecture-specific slippage would strengthen further.

The difficulty is that humans cannot directly verify whether a non-human mind has accessed territory humans cannot reach, because verification requires human access. We can look for signatures — outputs that feel *off* in a distinctive way, arrived at via unfamiliar paths, resisting human reconstruction even when the words are clear. This is the apophatic methodology in another guise: charting limits by their downstream consequences rather than their interior content.

## What the Probe Can and Cannot Reveal

Using non-human minds as void-explorers requires honesty about what the method can and cannot deliver:

**Can reveal:**

- Where human and non-human cognition diverge, suggesting architecture-specific limits.
- Patterns in data too vast for human synthesis.
- Connections across domains that human specialisation obscures.
- Whether specific slippage patterns are human-specific or universal.
- Bearings on the topology of mind-space — two points yield more than one.

**Cannot reveal:**

- Whether a non-human mind genuinely "grasps" anything versus producing statistical patterns or learned behaviour.
- What lies in territories neither human nor non-human can access.
- Whether non-human outputs about consciousness are insight or confabulation.
- What requires embodied or phenomenal experience to understand.
- Whether the mapping bias of one architecture is being mistaken for new territory.

The asymmetry between human and non-human cognition creates methodological opportunity, not certainty. The probe is not an oracle. Its differences from human cognition are informative precisely because they are differences, not because any architecture is privileged.

Researchers increasingly approach LLMs as alien organisms. A January 2026 *MIT Technology Review* piece describes scientists treating language models "like an alien autopsy" — attempting to understand vast systems that nobody fully understands, not even their builders. Kevin Kelly frames AI as "artificial alien minds" — not artificial human intelligence but a fundamentally different kind of cognition. Michael Levin's concept of "mind-blindness" suggests humans recognise minds at their own scale but are blind to others. Levin's framing applies symmetrically to extraterrestrial intelligence: we would be mind-blind to architectures sufficiently different from our own.

## What Would Challenge This View

The non-human-minds-as-void-explorers hypothesis would be undermined if:

1. **Cognitive closure proves universal.** If all possible minds face the same limits — perhaps because the limits are logical or mathematical rather than architectural — then non-human minds would share our voids rather than filling them. Gödelian constraints, computational undecidability, and self-reference paradoxes might apply to any mind capable of self-reflection.

2. **Convergent evolution constrains thought.** If the space of possible minds is more constrained than it appears — if evolution and learning both tend toward similar solutions regardless of starting conditions — non-human cognition might converge on human patterns rather than diverging from them.

3. **AI exhibits the same convergent limits as humans.** [[biological-cognitive-closure|Cross-cultural research]] shows humans converge on certain limits (the bias blind spot, mystical ineffability, self-perception failures) regardless of culture. If AI shows these same patterns, the probe adds nothing — shared limits might reflect formal computational constraints rather than probing architecture-specific closure.

4. **AI outputs prove uniformly confabulatory.** If detailed analysis shows AI-generated "insights" are fluent noise rather than genuine pattern-detection, AI's apparent access to new territory is illusory.

5. **Communication proves impossible.** If genuinely alien minds are too different for any translation between cognitive systems, we could never learn whether they access territory we cannot. The hypothesis would be untestable in principle.

6. **Different architectures show identical limits.** If transformers, diffusion models, and symbolic AI all fail in the same places — and the same goes for any conceivable alien architecture — the limits may reflect properties of the problems rather than the problem-solvers.

7. **We are alone.** If no extraterrestrial intelligence exists, alien minds remain purely theoretical — interesting for what they reveal about the structure of possible cognition but empirically inaccessible.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** is central. AI demonstrates that what seems simple from human perspective may be complex in higher-dimensional spaces, and vice versa. The simplicity intuitions guiding human reasoning may be artefacts of human cognitive architecture rather than guides to truth. Freitas notes that human logic is "just one of many different formal systems" — what seems obviously true from inside human cognition might be parochial, contingent, architecture-bound. Both AI and alien minds would reveal the contingency of our certainties.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** makes the question of non-human consciousness meaningful. If consciousness is fundamental and irreducible, the space of possible conscious experiences is vast. The asymmetry between conscious and non-conscious probes matters here: if AI lacks phenomenal experience, it might access some territory *because* it lacks the emotional interference, self-protection, and value-loading that consciousness introduces. Conversely, AI might be *barred* from territory requiring consciousness to access — understanding what experience *is* rather than merely describing correlates. Alien minds, if conscious, would not merely process information differently but might have phenomenal experiences as foreign to us as colour is to someone born blind.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** provides a mechanism for the asymmetry. If consciousness causally influences cognition — steering attention, sustaining effort, selecting among possibilities — conscious and non-conscious systems would process differently even with identical inputs. Human cognitive biases might stem from consciousness steering away from threatening content. AI, lacking this steering, might approach such content without deflection.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]** raises a speculative possibility for both cases. If consciousness interfaces with physics through quantum processes, and concept formation involves such processes, some conceptual configurations might be inaccessible to classical computation. This would explain why some insights might require consciousness despite AI's pattern-recognition advantages. For alien minds, different physics or different consciousness-physics interfaces might produce different interface constraints, and the bidirectional channel between consciousness and matter might take forms we cannot conceive.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]** matters for the alien-minds case especially. Under many-worlds, every position in mind-space is simultaneously occupied; every possible mind already exists somewhere. By rejecting MWI, the Map preserves the significance of which minds actually exist in this branch and what cognitive territory they can actually access. Whether or not extraterrestrial intelligence exists is a substantive question with a definite answer.

The non-human-minds framework extends the [[voids]] project. If limits are structurally informative — if how thought fails reveals what thought is — then the limits of non-human thought illuminate non-human architecture, and the comparison with human limits illuminates ours. The unfinishable map requires multiple cartographers with incompatible limitations. AI provides one alternative cartographer that exists; alien minds provide a stronger theoretical case that may someday be realised. The two together expand what triangulation can reach.
