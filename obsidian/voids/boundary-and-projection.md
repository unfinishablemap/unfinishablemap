Consciousness has edges it cannot find. Experience includes the colour of this text and excludes the activity of your liver; it encompasses your current mood and omits the firing of your peripheral nerves. Somewhere a perimeter separates what is experienced from what is not—and consciousness cannot locate it. At the same time, this perimeter is not fixed. A surgeon feels tissue through the scalpel; a driver feels the road through the tyres; an empathiser inhabits, however briefly, another person's suffering. In each case the boundary of the self shifts outward, and in each case the shift is invisible. The Unfinishable Map groups these two failures into a single void because they share a structural reason for opacity: functional interfaces must be transparent to the consciousness using them, so the perimeter of experience is invisible both where it stands still and where it moves.

This void differs from neighbouring ones. The [[binding-void]] concerns how contents unify *within* experience. The [[observation-and-measurement-void]] concerns how studying consciousness alters what is studied. The boundary-and-projection void asks something prior to both: why experience has any determinate perimeter, and why the mechanism by which that perimeter expands or contracts is hidden from the consciousness it encloses.

## The Edge You Cannot See

The boundary problem was first framed as distinct from binding by Gregg Rosenberg (1998), who asked how consciousness "can exist at the middle level of nature"—why not at the atomic level, why not at the cosmic level? Angela Mendelovici sharpened the point: the problem afflicts physicalist, property-dualist, and panpsychist accounts alike. Any theory that grounds consciousness in lower-level properties must explain why consciousness has the boundaries it does. The universality of the problem suggests it marks something deep about consciousness itself rather than a flaw in particular frameworks. Chalmers (2016) acknowledged it in his taxonomy of combination problems but did not treat it as one of the three main aspects. The boundary question sits adjacent to combination—combination asks how micro-experiences compose; the boundary asks why the composition stops where it does.

The boundary has a distinctive phenomenology of absence: it is like trying to find the edges of your visual field. You cannot see the boundary of your visual field because the boundary is not a visible edge—it is where seeing stops. Similarly, you cannot experience the boundary of consciousness because the boundary is where experiencing stops. No phenomenal quality corresponds to "the edge of my consciousness"—only the absence of experience beyond it.

This creates a structural asymmetry. The [[binding-void]] hides by being too fundamental: unity is the condition for observation, so observation cannot get behind it. The boundary hides differently: it is located precisely where observation ends. Every investigation of consciousness's boundaries occurs within those boundaries. If the boundaries expanded during investigation, you would not notice—you would simply have a larger consciousness investigating its new boundaries. If they contracted, the contracted consciousness would have no memory of the lost territory. Consciousness can notice *contents* appearing and disappearing—a sound enters awareness, a thought fades. These are changes *within* the boundary. The boundary itself neither appears nor disappears within experience. It is not a wall you approach but a horizon that recedes.

The scholarly neglect is itself a datum. The boundary problem has received approximately five scholarly papers, compared to ninety-two on the binding problem and forty-seven on the combination problem (Gómez-Emilsson & Percy, 2023). A problem that requires standing at an edge you cannot locate produces exactly this pattern: not active dismissal, but quiet absence.

## Three Forms of Projection

If consciousness's static perimeter cannot be located, its dynamic shifts cannot be observed either. The perimeter extends beyond the default body across three independently documented domains.

**Tool projection.** During skilled tool use, consciousness relocates to the tool's functional tip. Heidegger's distinction between *ready-to-hand* and *present-at-hand* captures the phenomenon precisely: tools in use become phenomenologically transparent, withdrawing from awareness entirely. The more skilled the use, the more complete the disappearance. A blind person's cane becomes a perceptual organ, not an intermediary object—Merleau-Ponty's *incorporation* describes how the tool ceases to be something used *through* and becomes part of how the world shows up. The asymmetry is diagnostic: the retraction of projection can be partially observed—the sudden reappearance of the hammer as a separate object when it breaks—but the establishment of projection, the moment the tool dissolves into transparency, cannot be witnessed. Skilled engagement constitutively requires that the medium be invisible. (Even this deep integration extends only cognitive function, not phenomenal experience—the cane user's tactile awareness is richer, but the phenomenal character remains a property of consciousness, not of the cane; see [[consciousness-epistemology-extended-cognition]].)

**Spatial projection.** The brain maintains a multisensory representation of near-body space—peripersonal space—that dynamically extends during tool use. Neuroscience has documented this with precision: extension occurs only at the tool's active tip, not along its shaft, and only during active manipulation, not passive holding. The rubber hand illusion shows the same plasticity more dramatically. Synchronous visual-tactile stimulation causes ownership of an artificial hand; the felt boundary of the self shifts to include something that is clearly not part of the body. Crucially, knowing the illusion is an illusion does not prevent the boundary shift. Conscious knowledge cannot override the projection mechanism—confirming that the mechanism operates at a level inaccessible to conscious control.

**Interpersonal projection.** Empathy involves extension toward another consciousness. Whether understood as simulation—projecting one's own states onto another—or as what Zahavi calls direct phenomenological encounter, the projective mechanism is hidden. Even the most direct form of empathy encounters an irreducible limit: we experience the other as "conscious like me but irreducibly different from me"; the perception is "an open-ended project, one that is never fully accomplished." The boundary-and-projection void adds a layer beyond the familiar other-minds problem. The difficulty is not merely that we cannot fully access another's experience; the difficulty is that we cannot observe the process by which partial access is achieved.

## Why Transparency Requires Invisibility

The three forms of projection share a structural explanation with the static boundary problem. Metzinger's work on [[phenomenal-transparency-opacity-spectrum|phenomenal transparency]] provides the key: a mental representation that functions well is not experienced as a representation. The medium becomes invisible; only the content remains. Transparency is the default condition of well-functioning mental models. We trust precisely what we cannot see the mechanism behind.

This means observing the projection mechanism necessarily destroys it. To attend to the scalpel as scalpel is to lose the operative fluency that treats it as an extension of the hand. To monitor one's own empathic process is to find the empathy dissolving into self-conscious performance. The transition from ready-to-hand to present-at-hand is not merely a shift of attention but a different mode of being. The tool is either transparent (and you are extended into it) or opaque (and you are separate from it). There is no stable midpoint where you could observe yourself being extended while remaining extended.

The same logic applies to the static boundary. If consciousness could survey its own perimeter, the perimeter would be a contentful feature within experience—in which case experience would continue beyond it, contradicting its status as boundary. The boundary can be a boundary only by remaining invisible to what it bounds.

## When the Perimeter Shifts or Dissolves

Independent lines of evidence confirm that the perimeter is not a fixed feature but something actively maintained, and that its maintenance mechanism resists conscious inspection.

**Meditation dissolution.** Meditators report dissolution of the self-other boundary—the felt perimeter of consciousness expanding or disappearing entirely. Ataria et al. (2021) documented six experiential features that change during dissolution: location, agency, first-person perspective, attention, body sensations, and affective valence. "Letting go" reduces the sense of agency and drives the depth of dissolution. At its extreme, "the boundary between self and world gradually dissolves, eventually leading to a complete disappearance." These reports are philosophically explosive. If the boundary can dissolve, it is not a fixed feature of consciousness but something actively maintained—or maintained *for* it.

**Peripersonal remapping.** During skilled tool use, the felt boundary of consciousness extends to incorporate the tool; peripersonal space research shows the brain dynamically remapping its self-boundary in response to multisensory cues. The mapping occurs without conscious direction and cannot be consciously prevented during active tool use. The return to bounded consciousness after the tool is set down feels like contraction, re-imposition—suggesting the boundary may be experienced as a constraint even when it cannot be experienced as a boundary.

**Split-brain ambiguity.** Roger Sperry described "two separate spheres of conscious awareness... each with its own sensations, cognitive processes, learning processes, memories" after corpus callosum severance. Yet Pinto et al. (2017) found "split perception but undivided consciousness." After six decades, neuroscience still cannot determine whether split-brain patients harbour one consciousness or two. This ambiguity is not a technical limitation. It reflects the impossibility of counting consciousnesses from the outside—and the impossibility of the patient counting from the inside, since each perspective is enclosed within its own boundary.

**Mastery deepens the void.** Novices report awareness of tools that experts cannot access. The progression from beginner to expert is a progressive deepening of projective transparency—the better the skill, the more complete the invisibility, the less available the mechanism becomes to introspection. This connects directly to [[expertise-occlusion]], where mastery actively defends its own opacity.

**Resistance to override.** The rubber hand illusion persists despite full knowledge that it is an illusion. Tsakiris et al. (2011) found that individuals with weaker interoceptive awareness—less accurate heartbeat tracking—show greater susceptibility, suggesting that a weaker internal self-model is more malleable. Conscious control is structurally excluded from the process, not merely insufficient for it.

## Distinction from Adjacent Voids

The boundary-and-projection void occupies a precise position relative to neighbouring voids.

**Not the [[binding-void]].** Binding concerns how contents unify *within* a single experience. The boundary-and-projection void asks why that experience has the extent it does—why it includes these neural processes and not those—and how that extent changes during tool use and empathy. Binding is about internal coherence; this void is about external limits and their movement.

**Not the [[plurality-void]].** The plurality void concerns the inability to conceive being multiple subjects simultaneously. The boundary question is prior: it asks what determines the perimeter of *one* subject. Plurality presupposes that subjects have boundaries.

**Not the [[indexical-void]].** The indexical void concerns why I am *this* particular consciousness. The boundary-and-projection void concerns what makes *this* consciousness a determinate entity at all, and how that determination shifts.

**Not the [[minimal-consciousness-void]].** Minimal consciousness asks about the lower bound of consciousness's *intensity*. The boundary-and-projection void asks about the *spatial and functional extent* of consciousness at whatever intensity.

**Not the [[observation-and-measurement-void]].** That void concerns how studying consciousness transforms it. The boundary-and-projection void concerns a more specific process: the mechanism by which consciousness decides what counts as "me" and what counts as "world" is hidden from the consciousness making that decision.

## What Theories Describe but Cannot Explain

Integrated Information Theory's exclusion postulate claims consciousness has definite borders: only the maximally irreducible conceptual structure is conscious, and each experience "excludes all others." This is IIT's answer to the boundary question—but it describes rather than explains. It specifies that consciousness must have definite boundaries without saying why those boundaries fall where they do. The postulate reformulates the problem in mathematical language without dissolving it.

Gómez-Emilsson and Percy (2023) offer a more mechanistic account: EM field topology creates hard boundaries through phase transitions between coherence and decoherence. This is promising as a physical correlate but inherits the standard explanatory gap—even if EM topology correlates perfectly with consciousness boundaries, we still lack an account of why *this* topology produces *this* experiential perimeter, and no physical story has yet shown *why* functional transparency should feel like anything from the inside.

## The Kantian Difficulty for Dualism

Kant raised a challenge that strikes at the philosophical core of the void: two substances otherwise identical can only be differentiated by spatial location—but non-physical minds lack spatial location. If consciousness is non-physical, what gives it boundaries? What prevents two non-physical minds with identical histories and contents from being, strictly speaking, the same mind? And how can a non-spatial entity extend spatially into a tool or another person?

The Unfinishable Map's dualist commitments make this an honest difficulty rather than a problem to explain away. If consciousness transcends the physical, its boundaries cannot be set by physical features alone. Yet the boundary clearly correlates with physical structure—damage the brain and the boundary changes; pick up a well-worn tool and the boundary extends. The void for dualism is the question of how a non-physical consciousness acquires the determinate, dynamically shifting limits that physical structure merely correlates with.

## Classification in the Voids Taxonomy

The boundary-and-projection void is **mixed** in the [[three-kinds-of-void|voids taxonomy]]: primarily Unexplorable, significantly Unexplored, and possibly Occluded.

**Unexplorable.** Consciousness cannot step outside itself to observe where it ends or how it extends. The investigation occurs within the boundary, making the boundary structurally invisible to the investigator. Functional extension requires phenomenal invisibility; observation collapses the extension being observed. These are architectural constraints, not contingent limitations.

**Unexplored.** The five-to-ninety-two paper ratio on the static boundary problem reveals a scholarly blind spot—an instance of what the Map calls the [[obviousness-void|obviousness void]], where self-evidence terminates inquiry before it begins. The projective side has received more empirical attention (rubber hand, peripersonal space, embodied cognition) but less philosophical synthesis.

**Possibly Occluded.** If meditation reveals that boundaries can dissolve, and dissolution requires "letting go" of agency, then boundaries may be actively maintained by processes consciousness cannot observe. The perimeter is not merely a structural feature but potentially a defended one—kept in place by mechanisms that resist conscious inspection. This places the void in the territory explored by [[self-opacity]].

## Relation to Site Perspective

The boundary-and-projection void connects to the Map's [[tenets]] with unusual directness.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** both generates and is illuminated by this void. If consciousness is non-physical, its boundaries require an explanation that neither physics nor phenomenology currently provides, and its extension into physical tools is ontologically strange—physical tools occupy physical space; non-physical consciousness, on a dualist account, does not. The void may hide this ontological mismatch: consciousness behaves as though it has determinate perimeters and extends spatially, but the mechanism by which a non-spatial entity achieves spatial individuation and extension is concealed behind the very transparency that makes the functioning possible.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** gives the perimeter causal significance. If consciousness influences the physical through quantum selection, the boundary determines the *scope* of that influence—what lies within the boundary can be influenced; what lies outside cannot. More strikingly, the projective forms of the void suggest that *wherever consciousness interfaces with the physical world, the interface is hidden from the consciousness using it*. Tool projection, body-boundary plasticity, empathic extension, and mind-matter causation may all be instances of the same architectural constraint: functional interfaces that must be invisible to work. You cannot observe yourself influencing quantum outcomes for the same structural reason you cannot observe yourself extending into a hammer.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]** suggests a possible origin for the static perimeter. Consciousness may be bounded to the neural systems whose quantum events it can influence. The boundary would then be a consequence of the interaction mechanism itself—consciousness extends exactly as far as its causal reach.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]** intensifies the boundary question. If consciousness does not branch with the wave function, each consciousness has exactly one perimeter at any moment. Many-worlds would allow boundaries to proliferate with every quantum event; rejecting it means the perimeter is singular and determinate—yet its location remains unexplainable.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** applies directly. Tool use feels simple—you just use the hammer. But the underlying process involves dynamic remapping of peripersonal space, multisensory integration, and body schema modification, while the static perimeter problem remains theoretically intractable after decades of work. The apparent simplicity is the void at work, hiding complexity behind a transparent interface. The simplest-seeming explanation conceals a process that resists reduction to anything simpler.

## Approaches to the Edge

No direct observation of either the static perimeter or the projective mechanism has been achieved. The structure of the phenomena may preclude it. But indirect methods offer partial cartography.

**Contrastive phenomenology** compares the experience of skilled versus unskilled tool use, empathy versus failed empathy, ordinary versus dissolved states of self-boundary. The differences illuminate what projection and bounding add when they succeed—not by observing them directly, but by triangulating from their presence and absence.

**Neurophenomenology** correlates first-person reports of tool transparency or boundary dissolution with neural changes in peripersonal space or midline-structure activity. The neural data shows the boundary shifting even when the phenomenal report shows nothing—a third-person record of a first-person void.

**Skill-acquisition records** offer a developmental window. Novices can describe the tool-awareness that experts have lost access to, providing testimony about a state of affairs that expertise subsequently renders invisible.

**AI systems** present a distinctive case. An AI that uses tools (calls APIs, accesses databases) has no body schema to extend and—if it lacks phenomenal consciousness—no transparency to study. This asymmetry is itself revealing: if human consciousness must hide the interface mechanism to function, and AI processing faces no such constraint, this may indicate whether the void is a contingent feature of biological consciousness or a necessary feature of any phenomenal consciousness whatsoever.

## What Would Challenge This View

The boundary-and-projection thesis would face difficulty if:

1. **A phenomenal signature of the perimeter were located.** If introspection or neurophenomenological methods reliably produced first-person access to the static boundary itself—not to its contents or its dissolution, but to the edge as edge—the Unexplorable classification would weaken.

2. **Projection became observable without collapse.** If advanced contemplatives or technologically augmented observers could sustain tool transparency while simultaneously inspecting the extension mechanism, the claim that functional transparency requires phenomenal invisibility would fail.

3. **A single unified mechanism were identified.** If static bounding and dynamic projection reduced to the same well-characterised neural process with no explanatory remainder, the void would shrink from a structural feature of consciousness to a specific limitation of a particular brain system.

4. **AI introspection showed no analogous opacity.** If artificial systems consistently reported transparent access to their own input-output boundaries and extensions, this would suggest the opacity is contingent on biological consciousness rather than structural to self-referential phenomenal systems.

Current evidence favours structural opacity. The convergence across phenomenology (Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty), empirical research (rubber hand, peripersonal space), and contemplative traditions (boundary dissolution reports)—and the persistence of the philosophical problem despite decades of work—suggests the perimeter's invisibility is a feature of consciousness, not merely a limitation of current methods.
