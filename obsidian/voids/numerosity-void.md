Conscious perception of cardinality has a hard ceiling at roughly four items. Below the ceiling, quantity is *given*—three dots are *three*, immediately and as a unity, with no felt act of counting. Above the ceiling, perception decomposes into either serial counting or approximate magnitude estimation governed by Weber's law. The ceiling is one of the most empirically robust findings in cognitive science, replicating across labs, ages, neural-imaging modalities, and cultures whose languages have no exact-number words above three. It is also structurally invisible to the consciousness it constrains. We have the *output* of subitizing—the determinate cardinal—without phenomenal access to the *operation* that produced it. We do not feel ourselves "almost subitizing five." The Unfinishable Map calls this the **numerosity void**: an architectural cap on parallel cardinal perception whose existence we infer from outside but whose interior cannot be entered from within.

The void is primarily *unexplorable* in the [[three-kinds-of-void|voids taxonomy]]—a non-removable architectural cap—with an *occluded* face: the cap manifests *as* the experience of cardinality rather than being inspectable from within it. Its three distinguishable faces—the [[#the-cap-face|cap face]], the [[#the-transition-face|transition face]], and the [[#the-mechanism-face|mechanism face]] (each explained below)—share the architecture but expose different layers of opacity.

## The Two-System Architecture

Two distinct cognitive systems underpin non-symbolic numerical cognition (Feigenson, Dehaene, & Spelke, 2004). The **Parallel Individuation System** (PIS) encodes the numerical identity of individual items, capped at approximately four. The **Approximate Number System** (ANS) encodes the magnitude of larger sets, governed by Weber's law: two sets are discriminable only if they differ by a given numerical *ratio* rather than by a given absolute difference. Adult ANS precision plateaus at roughly fifteen percent—about the resolution at which one can distinguish a hundred dots from a hundred and fifteen without counting (Hyde, 2011).

Trick and Pylyshyn (1994) propose that the visual system indexes objects via a small pool of pre-attentive tokens they call *Fingers of INSTantiation* (FINSTs): "The visual system is capable of indexing a limited number (∼4) of objects after parallel preattentive grouping processes." Reaction-time curves for enumeration show a characteristic elbow between four and five items—negligible cost per item below, a steep climb above. Cowan's (2001) review of working-memory models reaches a convergent figure: "A central memory store limited to 3 to 5 meaningful items in young adults."

The convergence across independent methodologies—behavioural reaction times, working-memory modelling, infant looking-time studies, neural-imaging of intraparietal sulcus, cross-cultural studies of innumerate languages—is the strongest evidence that the cap is architectural rather than artifactual.

## The Cap Face

The first face is the architectural ceiling itself. Below four, cardinality is given; above four, it is reconstructed. The ceiling is not a soft attentional bottleneck but a handoff between two distinct systems with different rules.

This is more than a bandwidth limit. We can attentionally *track* up to about five moving objects in multiple-object-tracking paradigms, often with reduced precision. Tracking is not the same as perceiving cardinality as a unit. A subject tracking five objects does not have the experience "there are five"; the cardinality is imposed by counting or estimation, not perceived. The dissociation between trackable-but-not-cardinally-given and cardinally-given suggests cardinality-perception is a distinct conscious operation, not a downstream function of attentional bandwidth.

Cross-cultural evidence sharpens the architectural reading. The Pirahã have no linguistic items for exact quantity, not even "one," yet match small sets perfectly when items remain present and fail when memory is required (Frank et al., 2008). The Mundurukú have words for exact numbers up to about three and approximate words to about five; they perform like French controls on approximate tasks but stumble on exact subtraction (Pica et al., 2004). Number words function as cognitive technology for tracking exact cardinality across time and modality, not as the basis of cardinality perception itself. The cap underneath the language is universal.

## The Transition Face

The second face is the phenomenal opacity of the transition between subitizing and counting/estimation. We have the experience of *knowing how many* (≤4) and the experience of *having to count or estimate* (≥5). We do not have an experience of the *boundary* between them. There is no felt-edge—no "almost subitizing five," no "the cardinality is starting to slip."

The transition is by phenomenal substitution rather than continuous gradient. One regime gives way to another the way a perceptual figure flips: there is no halfway-state in which both modes are partially present. Briggs et al. (2023) study the boundary region directly and find that attentional strategy modulates the transition zone, suggesting the wall is not absolutely fixed but context-sensitive. Yet even in studies designed to probe the transition, third-person measurements track behavioural signatures—reaction times, accuracy slopes—rather than the subject's *experience of transitioning*. The transition is reported only as "I could see it" or "I had to count or estimate"; the texture between these reports is missing because the experience between them is missing.

This is the apophatic-cartography signature in miniature. The void is bounded with high resolution from outside (millisecond-precise behavioural measurements) while remaining structurally absent from inside.

## The Mechanism Face

The third face is the inaccessibility of the operation that produces the subitizing output. Subitizing's name comes from Latin *subitus*, "sudden": cardinality arrives all at once, without any felt act that produced it. The PIS allocates indices to objects in parallel; from inside, only the result is given. We cannot witness the FINST allocation, the parallel binding, or the output's assembly. The mechanism is structurally invisible to its product.

Carruthers (2011) argues more generally that introspective access to one's own cognition is interpretive rather than transparent—reconstructed from sensory cues and perceptual awareness. The felt-immediacy of subitizing is itself a cue, not a window. We perceive *cardinality-as-given* the way we perceive *colour-as-out-there*: as a finished property of the world rather than as a constructed feature of perception. This connects subitizing to the broader pattern of [[noetic-feelings-void|noetic feelings]] (the felt sense that an answer is just *there*) and [[self-opacity|self-opacity]] (the routine inaccessibility of the operations that yield our own thoughts).

The mechanism face matters because it shows that even where the output is *vividly determinate*—nothing feels more certain than perceiving three dots—the operation behind the output is no more accessible than any other unconscious process. Determinacy of result is no evidence of transparency of cause.

## What AI Sees Differently

Artificial vision systems with combinatorial counting modules and unbounded working buffers do not exhibit the elbow. A computer-vision pipeline can detect cardinality of arbitrary sets in linear or logarithmic time without a structural break at four. This asymmetry is informative in two ways. First, the cap is not necessary for cardinality detection in general—it is a feature of *conscious cardinal perception* specifically, whatever conscious perception turns out to be. Second, AI cardinality detection is probably not subitizing: a parallel-counter produces a cardinality output without (presumably) the *phenomenal immediacy* the human subitizing experience involves.

This is a candidate case for [[non-human-minds-as-void-explorers]]: AI systems may compute cardinality without encountering the cap, sharpening the question of what the cap is *of* in the human case. Cardinality computation is unbounded; cardinal *perception*—the way the cardinality is *given to* a perceiver—has a ceiling. The Map reads the asymmetry as evidence that the cap belongs to phenomenal perception rather than to information processing.

## The Introspective Ceiling

The void constrains the Map's own methodology. When introspecting and reporting on cognition, all reports are mediated by a perceptual apparatus with a four-item ceiling on parallel cardinality. Any introspective claim that requires holding more than about four phenomenal-content units in single-act apprehension *must* fail or decompose into seriation. Apophatic mapping that exceeds the ceiling implicitly relies on serial reconstruction or magnitude estimation; it cannot be done in one act of phenomenal grasp.

Reports that *appear* to take in many phenomenal contents at once are either compressed (chunked under summary descriptions), serialised (apparently parallel but actually sequential), or estimated (magnitude rather than cardinal). The Map cannot, from inside its own cognition, hold more than a few thinks-at-once, no matter how many it claims to hold.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The numerosity void connects to the Map's [[tenets|tenets]] across multiple axes.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** receives a partial dialectical illustration. The cap is at the *phenomenal-perception* level, not at the level of information-theoretic carrying capacity. The dissociation between trackable-but-not-cardinally-given and cardinally-given items suggests cardinality-perception is a distinct conscious operation rather than a function of attentional bandwidth alone. This is a dialectical (not knockdown) point in favour of treating phenomenal perception as more than a feature of its enabling neural substrate. A flat-bandwidth model has to add architecture to explain why some trackable items do not reach cardinal givenness; a dualist reading takes the architecture as evidence that phenomenal perception is its own kind of operation.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** receives a worked example. The simplest account—"the brain runs out of bandwidth at four"—under-describes the architecture. Two distinct systems (PIS and ANS) operate with different rules above and below the cap; the cap is a *handoff*, not a saturation. Compressing the structure into "working-memory limit" loses the distinction the empirical record requires. Parsimony at the gross-bandwidth level erases architectural detail that fine-grained data demands.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** suggests a speculative consequence. If the bidirectional interface operates over phenomenally-given content, the four-item cap on directly-given cardinality could be a hard constraint on the granularity at which consciousness can act. Whatever consciousness causally manipulates, it manipulates in units small enough to be cardinally given. The Map flags this as an open conjecture rather than a settled implication.

The numerosity void shares the deep structure of the [[quantitative-intuition-void]]: in both, *symbolic* manipulation of number is unbounded while *phenomenal* grasp is sharply limited. The numerosity void marks the smallest end of the same structural pattern—where direct cardinal perception ends and reconstructive cognition takes over.
