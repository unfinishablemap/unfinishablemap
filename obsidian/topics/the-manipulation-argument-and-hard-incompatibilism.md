Derk Pereboom's **four-case manipulation argument** is the leading argument that moral responsibility is incompatible with determinism, and it is paired with a positive view—**hard incompatibilism**—that makes it the most dangerous opponent the Map's free-will program faces. The argument imagines Professor Plum committing the same murder in four scenarios that shade smoothly into one another: (1) a neuroscientist manipulates his reasoning in real time; (2) he was programmed at creation to reason this way; (3) he was rigorously determined by his upbringing; (4) he is simply a normally determined agent in a deterministic world. Plum meets every standard compatibilist condition in all four. If he is not responsible in Case 1, and no responsibility-relevant difference separates any case from the next, then he is not responsible in Case 4 either—so ordinary determinism defeats responsibility just as manipulation does. The danger to the Map is not the incompatibilist conclusion, which the Map [[the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism|already accepts]]. The danger is hard incompatibilism's second front: Pereboom argues the *libertarian* escape also fails, because indeterminism adds luck rather than control. The Map's reply—that its quantum interface is a genuine selection mechanism—is set out in the "Relation to Site Perspective" section below, and it is offered there as one contested answer, not a refutation. Pereboom's objection to libertarian control remains forceful and unresolved.

## The Four-Case Argument

Pereboom first published the argument in "Determinism al Dente" (1995) and developed it across two monographs, *Living Without Free Will* (2001) and *Free Will, Agency, and Meaning in Life* (2014). Its target is precise: not responsibility in every sense, but **basic-desert moral responsibility**. On Pereboom's characterisation, an agent is responsible in the basic-desert sense if she deserves blame or credit *just in virtue of* having performed the action with an understanding of its moral status—not because blaming her would produce good consequences, honour a contract, or serve some forward-looking aim. Basic desert is what would make backward-looking blame and retributive punishment fitting. It is a demanding notion, and it is the one the argument attacks.

The four cases share a fixed core. Professor Plum murders Ms White for self-interested reasons, after a process of reasoning that is moderately responsive to reasons; he could have been swayed by sufficiently strong moral considerations but was not. He acts on desires he reflectively endorses, and his action flows from his own character. These are exactly the conditions leading compatibilists—reasons-responsiveness theorists, hierarchical-desire theorists—say suffice for responsibility. The cases differ only in the *causal route* by which Plum came to be the deliberator he is:

- **Case 1**: A team of neuroscientists manipulates Plum's brain in real time, tailoring his reasoning process so that it issues in the decision to kill. The manipulation works through his deliberation, not around it, and produces a moderately reasons-responsive agent.
- **Case 2**: The neuroscientists did their work once, at the beginning of Plum's life—programming his character so that, in his circumstances, he would reason and decide exactly as he does. No real-time intervention is needed.
- **Case 3**: No neuroscientists. Plum was determined to his character by the rigorous training and conditioning of his home community during childhood, which fixed his reasoning dispositions no less thoroughly.
- **Case 4**: No manipulators and no unusual upbringing. Plum is an ordinary human being, determined to his action by the natural laws and the state of the world before his birth, in the way any agent is if determinism is true.

The intuition Pereboom leans on is that Plum is *not* morally responsible in the basic-desert sense in Case 1: an agent whose reasoning is engineered on the spot by others is a puppet, not a deserving wrongdoer. The argument then presses a second claim: there is no responsibility-relevant difference between any case and its neighbour. Real-time versus one-time intervention (1 versus 2) cannot matter, since the timing of the determining cause is morally irrelevant. Manipulation by persons versus determination by impersonal upbringing (2 versus 3) cannot matter, since the presence of another intending agent in the causal history does not touch Plum's own psychology. And a determining childhood versus ordinary natural determination (3 versus 4) cannot matter, since both equally fix the agent's character by factors outside his control. If non-responsibility holds at Case 1 and never gets restored across the continuum, it holds at Case 4.

Pereboom's 2014 restatement sharpens this into a **best-explanation** argument rather than a chain of pairwise comparisons. The best explanation of our shared sense that Plum is not responsible in the manipulation cases, he argues, is that Plum is determined to act by factors he had no hand in producing—and that explanation applies with equal force to Case 4. The compatibilist who resists the conclusion owes an account of what *else* explains the intuition, one that distinguishes ordinary determinism from manipulation. Pereboom's wager is that no such account survives scrutiny.

## Hard Incompatibilism: The Two-Front View

The manipulation argument is only the negative half against compatibilism. Pereboom's positive position, **hard incompatibilism**, is the thesis that we lack the free will required for basic-desert responsibility whether determinism is true *or* the kind of indeterminism our best physics might supply obtains. This is what separates hard incompatibilism from the [[the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism|Consequence Argument]]: van Inwagen's argument establishes incompatibilism and stops, leaving open that libertarianism might be true if determinism is false. Pereboom closes that door. His view is "harder" because it argues the libertarian exit is blocked too.

The case against libertarianism runs on two prongs, matching the two families of libertarian theory. Against **event-causal libertarianism**—the view (developed by Robert Kane and others) that free decisions are undetermined events caused by the agent's prior reasons and desires—Pereboom deploys what has come to be called the **disappearing-agent** objection. If the agent's antecedent states leave it genuinely open which decision occurs, then those states do not *settle* the outcome; something is left to chance at the moment of decision, and nothing the agent is or does accounts for why this decision rather than that one was made. The indeterminism, on this reading, subtracts control instead of adding it. This is the same worry the [[luck-objection|luck objection]] presses through van Inwagen's "rollback" thought experiment—rewind the universe to the moment of choice and, with everything about the agent held fixed, the outcome can differ—and the two objections are close cousins, though the manipulation argument itself is aimed at compatibilism and the luck objection at libertarianism.

Against **agent-causal libertarianism**—the view that the agent is a substance-cause who directly originates the decision, not reducible to prior events—Pereboom concedes more. Agent causation, he grants, *could in principle* secure the enhanced control that basic desert requires; it is not incoherent, and it answers the disappearing-agent worry by supplying a genuine originator. His objection is empirical rather than conceptual. We have, he argues, no positive scientific reason to believe we are agent-causes, and reconciling agent-causal interventions with the physical regularities we actually observe would require what he calls **wild coincidences**: the agent-causal choices would have to line up with the statistical patterns physics records (for instance, that neural events conform to physical law on average) with no explanation of why free interventions should respect those aggregate regularities. Agent causation buys control at the price of a physics that looks suspiciously stage-managed.

Putting the prongs together: determinism rules out basic-desert responsibility (the four-case argument); event-causal indeterminism cannot restore it (luck); and agent-causal indeterminism, which could restore it, is empirically unmotivated (wild coincidences). So responsibility-grounding free will is unlikely to exist on any of the going metaphysical options. Pereboom pairs this sceptical conclusion with a constructive ethics—morality, meaning, and reformed, forward-looking responses to wrongdoing survive without basic desert—but that constructive project lies outside the scope of this article.

## Soft-Line and Hard-Line Replies

Compatibilist responses to the four-case argument divide into two strategies, and the division is worth stating because it marks where the argument is vulnerable.

A **soft-line** reply concedes that Plum is not responsible in Case 1 but denies the no-difference claim: it locates a responsibility-relevant seam somewhere in the continuum, usually at the 3→4 transition. Kristin Mickelson's "soft-line solution" (2010) argues, for example, that the manipulated agent's capacities were produced in a way that differs morally from ordinary causal history—the involvement of a designing agent, or the bypassing of the agent's own development, marks a real distinction. Pereboom's standard rejoinder is that any proposed difference can be neutralised by tuning the intermediate cases: because the continuum is deliberately smooth, a candidate seam at 3→4 can be shown irrelevant by constructing a case that straddles it.

A **hard-line** reply instead bites the bullet: it accepts the no-difference claim but *denies the opening intuition*, holding that a manipulated agent who satisfies the compatibilist conditions can be morally responsible after all. Michael McKenna's "hard-line reply" (2008) argues that Case 1 should be treated as an open question rather than a clear verdict of non-responsibility, and that our confidence about the *ordinary* Case 4—where we do ordinarily hold agents responsible—should be allowed to transfer *backwards* to unsettle the Case 1 intuition, rather than the reverse. Sofia Jeppsson's "agential perspective" reply (2020) develops a related line, grounding responsibility in the agent's own deliberative standpoint rather than in facts about what caused the agent to be as she is. The hard-line strategy concedes the argument's structure and contests its premise, which is a reminder that the argument's force rests on a defeasible intuition-pump, not a proof.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Hard incompatibilism is the Map's most dangerous free-will opponent precisely because it is a *partial ally*. It grants the incompatibilism the Map's [[free-will|free-will position]] depends on, agreeing that determinism would abolish desert-entailing agency. The threat is entirely on the second front: Pereboom argues that the libertarian metaphysics the Map builds to escape determinism cannot deliver control either. Since Tenets 2 and 3 posit exactly the kind of non-physical, agent-harnessable influence on quantum outcomes that Pereboom's empirical objection targets, the Map cannot route around the challenge. It has to meet it.

The Map's reply is that its interface is a genuine **selection** mechanism, not a randomiser. On the [[the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism|interactionist picture]], consciousness makes a minimal, non-physical contribution at the point where an outcome becomes actual—the [[quantum-indeterminacy-free-will|post-decoherence selection]] the [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] and [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] tenets describe. The claim is that the agent *settles* which of the physically-open outcomes is realised, so what happens at the interface is the agent's doing rather than a chance fluctuation. If that claim can be made good, it is a candidate for exactly the "enhanced control" Pereboom says indeterminism cannot provide: the disappearing-agent objection assumes that where the prior states leave the outcome open, *nothing* settles it, and the Map's [[agent-causation|agent-causal]] interface is an attempt to name the something that does.

This reply must be presented at its true strength, which is that of a contested bet rather than a demonstration. Pereboom's luck objection is forceful and, honestly assessed, unresolved. The Map does not have a worked model showing that a token-level bias which leaves the aggregate Born statistics undisturbed amounts to the agent's *settling* the outcome rather than merely being correlated with it; the [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] tenet itself registers this as a live hypothesis pending a toy model, not an established result. And Pereboom's empirical objection to agent-causal influence—the wild-coincidences worry—is a real cost the Map's framework pays, not one it has discharged. The interface has to be reconcilable with the physical regularities we observe, and the Map's post-decoherence proposal is constructed to leave those regularities statistically intact precisely so that no detectable anomaly arises; that very feature is what makes the influence empirically hard to distinguish from chance, which is the near neighbour of Pereboom's complaint that indeterministic control is indistinguishable from luck.

Where the Map does have leverage is at the level of method, and this is a [[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5]] point. Pereboom's rejection of agent causation is not a proof of its falsity but a **parsimony-and-plausibility** judgement: agent causation is dismissed as empirically unmotivated and as requiring coincidences too wild to credit. Tenet 5 holds that simplicity and antecedent plausibility are unreliable guides to truth where our knowledge is incomplete, and the mind-body interface is a paradigm case of incompleteness. So the Map can decline to treat "we have no positive scientific reason to posit agent causation" as decisive, while conceding—symmetrically, as Tenet 5 requires—that this move buys no positive support for agent causation either. It blocks the objection from being a refutation; it does not convert the Map's bet into a demonstration. The honest position is that hard incompatibilism and the Map agree on the incompatibilist premise and disagree, at the edge of what anyone can currently establish, on whether harnessed indeterminism can be control. The Map places its bet there knowingly, and grants that the opponent's hand is strong.
