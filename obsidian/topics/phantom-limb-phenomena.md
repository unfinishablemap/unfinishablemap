Phantom limb phenomena are conscious experiences of body parts that are absent — most often after amputation, but also in people born without a limb (aplasia) and in cases of complete spinal cord transection where intact limbs become functionally absent to the brain. Roughly 60–80% of amputees experience phantom sensations, and a similar proportion experience phantom limb pain at some point, though prevalence and persistence vary widely (Ramachandran and Hirstein, 1998; Flor et al., 2006). The phantom can feel its position, attempt to move, ache, itch, cramp, and adopt impossible postures. It can be touched by referred sensation when the face or stump is stimulated, and it can be visually "moved" out of cramped postures using mirror feedback. The phenomenology is rich and the neuroscience is unusually well-developed.

The Unfinishable Map's reading: phantoms decisively refute the naive picture that bodily experience is read off from peripheral signals *at the felt site* — there are no signals from the absent limb, yet there is bodily experience referred to its location. Sophisticated peripheral-plus-central accounts, which acknowledge stump, dorsal-root-ganglion, spinal and cortical contributions to phantom phenomena, are not the target of that refutation and remain live options. What phantoms support is the more cautious claim that the felt body is constructed by the brain rather than transmitted from a peripheral source at the felt location, and this is the convergent view of modern pain science — Melzack's body-self neuromatrix theory, predictive-processing accounts of pain, and the biopsychosocial tradition all converge on it within physicalism. It is therefore not a Map-distinctive finding and it does not on its own embarrass any twenty-first-century materialist account of pain. What makes phantoms philosophically loaded for the Map is then three further features that *extend* the central-construction observation rather than independently re-confirming it: (i) phantom *qualia* are as vivid as ordinary bodily qualia, displayed in a clinically vivid setting where the central production conditions can be specified with unusual precision without narrowing the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] between specification and felt content; (ii) phantom pain is reliably modulated by purely visual and imaginative interventions, providing controlled evidence for [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|top-down causation]] on a nociceptive channel; and (iii) congenital phantoms — felt limbs in people who never had limbs — show, in the minority of aplasia cases that report them, that the bodily self-model is not built up entirely from sensorimotor experience with one's own specific limb, although whether the residual mechanism is best read as architectural or as developmental-mirror-neuron calibration from external bodies is the live empirical debate engaged [below](#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema). The three features are not three independent confirmations of central construction; they are three specifically-shaped extensions riding on the same base observation, and the [[#common-cause-null-audit|common-cause-null audit]] [below](#common-cause-null-audit) makes that calibration honest rather than letting a surface three-fold count over-supply inferential force. None of this proves dualism. All of it sharpens what an adequate theory of embodied consciousness must explain, and the two strongest contemporary physicalist articulations of that theory — Melzack's neuromatrix and the predictive-processing account that has now largely succeeded it as the working framework in pain neuroscience — are engaged directly [below](#neuromatrix) and [below](#predictive-processing) rather than treated as absent.

## What Phantoms Are Like

Phantom limb sensations are reported with the same first-person directness as sensations in intact limbs. Amputees describe feeling the position of the missing arm, sensing rings or watches worn at the time of amputation, attempting to gesture with it, and being startled when the phantom passes through a wall. Many report a *telescoping* progression in which the phantom shortens over months or years, sometimes ending with the phantom hand attached at the shoulder. Others report a *frozen* phantom locked in the posture the limb occupied at amputation, often the painful posture that prompted the amputation.

The pain dimension is more various. Phantom limb pain typically presents as burning, crushing, stabbing, or electrical sensations referred to specific regions of the absent limb. It is not the same as stump pain, which originates from the residual tissue and has clearer peripheral correlates. Phantom pain can be intermittent or constant, and is famously resistant to standard analgesics. A particularly striking variant is *pain memory*: phantom pain that reproduces the qualitative character and location of pre-amputation pain, sometimes years later, indicating that the phenomenal content of pain can be stored and re-instantiated by neural mechanisms in the absence of any current peripheral source. Patients commonly report that the affected phantom hand or foot is in an impossible posture — clenched fingernails digging into the palm of a hand that does not exist — and that the pain tracks the impossible posture rather than tissue damage at any actual location.

Phantom phenomenology violates several intuitive constraints on what experience can be. There is felt position without proprioceptive input. There is felt movement without efferent-afferent loops at the limb. There is felt pain without nociceptive transduction at the felt site. There is, in the congenital cases, felt limb-shape with no history of having had the limb at all (Brugger et al., 2000). Whatever the underlying mechanism, it has to be one in which the brain — or the brain-mediated [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] — generates the felt body in a way that does not require the body part to exist.

## Cortical Body Maps: From Remapping to Persistent Representation {#cortical-reorganisation}

The dominant neuroscientific account of phantoms in the 1990s, developed largely from work by Tim Pons, Vilayanur Ramachandran, Herta Flor and colleagues, located them in reorganisation of somatosensory and motor cortex. After deafferentation, the cortical territory that previously represented the absent limb does not go silent. Adjacent representations — for the face, on the lateral side of the hand area, and for the upper arm or stump on the medial side — were proposed to invade the deafferented region. Stimulation of the face or stump can evoke sensations referred to specific phantom locations, often with topographic precision: stroking specific regions of the cheek produces felt touch on specific phantom fingers (Ramachandran et al., 1992). Flor et al. (1995) reported magnetoencephalographically that the magnitude of this putative remapping correlated with the intensity of phantom pain, and the *maladaptive cortical plasticity* model — phantom pain as the perceptual signature of dysregulated invasion of the deafferented hand area — became the textbook story.

That picture has not survived the higher-resolution longitudinal work of the past decade. A sustained programme from Tamar Makin and colleagues has shown, across more than a decade of fMRI studies, that the cortical signature of the missing hand in amputees is closer to *preserved* than to *remapped*: amputees with stronger phantom pain show *stronger*, not weaker, residual representation of the absent hand in the former hand area, and the face-into-hand invasion that the older account predicted is largely absent in primary sensorimotor cortex when measured with modern techniques (Makin et al., 2013; Wesselink et al., 2019). The somatotopic organisation of individual finger representations remains detectable in patients many years post-amputation. The senior synthesis of this programme (Makin & Krakauer, 2023) rereads the wider literature: much of what older work treated as adaptive remapping is, on the newer evidence, potentiation of pre-existing architecture or methodological artefact of low-resolution localisation, with little of the deep cortical reorganisation that the standard mechanism required. This is an in-framework correction. The methodological standards by which Flor 1995 was originally accepted — MEG localisation precision, sample size, longitudinal sensitivity — are the same standards by which the longitudinal Makin programme finds the original picture wanting.

The strongest single test to date is Schone et al. (2025). The study performed fMRI in three adults *before* planned arm amputation and tracked them up to five years afterwards, the first such pre/post-amputation longitudinal design in the literature. Cortical hand and lip representations remained stable across the amputation event; the classic lip-into-hand-region shift was not observed in these participants. Because the design is within-subject and prospective, it rules out the cohort-confound interpretations that had haunted earlier cross-sectional remapping studies. Combined with Root et al. (2022), which finds a complex pattern of facial remapping in somatosensory cortex following *congenital but not acquired* hand loss, the leading reading is now that adult cortical body maps are remarkably stable through amputation and that the older "invasion" picture was an inferential overlay on lower-resolution data rather than a robust empirical finding.

For the Map this revision is welcome rather than awkward. The Flor-era story tried to explain phantom phenomenology by appeal to dynamic neural reorganisation following limb loss — a sufficient-mechanism story that lent itself to a deflationary reading on which phantoms were the perceptual artefact of cortical territory finding new uses. The newer picture says something stronger: the felt body persists in the cortex precisely because the cortical body model does, with or without the limb. That is the structural commitment a [[somatic-interface|somatic-interface]] reading of embodiment expects, and the kind of *persistent representation* a [[filter-theory|filter-theoretic]] reading can absorb without strain. The argument from phantoms now does not need to bridge from changing neural patterns to felt content; it bridges from a *stable* neural body model to felt content, which is the same [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] in a tidier empirical setting.

Peripheral and spinal mechanisms remain an essential part of the multilevel picture. Stump neuromas, ectopic firing in dorsal root ganglia, and spinal sensitisation all contribute, particularly to early phantom pain (Flor et al., 2006), and the central-versus-peripheral balance in maintaining chronic phantom pain is itself the subject of active debate. What the cortical-stability finding settles is not that periphery is irrelevant but that the central component is better described as persistent body-model activity than as dynamic remapping. Whichever multilevel mechanism turns out to be correct, the picture specifies which neurons fire under which conditions and does not bridge from those firings to the felt burning of a hand that does not exist. That is the explanatory gap in its most clinically vivid form.

## Mirror Therapy and Top-Down Modulation {#mirror-therapy}

In 1996, Ramachandran and Rogers-Ramachandran reported that placing a vertical mirror in the patient's midline so that the reflection of an intact limb appeared to occupy the position of the phantom could produce vivid felt movement of the phantom and, in some patients, immediate relief of phantom pain. The patient moves both hands together (or imagines moving the phantom while moving the intact hand); the visual experience of seeing the phantom move — often, *un-clenching* — can release the impossible posture and reduce the associated pain (Ramachandran and Rogers-Ramachandran, 1996).

Two claims have to be separated here. The first is *phenomenological modulation*: that mirror feedback can produce vivid felt movement of the phantom and, in some patients, immediate change in phantom-pain quality. This is well-attested in case series and small trials and is what the original Ramachandran demonstrations established. The second is *clinical efficacy*: that mirror therapy, considered as a clinical intervention, reliably reduces phantom limb pain across patient populations. Higher-quality placebo-controlled evidence on the clinical-efficacy claim is weaker than older systematic reviews suggested. Several randomised trials and meta-analyses report moderate reductions in phantom limb pain, particularly for upper-limb phantoms and for patients whose phantom is locked in a painful posture (Chan et al., 2007; Foell et al., 2014). Effect sizes are moderate, dropout is real, and not all patients respond. The mechanism remains debated: candidate explanations include reactivation of mirror-neuron systems, restoration of a normal visuomotor loop for the phantom, and reversal of the cortical remapping that the original work tied to phantom pain.

For the Map, the philosophically important feature is the causal structure. A purely visual stimulus — a mirror image — modulates the phenomenology and intensity of a pain with no peripheral generator at the felt site. The intervention does not target the periphery; it targets the patient's *visual experience* of their own body, which then influences nociceptive processing immediately and sometimes lastingly. This is among the cleanest available demonstrations that conscious perceptual content can shape pain processing without intermediate bodily intervention at the felt site. On the persistent-body-model reading developed [above](#cortical-reorganisation), what the mirror does is alter the visual evidence the brain's stable cortical body model is receiving about the missing limb, not reverse a remapping that may not have occurred in the first place.

This connects directly to [[clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation|other lines of evidence]] in which intentional mental activity changes the brain through routes that differ from peripheral or pharmacological intervention. Mirror therapy is not, on its own, decisive against materialism — neuronal accounts of how visual input modulates somatosensory and pain pathways are well-developed. "Top-down" in this context is not a synonym for "nonphysical": visual feedback, attention, expectation and affective appraisal are standard neural-level variables, and the predictive-processing reading of mirror therapy — visual prediction errors updating a high-precision generative body-model whose interoceptive predictions then drive pain inference — is taken up directly in the section [below](#predictive-processing). What phantoms add is the *causal-pathway shape*: the modulation runs through what the patient consciously sees and attends to rather than through the periphery of the limb that hurts. That shape is compatible with materialism, and it is also exactly the kind of pathway the Map's [[interactionist-dualism|interactionist reading]] expects; the choice between those readings is not settled by mirror therapy alone.

## Congenital Phantoms and the Innate Body Schema {#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema}

The most surprising phantom phenomena come from people born without the limb in question. Peter Brugger and colleagues documented the case of a woman with congenital bilateral upper-limb aplasia who reported vivid phantom arms and hands she had never possessed, with detailed phenomenology including the ability to gesture mentally, to feel rings on phantom fingers, and to position the phantoms in space (Brugger et al., 2000). Functional imaging during phantom movement showed activation of the sensorimotor areas one would expect for actual limb movement, including premotor and parietal regions.

Brugger's case is the most-cited entry-point into the congenital-phantom literature, but it is a single case, and the wider literature is more divided than the singular vividness of that report can convey. The base rates are themselves contested. Melzack et al. (1997) reported phantom limb sensations in roughly 20% of children with congenital limb deficiency, and subsequent reviews have placed estimates between roughly 10% and 40% depending on cohort, definition of phantom, and method of elicitation. Whatever the precise figure, the majority of people with limb aplasia do not report phantoms. Any reading of the congenital cases has to do justice to that asymmetry rather than treat the Brugger case as a paradigm everyone with aplasia instantiates.

The strongest published in-framework critic of the innate-default-schema reading is Price (2006), whose review of the same literature proposes a developmental / mirror-neuron alternative on which the "innate" body schema is in fact built up during a sensitive period from external visual experience of other people's bodies. Two empirical observations carry that account. The first is the prosthesis-fitting correlation: in cohorts of children with congenital limb deficiency, fitting of a functional prosthesis before age seven predicts later phantom occurrence, suggesting that the schema requires either an actual limb or a regularly-used prosthetic surrogate during a developmental window if it is to support a phantom in adulthood. The second is the broader pattern that children raised with sustained visual and motor exposure to bodies with the limb in question — through prostheses, through caregivers, through their own embodied imitation games — are more likely to develop the corresponding phantom than children with no such exposure. The strong "predisposed to generate" reading attributes the phantom to architecture; the developmental reading attributes it to the architecture's specific calibration during a sensitive period in which external bodies serve as the training signal.

Honest engagement with Price requires conceding that the developmental account does substantive work the Map's reading must answer rather than dismiss. On the question whether congenital phantoms establish that the body schema is wholly innate, Price is right that the published cohort data — the prosthesis-fitting correlation in particular — do not licence the strong claim. The article therefore should not, and now does not, claim that the brain is unconditionally predisposed to generate a phantom in the absence of the limb. It claims something weaker and more defensible: that under some developmental conditions, the brain has the capacity to generate a body schema with default human form even in the absence of the corresponding limb, and that this capacity locates the body model at a deeper level than simple peripheral history. Whether the capacity is innate-and-then-calibrated by external bodies, as Price proposes, or innate-and-then-modulated by sensorimotor experience with one's own body, as the Brugger-derived reading proposes, is exactly the live question the field has not yet settled — and which Crawford (2014) describes as "one of the longest running and most acrimonious debates" in the phantom literature.

Where the article can defend the structural-default reading against the developmental alternative is on the cortical-signature evidence Root et al. (2022) supply. Their fMRI study reports a complex pattern of facial remapping in somatosensory cortex following *congenital but not acquired* hand loss — that is, the cortical signature of congenital aplasia is empirically distinguishable from the cortical signature of post-injury amputation in a way that pure visual-exposure-during-a-sensitive-period accounts do not straightforwardly predict. If the schema were entirely calibrated from external visual experience of other bodies, the expected cortical signature in adulthood would be closer to the post-injury case where the limb's external presence in the visual environment was sustained. The differential signature suggests that the developing brain encodes its own missing-limb configuration in a way that is not reducible to its exposure to other bodies, even if external exposure plays a calibration role. This is a real point of contact between the structural-default reading and the published critic. It is not a refutation of Price's account — the predictive-processing-flavoured developmental theorist can absorb Root 2022 by treating the differential cortical signature as the downstream consequence of differential precision-weighting on body-model priors during development — but it is the kind of in-framework consideration Price's account must metabolise rather than the kind of framework-boundary disagreement that ends the conversation.

The natural materialist accommodation of the residual structural-default reading is the [[predictive-processing|predictive-processing]] account on which innate priors over body configuration ride the brain's hierarchical generative architecture, refined and calibrated rather than built from scratch by experience — engaged directly in the section [below](#predictive-processing). The empirical observation the Map relies on is the modest one: that the body model is located at a deeper level than peripheral sensorimotor history with the specific limb, and that some developmental conditions allow that model to generate a phantom even when the limb itself was never there. That is what the competing readings — Brugger's structural-default, Price's developmental-mirror-neuron, the predictive-processing innate-prior account — are all trying to specify mechanism for, and the Map's reading does not commit to which mechanism wins.

For the Map, congenital phantoms suggest that the [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] has structural commitments not exhausted by the body's actual configuration. The interface is parameterised on a default human body, and consciousness engages with that parameterisation when developmental conditions support it. This does not entail anything mystical about the phantom — the brain is still doing the work, and the developmental literature constrains the conditions under which that work produces a phantom — but it shifts the locus of the felt body from peripheral history with the specific limb to a more general neural architecture in a way that materialist accounts must accommodate and that filter-theoretic accounts can absorb. The shift is real even on Price's developmental reading: the schema is still located deeper than peripheral history with one's own limb, even if external-body exposure is doing the calibration work the structural-default reading attributes to innate architecture.

## What Phantom Pain Tells Us About Pain

Phantom limb pain provides one of the cleanest natural dissociations in the philosophy of pain. There is no tissue damage at the felt site. There is no peripheral nociceptive transduction at the felt location. There is, however, a fully phenomenal pain experience: aversive, urgency-laden, motivating, exactly the kind of phenomenal state that pain is supposed to be when it is being pain. This is the inverse of [[pain-asymbolia|pain asymbolia]], where nociceptive sensation is preserved but the affective dimension is missing.

Together these dissociations chart a logical landscape that no peripheralist account of pain can occupy. Pain can be present without peripheral damage at the felt site (phantom pain). Pain's affective dimension can be absent despite preserved peripheral signalling (asymbolia). Pain can be modulated, sometimes dramatically, by purely visual or attentional manipulations (mirror therapy, hypnotic analgesia). Whatever pain is, it is not simply the conscious read-out of nociceptive transduction at a tissue site. This is the convergent view of modern pain science — Melzack's neuromatrix theory, predictive-processing accounts, and biopsychosocial models all proceed from the assumption that pain is centrally constructed — and the Map accepts the convergent picture rather than offering it as a distinctive finding. What the Map adds is a reading of the construction itself: pain is a phenomenal state with its own conditions of existence, its own dissociable components, and its own susceptibility to top-down influence, and the standard production-conditions accounts specify these without bridging from specification to felt content.

The Map's [[pain-consciousness-and-causal-power|treatment of pain]] takes this seriously. Pain is real, phenomenal, and causally efficacious; it is not a transparent indicator of peripheral state, and it does not require the periphery to be present at all. Phantom pain is one of the strongest cases for the central-construction claim, because the absence of the periphery is not a theoretical assumption but an undeniable physical fact; it is also a case in which the gap between central construction and felt content is unusually stark, which is the philosophically loaded part that the central-construction story shared with neuromatrix theory does not by itself resolve.

## The Neuromatrix Picture and Where the Map's Reading Differs {#neuromatrix}

The contemporary physicalist account most directly tailored to phantom phenomena is Ronald Melzack's body-self neuromatrix theory (Melzack, 1990; Melzack, 2001). Developed in dialogue with the same phantom-limb evidence the Map's reading engages, neuromatrix theory holds that the felt body is generated by a distributed neural network — somatosensory, thalamocortical, limbic — whose characteristic activity Melzack called a *neurosignature*. The network is partly genetically determined and partly hard-wired in its broad architecture; peripheral input modulates the neurosignature but is not required for it to produce experience; cognitive-evaluative and affective neuromodules can shape the output. The theory was articulated in part to make sense of congenital phantoms: Melzack proposed the hard-wired component precisely because phantoms in people who have never had a limb cannot be accounted for by stored representations of past sensorimotor experience.

This is the canonical contemporary physicalist competitor to the Map's reading, and the honest engagement begins by conceding how much of the article's empirical picture neuromatrix theory already commits to. The brain-constructed felt body is a neuromatrix claim. Persistent central representation in the absence of the limb is a neuromatrix claim. An innate, default-human body schema modulable by sensorimotor experience is a neuromatrix claim. Top-down modulation of nociceptive output by cognitive and affective neuromodules is a neuromatrix claim. The article's three "philosophically loaded" features — vivid qualia without a peripheral source, top-down modulation of pain, and congenital phantoms — are not findings that neuromatrix theory must accommodate against the grain. They are findings the theory was constructed to explain.

This concession has direct consequences for how the explanatory-gap argument from phantoms must be stated. A version of the argument that ran "phantoms embarrass materialism because materialism appeals to peripheral signals" engages a position no twenty-first-century pain theorist holds. Neuromatrix theory, predictive-processing accounts of pain, and biopsychosocial models all proceed from the assumption that pain is centrally generated; the felt content is not a transparent read-out of nociceptive transduction at a tissue site, and saying so is not Map-distinctive but the convergent view across modern pain science. Phantoms therefore do not differentially embarrass materialism by collapsing a peripheralist refuge — that refuge is empty. What they do is supply a clinically vivid instance of the standard [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] in a setting where the central production conditions can be specified with unusual precision, and where the failure to bridge from production conditions to felt phenomenal content is correspondingly stark.

The Map's reading goes beyond neuromatrix theory not by explaining additional phantom phenomena but by declining the framework-boundary commitment neuromatrix theory inherits from its physicalist setting. Neuromatrix theory specifies the production conditions of phantom experience and the modulators; it does not, and on its own terms does not claim to, bridge from neurosignature activity to felt burning, itching, or postural content. The bridge is taken to be promissory — to be redeemed by some future identity, constitutive, or representational account — and the assumption that such an account is available within physicalist resources is the framework boundary at which the Map parts company. The Map accepts neuromatrix theory's mechanism story essentially in full; it reads that story as the empirical anatomy of a [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] rather than as the eventual reduction of phantom experience to neurosignature firing. This is a framework-boundary disagreement, honestly noted; it is not a claim that neuromatrix theory has been refuted on its own terms. On its own terms — as a working scientific account of how the brain generates the felt body — neuromatrix theory is the best contemporary articulation of what an adequate production-conditions story looks like, and the Map's quarrel is not with that articulation.

What phantoms then support, modestly, is one part of the Map's broader cumulative case. They show that the production-conditions story has to be a story about a stable, partly innate central body model rather than a peripherally-anchored one, and the persistent-representation evidence from the past decade ([above](#cortical-reorganisation)) tightens this. That convergence with the neuromatrix prediction is welcome rather than awkward for the Map: the [[filter-theory|filter-theoretic]] and [[somatic-interface|somatic-interface]] readings expect exactly the kind of persistent central body model neuromatrix theory describes, because that is the kind of structure a filter would have to maintain to do its work. The substantive disagreement is at the framework level, not at the empirical-mechanism level, and the article's argument is more honest with the boundary marked than with the boundary disguised as in-framework refutation.

## The Predictive-Processing Account and Where the Map's Reading Differs {#predictive-processing}

If Melzack's neuromatrix was the canonical materialist account of phantoms in the late twentieth century, its working successor in the contemporary pain-neuroscience literature is the Bayesian-brain / [[predictive-processing|predictive-processing]] account, on which the brain is a hierarchical generative model of its embodied condition and perception is active inference over interoceptive, proprioceptive, and exteroceptive signals. De Ridder, Vanneste & Freeman (2014) developed the canonical predictive-coding treatment of phantom percepts as resolutions of sensory uncertainty: when the brain's hierarchical body-model receives no contradicting input from a missing limb, the high-precision prior on body configuration continues to dominate inference, and the felt limb persists as the model's best guess in the absence of evidence to revise it. Kiverstein, Kirchhoff & Thacker (2022) gave the embodied predictive-processing theory of pain experience in which pain is interoceptive inference under precision-weighted prediction error, with affective salience and action constraints emerging from the same hierarchical machinery. Lersch et al. (2023) frame analgesia itself in Bayesian-brain terms, and Sanders, Casey & Saalmann (2021) extend the predictive-coding treatment to sensory disconnection more broadly. Notably, Makin's own lab now frames its programme as a predictive-coding test — investigating whether phantom limb pain relates to sensorimotor prediction error against the persistent cortical body-model. Predictive processing is not a friendly ambient framework the article can absorb; it is the strongest contemporary materialist competitor to the Map's reading of phantoms, and the engagement has to be earned on its own terms.

The first move is to grant how much of the article's empirical picture the predictive-processing account already handles, and to be specific about each of the "philosophically loaded" features the lede flagged. *Felt position without proprioceptive input*: the high-precision prior on body configuration dominates inference whenever contradicting prediction error is absent, and amputation is exactly the condition under which such error is absent. The persistent body model documented by the Makin programme [above](#cortical-reorganisation) is, on the predictive-processing reading, that prior in its neural realisation. *Felt pain without nociceptive transduction at the felt site*: pain is interoceptive inference over expected bodily state, not a read-out of nociceptive signalling; phantom pain is the model inferring damage or impossible posture in the absence of any peripheral signal that could revise the inference downward, with the impossible-posture phenomenology emerging from the same hierarchical mismatch the model uses to drive protective action. *Mirror therapy*: visual prediction errors update the body model's posture and movement priors; when the mirror provides reliable visual evidence of an unclenched, moving phantom, the prior is revised, and the interoceptive predictions that the prior drove are revised with it, including the pain inference. *Congenital phantoms*: innate priors over body configuration are exactly the hierarchical-Bayesian commitment the architecture makes; absence of postnatal contradicting evidence in aplasia leaves the prior to dominate inference in the same way amputation does, with the developmental literature ([above](#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema)) supplying the empirical question of when and for whom the prior generates a felt limb. The predictive-processing account is structurally well-equipped to absorb each feature, and the Map's reading should not pretend otherwise.

Honest engagement then identifies where the account does and does not earn its standing on its own terms. On its own terms, the predictive-processing account specifies which inferences are running, which priors are weighted, which prediction errors drive update, and which precision weights govern integration. What it does not specify — and on its own published commitments does not claim to specify — is why hierarchical Bayesian inference over interoceptive and proprioceptive signals is accompanied by felt burning, felt itching, felt impossible-posture cramp. The account treats the felt content as an explanandum that future work will redeem; the bridge from precision-weighted prediction error to the specific phenomenal character of phantom pain is taken to be promissory in the same way the neuromatrix-to-felt-content bridge [above](#neuromatrix) is promissory, and for the same structural reason. This is the foundational move where the engagement is honest about itself rather than question-begging: the predictive-processing account does not derive felt content from inference; it correlates inference with felt content under the working assumption that a future reduction is available. The assumption is permissible — the framework is at a stage of development at which such promissory notes are reasonable — and it is also not yet redeemed, and saying so is not a dodge.

The Map's reading parts company with the account at exactly the framework-boundary the neuromatrix engagement [above](#neuromatrix) named, and for symmetrical reasons. The Map accepts the predictive-processing mechanism story essentially in full as the working empirical anatomy of the [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] — innate priors over body configuration, hierarchical inference over peripheral signals, precision-weighted integration of visual and interoceptive evidence, update of the generative body-model by mirror feedback and graded motor imagery are exactly the architecture a filter-theoretic reading expects. What the Map declines is the framework-level commitment that the bridge from inference to felt content will be redeemed within physicalist resources. This is a framework-boundary disagreement, honestly noted; it is not a claim that the predictive-processing account has been refuted on its own terms, and it is not a claim that the Map can point to a phantom phenomenon the account cannot accommodate. The account accommodates the phenomenology comprehensively at the level of inference; the explanatory gap survives the accommodation at the level of felt content, which is the standard hard problem in its predictive-processing setting.

One mild Map-side observation is worth recording, because it bears on what the contest of readings looks like rather than on who has won it. The predictive-processing treatment of phantom pain trades on the same content-vocabulary the [[predictive-processing|concept page]] flags more generally: priors are *about* body configurations; prediction errors signal *mismatch* between model and embodied state; the model *infers* tissue damage. The content-vocabulary is empirically indispensable and metaphysically derivative — the underlying physics is statistical mechanics, and the aboutness, mismatch, and inference language summarises rather than grounds the dynamics. For the phantom case specifically, the Map notes that the felt impossible-posture pain has the phenomenal character of *being-about-an-unrealised-bodily-state*, and that phenomenal-aboutness is what the inferential vocabulary is borrowing without paying for. This is not a refutation of predictive processing on its own terms — the framework's response, that the phenomenal-aboutness is itself a derived feature of the hierarchical inference, is internally available — but it sharpens what the predictive-processing account is asking phantom phenomenology to fund. The Map declines to fund the promissory note from within physicalist resources; the predictive-processing theorist may legitimately defer to a future redemption; the disagreement at this stage is exactly that.

## Common-Cause-Null Audit of the Three Pillars {#common-cause-null-audit}

The article's structural argument leans on three "philosophically loaded" features flagged in the opening summary: vivid phantom qualia without a peripheral source, top-down modulation of pain by purely visual content, and congenital phantoms in a minority of aplasics. Presented as a list, the three features can read as three independent lines of evidence converging cumulatively on the Map's reading. Convergence-argument structures of that shape inherit a standard objection — the [[common-cause-null|common-cause null]] — which asks whether the N observations are independent triangulations or one underlying observation read N ways. The Map's editorial discipline now applies that audit to its own multi-pillar arguments in the article body rather than only in editor-internal review, and the present article is one of the cases where the audit changes the honest framing.

The shared upstream the audit identifies is the *brain-constructed felt body*. All three pillars rest on it. Vivid phantom qualia presuppose central construction (there is no peripheral source to generate them). Top-down modulation of phantom pain presupposes central construction (the modulation has somewhere to act because the pain's production conditions are central). Congenital phantoms presuppose central construction with sufficient default architecture to produce a body schema in some developmental conditions. The base observation is single. The pillars are not three independent confirmations of it; they are three specific extensions that ride on it. The N-fold count overstates the convergence's force in the same shape the [[apex/phenomenal-output-causal-machinery-dissociation|dissociation cluster]] flags more generally and that [[common-cause-null|the discipline article]] specifies as the failure mode to catch.

What the audit recovers as partially independent are the *additional* claims each pillar makes beyond the shared base. The explanatory-gap pillar adds *clinical specifiability* — phantoms are a setting where the central production conditions can be specified with unusual precision, sharpening the gap by reducing the residual mechanistic uncertainty that elsewhere supplies the materialist's promissory cover. The top-down causation pillar adds *causal-pathway-shape* — the modulation runs through what the patient consciously sees and attends to rather than through the periphery of the felt site, which is a directional claim the central-construction base does not itself license. The innate-body-schema pillar adds *deeper-than-personal-history* — the schema is located deeper than sensorimotor experience with one's own specific limb, on the qualified reading the [[#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema|congenital-phantoms section]] earns against Price's developmental alternative. These additions are not nothing; they are also not three independent lines of evidence in the way a surface list might suggest. The honest count is *one* observation of brain-constructed felt body, supplemented by three differently-shaped extensions, two of which (causal-pathway-shape and deeper-than-personal-history) carry partial independent force at the qualified level the article now defends them.

The consequence for the article's argumentative shape is calibrative rather than retractive. The Map's reading of phantoms does not depend on three-fold independent triangulation; it depends on the central-construction observation plus the residual gap between specification and felt content (engaged in the [[#neuromatrix|neuromatrix]] and [[#predictive-processing|predictive-processing]] sections) plus the pathway-shape direction (engaged in the [[#mirror-therapy|mirror-therapy]] section) plus the deeper-than-personal-history claim about body-schema architecture (engaged in the [[#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema|congenital-phantoms section]]). Stated this way, the cumulative case is honest about what is one observation and what is residual additional content; it is also honest about which of the residual extensions are partially absorbable by the strongest materialist competitors (predictive processing accommodates causal-pathway-shape as visual prediction error and absorbs deeper-than-personal-history as innate-prior architecture) and which sit at the framework boundary (the residual gap between specification and felt content). The audit therefore does not collapse the Map's reading; it specifies which of the reading's components carry which evidential weight and at what register. That specification is what the [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]] requires of cumulative arguments at the convergence layer, and what the [[common-cause-null|common-cause-null discipline]] requires of multi-pillar cases in the article body.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Phantom limb phenomena bear on three of the Map's [[tenets]] in distinct ways.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: Phantoms make the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] clinically vivid by displaying felt content whose central production conditions can be specified with unusual precision. The shift from maladaptive remapping to persistent representation tightens this picture rather than dissolving it: a stable, partly innate central body model is the explanandum, and the gap is between that stable activity and the felt burning, itching, or postural content it accompanies. None of this is news to neuromatrix theory or to the [[predictive-processing|predictive-processing]] account that has largely succeeded it as the working framework in pain neuroscience ([above](#neuromatrix), [above](#predictive-processing)) — both were constructed to specify precisely this kind of production-conditions story, and the predictive-processing account in particular accommodates each of the article's "philosophically loaded" features at the level of hierarchical inference. What phantoms do is exhibit the standard hard problem in a setting where the failure to bridge from production conditions to felt content is particularly stark; they do not differentially refute materialism and they do not establish dualism. The Map's reading is a framework-boundary commitment that the bridge will not be redeemed within physicalist resources, and phantoms are consistent with that reading rather than independent evidence for it — a calibration the [[#common-cause-null-audit|common-cause-null audit]] reinforces by separating the shared central-construction base from the residual extensions each pillar adds. Congenital phantoms further shift the explanandum by showing that the bodily self-model is not exhausted by sensorimotor history with a particular body — a point neuromatrix theory was first to make, predictive processing absorbs as an innate-prior commitment, and the Map inherits.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: Mirror therapy and the broader literature on top-down modulation of phantom pain provide evidence that what a patient *sees and attends to* causally influences the intensity and sometimes the resolution of pain. The intervention is not pharmacological, not peripheral, and not behavioural in any straightforward sense; it works through the patient's conscious experience of their own body. Materialist accounts can absorb this — visual cortex modulates somatosensory and affective processing through known pathways, and the predictive-processing reading frames mirror therapy as visual prediction errors updating a generative body-model whose interoceptive predictions then revise the pain inference ([above](#predictive-processing)) — and that absorption is a serious alternative, not a placeholder. The Map's [[interactionist-dualism|interactionist reading]] is that conscious selection among candidate body-models is doing real causal work and the mirror provides an environmental scaffold that lets it do so more reliably. The article does not claim that phantom phenomena settle the contest between these readings; it claims that the pathway-shape (conscious visual content → nociceptive modulation, without intermediate peripheral intervention at the felt site) is at least the kind of pathway interactionism predicts.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: The temptation to declare phantom limbs entirely explained by any one mechanism — first cortical remapping, more recently persistent cortical representation — repeats the move the Map resists in many contexts. Each of these accounts captures something real, and the empirical balance between them has shifted over the past decade. The inference from "we know the production conditions" to "we have explained the phenomenon" elides the [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] in either case. The Map accepts the neuroscience and resists the philosophical extension that converts a sufficient-mechanism story into a complete account.

What phantoms do *not* support is any claim of consciousness existing wholly independently of the brain. The phantom is not a free-floating limb-soul. It is a brain-mediated phenomenal state whose conditions of existence are neural, even when the limb is not. The Map's dualism is not the claim that consciousness operates without the brain; it is the claim that consciousness is not reducible to the brain. Phantoms exemplify the second without supporting the first.

## What Would Challenge This View?

The Map's reading would face difficulty if:

1. **Complete pharmacological control of phantom pain**: If a peripheral or pharmacological intervention reliably abolished phantom pain across patient populations with effects matching or exceeding mirror therapy, the case for top-down causation as a load-bearing line of evidence would weaken.

2. **Failure of mirror therapy under controlled conditions**: If well-powered randomised trials returned null results for visual-feedback interventions, the demonstrative force of the top-down modulation argument would diminish, even if the dissociation between peripheral signal and felt pain remained.

3. **Predictive, fine-grained neural correlates of phantom content**: The earlier formulation of this falsifier required "a fully reductive account" that bridges from neural activity to specific phenomenal content — but a bar set at *bridging* (rather than at the kind of evidence that would warrant believing a bridge is available) is structurally unfalsifiable, because the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] as Chalmers (1995) formulates it is a claim about the in-principle limits of third-person empirical theory rather than about the contingent state of current science. A falsifier that demands a metaphysical achievement no empirical theory can in principle reach is a moving target rather than a real defeater, and the discipline of [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential calibration]] requires specifying instead what kind of *evidence* would shift the balance. The honest reformulation: if neural-correlate-of-content work matured to the point of *predictive content-specific correlations* — neural-state readouts that predict the felt quality, location, and modal character of phantom experience in fine detail across subjects and conditions, with the predictions discriminating among reports the subjects had not yet generated (rather than post hoc fits to existing reports) — the explanatory-gap argument from phantoms would lose force, because the working assumption that the content-vocabulary is borrowing from phenomenology without paying for it would be undercut by demonstrated predictive success at the level of specific phenomenal character. The bar this version sets is *demanding*, not impossible: predictive content-decoding at finer than coarse-modality resolution does not yet exist for phantoms (or for normal somatic experience), but it is the kind of finding contemporary neural-decoding programmes are in principle aiming at, and partial results in vision and motor-imagery decoding suggest the empirical terrain is not closed. The falsifier should bite if such results obtain; it should not require the impossible.

4. **Vindication of the developmental account over the structural-default reading**: Price (2006) has already published the empirical critique the structural-default reading must answer: only a minority of people with limb aplasia report phantoms, prosthesis-fitting before age seven predicts phantom occurrence, and a developmental / mirror-neuron account on which the schema is calibrated during a sensitive period from external visual experience of other bodies fits the cohort data at least as well as the strong innate-default reading. The Map's residual reading currently leans on the Root et al. (2022) finding that the cortical signature of congenital aplasia differs from that of acquired amputation in a way the pure visual-exposure account does not straightforwardly predict. If subsequent cohort or imaging work showed that the differential cortical signature can be entirely explained by differential precision-weighting on body-model priors during development (Price's account in modern predictive-processing dress), or that the prosthesis-fitting correlation eliminates the unaccounted-for residue the structural-default reading currently rests on, the structural-default reading would weaken substantially. The Map's reading would survive as a claim about the body model being located deeper than peripheral history with one's own specific limb, but the stronger architectural claim would have to be retracted.

None of these would be definitive on their own, but each would shift the evidential balance the Map currently treats as supportive of its reading.
