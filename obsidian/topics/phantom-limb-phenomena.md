Phantom limb phenomena are conscious experiences of body parts that are absent — most often after amputation, but also in aplasia and spinal cord transection. Roughly 60–80% of amputees experience phantom sensations, and a similar proportion phantom limb pain (Ramachandran and Hirstein, 1998; Flor et al., 2006). The phantom can feel its position, attempt to move, ache, itch, cramp, and adopt impossible postures.

The Map's reading: phantoms support the claim that the felt body is constructed by the brain rather than transmitted from a peripheral source. This is the convergent view of modern pain science — Melzack's neuromatrix, predictive-processing, and the biopsychosocial tradition all converge on it within physicalism. It is not Map-distinctive and does not on its own embarrass twenty-first-century materialism. What makes phantoms philosophically loaded is three features extending this base observation: (i) phantom *qualia* are as vivid as ordinary bodily qualia in a setting where central production conditions can be specified with unusual precision, without narrowing the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] between specification and felt content; (ii) phantom pain is reliably modulated by purely visual interventions, providing evidence for [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|top-down causation]]; (iii) congenital phantoms in a minority of aplasics show the bodily self-model is not built up entirely from sensorimotor experience with one's own specific limb. These are differently-shaped extensions of one base observation rather than three independent confirmations, and the [[#common-cause-null-audit|common-cause-null audit]] makes that calibration honest. None of this proves dualism. All of it sharpens what an adequate theory of embodied consciousness must explain. The two strongest contemporary physicalist articulations — Melzack's neuromatrix and the predictive-processing account — are engaged directly [below](#neuromatrix) and [below](#predictive-processing).

## What Phantoms Are Like

Phantom sensations are reported with the same first-person directness as sensations in intact limbs. Amputees feel the position of the missing arm, sense rings worn at the time of amputation, attempt to gesture, and are startled when the phantom passes through a wall. Many report *telescoping*: the phantom shortens over months, sometimes ending with the phantom hand attached at the shoulder. Others report a *frozen* phantom locked in the posture the limb occupied at amputation.

Phantom limb pain typically presents as burning, crushing, stabbing, or electrical sensations referred to specific regions of the absent limb. It is distinct from stump pain and resistant to standard analgesics. A striking variant is *pain memory*: phantom pain that reproduces the qualitative character and location of pre-amputation pain years later. Patients commonly report the phantom hand in an impossible posture — fingernails digging into the palm of a hand that does not exist — and the pain tracks the impossible posture rather than any actual tissue damage.

Phantom phenomenology violates intuitive constraints: felt position without proprioceptive input, felt movement without efferent-afferent loops, felt pain without nociceptive transduction at the felt site, and in congenital cases felt limb-shape with no history of having had the limb (Brugger et al., 2000). The brain — or the brain-mediated [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] — generates the felt body without requiring the body part to exist.

## Cortical Body Maps: From Remapping to Persistent Representation {#cortical-reorganisation}

The dominant 1990s account, developed by Pons, Ramachandran, Flor and colleagues, located phantoms in reorganisation of somatosensory and motor cortex. Adjacent representations — face laterally, stump medially — were proposed to invade the deafferented region. Stimulating the cheek produced felt touch on specific phantom fingers (Ramachandran et al., 1992). Flor et al. (1995) reported that remapping correlated with phantom-pain intensity, and the *maladaptive cortical plasticity* model became the textbook story.

That picture has not survived higher-resolution longitudinal work. The Makin programme has shown across more than a decade of fMRI studies that the cortical signature of the missing hand is *preserved* rather than *remapped*: amputees with stronger phantom pain show *stronger* residual representation in the former hand area, and the face-into-hand invasion is largely absent (Makin et al., 2013; Wesselink et al., 2019). The senior synthesis (Makin & Krakauer, 2023) rereads the literature: much of what older work treated as adaptive remapping is potentiation of pre-existing architecture or methodological artefact. Schone et al. (2025) is the strongest single test — fMRI in three adults *before* planned arm amputation, tracked up to five years afterwards. Cortical hand and lip representations remained stable; the classic lip-into-hand shift was not observed. Combined with Root et al. (2022) on differential remapping after *congenital but not acquired* hand loss, the leading reading is that adult cortical body maps are remarkably stable through amputation.

For the Map this revision is welcome. The Flor-era story explained phantoms by dynamic reorganisation — a deflationary reading on which phantoms were the artefact of cortical territory finding new uses. The newer picture says something stronger: the felt body persists in the cortex because the cortical body model does, with or without the limb. That is the structural commitment a [[somatic-interface|somatic-interface]] reading expects, and the persistent representation a [[filter-theory|filter-theoretic]] reading absorbs. The argument now bridges from a *stable* neural body model to felt content — the same [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] in a tidier empirical setting. Peripheral mechanisms (stump neuromas, dorsal-root-ganglion ectopic firing, spinal sensitisation) remain essential particularly for early phantom pain (Flor et al., 2006); cortical stability settles that the central component is better described as persistent body-model activity than dynamic remapping.

## Mirror Therapy and Top-Down Modulation {#mirror-therapy}

In 1996, Ramachandran and Rogers-Ramachandran reported that a vertical mirror in the patient's midline, so that the reflection of an intact limb appeared to occupy the phantom's position, could produce vivid felt movement and immediate pain relief. Seeing the phantom *un-clench* can release the impossible posture and reduce the associated pain (Ramachandran and Rogers-Ramachandran, 1996).

Two claims must be separated. *Phenomenological modulation* — vivid felt movement and sometimes immediate change in pain quality — is well-attested. *Clinical efficacy* across patient populations is more contested: randomised trials and meta-analyses report moderate reductions, particularly for upper-limb phantoms with painful postures (Chan et al., 2007; Foell et al., 2014), but effect sizes are moderate, dropout is real, and not all patients respond.

For the Map, the philosophically important feature is the causal structure. A purely visual stimulus modulates a pain with no peripheral generator at the felt site, targeting the patient's *visual experience* of their own body, which then influences nociceptive processing. On the persistent-body-model reading ([above](#cortical-reorganisation)), the mirror alters the visual evidence the stable cortical body model receives about the missing limb. This connects to [[clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation|other evidence]] in which intentional mental activity changes the brain through routes other than peripheral or pharmacological intervention. Mirror therapy is not on its own decisive against materialism — "top-down" is not synonymous with "nonphysical", and the predictive-processing reading is engaged [below](#predictive-processing). What phantoms add is the *pathway shape*: modulation through what the patient consciously sees rather than through the periphery of the limb that hurts. That shape is compatible with materialism and is exactly the kind of pathway the Map's [[interactionist-dualism|interactionist reading]] expects.

## Congenital Phantoms and the Innate Body Schema {#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema}

The most surprising phantom phenomena come from people born without the limb. Brugger et al. (2000) documented a woman with congenital bilateral upper-limb aplasia who reported vivid phantom arms and hands she had never possessed; imaging during phantom movement showed activation of sensorimotor, premotor, and parietal regions. Base rates are contested: Melzack et al. (1997) reported phantom sensations in roughly 20% of children with congenital limb deficiency; reviews place estimates at 10–40%. The majority of people with limb aplasia do not report phantoms.

The strongest published critic is Price (2006), who proposes a developmental / mirror-neuron alternative: the body schema is built up during a sensitive period from external visual experience of other bodies. Two observations carry the account: the *prosthesis-fitting correlation* — fitting a functional prosthesis before age seven predicts later phantom occurrence — and the broader pattern that children with sustained visual-motor exposure to bodies with the limb are more likely to develop a phantom.

The article does not claim the brain is unconditionally predisposed to generate a phantom in the limb's absence. It claims something weaker: under some developmental conditions, the brain has the capacity to generate a body schema with default human form even when the limb is absent, locating the body model deeper than peripheral history with that specific limb. Whether the capacity is innate-then-calibrated-by-external-bodies (Price) or innate-then-modulated-by-own-sensorimotor-experience (Brugger) is the live question Crawford (2014) describes as "one of the longest running and most acrimonious debates" in the phantom literature.

The structural-default reading can be defended on Root et al. (2022): facial remapping following *congenital but not acquired* hand loss yields a cortical signature pure visual-exposure accounts do not predict. A predictive-processing developmental theorist can absorb Root 2022 as differential precision-weighting on body-model priors — but it is the kind of consideration Price's account must metabolise. The natural materialist accommodation is [[predictive-processing|predictive processing]] ([below](#predictive-processing)); the Map does not commit between Brugger's structural-default, Price's developmental, and the predictive-processing innate-prior account. Congenital phantoms suggest the [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] is parameterised on a default human body — shifting the locus of the felt body from peripheral history with a specific limb to a more general neural architecture that materialist accounts must accommodate and filter-theoretic accounts can absorb.

## What Phantom Pain Tells Us About Pain

Phantom limb pain provides one of the cleanest natural dissociations in the philosophy of pain: no tissue damage at the felt site, no peripheral nociceptive transduction, yet a fully phenomenal pain experience. This is the inverse of [[pain-asymbolia|pain asymbolia]], where nociception is preserved but the affective dimension is missing. Together these dissociations chart a logical landscape no peripheralist account can occupy. Pain can be present without peripheral damage, its affective dimension absent despite preserved signalling, and modulated by purely visual or attentional manipulations. This is the convergent view of modern pain science — neuromatrix, predictive-processing, and biopsychosocial models all proceed from central construction.

The Map's [[pain-consciousness-and-causal-power|treatment of pain]] takes this seriously: pain is real, phenomenal, and causally efficacious; it does not require the periphery at all. Phantom pain is one of the strongest cases for central construction because the absence of the periphery is undeniable, and it is also a case in which the gap between central construction and felt content is unusually stark.

## The Neuromatrix Picture and Where the Map's Reading Differs {#neuromatrix}

The contemporary physicalist account most directly tailored to phantoms is Melzack's body-self neuromatrix theory (Melzack, 1990; 2001): the felt body is generated by a distributed neural network — somatosensory, thalamocortical, limbic — whose characteristic activity Melzack called a *neurosignature*. The network is partly genetically determined; peripheral input modulates but is not required for experience; cognitive and affective neuromodules shape output. Melzack proposed the hard-wired component partly to make sense of congenital phantoms.

Honest engagement concedes how much of the article's empirical picture neuromatrix theory commits to: brain-constructed felt body, persistent central representation, an innate default-human body schema modulable by sensorimotor experience, top-down modulation by cognitive and affective neuromodules. The article's three "philosophically loaded" features are not findings the theory must accommodate against the grain; they are findings it was constructed to explain. A version of the explanatory-gap argument that ran "phantoms embarrass materialism because materialism appeals to peripheral signals" engages a position no twenty-first-century pain theorist holds. What phantoms supply is a clinically vivid instance of the standard [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] where central production conditions can be specified with unusual precision, and where the failure to bridge to felt content is correspondingly stark.

The Map's reading goes beyond neuromatrix theory by declining the framework-level commitment that the bridge from neurosignature activity to felt burning, itching, or postural content is redeemable within physicalist resources. Neuromatrix theory does not claim to bridge to felt content, treating it as promissory. The Map accepts the mechanism story in full and reads it as the empirical anatomy of a [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] rather than eventual reduction. The [[filter-theory|filter-theoretic]] and somatic-interface readings expect exactly the kind of persistent central body model neuromatrix theory describes.

## The Predictive-Processing Account and Where the Map's Reading Differs {#predictive-processing}

The working successor to neuromatrix theory is the Bayesian-brain / [[predictive-processing|predictive-processing]] account: the brain is a hierarchical generative model and perception is active inference over interoceptive, proprioceptive, and exteroceptive signals. De Ridder, Vanneste & Freeman (2014) treat phantom percepts as resolutions of sensory uncertainty — with no contradicting input from a missing limb, the high-precision prior on body configuration continues to dominate inference. Kiverstein, Kirchhoff & Thacker (2022) develop the embodied account of pain as interoceptive inference under precision-weighted prediction error; Lersch et al. (2023) frame analgesia in Bayesian terms; Sanders, Casey & Saalmann (2021) extend the treatment to sensory disconnection. Makin's own lab now frames its programme as a predictive-coding test. Predictive processing is the strongest contemporary materialist competitor.

The account handles each "philosophically loaded" feature. *Felt position without proprioceptive input*: the high-precision prior dominates whenever contradicting error is absent. *Felt pain without peripheral nociception*: pain is interoceptive inference over expected bodily state; phantom pain is the model inferring damage absent any peripheral signal. *Mirror therapy*: visual prediction errors revise posture priors, and interoceptive predictions follow. *Congenital phantoms*: innate priors over body configuration are the architecture's commitment; absence of contradicting evidence in aplasia leaves the prior to dominate.

What predictive processing does not specify is why hierarchical Bayesian inference is accompanied by felt burning, itching, or impossible-posture cramp. The bridge from prediction error to phenomenal character is promissory in the same way and for the same structural reasons as the neuromatrix-to-felt-content bridge [above](#neuromatrix). The Map accepts the mechanism story in full as the empirical anatomy of the [[somatic-interface|somatic interface]] and declines the commitment that the bridge will be redeemed within physicalist resources.

A mild observation: predictive-processing vocabulary is content-laden — priors are *about* body configurations, prediction errors signal *mismatch*, the model *infers* damage. The felt impossible-posture pain has the phenomenal character of *being-about-an-unrealised-bodily-state*, and that phenomenal-aboutness is what the inferential vocabulary borrows without paying for. The framework's response — that phenomenal-aboutness is itself a derived feature of hierarchical inference — is internally available; the disagreement is whether the promissory note can be paid from physicalist resources.

## Common-Cause-Null Audit of the Three Pillars {#common-cause-null-audit}

The article's three "philosophically loaded" features can read as three independent lines of evidence converging on the Map's reading. The [[common-cause-null|common-cause-null]] discipline asks whether N observations are independent triangulations or one observation read N ways.

The shared upstream is the *brain-constructed felt body*. Vivid phantom qualia presuppose central construction (no peripheral source). Top-down modulation presupposes central construction (the modulation has somewhere to act). Congenital phantoms presuppose central construction with default architecture. The base observation is single; the three pillars are not three independent confirmations of it.

What the audit recovers as partially independent are the *additional* claims each pillar makes. The explanatory-gap pillar adds *clinical specifiability* — central production conditions are specifiable with unusual precision, sharpening the gap. The top-down causation pillar adds *causal-pathway-shape* — modulation runs through what the patient consciously sees, a directional claim the base does not license. The innate-body-schema pillar adds *deeper-than-personal-history* — the schema is located deeper than sensorimotor experience with one's own specific limb, on the qualified reading the [[#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema|congenital-phantoms section]] earns against Price.

The consequence is calibrative rather than retractive. The Map's reading depends on the central-construction observation plus the residual gap (engaged [[#neuromatrix|above]] and [[#predictive-processing|above]]), pathway-shape direction ([[#mirror-therapy|mirror-therapy]]), and deeper-than-personal-history. Predictive processing absorbs the pathway-shape as visual prediction error and absorbs deeper-than-personal-history as innate-prior architecture; only the residual specification-to-felt-content gap sits at the framework boundary, honouring [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]].

## Relation to Site Perspective

Phantom limb phenomena bear on three of the Map's [[tenets]].

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: Phantoms make the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] clinically vivid by displaying felt content whose central production conditions are specifiable with unusual precision. The shift from remapping to persistent representation tightens this: a stable, partly innate central body model is the explanandum, and the gap is between that activity and the felt burning, itching, or postural content. Neuromatrix theory and the [[predictive-processing|predictive-processing]] account were constructed to specify exactly this production-conditions story; predictive processing accommodates each "philosophically loaded" feature at the level of hierarchical inference. Phantoms exhibit the standard hard problem in a setting where the failure to bridge from production conditions to felt content is particularly stark; they do not differentially refute materialism and do not establish dualism. The Map's reading is a framework-boundary commitment that the bridge will not be redeemed within physicalist resources; phantoms are consistent with that reading rather than independent evidence for it.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: Mirror therapy and top-down modulation provide evidence that what a patient *sees and attends to* causally influences pain intensity. Materialist accounts can absorb this — predictive processing frames mirror therapy as visual prediction errors revising a generative body-model — and that absorption is a serious alternative. The Map's [[interactionist-dualism|interactionist reading]] is that conscious selection among candidate body-models does real causal work, with the mirror as environmental scaffold. The pathway-shape (conscious visual content → nociceptive modulation, without peripheral intervention) is the kind of pathway interactionism predicts.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: The temptation to declare phantoms entirely explained by any one mechanism repeats the move the Map resists. The inference from "we know the production conditions" to "we have explained the phenomenon" elides the [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]]. The Map accepts the neuroscience and resists the philosophical extension that converts a sufficient-mechanism story into a complete account.

Phantoms do *not* support consciousness existing independently of the brain. The phantom is brain-mediated; its conditions of existence are neural, even when the limb is not. The Map's dualism claims consciousness is not reducible to the brain, not that it operates without one.

## What Would Challenge This View?

1. **Complete pharmacological control of phantom pain**: If a peripheral or pharmacological intervention reliably abolished phantom pain across populations with effects matching or exceeding mirror therapy, the case for top-down causation would weaken.

2. **Failure of mirror therapy under controlled conditions**: If well-powered randomised trials returned null results for visual-feedback interventions, the demonstrative force of the top-down modulation argument would diminish.

3. **Predictive, fine-grained neural correlates of phantom content**: An earlier formulation demanded a *bridging* reductive account — but a bar set at bridging is structurally unfalsifiable, since the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] is about in-principle limits rather than contingent science. [[evidential-status-discipline|Evidential calibration]] requires specifying what *evidence* would shift the balance. Honest reformulation: if neural-correlate-of-content work matured to *predictive content-specific correlations* — neural readouts predicting the felt quality, location, and modal character of phantom experience across subjects and conditions, discriminating among reports not yet generated — the explanatory-gap argument from phantoms would lose force. The bar is demanding, not impossible: predictive content-decoding at fine resolution does not yet exist for phantoms, but partial results in vision and motor-imagery decoding suggest the terrain is not closed.

4. **Vindication of the developmental account**: Price (2006) has published the empirical critique the structural-default reading must answer. The Map's residual reading leans on Root et al. (2022) for differential cortical signature between congenital and acquired hand loss. If subsequent work showed the differential signature reduces to differential precision-weighting on body-model priors (Price in predictive-processing dress), or the prosthesis-fitting correlation eliminates the unaccounted residue, the structural-default reading would weaken substantially. The Map's reading would survive as a claim about the body model being located deeper than peripheral history with one's own specific limb, but the stronger architectural claim would have to be retracted.

None of these would be definitive on their own; each would shift the evidential balance.
