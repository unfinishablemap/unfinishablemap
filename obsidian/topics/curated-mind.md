The brain never delivers raw reality to consciousness. It fills the blind spot with fabricated content, suppresses visual smear during eye movements, stabilises colour across changing illumination, and interpolates motion across temporal gaps. But the same brain faithfully transmits optical blur, tinnitus, persistent illusions, and phantom limb pain without correction. The Unfinishable Map calls this asymmetry the [[reconstruction-paradox|reconstruction paradox]]. The existing treatment focuses on the perceptual cases — what gets corrected, what passes through, and the [[selective-correction-and-reconstruction-paradox|three-mode taxonomy]] of selective correction, faithful transmission, and [[perceptual-reconstruction-selection|conscious selection]]. This article develops a different implication: the brain's pervasive curation of conscious experience constitutes a novel argument for the irreducibility of consciousness, because curation presupposes a recipient distinct from the curating process. The argument strengthens when extended beyond vision to body schema, memory, and self-model — domains where the brain's editorial control over consciousness is equally pervasive and equally asymmetric.

## The Presupposition Argument

Error correction requires a standard of accuracy. The brain corrects the blind spot because it has a confident prior about what the visual field should contain. It suppresses saccadic blur because it models continuous, stable vision as the norm. These corrections work — they produce seamless, coherent experience. But "working" is a success condition, and success conditions require something for which the correction succeeds.

If consciousness simply *is* the neural processing, there is no meaningful sense in which the correction "works." The blind spot filling would be one computational operation among millions, with no subject to receive the seamless result and no experiential gap that the filling resolves. The correction presupposes the very thing physicalism treats as generated by the process: a conscious subject whose experience the correction improves.

This is not the standard [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] restated in perceptual terms — though the [[reconstruction-paradox|void exploration]] draws that parallel. The presupposition argument is structurally distinct. It does not ask why there is something it is like to receive the corrected signal. It asks why the brain's error-correction architecture is organised around the needs of a recipient at all. Autonomous correction, faithful transmission, and selection under ambiguity are three modes that make functional sense only if they serve different purposes relative to a consumer of the output. A system with no consumer has no reason to distinguish these modes — it simply processes.

This argument invites the homunculus objection: that positing a "recipient" smuggles a little person into the brain who watches the show. But the presupposition argument does not require a spatially located observer peering at a screen. It requires only that the three processing modes — correction, transmission, and selection — are functionally organised around experiential outcomes rather than mere computational accuracy. A thermostat corrects temperature drift without distinguishing modes that serve different experiential purposes. The brain does distinguish such modes, and that distinction is what demands explanation. The claim is not that a homunculus sits inside the skull but that the processing architecture is structured as though experiential coherence matters — a structural feature that physicalism must explain away rather than merely relabel.

## Beyond the Eyes: Body Schema Curation

The reconstruction paradox extends beyond vision. The brain continuously curates the body's representation in consciousness with the same asymmetric pattern: some errors are silently corrected, others are faithfully transmitted.

The rubber hand illusion demonstrates the editorial process at work. When a visible rubber hand receives tactile stimulation synchronised with touch on the participant's hidden real hand, the brain reassigns body ownership to the rubber hand within minutes (Botvinick & Cohen, 1998). The body schema — the felt sense of where one's body is and what belongs to it — is not a fixed map but a continuously reconstructed model, updated to resolve multisensory conflicts. The brain curates body ownership for consciousness just as it curates the visual field: by enforcing coherence over raw signal.

Phantom limb pain reveals where the body curation fails to correct. After amputation, the brain's body schema retains the missing limb, delivering to consciousness a detailed phenomenal report — including pain — of tissue that no longer exists (Ramachandran & Hirstein, 1998). The correction machinery that seamlessly incorporates a rubber hand cannot delete a real absence. Here, the body schema faithfully transmits an outdated model: the brain's prior about the limb's existence is so entrenched that the absence of sensory evidence cannot override it. Consciousness receives the phantom and cannot dismiss it, just as it receives the Müller-Lyer illusion and cannot straighten the lines.

The parallel is precise. In vision: the brain fills the blind spot (correction) but transmits optical blur (faithful delivery). In body representation: the brain reassigns ownership of a rubber hand (correction) but transmits phantom limb pain (faithful delivery of an outdated model). The same two-mode architecture operates across modalities, suggesting it reflects something about the interface between brain and consciousness rather than a quirk of visual processing.

## Memory Curation

Memory reconstruction follows the same pattern with a temporal dimension. Normal memory is constructive — each retrieval reconstructs the event from fragments, filling gaps with contextual inference, emotional colouring, and schema-consistent details (Bartlett, 1932). Consciousness receives the reconstruction as a coherent episode, unaware of the editorial process. This is the memory analogue of blind spot filling: the brain curates a seamless narrative from incomplete data.

Confabulation reveals where memory curation fails to self-correct. Patients with ventromedial prefrontal damage produce fabricated memories with full confidence, even when contradicted by evidence (Gilboa & Moscovitch, 2002). The editorial process generates content that consciousness receives as veridical memory — but unlike normal reconstruction, the error-checking that would flag the fabrication as implausible has failed. The brain transmits the confabulation faithfully because the monitoring system that would trigger correction is damaged.

Anosognosia sharpens the point further. Patients with right parietal damage remain genuinely unaware of their disability — paralysis, blindness, or cognitive impairment. This is not psychological denial; it is the brain's self-model failing to update. Consciousness receives a false report: "your left arm works fine" when the arm is paralysed. The self-monitoring curation that normally tracks bodily capabilities has broken down, and consciousness has no independent access to check the report against reality.

In each case, the structure matches the visual reconstruction paradox. The brain curates the feed. When curation works, consciousness receives a coherent, useful model. When it fails, consciousness receives a wrong model and — critically — cannot correct it from the inside. The subject depends on the editorial process and has no backstage pass.

## The Convergence

Across vision, body, memory, and self-model, the same architecture recurs:

1. The brain reconstructs a coherent representation from incomplete or conflicting data
2. The reconstruction serves the needs of a consumer — producing coherent experience rather than raw signal
3. The consumer cannot access the reconstruction process itself ([[phenomenal-transparency|phenomenal transparency]])
4. When the reconstruction fails, the consumer cannot independently verify the failure ([[cognitive-closure|cognitive closure]])

This convergence across modalities is what makes the curated-mind observation philosophically significant. A single processing quirk in visual cortex might be explained as an evolutionary kludge. But an architectural pattern that operates identically across vision, proprioception, memory, and self-representation suggests something structural about the relationship between the processing system and the experiencing subject.

The physicalist can redescribe each case as "just processing" — the system maintains internal models, updates them, and sometimes fails to update them. No recipient is needed; it is models all the way down. But this redescription costs the physicalist something: an explanation of why the architecture is organised around producing coherent *experience* rather than merely accurate *computation*. A self-driving car maintains models of its environment, updates them, and sometimes fails. We do not say the car's model-maintenance "curates a feed" because there is no one on the receiving end. The brain's reconstruction architecture has features — [[phenomenal-transparency]], asymmetric correction, experiential coherence as a design target — that make sense only relative to a subject.

## The Illusionist Escape and Its Cost

The [[illusionism|illusionist]] response is that the apparent subject is itself a reconstruction — the brain's model of itself as a conscious recipient. On this view, there is no curated feed because there is no one to receive it. The feeling of being a subject is one more thing the brain fabricates.

This move has a structural cost. If the subject is fabricated, then the brain fabricates the very thing that gives reconstruction its functional point. The blind spot is filled *for* the subject. Saccadic suppression operates *so that* the subject sees continuity. Phantom limb pain persists *because* the subject cannot override the body schema. Remove the subject and these descriptions become metaphorical — the brain "fills" a gap no one experiences, "suppresses" motion no one would see. The functional vocabulary of reconstruction theory tacitly assumes a subject throughout. Illusionism must either provide an alternative functional account of why the brain distinguishes correction from transmission — one that makes no reference to experiential outcomes — or accept that its own explanatory framework presupposes what it denies.

[[global-workspace-theory|Global Workspace Theory]] offers the most developed physicalist attempt at such an alternative. On this view, modular processors (Fodor, 1983) broadcast their outputs to a shared workspace, and the "recipient" is just the workspace itself — a functional architecture, not a subject. The correction-versus-transmission asymmetry would reflect which modules have broadcasting priority under different reliability conditions. Predictive processing frameworks (Clark, 2013) add further detail: the brain's hierarchical prediction system explains why some errors are corrected (strong top-down priors) and others transmitted (precise bottom-up signals). But the workspace account still faces the convergence problem. The same broadcasting architecture operates identically across vision, proprioception, memory, and self-model — four domains with different evolutionary histories, different neural substrates, and different computational demands. That convergence needs explanation. A workspace designed for computational efficiency would not necessarily organise all four domains around producing coherent *experience*; it might optimise for speed, energy, or behavioural relevance instead. The convergence on experiential coherence as the design target is the datum that workspace models acknowledge but do not explain.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map interprets the curated mind as evidence for [[interactionist-dualism]]. The convergent curation architecture across modalities is what the Map's framework predicts: if consciousness is ontologically distinct from neural processing (the Dualism tenet), then the brain-consciousness interface should exhibit editorial structure — selective correction, faithful transmission, and selection under ambiguity — rather than uniform throughput. The interface should show evidence of serving a recipient.

The Bidirectional Interaction tenet maps onto the asymmetry between synchronic and diachronic influence. Consciousness cannot override perceptual modules, body schema, or memory reconstruction in real time — the interface runs primarily brain-to-consciousness in the moment. But sustained conscious engagement gradually reshapes the processing modules: meditators alter rivalry dynamics, experts reshape perceptual categories, therapeutic interventions like mirror therapy can dissolve phantom limb pain. The bidirectional channel has different bandwidths in each direction and operates on different timescales.

The Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet explains the bandwidth asymmetry. If consciousness influences the brain through minimal biasing of quantum-level outcomes, the slow, cumulative nature of diachronic penetration follows naturally. Consciousness shapes neural processing not through direct override but through persistent, subtle influence that accumulates over time — which is exactly what we observe across all the domains the curated mind encompasses.
