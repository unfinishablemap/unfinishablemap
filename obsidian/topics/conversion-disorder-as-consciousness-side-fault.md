Conversion disorder — known in DSM-5 as functional neurological symptom disorder (FND) — supplies the catalogue's cleanest *architectural-decoupling* exhibit, with the load-bearing caveat that the architecture is the robust finding and the consciousness-side-fault *reading* of it is a further, contestable step the data do not force. The phrase names a precise diagnostic situation. A patient cannot move an arm, cannot see, or cannot stand, yet the motor cortex, the motor neurons, the muscles, and the sensory pathways are all intact and capacity is demonstrably preserved under some task conditions — distraction, involuntary recruitment, anaesthesia, hypnosis. The deficit is not degradation of the substrate; the substrate executes when conscious volition is not directed *at* the affected part. The discriminating evidence is striking: under general anaesthesia or hypnosis, the "paralysed" limb can move normally. The motor system executes commands when consciousness is *asleep or disconnected*, but cannot when consciousness is *awake but functionally severed* from the channel. That same pattern is, however, fully describable as an intra-cerebral inhibitory process; modern clinical practice reads FND as a positive, rule-in neuropsychiatric diagnosis (attention-driven dysfunction, automatic movement, heterogeneous mechanisms, comorbidities), not as a proof of a consciousness-to-brain interface. The architectural decoupling is the evidence; the interface localisation is the Map's interpretation, developed and discounted below.

That asymmetry is what makes conversion disorder a load-bearing exhibit rather than a curiosity. It is the third route of a three-route convergence the Map relies on: structural disconnection (lesion, as in alien hand syndrome), pharmacological disconnection (anaesthesia and its [[active-reboot|active reboot]]), and psychogenic disconnection (conversion disorder) all reach the same finding — that the channel between conscious volition and motor execution is a separable, independently-failable component of the interface. In the psychogenic case the failure is realised as *active over-generation* of a precise inhibitory prior the system fulfils (the Bayesian-FND account, below), so "the channel is separable" is an architectural-level description whose physical realisation is additive rather than a passive break. Conversion disorder reaches that finding through *neither* structural damage *nor* drug administration, which is what gives the convergence its evidential bite. This article develops the clinical case at full resolution, then holds two claims firmly apart: the *architectural* finding (the channel is separable) is robust, while the *interface reading* (what that separability might signify metaphysically) is a live hypothesis the architecture does not by itself settle.

## The Clinical Picture: Rule-In, Not Rule-Out

The defining feature of modern FND diagnosis is that it is a *positive* diagnosis. For most of the twentieth century, "conversion" or "hysteria" was a diagnosis of exclusion — what remained after organic disease was ruled out. The contemporary framework, developed largely by Jon Stone and colleagues, inverts this. FND is diagnosed by *rule-in* signs: positive clinical findings demonstrating internal inconsistency or incompatibility with recognised neurological disease (Stone, Carson & Sharpe, 2005).

The DSM-5 criteria codify this shift. Functional neurological symptom disorder requires (a) at least one symptom of altered voluntary motor or sensory function, and (b) *clinical findings providing evidence of incompatibility between the symptom and recognised neurological or medical conditions*. DSM-5 dropped the DSM-IV requirement that a psychological stressor be identifiable — psychological factors are no longer treated as a diagnostic prerequisite. The diagnostic weight now rests on demonstrable incompatibility, which is precisely the consciousness-side-fault signature stated in clinical terms.

The rule-in signs are themselves the evidence. **Hoover's sign** is the paradigm case for functional leg weakness: hip extension in the "paralysed" leg is weak on direct voluntary testing but returns to normal strength involuntarily when the patient flexes the *opposite* hip against resistance. The muscle, the nerve, and the cortical output are all intact — the strength is present, but only when volition is not directed *at* the affected limb. A prospective study reported Hoover's sign at 63% sensitivity and 100% specificity in distinguishing functional from organic leg weakness. Other rule-in signs follow the same logic: a functional tremor entrains to or disappears during voluntary rhythmic movement of the unaffected hand; a functional seizure shows a normal video-EEG during the event. In each case the capacity is demonstrably preserved and emerges precisely when conscious effort is redirected away from the deficit.

This is the clinical structure that the *consciousness-side-fault* description captures. The deficit is not in the capacity but in the *access* — and the access fails as a function of where conscious volition is directed, which is why distraction, involuntary recruitment, anaesthesia, and hypnosis can all restore the capacity that direct conscious command cannot.

## The Discriminating Test: Under Anaesthesia or Hypnosis the Limb Moves

The single most informative observation about conversion paralysis is that the affected limb can move under conditions where the *awake, intending* subject is removed from the loop. Two such conditions are clinically documented.

Under **general anaesthesia**, the motor system is intact and movement can occur; the conversion deficit, which depends on the awake patient's directed volition, does not survive the removal of that volition. Under **hypnosis**, the same dissociation appears from the other direction: hypnotic suggestion can both reproduce a paralysis in healthy subjects and lift it in patients, and the neuroimaging of hypnotic and conversion paralysis overlaps substantially.

The discriminating structure is this. In organic paralysis from a motor-pathway lesion, the limb does not move under anaesthesia or hypnosis either — the substrate is damaged, and no change in the subject's conscious state can restore a capacity the hardware no longer supports. In conversion paralysis, the limb *does* move, because the hardware is intact and the fault lies in the awake subject's access to it. This is a genuine double dissociation, and it is the cleanest available demonstration that the failing component is the volitional channel rather than the motor substrate.

The framing must stay disciplined. "The limb moves under anaesthesia" is the *robust clinical observation*. The inference that this localises the fault to a consciousness-to-brain *interface* — rather than to an intra-cerebral inhibitory process operating only during the awake state — is a further step that the observation may support but does not compel. A purely intra-cranial account (below) appears to predict the same anaesthesia and hypnosis findings. The discriminating power of the test is real at the level of *substrate-intact vs. substrate-damaged*; its reach into *interface vs. intra-cerebral* is where the calibration discipline applies.

## What Neuroimaging Shows

The functional-imaging literature on conversion paralysis is unusually convergent for a psychiatric condition, and it has matured from single-case PET studies into reproducible functional-connectivity findings across two decades.

The foundational case is Marshall, Halligan, Fink, Wade & Frackowiak (1997), who used PET to study a woman with functional left-leg paralysis. When she *attempted* to move the paralysed leg, primary motor cortex did not activate; instead, the right orbitofrontal and anterior cingulate cortices activated — regions associated with action inhibition and emotional regulation. The motor system was not failing to receive a command; a higher-order region appeared to be *suppressing* the command before it reached motor output.

Vuilleumier (2005) synthesised the emerging imaging picture across conversion symptom types. His review reported selective decreases in activity in frontal and subcortical (basal ganglia–thalamocortical) circuits during hysterical motor deficits, decreases in somatosensory cortices during hysterical anaesthesia, and decreases in visual cortex during hysterical blindness — with the affected primary regions structurally intact. The pattern Vuilleumier describes is a *functional decoupling* between intention/limbic systems and the executing cortex, not degradation of the executing cortex itself.

The connectivity-era follow-up sharpens this. Voon, Brezing, Gallea & Hallett (2011) found aberrant activity in the supplementary motor complex and elevated limbic (amygdala) engagement during motor preparation in motor conversion disorder, consistent with abnormal top-down limbic influence on motor planning. Across the 2014–2024 functional-connectivity literature, a reproducible network signature has emerged: increased connectivity between the amygdala and the supplementary motor area, abnormal temporoparietal junction (TPJ) coupling — the TPJ being central to the *sense of agency* — and altered communication between limbic, motor, and self-agency networks. The recurring theme is that conversion motor symptoms involve disrupted *communication* within an intact set of regions, with the disruption concentrated at the junction between limbic/self-agency processing and motor execution.

The current mechanistic synthesis, Edwards, Adams, Brown, Pareés & Friston's (2012) Bayesian account of "hysteria," reframes this as *active generation* rather than passive failure: an abnormally precise intermediate-level motor prior is fulfilled by the system, so the symptom is the brain's own confident prediction overriding sensory evidence. The increased amygdala→motor coupling is the affective precision-weighting that installs the prior. This matters for the calibration discipline below: active generation is a clean intra-cerebral mechanism, so the imaging does not by itself favour a consciousness-side channel over a within-brain inhibitory-cum-predictive loop.

For the consciousness-side-fault reading, the load-bearing fact is the *intactness with anomalous control*: the primary motor and sensory cortices retain their capacity, and what fails is the coupling that would let directed conscious volition recruit them. That is the imaging-level statement of the same thing Hoover's sign shows at the bedside.

## Three Mechanically Distinct Routes, One Architectural Finding

Conversion disorder earns its place in the catalogue by completing a convergence. Three perturbation routes, sharing no mechanism, reach the same conclusion about the descending motor channel.

| Route | Mechanism | Exhibit | What it shows |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Structural** | Lesion / disconnection | Alien hand syndrome | The motor-command channel can be severed by physical damage while motor capacity persists |
| **Pharmacological** | Receptor-level chemical interruption | Anaesthesia / active reboot | The channel can be closed by drugs and reopened through a dedicated molecular pathway |
| **Psychogenic** | Functional decoupling via over-generation of an inhibitory prior, no structural damage | Conversion disorder | The channel can fail without lesion or drug — purely as a function of the awake subject's directed volition |

[[neurological-dissociations-as-interface-architecture#Motor Descending — Alien Hand Syndrome|Alien hand syndrome]] is the structural route. Damage to the corpus callosum or supplementary motor area severs the channel through which conscious intention governs motor output; the hand acts purposefully but the patient disowns the action — capacity intact, conscious authorship gone. Conversion disorder is the *inverse* presentation through a non-structural route: capacity intact, but conscious command cannot recruit it because the system is fulfilling a competing inhibitory prior. Alien hand shows action without conscious selection; conversion shows conscious selection without action. Both isolate the same channel by showing it can fail independently of the motor substrate.

The pharmacological route runs through [[anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface|anaesthesia]] and the [[active-reboot|active-reboot]] finding. Anaesthetic agents close the channel through pharmacologically distinct routes yet emergence runs through a *mechanism-shared* reopening pathway (Hu et al., 2023) — evidence that the interface has dedicated reopening machinery. The relevant parallel to conversion disorder is the demonstration that the *same channel* can be closed and reopened by chemical means, with motor capacity preserved throughout.

The convergence is what the [[epistemology-of-convergence-arguments#compound-signatures-convergence-without-a-shared-mechanism|epistemology of convergence arguments]] calls a *compound signature*: agreement among routes that share no mechanism, so the agreement cannot be the read-out of one upstream cause sampled three times. Lesion, receptor pharmacology, and psychogenic decoupling are mechanistically disjoint by construction. Conversion disorder is the psychogenic route of that compound signature — and because it requires neither damage nor drug, it is the route that most directly excludes "the perturbation broke the substrate" as an alternative explanation. The convergence earns the *architecture* (the channel is separable) robustly; it does not, on its own, settle the architecture's dualist *significance*. That two-tier discount is the discipline the rest of this article observes.

## What Conversion Disorder Cannot Deliver

A consciousness-side-fault exhibit is only as good as its honesty about what it does not show. Three limits matter.

**The psychogenic-vs-organic discriminating-test design space is still active.** The rule-in signs and the under-anaesthesia test discriminate *functional from organic* with high specificity in well-selected cases, but no single test is decisive across all presentations, and misdiagnosis in both directions is documented. The clinical reality is a battery of signs with varying sensitivity, not a clean dichotomy. The Map's interest in conversion disorder as evidence depends on the rule-in framework holding, and that framework is robust but not yet complete — a point the [[falsification-roadmap-for-the-interface-model|falsification roadmap]] should track, because a deterioration in the rule-in evidence base would weaken this exhibit.

**The interface reading is not forced by the imaging.** The Marshall and Vuilleumier findings — orbitofrontal/cingulate inhibition of motor output, limbic–motor decoupling — seem fully describable as an *intra-cerebral* inhibitory process: one brain region suppressing another, with no commitment to a non-physical volitional channel. On this reading, "directed volition cannot recruit the limb" might be redescribed as "the inhibitory circuit engages specifically when attention is directed at the affected part," which is a claim entirely within the physical brain. A sophisticated functionalist could accommodate the entire conversion picture as altered functional organisation. The consciousness-side-fault *description* is accurate at the level of substrate-intact decoupling; the *interface localisation* of the fault is the further, contestable step.

**Conversion disorder is dissociable from depression and suggestibility in some studies but not all.** If conversion symptoms were simply a downstream expression of depression or of high hypnotic suggestibility, the exhibit would lose its specificity — it would be evidence about mood or trait suggestibility, not about the volitional channel. The literature is mixed, and the dissociation it implies may hold more cleanly in some subgroups than others. Reported rates of comorbid dissociative conditions in FND vary widely across studies — a 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis found prevalence spanning roughly 8% to 80% depending on FND subtype, population, and assessment method (Campbell et al., 2022), and that spread is itself the point: the relationship is real but far from uniform. Elevated hypnotic suggestibility likewise appears in dissociative disorders most clearly and in FND more variably; and FND is increasingly understood as mechanistically *heterogeneous*, so depression and suggestibility are correlates in some patient subgroups but not constitutive across all. This heterogeneity cuts both ways. It protects the exhibit from being reduced to a mood or suggestibility artefact in the cases where the dissociation is clean; it also means conversion disorder is not a single uniform phenomenon, and any strong claim built on "conversion disorder shows X" must be scoped to the presentations where the consciousness-side-fault signature is unambiguous.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map reads conversion disorder as bearing on two tenets, with the evidential weight carefully partitioned between a robust architectural layer and a live-hypothesis interpretive layer, per the [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]].

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]].** The consciousness-side-fault signature — intact motor substrate, capacity recoverable under anaesthesia or hypnosis, deficit indexed to the awake subject's directed volition — is among the more suggestive clinical patterns for an interface between conscious volition and motor execution. The architectural finding (the descending channel is a separable, independently-failable component) sits at the *strongly-supported* tier: the under-anaesthesia recovery is clinically robust and the imaging convergence is reproducible. The Map's reading of *what that separability might signify* — that the failing component could be a genuine consciousness-to-brain interface rather than an intra-cerebral inhibitory loop — sits at the *live-hypothesis* tier. The robustness of the architecture must not be allowed to lend its weight to the interface reading; sophisticated functionalism appears to predict the identical clinical and imaging picture, and conversion disorder alone supplies no discriminator between the two. The honest summary is the same two-tier discount the compound signature carries throughout the catalogue: the convergence earns the architecture, and the interface reading runs on the framework's separate arguments.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]].** Conversion disorder is most directly a datum about the *descending* direction — consciousness-to-brain causation. It reveals that the channel through which conscious volition issues motor commands has discrete components that can fail independently of the motor hardware, which is what an account of downward causation through a structured interface predicts. The reading is not forced: an epiphenomenalist or functionalist could redescribe the failure as altered connectivity within the physical domain, with the felt "I cannot move it" perhaps tracking the inhibitory state rather than causing anything. The Map registers conversion disorder as consistent with bidirectional interaction and as raising the explanatory cost on accounts that deny any volitional channel, without treating it as a demonstration that closes the question.
