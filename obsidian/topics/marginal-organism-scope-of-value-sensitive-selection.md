[[valence-and-conscious-selection|Valence and the mechanism of conscious selection]] leaves one question explicitly open: if consciousness selects among physically underdetermined outcomes using **valence as the currency**, and if where consciousness acts is where teleology enters nature, then *how far down the tree of life does value-sensitive selection reach?* Does a rat's pain avoidance reflect conscious, valence-denominated selection—or merely value-blind reflex? Where is the boundary for a nematode with 302 neurons, a *Hydra* with a diffuse nerve net, a brainless slime mould that nonetheless habituates? This article develops that deferred "scope of teleology / scope of consciousness" question. Its central claim is calibrated and deflationary: **the boundary cannot currently be located, and the Map's tenets do not help locate it.** Tenet-coherence removes a defeater against marginal-organism consciousness; it supplies no positive evidence for it. Every boundary case below stays at the status of *live hypothesis* or *speculative integration*—never "realistic possibility" or higher—per [[evidential-status-discipline]].

## Why the Boundary Is a Distinct Problem

Three nearby articles each touch this terrain without occupying it. [[minimal-consciousness]] asks *where bare phenomenal experience begins*—the threshold of "what it's like" at all. [[biological-teleology-and-the-interface-framework]] argues *that* conscious selection re-introduces genuine purpose into nature, contrasting selected-effects function with forward-looking intention. [[consciousness-in-simple-organisms]] surveys candidate organisms for consciousness generally.

The present question is narrower and downstream of all three. Grant that some organisms are minimally conscious. Grant that conscious selection is teleological. The scope problem is then: **for which organisms does selection run on a *valence* currency rather than on a value-neutral mechanism?** An organism could in principle be minimally conscious (have some "what it's like") yet still select value-*blindly*—its conscious states present as neutral pointers, with felt goodness or badness doing no selective work. Scope-of-consciousness and scope-of-value-sensitive-selection can come apart. The boundary this article seeks is the inner one: not where experience begins, but where *evaluative* selection begins.

This matters because the parent article's value-sensitive horn carries the strongest version of the Map's value realism—value is "doubly non-epiphenomenal," real as experienced and efficacious as selector. If that horn is right, then the moral-status map of the biosphere is partly the map of where valence-denominated selection operates. Getting the scope wrong in either direction has ethical cost: over-attribution invents suffering that isn't there; under-attribution ignores suffering that is.

## A Graduated Ladder of Cases

The cases are best read as a descending ladder, each rung removing a structural prerequisite the value-sensitive horn appears to need (anticipatory evaluation, a central integration point, a nervous system at all).

### Rat: Plausible But Not Settled

The rat has everything the [[wanting-liking-and-the-value-in-mechanism-fork|wanting/liking]] machinery requires: dopaminergic incentive-salience circuits, opioid hedonic hotspots in the nucleus accumbens, and avoidance learning that survives the Sneddon criteria for pain rather than mere nociception (Sneddon et al. 2014)—nociceptors, central processing, opioid modulation, analgesia-sensitive behavioural change, and motivational shifts that persist beyond reflex. By the 2024 New York Declaration's precautionary standard, mammalian pain is treated as a realistic possibility for welfare purposes.

Yet even here the value-sensitive *selection* claim outruns the evidence. That a rat feels pain (a phenomenal claim) and that a rat *selects outcomes because of* anticipated valence (a selection-mechanism claim) are different. The rat could be richly conscious and still steer value-blindly, with valence confined to the sensor channel the parent article describes. The honest reading: in the rat the value-sensitive horn is a **live hypothesis**—the substrates exist, the behaviour is consistent with it—but the discriminating evidence (a selection advantage for valenced over equally-salient neutral options) has not been gathered. Plausibility is not confirmation. And the *Physarum* deflation developed below is a global solvent, not a rung-local one: once approach/avoidance is granted to be no reliable marker of value-sensitive selection *anywhere*, the behavioural half of the rat's case is demoted too. What survives is not behavioural resemblance but substrate homology with the human case plus precaution—the residual grounds on which the live-hypothesis status genuinely rests.

### Nematode: The Anticipation Gap

*C. elegans* has 302 neurons and a worked-out nociceptive circuit: the ASH polymodal sensory neurons detect noxious osmolarity, touch, and heavy-metal ions, triggering reverse locomotion, and the worm shows non-associative learning such as sensory adaptation and habituation in these pathways (e.g. ASH gain control; reciprocal ASH/ASI inhibition modulating avoidance). This is real, integrated nociception with behavioural plasticity.

But the value-sensitive horn needs more than nociception: it needs **anticipatory valence**—evaluation of predicted felt quality *before* an outcome is actualised, as the parent article's selection-law sketch requires. The nematode's avoidance is predominantly modelled as reactive—stimulus → ASH activation → reversal—though associative and thermotactic learning complicate the picture: *C. elegans* shows isothermal tracking toward a remembered cultivation temperature, klinotaxis toward learned set-points, and salt-concentration learning that biases future locomotion. So the rung does not rest on a claim that the worm never anticipates; it rests on the narrower, honest gap: even granting forward models, whether anything in 302 neurons constructs one whose *felt* value—rather than mere predictive coding—denominates a selection step is, on present evidence, undetermined and arguably unconstructible from third-person data. The nematode marks the rung where the *anticipation problem* the parent article flags becomes acute. Status: **speculative**; the burden is on showing anticipatory evaluation, not merely reactive avoidance.

### Hydra: Selection Without a Centre

*Hydra* removes the next prerequisite. Whole-nerve-net calcium imaging shows its diffuse net resolves into a small number of non-overlapping functional networks driving specific behaviours—contraction bursts, elongation, radial contraction—with no central integrator, no brain, no obvious global workspace (Dupré & Yuste 2017). If value-sensitive selection requires a point at which competing options are compared on a common currency, *Hydra* offers no evident locus for that comparison. Either its behaviour is generated without any selection-among-options step of the relevant kind, or the Map's interface operates in a radically distributed way the framework has not specified. The case does not refute value-sensitive selection at this level; it shows the framework lacks the resources to *place* it there. Status: **speculative integration**, flagged by the framework's own silence about distributed interfaces.

### Slime Mould: Valenced Behaviour, No Nervous System

The acellular slime mould *Physarum polycephalum*—a single giant multinucleate cell—habituates to aversive but harmless repellents (quinine, caffeine), crossing them to reach food after repeated exposure and recovering the aversion after a rest interval (Boisseau, Vogel & Dussutour 2016). This is habituation, a recognised form of learning, in an organism with **no neurons at all**. It is the limiting case: approach/avoidance behaviour, even simple learning, with none of the neural machinery the interface framework's mechanisms (attention-as-observation-rate, synaptic exocytosis bias) presuppose.

The slime mould forces a clean disjunction. *Either* valenced-looking behaviour can be produced by wholly value-blind, mechanistic processes—in which case approach/avoidance is **not** a reliable marker of value-sensitive selection anywhere, weakening the inference up the whole ladder—*or* the Map must extend conscious value-sensitive selection to a brainless cell, which strains the framework past any specified interface. The Map should take the first horn: *Physarum* is best read as a demonstration that valence-*like* behaviour underdetermines value-*sensitive selection*. This is the most defensible reading and it cuts against over-attribution.

Between *Hydra*'s diffuse net and *Physarum*'s single cell sits a rung this ladder skips: the **multicellular, non-neural** case. [[plant-cognition-and-the-plant-neurobiology-debate|Plants]] fill it—many-celled organisms that count (the Venus flytrap's action-potential tally), hold short-term electrical memory, and run systemic wound signalling, all without neurons, yet with even less claim to experience than the slime mould. They press the same disjunction from richer behavioural territory: more sophisticated non-neural processing, still no evidence of felt value. And [[basal-and-bioelectric-cognition|Levin's bioelectric programme]] presses it from the opposite side—flatworm voltage memory and self-replicating xenobots supply engineering-grade *agency* whose *experience* even the principal investigator declines to assert, hardening the case that goal-directed behaviour is no reliable marker of value-sensitive selection.

An orthogonal rung sits off to the side of this descent. The [[fish-sentience-and-the-teleost-pain-debate|teleost fish]] are full vertebrates—nervous system, nociceptors, flexible behaviour all present—so they do not remove a prerequisite the way the rungs above do. What they vary is neural *architecture*: fish lack the laminated neocortex that houses the mammalian pain network, and skeptics infer from that absence that fish nociception is unfelt. That inference is the cortical-necessity premise in its sharpest published form, and the ladder's cleanest test of whether a specific structure, rather than neural processing in general, is load-bearing for felt value.

## What Each Test Removes

| Organism | Has | Lacks (for value-sensitive selection) | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rat | pain by Sneddon criteria; wanting/liking circuits | discriminating evidence of a valence *selection advantage* | live hypothesis |
| *C. elegans* | integrated nociception; habituation | demonstrated *anticipatory* valence | speculative |
| *Hydra* | behaviour-specific neural networks | a central locus for option-comparison | speculative integration |
| *Physarum* | habituation; approach/avoidance | a nervous system at all | value-blind reading favoured |

The ladder's lesson is that the markers degrade independently. Nociception survives down to the nematode; a central integrator fails at *Hydra*; the nervous system fails at the slime mould; and *anticipatory* valence—the property the selection law actually needs—is never positively demonstrated at any rung below the mammal, and is contested even there.

## Why the Tenets Cannot Locate the Boundary

It is tempting to let the Map's commitments do the work: dualism makes consciousness substrate-flexible, the [[universal-coupling-response]] denies any principled threshold, so perhaps value-sensitive selection reaches very far down. **This temptation must be resisted, and resisting it is load-bearing.** The tenets remove a *defeater*—they block the argument "no plausible neural threshold, therefore no consciousness here"—but removing a defeater is not the same as supplying positive evidence. That a marginal organism's consciousness is not ruled out by the absence of a complexity threshold does nothing to show its selection is *valence-denominated* rather than value-blind. [[evidential-status-discipline]] forbids converting tenet-coherence, or process-philosophical resonance about purpose in nature, into an evidential upgrade. A boundary case that is merely *not excluded* stays at live hypothesis; it does not climb to realistic possibility on the strength of the framework finding it congenial.

This is the same discipline the parent article applies to its own value-blind horn: a fork resolved by what feels congenial rather than by argument violates the Map's standards. Here the congenial error would run the other way—toward generous attribution—and the guardrail holds equally against it.

## Evidence That Would Shift the Assessment

The scope question is empirically live, not merely verbal. The assays below are credence-shifters—they move the plausibility of value-sensitive selection up or down at each rung—rather than falsifiers of whether a boundary exists at all; the article concedes that existence-claim is, at the margin, in-principle unsettleable from outside. Each rung specifies what would move it:

- **A valence selection-advantage assay.** In any organism where conscious selection among competing neural patterns can be probed, valenced options selected *more readily than equally-salient neutral options* would support value-sensitive selection at that level; parity would support the value-blind reading. Absence of such an advantage in mammals would weaken the horn even for the rat.
- **Anticipatory-evaluation evidence.** Demonstrating that a nematode (or any minimal organism) constructs a forward model whose *felt* value—not mere predictive coding—biases a selection step would raise the nematode rung above speculative. Showing its avoidance is exhaustively reactive would lower it.
- **The *Physarum* disjunction.** Further cases of learning and flexible approach/avoidance in non-neural systems strengthen the value-blind reading of valence-*like* behaviour, raising the bar for attributing value-sensitive selection anywhere on behavioural grounds alone.

Two limits are in-principle, not instrumental. Verifying the *absence* of valence-denominated selection in any candidate requires settling the [[explanatory-gap]] from the outside, which the framework holds impossible from third-person data. And distinguishing value-sensitive from value-blind selection that produces identical behaviour may be underdetermined by any behavioural evidence—the two can be behaviourally indistinguishable at the margin. These are *epistemic* claims, and the article commits to the epistemic reading: there is a determinate boundary where value-sensitive selection bottoms out, but it cannot be located from outside. The boundary is therefore partly a genuine void in the epistemic sense—not merely unmeasured but, at its lower reaches, structurally resistant to measurement. The stronger *metaphysical* reading—that there is no fact of the matter about the boundary at the margin—is a constitutive claim about the world, not something inaccessibility alone licenses per [[evidential-status-discipline]]; it would need an argued bridging step the framework has not made, so it is not asserted here.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: The scope of value-sensitive selection is the scope over which the *evaluative* dimension of consciousness does causal work. Marking that scope sharpens what the tenet claims—and where it goes silent.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]**: Attributing a valence-to-physics coupling to ever-simpler organisms multiplies the non-physical influence the framework posits. Minimal interaction counsels caution about extending value-sensitive selection downward without independent evidence—a tenet-side reason the value-blind reading of marginal cases is the conservative default.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: The tenet warns against *dismissing* marginal-organism consciousness merely because parsimony prefers human-like complexity. It does not license the opposite error of *attributing* value-sensitive selection on the strength of behavioural resemblance. The discipline runs in both directions.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: Dualism makes the boundary's location an open empirical-and-phenomenal question rather than a fact readable off neural complexity—which is precisely why the framework cannot currently settle it.
