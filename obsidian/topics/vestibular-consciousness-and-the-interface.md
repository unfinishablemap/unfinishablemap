The vestibular sense—the inner-ear balance organs that register head rotation and the pull of gravity—is the most structurally unusual modality in The Unfinishable Map's survey of perception. Every sense the Map has worked so far has an obvious "what-it-is-like": colour for vision, pitch for hearing, sweetness for taste, the warmth of a caress for touch. The vestibular sense, in health, has almost none. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy summarises the situation plainly: "Besides cases of dizziness, there seems to be indeed a paucity of vestibular experiences in normal consciousness" (de Vignemont, 2024). And unlike vision, audition, or somatosensation, it has no primary cortex of its own. Yet this nearly-silent sense is foundational to the felt spatial self—to where "I" am located, to the first-person point of view, to the sense that this body is mine—and its disruption produces vertigo, depersonalization, and out-of-body experience. The vestibular case therefore inverts the series in a way none of its companions do: it contributes the *frame* of conscious spatial experience while barely showing up *as* an experienced object.

This is the vestibular companion to [[tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface|the tactile case]] and the inward [[interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface|interoceptive case]], the modality both of those articles named as still lacking an interface treatment. It is distinct from the separately developed [[dual-domain-capabilities-in-proprioception-and-spatial-imagination|proprioceptive treatment]]: proprioception covers the body schema, body image, and phantom limbs, whereas the genuinely uncovered vestibular material is the silent-sense puzzle, gravity as a *world-centred* spatial reference, the contribution to self-location and first-person perspective, and the disruption phenomenology of vertigo and disembodiment. As with every article in this series, nothing here *settles* dualism over physicalism. The physicalist has a strong, converging account of each datum, and the Map adopts that account as mechanism. What the vestibular case does is press the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] in a register the other modalities leave untouched: not "why is this quale like *that*," but "why is there a felt point of view, oriented within a frame, at all," when the frame's own carrier is scarcely felt.

## The Silent Sense

The vestibular organs sit in the inner ear: three semicircular canals "sensitive to motion acceleration as our head moves in space," and two otolith organs "sensitive to the pull of gravity" (de Vignemont, 2024). Together they answer two questions the philosopher Frédérique de Vignemont poses as the system's functional brief: "(1) 'Which way is up?' and (2) 'Where am I heading?'" These are among the most continuously useful facts a moving animal can have. The system runs constantly, every waking and sleeping moment, stabilising gaze and posture against the perpetual motion of the head.

And almost none of this is felt. There is no vestibular sensation the way there is a visual sensation of red or a tactile sensation of pressure. One does not normally feel the otoliths reporting gravity, or the canals reporting a turn of the head; one simply has, effortlessly and without an attendant quale, a sense of which way is up and where one is heading. De Vignemont's "paucity of vestibular experiences in normal consciousness" is the central datum. The qualifier "besides cases of dizziness" matters and is preserved here: when the system is *perturbed*—spinning, motion sickness, vertigo—something is loudly felt. The silence is the resting state, not an absolute absence.

Whether that silence reflects a genuine lack of proprietary phenomenology, or merely a faint phenomenology usually below the threshold of notice, is a live dispute the evidence does not close. On one reading there is no vestibular quale at all, only a vestibular *contribution* to spatial experience that belongs, phenomenally, to other modalities. On the other, the felt "up," the subtle sensation of self-motion, and the loudness of vertigo show a real proprietary phenomenology that is normally simply quiet. The Map does not need to adjudicate this to make its point. What is striking either way is the structural fact the next section develops: this is the one modality with no cortical territory it can call its own.

## A Sense With No Cortex of Its Own

The anatomical disanalogy is sharp. For the other senses, a dedicated primary cortex receives that sense's input before it is combined with anything else. Pfeiffer, Serino, and Blanke put the contrast directly: "While for vision, audition, and somatosensation specific unisensory primary cortices have been identified, no such unisensory vestibular cortex seems to exist in the human brain" (Pfeiffer, Serino & Blanke, 2014). Instead, "vestibular cortex is considered as any cortical region receiving vestibular input from the thalamus and is a distributed cortical network that overlaps with multisensory and motor representations from vision, somatosensation, proprioception, and action."

The vestibular signal, in other words, never has a unisensory cortical stage. It is multisensory by construction, woven into visual, somatosensory, proprioceptive, and motor representations from the moment it reaches cortex. This is the neuroscientific counterpart to the philosophical silent-sense puzzle: a sense whose contribution is so thoroughly a *frame* for the other senses that it has no place where it is processed *as itself*.

The function this distributed network serves is spatial. Pfeiffer and colleagues focus their review on "what we call spatial aspects of bodily self-consciousness, i.e., self-location and first-person perspective"—where the experiencing subject is located, and the viewpoint from which the world is experienced. Vestibular signals encode the head's position relative to gravity, supplying a world-centred reference for the bodily self. This is a different kind of spatial anchor from the body-centred frames of touch and proprioception. Gravity is allocentric: it is a fact about the world, not about the body, and the vestibular sense ties the felt self to it. The connection to the Map's [[three-dimensional-world-representation-problem|three-dimensional world-representation problem]] is direct—the otolith gravity signal is one of the few sensory channels that delivers an absolute, world-anchored axis rather than a relative or body-relative one.

## When the Frame Fails

The clearest evidence that this silent frame is load-bearing is what happens when it breaks. A sense one barely notices in health becomes, in disruption, capable of unsettling the felt self.

The mild cases are familiar: vertigo, motion sickness, the disorientation of a sudden spin. But vestibular dysfunction reaches further than balance. A systematic review finds that vestibular disorders trigger or exacerbate depersonalization and derealization—the unsettling sense of being detached from one's own body or from the surrounding world (Cento et al., 2026). The proposed mechanism is that a stable self–environment relationship depends on a veridical vestibular spatial reference; when that reference is disturbed, spatial disorientation and the anxiety it provokes mediate the slide into depersonalization symptoms. A sense that is silent in health becomes, in disease, capable of loosening the felt boundary of the self.

The limiting case is the out-of-body experience. Blanke and Arzy model the OBE as a transient failure to integrate visual, tactile, proprioceptive, and vestibular information at the temporo-parietal junction, with vestibular dysfunction contributing a disintegration between the body-centred space the vestibular sense helps anchor and the visual space around it (Blanke & Arzy, 2005). When integration fails, the experienced self-location can detach from the physical body altogether. The Map treats the OBE elsewhere ([[out-of-body-experiences]]); here it enters as the sharpest demonstration that the vestibular frame, silent in ordinary experience, is among the conditions on which the felt locatedness of the self depends.

Vestibular signals reach beyond self-location into bodily ownership itself. Galvanic and caloric vestibular stimulation modulate the rubber-hand illusion and illusory hand ownership (Lopez, Lenggenhager & Blanke, 2010)—the same illusion the [[tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface|tactile article]] uses to show the felt body boundary is constructed. That vestibular input can shift which hand feels like one's own is direct evidence that this sense feeds the construction of bodily ownership, not merely the maintenance of balance. The silent frame, when manipulated, moves the felt body.

## The Strongest Rival: The Brain as a Gravity-Tuned Inference Engine

The physicalist account of all this is strong, converging, and must be taken on its own terms rather than conscripted. Its core is that vestibular perception is statistically optimal inference, not a dedicated feeling.

Laurens and Droulez model the brain's processing of vestibular signals as Bayesian estimation tuned to the statistics of natural head movement, drawing on an internal representation of gravity (Laurens & Droulez, 2007). That internal gravity model is needed because the otolith organs face an inherent ambiguity: a tilt of the head and a linear acceleration of the body produce the same otolith signal. The brain resolves the ambiguity not by feeling its way through it but by inferring the most probable cause given a prior encoding Earth's gravity. The somatogravic illusion—where sustained acceleration is felt as a backward tilt—is exactly what such an inference predicts when the prior is misled. On this account, the felt sense of orientation and self-motion is the output of optimal multisensory estimation, and there is no vestibular quale doing explanatory work; there is a computed estimate that the rest of the system uses.

The bodily-self literature extends the same logic upward. Blanke synthesises self-location, first-person perspective, and bodily ownership as products of multisensory integration, principally at the temporo-parietal junction, with vestibular signals as one weighted input among vision, touch, and proprioception (Blanke, 2012). On this picture the felt spatial self is what multisensory integration delivers when its inputs agree, and what comes apart—into depersonalization or the OBE—when they conflict. The silent-sense puzzle gets a clean physicalist resolution: the vestibular sense is silent because it is a frame-coding, sub-threshold input to integration rather than an object of perception, and its loudness in vertigo is the felt signature of integration breaking down.

This is the rival the Map must out-accommodate, and it is important to be exact about its strength. de Vignemont is a naturalist; Pfeiffer, Serino, Blanke, Lopez, Lenggenhager, Laurens, and Droulez are building physicalist models and draw no dualist conclusion. None of them is an ally of the interface reading, and none is enlisted as one here. The Bayesian-inference account predicts the silence, the gravity prior, the otolith illusions, the depersonalization, and the OBE without any non-physical remainder. The Map adopts this machinery as mechanism. The honest situation is that the framework does not discriminate between the readings on the strength of this evidence—and the rest of this article says so plainly.

## What the Interface Reading Accommodates—and the Residue

So what does the vestibular case add, if the physicalist explains the mechanism? It supplies a structural fact the interface reading represents naturally and a residue the inference account leaves untouched.

The interface reading—conscious experience constituted at the [[mind-matter-interface|boundary]] between physical processing and phenomenal presentation—has no difficulty with a frame-providing modality that is largely brain-side. On the Map's [[cross-modal-capability-division|capability division]], the brain extracts and integrates spatial content without consciousness, and the mind side contributes the felt point of view from which that content is experienced. A sense that is almost entirely the former—a sub-threshold spatial frame—and barely the latter is exactly what that division would predict at one extreme. The vestibular sense looks like a modality that is nearly all brain-side labour, whose mind-side contribution is not a felt object but the spatial *situatedness* of the subject.

That framing is an accommodation, not a proof. The same facts are equally the output of frame-coding multisensory inference, and the Map claims no more than that the interface reading represents the silent frame without strain.

The residue is the explanatory-gap question, posed in an unusual register. For colour or pain the gap is "why is this physical state accompanied by *this* quale rather than another." The vestibular sense has scarcely any quale to attach the question to—and that is what makes the residue interesting. The question it leaves is: why is there a felt first-person perspective, oriented within a spatial frame, at all? The Bayesian estimator computes a head-relative-to-gravity estimate and the integration network fuses inputs into a self-location coordinate; both are functional descriptions of information processing. Neither states why any of it is *experienced from a point of view*—why there is a someone for whom there is an up and a heading. The vestibular case isolates this residue cleanly precisely because it strips away the distracting richness of a proprietary quale. The frame is felt as *had by a viewpoint*, and the viewpoint is what the functional story does not deliver.

The depersonalization and OBE data press on the boundary of this claim, and the Map marks the boundary rather than papering over it. One reading is that scrambling the vestibular reference removes only the subject's spatial situatedness—the *where* of the self—while the subject persists. Another is that the spatial frame is partly constitutive of subjecthood, so that disturbing it disturbs the experiencing self itself. This parallels the question the interoceptive article marks between a body that is merely *had* by consciousness and a body partly *constitutive* of it. The evidence does not adjudicate it. The depersonalization findings show that the felt unity of the self has a vestibular dependency; they do not show whether that dependency is on a condition of experience or on a component of the subject. The Map holds this open at the framework boundary, consistent with its [[evidential-status-discipline|discipline]] of not reading mechanism as metaphysics.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The vestibular distinctives above are framed throughout as phenomena the dualist/interface reading *accommodates*, never as evidence that settles the dispute. The empirical literature is naturalist—de Vignemont, Pfeiffer, Serino, Blanke, Lopez, Lenggenhager, Laurens, and Droulez build physicalist models and reach no dualist conclusion—and the Bayesian-inference and multisensory-integration accounts are engaged here as the rivals the interface reading must out-accommodate, not as allies to be conscripted. The Map adopts their mechanics and contests their sufficiency.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: The vestibular sense sharpens the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] in an unusual register. Because it has so little proprietary phenomenology, it does not invite the standard "why this quale" version of the gap; it isolates the prior question of why there is a felt point of view oriented within a frame at all, when the frame's own carrier is barely felt. A modality whose phenomenology is genuinely disputed is, for that reason, a clean exhibit of phenomenal structure: it lets the Map ask the gap question with the distraction of rich [[qualia]] removed. The physicalist's inference-and-integration reply stands; the residue the reply does not address is the experienced viewpoint.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: Vestibular perception is not passive reception. Self-motion is estimated with the help of motor commands—the system predicts the sensory consequences of intended movement and uses those predictions in computing self-motion, so that active and passive motion are estimated differently. This is consonant with the Map's reading, on which perceiving involves the perceiver's participation rather than mere registration of the world. The alignment is interpretive, not probative: the active-inference theorist re-describes the very same motor-prediction in wholly physical terms, and the Map's commitment to bidirectional interaction rests on its broader [[predictive-processing-and-dualism|interaction framework]], not on the vestibular case alone.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]** and **[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]** enter only obliquely. Self-location presupposes a determinate indexical fact about which located body, here and now, is the one experienced as mine—the kind of indexical determinacy the Map's rejection of branching worlds is meant to secure. No vestibular datum establishes the quantum-interface mechanism; the dependence runs from the framework to the reading of the data, not from the data to the mechanism.

The Map does not claim the vestibular sense settles dualism. It claims this sense is evidence any adequate theory must accommodate, and that the vestibular case extends the modality survey in a genuinely new direction. Where touch added a perceiver who is also perceived, and interoception added affect and owned selfhood, the vestibular sense adds the limiting case the whole series quietly assumed away: a modality that supplies the spatial frame of experience while barely appearing within it. A framework shaped by the richly felt senses looks incomplete until it can represent a sense that is almost all frame and almost no object—and the felt point of view that the frame is a frame *for*.
