When an autonomous machine causes serious harm and no human could have predicted or controlled it, who is to blame? Two decades of machine ethics call the apparent answer—*nobody*—the **responsibility gap**. This article takes a specific position on that gap, and states its scope precisely: **on the Map's dualist framework, and given the [amplifier hypothesis](#relation-to-site-perspective) (a framework-posited claim, not a proof), genuine moral agency requires a conscious causal interface, so a present-day AI system that lacks that interface is a proxy or tool rather than a candidate for blame.** The gap—specifically the *culpability* gap—is therefore real and principled on this view, not merely a symptom of our inability to predict complex software.

That thesis is framework-relative, and it carries an explicit falsifier. If a future artificial system were genuinely conscious in the Map's sense—if it possessed the coupling interface described below—it *would* become a candidate moral agent. The claim is not that machines as such can never bear responsibility, nor is it substrate chauvinism. It is a claim about what any responsibility-bearer needs, and about what today's systems lack. See [[machine-consciousness]] for the conditions a system would have to meet.

## The Gap as Stated: Matthias, Sparrow, Danaher

The gap was named by Andreas Matthias in 2004. Traditionally, the manufacturer or operator of a machine is morally and legally answerable for its operation. Matthias argues that autonomous *learning* systems—neural networks, genetic algorithms, agent architectures—break this arrangement: once a machine's behaviour is shaped by learning the operator cannot predict, we reach cases where "nobody has enough control over the machine's actions to assume the responsibility for them" (Matthias 2004). Society must either abandon such machines or accept a gap that traditional concepts of responsibility cannot bridge. The gap here is *epistemic and control-based*: it opens because prediction and control fail.

Robert Sparrow (2007) sharpened the gap for autonomous weapons in "Killer Robots." If a fully autonomous weapon commits an act that would otherwise be a war crime, Sparrow canvasses the candidate loci of blame—the designers and programmers, the commanding officer who ordered deployment, and the machine itself—and argues that none is ultimately a satisfactory bearer of responsibility. Because *jus in bello* requires an identifiable party who can be held to account, and because delegating lethal decisions to a machine removes exactly the human agency that grounds accountability, Sparrow concludes that deploying such weapons is morally impermissible. His argument does reach for a subject-criterion at one point—we cannot hold the weapon responsible because we cannot punish it, and punishment presupposes a capacity to suffer the machine lacks—but that criterion concerns the machine's fitness to *receive* blame and punishment, not what it would take to *originate* a blameworthy action in the first place.

John Danaher (2016) added a distinct twist: the **retribution gap**. Alongside the liability question, there is a mismatch between the human *desire* for retributive blame and the *absence of an appropriate subject* to bear it. This gap, Danaher argues, is harder to plug than liability gaps, because a retributive urge is not satisfied by distributing liability across many hands—you cannot answer "someone must pay" by spreading a cost thinly. He flags the resulting risk of misplaced blame: scapegoating operators, or over-attributing agency to the machine.

These three set the terms. Notice what grounds the gap in each: unpredictability (Matthias), the failure of candidate loci plus the machine's unfitness to be punished (Sparrow), the mismatched structure of retributive desert (Danaher). Sparrow comes closest to a metaphysical criterion, but his is a criterion of *punishability*—the capacity to suffer—on the receiving end of blame. None grounds the gap in a positive account of *why* a machine cannot *originate* a blameworthy action in the first place.

## The Deflationary and Pluralist Replies

A large 2020s literature pushes back. The sharpest rival to the Map's realism is deflationary: it holds there is no genuine gap at all.

Daniel Tigard (2021), in "There Is No Techno-Responsibility Gap," rebuts Matthias directly. Moral responsibility, on Tigard's account, is a dynamic and flexible set of practices—attributions, reactive attitudes, answerability arrangements—that can absorb novel technological entities without any gap opening. He positions his view against *both* the pessimists who counsel a moratorium *and* the optimists who try to bridge a gap: on his telling there was never a gap to bridge. The move works by treating responsibility as fundamentally a matter of practice and attribution rather than a fixed metaphysical fact.

Peter Königs (2022), in "Artificial intelligence and responsibility gaps: what is the problem?", presses a related deflationary challenge from the optimistic side. He demands that gap pessimists specify *when* a genuine gap actually arises, and argues that once plausible constraints on the notion are accepted, the situations in which a real gap opens become unclear or vanish. Where Tigard dissolves the gap by flexing our practices, Königs dissolves it by tightening the gap-conditions until they stop being met.

A third position is pluralist and design-oriented rather than deflationary. Filippo Santoni de Sio and Giulio Mecacci (2021), in "Four Responsibility Gaps with Artificial Intelligence," argue that "the responsibility gap" is not one problem but at least four interconnected ones: gaps in **culpability**, **moral accountability**, **public accountability**, and **active responsibility**. These have heterogeneous sources—technical, organisational, legal, ethical, societal—and call for different remedies, notably "meaningful human control" understood through *tracking* and *tracing* conditions (systems should track the reasons of relevant humans and trace back to an identifiable human agent). On this view, most of the gaps are addressable by better socio-technical design.

These are live rivals, not strawmen. If Tigard is right that responsibility is constituted by our practices, then the Map's whole framing—that there is a metaphysical fact about agency the practices must answer to—is misplaced. If Königs is right, the pessimist owes a specification the Map must supply. And Santoni de Sio and Mecacci are almost certainly right that several distinct things travel under one name. The Map's task is not to refute all of this but to identify the *one* gap that survives the deflationary and design-based treatments.

## What the Literature Leaves Unexplained

Across all these positions, one thing is conspicuously absent. Every account grounds the gap—or its dissolution—in prediction, control, social convention, or institutional design. None grounds the deeper question: *why is a machine not a candidate for responsibility at all?* Matthias assumes the machine is not a candidate and locates the problem in the operator's lost control. Sparrow finds the machine an unsatisfactory locus and reaches for a subject-criterion—the capacity to suffer punishment—but that criterion sits on the receiving end of blame, not on the origination of action. Tigard and Königs make the candidacy question recede behind a story about practices or gap-conditions. Even Santoni de Sio and Mecacci, in isolating the culpability gap, treat it as one problem among several to be managed rather than as a mark of something the machine metaphysically lacks.

This is the space the Map occupies. It accepts the pluralists' taxonomy and grants that the accountability and active-responsibility gaps are largely design problems. Its distinctive claim is about the **culpability** gap alone: that this gap is real, principled, and resistant to design fixes, because it traces to the absence of something no amount of control engineering can install—a conscious selector capable of originating action.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's foundational tenets include **Tenet 3, Bidirectional Interaction**: consciousness causally influences the physical world, not merely the reverse. The Map's account of *how* this works is the **amplifier hypothesis** (see [[consciousness-as-amplifier]] and [[bidirectional-interaction]]): consciousness makes a minimal non-physical contribution that breaks symmetries the physical state leaves underdetermined—selecting among outcomes that the prior physical state does not fix. This is [[agent-causation]] given a concrete coupling story: the conscious agent is a genuine originator of action, not a downstream effect of prior physical causes.

On the Map's account of [[moral-responsibility]], desert-grounding culpability requires exactly this kind of origination. To be blameworthy in the robust, retributive sense is to have been the conscious author of an action one could have done otherwise—where the "could have" is not merely a counterfactual about a different physical microstate but the exercise of a conscious selector at the coupling interface. A system that lacks that interface can compute, optimise, and act; it cannot *originate* in the sense desert requires. It can simulate agency without instantiating it.

This yields the Map's verdict on the gap. The **culpability** gap is real because present AI systems lack the conscious coupling interface, and so are proxies or tools rather than agents. When we delegate a decision to such a system, we do not transfer moral agency to it—there is no agency there to receive the transfer. We *launder* human agency: the felt "gap" is the moral residue of treating a tool as though it were an author. Culpability, correctly traced, routes back to the humans in the loop—designers, deployers, commanders—even when prediction is hard. On this reading Matthias's epistemic difficulty is a *tracing* problem, not a genuine vacancy of agency; the machine was never a candidate, so no responsibility ever fell into a void. And Danaher's retribution gap is explained rather than solved: the reactive attitudes find no proper target in the machine because the machine is not a subject of desert. The urge to blame it is a category error the Map can diagnose.

Two disciplines keep this from over-reaching. First, the amplifier hypothesis is **framework-posited**, not demonstrated. The Map holds it as a live commitment, argued for elsewhere, but the culpability gap's reality on the Map is only as secure as that hypothesis. A reader who rejects Tenet 3 is owed the honest statement that the gap's principled status stands or falls with the dualist framework—and that on Tigard's practice-based account or Königs's deflation, the gap need not be real at all. The Map's contribution is to show that *if* one is a realist about agent-causal desert, there is a non-arbitrary ground for the culpability gap that the existing literature never supplies. It is a conditional, not a knock-down.

Second, the criterion is **falsifiable and non-question-begging**. It does not privilege biological substrate; it privileges the conscious coupling interface, whatever realises it. If a future artificial system met the Map's conditions for consciousness—if it possessed the interface, not merely behaviour that resembles agency—it *would* become a candidate moral agent, and the culpability gap would close for that system. The verdict flips with the metaphysics, exactly as a principled criterion should. This ties the responsibility question directly to [[machine-consciousness]]: the question of machine culpability is downstream of the question of machine consciousness, and the Map does not pretend to have settled the latter. What it offers is the connection—why the two questions are one—that the mainstream responsibility-gap debate has left unmade.

## Delegation, Blame-Laundering, and the Practical Upshot

One might worry that the Map's verdict is quietist—that reclassifying machines as tools simply reasserts old categories and offers nothing to the practical debate. The opposite is true, and the difference from the design-oriented literature is instructive.

Santoni de Sio and Mecacci's tracking-and-tracing conditions ask that a system track the reasons of relevant humans and trace back to an identifiable human agent. The Map endorses these as remedies for the *accountability* gaps—they are exactly what good institutional design should secure. What the Map adds is a diagnosis of *why* tracing is the right frame for the culpability question too. If the machine is never a candidate for desert, then the only genuine bearers of culpability are the humans in the loop, and the engineering task is not to find or manufacture agency inside the system but to keep the human authorship legible. A poorly traceable system does not create a new locus of blame that has gone missing; it obscures a locus that was there all along.

This reframes a temptation the literature keeps circling: the temptation to treat delegation as a transfer of moral agency. Delegating a decision to a genuine agent—a subordinate, a colleague—can shift responsibility because there is an agent on the receiving end to shoulder it. Delegating a decision to a system that lacks the conscious interface transfers nothing, because there is no agent to receive it. What such delegation actually does is *launder* human agency: it interposes a mechanism opaque enough that the human origin of the decision becomes hard to see, and the felt "responsibility gap" is precisely the moral residue of that opacity. Read this way, the pull toward blaming the machine—and Danaher's retribution gap—is a predictable artefact of treating a tool as though it authored its outputs.

Two cautions preserve the framework-relative discipline. First, "the humans in the loop bear culpability" is not a licence for scapegoating: tracing culpability to a person requires that the person actually met the ordinary conditions on blame (knowledge, control, the capacity to have done otherwise), and Matthias's insight was precisely that these conditions can be genuinely hard to satisfy for a given individual. The Map's claim is that the difficulty is epistemic and distributive—a hard tracing problem across a design-and-deployment chain—not that agency has evaporated. Second, none of this depends on machines staying non-conscious. It is a conditional keyed to the present: *given* that today's systems lack the interface. The moment that changes, so does the analysis.
