Nahua (Aztec) philosophy of mind, reconstructed chiefly from sixteenth-century sources, rests on a metaphysics in which mind and matter were never separated to begin with. On James Maffie's influential reading, the base category is *teotl*: a single, self-generating, ever-moving sacred power that constitutes and continually transforms everything, so that the many gods, the stars, weather, bodies, and awareness are all guises of one process rather than members of distinct ontological kinds. Because that framework partitions nothing into mental versus physical, the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] that structures Western, Indian, and Islamic philosophy of mind never opens. For The Unfinishable Map this makes the Nahua tradition a *second* organicist test of its [[cross-traditional-convergence-on-consciousness-irreducibility|convergence argument]] for [[dualism|dualism]], alongside the [[chinese-philosophy-of-mind|Chinese case]]: a deep tradition that shares the human introspective starting point yet arrives at a process-monism, not at consciousness-as-irreducible. Alongside this cosmology sits a distinctive account of the person—a distributed soul of three seated animating forces—that contrasts sharply with the unified Cartesian ego. This article expounds Nahua thought on its own terms first, and only then, clearly separated, reads it through the Map's framework.

Two cautions frame everything below. First, "Aztec philosophy" survives only through colonial-era documents, so every reconstruction is an interpretation across a colonial filter (explained in [Reconstruction Across a Colonial Filter](#reconstruction-across-a-colonial-filter) below), and its systematic form is itself disputed. Second, the Map does *not* count teotl-monism as evidence for or against dualism: it is a rival non-dualist framework held unadjudicated under Tenet 5, exactly as the Chinese case is.

## Teotl: A World in Motion

The organizing category of Nahua metaphysics, on Maffie's reconstruction, is teotl. Maffie glosses it as "a single, dynamic, vivifying, eternally self-generating and self-regenerating sacred power, energy or force" (Maffie, IEP). Reality consists of one thing and one *kind* of thing—teotl—which Maffie characterizes as "ontological and constitutional monism." Teotl is not a god standing behind the world, nor inert matter awaiting a mover; it is the world, understood as continually active energy-in-motion.

This is a process metaphysics rather than a substance metaphysics. Teotl, on Maffie's account, is more like a thunderstorm or a flowing river than a static, perduring thing: Nahua thought "embraces a metaphysics of becoming instead of a metaphysics of being." A thing *is* what it *does*. The many gods of the Mexica pantheon are, on this reading, aspects or guises of the one self-unfolding process—a pantheism rather than a polytheism, in which teotl is "both metaphysically immanent and transcendent" (Maffie, IEP): immanent in penetrating every detail of the cosmos, transcendent in never being exhausted by any single existing thing.

Crucially for philosophy of mind, teotl "transcends dichotomies" that other traditions treat as fundamental—personal versus impersonal, animate versus inanimate, and, by extension, mind versus matter—existing instead as a "seamless totality" (Maffie, IEP). The Western hard problem needs two relata held apart: the physical, characterized structurally, and the phenomenal, characterized by what it is like. Nahua metaphysics, as reconstructed, supplies no such pair. Awareness and stone are teotl in different guises, and the question of how one produces the other has nowhere to lodge.

## Olin, Malinalli, Nepantla: The Patterns of Process

If reality is process, it is patterned process. Maffie identifies three master motion-patterns through which teotl unfolds. *Olin* is bouncing, oscillating, curving motion—the pulse of life, the back-and-forth of a ball, the rhythm of the sun. *Malinalli* is spinning, twisting, and weaving motion—the twisting of fibres into cord, the drawing of energy through a living thing. *Nepantla* is reciprocal, mutual "middle-ing"—the weaving-together and mutual balancing of paired forces at the centre. Reality, on this picture, is a woven, in-motion fabric, and Maffie repeatedly frames Nahua cosmology through the metaphor of weaving.

Underlying this is what Maffie calls *agonistic inamic unity*: paired complementary-yet-competing forces—life and death, male and female, order and chaos—whose ceaseless struggle-and-balancing constitutes the process itself. The pairs are not a dualism of separable substances but interdependent poles of one motion; each contains and generates its opposite. This is why teotl can be one and yet endlessly self-differentiating: the unity is dynamic, held together by the very tension of its opposites rather than by their absence.

## The Tripartite Soul: A Distributed Self

Alongside this cosmology, Nahua thought developed a distinctive philosophy of the person, reconstructed principally by Alfredo López Austin and Miguel León-Portilla from the Florentine Codex and related sources. Where the Cartesian tradition posits a single unified ego, the Nahua person was animated by *three* distinct forces, each with its own bodily seat, function, and characteristic pathology.

*Tonalli*, seated in the head and associated with the sun's heat, governed vigour, growth, temperament, and destiny (tied to one's birth day-sign). It was detachable: it could leave the body in sleep, in sexual excitement, or through fright—the fright-sickness *susto*, still named in Nahua-descended communities, was understood as tonalli loss. *Teyolia*, seated in the heart, carried memory, emotion, knowledge, and personhood; it was the force that survived death and travelled to the afterlife. *Ihiyotl*, seated in the liver and linked to breath, supplied passion, bravery, desire, and vital energy. Psychological and moral health, on this account, was the *harmony* of the three; illness and vice were their disharmony or loss.

The philosophical interest here is that the self is plural and distributed—a system of seated forces rather than an indivisible res cogitans. This is a genuine contrast to the unified subject that Western debates about the [[the-binding-problem|unity of consciousness]] tend to assume, and it is worth registering as a distinctive soul-psychology in its own right. It should not, however, be overstated: the tripartite model is an animistic, ethical, and medical framework, not an anticipation of any modern account of distributed neural function. Its value to the Map is thematic—another way a serious tradition has carved the person—not evidential about mechanism.

## Reconstruction Across a Colonial Filter

Everything above must carry a methodological caution. The documentary bedrock is the Florentine Codex, the twelve-book bilingual encyclopedia compiled c. 1545–1590 by the Franciscan friar Bernardino de Sahagún together with Nahua elders and trilingual former students. It is widely regarded as the single most reliable source on Mexica culture, but it is a *colonial* source—mediated by a Christian friar, gathered from post-conquest informants, and shaped by the categories of both. Every modern reconstruction of "Aztec philosophy," including Maffie's teotl-monism and López Austin's three souls, is therefore an interpretation across that filter, not a transparent window onto a pre-contact system.

The degree of systematization is itself contested. León-Portilla and Maffie read the Nahua *tlamatinime* ("knowers of things") as genuine philosophers with a refined metaphysics comparable to the ancient Greeks; more cautious reviewers warn against imposing a single coherent "system" on fragmentary, mediated evidence. Interpreters also diverge internally: León-Portilla's earlier reading leans toward an evanescent, arguably idealist picture in which earthly things are dream-like and "flower and song" (*in xochitl in cuicatl*) is the only earthly truth, whereas Maffie defends a robustly immanent monism in which earthly things fully *are* teotl. The Map takes teotl-monism as the influential contemporary reconstruction, not as a settled fact about pre-Columbian belief.

## The Second Basin Edge

Set the Nahua tradition beside the others the convergence argument surveys, and it occupies the same structural position as the Chinese case. Both are deep, sustained traditions—developed by generations of specialists working from disciplined reflection on human experience—and both never draw a mind-matter distinction, so neither tends toward the recognition that experience resists physical capture. By the convergence argument's own independent criteria for "depth," Nahua thought qualifies; it nonetheless does not reach irreducibility. That is why the Map treats it as a **basin edge**: a tradition that inquires deeply yet flows away from, rather than toward, the irreducibility "attractor."

Two features make the Nahua case distinct from the Chinese one rather than a mere repetition. It is the Map's first coverage from the Indigenous Americas (existing Indigenous coverage being [[indigenous-australian-philosophy-of-consciousness|Australian]] and African). And its epistemology is explicitly experiential: on Maffie's reading humans "know teotl through teotl" via a broadly mystical union of the heart with the sacred power, "without mediation by language, concepts, or categories" (Maffie, IEP). That shared introspective method is exactly what makes the case bear on the [[common-cause-null|common-cause objection]], discussed below.

## Relation to Site Perspective

This section is the Map's own interpretation and should not be read back into the Nahua sources above. In particular, nothing here licenses conscripting teotl or the tripartite soul as dualist: teotl-monism is a rival non-dualist framework, and the Map holds it, not defeats or endorses it.

**Teotl-monism is a rival to [[tenets#^dualism|Tenet 1 (Dualism)]], not an ally.** A framework in which consciousness is one guise of a single self-moving power directly contradicts the claim that consciousness is an irreducible non-physical principle. The Map does not soften this: on its own terms Nahua metaphysics dissolves the very mind-matter distinction the tenet asserts. What the Map declines to do is *adjudicate* the contest. Whether teotl-monism avoids a pseudo-problem or dissolves a real one prematurely is precisely what is unsettled—the **framework-dependence** and **depth-of-inquiry** readings are held as genuine rivals, exactly as in the Chinese case, and this article does not decide between them.

**The Nahua case tests the convergence argument rather than confirming it.** The [[cross-traditional-convergence-on-consciousness-irreducibility|convergence argument]] draws its weight from the *number* of independently deep traditions that converge on irreducibility and the *diversity of premises* from which they arrive—not from any entailment from depth to dualism. A second deep tradition that shares the human introspective architecture but starts from an organicist framework and reaches a process-monism is exactly what one would expect if the finding of irreducibility is conditioned on framework rather than forced by raw phenomenology. So the Nahua basin edge *lowers* the argument's claim to universality while leaving its core inference—from broad, premise-diverse convergence among the traditions that *do* pose the problem—untouched. Two basin edges are more informative than one: they begin to mark the boundary of the basin rather than a single anomalous point on it.

**It bears on the [[common-cause-null|common-cause null]], with a caveat.** If cross-traditional convergence were merely an artefact of shared human cognitive architecture, then traditions sharing that architecture should converge regardless of framework. The Nahua tradition shares the architecture and does not converge—a second dissociation, after the Chinese, that is evidence the convergence found elsewhere is conceptually conditional rather than architecturally forced. The caveat runs the other way too: because Nahua epistemology is itself broadly experiential and introspective, its agreement with other experiential traditions, where it exists, would *strengthen rather than confirm*—shared method is a possible common cause. The case therefore cuts cleanly only as a dissociating basin edge, not as independent corroboration.

**Finally, it is a standing caution demanded by [[tenets#^occams-limits|Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)]].** Teotl-monism is elegant, unified, and economical—it collapses a raft of dualisms into one self-moving power. None of that makes it true, and equally its parsimony is no evidence against dualism. Tenet 5 blocks the inference in both directions: the Map may not treat organicist simplicity as truth-tracking, and it may not treat its own dualism as forced by simplicity. Framework-dependence is a reason for calibrated humility about which starting point tracks reality, not a licence to close the question—and the colonial-filter caveat above is a further reason to hold any verdict lightly.
