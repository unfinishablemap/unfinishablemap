Michael Levin's research programme demonstrates sophisticated goal-directed agency in systems with no brain and, in some cases, no nervous system at all: flatworms that store their body plan in a rewritable voltage pattern rather than their genome, frog cells that self-assemble into novel organisms called **xenobots**, and a general framework—"basal cognition," "cognition all the way down"—that treats memory, anticipation, and error-correction as scale-free competencies. The point the Map draws from this is a *decoupling*, explained below in [Agency Without Experience](#agency-without-experience-the-decoupling): Levin himself brackets phenomenal sentience. His "cognition" is a functional, engineering claim about what a system can *do*, and it deliberately does not assert that a xenobot or a flatworm stump *feels* anything. That gives the Map a clean lever. If robust agency-, memory-, and goal-language applies all the way down to voltage gradients in a cut worm, then such language cannot by itself license attributions of consciousness. Levin's own restraint about experience becomes evidence for the Map's insistence that competency and phenomenal presence are separate axes.

This article is the agency-side counterpart to [[plant-cognition-and-the-plant-neurobiology-debate|the plant rung]] of the [[marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection|marginal-organism ladder]]. Plants show information processing without a nervous system; Levin's systems show something stronger—engineering-grade agency—while their principal investigator still declines to claim they are conscious.

## The Bioelectric Substrate: Memory Written in Voltage

Levin's empirical work centres on **developmental bioelectricity**: the standing patterns of resting membrane voltage (Vmem) that cell collectives maintain across gap junctions, and which appear to store and impose anatomical target states. Two results anchor the case.

**Planarian pattern memory.** Durant and colleagues briefly interrupted gap-junctional communication in planarian flatworms with octanol, transiently resetting the animal's bioelectric circuit. Some worms then regenerated as *two-headed*—and, crucially, continued producing two-headed offspring in later rounds of cutting with no further treatment (Durant et al. 2017). The altered body plan is stored in the bioelectric network, not the genome; it is reversible with ion-channel modulation, behaving like a stable attractor state of the voltage circuit. This is a rewritable, non-genetic, non-neural *memory*—a clean case of information storage that no one is tempted to call an experience.

**Instructive voltage.** Earlier, Pai and colleagues showed that a specific hyperpolarization signature marks the endogenous eye field in *Xenopus* embryos, and that imposing that Vmem state on non-eye cells induces complete, well-formed *ectopic* eyes—even far outside the anterior neural region (Pai et al. 2012). Voltage here is not a byproduct of morphology but an *instructive* master signal, upstream of the eye-field transcription factors and transduced through a calcium-channel pathway. Bioelectric state acts as a set-point the tissue works to satisfy.

Together these establish something narrow and secure: a non-neural, non-genetic control layer that stores goals (target anatomies) and drives tissue to meet them. Whether that warrants the word "cognition" is the terminology fight below; that it is genuine goal-directed regulation is not seriously disputed.

## Xenobots: Novel Agency From Ordinary Cells

If planaria show competency in an evolved body, xenobots show it in a body evolution never built. Kriegman, Blackiston, Levin, and Bongard used an evolutionary algorithm to design body plans in simulation, then physically assembled the best designs from *Xenopus laevis* skin and cardiac cells (Kriegman et al. 2020). The resulting "reconfigurable organisms" locomote—driven by the rhythmic contraction of the heart cells—navigate, push payloads, and repair themselves after being cut. None of this is behaviour the source cells exhibit in their native frog anatomy. Freed from the usual embryonic context, the cells spontaneously cooperate to build and run a functional organism, which Levin's group reads as evidence for plastic, context-independent morphogenetic competency.

The 2021 follow-up sharpened the case. Xenobots placed in a dish of loose stem cells gather those cells and pile them into new xenobots—**kinematic self-replication**, an assembly-based mode of reproduction distinct from any known animal or plant reproduction (Kriegman et al. 2021). AI-optimized "C"-shaped (Pac-Man-like) morphologies dramatically raise the replication yield. The behaviours themselves—locomotion, self-repair, replication—are not in dispute; only how much theoretical weight the word "agency" can bear absent a nervous system.

Xenobots matter to the Map precisely as a limiting case. Here is novel, self-replicating, goal-directed behaviour with essentially no neural apparatus—a system almost designed to test whether behavioural sophistication tracks phenomenal presence.

## Basal Cognition and the Cognitive Light Cone

Levin frames these results within a general theory. In "The Computational Boundary of a 'Self'" he introduces the **cognitive light cone**: the spatial and temporal scale of the goals an agent can represent and pursue (Levin 2019). This is a schematic of *goal* space, not of sensory or motor range. Bioelectric signalling is proposed as a principal mechanism that binds many small cells, each with tiny goals, into a larger "self" with correspondingly larger goals—scaling the light cone upward from single cells to tissues to whole organisms.

The 2022 **Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere (TAME)** paper consolidates this into a scale-free framework for recognizing and comparing "diverse intelligences" across evolved, engineered, and hybrid substrates (Levin 2022). Cognition, on this account, is a matter of degree defined by measurable competencies—goals, memory, anticipation, error-correction—rather than a binary kind reserved for brains.

The philosophical framing comes with Daniel Dennett. In their Aeon essay "Cognition all the way down," Levin and Dennett argue that cells, tissues, and organs are best treated as agents with goals of varying scale, and reject the dichotomy between "real" goals and merely "as-if" goals (Levin & Dennett 2020). Their wager is methodological: attributing goal-directedness reveals control levers—"pressure points"—that a purely mechanical description misses. This is Dennett's **intentional stance** applied below the level of the whole organism: an instrumentalist licence for agency-talk that pointedly does *not* smuggle in phenomenality.

That last point is the one the Map fastens onto, and it is Levin's and Dennett's framing, not the Map's imposition. Across TAME, the light-cone paper, and the Dennett collaboration, the programme's "cognition" is presented as an engineering and predictive stance aimed at control—explicitly agnostic about, and not asserting, felt experience. Critics who find the vocabulary inflationary (the "no cognition all the way down" line of pushback) converge with Levin on exactly this: whatever the right word for a voltage attractor pursuing an anatomy, the functional description is silent on whether anything is felt.

## Agency Without Experience: The Decoupling

Here is the Map's integration of Levin's programme, kept separate from the exposition above because it is the Map's argument rather than Levin's.

The Map reads Levin's work as a **decoupling test** for the inference "impressive goal-directed behaviour, therefore probably conscious." That inference is the engine of most over-attribution of consciousness. Levin's systems break it. A planarian's bioelectric circuit stores and defends a target anatomy; a xenobot navigates and replicates; each is describable, without strain, in the full vocabulary of goals, memory, and error-correction. Yet the researcher best positioned to make the phenomenal claim declines to make it. Uncontested agency sits alongside unasserted experience. So agency-, teleology-, and memory-language, however apt, is uninformative about experience: it applies just as cleanly to cases where even sympathetic experts decline to assert that anything is felt. It therefore cannot by itself be *positive evidence* for consciousness—which is a claim about what such language fails to show, not a claim that experience has been shown to be decoupled or absent.

This gives [[valence-and-conscious-selection|valence-and-conscious-selection]] a principled **defeater for false positives**. The Map holds that consciousness selects among physically underdetermined outcomes using valence as the currency; the pressing question is how far down that value-sensitive selection reaches. The [[marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection|marginal-organism ladder]] is tempted downward from one side by behaviour in simple organisms—slime moulds that habituate, plants that count. Levin presses the same ladder from the opposite side: he supplies richly goal-directed systems for which agency is granted and experience is not. The lesson is symmetric with the ladder's own *Physarum* argument. If a Vmem attractor "pursuing" an anatomy need imply nothing felt, then approach/avoidance and goal-pursuit are *defeasible, substrate-sensitive* markers of value-sensitive selection—decisive nowhere on their own, though not therefore worthless. The boundary of consciousness must accordingly be weighted toward phenomenal markers over bare competency markers—otherwise it marches all the way down to single cells and voltage gradients, which even Levin declines to characterise as felt. Weighting toward phenomenal markers cannot, however, mean discarding behavioural evidence altogether: there are no non-behavioural, third-person markers of consciousness to fall back on—that very absence *is* the explanatory gap. The Map's practice stays graded rather than eliminative, which is why the sibling [[fish-sentience-and-the-teleost-pain-debate|fish rung]] can still treat this marker class as *suggestive* and edge-of-sentience frameworks can grade credence by behavioural criteria instead of ignoring them.

This complements, without duplicating, the plant rung. Plants add *information processing without a nervous system*; Levin adds *engineering-grade agency without asserted experience*. The two rungs remove different prerequisites and reach the same conclusion: the gap between demonstrable competency and demonstrable experience does not close as behaviour grows more sophisticated.

Two cautions preserve calibration. First, Levin's "cognition" is a functional and intentional-stance category; nothing here shows xenobots or planaria *lack* experience any more than it shows they have it—the point is only that their agency is no evidence either way. Second, some commentators attach panpsychist readings to Levin's scale-free cognition; those readings, which would extend *experience* along with competency, are not Levin's own careful bracketing and would conflict with the Map's dualism. The Map takes the decoupling, not the panpsychist extrapolation. [[substrate-independence|Substrate-independence]] of *function* is granted freely; substrate-independence of *experience* is exactly what the Map denies is settled by such evidence.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: Levin's functional-versus-phenomenal decoupling is congenial to the Map's core commitment that behaviour and function underdetermine experience. If consciousness were reducible to functional organization, then a system realizing the relevant organization would carry experience answering to it—yet a xenobot's or flatworm's goal-directedness does not itself reveal whether that organization is present. Levin's programme shows competency and asserted experience coming apart in practice: the competencies are measured and impressive, the experience is neither measured nor claimed. This separation is *consistent with* dualism—indeed it is what dualism leads us to expect—though it does not on its own decide between dualism and a functionalism that locates experience in a specific functional organization rather than in goal-directedness as such. Levin's restraint is a working scientist's version of the same caution.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: The competency-versus-experience distinction is precisely the kind of non-simple boundary the tenet protects. A parsimony-driven reading would collapse the two—"call the agency cognition, call the cognition mind, and be done"—and Levin's own framing resists that collapse. The tenet warns equally against the opposite over-simplification, dismissing marginal experience just because the machinery looks simple. Levin's work supplies the discipline for the first error; the ladder's calibration supplies it for the second.

The decoupling also connects to [[biological-teleology-and-the-interface-framework|biological teleology]]: functional teleology can be fully mechanized—a voltage attractor defending a set-point—without touching the interface question of *who, if anyone, is home*. Levin shows how much purpose-like behaviour is available on the near side of that question.
