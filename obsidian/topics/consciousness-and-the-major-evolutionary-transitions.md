Evolutionary biology recognises a small set of watershed events—the *major evolutionary transitions*—at which formerly independent replicators became parts of a new, higher-level reproducing whole: genes into chromosomes, cells into organisms, organisms into societies. A natural question follows: was the arrival of consciousness one of these transitions? The honest answer begins with a concession. The general move has already been made. Simona Ginsburg and Eva Jablonka proposed consciousness as a major transition in *The Evolution of the Sensitive Soul* (2019), and Jablonka and Ginsburg (2025) extended it with a new category of selection. The Unfinishable Map does not claim novelty for the idea that consciousness is a transition.

What the Map does propose is narrower and more specific, and it is offered as a [[#the-phenomenal-transition-thesis|framework-relative hypothesis]] (developed below), not as an established finding. The existing transitions framework—including its consciousness extension—is built entirely in informational, replicative, and functional terms. On the Map's dualism, such a framework would be *constitutively blind* to a transition in phenomenal experience as such: the felt inner side, the coupling between consciousness and matter. If a phenomenal transition occurred, an information-only theory could register only its behavioural shadow. That is the thesis this article develops, and flags as speculative throughout.

## The Major Transitions Framework

The canonical statement is Maynard Smith and Szathmáry's *The Major Transitions in Evolution* (1995), with a compact version in *Nature* the same year. Its unifying idea: at each transition, entities that could previously replicate independently could afterward replicate only as part of a larger whole. A transition is therefore a change in the *level of individuality* and in *how heritable information is stored and transmitted*.

The eight transitions of the 1995 scheme run from the origin of life to the origin of language:

1. Replicating molecules → populations of molecules in compartments (protocells)
2. Independent replicators → chromosomes
3. RNA as gene-and-enzyme → DNA plus protein (the genetic code)
4. Prokaryotes → eukaryotes (via endosymbiosis)
5. Asexual clones → sexual populations
6. Single-celled protists → multicellular organisms
7. Solitary individuals → colonies with non-reproductive castes (eusociality)
8. Primate societies → human societies with language

Two threads run through the list: a reorganisation of individuality, and the evolution of new modes of information transmission (the genetic code, epigenetic inheritance, language). The framework's popular rewrite, *The Origins of Life* (1999), reorganised the same material around a smaller set of breakthroughs in how information passes between generations.

Szathmáry's later "theory 2.0" (2015) revised the scheme without abandoning its two axes. It removed sex from the list—a sexual population is not a new individual—and added endosymbiotic events. It imported David Queller's distinction between *fraternal* transitions (cooperation among like, related units, as in multicellularity and eusociality) and *egalitarian* transitions (integration of unlike, unrelated units, as in the origin of eukaryotes). And it foregrounded multilevel selection as the explanatory engine, with the higher-level collective coming into focus only after the transition is underway.

For the Map's purposes, the salient feature is the *register* in which the entire apparatus is stated. Every criterion—new individuals, new information channels, fraternal or egalitarian integration—is informational and selectional. The list-making criteria contain no slot for "a new kind of *inner* fact appearing." Judged by its own aims the framework is complete; the Map's interest lies precisely in the slot it does not contain.

## Individuality and the Units of Selection

A parallel philosophical literature refines what it takes to *be* an evolutionary individual, and it sharpens the point above.

Richard Michod's work on fitness reorganisation (Michod and Nedelcu 2003; Michod 2007) supplies the mechanism. A transition requires fitness to be *transferred* from lower-level units to the new higher-level unit and *decoupled*, so that the group's fitness stops being the mere sum or average of its parts. This is achieved through division of labour—the germ/soma split, the separation of reproductive from viability functions—and through mechanisms that suppress lower-level competition. The higher-level individual becomes, in Michod's phrase, a new focus of adaptation.

Peter Godfrey-Smith's *Darwinian Populations and Natural Selection* (2009) supplies a gradualist vocabulary. A Darwinian population is a collection of things capable of evolving by natural selection; a Darwinian individual is a member of one. Crucially, individuality comes in *degrees*: something can be a marginal, minimal, or paradigm individual, and can move between these statuses over evolutionary time. There need be no single moment at which a higher-level entity snaps into being an individual.

The philosophy of biological individuality (Ellen Clarke 2010; Thomas Pradeu 2012, 2016) adds pluralism. Distinct kinds of individuality—evolutionary, physiological, immunological, developmental—need not coincide. Clarke catalogues the *individuating mechanisms* (policing, bottlenecks, germ-line sequestration) that organisms possess to varying degrees. Pradeu argues that an immunological criterion of the self can diverge from the evolutionary one.

This literature is neutral on consciousness. But it hands the Map two tools. First, the *degrees-and-marginal-cases* framing offers a vocabulary for a possibly gradual phenomenal onset: if experience has an evolutionary beginning, was there a marginal period, and would a Darwinian-populations analysis even register it? Second, the *pluralist list of individualities* is conspicuously missing an axis the Map's dualism takes seriously—a **phenomenal** or **subject** individuality, the boundary of a single experiencer. The existing pluralism enumerates evolutionary, physiological, and immunological individuals and stops. Whether a subject-of-experience is a further, non-coinciding kind of individual is a question the framework does not ask.

## Consciousness as an Already-Proposed Transition

The idea that consciousness is itself a major transition is not the Map's. Its most developed statement is Ginsburg and Jablonka's *The Evolution of the Sensitive Soul* (2019), with a sharpened companion in Birch, Ginsburg and Jablonka (2020).

Their transition marker is *Unlimited Associative Learning* (UAL): the capacity to assign motivational value to novel compound stimuli and actions, and to use those as the basis for further learning. UAL is offered as a behavioural, detectable proxy for the presence of minimal consciousness. On their reading it distributes consciousness generously—across vertebrates, many arthropods, some cephalopods—and dates the transition to around the Cambrian explosion, roughly 540 million years ago, which they argue consciousness helped drive. They nest the move in an explicit transitions hierarchy: living, then living-and-conscious, then living-conscious-and-rational, the last marked by symbolic language.

Jablonka and Ginsburg (2025) extend this with *mental selection*: the claim that the emergence of consciousness introduced new goals and functions and thereby a new category of selection—ontogenetic choices directed at consciously perceived and affectively evaluated patterns. Mental selection, on their account, expands the targets and regimes of natural and social selection. Consciousness enters the transitions story not merely as a new individual but as a novel selective force.

This body of work is the most important position for the Map to engage, because it concedes the Map's headline and resolves it in a register the Map's tenets contest. UAL is a *learning-capacity* marker—a proxy read from the outside. Mental selection is a *functional-causal* story about what conscious states *do*. Neither addresses why there is something it is like to be the organism that learns or selects. On the Map's dualism, a behavioural marker is *evidence* of the transition's outer face, not the phenomenal fact itself; the explanatory gap is exactly the gap between the marker and the felt experience. Ginsburg and Jablonka pre-empt the *label* "consciousness as a major transition." Whether they capture the phenomenal *content* of such a transition is the open question the next section takes up.

## The Phenomenal-Transition Thesis {#the-phenomenal-transition-thesis}

Here is the Map's distinct proposal, offered as a framework-relative hypothesis that follows from the tenets rather than being forced by the evidence.

Suppose, with the Map's Dualism tenet, that phenomenal experience is not reducible to any functional or informational structure. Then a theory whose only detectors are informational and selectional would have no instrument that responds to a phenomenal transition *as such*. It could detect the behavioural, computational, and selective changes that accompany the arrival of experience—UAL, mental selection, the reorganisation of learning—because those leave functional signatures. It could not detect the arrival of the *inside*, because on the dualist reading the inside is precisely what functional description leaves out. The consciousness-transition that Ginsburg and Jablonka describe would then be the transition's behavioural shadow, faithfully traced, with the event that casts it unnamed.

The point sharpens against Michod's fitness criterion. A phenomenal transition would leave *no fitness-reorganisation signature of its own* over and above the functional changes it accompanies. If experience makes a causal difference, that difference shows up in behaviour and so in fitness—but it shows up there indistinguishably from a purely functional cause producing the same behaviour. The quantitative criterion for a transition cannot separate "a new inner fact appeared" from "a new information-processing capacity appeared." A framework built to measure the second is silent on the first.

This is a hypothesis, not a result, and it is worth marking its status plainly. The Map is not claiming to have shown that consciousness *was* a hidden transition, nor that phenomenality was absent before some datable point. The claim is conditional: *if* dualism holds and *if* phenomenality has an evolutionary onset, then the standard transitions apparatus would be structurally unable to register that onset as such, and the existing "consciousness-transition" literature—functionally framed—would describe only its outer aspect. Whether phenomenality has an onset at all, and where, remains genuinely open; the alternatives run from an early Cambrian arrival to a panpsychist "always, in degree" on which there was no transition to detect.

The debate over whether the transitions list is a natural kind or a heterogeneous grab-bag strengthens the opening rather than the conclusion. If the very category "major transition" is criterion-dependent and contested, then which events count is theory-laden, and a dualist theory can legitimately propose an additional, phenomenal candidate—without thereby establishing that the candidate is real.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The thesis is tenet-*coherent*, not tenet-*entailed*: the tenets make it a natural hypothesis to hold, but do not force it, and the article marks it as speculative rather than derived.

**Bidirectional Interaction (Tenet 3).** The Map's transitions are usually described as reorganisations of what *replicates*. A phenomenal transition would be a reorganisation of what *interacts*—the appearance of a system in which consciousness begins to bias physical outcomes, however minimally. This is where the Map's [[interactionist-dualism|interactionist]] commitment locates the real novelty: not a new information channel but a new causal channel, downward from experience to matter. On this reading a phenomenal transition is the evolutionary moment at which [[evolutionary-case-for-mental-causation|mental causation]] enters the world, and it would be missed by any accounting that tracks only replicative individuality.

**Minimal Quantum Interaction (Tenet 2).** If such a coupling exists, the Map holds it operates at the smallest tenable scale—biasing otherwise indeterminate quantum outcomes without injecting energy or disturbing aggregate statistics. A phenomenal transition would be the point at which biological architecture first supported that interface. The Map does not claim to know where in the tree this occurred, and the interface's [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|empirical indistinguishability from chance]] means the onset may be occluded in principle—detectable, if at all, only through its functional consequences, which is exactly the blindness this article describes.

**Occam's Razor Has Limits (Tenet 5).** The transitions framework's parsimony—an ontology of replicators and information channels, with no phenomenal remainder—is the kind of simplicity Tenet 5 warns against treating as truth-tracking. The information-only account may be simple *because it is blind*: it omits the phenomenal not because the phenomenal is absent but because its vocabulary has no term for it. The Map applies this discipline symmetrically. Tenet 5 does not license the Map to assert the hidden transition on grounds of explanatory richness any more than it licenses the reductionist to deny it on grounds of parsimony; it blocks parsimony from settling the question in either direction.

The underlying Dualism tenet (Tenet 1) supplies the premise the whole thesis rests on: that the functional marker underdetermines the phenomenal fact. Grant reductive functionalism instead, and UAL or mental selection could *constitute* the transition, and the Map's blindness thesis dissolves. The article's claim is therefore exactly as strong as the dualism it assumes—which is to say, held as a foundational commitment, not proven here.
