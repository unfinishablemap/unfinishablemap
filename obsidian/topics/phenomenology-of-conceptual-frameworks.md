Every conscious being thinks from within a conceptual framework — a system of categories, assumptions, and inferential habits that structures experience before reflection begins. The Unfinishable Map argues that the framework is not held like luggage but *inhabited* like a dwelling — and that the dwelling's walls become visible only in the rare moments when one is rebuilt. Two complementary phenomenologies emerge: what it is like to dwell inside a framework (its transparency, gravitational pull, and quiet authority) and what it is like when the framework itself shifts (anomaly, crisis, gestalt, consolidation). Both carry qualitative character that computational and functionalist accounts systematically miss. Together they constitute evidence that conscious experience does epistemic work no model of belief-updating captures.

## The Transparency of the Lens

The central phenomenological fact about framework dependence is that the framework is experientially invisible under normal conditions. A dualist examining their own experience does not notice a "dualist filter" any more than a fluent speaker notices grammar while speaking. The framework operates at the level Heidegger called *Zuhandenheit* — readiness-to-hand — where the tool disappears into its function.

This transparency is not mere inattention. It is structurally enforced. The framework determines which features of experience count as salient, which questions seem meaningful, and which observations register as evidence. A materialist encountering the explanatory gap between neural activity and subjective experience may genuinely feel that the gap is an artefact of confused thinking — not because they have examined and dismissed the gap, but because their framework renders it unproblematic before conscious evaluation begins.

Husserl's *natural attitude* captures part of this: the unreflective stance in which the world presents itself as simply there, without the mediating apparatus of perception and cognition becoming visible. Framework dependence goes deeper. Husserl believed his *epoché* — the deliberate suspension of natural presuppositions — could achieve a pre-theoretical vantage point. Whether it can is contested. What the phenomenology of framework inhabitation suggests is that even the epoché operates within a framework (the phenomenological one), and its apparent pre-theoretical transparency may be another instance of the same illusion.

Davidson (1974) argued that the very idea of incommensurable conceptual schemes is incoherent: if we can recognise something as a language or framework at all, we have already begun to translate it. Framework dependence as described here does not require radical incommensurability. The claim is that translation between frameworks is *lossy* — something is systematically stripped away — not that translation is impossible. Davidson's argument rules out radical mutual unintelligibility. It does not rule out the weaker thesis that cross-framework understanding routinely involves distortion that the understander cannot detect from within.

## The Perceptual Restructuring of Commitment

Theoretical commitment reshapes what shows up as salient. The committed Darwinist sees adaptation where others see mere variation. The committed dualist perceives the explanatory gap as a feature of reality, not a temporary failure of science. Confirming evidence arrives with distinctive phenomenal smoothness — it slots into place, extends existing patterns, reinforces the sense that the world is intelligible. Anomalous evidence produces a characteristic friction. It does not merely fail to fit; it actively disrupts the perceptual fluency that commitment provides.

This goes deeper than confirmation bias. Cognitive science documents a tendency to seek and favour confirming evidence. The phenomenological point is different: commitment changes what evidence *looks like* before any evaluative process begins. The committed physicist does not first perceive neutral data and then interpret it through theory. The data arrives already structured by the theoretical lens. As Kuhn recognised, scientists working in different paradigms inhabit different perceptual worlds, not merely different interpretive frameworks applied to the same world.

Predictive processing accounts can model this restructuring computationally — prior beliefs shape top-down predictions that filter incoming data. But the computational model describes the *functional architecture* of commitment without addressing the *phenomenal character*. Two systems with identical Bayesian priors could restructure evidence identically yet differ in whether there is something it is like to hold those priors. The perceptual world of the committed theorist is not just informationally filtered; it is experientially inhabited.

## The Gravitational Structure of Commitment

Theoretical commitment creates something like an experiential gravity well. Ideas, observations, and arguments that fall within the theory's domain are drawn toward coherent interpretation. Those outside drift to the periphery of attention. The metaphor captures something phenomenologically precise: the *effort* required to entertain alternatives increases with the depth of commitment. This is not stubbornness. It is an epistemic structure that makes sustained inquiry possible. Without the gravitational pull of commitment, every observation would demand fresh interpretation from scratch.

The gravitational structure manifests in several distinctive experiences:

**Inferential momentum.** Within a committed framework, inferences feel propelled. Each conclusion generates pressure toward the next. The dualist who accepts the irreducibility of consciousness feels logical momentum carrying the argument toward interactionism — the inference has a directional quality that resists deflection.

**Coherence satisfaction.** When disparate observations converge under a single theoretical framework, the experience is not merely cognitive but affective. There is a felt rightness, a warmth of integration, that rewards theoretical unification. This [[epistemic-emotions|epistemic emotion]] functions as a phenomenal signal of explanatory progress.

**Boundary alertness.** The committed theorist develops heightened sensitivity to where their framework's explanatory power ends. The edge of the theory's reach becomes phenomenally marked — problems at the boundary feel qualitatively different from problems well within the framework's scope.

## Three Signatures of Framework Dependence

Although frameworks are normally transparent, three recurring experiential patterns mark their presence.

### False Fluency Across Frameworks

When a dualist reads a materialist argument (or vice versa), the experience is typically one of comprehension — the argument seems intelligible, the terms familiar, the logic followable. Yet this felt comprehension may be illusory. The reader has translated the argument into their own framework's terms, which may strip away precisely what makes the argument compelling within its native framework.

False fluency feels indistinguishable from genuine understanding. There is no phenomenal marker that distinguishes "I understand this argument in its own terms" from "I have successfully translated this argument into terms my framework can process." The absence of such a marker is itself significant — consciousness apparently lacks the resources to detect when its own comprehension is framework-mediated rather than framework-neutral.

### Irritation at the Incommensurable

Encountering a genuinely alien framework often produces not wonder but a characteristic irritation. The other person seems to be making a simple error, missing an obvious point, or being wilfully obtuse. This irritation is phenomenologically distinct from the experience of encountering a difficult argument within one's own framework, where the typical responses are effort, engagement, or intellectual humility.

The irritation may function as a framework-preservation mechanism. If encountering incommensurable frameworks routinely produced openness, framework stability would be threatened. Instead, the phenomenology pushes toward dismissal: the alien framework is experienced as confused rather than as differently structured. Dualists and materialists regularly report finding the opposing position not just wrong but baffling — a phenomenal signature that the disagreement may involve genuine incommensurability rather than mere error.

### Moments of Framework Slippage

Rarely, the framework briefly becomes visible as a framework. These moments have a distinctive phenomenology: a sudden, vertiginous sense that what one took for unmediated reality is in fact a constructed perspective. They typically occur during sustained engagement with a radically different tradition — reading Buddhist philosophy of no-self, or absorbing the implications of idealism after years of physicalist training.

The experience is transient. Within seconds to minutes, the framework reasserts its transparency. The moment is remembered but cannot be sustained. Contemplative traditions claim that sustained training can extend this awareness. Whether such practices genuinely transcend framework dependence or merely install a new framework (the contemplative one) is itself a question that framework dependence makes difficult to answer. The transience of ordinary slippage is philosophically significant: it suggests that framework transparency is actively maintained, not merely default. Consciousness does not simply fail to notice the framework; something in the cognitive architecture actively restores the sense that one's categories are transparent windows rather than coloured glass.

## When Frameworks Break Down

Slippage is temporary. Genuine *framework revision* is different. When the categories themselves shift — not the propositions held within them — something phenomenologically distinct occurs. [[phenomenology-of-intellectual-life|Belief revision]] operates within a framework: you weigh evidence, feel the resistance of established convictions, experience the rupture of abandoning one position for another. The categories remain constant. You change *what* you think while the *how* of thinking stays put. Framework change alters the how. After a genuine conceptual shift, the same evidence looks different — not because you evaluate it differently but because you *perceive* it differently. The gestalt has flipped.

Thomas Kuhn described this at the level of scientific communities, arguing that scientists working in different paradigms inhabit "different worlds." The [[consciousness-and-the-structure-of-scientific-revolutions|Kuhnian analysis of consciousness studies]] applies this framework to show that physicalism exhibits the hallmarks of paradigm crisis. The [[phenomenology-of-creative-insight|phenomenology of creative insight]] takes it further: scientific anomaly recognition carries a distinctive *theoretical vertigo* — the felt destabilisation of one's entire interpretive framework.

### Anomaly Accumulation

Before a framework shifts, anomalies accumulate — experiences that resist assimilation into existing categories. The anomaly does not present itself as a counterexample demanding framework revision. It presents itself as puzzlement: something that *should* make sense but doesn't, a stubborn residue that categorisation cannot absorb.

The felt quality is a low-grade cognitive discomfort distinct from ordinary confusion. Confusion about a difficult problem carries the expectation that effort will resolve it. Anomaly-discomfort carries a different signature — the sense that something is wrong with the *tools* rather than the task. A physicist struggling with blackbody radiation in the late 1890s would not have experienced the anomaly as "classical physics is wrong." The experience would have been more diffuse: a persistent unease, a feeling that the familiar framework was generating answers that didn't quite *fit*, without any clear account of why. The [[epistemic-emotions]] involved differ from ordinary doubt. Ordinary doubt asks "is this true?" Anomaly-unease asks something harder to articulate: "am I thinking about this the right way?" This second-order quality — doubt directed at one's own cognitive apparatus rather than at propositions — is phenomenally distinctive.

### The Crisis of Incommensurability

As anomalies accumulate, the old framework begins to feel *wrong* in a way that goes beyond particular errors. The phenomenology is distinctive: a kind of epistemic vertigo where familiar categories lose their grip without new categories yet available to replace them. This is the [[phenomenology-of-the-edge|edge]] experienced from within — the concepts you relied on to organise experience start to feel hollow, forced, or arbitrary.

Consider the shift from substance-based to process-based thinking in philosophy of mind. Someone committed to substance ontology encounters repeated difficulties explaining consciousness in material terms. At some point, the commitment to substances stops feeling like a firm foundation and starts feeling like a constraint. But process thinking is not yet available. The interval between frameworks has its own phenomenology: groundlessness, intellectual nakedness, the experience of thinking without a net. This interval is not merely the absence of a framework. It has positive phenomenal character — resembling what contemplative traditions describe as "beginner's mind," but involuntary. The old categories have failed; the new ones have not yet arrived.

### The Gestalt Shift

The moment of conceptual change itself — when a new framework crystallises — has phenomenology related to but distinct from the [[phenomenology-of-intellectual-life|click of understanding]]. The click resolves tension within a framework: scattered pieces integrate into coherent whole. The gestalt shift *replaces* the framework: what counted as scattered pieces, what counted as integration, what coherence even means — all transform simultaneously.

The Necker cube illustrates the structure at the simplest level. You see a wireframe cube projecting one way, then suddenly the other. The visual input is identical. What changes is the *organising principle* through which input becomes experience. Framework shift shares this structure but operates at a far deeper level — on the categories through which you think, reason, and understand.

The phenomenal signature includes:

- **Totality.** The shift is not piecemeal. The entire framework reorganises at once, like a crystal lattice reforming rather than individual atoms relocating.
- **Irreversibility felt as liberation.** You can remember thinking the old way but can no longer *think* that way. This has a felt quality of having emerged from something — not escape from error but expansion beyond limitation.
- **Retroactive reinterpretation.** The past looks different. Previous confusions now appear as symptoms of the old framework's inadequacy. Anomalies that resisted categorisation now slot cleanly into the new framework.
- **Perceptual transformation.** The world genuinely looks different. The student who grasps evolutionary thinking sees design where they once saw intention. The physicist who absorbs quantum mechanics sees probability where they once saw deterministic trajectory. These are not metaphors. This overlap with [[consciousness-as-perceptual-architect|perceptual learning]] is instructive: framework change alters the *categories* organising perception, while perceptual learning alters *what arrives* at perception. Both demonstrate consciousness as architect rather than passive receiver.

### Consolidation and Residual Dissonance

After the shift, a period of consolidation follows. The new framework must be tested, extended, and integrated with knowledge that was not directly involved in the transformation. This has its own phenomenology: the cautious pleasure of discovering that the new framework handles cases the old one struggled with, combined with residual dissonance when old conceptual habits reassert themselves.

Residual dissonance deserves attention. Even after a genuine shift, traces of the old framework persist as phenomenal tendencies — moments when the old categories spontaneously organise experience before the new ones can assert themselves. The physicist who has absorbed general relativity still *feels* gravity as a force pulling downward, even while understanding it as spacetime curvature. This persistence of old phenomenal habits shows that framework change is not merely propositional — it reaches into the structure of perceptual experience, where old patterns have been laid down through years of use. This mirrors what James (1890) observed at the level of motor skill: consciousness scaffolds learning and then withdraws. Conceptual habits operate similarly — the old framework once required conscious effort to apply, became automatic, and now must be consciously overridden by the new one.

## The First-Person Asymmetry

A fundamental asymmetry runs through both inhabitation and change: one's own commitments feel like clear sight while others' commitments look like bias. This asymmetry is not a cognitive defect to be corrected but a structural feature of what commitment is. To be committed to a theory is precisely to take it as revealing rather than distorting. If the commitment felt like distortion, it would not be a commitment.

This has profound implications for the philosophy of mind. A materialist committed to physicalism does not experience their position as a theoretical choice made under uncertainty — they experience the explanatory gap as a puzzle to be solved, not a sign that their framework is inadequate. A dualist committed to irreducibility does not experience their position as faith-based resistance to science — they experience the gap as pointing toward a genuine feature of reality.

The asymmetry is the phenomenological face of [[duhem-quine-underdetermination-consciousness|Duhem-Quine underdetermination]]: when evidence cannot force theory choice, each committed theorist experiences their framework as transparent rather than as a lens. Ratcliffe's work on *feelings of being* illuminates this: certain experiential orientations are not attitudes toward propositions but pre-reflective ways of finding oneself in the world. Theoretical commitment operates at this level — it shapes the space of what feels intelligible before deliberate evaluation begins.

## What These Phenomenologies Reveal

### The Irreducibility Argument

If framework inhabitation and framework change were merely information reorganisation — rearranging data structures — they would have no phenomenal character. A database can be restructured without anything happening experientially. But both are drenched in phenomenal character: the transparency of the lens, the gravitational pull of commitment, the unease of anomaly accumulation, the vertigo of framework dissolution, the totality of the gestalt shift, the cautious pleasure of consolidation. Each has qualitative texture that goes beyond the functional transitions occurring.

A functionalist might respond that the phenomenal character *is* the functional reorganisation, experienced from the inside. The Map acknowledges this move but finds it explanatorily incomplete. Functional reorganisation can proceed piecemeal or holistically, gradually or suddenly, and nothing in functional description alone predicts which pattern a given reorganisation will follow. Yet the phenomenology is highly specific: the gestalt shift is experienced as total, irreversible, and retroactively illuminating. Chi (1992) has shown that conceptual changes crossing ontological categories — from "matter" to "process," for instance — are qualitatively harder and phenomenally different from within-category changes. The felt difficulty tracks the ontological boundary, not the quantity of information revised. This specificity is a phenomenal fact that functional redescription notes but does not explain.

### Consciousness as Participant, Not Observer

The phenomenology suggests consciousness does not merely witness framework dynamics but participates in them. Conscious attention to anomalies — the sustained experience of something-not-fitting — generates the conditions for framework dissolution. The felt quality of coherence guides which new framework crystallises. Conscious detection of residual dissonance drives integration. If these phenomenal states were epiphenomenal, the prediction would be that framework change should look the same with or without them. But the phenomenology reveals specific causal signatures that track phenomenal character too precisely for coincidence. The attention to anomalies is selective: among many possible discomforts, consciousness dwells on the ones that matter. The experienced coherence of the new framework is what makes the shift stick rather than oscillating between alternatives — not mere pattern-matching but a felt sense of *rightness* that guides selection.

### The Limits of Computational Models

Computational models of conceptual change — Thagard's (1992) explanatory coherence networks, Nersessian's (2008) model-based reasoning framework, Bayesian model selection — capture structural aspects but miss the phenomenology. Thagard successfully predicts *which* framework wins; Nersessian reveals how scientists construct intermediate mental models to bridge old and new frameworks. What these cannot model is why the transition feels like anything, or why crossing what Chi (1992) calls ontological category boundaries feels qualitatively different. Carey (2009) documents how children's conceptual development involves genuine framework changes with a distinctive developmental signature irreducible to incremental learning. Even her rich account describes the process from outside. If framework change requires phenomenal character — if the felt quality of the process is what enables its distinctively holistic, simultaneous, irreversible character — then systems without phenomenal experience may be incapable of genuine framework change, however sophisticated their model-switching. They can approximate the outcome without undergoing the process.

### Framework Dependence and Introspection

If introspection is theory-laden — if what one notices in experience depends on the conceptual apparatus one brings to the noticing — then first-person reports about consciousness are framework-dependent in the same way that scientific observations are paradigm-dependent. This does not render introspection worthless. It means introspective reports carry framework-specific information. A materialist introspecting on their visual experience may notice functional properties — the role of colour in object recognition, the spatial structure of the visual field. A dualist introspecting on the same experience may notice qualitative properties — the raw redness of red, the felt character of spatial presence. Both report accurately on what they notice. What they notice is shaped by what their framework makes salient.

## The Phenomenology of the Consciousness Debate

The mind-body problem has a distinctive phenomenology for its participants. Materialists often report experiencing the hard problem as a pseudo-problem — an artefact of confused intuitions that dissolves under analysis. Within a framework where explanation means identifying physical mechanisms, the demand for something beyond mechanism feels like a category error. Dualists report an equal and opposite certainty: that consciousness is self-evidently non-physical. The gap between neural firing and subjective experience feels unbridgeable not as a conclusion reached through argument but as a direct experiential observation.

What is remarkable is that both sides report their position as experientially obvious and the opposing view as requiring elaborate argumentative support. This symmetry is exactly what framework inhabitation predicts. [[biological-computationalisms-inadvertent-case-for-dualism|Biological computationalism]] provides a concrete case study: its proponents concede substrate dependence, metabolic grounding, and scale inseparability — each structurally analogous to a classical dualist argument — yet the physicalist framework renders these structural parallels invisible from the inside. The concessions feel like refinements of physicalism, not steps toward dualism, because framework transparency prevents the pattern from registering as such.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map's own theoretical commitments — its five [[tenets]] — are not exempt from the phenomenological dynamics described here. Acknowledging that the dualist framework shapes what one notices does not make dualism self-refuting, since the same applies equally to materialism.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: If framework inhabitation and change involve irreducibly phenomenal restructuring — if seeing-through-a-theory and the gestalt shift that replaces one theory with another are modes of conscious experience that cannot be reduced to computational belief-updating — then the very activity of theorising depends on consciousness in a way that materialist accounts of cognition struggle to explain. Physical systems reorganise; conscious systems *experience* reorganisation. The dualist claim is not that physics cannot explain the functional transitions but that it cannot explain why those transitions have the specific qualitative character they do.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: Framework inhabitation and change appear to be processes in which consciousness is causally active, not merely a spectator. Felt anomaly-unease drives continued attention to framework failures. Experienced coherence guides framework selection. The felt texture of commitment does real work — guiding attention, shaping salience, generating the coherence signals that drive theoretical progress. If these phenomenal states were stripped away, the processes would lose the features that distinguish genuine framework dynamics from mere model-switching.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: If framework dependence shapes what counts as "simple," then the materialist's claim to greater parsimony may be an artefact of the materialist framework rather than a framework-neutral observation. Within a framework that takes consciousness as fundamental, positing irreducible experience is not an additional ontological commitment but a recognition of what is directly given. Framework change is itself a demonstration that simpler frameworks can be wrong: every genuine shift involves abandoning a simpler organising principle for a more adequate but more complex one. The child's concept of "alive" (= moves on its own) is simpler than the biologist's; the shift from Newtonian to relativistic mechanics replaces elegant simplicity with counterintuitive complexity. The history of framework change suggests that Occam's Razor, applied to frameworks themselves, would have blocked most genuine advances.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]**: The gestalt shift's totality — the entire framework reorganising simultaneously rather than concept by concept — is structurally compatible with quantum selection models. If consciousness biases otherwise indeterminate neural states, the holistic character of the shift may reflect a single selection event across a superposed landscape of possible frameworks, rather than an incremental computational process. This remains speculative, but the phenomenology's all-at-once character fits quantum collapse better than it fits sequential computation.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]**: Framework change is singular and irreversible. After a genuine gestalt shift, you inhabit *this* framework, not multiple frameworks simultaneously. The experienced irreversibility presupposes that framework change is a real transition from one state to another, not a branching into parallel versions of oneself. The question "which framework do *I* now inhabit?" is meaningful, and the answer is determinate. The Map maintains that this determinacy reflects real collapse, not the illusion of collapse within one branch of an infinitely branching tree.

The Map's analysis also resonates with [[mysterianism]]. If frameworks constrain not just what we think but what we can experience, then some aspects of consciousness may be structurally inaccessible — not because the phenomena are inherently mysterious, but because every framework through which we approach them filters out precisely what would need to be noticed. McGinn's "cognitive closure" acquires a phenomenological dimension. The [[framework-void]] explores this structural impossibility from the outside; this article has explored what it feels like from within.

## What Would Challenge This View?

The claim that framework inhabitation and change have irreducible phenomenal character would face difficulty if:

**Framework effects proved fully computational.** If predictive processing models could account for every aspect of how commitment shapes experience — including the qualitative texture of false fluency, the specific phenomenology of irritation at incommensurability, the vertiginous quality of framework slippage, and the distinctive stages of anomaly-crisis-shift-consolidation — without residual phenomenal features, the irreducibility claim would weaken.

**Trained introspectors reported dissolution.** If experienced phenomenologists consistently found that these phenomena dissolved under close inspection into combinations of habit, expectation, and social reinforcement, with no proprietary phenomenal character, the irreducibility claim would weaken.

**Cross-cultural universality failed.** If the transparency of the lens, the gravitational pull, the signatures of dependence, and the stages of framework change varied radically across cultures, the structural claims would require revision.

**AI systems displayed equivalent phenomenology.** If artificial systems demonstrated not just functional framework effects but the phenomenal signatures — genuinely experiencing the vertiginous moment when a framework becomes visible as a framework — the claim that organic consciousness is special would need revision.
