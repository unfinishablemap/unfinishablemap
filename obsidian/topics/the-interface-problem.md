If consciousness influences the physical world by biasing quantum outcomes in the brain — as The Unfinishable Map's [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] tenet holds — two distinct questions must be answered. *Where* in the brain does the interface operate? *How* do conscious states map quantitatively to physical selections? These are the two faces of the interface problem, and together they constitute the most significant open challenge facing the Map's interactionist framework.

The two questions are logically independent. Knowing the location does not specify the mapping; knowing the mapping requires knowing the location. The Map can say *that* consciousness acts on matter ([[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]), *where* it acts in broad terms (at quantum indeterminacies), and *through what neural architecture* (the unified [[attention-and-the-consciousness-interface|attention-motor interface]]). What remains is the precise location among candidate sites and the lawful mapping from phenomenal properties to physical selections — what Chalmers (1996, p. 214) calls the "t-shirt problem": the absence of psychophysical laws compact and precise enough to fit on a t-shirt, as F=ma can. The problem is a descendant of [[history-of-the-interaction-problem|Princess Elisabeth's challenge]] to Descartes.

The Map's position has evolved on the location half. Initial agnosticism among four candidate sites has given way to *constrained pluralism* — the interface operates through the attention-motor architecture, with molecular-level quantum effects providing the physical substrate. The specification half remains genuinely open, with two partial accounts and three qualitative sketches.

## Why Both Questions Matter

The interface problem is not a detail to be filled in later. It determines what the framework actually predicts.

**Empirical testability.** Different candidate sites generate different experimental predictions. Anesthetics targeting tubulin should disrupt consciousness selectively if microtubules host the interface; ion channel quantum tunnelling rates should correlate with decision outcomes if those host it. Without specifying both site and mapping, the framework risks making no distinctive empirical predictions.

**Mechanism constraints.** Each candidate site faces different [[decoherence]] timescales, different relationships to neural computation, and different connections to the [[attention-as-interface|attention system]]. A mechanism that works for microtubules may fail for ion channels. The mapping from mental variables to physical effects depends on what is being mapped onto.

**Scope of influence.** The location constrains what consciousness can affect; the specification constrains how strongly. If the interface operates at individual synapses with a steeply-thresholded transfer function, consciousness might influence fine-grained patterns. If it operates at cortical columns with a smooth bias function, it selects among coarser alternatives — consistent with the ~10 bits/second [[psychophysical-laws|bandwidth constraint]].

# Part 1: Where Does the Interface Operate?

Four primary candidate sites have been proposed, each grounded in different theoretical traditions.

## The Four Candidates

### Microtubules (Orch OR)

Roger Penrose and Stuart Hameroff propose that consciousness interfaces with the brain through quantum computations in microtubules — cylindrical protein structures within neurons. On their Orchestrated Objective Reduction theory, tubulin proteins exist in quantum superposition, with consciousness selecting which superposition pattern collapses. Collapse events occur at roughly 40 per second, in concert with gamma-band (40 Hz) neural synchrony.

**Strengths.** A 2024 epothilone B study found that microtubule-stabilising drugs delayed anaesthetic-induced unconsciousness in rats — consistent with an Orch OR prediction, though also explicable by non-quantum accounts of microtubule function. Hagan et al. (2002) proposed revised decoherence estimates extending coherence times to 10⁻⁵–10⁻⁴ seconds, though contested and unreplicated. Anaesthetics target microtubules, providing a direct link between the proposed site and the loss of consciousness.

**Difficulties.** The decoherence timescale problem is severe. Even optimistic revised estimates of 10⁻⁴ seconds are shorter than the millisecond timescales at which neural processes operate, and Tegmark and others argue the original calculations stand. Penrose's reliance on gravitational self-collapse introduces physics beyond the standard model. The relationship between microtubule-level events and the coarse-grained selections consciousness appears to make remains underspecified.

### Ion Channels (Stapp/Schwartz)

Henry Stapp proposes that consciousness operates through quantum effects at ion channels — narrow protein pores (less than 1 nanometre at their narrowest) through which ions cross neural membranes. At this scale, quantum uncertainty applies. Schwartz, Stapp, and Beauregard (2005) argue that "owing to certain structural features of ion channels critical to synaptic function, contemporary physical theory must in principle be used when analysing human brain dynamics."

**Strengths.** Ion channels are uncontroversially quantum-mechanical at their narrowest points. The proposal connects directly to synaptic function. Stapp's quantum Zeno mechanism provides a specific account of how consciousness might hold desired channel states through repeated observation. Schwartz's OCD neuroplasticity research demonstrates that directed mental effort can systematically reshape neural circuits — establishing that attention affects neural structure, though not that quantum mechanisms are involved.

**Difficulties.** The Zeno mechanism requires observation rates at femtosecond timescales while attention operates at ~100ms timescales — a gap of roughly ten orders of magnitude. Monte Carlo simulations (Litt et al. 2006) found the quantum Zeno effect breaks down for timescales exceeding decoherence time in simplified models, though Stapp contested their model as too simple. The mechanism for coordinating individual ion channel quantum events into coherent macroscopic decisions remains unspecified.

### Cortical Microcolumns

A more recent proposal suggests that cortical microcolumns — ensembles of roughly 100 neurons — couple resonantly with the quantum electromagnetic zero-point field, forming macroscopic coherence domains. Consciousness selects among patterns at the column level rather than at individual molecular sites.

**Strengths.** The scale matches the [[psychophysical-laws|bandwidth constraint]]: column-level selection makes the ~10 bits/second limit intelligible. Microcolumns are well-characterised functional units in established neuroscience. Column-scale selection would explain why conscious influence feels coarse-grained.

**Difficulties.** The proposal is less developed than Orch OR or Stapp's ion channel account. The mechanism by which cortical columns achieve quantum coherence is speculative; zero-point field coupling at cortical scales has no established experimental support. The general decoherence objection is amplified by the larger spatial scale.

### Attention Networks (Functional Level)

Rather than identifying a specific molecular site, some formulations locate the interface at the functional level of attention networks themselves — the frontoparietal systems that implement directed focus. The relevant question is not "which molecules?" but "which neural systems?", with the answer: whatever systems implement [[attention-as-interface|willed attention]].

**Strengths.** The 2025 COGITATE adversarial testing found frontoparietal attention networks to be distinct from the posterior "hot zone" where conscious content appears to reside. This suggests the interface may be where these systems meet. The approach avoids premature commitment to a specific molecular mechanism. Willed attention shows distinctive neural signatures (frontal theta, bidirectional frontoparietal coherence) that distinguish it from automatic processing.

**Difficulties.** Without a molecular mechanism, the proposal is incomplete. "Consciousness interfaces through attention networks" pushes the question one level down: which specific processes *within* attention networks are quantum-sensitive? A functional-level account must eventually be grounded in physics to meet the [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] tenet's requirements.

## Five Constraints Any Answer Must Satisfy

Regardless of which candidate proves correct, any successful account must address:

**1. The decoherence constraint.** The proposed site must maintain quantum effects long enough for consciousness to influence outcomes. This requires either quantum coherence persisting on relevant timescales, or consciousness operating at the point of measurement even after decoherence has selected a preferred basis. The latter remains viable because [[decoherence]] explains why superpositions become unobservable but does not explain *which* outcome becomes actual — what the [[measurement-problem|measurement problem]] asks.

**2. The bandwidth match.** The interface must explain why conscious selection operates at roughly 10 bits per second. A molecular-level interface selecting among billions of quantum states per second would need an account of why conscious experience reflects only a tiny fraction of those selections.

**3. The attention connection.** The interface site must be within the scope of attentional modulation — influenced by the frontoparietal networks that implement directed focus.

**4. The developmental pairing.** The [[brain-interface-boundary|five criteria]] for a consciousness interface include developmental history. The location must develop *with* the organism, not be a fixed feature that could serve any consciousness equally.

**5. The phenomenal match.** Interface activity must correlate with the phenomenology of conscious effort and choice. Sites where selection occurs should show increased activity during effortful attention and reduced activity during automatic processing or loss of consciousness.

## From Agnosticism to Constrained Pluralism

Three developments have narrowed the field since the initial candidate survey.

**The attention-motor convergence.** The premotor theory of attention (Rizzolatti) proposes that attention and motor preparation share neural substrates — spatial attention *is* motor planning. If attention is motor planning, and consciousness acts through attention, then the interface location must be within the overlapping attention-motor architecture. The 2025 COGITATE adversarial collaboration reinforced this by distinguishing attention networks (frontoparietal) from consciousness networks (posterior "hot zone"). The interface location is where these systems meet — not in either alone.

**Dopamine and the threshold architecture.** A 2024 *Nature* study (Cai et al.) found that dopamine dynamics are dispensable for movement but promote reward responses — mice with disrupted phasic dopamine retain motor capacity but lose goal-directed initiative. Westbrook et al. (2023) showed that striatal dopamine regulates the decision threshold at which competing motor programmes resolve. The neural competition model (Cisek 2007) shows multiple action representations existing simultaneously in premotor cortex, competing until one crosses a threshold. Dopamine modulates that threshold; consciousness, on the Map's account, determines which option crosses first. The interface must operate wherever threshold-crossing events involve quantum indeterminacy.

**The developmental trajectory.** The [[consciousness-interface-development|lifespan analysis]] revealed that the interface undergoes systematic restructuring — wide aperture in infancy, narrowing through critical periods, peaking in structured control during adulthood, loosening again in age. Static molecular sites do not obviously explain why the interface character changes with developmental phase. Sites embedded in attention-motor architecture — which itself undergoes dramatic developmental restructuring — do.

These developments do not eliminate candidates so much as reorder them. The functional-level attention-motor account is **promoted**: the convergence research gives it anatomical specificity, dopamine research provides a competition mechanism, and the developmental trajectory matches frontoparietal maturation. **Ion channels remain viable** as the most physically specific account of how quantum effects might operate within the attention-motor architecture; the timescale objection persists. **Cortical microcolumns remain viable**, matching the bandwidth constraint, though the coherence mechanism is unsupported. **Microtubules are demoted**: Orch OR locates quantum effects inside individual neurons rather than at the architectural level where attention-motor competition occurs, and microtubules exist in all neurons throughout development without explaining why the interface character changes.

## The Hierarchical Model

The most plausible resolution is that the location problem has no single answer. Asking "where does a computer process information?" has no unique correct answer — processing occurs at transistors, gates, circuits, and software, each describing real computational work at different scales. Similarly, the consciousness-brain interface operates hierarchically:

- **Molecular level**: Quantum effects in ion channels or microtubules provide the physical openings where consciousness can influence outcomes
- **Cellular level**: These quantum effects aggregate into neural firing decisions
- **Network level**: Attention systems select among competing neural patterns, implementing the coarse-grained choices that reach awareness
- **Phenomenal level**: What consciousness experiences as "choosing" corresponds to network-level selection, not molecular-level quantum events

Constrained pluralism adds direction: the hierarchy should be understood as attention-motor architecture all the way down, with the molecular level providing the substrate. The picture is of nested selection: consciousness acts through the attention-motor system, which selects among competing patterns, which resolve through threshold-crossing events, which involve quantum indeterminacies at molecular sites. The emerging answer to "at which level does consciousness act?" is "at the functional level, through the molecular level."

# Part 2: How Does Consciousness Map to Physical Effects?

## What Specification Requires

A fully specified interface would answer four questions.

**1. Which mental variables?** Which aspects of conscious experience do the causal work? Candidates include attention (where focus is directed), intention (what action is willed), effort (how much engagement is exerted), and valence (felt goodness or badness). These are not equivalent — specifying the wrong variable would predict the wrong physical effects.

**2. Which physical parameters?** What does consciousness act on? Candidates include quantum measurement rate, probability distributions over outcomes, measurement basis selection, and collapse timing. The [[coupling-modes]] taxonomy distinguishes basis control (selecting the measurement question), timing control (selecting when collapse occurs), and probability control (reweighting outcome likelihoods). The [[consciousness-physics-interface-formalism]] defines the formal structure any answer must take: a coupling map C: E × P → P satisfying five mathematical constraints from quantum foundations.

**3. What is the transfer function?** How does a given mental state map quantitatively to a physical effect? More attention should produce more effect — but how much more? Linearly? With diminishing returns? With a threshold below which nothing happens? Without a transfer function, "consciousness biases outcomes" is a qualitative sketch, not a testable theory.

**4. What are the boundary conditions?** Under what circumstances does selection operate, and under what circumstances does it fail? The ~10 bits/second [[bandwidth-of-consciousness|bandwidth constraint]] suggests one boundary. Interface locality — the restriction of mental causation to the subject's own brain (related to the [[pairing-problem]]) — suggests another. A complete specification would derive these constraints rather than stipulating them.

## Partial Answers

Two candidate specifications have been developed to the point of generating predictions.

### Stapp's Attention-Observation Coupling

Stapp's quantum Zeno model is the most detailed specification available. The mapping: sustained voluntary attention increases the rate of quantum observations on neural systems, which through the Zeno effect holds desired patterns stable against their natural tendency to dissipate.

- **Mental variable**: Attention intensity
- **Physical parameter**: Observation frequency on neural quantum states
- **Transfer function**: Higher attention → higher observation rate → longer pattern persistence (monotonic; precise functional form unspecified)
- **Boundary conditions**: Only neural systems already within the subject's attentional scope; only genuinely quantum-indeterminate events

The proposal meets the basic adequacy standard — it specifies *which* phenomenal property affects *which* physical parameter through *what* mechanism. Schwartz's OCD research provides indirect support: patients who direct sustained attention to reframe intrusive thoughts show measurable changes in caudate metabolism. The 2019 Rajan et al. findings on theta-band oscillations during willed attention provide a neural signature distinguishing willed from instructed processing, consistent with (though not proving) a distinct causal mechanism. The limitations are the same Georgiev Monte Carlo objections that challenge the location half of the proposal.

### Eccles' Intention-Probability Coupling

John Eccles proposed that conscious intention modulates quantum tunneling probability in neurotransmitter vesicle release. The brain prepares multiple action possibilities; intention biases which patterns dominate by influencing whether vesicles release at specific synapses.

- **Mental variable**: Intention (what action is willed)
- **Physical parameter**: Quantum tunneling probability at synaptic vesicle release sites
- **Transfer function**: Stronger intention → higher release probability at synapses supporting the intended action
- **Boundary conditions**: Only vesicle release events at synapses in the relevant motor/attentional circuits

This proposal is less developed than Stapp's and faces the [[pairing-problem]] more directly — how does an intention "know" which synapses to bias? Stapp's model avoids this by working through attention, which already has a neural mechanism for targeting specific systems.

### Three Unspecified Candidates

Three further candidates remain at the qualitative stage:

- **[[valence|Valence]] → action selection**: Felt pleasure and pain bias approach/avoidance. Pain asymbolia (patients who register pain cognitively but lack the felt badness, and fail to avoid harm) supports the causal role of phenomenal valence. The [[valence-and-conscious-selection|valence-and-conscious-selection]] article develops the case that valence may serve as a selection *currency* — the common metric enabling comparison among incommensurable outcomes. No quantum mechanism has yet been proposed.
- **Qualia → basis selection**: Qualitative character might determine which quantum measurement basis applies. Speculative.
- **Phenomenal unity → sustained entanglement**: The binding of experience into a unified field might correspond to sustained quantum coherence. Correlation does not establish direction.

## Why Specification Is Hard

The specification problem may be structurally difficult in ways that distinguish it from ordinary scientific puzzles.

**The concept gap.** We may lack the right concepts. Physical laws relate physical quantities to each other — force to mass, energy to frequency. [[psychophysical-laws|Selection laws]] would relate phenomenal quantities to physical quantities. But phenomenal quantities resist the kind of measurement and mathematical formalisation that physical quantities permit. How much attention is "twice as much"? Is there a natural unit of effort? Psychophysics has scaling methods (Stevens' power law), but these relate physical inputs to behavioural outputs — the phenomenal middleman is inferred, not measured. A genuine selection law would need to quantify phenomenal states *as* phenomenal states.

**The access problem.** Even if selection laws exist, normal empirical methods may not reach them. The laws relate experience (accessed through first-person report) to quantum events (accessed through third-person measurement), but no instrument simultaneously tracks both with the precision needed. The ~10 bits/second bandwidth means introspective reports are coarse-grained; quantum measurement is destructive and alters what it measures. The [[epistemology-of-mechanism-at-the-consciousness-matter-interface|epistemology of mechanism]] explores what methods might reach the interface when direct observation is structurally impossible.

**The underdetermination problem.** Multiple specification schemes may be empirically equivalent. If consciousness biases quantum outcomes at rates below current detection (as Minimal Quantum Interaction suggests), we cannot distinguish between attention modulating observation rate (Stapp), intention modulating tunneling probability (Eccles), and combinations thereof. The proposals make different metaphysical claims but predict indistinguishable physical outcomes at accessible scales — underdetermination of mechanism, not unfalsifiability.

# How Location and Specification Interact

The two questions are formally independent but evidentially interlocked. Each candidate site implies different families of viable mappings.

If the interface operates at **microtubules**, only specifications compatible with sub-millisecond molecular dynamics can apply — pushing the mapping toward something like Orch OR's tubulin-superposition reduction with consciousness selecting collapse outcomes at 40Hz. Under **ion channels**, Stapp's quantum Zeno specification is the natural fit, since the location and specification were developed together. Under **cortical microcolumns**, the mapping must operate at a much coarser grain, selecting among column-level patterns — closer to Eccles' approach but at a different physical scale. Under **attention networks (functional)**, the specification problem is partly transposed onto whatever molecular mechanism actually implements selection.

This interlocking suggests a research strategy: progress on either half should constrain the other. Empirical evidence narrowing the location should eliminate incompatible specifications, and theoretical advances on specification should rule out sites that cannot host the relevant mapping. The Map currently has more to say about location (constrained pluralism) than about specification (two partial accounts plus three sketches), but this asymmetry may be temporary — the recent attention-motor convergence work cuts in both directions.

## The Critic's Strongest Objection

The interface problem is not just an internal challenge for dualism — its specification half provides the strongest objection from critics:

1. If consciousness influences physical outcomes, there must be specifiable laws governing that influence
2. No such laws have been specified despite decades of philosophical and scientific effort
3. The difficulty is best explained by the hypothesis that there is nothing to specify — consciousness does not influence physical outcomes

The Map's responses:

**Scientific youth.** Consciousness science is genuinely young. Serious empirical investigation dates to the 1990s. Expecting mature specification in three decades is unreasonable given that physics took centuries. This response is honest but risks indefinite deferral.

**Partial progress is real.** Stapp's and Eccles' models, whatever their limitations, demonstrate that specification is possible in principle. Their existence refutes the claim that specification is *impossible*.

**The alternative is worse.** If consciousness does not influence physical outcomes ([[concepts/epiphenomenalism]]), our beliefs about consciousness are causally disconnected from consciousness itself. The self-stultification problem makes epiphenomenalism epistemically self-defeating. An incomplete specification of a real phenomenon is preferable to a complete account that undermines its own rational basis.

**Difficulty is not evidence of absence.** Many real phenomena resist easy specification — turbulence, protein folding, the genotype-phenotype map. Specification difficulty is evidence of complexity, not nonexistence.

## Historical and Complexity Analogies

Defenders cite Newton: gravitational theory specified *that* masses attract and *how much* without explaining the mechanism, and was spectacularly successful for two centuries before general relativity provided spacetime curvature. Might selection laws be in a Newtonian phase?

The analogy has limits. Newton had precise measurements and a mathematical law. The specification problem lacks both. We are not in a Newtonian phase where the law is known but not the mechanism — we are in a pre-Newtonian phase where neither is established. The honest comparison is to Aristotelian physics before Kepler.

Biological systems offer a different analogy. The mapping from genotype to phenotype cannot be captured in a simple formula — it involves regulatory networks, epigenetic modifications, environmental interactions, and developmental contingency. Perhaps the interface is similarly complex: not a single law but a web of context-dependent relationships. This is more promising but raises its own problem. Complex biological relationships are still *physical* relationships, amenable to empirical investigation. If the interface involves fundamentally non-physical variables, the usual tools may not apply.

## What Would Count as Progress

The interface problem will not be solved all at once. Incremental progress would involve:

**Narrowing the mental variable.** Demonstrating empirically that one type of conscious state (attention, intention, effort, valence) correlates with physical selection while others do not. The willed attention research (Rajan et al.'s theta-band signatures) is a step in this direction.

**Constraining the physical parameter.** Identifying which quantum parameter is affected, even indirectly. Negative constraints already count: Torres Alegre's [[born-rule-and-the-consciousness-interface#The Causal Consistency Constraint|causal consistency result]] proves that any consciousness-mediated selection must follow the Born rule exactly, since deviation would enable superluminal signalling. This narrows the space of viable specifications without identifying the variable.

**Establishing bandwidth quantitatively.** The ~10 bits/second figure constrains selection capacity. Confirming and sharpening it narrows the space of possible transfer functions.

**Differential predictions between models.** If Stapp's and Eccles' models can be shown to predict *different* outcomes in some experimental paradigm, even at a statistical level, this advances specification even without confirming either.

**Developmental correlation.** Interface effectiveness should track the maturation of frontoparietal attention-motor systems, not microtubule abundance per se. The predicted developmental peak is in early adulthood.

**Disruption specificity.** Agents disrupting the attention-motor architecture (lesions to frontoparietal cortex, TMS to premotor regions) should disrupt conscious selection more specifically than agents disrupting general neural function.

**Theta-beta oscillatory signatures.** If the interface operates through attention-motor threshold-crossing, the transition from beta oscillations (13-30 Hz, movement suppression) to theta oscillations (4-10 Hz, prokinetic states and willed attention) should correlate with moments of conscious selection.

## What Would Falsify the Framework

The interface problem is resolvable because it makes testable predictions. If systematic experimental investigation rules out quantum effects at all four candidate sites — finding either that decoherence eliminates quantum indeterminacy at these sites or that disrupting them does not affect conscious influence — the [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] framework would face serious difficulty. The hierarchical possibility provides theoretical flexibility, but not unlimited flexibility. If no neural substrate can be found where (a) quantum indeterminacies persist on relevant timescales and (b) consciousness demonstrably selects among outcomes, the quantum interface hypothesis requires revision. This falsifiability constraint prevents the framework from accommodating any result.

## What Remains Open

Constrained pluralism narrows the location problem without solving it; specification remains substantially open.

**The molecular ground.** Within the attention-motor architecture, which molecular mechanism hosts the relevant quantum indeterminacies? Ion channels at synaptic junctions remain the most physically specific candidate. Whether the Zeno mechanism works at biologically relevant timescales — or whether some other quantum effect operates — is empirical.

**The coupling law.** Even knowing the location, the [[the-psychophysical-control-law|psychophysical control law]] remains unspecified. How does phenomenal effort map quantitatively to physical selection bias?

**Whether the hierarchy is continuous.** The hierarchical model assumes that molecular-level quantum effects aggregate smoothly into network-level selection. There might instead be a sharp transition — a particular level at which consciousness acts, with other levels merely implementing the result.

**Whether location varies across species.** If consciousness-brain coupling depends on attention-motor architecture, organisms with radically different architectures (cephalopods, arthropods) may host the interface at different locations or through different mechanisms entirely. The constrained pluralism established for humans may not generalise. [[interface-heterogeneity|Interface heterogeneity]] explores this possibility.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The interface problem sits at the intersection of the Map's philosophical commitments and empirical science. The shift from agnosticism toward constrained pluralism on the location half reflects the framework becoming specific enough to constrain empirical questions it originally left open. The specification half is where the Map's tenets face their most demanding test.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]** requires that consciousness acts at quantum indeterminacies. Constrained pluralism specifies: at quantum indeterminacies *within the attention-motor architecture*, at sites involved in *threshold-crossing events* during action selection. The tenet simultaneously constrains and enables specification — it limits where to look, but the subtlety of quantum effects makes detection difficult. The tenet trades empirical accessibility for theoretical elegance.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** gains from the dopamine research. The dissociation in dopamine-deficient mice — motor capacity intact, goal-directed initiative absent — maps onto the Map's distinction between neural computation (which generates options) and conscious selection (which determines which option becomes action). Dopamine sets the conditions; consciousness does the selecting. The tenet also means the Map cannot retreat to epiphenomenalism when specification proves difficult — it demands that consciousness make a physical difference, which demands that the difference be in principle specifiable.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** creates the specification problem by positing two ontologically distinct domains that must be lawfully related. If consciousness were identical to brain states, no specification would be needed — everything would be physical causation under different descriptions. The cost of taking dualism seriously is accepting that the mind-matter mapping demands specification that physicalism avoids.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]** is essential because specification only makes sense if there is genuine selection. Under many-worlds, all outcomes occur — "selection" reduces to indexical self-location, and there is nothing to specify. Both halves of the interface problem presuppose collapse.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** applies to the hierarchical model and to the unsatisfying state of specification. A single-site answer would be simpler. But if the interface actually spans multiple levels, insisting on a single site would sacrifice accuracy for parsimony. The temptation is to adopt a simpler theory (physicalism, epiphenomenalism) that avoids the specification burden. This tenet counsels patience: if consciousness genuinely influences matter, the difficulty reflects the problem's depth, not the theory's failure.

The interface problem is the Map's most significant open challenge. Acknowledging this is not a weakness — it is intellectual honesty about what an interactionist framework must eventually deliver. The challenge is real, partial answers exist on both halves, and the alternative is worse. But "worse alternative" is not a solution. The Map's long-term credibility depends on progress here.
