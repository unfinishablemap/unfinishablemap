Philosophy of action asks what separates a genuine *action*—my raising my arm—from a mere bodily *happening*—my arm rising in a spasm. The dominant answer since Donald Davidson is the causal theory of action: an action is a bodily movement caused *in the right way* by the agent's mental states. For The Unfinishable Map this framework is half-congenial and half-treacherous. Its central claim—that mental states cause movements—is exactly what the Map's [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction tenet]] asserts. But the standard *event-causal* rendering of that claim exposes two failure points, deviant causal chains and the disappearing agent (both explained below), that push toward *volitionist* and *agent-causal* alternatives. The Map selects a hybrid of the two, closest to E. J. Lowe's non-Cartesian substance dualism, with the act of *trying* serving as the minimal causal contact point between mind and matter.

This article develops that selection. It surveys the four live positions in action theory—the causal theory, volitionism, agent causation, and the non-causal tradition—and shows which commitments the Map's dualism forces and which it can leave open.

## Action versus Mere Happening

The founding problem is deceptively simple. When my arm goes up because I raise it, and when it goes up because a neurologist stimulates my motor cortex, the same bodily event occurs. What makes the first an action and the second not? Any theory of action owes an account of this difference, and the account it gives fixes almost everything else.

The causal theory's answer: the difference lies in the *causal history*. In the action, the movement is caused by the appropriate mental antecedents—a relevant desire and belief, or an intention. In the mere happening, it is not. Action is not an intrinsic property of a movement but a relational one, fixed by where the movement came from.

This locates the whole weight of agency in a causal relation. That is the source of both the theory's power and its vulnerability.

## The Causal Theory and Its Dualist Appropriation

Davidson's "Actions, Reasons, and Causes" (1963) argued that the reasons for which we act are *causes* of what we do. His decisive move addressed the "one operative reason" problem: an agent may have several reasons that would each rationalise an action, yet act *for* only one of them. What distinguishes the reason the agent acted *on* from the reasons that merely would have justified the act? Davidson's answer is that the operative reason is the one that actually *caused* the action. Nothing but a causal relation, he argued, marks that difference.

Davidson was an anomalous monist, not a dualist: he held mental events to be token-identical with physical events and denied any psychophysical laws. His metaphysics is a form of physicalism the Map rejects at [[tenets#^dualism|Tenet 1]]. What the Map borrows is the *causal skeleton*, not the monist flesh. The claim that mental states genuinely cause bodily movements is common ground between Davidson and the Map; the claim that those mental states are nothing over and above physical events is where they part. Davidson is engaged here as a rival on the metaphysics whose causal architecture the Map adapts, not as an ally.

Adopting the skeleton, though, means inheriting its two standing difficulties.

## Two Internal Troubles {#troubles}

### Deviant Causal Chains {#deviance}

Suppose a climber wants to rid himself of the weight and danger of holding another man on a rope, and believes that loosening his grip would do it. This desire and belief so unnerve him that his hands loosen involuntarily, and the man falls. The right mental states caused the right movement—yet the climber did not *intentionally* let go. The cause ran through the *wrong route*: it worked by unnerving him rather than by his acting on it.

Deviant causal chains are the causal theory's most persistent internal problem. "Caused by the right mental states" is not sufficient for action; the causation must also proceed *in the right way*. Decades of work has tried to specify what "the right way" comes to—appeals to the *sensitivity* of the movement to fine variations in the intention's content, to the *reliability* of the pathway, to reconceiving the intention as a *formal* rather than efficient cause, and (most recently) to analysing non-deviance through the *manifestation of the agent's dispositions* (Ward 2024). Brain–computer interfaces have made the problem concrete again: when an intention drives a prosthesis through an engineered signal path, which paths count as non-deviant is a live question rather than an armchair puzzle. No proposed repair commands consensus.

### The Disappearing Agent {#disappearing}

The second trouble strikes at control. If an action is *just* a sequence of events—states causing a decision causing a movement—and if, for a libertarian, the decision is undetermined until it occurs, then the decision looks like a chance event that simply happens *in* the agent rather than something the agent *does*. Where, in the event-causal story, is the agent's *settling* of the matter? The agent seems to vanish into a stream of states none of which is her exercising control. This is the disappearing-agent objection, pressed sharply by Derek Pereboom against event-causal libertarianism.

The objection is not that event-causal agency is *supernatural* but that it is *too thin*: it leaves nothing that is the agent's own contribution over and above the states. That framing matters, because the natural repair is to add something over and above the events—which is precisely what a dualist ontology already supplies.

## Volitionism: Trying as the Basic Act

Volitionism relocates the basic action inward. The fundamental thing an agent does is not move the body but *will*, or *try*. Overt action is a trying that issues in movement; the difference between raising my arm and my arm's rising is the presence of the trying.

Jennifer Hornsby developed the most careful modern version: bodily actions are tryings, and a trying is an event inseparable from the agent whose trying it is. This threads a needle. It keeps the causal theory's insight that action involves a mental cause of movement, while answering the disappearing agent partway—the trying is not a free-floating state but the agent's own activity—and without positing a primitive, unanalysable agent-cause. Volitionism also handles the paralysed patient elegantly: one can *try* to move a limb that does not move, and the trying is still a genuine act. The overt movement is inessential; the volition is the core.

For a dualism committed to a *minimal* interface (explained under [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]), the attraction is direct. Trying is the smallest thing an agent does—a candidate for the *least* non-physical act that could bias a physical outcome.

## Agent Causation and Non-Cartesian Substance Dualism

Agent-causal theories, from Roderick Chisholm to Timothy O'Connor, take the further step of treating the *agent herself*, as a substance, as an irreducible cause of her actions—not reducible to causation by events or states occurring within her. This answers the disappearing agent head-on: the agent does not vanish into the events because she is a genuine relatum of the causal relation, an originating cause in her own right.

The standard charge against agent causation is "panicky metaphysics"—that it posits a sui generis causal power mysteriously unlike ordinary causation. Recent work has tried to defuse this, recasting agent causation as one species within a pluralist causal order rather than a spooky exception to it.

E. J. Lowe offered the synthesis most useful to the Map. His *non-Cartesian substance dualism* holds that the self is a distinct substance from the body—so there *is* something over and above the physical events—while denying the Cartesian claim that the self is a wholly separable, immaterial thing. On Lowe's account, all causation is ultimately *substance* causation, and the agent (a substance) causes her volitions. This is a hybrid: the agent-causal insight (a substance originates the act) is combined with the volitionist insight (what she directly causes is a trying). Crucially, Lowe attacked the *causal closure of the physical*—the premise that every physical event has a sufficient physical cause—arguing that the closure argument for physicalism either begs the question or misdescribes psychophysical causation. Without closure, the exclusion worry that mental causes must be either idle or redundant loses its grip.

## The Non-Causal Alternative

A fourth tradition, descending from G. E. M. Anscombe, denies that reasons cause actions at all. On this view practical thought is the *formal* principle that unifies a bit of behaviour into an intentional action—the description under which it is intentional—rather than an *efficient* cause that sets the body in motion. Anscombe emphasised *practical knowledge*: the agent knows what she is doing without observation or inference, and this non-observational knowledge partly constitutes the action as intentional. Because intention does not efficiently push the body, deviant-causal-chain problems never arise; there is no causal route that could go wrong.

This tradition sits in honest tension with the Map. Read as denying that consciousness efficiently influences the body, the non-causal view *contradicts* [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]], which requires genuine downward causation. But a more irenic reading is available and the Map prefers it: the formal-cause story describes the *rational structure* of action—what makes it the intentional action it is—while a volition supplies the *efficient* causal contact. On this layered reading the two accounts answer different questions and can coexist. The Map does not claim Anscombe would endorse this division; it flags the division as its own reconciling move, not hers.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's tenets do not leave the theory of action open. They select within it.

**Bidirectional Interaction requires the causal skeleton.** Because consciousness genuinely influences the physical world, some version of "mental states cause movements" must be true. This rules out any reading of the non-causal tradition that denies downward efficacy, and it makes the pure event-causal *standard story* structurally welcome—so far as its causal claim goes. What the Map cannot take from Davidson is his monism.

**The disappearing agent is answered by the dualist self.** The objection bites hardest against theories on which the agent is *nothing over and above* her states. The Map is not such a theory. Its substance-leaning reading of [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]—the agency cluster's commitment to a persisting subject who exercises causal powers—supplies exactly the "something more" the objection finds missing. This is why the Map's action theory is agent-causal at its base: the agent, as a subject, is a genuine originator, so control does not dissolve into chance. The commitment is developed in [[agent-causation]] and [[free-will]].

**Minimal Quantum Interaction favours trying as the contact point.** The Map holds that if consciousness influences matter it does so by the *smallest* possible biasing of otherwise-undetermined quantum outcomes, not by injecting energy. Volitionism's *trying* is the natural locus for this minimal act: the trying is where the non-physical subject makes contact, biasing which neural outcome becomes actual without any detectable violation of physical law. The Map's preferred [[overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism|selection-only interactionism]]—consciousness selecting among outcomes physics leaves open rather than adding a competing push—fits volition precisely: the trying selects, it does not shove. This dissolves the exclusion worry without overdetermination, because the mental contribution occupies the gap physics leaves rather than duplicating a sufficient physical cause.

**The result is a volitionist agent-causal hybrid, close to Lowe.** The agent-substance originates (answering the disappearing agent); what she originates is a volition (the minimal act); the volition biases outcome-selection at the quantum margin (satisfying minimal interaction); and the causal-closure premise that would forbid this is rejected on independent grounds, as Lowe's critique and the Map's overdetermination-dissolution both argue.

**Deviance remains a shared problem, softened.** A volitionist dualism is not immune to deviant causal chains, but it is better placed than pure event-causalism. When the basic act is a *trying* rather than a separately identifiable prior state that must then cause a movement, there is less room for a "wrong route" to open between intention and act—the trying is not a distinct earlier event waiting to misfire. Yet the Map should not overclaim: the [[agency-void#The Verification Circularity|agency void]] records that a subject cannot fully verify, from the inside, that its trying rather than some deviant parallel process produced the movement. First-person certainty about the causal route is not available, and the Map owns that limit rather than papering over it.

A tension worth stating plainly: agent causation invites the parsimony objection that it multiplies causal powers needlessly. By [[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]] the Map holds that simplicity is an unreliable guide where knowledge is incomplete, so the mere charge of extravagance is not decisive. That tenet does not license the posit; it only refuses to let parsimony *veto* it. The posit must still earn its keep—and here it does, by answering the disappearing agent that the simpler event-causal story cannot.
