The Unfinishable Map's [[neurological-dissociations-as-interface-architecture|neurological dissociation work]] — an interface-architecture reading built on the Geschwind [[disconnection-neuroscience|disconnection-syndrome framework]] — maps the mind-brain interface by examining what happens when specific neural pathways are destroyed. Clinical dissociation offers something arguably more revealing: selective disconnections that arise without structural brain damage. In dissociative identity disorder, depersonalization-derealization disorder, dissociative amnesia, and conversion disorder, no gross destructive lesion is required for specific aspects of experience to go offline—the substrate is undamaged, not unchanged. Neuroimaging reveals altered functional connectivity, agency, salience, and predictive-control networks—mostly selective disconnections between intact processing regions, though conversion disorder (examined below) involves heightened rather than severed coupling. Modern physicalism (functionalism, global workspace theory, IIT, and the now-dominant predictive-processing accounts) accommodates this by treating consciousness as dependent on connectivity patterns rather than raw neural activity. Clinical dissociation tests whether that relationship behaves like a higher-order physical property or a functional bridge between domains.

## The Dissociative Spectrum

Pierre Janet (1889) introduced dissociation as a splitting of the mental field—a psychological fracture separating awareness, memory, identity, and motor control into parallel streams. His framework was psychological rather than neurological: he attributed dissociation to a weakness in the integrative capacity of consciousness itself, not to brain damage. That emphasis on consciousness as an integrative capacity distinct from the neural substrate anticipates the Map's interface architecture, despite a wholly different theoretical setting.

Modern clinical dissociation spans a spectrum from normal (absorption in a novel, highway hypnosis) to pathological (DID, dissociative fugue). What unifies the spectrum is selective disconnection: specific aspects of experience or function become inaccessible to consciousness while others continue normally. The selectivity is the evidential key. Random neural degradation would produce random experiential degradation. Clinical dissociation produces structured, predictable disconnections that map onto specific interface channels.

This is the hardest intact-substrate test a theory of consciousness can face. In dissociative amnesia, DID, and depersonalisation, conscious access decouples while the brain remains anatomically intact—no lesion, no atrophy, no pharmacological blockade—so whatever does the separating cannot be attributed to missing tissue. The Map reads these states as the cleanest available exhibit of *channel architecture*: conscious access organised into separable channels that can be partitioned without anything being broken—a serious interpretation the data make available, not one they force.

### The Tulving Channels as Scaffolding

The argument uses Endel Tulving's three-mode framework only as scaffolding ([[anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness|developed at the concept tier]]): *anoetic* consciousness accompanying procedural skill, *noetic* semantic memory's feeling of knowing, *autonoetic* episodic memory's re-inhabited past. Across most consciousness disruptions these degrade in a fixed order—autonoetic first, anoetic last. Clinical dissociation is the case where that ordering appears *without any substrate change to drive it*: the highest, most reconstruction-intensive channel is the one that disconnects, compartmentalises, or unbinds, while the lower channels carry on.

## Four Clinical Windows

### Dissociative Identity Disorder: Fragmented Expression, Unified Substrate

DID presents the philosophically sharpest case. Distinct personality states maintain separate experiential streams, autobiographical memories, and self-concepts within a single anatomically intact brain. Some alters report no awareness of others' experiences. EEG and fMRI studies reveal measurable differences between alter states—different autonomic responses, different neural activation patterns, different perceptual processing—yet no structural brain lesion accounts for the separation.

DID's aetiology is itself contested, and the contest constrains how much weight any interface reading can carry. The trauma model takes the compartmentalisation to arise from a dissociative response to severe early abuse; the sociocognitive (fantasy/role-enactment) model takes it to be substantially shaped by suggestion, expectation, and culturally available scripts (Spanos 1994; Lilienfeld, Lynn, Kirsch et al. 1999). Lynn, Polizzi, Merckelbach, Chiu, Maxwell, van Heugten and Lilienfeld (2022) review three decades of this dispute, argue neither model alone is adequate, and propose a transtheoretical framework in which sleep disturbance, impaired inhibition, hyperassociation, and source-monitoring deficits interact. The Map does not adjudicate the aetiology, but the caveat is load-bearing: if alter-formation is partly socially mediated, the case for a non-physical subject expressing itself through fragmented channels weakens at exactly the step the interface reading needs.

The physicalist reading: DID may indicate that one brain can produce multiple conscious subjects, suggesting consciousness is a product of brain organisation. The dualist reading—developed in the Map's [[split-brain-consciousness]] article—holds that a single non-physical mind expressing itself through a trauma-disrupted interface produces fragmented output without fragmented consciousness; the alters may be different configurations of the interface rather than different minds. What survives the aetiological dispute is the selectivity: even on a sociocognitive origin, the partition falls along the autonoetic channel rather than scrambling memory systems indiscriminately, and that is the pattern calling for explanation.

The pattern may tilt toward the interface reading in one important respect, though it does not settle the matter. DID partitions autobiographical access *between* alter states: each alter carries its own autobiographical timeline and sense of self, cross-alter autonoetic access is selectively severed, while semantic knowledge and procedural skills typically transfer across the partition. The highest channel is compartmentalised; the lower channels remain largely unified. The geometry differs from dissociative amnesia—partition between selves rather than disconnection of one self from its past—but the selectivity targets the same channel. Reinders and colleagues' "One Brain, Two Selves" (2003) used PET to compare regional cerebral blood flow as the same patient occupied different identity states processing the same autobiographical material, reporting distinct rCBF patterns (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior associative regions) for the different senses of self: *one intact brain* supporting differentiable patterns of self-related access—not different tissue, but different organisation of access within it, the connectivity-pattern signature the channel reading predicts. (This is an early-paradigm PET study with only eleven participants; Lynn et al.'s 2022 review remains the load-bearing post-2020 anchor for the aetiological dispute.)

The cross-state loss is shallower than "one alter cannot reach another's memories" suggests: Marsh, Dorahy, Butler, Middleton, de Jong, Kemp and Huntjens (2021) found DID patients who subjectively report inter-identity amnesia on free *recall* still show significant cross-identity transfer on objective *recognition* tests—recognising more of the other identity's event than instructed simulators or controls. The substrate demonstrably retains and integrates the information; what is lost is metacognitive access and felt ownership, not a structural severance. This recall/recognition dissociation is the concrete anchor for the connectivity-physicalist's claim that the substrate keeps integrating—exactly what a reading on which integration just is consciousness would predict. The selective disruption of felt unity, with latent retention preserved, still maps onto interface channel disruption rather than general degradation; what it no longer licenses—for either reading—is calling the cross-state integration itself severed.

### Depersonalization-Derealization: The Self-Model Channel Goes Silent

Depersonalization-derealization disorder (DPDR) reads like a phenomenological experiment. Patients report that their own thoughts, body, and actions feel unreal or detached—as though observing themselves from outside; the world appears flattened and distant, emotional colouring drained while cognitive processing remains intact. DPDR disconnects what the Map's interface architecture calls the [[neurological-dissociations-as-interface-architecture|self-model ascending channel]]—the component delivering felt ownership and reality. Core sensory and motor processing continue; what vanishes is the felt sense that any of this is happening to *them*.

The early account—Sierra and Berrios (1998)—read DPDR as a disconnection between integrative self-representation and emotional processing, consistent with the reduced insula/amygdala activation DPDR patients show while perceptual cortex functions normally. The contemporary account is sharper and, for the dualist reading, more demanding. Seth, Suzuki and Critchley (2012) model conscious presence as successful interoceptive prediction—the felt sense of "this is real, this is mine" is the brain explaining away interoceptive prediction errors—and Ciaunica, Seth, Limanowski, Hesp and Friston (2022) extend this to DPDR specifically: depersonalisation is an *imbalanced precision weighting toward self-related priors*, with impaired attenuation of self-generated signals—an active-inference pathology rather than a channel falling silent.

This reframing matters because it absorbs the objection the older framing seemed to raise. The tempting move—pressing functionalism with a precision problem, since the functional alteration is narrow (emotional integration) yet the experiential change is in *felt reality itself*—is exactly what the interoceptive-inference account dissolves: a disturbance in how interoceptive predictions are precision-weighted is *expected* to produce a globally felt loss of mineness, because presence just is the successful weighting of those predictions. The broad phenomenological shift tracks a correspondingly fundamental computational variable, so the precision objection does not by itself favour an interface reading. The reading the Map favours—that the functional connection delivering the affective stamp of ownership has been severed—remains available, but now competes with a fully specified physicalist rival rather than filling a gap.

Depersonalisation is the most diagnostic of the three intact-substrate cases because it dissociates the *internal structure* of the autonoetic channel rather than disconnecting the channel as a whole. The patient remembers their life and can re-access episodes propositionally—the content is present and retrievable—but the felt sense that the memories and experiences are *theirs* is attenuated. The phenomenology is captured in the measurement literature: the Cambridge Depersonalisation Scale (Sierra and Berrios, 2000) and its later factor analysis (Sierra, Baker, Medford and David, 2005) isolate a recurrent factor the authors label *Anomalous Subjective Recall*—a disturbance in the felt quality and ownership of autobiographical memory distinct from any failure of content retrieval. The content channel delivers; the mine-ness it normally carries is decoupled. This suggests the autonoetic channel is not a single primitive but a bundle of *separable internal features*—at least content, mine-ness, and pastness—that bind together in health and can be unbound without substrate damage. A production account is not refuted by this: it can posit a dedicated self-attribution or source-monitoring mechanism whose selective disruption removes ownership while sparing content. What depersonalisation establishes cleanly is the *separability* of mine-ness from content; that an interface architecture rather than a self-attribution module is the better explanation is the live hypothesis, not the datum ([[phenomenology-of-memory-and-the-self|the phenomenology-of-memory article]] develops mine-ness as a phenomenal feature).

### Dissociative Amnesia: The Memory Channel Disconnects

Dissociative amnesia produces inability to recall autobiographical information—sometimes entire years of life—without any detectable memory-trace destruction. Unlike organic amnesia from hippocampal damage, dissociative amnesia is often reported to be reversible, sometimes abruptly: recovery occurs when the dissociative barrier lifts. But reversibility does not by itself establish intact storage, accuracy, or non-reconstructive retrieval—memory reconstruction, source-monitoring failure, state-dependent retrieval, and suggestibility remain live alternatives.

This pattern, *if it holds*, distinguishes interface disconnection from substrate damage. In organic amnesia the memories are degraded or destroyed—the hard drive is damaged. The interface reading takes dissociative amnesia to leave memories intact but inaccessible, the connection between storage and awareness severed, with access recovering when it restores. Mangiulli, Otgaar, Jelicic and Merckelbach (2022) review the case literature and argue this "successfully stored but inaccessible" framing is poorly evidenced—most reported cases fail to rule out ordinary forgetting, malingering, or non-encoding—so the reversibility the interface reading leans on is contested at the level of the data, not just its interpretation. The cases that do hold admit intra-brain redescription: *metarepresentational inhibition*, where a higher-order process withholds retrieved content from the global broadcast, and ordinary retrieval-suppression (Anderson and Hanslmayr's motivated-forgetting work) both predict the same reversibility the interface reading treats as its signature. The dissociative reading must therefore claim less here than the older sources suggested.

Where the pattern does hold, the selectivity is still telling. Dissociative amnesia typically affects autobiographical memory while leaving procedural skills, semantic knowledge, and working memory intact. The patient can drive a car, speak their language, perform their job—but cannot recall who they are or what happened to them. The selectivity does not always respect a clean episodic/semantic line: in some cases *personally relevant* semantic memory is also affected while impersonal semantic knowledge survives (Staniloiu and Markowitsch, 2014). This sharpens rather than blunts the channel reading—what separates tracks *self-relatedness*, the autonoetic, mine-tagged content, rather than a coarse memory-system boundary.

Two features make the cases that hold load-bearing. First, the substrate is intact: structural neuroimaging is typically unremarkable, with altered prefrontal–limbic activity rather than damage. Second, recovery is reconnection, not reconstruction: when the dissociative barrier lifts, autobiographical memories typically return as a body rather than being relearned piecemeal, sometimes abruptly (Staniloiu and Markowitsch, 2014). On a production reading, a substrate that could not yield autobiographical access yesterday yields the whole of it intact today with no tissue change between—the same explanatory difficulty terminal lucidity poses, here without any neurodegeneration to complicate the picture. The inference is not forced—metarepresentational inhibition predicts the same reversibility—but the reconnection signature respects functional architecture rather than scrambling it.

### Conversion Disorder: Volitional Motor Control Captured, Not Cut

Functional neurological disorder (FND)—historically *conversion disorder*, the DSM-IV term—produces genuine neurological symptoms such as paralysis, blindness, seizures, and anaesthesia without corresponding neurological damage. A patient may be unable to move their arm despite intact motor cortex, intact motor neurons, and intact muscles. Under anaesthesia or hypnosis, the "paralysed" limb may move normally. Modern practice diagnoses FND by *positive* rule-in signs (Hoover's sign, tremor entrainment) rather than as a diagnosis of exclusion—reflecting the shift to the active-generation model below.

It is tempting to read this as a severed descending channel—the conscious command line cut while the motor system stays capable, mirroring [[anarchic-hand-and-action-ownership|anarchic hand]] without the lesion. The contemporary FND literature points the other way: the mechanism is not a *missing* connection but an *overactive* one, the symptom actively generated rather than passively lost.

Voon et al. (2010) found *greater* functional connectivity between the right amygdala and right supplementary motor area in conversion patients, with amygdala activity predicting subsequent motor-area changes rather than the reverse—heightened limbic influence over motor preparation, not a disconnected channel; Hassa et al. (2017) replicated the direction in functional hemiparesis. Edwards et al.'s (2012) Bayesian model of "hysteria" explains the symptom as an abnormally precise intermediate-level motor prior the system fulfils, *generating* paralysis or tremor as the brain's own confident prediction over contrary sensory evidence. The motor system is not cut off from volition; a self-generated prior captures it. (Vuilleumier (2005) and Brown (2004) earlier described altered prefrontal–motor dynamics, Brown's representational-inhibition model already in this active-generation family.)

Active generation by an aberrant prior is a clean intra-brain mechanism: "intact motor system plus a functional symptom" is explained without positing any consciousness-side channel, so the dissociative reading must claim less here than the other three windows allow. Conversion disorder still shows volitional motor output can be abolished with the substrate intact; what it no longer shows is a channel *going offline*. [[conversion-disorder-as-consciousness-side-fault|Conversion disorder as the consciousness-side-fault paradigm]] develops this at full resolution.

## The Systematic Pattern

Across all four conditions, the same structural logic repeats:

| Condition | What Disconnects | Regional Processing | Connectivity Change | Interface Channel |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| DID | Felt cross-state access (substrate retention intact) | Intact per-alter | Altered between states | Bilateral/unity |
| DPDR | Felt ownership, emotional stamp | Intact sensory/cognitive | Down-weighted interoceptive precision | Self-model ascending |
| Dissociative amnesia | Autobiographical access | Storage (disputed) | Inhibited/suppressed retrieval | Memory ascending |
| Conversion disorder | Volitional motor control | Intact motor capacity | *Increased* limbic→motor coupling (active generation) | Motor descending |

Each condition selectively disrupts connectivity between otherwise capable processing regions—the individual systems retain their capacity while the functional connection between them fails. The disconnections are selective, predictable, and map onto the same interface architecture that [[neurological-dissociations-as-interface-architecture|neurological dissociations]] reveal through structural damage.

The convergence across disconnection types may be the most suggestive evidence, but it has to be read at the right tier. The Map's own [[common-cause-null|common-cause discipline]] requires screening the converging routes for a shared upstream cause before counting them as independent confirmations, and a candidate is visible: predictive processing reframes three of these windows—conversion as an aberrantly precise motor prior, DPDR as altered interoceptive precision-weighting, dissociative amnesia (where it holds) as inhibited retrieval—as *precision-weighting pathologies* in one active-inference framework. If a single mechanism generates the converging routes, the convergence reads one upstream architecture several times and the [[epistemology-of-convergence-arguments|compound signature]] loses most of its force. The honest reply is partial: "precision-weighting" is a loose family, not one mechanism—retrieval-suppression, interoceptive precision-imbalance, and an overactive motor prior are different enough that the shared label is a resemblance, and DID's cross-state access failure resists the precision framing altogether. The routes' residual independence is weaker than four-fold. [[contemplative-pathology-and-interface-malfunction|Contemplative pathology]] and [[voids/anesthesia-void|pharmacological dissociation]] add further routes that do not reduce to the precision-weighting family, so their agreement on one channel taxonomy retains some [[epistemology-of-convergence-arguments#compound-signatures-convergence-without-a-shared-mechanism|compound-signature]] structure—but the reach is discounted: the signature earns the *architecture* while leaving its dualist *significance* to be argued separately.

## What the Dualist Reading Adds — and What It Does Not

The strongest physicalist reply is worth stating at full strength before the interface reading is pressed, far from the weak "connectivity does it somehow" gesture the dissociative pattern is sometimes taken to embarrass. On a developed functionalist, global-workspace, or predictive-processing self-model account, consciousness just *is* the right kind of functional organisation—and selective disruptions of access, ownership, memory, and motor agency are precisely what such an architecture predicts when specific couplings degrade. The four windows become diagnostic probes of functional organisation, not evidence that a non-physical mind has failed to couple. Functional organisation is where functionalism began, so reading consciousness off connectivity is the theory's home move, not a retreat forced by the data. This rival is fully specified and predicts the same selectivity the interface model does; the dissociative evidence constrains both readings rather than establishing either.

Only crude identity theory—consciousness *is* neural activity—fails straightforwardly here: the processing regions retain their capacity while experience is disrupted. The evidence discriminates sharply against that crude localisation picture but not against the developed functionalist rival just stated. [[global-workspace-theory|Global workspace theory]] and [[integrated-information-theory|integrated information theory]] both accommodate dissociative phenomena as connectivity disruptions, just as they accommodate [[anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface|anaesthetic unbinding]]; the predictive-processing accounts are the sharpest version, and the common-cause objection they raise is the strongest physicalist challenge here.

The two readings divide over the nature of that connectivity relationship. The physicalist takes it for a higher-order physical property fully constituted by the connectivity pattern; the interface model takes the pattern for the physical side of a bridge, so disrupting it disconnects consciousness from the brain rather than disrupting consciousness itself. On the interface reading, trauma, stress, or psychological conflict disrupts specific channels without degrading the processing regions—the mind remains; its access to the body changes.

### The Intact-Substrate Structural Argument

The intact-substrate cases—amnesia, DID, depersonalisation—converge on one structural claim single-case evidence cannot make alone: *conscious access separates selectively while the substrate stays whole*, so the partitioning must be described at the level of connectivity patterns between intact regions rather than which structures exist. This locates the disagreement precisely. A binary "consciousness on/off" production theory cannot reach the data: it predicts uniform impairment, and dissociation is the opposite of uniform. The live contest is between a *sophisticated production reading* and the *filter reading*, both operating at the connectivity level.

The sophisticated production reading explains the selectivity by differential functional vulnerability: autobiographical-self networks (medial prefrontal, posterior midline) are more easily knocked into an altered functional regime than semantic or procedural networks. Its mechanism cost is the additional commitment that the functional-vulnerability ordering matches the autonoetic-channel ordering across all three geometries—disconnection, compartmentalisation, feature-unbinding—on an intact substrate where there is no lesion topography to anchor the prediction. The filter reading reads the same selectivity off architecture: the highest-bandwidth, most reconstruction-intensive channel is the one most easily decoupled and the one whose internal bindings can come apart. Its mechanism cost is the metaphysical commitment to an interface, paid once at the tenet level rather than per-case. Neither gets the connectivity geometry for free; the asymmetry the comparison turns on is that the production reading must additionally explain *why* the vulnerability ordering tracks the autonoetic channel on an intact substrate. The discriminating test the catalogue still lacks—a targeted, reversible perturbation of an autonoetic-supporting connectivity pattern that spares its noetic-supporting subnetworks—would separate them; dissociative states approximate it naturally but without experimental control over what is perturbed.

## The Trauma Connection

The trauma association is real but uneven, and the blanket claim that clinical dissociation is "caused by trauma" overstates it. DID is the strongest case—correlated with severe early abuse—but that very link is what the sociocognitive model contests (Lynn et al., 2022), so it is strongly-but-contestedly trauma-linked rather than settled. DPDR and dissociative amnesia are more qualified: both often follow stress, yet many DPDR cases lack a clear traumatic trigger, and amnesia's trauma framing inherits the evidential doubts of its "stored-but-inaccessible" reading (Mangiulli et al., 2022). FND is the weakest link—current criteria no longer require an identifiable stressor, and the field has moved past the Freud–Breuer "hysteria" framing that once made distress definitional. This matters because the interface model's "consciousness withdraws from threatening channels" reading needs the trauma link to be load-bearing; where it is contested or absent, the reading claims less.

Where the trauma link holds, the interface model reads it the same way throughout: trauma leaves the processing regions intact but selectively disrupts connectivity between them—the mind protecting itself by severing access to unbearable memories, withdrawing felt ownership of threatening experience, fragmenting expression across states that each carry only part of the load. Physicalists explain the same pattern through stress-mediated changes in functional connectivity—cortisol, norepinephrine, and other mediators alter regional coupling. What stands in need of explanation is the *apparent clinical selectivity*: dissociation targets precisely those channels carrying threatening content while preserving channels needed for ongoing function. The interface model offers consciousness withdrawing from specific channels to protect itself; an affective-salience physicalism replies that salience biases which couplings stress mediators disrupt, so the apparent purposiveness is the expected output of threat-weighted prediction. The selectivity is real; which framework earns it the clinical data do not settle.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: Clinical dissociation may be among the more suggestive empirical evidence for the Map's position, though the data do not decide between the interface model and a connectivity-based physicalism—and the common-cause objection above tempers even the suggestive reading where one precision-weighting mechanism may underlie several routes. Selective connectivity disruption between otherwise capable processing regions is the kind of pattern dualism's interface model would lead one to expect, but modern physicalism accommodates it by locating consciousness in connectivity. Dualism reads the relationship as a bridge between domains; physicalism owes an account of why *this* connectivity pattern generates experience while neighbouring patterns do not. Neither reading is forced by the dissociative data alone.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: Conversion disorder shows volitional motor output can be abolished with the motor substrate intact. The corrected mechanism (an aberrant self-generated motor prior actively producing the symptom, rather than a severed channel) makes this the *weakest* of the four windows for the bidirectional reading, since active generation is fully redescribable within one domain. Each condition disrupts a specific direction or channel of mind-brain communication, consistent with—but not forced by—information flowing both ways through separable pathways, which a functionalist can redescribe as altered connectivity within a single domain.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]**: If consciousness interfaces with the brain through quantum-level processes requiring coordinated neural activity, then psychological disruption of that coordination—without structural damage—could be made consistent with the dissociative pattern: the interaction site physically intact but functionally disrupted, like a radio whose antenna is misaligned rather than broken. This is a consistency claim, not a novel prediction; the clinical evidence does not discriminate a quantum-interface explanation from network-level ones.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: A crude identity theory must give ground to handle dissociative evidence, moving from raw neural activity to functional organisation and specific connectivity patterns. This is not fairly read as ad-hoc epicycle-adding: functional organisation is functionalism's natural home, not a retreat invented to absorb the data, and the predictive-processing reframings above are independently motivated. The interface model is more complex ontologically (two domains rather than one) but offers an arguably more unified picture: one framework—selective channel disruption—covers both neurological and clinical dissociations. Whether that unification outweighs the ontological cost is exactly the trade-off Occam's razor is unreliable at adjudicating under incomplete knowledge; the razor does not settle the contest here.
