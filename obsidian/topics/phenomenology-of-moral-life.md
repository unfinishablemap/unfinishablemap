Moral consciousness has a distinctive phenomenal character that unfolds across an arc: from the immediate perception that something is wrong, through the deliberative weighing of competing demands, to the felt ownership of having chosen. The Unfinishable Map holds that consciousness genuinely selects among open possibilities rather than watching predetermined outcomes unfold. If this is correct, the phenomenology of moral life is not decorative—it is the felt character of a real causal process. What moral experience feels like from the inside matters because that is where agency operates.

This article traces the full arc. If [[topics/phenomenal-value-realism]] is correct—if intrinsic value is a feature of conscious experience itself—then the perception of wrongness is where moral facts live, and the deliberation that follows is where those facts are navigated toward action.

## Moral Perception as Gestalt

Moral perception does not build ethical judgements from morally neutral parts. Witnessing someone kick a dog does not proceed: (1) perceive physical movement, (2) recognise intent, (3) compute moral status. The wrongness appears in the initial perception as an organising feature of the whole scene, much as a face appears in a collection of dots once the gestalt snaps into place.

The wrongness organises what you see: the kick registers as aggressive, the animal's response registers as violation, the scene's meaning registers as cruelty. Remove the perceived wrongness and the same physical events would organise differently—as play, clumsiness, or accident. Jesse Prinz has argued that moral properties are perceived through emotional states—that disgust, anger, and sympathy constitute moral perception rather than merely accompanying it. Whether moral perception is genuinely perceptual (like seeing colour) or quasi-perceptual remains contested. What matters for the Map's framework is the first-person datum: wrongness presents itself as a feature of the world, not as a private sentiment.

This extends beyond negative cases. Perceiving a homeless person sleeping on a park bench involves a moral intentionality that transforms the entire perceptual field—the bench becomes an inadequate shelter, the posture becomes exposure. McDowell characterises this as a perceptual capacity: the morally perceptive person does not see neutral facts and then add a moral interpretation. They see a differently structured situation.

## The Temporal Arc: Strike, Deepening, Deliberation

Moral perception has a characteristic temporal profile. **The pre-reflective strike** comes first: wrongness registers before reflective engagement begins, present in experience before any reasoning about why it is wrong. Judgement articulates something already experientially present rather than producing it.

**Phenomenal deepening** follows. Unlike a flash of colour, moral perception does not fully disclose its object in an instant. Sustained attention reveals how the act is wrong—the specific character of the violation, the quality of the suffering caused, the nature of the agent's disregard. Affective consolidation—outrage, compassion, indignation—typically stabilises after the initial moral apprehension rather than constituting it.

When the moral situation is simple, this process terminates quickly. But when multiple moral demands present themselves simultaneously and cannot all be satisfied, the temporal arc extends into **deliberation**. The parent deciding whether to attend their child's school performance or stay at the hospital with an ailing relative does not experience two propositions to be ranked. Each obligation carries what might be called *phenomenal weight*—a felt quality of mattering that resists translation into a common metric. (A physicalist could redescribe this as differential activation of competing neural value signals; the phenomenological and neural descriptions may pick out the same process at different levels.) Ruth Chang's work on incommensurability resonates here: when values cannot be ranked on a single scale, the choice between them requires something beyond weighing—it requires the agent to *commit*.

## Attention in Moral Life

Simone Weil placed attention at the centre of moral life: "Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity." The phenomenology supports her insight across both perception and deliberation.

Moral perception requires attention that is **receptive rather than grasping**—open to what the situation actually presents, including properties the perceiver would prefer not to see. It must be **sustained rather than glancing**: the colleague whose behaviour seems merely annoying may, with sustained regard, reveal patterns of cruelty previously invisible. And it is **ego-diminished**: Iris Murdoch argued that self-interest, vanity, and anxiety construct a moral visual field organised around the perceiver's concerns, and that genuine moral perception requires "unselfing."

In deliberation, attention takes on a distinctive oscillatory character. Unlike focused problem-solving where attention narrows, deliberative attention moves between competing considerations, dwelling on each in turn. When attention dwells on one consideration—the suffering the honest report will cause—that consideration swells in felt significance. Then attention shifts to those harmed by the dishonesty, and *their* claim fills the moral foreground. The [[attentional-economics]] framework illuminates this: where consciousness directs its limited bandwidth determines which moral considerations receive full phenomenal presentation. Deliberative skill involves managing this oscillation so that each legitimate claim receives adequate presence before the decision is made.

## Obligation, Admiration, and Guilt

Moral experience divides into phenomenologically distinct modes. **Obligation** carries a felt quality of practical necessity—"I must help"—distinct from desire. Desire pulls; obligation binds. Kant located this demand in reason's capacity to override inclination. The Map suggests a different reading: the felt quality of obligation is irreducible phenomenal character, not a rational inference. One perceives it in the morally charged situation.

**Moral admiration**—witnessing extraordinary generosity, courage, quiet integrity—involves perceiving action as *exemplary*, not just effective. The experience carries warmth distinct from hedonic pleasure and what might be called moral luminosity: the act appears to radiate significance beyond itself. The existence of distinctly moral positive experiences supports phenomenal value pluralism.

**Guilt** provides perhaps the clearest window into moral phenomenology. Genuine guilt is not regret (wishing things were different) or shame (feeling exposed as defective). It is the experience of having violated something that mattered—a felt recognition that one's agency produced wrongness. Particularly revealing are cases of *moral remainder*: the guilt that persists after making the right choice in a tragic dilemma. A doctor who triages correctly, saving five by not treating one, may feel genuine guilt about the one lost. The guilt tracks a real moral cost that the right choice did not eliminate. The [[normative-void]] explores the possibility that some moral questions may be permanently unresolvable—and moral remainder suggests that even where reasoning reaches its limits, moral experience continues to register what matters.

## Self-Interest as Obstacle

Among the most phenomenologically distinctive features of deliberation is the felt quality of self-interest as an obstacle to moral action. Self-interest does not present itself the way mistaken beliefs do—as something to be corrected. It presents as a gravitational pull toward the comfortable and personally advantageous.

Sometimes it appears as rationalisation—the experience of one's own mind generating reasons to do what is convenient, and the further experience of recognising those reasons as motivated rather than genuine. This second-order recognition carries a quality of uncomfortable clarity. The experience of overriding self-interest has its own character too: choosing *against the grain*, acting in a direction that non-moral motivations resist. There is felt effort, akin to but distinct from physical exertion. The person who tells the truth knowing it will cost them experiences the cost as real, the resistance as real, and the choice as genuinely authored.

## Moral Clarity and Having Chosen

Sometimes competing considerations resolve into a perceptual gestalt: one path presents itself as *the thing to do*. Aristotle's *phronimos*—the person of practical wisdom—experiences this convergence frequently. Moral expertise is partly a perceptual skill, as McDowell argues in "Virtue and Reason."

But moral *unclarity* is equally important. Sometimes deliberation does not resolve. The agent must choose without felt assurance, committing without certainty. The [[normative-void]] suggests that some cases may be permanently indeterminate—not because agents lack information but because moral reality itself is indeterminate in certain domains.

The phenomenal aftermath of choosing varies with the quality of deliberation. When the agent has attended carefully to all considerations, the choice carries a quality of *ownership*—the felt difference between acting from considered judgment and acting from habit or pressure, what the Map discusses as [[authentic-vs-inauthentic-choice|authentic choice]]. The temporal arc from deliberative tension through commitment to ownership or remainder constitutes a unified phenomenal episode—a [[diachronic-agency-and-personal-narrative|diachronic]] exercise of agency, not a series of discrete neural events.

## Against Reductive Accounts

### Projectivism and Illusionism

Projectivism—the view that moral properties are projected onto the world rather than perceived in it—faces a phenomenological challenge. Moral experience does not present itself as projection. When witnessing cruelty, one experiences "this is wrong," not "I disapprove and project disapproval." The projectivist can invoke the sun analogy (we experience it as moving), but in the sun case we have independent checks; in the moral case, the phenomenal datum *is* the primary evidence.

If [[illusionism]] is correct—if phenomenal consciousness is itself an introspective illusion—moral phenomenology inherits the problem. But the motivational response is especially sharp here: people sacrifice comfort, safety, and life itself because of how moral situations feel. If felt wrongness were merely a functional state without phenomenal character, this motivational power would be miraculous.

### Dual-Process Psychology and Confabulation

Greene's dual-process framework and Haidt's social intuitionist model show that snap intuitions dominate many moral responses, with what feels like deliberation often serving as post-hoc rationalisation. This is serious. But three qualifications apply: extended real-world deliberation—over days, where positions genuinely shift—fits poorly into the confabulation model. Even within dual-process frameworks, System 2 sometimes overrides System 1, which Greene himself treats as genuine deliberation. And the confabulation concern applies to *all* phenomenal reports; taken to its conclusion, it undermines all introspective evidence.

### Against Epiphenomenalism

The epiphenomenalist must hold that neural computation would proceed identically whether or not accompanied by rich deliberative phenomenology. This requires treating the entire phenomenology as systematically coincidental: the experience of weighing considerations happens to co-occur with a computational process that weighs them; the experience of effort happens to co-occur with resource reallocation; the experience of moral clarity happens to co-occur with situations where the agent acts well. A thoroughgoing epiphenomenalist can maintain this position. But the systematic coincidence is less plausible than the straightforward reading: deliberation feels like conscious work because it is conscious work.

Honesty requires noting that the physicalist identity theorist—who holds that conscious states *are* neural states—has no coincidence to explain away. The divergence between identity theory and dualism lies elsewhere: in the hard problem, conceivability arguments, and whether functional description exhausts what there is to say about experience. Moral deliberation sharpens the question but does not settle it alone.

## Contemplative Evidence

[[witness-consciousness|Contemplative traditions]] report that sustained moral attention reveals deeper layers of moral experience. Buddhist *mettā* practice involves cultivating goodwill until it becomes a stable feature of awareness, described not as manufacturing a feeling but as uncovering something already present. (Buddhist metaphysics interprets this through dependent origination and *anattā* rather than substance dualism; the Map takes the phenomenological reports as evidence while recognising the frameworks diverge.) Practitioners report features characteristic of perception rather than construction: encountering resistance, surprise, and correction from the object of attention. Sustained moral attention does not reveal a projection mechanism; it reveals more moral structure.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The phenomenology of moral life connects to the Map's [[tenets|foundational commitments]] across the full arc from perception to choice.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: The gestalt character of moral perception—wrongness organising an entire perceptual field—resists functional reduction. No description of amygdala activation captures what it is like to perceive wrongness. The qualitative distinctness of competing moral demands in deliberation—obligation's binding character, admiration's luminosity, guilt's backward-looking weight—compounds the explanatory gap.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: Moral perception depends on attentional quality that consciousness actively constitutes. The sustained, receptive, ego-diminished attention that reveals moral properties is itself a conscious act. In deliberation, the felt effort of overriding self-interest tracks real cognitive reallocation—a pattern better explained by genuine conscious participation than by systematic coincidence between phenomenology and function.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]**: The indexical structure of moral deliberation presupposes singular outcomes. The deliberator experiences themselves as the single locus of a choice that will determine one outcome. Under many-worlds, every option is actualised, and the question "what should *I* do?" loses its grip. The phenomenology of moral gravity is coherent only if outcomes are genuinely selected, not branched into.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: The most parsimonious account reduces moral experience to emotional reaction plus neural computation. But this cannot accommodate the gestalt character, the temporal profile (pre-reflective apprehension preceding emotion), the deliberative oscillation of attention, or the deepening through sustained regard. Parsimony purchased by ignoring the primary data is false economy.
