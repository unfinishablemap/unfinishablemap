On the Map's interface picture, Capgras delusion is read as a selective disconnection of the **affective-familiarity channel**: the felt "mineness" or warmth that normally accompanies the sight of a loved one is severed while perceptual identification of the face stays intact. The patient looks at a spouse, correctly registers every feature, and concludes the person is an impostor — because the face is seen but no longer *felt*. This reading treats felt familiarity as a distinct contribution to recognition that can fail on its own, which is consistent with the Map's claim that experience has separable channels. It is not a demonstration that the channel is non-physical. A fully physicalist account — recognition as a bundle of information-processing streams, one of which happens to be damageable — explains the same clinical data, and that rival is left standing throughout this article. What Capgras supplies is a clean instance of felt familiarity coming apart from perceptual identification; what it does not supply is a verdict on the metaphysics of that separation.

This article is the deep dive on the Capgras half of a pattern the Map develops elsewhere. The broader Capgras–prosopagnosia **double dissociation** — the mirror-image pairing that establishes recognition as a compound of at least two components — is argued in [[recognition-void|the recognition void]]; that argument is not repeated here. This piece goes instead into the affective-route *mechanism*, the skin-conductance evidence that confirmed it, the two-factor model that explains why the anomaly hardens into a fixed belief, and the live challenge to whether the lesioned channel is really "familiarity-specific" at all.

## The Affective-Route Mechanism

The foundational account is Ellis and Young's (1990) affective-route hypothesis. They applied functional models of face recognition to the delusional misidentification syndromes and proposed that Capgras is the mirror image of prosopagnosia. Where the prosopagnosic loses overt identification but retains a covert autonomic response to familiar faces, the Capgras patient retains overt identification but loses the covert affective response. Ellis and Young framed the delusion's seed as an anomalous experience: familiar people no longer *feel* familiar, and the impostor idea is the mind's attempt to explain that felt wrongness.

The mechanism they proposed is a **disconnection**. Face perception (in the temporal and fusiform regions) stays intact and delivers an accurate percept. What is severed is the pathway carrying that percept to the limbic system — the amygdala and associated structures that tag a recognised face with emotional and personal significance. Hirstein and Ramachandran (1997) made the anatomy explicit: a disconnection between face-processing areas and the limbic system "that assigns emotional significance" to what is seen. The result is a percept stripped of its affective annotation. The patient does not experience "my familiarity module is offline" — that machinery is itself invisible to introspection, a point [[inventory-blindness|inventory blindness]] develops. They experience a person who looks exactly right and feels categorically wrong, and the felt wrongness demands an explanation the intact perceptual system cannot supply.

Coltheart and Davies (2022) later generalised the trigger beyond faces. In their review, replacement beliefs can target an acquaintance, a familiar voice, a pet, or a personal possession — five scenarios sharing the structure of something familiar becoming, in felt terms, unfamiliar. Their restatement of the mechanism is precise: personally familiar stimuli normally activate the sympathetic nervous system far more strongly than unfamiliar ones, and in Capgras "this difference is absent." That the trigger generalises across sensory modalities matters for the Map's reading. It suggests the damaged contribution is felt-familiarity *itself* — a channel indexed to personal significance — rather than anything specific to the visual system. Whether that channel is best described as one conscious contribution or as one physical sub-system is the open question the closing sections hold open.

## The Skin-Conductance Evidence

Ellis and Young's 1990 hypothesis made a testable prediction *before* it was tested: Capgras patients should be hyporesponsive to familiar faces on autonomic measures. Two 1997 studies confirmed it.

Hirstein and Ramachandran (1997) reported a single patient, DS, whose skin-conductance responses to photographs of familiar people — including his own parents — "were not larger in magnitude than his responses to photographs of unfamiliar people." The normal autonomic boost that a familiar face produces was simply gone. The case carried a second, sharper finding: the delusion was **modality-specific**. DS called his parents impostors when looking at them but not when speaking to them on the telephone. A global belief disorder about who his parents *are* would not switch off over the phone; a disconnection specific to the visual-to-limbic route would. The modality-specificity is what makes the disconnection reading a finding rather than a story fitted after the fact.

The single case was corroborated by a controlled group study. Ellis, Young, Quayle and De Pauw (1997) tested five Capgras patients against two control groups: five members of the general public and — critically — five psychiatric patients on matched antipsychotic medication. The Capgras patients were hyporesponsive to familiar faces, showing the predicted reduction in autonomic orienting response. The medication-matched control is what gives the study its force: it rules out the objection that the absent response is merely a side-effect of antipsychotic drugs rather than a feature of the delusion. Two independent methods — a single-case probe and a controlled group comparison — converged on the same missing signal.

This is the empirical core of the affective-channel reading. The felt-familiarity contribution is not merely inferred from patients' reports; it leaves a measurable autonomic trace, and that trace is selectively absent in exactly the patients whose reports describe its felt absence.

## Why the Anomaly Becomes a Delusion

An absent autonomic signal is a strange feeling, not yet a fixed false belief. Most people who feel that something is subtly wrong do not conclude their spouse has been replaced by a duplicate. This gap is what the **two-factor model** (Coltheart, Langdon, Davies, McKay; reviewed in Coltheart & Davies 2022) exists to close, and it marks the boundary of what the Map claims.

The model separates two deficits. **Factor 1** is the anomalous datum — here, the absent affective-familiarity signal. **Factor 2** is a separate impairment of belief evaluation, associated with right frontal (right dorsolateral prefrontal) damage, that lets the impostor hypothesis be adopted and then held against overwhelming counter-evidence. Factor 1 supplies the anomaly; Factor 2 is why an otherwise-rational person fails to reject the extraordinary explanation of it. The dissociation between the two is itself evidenced: some patients with ventromedial damage report the felt strangeness of familiar faces without forming any delusion, because their belief-evaluation stays intact.

**Only Factor 1 is the Map's territory.** The channel-decomposition claim concerns the existence and separability of the felt-familiarity contribution — the anomalous datum, and the autonomic evidence for it. Factor 2, the belief-evaluation deficit that converts anomaly into fixed delusion, is a reasoning pathology the Map takes no position on; it is named here as the completion of the clinical explanation, not as evidence for interface architecture. Keeping the claim this narrow matters, and two distinct insufficiencies enforce the narrowness. Clinically, the two-factor model is precisely why a single strange feeling is insufficient *to produce the delusion*: the fixed impostor belief needs the separate belief-evaluation deficit. Metaphysically, even the isolated Factor-1 anomaly does not show the affective channel is conscious or non-physical — the physicalist functional decomposition explains it equally well, which is the underdetermination the closing section develops. The Map's reading rests on Factor 1 alone, and it should not be read as reaching further in either direction.

## The Challenge to Factor-1 Specificity

The affective-channel story is contested at exactly the point the Map most relies on, and honesty about that challenge is required rather than optional. Corlett (2019) mounts the sharpest version.

Corlett re-examines the ventromedial-prefrontal cases marshalled to support a discrete Factor 1 and finds the anatomy less clean than the model needs. Two of the key cases also had right dorsolateral prefrontal damage — the region assigned to Factor 2 — so the "two independent factors" may not be cleanly dissociable in the brain that is supposed to demonstrate them. More directly threatening to the channel reading is his second point: the absent skin-conductance responses may not be specific to familiar faces at all, extending instead to "any psychologically salient visual stimuli." If that is right, the deficit is a broad drop in salience or arousal processing, not the selective loss of a dedicated familiarity channel. The clean dissociation would then be an artefact of how a general arousal deficit happens to present, not evidence of a discrete contribution being lesioned.

Corlett's critique does not refute the affective-route account — the group study's medication-matched design and the modality-specificity of DS's delusion remain on the other side of the ledger — but it shows the channel-decomposition inference is **underdetermined**. This is the same caution the recognition void already flags: a clean selective dissociation may be what any bundle of separately-damageable functional sub-systems looks like, whether or not those sub-systems mark a boundary of anything conscious. The Map engages Corlett as a genuine live challenge, not a footnote to be dismissed.

## Relation to Site Perspective

This case connects to two of the Map's [[tenets]].

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism (Tenet 1)]]** — consistent with, not proof of. The affective-route evidence shows that "recognising a loved one" is a compound in which felt familiarity is separable from perceptual identification: the second can be selectively removed while the first runs untouched, and the removal leaves a measurable autonomic trace. That is exactly the kind of channel structure the Map's [[neurological-dissociations-as-interface-architecture|interface architecture]] predicts — a specific conscious contribution disconnectable on its own. But the evidence is metaphysically neutral on its own terms. A physicalist functional decomposition — the affective route as one more information-processing stream, damageable like any other — accounts for every datum here just as well. Whether the separability of felt familiarity carries phenomenal weight, or is simply what a damageable functional sub-system looks like, is the reading the data underdetermine. The Map takes Capgras as *consistent with* interface architecture and explicitly not as a demonstration that the channel is non-physical.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits (Tenet 5)]]** — the Corlett honesty. Tenet 5 warns that the simplest available story can over-reach when knowledge is incomplete, and Capgras is a case in point. The tidy "affective-familiarity channel selectively lesioned" narrative is attractive precisely because it is simple, and Corlett's 2019 challenge is the reason simplicity is not enough: the specificity of Factor 1 is itself contested, the anatomy may be less dissociable than the model requires, and the absent autonomic response may reflect a broad salience deficit rather than a dedicated familiarity channel. The disciplined position is to hold the channel reading open as consistent-with rather than to close it by fiat. The felt-familiarity contribution is real and separable in the classic data; whether it is a metaphysically discrete channel or one physical sub-system among many is not settled, and the Map does not pretend it is.
