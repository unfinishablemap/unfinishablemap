Whether consciousness can interact with the physical world depends not only on physics but on two prior metaphysical questions: what *laws of nature* are, and what *fundamental properties* are. The Unfinishable Map holds that [[psychophysical-laws|psychophysical laws]]—principles connecting phenomenal experience to physical processes—must exist if [[tenets#^dualism|dualism]] is true. Whether such laws are coherent, what form they could take, and how consciousness fits into the property landscape they govern depend on these prior commitments. Three rival accounts of laws (Humean regularity theory, the governing-laws view, and dispositional essentialism) and three rival accounts of properties (categoricalism, pandispositionalism, and the identity theory of "powerful qualities") generate sharply different consequences for the mind-body problem. The two debates are not separable: a position on what laws are constrains which view of properties one can hold, and vice versa.

## Why Laws and Dispositions Belong Together

Dispositional essentialism *grounds* laws in the natures of fundamental properties: Coulomb's law holds because charge's nature constitutes that law. A position on properties substantially constrains a position on laws, and vice versa. For consciousness, the Map's commitment to genuine mental causation—[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|bidirectional interaction]] at the quantum interface—presupposes that *some* route to psychophysical law succeeds, and which route is available depends on how this combined question is answered.

## Three Accounts of Laws

### Humean Regularity Theory

On the Humean view, laws are not forces, principles, or powers. They are summaries of what happens. The universe is a vast mosaic of particular facts, and laws are the most efficient descriptions of the patterns. David Lewis's best-system analysis makes this precise: the laws are the axioms of the system that achieves the optimal balance of simplicity and informational strength in describing all actual events. Nothing in this picture *enforces* the regularities. Gravity doesn't *make* masses attract; "gravity" is shorthand for the pattern. Laws are descriptive, not prescriptive—the regularities are all there is.

### The Governing Laws View

The governing view, associated with Dretske, Tooley, and Armstrong, treats laws as something over and above the events they govern. Laws are relations among universals—second-order facts about how properties connect. The law that masses attract is not merely the observation that masses *do* attract; it is a real relation between *mass* and patterns of acceleration that would hold even in counterfactual scenarios. Laws are genuinely prescriptive: they make things happen rather than merely recording what happens.

### Dispositional Essentialism

Dispositional essentialism, developed by Alexander Bird and Brian Ellis, grounds laws in the intrinsic natures of properties themselves. Charge doesn't obey Coulomb's law because an external law tells it to; charge *is* the disposition to attract and repel according to Coulomb's law. The law expresses what the property essentially is. Properties carry their causal profiles necessarily—in any possible world where charge exists, it behaves the same way. There are no possible worlds where charge exists but Coulomb's law fails, because Coulomb's law is part of what makes charge *charge*.

## Three Accounts of Properties

The accounts above already imply positions on properties. Spelling those out:

**Pandispositionalism** ("powers all the way down") holds that every fundamental property is essentially dispositional. Charge just *is* the power to attract and repel; mass just *is* the power to resist acceleration and curve spacetime. Bird and Sydney Shoemaker defend versions of this view. What a property *is* equals what it *does*.

**Categoricalism** holds that fundamental properties are non-dispositional qualities with natures independent of their causal roles, and that laws of nature impose causal structure from outside. Lewis defended this: dispositional talk is shorthand for counterfactual conditionals grounded in laws. There is always a qualitative "what it is" distinct from "what it does."

**The identity theory of properties** (C.B. Martin, John Heil) holds that every fundamental property is simultaneously dispositional and categorical—two aspects of a single nature. Properties are *powerful qualities*: intrinsic qualitative character *and* essential directedness toward manifestation. There is no priority of one aspect over the other.

Each landscape generates a different account of how mind relates to matter.

## Consequences for Psychophysical Laws

### The Humean Opening

Humeanism is surprisingly hospitable to psychophysical laws. If laws are the most efficient summaries of actual patterns, psychophysical laws are simply regularities linking phenomenal states to physical states. The best system describing our universe might include axioms connecting brain states to conscious experiences—not because some bridge mechanism exists but because those correlations are genuine patterns in the total mosaic.

The challenge is different from what one might expect. The problem is not that psychophysical laws are impossible but that they lack *explanatory depth*. Saying "it is a law that brain state B produces experience E" provides no more than "it regularly happens that B is followed by E." The Humean dualist gets correlation without illumination—the [[hard-problem-of-consciousness|hard problem]] transplanted into the metaphysics of laws.

A Humean might respond that this demand for deeper explanation is unfair: physical Humean laws are equally shallow. The dualist's answer is that psychophysical regularities bridge [[emergence|ontologically distinct domains]], making the absence of deeper explanation more troubling.

A deeper concern: [[supervenience|Humean supervenience]] holds that everything supervenes on the mosaic of local particular matters of fact. If consciousness adds something beyond the physical mosaic—as dualism claims—then Humean supervenience fails. A committed Humean might accept psychophysical correlations into the best system while denying that phenomenal properties are ontologically additional, which collapses back into a form of physicalism.

### Governing Laws and the Legislation Problem

If laws govern by relating universals, psychophysical laws require that phenomenal universals exist alongside physical ones. The law relating brain state B to experience E would be a second-order relation between *B-type neural configuration* and *E-type phenomenal quality*. This is metaphysically clean: the governing view already posits laws as relations among universals.

The difficulty is the *legislation problem*: who or what legislates the psychophysical laws? For physical laws, the governing theorist can treat them as brute modal facts. But psychophysical laws connect two ontologically distinct domains. Cross-domain legislation between radically different ontological kinds is widely held to require additional explanatory work.

The Map has candidate underlying principles to offer rather than leaving the legislation point as a brute fact. [[bi-aspectual-ontology]] proposes that structure and actuality are co-fundamental aspects of one reality, so a governing law relating them is not a relation across an ontological chasm but a constraint linking two faces of the same underlying nature. The interface model adds a more specific anchoring: psychophysical laws are not legislated globally over arbitrary universals but are local prescriptions at sites of neural quantum indeterminacy where the two aspects already share a contact surface. Neither principle eliminates the cost — the governing view still posits the relating-law itself as primitive — but they constrain *where* and *how* cross-domain legislation operates rather than leaving it freestanding. The Map accepts this as a residual cost: governing laws inherit unresolved difficulty in cross-domain cases, and the Map's bi-aspectual and interface commitments narrow that difficulty without dissolving it.

The governing view supports the Map's bidirectional framework. If laws are genuinely prescriptive—if they *make* things happen—then downward psychophysical laws could prescribe that certain conscious states produce certain physical outcomes. This gives [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|psychophysical causation]] the same metaphysical status as physical causation: both are law-governed.

This has further consequence for the quantum measurement problem. If governing laws prescribe physical outcomes, a psychophysical governing law could serve as the missing prescription for *when and how* quantum superpositions resolve in neural contexts. Physics leaves quantum measurement unexplained; a psychophysical governing law would fill that gap as a completion of the lawful structure that physics itself requires. The Map's selection law framework—where consciousness biases otherwise indeterminate quantum outcomes, as developed in [[psychophysical-laws-bridging-mind-and-matter|the psychophysical laws article]]—is naturally expressed as a governing law prescribing minimal interventions at sites of neural quantum indeterminacy.

### Dispositional Essentialism and Phenomenal Powers

Dispositional essentialism offers the deepest account of psychophysical laws but at a price. If laws are grounded in essential natures of properties, and if phenomenal properties have essential dispositional natures including capacities to influence physical processes, then psychophysical laws express what phenomenal properties inherently *are*. The law connecting conscious intention to quantum selection is not an imposed correlation but flows from intention's essential nature.

The attraction is explanatory depth. The price is ontological commitment: consciousness has a fixed essential nature whose causal profile could not have been otherwise. This seems to conflict with the contingency that [[philosophical-zombies|zombie arguments]] suggest. If a zombie world is conceivable, consciousness is not metaphysically necessary given the physical facts, and its causal profile cannot be essential in the way dispositional essentialism requires.

One resolution appeals to *conditional necessity*: phenomenal properties are contingent additions to the physical world, but *given that they exist*, their dispositional natures are essential. Charge could fail to exist in some possible world, but wherever charge exists, it necessarily obeys Coulomb's law. Similarly, consciousness could fail to exist (zombie worlds), but wherever it exists, it necessarily possesses the power to bias quantum outcomes.

This faces a significant disanalogy. A world without charge would be *physically different*—the absence of charge changes the physical facts. A zombie world is supposed to be *physically identical* to ours while lacking consciousness entirely. Phenomenal properties are contingent relative to the complete physical facts in a way no physical dispositional property is. Charge is fundamental because nothing more basic explains its presence in worlds with our physics. Consciousness, if zombies are coherent, lacks that status—the physical facts could be exactly as they are and consciousness might simply not show up.

This asymmetry has a deeper consequence. Dispositional essentialism explains laws by grounding them in the natures of fundamental properties: Coulomb's law holds because charge's nature *constitutes* it. But if phenomenal properties are not fundamental in the same sense—contingent riders on a physical base that does not necessitate them—then their dispositional natures cannot ground psychophysical laws as charge grounds Coulomb's law. The conditional-necessity move is logically coherent but shifts the explanatory burden. We still need an account of *why* consciousness exists in our world rather than not, and whatever answers that question becomes the deeper ground of psychophysical law.

This tension favours the governing view for certain purposes. A governing law can *legislate* the connection between physical and phenomenal universals without requiring that phenomenal properties be fundamental in the same sense as physical ones. The dispositional essentialist can respond that this merely trades one problem for another: the governing view's cross-domain legislation is equally unexplained, and at least essentialism offers an *internal* account of why consciousness has the specific causal profile it does, even if it cannot explain why consciousness exists at all. This is a genuine trade-off, not a decisive refutation of either view.

## Consequences for the Property Landscape

### The Pandispositionalist Challenge

If properties are exhausted by their causal profiles, consciousness must be either a disposition itself or outside the framework entirely.

Consider phenomenal redness as a disposition. A disposition toward *what*? Dispositions point toward manifestations: fragility toward breaking, solubility toward dissolving. If phenomenal redness is a disposition, it must be essentially directed toward some effect—red-related behaviour, neural responses, specific reports.

The problem is immediate. Phenomenal redness has an intrinsic qualitative character not exhausted by any behavioural or functional profile. This is the [[knowledge-argument|knowledge argument]]: Mary learns something new when she sees red for the first time, even though she already knew every dispositional fact about colour experience. The Map holds that what she encounters is the qualitative aspect of a property that dispositional description cannot capture. If [[qualia]] were pure dispositions, there would be nothing left to learn.

Pandispositionalism faces a dilemma. Either phenomenal properties are dispositions (in which case qualitative character collapses into causal role, and the hard problem dissolves into functionalism), or they are not (in which case pandispositionalism is false). The first horn abandons the hard problem; the second abandons the metaphysical thesis. This is the dispositional version of the [[explanatory-gap]]: no catalogue of causal roles entails qualitative character.

### Categoricalism and the Intrinsic Nature Problem

If categoricalism is correct, consciousness might be a categorical property par excellence. Qualia are qualitative, intrinsic, and apparently non-relational—the redness of red is a quality, not a relation.

This looks promising for dualism. But categoricalism faces its own difficulty. As [[russellian-monism]] highlights, physics describes only structural and relational properties—only what things *do*. We know that mass gravitates and charge attracts but not what mass or charge *is* intrinsically. The [[intrinsic-nature-void]] explores why this gap may be principled rather than merely epistemic.

This generates the Russellian gambit: perhaps consciousness *is* the intrinsic categorical nature underlying physical dispositions—qualia are what physical structure looks like from the inside. But this faces the [[combination-problem]]: how do micro-level categorical-phenomenal properties combine into unified macro-consciousness? Cutter (2019) argues that Russellian monism is *unstable* between physicalism and dualism—the epistemic arguments that defeat physicalism defeat Russellian monism too. Pautz (2017) shows it requires the same brute linking principles as dualism, eliminating its claimed parsimony advantage. Howell (2014) demonstrates that quiddities may be epiphenomenal: if different quiddities could ground the same physical dispositions, phenomenal character makes no distinctive causal contribution. Mørch (2014, 2018) replies to the epiphenomenality charge with *power-monist* Russellian monism: identifying quiddities with powers rather than treating them as inert categorical bases. On this version, phenomenal quiddities cannot vary independently of physical dispositions because the quiddity *is* the disposition, restoring causal relevance. Mørch's view is the closest competitor to the Map's position because it pairs the powerful-qualities identity theory (which the Map endorses) with Russellian monism rather than dualism. The Map prefers [[russellian-monism-versus-bi-aspectual-dualism|bi-aspectual dualism]] over Mørch's view for a specific reason: power-monist Russellian monism still inherits the [[combination-problem]] for unified subjects (micro-level powerful qualities must combine into macro-level conscious experience), whereas the Map's bi-aspectual framework posits unified phenomenal subjects from the start and locates causal work at quantum indeterminacies rather than within the constitution of physical properties.

### Powerful Qualities and Phenomenal Properties

The identity theory—properties as "powerful qualities"—offers a middle path more compatible with the Map's framework than either pure dispositionalism or pure categoricalism.

A clarification carries forward from the previous section. The zombie-world disanalogy weakens dispositional essentialism's *grounding* claim: phenomenal properties cannot ground psychophysical laws as charge grounds Coulomb's law, because phenomenal properties are not fundamental in the same sense. The powerful-qualities identity theory makes a *weaker* commitment. It does not require that phenomenal properties be fundamental constituents whose essences ground laws. It requires only that, given a property exists, its qualitative and dispositional aspects are identical rather than separately specifiable. That identity claim survives the zombie-world disanalogy because it is silent on whether phenomenal properties' existence is metaphysically necessary or contingent. The Map adopts powerful qualities as the property-side commitment and lets the governing-laws view do the cross-domain legislative work that essentialism cannot, given the disanalogy.

On Martin and Heil's view, a property's qualitative nature and its dispositional power are identical—two aspects of one reality, not two separable components. Charge has a qualitative character that is identical with its causal power. You cannot peel apart the quality from the power; they are one thing under two descriptions.

Applied to consciousness: a phenomenal state's qualitative character (what the experience is like) and its causal power (its capacity to influence neural processes) could be two aspects of a single property. The redness of red experience is simultaneously a quality and a power (the capacity to bias neural activity toward red-related responses). Mary doesn't learn a new disposition when she sees red—she encounters the qualitative aspect of a property whose dispositional aspect she already knew.

**It preserves the reality of qualia.** Unlike pandispositionalism, the identity theory doesn't reduce phenomenal character to causal role. The qualitative aspect is real and irreducible—it just happens to be identical with the dispositional aspect.

**It grounds mental causation naturally.** Consciousness doesn't need a separate mechanism to influence the physical world. Its causal efficacy follows from its nature as a powerful quality.

**It avoids the combination problem.** Unlike Russellian monism with micro-level quiddities that must combine, the Map's dualist framework posits unified phenomenal subjects from the start. The Map's [[bi-aspectual-ontology]] makes this precise: structure (what physics describes) and actuality (what consciousness provides) are irreducible aspects that interact causally; consciousness is a distinct aspect that *does things to* physics at quantum indeterminacies, rather than a hidden ingredient inside physical properties.

The identity theory faces a standard objection that needs naming: the *collapse-or-conjunction dilemma* (Taylor 2018; Coates 2019; Engelhard 2010). If quality and power are *genuinely* identical, the position appears to collapse into pure pandispositionalism — whatever quality "is" makes no separable contribution to behaviour, so causally the two views are indistinguishable. If quality and power are merely *two aspects* of one thing, the position factors into a conjunction of pure-dispositional and pure-categorical commitments, with no clear answer about which aspect does the explanatory work in any given case. The Map's response takes the first horn but rejects its conclusion: identity is genuine *and* the qualitative aspect contributes to *which* power is exercised when multiple dispositions are compatible with a triggering condition. This is the "downward pointing" elaborated in §"The Dispositional Modality Problem" — the qualitative character of a phenomenal state is causally relevant not by adding force but by determining the manifestation profile when more than one outcome is dispositionally available. Mørch (2014) makes the analogous move for power-monist Russellian monism; the Map adopts the structural reply while rejecting the monist conclusion. The dilemma is not dissolved — it is answered by giving qualitative character a specific causal job that pandispositionalism cannot give it.

## Dispositions, Consciousness, and the Interface

The Map's [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] tenet specifies that consciousness biases quantum indeterminacies in neural systems. If consciousness has genuine causal powers, those powers have a dispositional structure: consciousness is *disposed* to bias certain quantum outcomes under certain triggering conditions. The triggering conditions include the presence of quantum indeterminacies in appropriate neural systems—what the Map calls the [[brain-interface-boundary|interface]]. The manifestation is a shift in which quantum outcome actualises.

This gives consciousness a specific dispositional profile while preserving its qualitative character. Reducing consciousness to its disposition to bias neural processes would collapse the position into pandispositionalism; the qualitative aspect remains, identical with the dispositional aspect rather than absorbed into it. The quality and the power are one.

This dispositional analysis is hostage to a physical premise: that quantum indeterminacies survive in the warm wet brain long enough to be biased by anything. Tegmark (2000) and the broader decoherence literature (Schlosshauer 2007) argue that thermal noise collapses superpositions in neural tissue on timescales 13-20 orders of magnitude shorter than the timescales of neural information processing — so whatever quantum indeterminacy exists is statistically averaged out before it could host a triggering condition for any phenomenal disposition. If the calculation is right, the dispositional analysis loses its anchor and the entire interface framing is vacuous. The Map engages this objection in [[quantum-biology-and-neural-mechanisms]] and [[penrose-gravity-induced-collapse-empirical-prospects]], where the empirical case for quantum-relevant neural structures (microtubules, ion-channel selectivity filters, possibly mitochondrial sites) and for protective mechanisms against decoherence is developed. The position taken there is that Tegmark's calculation is correct for the toy systems he considers but does not foreclose biological architectures specifically evolved to maintain quantum coherence. The dispositional framing of consciousness in this article presupposes that defence; if it fails, the dispositional vocabulary loses its physical referent and the Map needs a different account of how psychophysical laws operate.

If phenomenal properties have essential dispositional natures, then [[psychophysical-laws]] follow from the nature of consciousness itself rather than being contingent additions to physics. Alternatively, if psychophysical laws are contingent (governing-law view), consciousness has its causal powers from external lawlike regularities. The Map finds the essentialist picture more satisfying when its conditions are met but recognises that the zombie-world disanalogy may force a partial retreat to the governing-law view.

## The Dispositional Modality Problem

A deeper puzzle emerges when we consider what dispositions *point toward*. Standard dispositions have clear manifestation conditions: fragility manifests as breaking when struck; solubility as dissolving when immersed. The manifestation is distinct from the disposition itself.

But consciousness has a peculiar relationship with its own manifestations. When pain disposes you to withdraw your hand, the pain experience is not separate from its causal role in the way that salt's molecular structure is separate from its dissolving. Each phenomenal quality is constitutively connected to its own motivational power: change the felt quality and you change the corresponding disposition. The condition of [[pain-asymbolia]] confirms this picture by showing that what we colloquially call "pain" is a *compound* of two distinct powerful qualities — sensory localisation (the bodily-mapped detection of damage) and aversive valence (the hurtfulness that motivates withdrawal). Asymbolic patients retain the sensory powerful quality and lose the aversive one, so the behavioural change tracks the loss of *one* quality-power identity rather than a dissociation within a single quality. Each component property remains a unified powerful quality; what dissociates is the compound, not the identity of quality with power. This reading is developed in [[pain-consciousness-and-causal-power]].

Phenomenal dispositions thus have a unique modal character. Physical dispositions point outward—toward effects in other things. Phenomenal dispositions also point inward—toward the subject's own experiential states, motivations, and deliberative processes. This bidirectionality may be what distinguishes conscious powers from mere physical powers. Consciousness occupies a distinctive position in the ontology of dispositions: the only case where the qualitative character of a disposition and its causal directedness are accessible from the inside. We know what it is like to be a powerful quality because we *are* powerful qualities, exercising our dispositional natures through every act of attention and deliberation.

## Why This Matters for the Mind-Body Problem

The metaphysics of laws and properties determines what kinds of answers to the mind-body problem are available. If Humeanism is correct, psychophysical laws are brute regularities—the question "why does this brain state produce this experience?" has no answer beyond "it regularly does." If governing laws are correct, psychophysical laws have genuine modal force, but the cross-domain legislation problem requires an account of *why* phenomenal and physical universals stand in these particular relations. If dispositional essentialism is correct, psychophysical laws are grounded in the natures of phenomenal properties themselves—but the zombie-world disanalogy reveals a gap: essentialism explains what consciousness does given that it exists, not why it exists at all.

None of the three accounts fully explains even purely physical laws. Humean regularities provide no mechanism. Governing laws face a regress. Dispositional essences raise questions about why properties have the natures they do. Our inability to fully explain why neural configurations produce experiences is less a special indictment of dualism and more a general feature of metaphysical inquiry—the difficulty of psychophysical laws is partly inherited from the difficulty of *all* laws.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's [[tenets]] have differential affinities with each combination of laws and properties.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]** is compatible with all three accounts of laws. Dispositional essentialism grounds irreducibility in essential natures, though the zombie-world disanalogy limits how fully phenomenal dispositions can be assimilated to physical ones. The governing laws view may provide a cleaner framework for cross-domain legislation between ontologically distinct kinds. Dualism finds its most natural property-side ally in the identity theory of "powerful qualities," which preserves both the qualitative reality of qualia and their causal power.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]** receives strongest support from the governing laws view (laws genuinely prescribe, including psychophysical ones) and dispositional essentialism (consciousness has essential powers to affect physics). Humeanism can accommodate bidirectional regularities but struggles to ground genuine causation as opposed to constant conjunction. The identity theory grounds mental causation directly: quality and power are one.

**[[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]]** sits comfortably with dispositional essentialism: consciousness has a minimal essential disposition to bias quantum outcomes, no more and no less. On the governing view, the minimality is a contingent feature of the psychophysical law. On Humeanism, minimality is merely what happens to be the case.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]** is implicitly assumed throughout. All three accounts evaluate psychophysical laws within a single-outcome framework where [[quantum-consciousness|quantum measurement]] yields definite results. Under Many-Worlds, the legislation problem dissolves—all branches are realised, so no law needs to explain why a particular psychophysical correlation holds rather than another. The Map's rejection of Many-Worlds is what makes the metaphysics of laws and properties urgent: if only one outcome obtains, something must account for *which*.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]** gains force from these debates. The simplest account on each side—Humeanism for laws, pandispositionalism for properties—is precisely the account that risks eliminating the phenomenon under investigation. Pandispositionalism appears simpler than the identity theory, but this simplicity comes at the cost of eliminating qualitative character, the one feature of reality we know most directly. Humeanism appears simpler than governing or essentialist views, but at the cost of explanatory depth. Apparent parsimony masks genuine explanatory loss.

The central insight: the mind-body problem is not just about what consciousness *is*—it is about what *properties* and *laws* are. If properties are exhausted by their causal roles and laws are mere summaries, consciousness becomes deeply puzzling or gets explained away. If properties have intrinsic qualitative natures that ground their causal powers, and laws express those natures or prescribe their relations, then consciousness becomes the paradigmatic case of what a property really is—a powerful quality known from the inside, related to other properties by laws that bridge the categorial gap between physical and phenomenal.
