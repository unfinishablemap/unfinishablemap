The Map commits to dualism: consciousness is irreducible to physical process. That commitment leaves a question it has not resolved. Granting a non-physical aspect of mind, *is consciousness constitutive of it* — so that the non-physical aspect cannot exist without being conscious — *or is consciousness a quality it sometimes bears and sometimes lacks*, so that the non-physical aspect can persist unconscious, as in dreamless sleep? The first is the **essential-consciousness** thesis; the second the **contingent-consciousness** thesis. They are compatible with the same dualist starting point and divide three of the most sophisticated traditions ever to examine the question.

This is not a verbal dispute. It changes what survives dreamless sleep, what (if anything) persists between incarnations or after death, whether "pure consciousness" without an object is the deepest layer of mind or a contradiction in terms, and which Western theory of the minimal self the Map should adopt. The classical Indian schools mapped these consequences with a rigour Western philosophy has rarely matched. This article surveys the positions, then states the Map's reading — honestly, as a metaphysical commitment the evidence underdetermines rather than forces. The framing follows the [[indian-philosophy-of-mind|Indian philosophy of mind survey]], which raises this open question without resolving it.

## Two Theses, Sharply Drawn

The dispute is best fixed by what each thesis says about the limiting case: a stretch of existence in which the candidate subject is, by hypothesis, not conscious of anything. Dreamless sleep is the standard test case; the gap between death and any putative continuation is another.

**Essential consciousness.** Consciousness is the nature of the non-physical aspect, not one of its properties. There is no unconscious phase of it to describe, because "unconscious non-physical mind" names nothing. Apparent gaps (dreamless sleep) are gaps in *content* or in *memory*, not gaps in *consciousness itself* — awareness continues, contentless and unremembered.

**Contingent consciousness.** The non-physical aspect is a substance or bearer that *has* consciousness when conditions are met and lacks it otherwise. Consciousness comes and goes; the bearer persists across the gap, genuinely unconscious during it. The self is not *what is aware* but *the thing that becomes aware when connected appropriately*.

The two make incompatible predictions about the same datum. Both must explain why we report dreamless sleep as a blank. The essentialist says: awareness continued but laid down no episodic trace, so nothing is recalled. The contingentist says: there was nothing to recall because awareness was genuinely absent and only the bearer persisted. No introspective report can decide between "I was aware but remember nothing" and "I was not aware," because both predict the same blank — a point the Map's [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]] makes central, returned to under "The Map's Reading" below.

## Advaita Vedanta: Consciousness as Essence

Advaita gives the cleanest essential-consciousness view in any tradition. Shankara (c. 788–820 CE) holds that consciousness (*cit*) is *svaprakasha* — self-luminous, revealing itself without requiring a further awareness to be known. Consciousness is not a property of *atman*; it *is atman*, identical with *Brahman*, the sole reality. On this view there can be no unconscious self, because "self" and "consciousness" pick out the same thing.

The load-bearing move is the treatment of dreamless sleep (*sushupti*). Far from a counterexample, Advaita treats it as confirmation. On waking, one reports "I slept well; I knew nothing." Advaita's analysis: the judgement "I knew nothing" reports a *known* absence of content, and an absence can be known only by a witness present throughout. The *Mandukya Upanishad*'s analysis of the three states — waking, dream, dreamless sleep — places a continuous witnessing consciousness (*prajna*) beneath all three. What is absent in dreamless sleep is the *object* and the *ego that grasps objects*, not awareness. See [[witness-consciousness]] for the *sakshi* (witness-self) this analysis turns on.

The argument for self-luminosity is a regress argument. If a cognition required a second cognition to be known, the second would require a third, and so on without end; nothing would ever be known. The regress terminates only if consciousness reveals itself. Self-luminosity therefore is not an empirical finding but the terminating condition of a regress — which is exactly why the contingentist can reject it without contradicting any datum: deny that the regress must terminate in self-luminosity (perhaps it terminates in a causal condition instead), and the argument loses its grip.

## Nyaya: Consciousness as Adventitious Quality

Nyaya offers the most explicit contingent-consciousness view in classical thought, and the one that motivates this article. The self (*atman*) is a non-physical substance, but consciousness is an *adventitious* quality (*agantuka guna*) — it arises when the self connects with *manas* (the internal organ) and through *manas* with the senses, and it lapses when that connection lapses. According to the *Nyaya Sutra* (Gautama, c. 2nd century CE, 1.1.10), the self is the locus of desire, aversion, effort, pleasure, pain, and cognition — but none of these defines it. During dreamless sleep the self exists without consciousness. The self persists; awareness is intermittent.

This makes the Nyaya *atman* closer to an Aristotelian substance bearing properties than to Descartes' *res cogitans*, which is defined by thinking and so cannot exist unconscious. Nyaya explicitly *rejects* self-luminosity: consciousness does not reveal itself but arises through causal processes and requires the *manas*-*atman* contact. Where Advaita's regress argument terminates in a self-revealing consciousness, Nyaya terminates the regress in a causal condition — apperception (*anuvyavasaya*), a distinct later cognition directed at the prior one, when the conditions for it obtain. Consciousness of consciousness is a further event, not a built-in luminosity.

Nyaya's reading of the dreamless-sleep report is the mirror image of Advaita's. "I knew nothing" reports a genuine absence of cognition, not a witnessed absence: the self was there, the *manas*-self contact was withdrawn, no cognition arose, and there is nothing to recall because nothing occurred. Both traditions accommodate the identical waking report; the report does not adjudicate.

## Samkhya: The Purusha Question

Samkhya complicates the binary and is worth separating because it is easy to misread. *Purusha* (pure consciousness) is, on the standard reading, *essentially* conscious — it is changeless witnessing (*saksin*) and never becomes unconscious. But Samkhya places all *mental activity* — intellect (*buddhi*), ego (*ahamkara*), coordinating mind (*manas*) — within *prakriti* (materiality). So Samkhya answers the essential/contingent question with a distinction the binary obscures: the *witness* is essentially conscious, but *cognition* (which the binary's "consciousness" often means) belongs to matter and is therefore intermittent.

This dissolves the dreamless-sleep puzzle differently again. *Purusha* never sleeps — witnessing is constant. What ceases in dreamless sleep is the *sattvic* transparency of *buddhi* that lets matter "reflect" *purusha*; the material instrument goes dark, the witness does not. The Samkhya position therefore is not a third point on the same line but a relocation of the question: *which thing* is the candidate for the non-physical aspect? If it is the witness, Samkhya is essentialist; if it is the cognitive apparatus, that apparatus is material and the question of its "consciousness" is a question about matter. The Map's framing — a single non-physical aspect that either is or is not essentially conscious — has to choose which of Samkhya's two principles it most resembles. (For the *purusha*-*prakriti* interaction problem this raises, see [[indian-philosophy-of-mind]].)

## Western Parallels

The Western canon engages the same fault line, usually without naming it as cleanly.

**Descartes: essential by definition.** *Res cogitans* is a thinking thing whose essence is thought; it cannot exist without thinking. Descartes bit the bullet and claimed the mind thinks continuously, even in dreamless sleep and the womb — a commitment Locke ridiculed precisely because it follows from essentialism plus the apparent fact of unremembered gaps. Descartes is the Western Advaita on this axis (without the non-dualism).

**Locke: against essential thought.** Locke (*Essay* II.i.10–19) argued that the soul does not always think, treating dreamless sleep as a genuine gap in thought and using it to attack the Cartesian essence-of-thought doctrine. Locke is closer to Nyaya: a persisting mental substance need not be continuously conscious.

**Phenomenology: the constitutive reading.** Dan Zahavi's minimal self holds that first-personal givenness ("for-me-ness") is constitutive of phenomenal consciousness — wherever there is experience, there is a subject it is for. This is an essentialist claim *about phenomenal consciousness*, though Zahavi is cautious about whether the same for-me-ness grounds identity across gaps. Thomas Metzinger's contrasting position — that for-me-ness is a contingent feature, in principle absent in "minimal phenomenal experience" with a "zero-person perspective" — is the contingentist rival. The Map's adoption of the Zahavian over the Metzingerian reading, developed in [[self-and-self-consciousness]], is the Western form of siding with essential over contingent consciousness, and is subject to the same evidential caution.

The parallels are structural, not exact. Descartes' continuous thought is a claim about a *thinking substance*; Advaita's continuous witnessing is a claim about *contentless awareness as the sole reality*; Zahavi's constitutive for-me-ness is a claim about the *form of phenomenal experience*. They agree only on the schematic point: consciousness is not a property the subject can shed while persisting.

## Why the Question Resists Empirical Settlement

The dispute looks empirical — surely we could find out whether anything is experienced in dreamless sleep — but it resists settlement for a structural reason. Every candidate datum is a *report*, and reports are laid down by memory systems that are demonstrably suppressed in the relevant states. So the two theses make the same prediction about all available evidence:

| Datum | Essentialist reading | Contingentist reading |
|---|---|---|
| "I slept dreamlessly, knew nothing" | Awareness continued; no episodic trace laid down | No awareness; bearer persisted; nothing to recall |
| Anaesthesia with no recall | Possible contentless awareness, unremembered | Genuine absence of awareness |
| Minimal phenomenal experience reports | Bare for-me-ness present, content stripped | Reconstruction by a re-formed self-model |

Each row is a place where awareness-without-memory and absence-of-awareness predict the identical observable. This is the [[sleep-consciousness-void|sleep-consciousness void]] in its sharpest form: the question "where does consciousness go in dreamless sleep?" may be one that no first-person report can answer, because the reporting apparatus is exactly what is offline. Contemplative claims to have "witnessed" deep sleep (the *turiya* tradition) raise the evidential stakes but do not close the gap — a felt sense of having witnessed is not, by itself, evidence of veridical witnessing, the same discipline the Map applies to other revelatory-feeling reports. [[contentless-awareness-evidence|What could count as evidence here]] — including Metzinger's minimal-phenomenal-experience program alongside the *turiya* reports — is taken up separately.

## The Map's Reading

The Map leans toward **essential consciousness of the non-physical aspect** — and states the lean honestly as a metaphysical commitment, not an empirically forced result. Three considerations seem to pull this way, and none of them, on its own, looks able to settle the thesis; the evidence may be compatible with either reading throughout.

**Coherence with the existing self-commitments.** The Map already adopts the Zahavian constitutive minimal self over Metzinger's contingent self-model, already treats first-personal givenness as the bearer of indexical identity ([[haecceity]]), and already rests [[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]] on *this* experience being *mine*. Essential consciousness appears to cohere with this cluster: a non-physical aspect that can lapse into genuine unconsciousness and later resume might reopen the question of whether the resumed subject is numerically the same, which could strain the indexical-identity commitment. Contingent consciousness seems to cohere with it less well — though a contingentist may reply that the persisting bearer secures identity across the gap as well as continuous awareness would, so even this consideration is not framework-neutral. This is a coherence consideration — it raises the framework-internal weight of the essentialist reading without raising its evidential status, exactly the distinction the [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]] forbids collapsing.

**The witness intuition.** The Advaita reading of dreamless sleep — that a knowing of the blank requires a witness of the blank — has genuine phenomenological pull. But the pull seems to *constrain* rather than to fix the verdict. It may remove the simplest eliminativist reading (that there is straightforwardly nothing there) without ruling out the Nyaya reading, on which "I knew nothing" reports a retrospectively-inferred absence rather than a witnessed one. At most the argument suggests the contingentist owes an account of the dreamless-sleep judgement; it gives no reason to think the account cannot be given.

**Tenet pressure that does not reach the conclusion.** [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]] requires that consciousness be irreducible to physical process. It does *not* require that the non-physical aspect be essentially conscious — Nyaya is a fully dualist position on which it is not. Dualism removes the physicalist defeater for taking consciousness seriously as fundamental; it leaves the essential/contingent question open. Treating the dualism tenet as if it settled the essentialist reading would be the precise failure mode the discipline guards against: a tenet removing a defeater, then being silently read as upgrading the verdict.

What the Map should *not* claim: that essential consciousness is established, that Advaita's regress argument is decisive, or that the contingent reading is ruled out by the tenets. The honest verdict is that the essential reading seems the better *fit* with the Map's other commitments, that the question is open and may be underdetermined by any available report, and that the lean is a metaphysical commitment held provisionally — perhaps defeasible if the indexical-identity cluster it coheres with were itself revised. Nyaya stands as a fully dualist rival the Map declines on coherence grounds rather than evidential ones; the evidence does not adjudicate between them.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: The question presupposes dualism (it asks about the nature of an already-granted non-physical aspect) and suggests that dualism alone does not fix that nature. Nyaya appears to be a dualism on which the self is not essentially conscious; the Map's essentialism is a further commitment layered on top of the tenet, and must be labelled as such rather than smuggled in as a consequence of it.

**[[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]]**: The essential reading coheres with the indexical-identity commitment underlying No Many Worlds — a subject that never lapses into genuine unconsciousness raises no question about whether the resumed subject is the same one. This is the strongest internal pull toward essentialism, and is framework-internal coherence rather than evidence.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: A subject whose influence on physical outcomes is real (the Map's reading of agency) is more straightforwardly the Zahavian constitutive subject than a Nyaya bearer that is conscious only intermittently — though Nyaya's own causal chain from self through *manas* to the senses shows a contingentist can still be an interactionist.

**[[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]]**: Two millennia of rigorous Indian debate produced both readings, each defended by sophisticated argument, neither dismissable as naive. The persistence of the disagreement among careful investigators is itself a reason not to expect parsimony to settle it — the simpler-looking view (no contentless awareness in dreamless sleep) is not thereby the better-supported one.
