The four-quadrant dualism taxonomy (see [[four-quadrant-dualism-taxonomy]]) sorts mind–body positions along two axes: how much ontological weight the mental side carries and how much the physical side carries. Three of its four cells are densely populated. The fourth — Q3, where mind is thick and physics is thin but **both remain ontologically real** — is nearly empty of *dualists*, even though several famous philosophers seem to live nearby. This article asks why. The short answer is that Q3 is dynamically unstable: positions placed there tend to drift across its lower edge into monism (idealism), shedding the physical pole entirely. The forces driving that drift, the historical record of who slid where, and the conditions that would hold a position stably at Q3-as-dualism are the subject of what follows.

The companion article [[mechanism-costs-dualism-thickness-quadrants]] introduced this observation in passing — that "a genuine Q3 dualism appears rare in the literature precisely because, once the mind does this much work, the physical side tends to lose explanatory weight and the position seems to slide toward Q3-monism." This article gives that remark the treatment it deserves. The claims here come in two registers, kept separate throughout: the *historical trajectory* claims (who slid where, and why) aim at accuracy; the *stabilisation* analysis and the Map's *self-positioning* are reasoned methodological positions, not established results, and are flagged as such per [[evidential-status-discipline]].

## The Sliding-Boundary Problem, Named Cleanly

Call it the **Q3 sliding-boundary problem**: when the mental side of a dualism is made thick enough to carry most of the explanatory work, the physical side tends to lose its grip on a distinct ontological role, and the position slides downward across the Q3/Q3-monism boundary into idealism.

The mechanism of the slide is an explanatory squeeze. A dualist owes an account of *why there are two ontological kinds* rather than one. The usual justification for keeping a physical pole is that it does work the mental pole cannot — it grounds the regularities science describes, the public measurable world, the resistance of matter to thought. But as the mental side is enriched to explain perception, lawful structure, and the felt world from the inside, the physical side's residual job shrinks. At the limit, "physical" names nothing but *how the mental looks from outside*. Once that happens, parsimony pressure becomes nearly irresistible: a pole that does no independent explanatory work and is definable wholly in terms of the other pole is a candidate for elimination. The position has become monist whether or not its holder announces the move.

The slide leaves a characteristic residue. Where the interactionist dualist owes an *interface* account (how do the two kinds touch?), the position that has slid to [[idealism]] instead owes a **transparency problem**: why does the intrinsic mental reality appear, from the outside, *exactly* as impersonal lawful physical structure? Bernardo Kastrup's formulation — that "matter is the extrinsic appearance of mental processes" — is the canonical contemporary statement (see [[analytic-idealism-and-mind-centric-metaphysics]] for the Map's framing). The transparency problem is not lighter than the interface problem; it is a different debt with a different shape. Trading the first for the second is precisely what the slide accomplishes.

## The Historical Record

The literature offers a striking pattern: positions near Q3 are overwhelmingly *monist*, and the few that began as dualisms tend to have a trajectory toward the monist edge. The trajectory claims below are the article's accuracy-register commitments.

**Berkeley started monist.** George Berkeley's immaterialism was never a dualism that slid; it began at the idealist limit. *Esse est percipi* — to be is to be perceived — denies mind-independent material substance outright. Berkeley is useful here as a fixed point: he shows where the lower edge of Q3 *is*, and what a position looks like once it has fully arrived there. There is no physical pole to lose because there never was one.

**Leibniz's monadology is idealist-leaning monism.** On the standard scholarly reading, Leibniz's mature metaphysics treats monads — simple, immaterial, perceiving substances — as the only genuine substances; corporeal mass is a *well-founded phenomenon*, not a second ontological kind. The view is sometimes called "panpsychic idealism." Bodies have no reality "over and above" their standing as the harmonised contents of monadic perception. This is not a dualism that resists the slide; it seems instead a position already on the monist side of the boundary, with the *appearance* of matter recovered through pre-established harmony rather than through a distinct physical substance.

**Kastrup's analytic idealism is explicitly monist.** This is the attribution most worth stating carefully, because it is easy to mistake a mind-first metaphysics for a dualism. Kastrup is unambiguously a monist: all that exists is one universal consciousness, and each organism is a *dissociated* localisation of it. Matter is the extrinsic appearance of mental processes. Kastrup positions himself in explicit opposition to dualism, which he treats as multiplying ontological kinds without need. Analytic idealism is therefore not a Q3 *dualism* at all — it is the destination the slide arrives at, articulated as a deliberate and defended position rather than as an accident.

**Whitehead's process philosophy resists landing as dualism.** Alfred North Whitehead's metaphysics is monistic in its actual entities: everything actual is composed of *actual occasions*, each with a dipolar structure — a physical pole (prehending other occasions) and a mental pole (prehending eternal objects). Whitehead explicitly frames this dipolarity as an *alternative* to Cartesian dualism, not a version of it. His panexperientialism is usually read as avoiding both the idealist claim that nature is mere appearance and the materialist claim that mind is derivative. The result sits near the neutral-monist or dual-aspect region rather than at stable Q3 dualism: the two "poles" are aspects of one kind of actual occasion, not two ontological kinds. Whitehead may be best read as having dissolved the slide by refusing the two-substance framing from the start.

**Myers and James are the rare Q3-stable-dualism candidates.** Frederic Myers's subliminal-self framework and William James's filter (or "transmission") theory of mind both enrich the mental side substantially — a vast subliminal region, a "mother-sea" of consciousness the brain admits — while keeping a genuine physical brain that does real selective work. This is the closest the literature comes to a thick-mind / thin-but-real-physics dualism. The Map treats James's filter theory as a framework it adopts (see [[william-james-consciousness]]). But the case is not clean: James also flirted with **neutral monism** — the doctrine that "pure experience" is the one fundamental stuff, neither mental nor physical until *taken* in a context. That doctrine sits in tension with the filter theory, which presupposes something (a brain, a reservoir) to filter. James never fully resolved the tension; the unresolved version is what makes him a *candidate* for Q3-stable dualism rather than a clear instance.

**Tibetan and Yogācāra mind-only schools slide toward the non-dual.** The Cittamātra ("mind-only") tradition, influential in Tibetan Buddhism, treats experienced objects as mentally constituted representations conditioned by the *ālaya-vijñāna* ([[yogacara-alaya-vijnana-storehouse-consciousness|storehouse consciousness]]). Whether Yogācāra is straightforwardly *idealist* is contested among modern scholars — some read it as an epistemic thesis about how experience is structured rather than a metaphysical denial of external matter. What is clearer is that the tradition's trajectory is toward **non-duality** (the *alaya* read as an absence of subject–object division) rather than toward a stable two-kind ontology. Either reading places it on the monist or non-dual side of the boundary, not at Q3-dualism.

The pattern is consistent: where the mind is made to carry the explanatory load, the position is found at or sliding toward the monist edge. Genuine, stable Q3 *dualism* — both poles thick-enough and ontologically real — is the exception, not the rule.

## Why the Slide Happens: Structural Reasons

Two forces, working together, drive positions off Q3-dualism and into monism.

**Parsimony pressure on a thin physical substance.** A physical pole that has been thinned to "the outside appearance of the mental" is maximally exposed to Occam's razor. It posits a second ontological kind while doing no work the first kind does not already do. The Map's tenet that [[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]] resists treating thinness as an automatic virtue — but it does not license keeping a pole that has been argued into explanatory vacancy. Parsimony is a real pressure even for a position that denies it the final word.

**Reduced explanatory burden undermines distinctness.** The justification for two kinds is that each does something the other cannot. As the mental side is enriched to explain qualitative character, lawful regularity, and the structure of experience from within, the physical side's distinctive contribution erodes. Eventually the only thing "physical" picks out is a *mode of presentation* of the mental — and a mode of presentation is not a second ontological kind. The slide is not a mistake the philosopher makes; it is the position telling the truth about itself. Once the mind does this much work, calling the residue "physical and real" becomes a label without a referent.

## What Would Stabilise Q3 Dualism

This section shifts register. The trajectory claims above aim at historical accuracy. The analysis here is a *reasoned, methodological* position — an account of what conditions *would* hold a thick-mind dualism stably distinct from idealism. It is offered as a structural argument, not as a demonstrated result, in keeping with [[evidential-status-discipline]].

The diagnosis suggests the cure. A position slides because its physical pole loses independent work. So the stabilising conditions are whatever keeps the physical side doing genuine, irreducible work that the mental side does not absorb.

**A selection-only channel gives physics some work.** If the mental side influences the physical only by *selecting among physically permitted outcomes* — biasing which of several quantum-allowed results obtains, without injecting energy or content — then the physical side retains a job the mental side cannot do: it *supplies the menu*. The space of permitted outcomes, the lawful structure that fixes which results are even available, is physical work the mind does not perform. The mind chooses; physics constrains the choice set. On this reading (see [[selection-only-channel]]), the physical pole is thin but not vacant — it is doing real, distinct, non-eliminable work. A pole that supplies the option space is not a mere appearance of the pole that selects within it. This is the first stabiliser.

**Bidirectional symmetry forces both poles to be real.** A channel that runs only one way invites collapse: if the physical merely receives mental influence and feeds nothing back, it can be re-described as the mental's downstream appearance. But if the channel is genuinely **bidirectional** — physical states shaping experience *and* experience biasing physical outcomes — then each pole is causally answerable to the other, and neither can be reduced to a mode of presentation of its partner. Reduction requires asymmetry: the reduced kind must be derivable from the fundamental one. A symmetric two-way dependence resists that derivation in both directions at once. The Map's [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] commitment is, read this way, not only an interaction claim but an *anti-slide* claim: it is among the conditions under which a thick-mind dualism could stay dualist. This is the second stabiliser.

**The Map's tenets, taken jointly, do the stabilising.** Neither condition alone is decisive, but together — and in concert with the rest of the Map's framework — they perhaps describe a position that resists the slide. [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] keeps the channel narrow (so the mind is not inflated into doing *all* the work, which would invite the slide) while keeping physics doing the bulk of causal labour outside the indeterminacy sites. [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] supplies the symmetry. The result is a position where the physical pole is thin in *thickness* (it is largely complete, intervened upon only at probabilistic junctures) yet thick in *explanatory indispensability* (it supplies the entire lawful menu the mind selects within). That decoupling — thin substance, indispensable role — is exactly what a stable Q3-adjacent dualism needs and what the slide destroys.

It is worth being explicit about a limitation of this analysis. "What stabilises Q3" is an argument about the *logical geography* of the cell, not a proof that any actual position succeeds in occupying it. Whether the Map's framework in fact achieves stable occupancy depends on whether its interface can be specified non-arbitrarily — the open question the companion taxonomy assigns as Q1's articulability burden and which applies here too.

## The Structural Analogy to the Many-Worlds Branch Problem

The Q3 slide has a structural cousin in the interpretation of quantum mechanics, and naming it sharpens the diagnosis. The Many-Worlds Interpretation (MWI) faces a *branch-explanation problem*: having posited that all outcomes occur on branches of the universal wavefunction, it owes an account of why our experience picks out *this* branch with *these* probabilities — why the Born-rule weights mean anything experientially when, ontologically, every branch is equally real (see [[probability-problem-in-many-worlds]]).

The analogy runs as follows. In both cases, an ontology that has been made *uniform* — all-branches-real for MWI, all-mental for idealism — must then recover the *apparent structure* that the uniformity dissolved. MWI must recover why one branch is experientially mine; idealism must recover why the mental ground appears as lawful impersonal matter (the transparency problem). Both are *recovery debts* incurred by removing a distinction the world appears to honour: the distinctness of *the* actual outcome (MWI) or the distinctness of physical from mental (idealism). The Map's rejection of MWI under the [[tenets#^no-many-worlds|No Many Worlds]] tenet rests on holding that indexical identity — *which* outcome is actually mine — is a real distinction not to be dissolved. The same instinct motivates resisting the Q3 slide: the distinctness of the physical pole is a real distinction not to be dissolved into "appearance of mind." In both cases the Map's move is to refuse the uniformity that generates the recovery debt, on the grounds that the dissolved distinction was load-bearing.

The parallel is structural, not a claim that idealism *is* MWI. But it shows that the Map's anti-slide instinct at Q3 and its anti-MWI commitment share a single underlying methodological pattern: do not pay a heavy recovery debt to buy an ontological uniformity whose chief attraction is parsimony, when the dissolved distinction was doing real explanatory work.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Where does the Map sit on the sliding boundary? The reasoned answer — again a methodological position, not a settled result — is that the Map occupies a *tenets-stabilised region* that excludes the idealist limit of Q3 by construction.

The [[four-quadrant-dualism-taxonomy|four-quadrant taxonomy]] reads the Map's default position as physical-thin plus mind-medium, sitting near Q1 with room to expand along the mind axis if Myersian or Jamesian evidence warrants. This article's analysis refines that picture: the Map is not committed to a single cell but to a *region* whose lower-left edge (the idealist limit of Q3) is closed off by the tenets working jointly. If Myersian or Jamesian evidence pushed the Map's mind-side thickness upward — toward a genuinely Q3-adjacent thick-mind dualism — the selection-only channel and bidirectional symmetry are what would keep that expansion *dualist* rather than letting it slide into idealism. The Map's Dualism tenet rules out the idealist limit directly; the analysis here explains the *mechanism* by which the other tenets enforce that ruling rather than leaving it as a bare stipulation.

So the Map's self-positioning is best stated as: a Q1-anchored region with sanctioned room to expand toward Q3-thickness, where the tenets jointly act as a *floor* preventing the slide past the dualism/idealism boundary. Whether the Map should be called "Q3-stable," "Q1/Q3-border," or "Q1-with-Q3-headroom" is partly terminological and partly underdetermined by the evidence the catalogue currently has in hand; the substantive claim is that the tenets supply exactly the anti-slide conditions this article identifies. That claim is a structural argument about the framework's geography, advanced with the evidential modesty the rest of the catalogue observes — it shows what the Map's commitments *would* secure, not that occupancy has been demonstrated.
