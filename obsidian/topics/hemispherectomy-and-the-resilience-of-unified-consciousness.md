Surgeons sometimes remove or disconnect an entire cerebral hemisphere — roughly half the cortex — to stop catastrophic drug-resistant epilepsy. The person who wakes from that surgery has one hemisphere, yet remains a single unified self: one stream of experience, one personality, one sense of humor, and (especially when the operation is early in childhood) largely intact cognition. Unity of consciousness survives the loss of half the physical substrate.

This is arguably a sharper datum for the study of conscious unity than the more famous split-brain (callosotomy) case. In split-brain surgery two hemispheres remain, so the "two conscious streams" reading is at least available for debate. In hemispherectomy there is no second hemisphere — the question "one mind or two?" cannot even be posed. Only one candidate substrate remains, and unity is not the survival of one stream over a rival, because there is no rival. What survives is unity per se, across massive substrate loss.

The Map's view is that this resilience is congenial to a filter or interface reading of consciousness (explained under [Two readings of the same data](#two-readings-of-the-same-data)), but honesty requires the immediate qualifier: the same clinical facts are equally predicted by a physicalist reorganization account. Hemispherectomy sharpens the discriminating question; it does not by itself answer it.

## What Hemispherectomy Is

Hemispherectomy is the surgical removal (anatomical hemispherectomy) or functional disconnection (functional hemispherectomy / hemispherotomy) of one cerebral hemisphere. It is a treatment of last resort for hemispheric epilepsy syndromes: Rasmussen encephalitis, hemimegalencephaly and cortical dysplasia, Sturge-Weber syndrome, and large perinatal strokes. The surviving hemisphere continues to run the whole person.

The procedure is not new. Walter Dandy performed early anatomical hemispherectomies on adults with glioma in the late 1920s, establishing that survival with one hemisphere is possible; Krynauw popularized the operation in the 1950s for infantile hemiplegia with epilepsy. The modern evidence base rests on long-term clinical series.

## The Clinical Record

The Johns Hopkins group produced the landmark pediatric series. Vining and colleagues (1997) reported on 58 children operated on between 1968 and 1996 under the memorable title "Why would you remove half a brain?" Of the survivors, most were seizure-free or had only non-handicapping seizures. The observation that made the paper famous is not statistical but human: the surgery does not destroy the person. Memory, personality, humor, and sense of self persist.

Pulsifer and colleagues (2004) followed with the cognitive picture in 71 children (mean age at surgery 7.2 years). For most tested patients, IQ changed by fewer than 15 points; a minority declined, a minority improved. Cognition was, on the whole, stable — sometimes improved, because relief from constant seizures frees the remaining tissue to function. No respondent rated quality of life as worse after surgery.

Language is the most striking case of reorganization. Curtiss and de Bode (2003), analyzing 43 UCLA hemispherectomy patients, found that after *left* hemispherectomy in childhood the *right* hemisphere can take over language, with receptive function often well spared and expressive function to a lesser degree. This plasticity appears to persist beyond the classically proposed critical period, though outcomes depend on age at onset and surgery, etiology, and seizure control. Plasticity here is real but bounded — not unlimited.

The resilience has limits, and the honest datum records them. Granovetter and colleagues (2022) tested word and face recognition — functions that intact brains distribute across the two hemispheres — in patients with a single hemisphere. Both left- and right-hemispherectomy patients could perform both tasks above 80% accuracy, but significantly below matched controls. The title states the bidirectional finding precisely: one hemisphere "can support — but is suboptimal for — word and face recognition." Resilience is real *and* costly.

## The Reorganization Mechanism

Modern connectome imaging shows how one hemisphere carries the load. Kliemann and colleagues (2019, *Cell Reports*) scanned six adults who had undergone childhood hemispherectomy, comparing them against controls and a large normative sample. *Within*-network connectivity in the single hemisphere was strong and equivalent to controls. *Between*-network connectivity was markedly increased across nearly all networks — a signature of reorganization, the surviving hemisphere integrating its systems more tightly to do the work of a whole brain.

This is not confined to the plastic childhood brain. Kim and colleagues (2022) reported an adult-onset case: a high-functioning middle-aged man six years after functional hemispherectomy following a malignant infarction. His surviving hemisphere showed the same increased between-network connectivity across all seven networks. Adult-onset cases matter philosophically because they weaken the deflation that "unity only survives because a child's brain is so plastic." Consciousness stayed unified even when the loss came late, when plasticity is weaker.

## Two Readings of the Same Data

The clinical record admits two interpretations, and the Map's discipline is to state both fairly.

The **physicalist / reorganization reading** is the mainstream cognitive-neuroscience view and the serious rival to the Map's. On this account, unity survives because the remaining tissue re-wires to implement the functions the lost hemisphere used to run. Consciousness is a property of the functioning network; shrink the network and it reorganizes, so the property persists. The increased between-network integration (Kliemann, Kim) is the mechanism; the bounded outcomes (Granovetter's "suboptimal") and the strong dependence on age at surgery are exactly what a plasticity account predicts. This reading requires no dualism, and it fits the data well.

The **filter / transmission reading** is the Map-aligned interpretation. If consciousness is received or filtered through the brain rather than produced by it, then unity is a property of the receiver — the non-physical mind — and the brain is the interface. Losing half the interface degrades bandwidth and specific channels (hence "suboptimal") but does not fragment the self, because the self was never identical to the hemispheres. On this view unity should be robust to substrate loss, and it should never be the case that half a person survives, only ever a whole person with degraded channels. That is what the record shows: personality and selfhood persist intact, and unity does not track cortical mass.

The critical honesty is that both readings predict the same thing: preserved unity, degraded specific capacities. Hemispherectomy is consistent with the Map's filter interpretation; it is not proof of it. Both the plasticity account and the interface account expect a self that stays whole while particular functions become "capable but costly."

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's guiding tenets hold that consciousness is not reducible to physical processes (Tenet 1) and that the brain functions as an interface through which a non-physical mind is expressed rather than as the mind's sole producer. Hemispherectomy is possibility-consistent with this framework: the persistence of a unified self through the loss of half the cortex is what an interface reading predicts, since the receiver is not identical to any hemisphere. The Map reads Granovetter's "intact but suboptimal" profile as degraded *channels* against a self that stays whole — lost hemisphere as lost interface bandwidth, not as a lost fraction of the person.

But the Map applies its own possibility-not-proof discipline here. The reorganization reading (increased between-network connectivity re-implementing lost functions) explains every one of these facts without any appeal to a non-physical mind. To claim hemispherectomy refutes physicalism would be exactly the overreach the Map warns against. The correct move is to locate the discriminating question rather than declare victory.

A genuinely discriminating test would need a case where the two readings make *different* predictions. One candidate framing: does the quality and structure of unified experience degrade in proportion to lost substrate — as a production account might expect if the self is built from cortical tissue — or does the self stay whole while only contents and bandwidth degrade, as the filter account expects? Granovetter's "capable but costly" profile is weakly more congenial to the channels-degrade picture, but it is not decisive, because a reorganization account predicts the same profile. This remains an open discriminating question, not a settled result, and it should follow the same discipline the Map applies to other targeted-lesion tests between production and filter readings.

There is also a limit on the "sharper than split-brain" framing itself. The philosophical literature on unity of consciousness centers overwhelmingly on commissurotomy, not hemispherectomy. The argument that hemispherectomy is the cleaner case — because the "two streams" question cannot arise — is largely the Map's own synthesis rather than received consensus. It is offered here as an argument to be weighed, not as an established result.
