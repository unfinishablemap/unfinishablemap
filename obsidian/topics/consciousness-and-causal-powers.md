If consciousness has genuine causal powers—if it does more than merely accompany physical processes—then the nature and scope of those powers becomes central to philosophy of mind. The question is not simply whether consciousness causes anything (addressed in [[mental-causation-and-downward-causation|mental causation]] and [[evolutionary-case-for-mental-causation]]) but what *kind* of causation it exercises and what limits constrain it. The Unfinishable Map holds that consciousness exercises a specific and limited causal influence: biasing quantum indeterminacies in neural systems toward certain outcomes without violating conservation laws—narrower than unconstrained psychokinesis, bounded in ways this article specifies.

Different answers lead to radically different worldviews. If consciousness has no causal powers ([[concepts/epiphenomenalism]])—the default [[the-epiphenomenalist-threat|the epiphenomenalist threat]] shows most frameworks converge on—everything we do would happen exactly the same if we were unconscious automata. If consciousness has unlimited powers, we enter magic and pseudoscience. The Map stakes out middle ground: real but circumscribed causal influence, operating within physical constraints.

## What Causal Powers Could Mean

When we say consciousness has "causal powers," what might this mean?

**Direct physical force**: Consciousness could push particles around, inject energy, exert forces—classical psychokinesis, ruled out by the Map's [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] tenet. No replicable evidence supports it; parapsychology's positive findings have not survived methodological scrutiny, and the move would violate conservation laws.

**Selection among alternatives**: Consciousness could be the locus of choice among physically permissible outcomes where the laws of nature leave the result undetermined. This is the Map's position. Physics specifies probabilities; phenomenal selection biases which actualizes—no energy added, no force exerted. The [[metaphysics-of-information-under-dualism|metaphysics of information under dualism]] frames this as syntactic constraints from physics, semantic selection from consciousness.

This framing presupposes a non-Everettian interpretation: under [[concepts/many-worlds|Many-Worlds]], all physically permitted outcomes actualise on parallel branches and there is nothing for consciousness to select. The Map's rejection of MWI is not free: whichever interpretation is right, *something* must be added—MWI adds branches the Schrödinger equation does not single out for actualisation; the Map adds a selection mechanism. The Map judges branch-multiplication as the higher cost, carried by the indexical argument rather than parsimony (see [[tenets#^no-many-worlds|Tenet 4]] and [[arguments/many-worlds-argument|Against Many-Worlds]]).

**Pattern amplification**: Consciousness could sustain neural patterns through repeated "observation" in the quantum sense. The [[stapp-quantum-mind|quantum Zeno effect]] suggests attention holds neural states in superposition longer than they would naturally persist—causal power as holding rather than pushing. Seven [[amplification-mechanisms-consciousness-physics|amplification mechanisms]] have been proposed for how quantum-level influence scales to macroscopic neural effects.

**Organizational influence**: Consciousness might affect how neural components organize without affecting their individual behaviour—a high-level property constraining lower levels through organizational feedback. The challenge is specifying how organization contributes novel powers without violating lower-level laws.

## Scope and Limits

If consciousness has causal powers, those powers must have limits—otherwise every conscious wish would reshape reality. What constrains phenomenal causation?

### The Interface Constraint

Consciousness interfaces with the physical world specifically at quantum indeterminacies in neural systems. This localizes the causal power: no direct influence on distant objects, other people's brains, or macroscopic events. The locality accounts for an asymmetry: you can decide to raise your arm because the interface is in your own neural activity; you cannot telekinetically move someone else's. See [[attention-as-interface]] and [[the-interface-problem|the interface location problem]].

### The Physical Constraint

Even at the interface, consciousness doesn't violate physics. The [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] tenet specifies biasing outcomes physics already allows. This preserves [[conservation-laws-and-mental-causation|conservation laws]]: when consciousness selects among energetically equivalent quantum outcomes, no energy is transferred—all possible outcomes already exist in the wavefunction with their associated energies. Consciousness selects a path through possibility space; it doesn't inject resources.

### The Probabilistic Constraint

[[quantum-probability-consciousness|Quantum mechanics provides probabilities, not certainties]]. Consciousness operates within probabilistic bounds—you cannot guarantee an extremely unlikely outcome by wanting it strongly enough. The [[causal-consistency-constraint|causal consistency constraint]] (Torres Alegre 2025, a recent arXiv preprint not yet independently confirmed) argues those bounds are structurally fixed: under purification, no-signalling forces the Born form. This may explain why voluntary action feels effortful—consciousness shifting probabilities against statistical noise from countless quantum events.

### The Temporal Constraint

[[psychophysical-laws|Psychophysical coupling]] operates in real time. Consciousness cannot retroactively change past outcomes or influence future states disconnected from present neural activity.

## What the Map Hypothesises Consciousness Does

Given these constraints, what causal work would consciousness perform under the Map's framework?

### Architecture and Implementation Candidates

The Map's commitment is layered. Phenomenal Biasing is the *architecture-level* claim: consciousness exercises causal influence by being phenomenally laden, with felt qualities (valence, value, salience) entering the causal story in a way sub-personal computational role does not exhaust. Selection of Action, Sustained Attention, and Volitional Modulation describe *candidate implementation mechanisms* through which the architecture might be realised in neural systems. The framework commits to the architecture, not to any single implementation; the candidates need not all be jointly operative.

This matters for quantitative objections. The twelve-to-sixteen-orders-of-magnitude decoherence gap discussed under Sustained Attention is a constraint on the Zeno *candidate*, not on the architecture. If unresolvable, an alternative implementation would carry the load; Phenomenal Biasing itself does not require a specific quantum-physical mechanism.

These mechanisms are hypothesised, not empirically established. Rival accounts (predictive processing, identity theories) predict the same phenomenology by different mechanisms. The case for the Map's reading rests on framework coherence and phenomenological fit; evidence-grade is laid out in §"The Evidential Question."

### Selection of Action

Faced with multiple options—speak or stay silent, approach or avoid—consciousness biases which neural pattern wins. Alternatives exist as superposed possibilities; consciousness influences which collapses into actuality. The selection is a genuine causal contribution rather than unlimited free will (see [[topics/free-will]]): without consciousness, outcomes follow physical probability distributions; with consciousness, phenomenal factors (desires, values, deliberations) shift those distributions.

### Sustained Attention

Consciousness can hold neural patterns active through sustained attention. The quantum Zeno effect proposes that repeated "observation" of a neural state prevents it from decohering—watching a pot that never boils (see [[stapp-quantum-mind]] and [[structure-of-attention]]).

The Zeno proposal faces a serious quantitative gap. Standard decoherence calculations for neural superpositions in warm, wet brain tissue give coherence times of $10^{-13}$ to $10^{-20}$ seconds; attention runs at 10–30 Hz. The mechanism would need observations at $\geq 10^{13}$ Hz—twelve to sixteen orders of magnitude faster than the attentional phenomenology. Closing the gap requires protected microenvironments (the contested Penrose-Hameroff microtubule conjecture) or an alternative quantum mechanism. The coherence-time requirement remains a critical vulnerability.

Whatever the mechanism, the causal power posited is persistence: consciousness makes certain neural configurations more stable, enabling planning, reasoning, and deliberation. The phenomenology of sustained attention is consistent with this picture but also with rival accounts in which felt effort reflects mesoscopic neural reverberation, attentional gain modulation, and noradrenergic locus coeruleus modulation of cortical signal-to-noise. Suggestive, not decisive; see [[phenomenology-of-consciousness-doing-work]].

### Phenomenal Biasing

The character of conscious experience—what it feels like—might itself contribute causal influence. [[pain-consciousness-and-causal-power|Pain]] provides the paradigm case: pain's unpleasantness doesn't merely represent tissue damage but causally motivates avoidance. [[pain-asymbolia]]—patients detect nociceptive stimuli but lack the felt awfulness—shows intact sensory detection without the extended avoidance behaviour pain's felt character normally drives.

The dissociation constrains the simplest representational claim (that felt awfulness just is nociceptive activation) but does not on its own establish the Map's reading. A predictive-processing account treating affective valence as a separate hierarchical prior predicts the same dissociation. The Map's actual disagreement with PP is at the bridge from sub-personal valence-prior to felt awfulness: PP treats that bridge as constituted by computational role; the Map treats felt awfulness as adding causal work the sub-personal prior alone does not perform.

Different phenomenal qualities produce different responses: sharp pain provokes withdrawal, burning pain motivates cooling, aching pain produces protective immobility. The [[valence-and-conscious-selection|selection currency thesis]] develops this into a specific mechanism: valence as the common metric by which consciousness evaluates competing options. This requires [[psychophysical-laws]] specifying how phenomenal character maps to action tendencies. See [[topics/phenomenal-value-realism]] for ethical implications.

### Volitional Modulation

The [[volitional-control|phenomenology of volitional control]]—including conscious veto and the "whether" component of intentional action (Brass & Haggard 2007)—offers first-person evidence consistent with variable causal engagement. On the Map's reading, the phenomenology of variable effort tracks variable causal engagement; the rival reading takes it as variable noradrenergic gain. The two are observationally indistinguishable at the phenomenology—a live underdetermination flagged in §"Absence of Direct Evidence." The Map's residual claim is that the phenomenally laden component of effort is not paraphrasable as locus-coeruleus modulation without remainder, because that description omits the felt valence the Phenomenal Biasing architecture treats as causally relevant.

Resisting a strong impulse feels effortful in a way going along with it does not. The intensity of felt effort plausibly tracks the magnitude of phenomenal contribution to selection. The framework registers graded effort as evidence for variable rather than binary engagement, without committing to a specific physical implementation.

A deeper objection sits one register lower than the noradrenergic rival. [[topics/eliminative-materialism|Eliminative materialism]] holds that the grammatical scheme this article uses—"consciousness selects," "consciousness sustains"—is folk-psychological reification awaiting redescription in sub-personal vocabulary (locus-coeruleus modulation, predictive-processing priors, gain-control circuitry). The Map's framework is not eliminativism-compatible: it carries a referent for "consciousness" as bearer of phenomenal properties, argued for on independent grounds ([[arguments-against-materialism]], [[phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence]]). What the Map disputes is not the redescription's in-principle availability but its adequacy to the explanandum (felt phenomenal valence as the thing being modulated, not just the modulation itself). This is a framework-boundary disagreement, honestly noted.

## What Consciousness Does Not Do

Equally important are the limits if the Map's framework is correct.

**No action at a distance**: No direct influence outside the neural systems to which consciousness is coupled. No mentally moving objects, reading minds, or affecting distant quantum systems.

**No energy creation**: No new energy, no thermodynamic violations. Causal effects work through selection among energy-conserving alternatives.

**No macroscopic violations**: No suspending gravity, reversing entropy, or breaking statistical patterns at scales where quantum effects average out.

**No guaranteed success**: Consciousness biases probabilities, not certainties. Voluntary action can fail.

**No retroactive influence**: Consciousness operates in real time. The causal arrow points forward.

These constraints distinguish the Map's position from pseudoscience while preserving genuine mental causation.

## The Philosophical Significance

Why does the nature of consciousness's causal powers matter?

### For Free Will

If consciousness has no causal powers, libertarian [[topics/free-will]] is impossible. The [[trilemma-of-selection]] makes this explicit: at any indeterminate decision point, the options are determinism, randomness, or mental causation—only genuine causal powers deliver the third. The [[luck-objection]] cuts against constrained and unconstrained powers alike: whatever consciousness does at the gap, what grounds the specific bias remains live. The Map's middle position anchors selections in phenomenal contents (values, desires, deliberations) rather than uncaused arbitrary choices, but whether those contents need further grounding is left open.

### For Ethics

If consciousness causally influences behaviour through its phenomenal character, then what experiences feel like matters morally. Pain's badness is not merely representational but causally connected to harm. This grounds [[topics/phenomenal-value-realism]]—phenomenal properties having intrinsic ethical significance.

### For the Mind-Body Problem

If consciousness has specific, limited causal powers operating through quantum selection, this constrains theories of consciousness. Theories making consciousness epiphenomenal mislocate the phenomenology; theories making consciousness omnipotent overrun the framework's other constraints. A theory consistent with the Map's commitments must explain how phenomenal properties acquire the causal profile described—influence at neural quantum indeterminacies, nowhere else. Empirical calibration is in §"The Evidential Question."

### For Metaphysics

If consciousness has causal powers irreducible to physical forces, this supports [[interactionist-dualism|dualism]]. The physical world is not [[causal-closure|causally closed]]; mental properties add to the causal story, with implications for reductionism, emergence, and natural law. The deeper question is whether consciousness *is* a disposition, *has* dispositions, or reveals a category [[consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-laws-and-dispositions|dispositional ontology]] cannot accommodate. See [[psychophysical-laws-bridging-mind-and-matter]] for formalisation attempts.

## The Evidential Question

What evidence could support or refute claims about consciousness's causal powers?

### Introspective Evidence

We seem to experience our conscious states influencing our actions. The evidential weight of this is limited. Introspective causal attributions are subject to systematic distortion: Nisbett and Wilson (1977) documented confident confabulation of causal narratives for behaviour subjects did not consciously initiate; Wegner (2002) showed the *feeling* of conscious will dissociates from actual causal involvement under controlled conditions. The Map's reading does not require token-by-token reliability—only that the global pattern of phenomenally laden action is not exhaustively explained by sub-personal confabulation. The *prima facie* phenomenology survives as suggestive but unweighty evidence; the load-bearing evidence sits elsewhere (evolutionary, comparative, clinical).

### Evolutionary Evidence

If consciousness evolved by natural selection, it must have affected fitness. The [[evolutionary-case-for-mental-causation]] argues only causally efficacious traits are selected. This doesn't tell us *what kind* of causal powers consciousness has, only that it has some.

### Comparative Cognitive Evidence

Humans possess cognitive capacities (logical reasoning, metacognition, cumulative culture) that great apes with sophisticated unconscious processing lack. If these require conscious processing—and the cognitive-load literature offers contested but suggestive evidence that they do—consciousness causally contributes to cognition. Dual-process distinctions, debates over whether "unconscious System 1" is conscious-but-inaccessible, and meta-analytic challenges to dual-task interference complicate the inference. Suggestive rather than dispositive; see [[baseline-cognition]].

### Clinical Evidence: Cognitive Work Without Motor Output

Cognitive motor dissociation (CMD) is the strongest clinical exhibit against epiphenomenalism. Patients meeting behavioural criteria for unresponsive wakefulness show, via fMRI or EEG, robust task-locked neural responses to commands like "imagine playing tennis"—understanding language, sustaining attention, forming task-relevant patterns with no overt behaviour. Bodien, Claassen et al. (2024, *NEJM*) found CMD in approximately 25% of behaviourally unresponsive patients. Consciousness continues to *do* cognitive work when the motor channel is severed—harder for epiphenomenalist than interactionist readings. See [[topics/consciousness-disruption-and-the-mind-brain-interface#covert-consciousness-awareness-without-expression|Consciousness Disruption]].

### Absence of Direct Evidence

We lack direct empirical evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes in brains. The hypothesis is *compatible* with current evidence but not *required* by it. A physicalist can identify mental states with neural states, making mental causation a subset of physical causation. [[brain-internal-born-rule-testing]] catalogues what evidence would close this gap mechanism by mechanism.

The Map's response: identity theories face their own difficulties. If mental states just *are* neural states, the qualitative character of experience has no explanatory role—the neural state does all the work, and phenomenal character is epiphenomenal in all but name. The Map's position requires the independent [[dualism]] argument; the causal powers framework describes *how* non-physical causation might work if dualism holds, not that it does.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] tenet commits to consciousness having causal powers. The [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] tenet specifies the nature and scope of those powers: influence at quantum indeterminacies, nowhere else.

This framework has several virtues:

**Ontological restraint**: It grants consciousness causal efficacy without multiplying unexplained powers. One *kind* of mechanism—quantum selection at the phenomenal-physical interface—suffices, without committing to a single implementation. The restraint presupposes a single conscious subject coordinating candidate mechanisms—a [[unity-of-consciousness|unity-of-consciousness]] assumption the split-brain literature complicates (Bayne 2010; Schechter 2018) and the Map carries as load-bearing background.

**Empirical compatibility**: The architecture is permitted by current physics; specific candidates face unresolved quantitative constraints. The Zeno proposal requires protected microenvironments extending neural coherence by twelve to sixteen orders of magnitude (the contested Penrose-Hameroff conjecture; see [[quantum-biology-and-neural-mechanisms]]) or an unspecified alternative.

**Explanatory power**: The framework explains effort, voluntary-control limits, attention-persistence correlation, and the evolutionary value of consciousness—from a single mechanism.

**Philosophical coherence**: By locating mental causation at genuine physical indeterminacies, the Map aims to avoid both epiphenomenalism and overdetermination. Kim's exclusion objection assumes physical causes are *sufficient* for every physical effect; if so, an additional mental cause must either duplicate the work (overdetermination) or do nothing (epiphenomenalism). Selection-only interactionism denies the sufficiency premise locally: physics specifies candidate set and Born-rule probabilities, not which candidate actualises. The physical contribution is necessary but not sufficient; mind realises one candidate. See [[overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism]] for the full argument.

The dissolution has a price. The [[causal-consistency-constraint|causal-consistency constraint]] (Torres Alegre 2025) requires mental selection preserve Born statistics: any scheme respecting no-signalling must be indistinguishable from unbiased Born per event. On the strictest reading of [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Tenet 2]], the hypothesised bias leaves no per-trial signature (bounded by approximately $\varepsilon^2 / (2 \ln 2)$ bits per trial). The honest characterisation: framework-coherence-supported, per-trial operationally undiscriminable by construction. Discrimination remains available at ensemble and correlation levels—[[brain-internal-born-rule-testing]] catalogues surviving families (sequential-dependency tests, intention-versus-spontaneity contrasts, Bell-inequality-style tests on phenomenally correlated events).

The Popperian objection that this is a *degenerating research programme* deserves direct answer. The framework forbids per-trial discrimination by design—a structural feature of strict Tenet 2 plus no-signalling, not a refinement introduced to evade falsification. A degenerating programme closes discriminating tests at all levels; the Map's framework is per-trial undiscriminable by construction but ensemble- and correlation-level discriminable in principle. If every adjacent-level test returned null at the required precision, the framework would inherit the degenerating-programme problem then; the catalogue's discriminating families have not yet been run at brain-internal precision, and the verdict is open.

The cost is commitment to quantum effects in the brain at timescales relevant to neural processing—speculative. The Map accepts this as the price of coherent [[interactionist-dualism|interactionist dualism]].
