Meditative traditions disagree about how the deepest states *feel*. Buddhist equanimity is reported as affectively flat—neither pleasant nor unpleasant. Bhakti and Sufi devotional practice culminate in overwhelming love and ecstatic joy. Christian contemplation oscillates between consolation and aridity. Daoist practice reports neither flatness nor ecstasy but unforced naturalness. These are not interpretive disagreements layered over a common experience; they are reported differences in the felt valence of advanced states. The [[comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions|comparative phenomenology of meditative traditions]] names this divergence in a single sentence and closes with a hypothesis. This article develops the hypothesis at full resolution: that affective tone may covary with *which mode of the consciousness-physics interface a tradition trains*, rather than tracking what consciousness "feels like" at its base.

Selected authoritative sources across these traditions foreground contrasting affective ideals at the depths of practice. Whether those reports reflect distinct underlying states, different stages of a single trajectory, tradition-specific reporting conventions, or distinct trained engagement modes is itself underdetermined—a caveat developed below. The interface-mode reading of *why* the divergence occurs is this article's hypothesis—built on the textual record, but not licensed by it. Keeping those two things apart is the central discipline of the piece, in the sense [[evidential-status-discipline|evidential-status discipline]] requires: the data the hypothesis explains cannot be counted as evidence that promotes the hypothesis past "a serious possibility."

## Four Affective Profiles

### Buddhist Equanimity (*upekkhā*)

The Theravāda tradition treats affective neutrality not as emotional poverty but as a refined attainment. In the *jhāna* progression, the third and fourth absorptions are characterised by the fading of even the subtle pleasure (*sukha*) of earlier stages, leaving *upekkhā*—equanimity. Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* (5th century CE) describes the fourth jhāna as a state in which feeling is "neither-painful-nor-pleasant" and equanimity is purified to its highest point. This fourth-jhāna equanimity arises within an advanced *concentrative absorption* trajectory and should not be conflated with the open-monitoring equanimity of bare insight practice; the two are distinct attainments that the tradition reaches by different routes, even though both land on the disengagement of valence. *Upekkhā* is also one of the four *brahmavihāras* (divine abodes), where it functions as even-minded regard unbiased by attraction or aversion. The affective signature is the *absence* of valenced reactivity: not numbness, but the dropping-away of the pleasant/unpleasant axis along which ordinary affect runs. Recent intensively-sampled neurophenomenology of advanced concentrative absorption (the jhāna sequence) in long-term practitioners reports that the deepening absorptions track measurable shifts in functional connectivity and broadband neural dynamics alongside the practitioner's phenomenological ratings of fading valence (Chowdhury et al., 2025, *NeuroImage*; Demir et al., 2025, *Cerebral Cortex*). These are single-case studies that establish *that* the equanimity trajectory has a distinctive neural profile, not yet *why* its valence flattens—but they move the Buddhist datum from textual report alone toward replicable observation.

### Bhakti and Sufi Devotional Ecstasy

The devotional traditions report the opposite pole. In the *Rasa* theory inherited from Sanskrit aesthetics and developed by Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava theologians such as Rūpa Gosvāmī (*Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu*, 16th century), devotional experience is analysed as *bhakti-rasa*—a sustained aesthetic-emotional "taste" or savour, with love (*prema*) as its ground. Far from being purged, emotion is cultivated, refined, and intensified toward ecstatic union with the divine beloved. Sufi practice reports a structurally similar peak: states of *wajd* (ecstasy) and the *fanā'* (annihilation of the self in God) described in the Sufi tradition—paradigmatically by Rūmī—are suffused with intense love and longing. The affective signature here is maximal positive valence, directed at a beloved object, rather than the disengagement of valence. The tradition is not internally uniform on this, however: al-Junayd of Baghdad is the emblem of *sober* (*ṣaḥw*) Sufism, which treats the return from ecstatic intoxication (*sukr*) to lucid, integrated awareness as the higher attainment. Citing the ecstatic pole as the Sufi affective ideal foregrounds one wing of a tradition that contains its own sober counter-current, and that internal range is itself a small instance of the stage- and mode-dependence this article is tracking.

### Christian Oscillation: Consolation and Aridity

The Christian contemplative tradition reports neither stable flatness nor stable ecstasy but a characteristic *alternation*. John of the Cross (*The Dark Night of the Soul*, c. 1585) describes prolonged "aridity"—the withdrawal of all felt spiritual sweetness—as a purgative phase that strips the soul's attachment to consolation itself. Teresa of Ávila describes spiritual "delight" and consolation in the higher mansions of *The Interior Castle* (1577). *The Cloud of Unknowing* (c. 1375) frames the contemplative's work as reaching toward God through a "naked intent" that must persist *through* the absence of felt response. The affective signature is temporal: valence swings between presence and withdrawal, and the discipline lies in holding steady across the swing rather than securing one pole.

### Daoist Naturalness (*ziran*)

The Daoist sources report an affective tone that resists placement on the valence axis altogether. The *Zhuangzi* and *Liezi* describe the sage's state as *ziran*—self-so, spontaneous, unforced—coupled with *wu wei*, action without contrivance. The famous "fasting of the heart-mind" (*xin zhai*) and "sitting in forgetfulness" (*zuowang*) passages in the *Zhuangzi* describe ordinary striving and emotional turbulence falling away, leaving responsiveness without imposition. The affective signature is neither the cultivated intensity of *bhakti* nor the purified neutrality of *upekkhā*, but ease: a tone of fittingness or flow that the tradition declines to characterise in terms of pleasure or its absence. The parent article calls this tone "without emotional coloration"; the present reading sharpens that to *ease* rather than blankness—the difference between an affect the tradition refuses to score on the pleasant/unpleasant axis and the simple absence of affect. *Ziran* is responsive, not anaesthetised, which is why it is not a second instance of *upekkhā*'s disengaged neutrality.

## The Structural Shape: Tone Covaries With Interface Mode

Laid side by side, the four profiles do not form a random scatter. They appear to sort by *what the tradition is training the practitioner to do with consciousness*. The Map's [[meditation-and-consciousness-modes|two-mode framework]] distinguishes active selection (consciousness engaging the brain through attention, holding patterns stable) from passive witnessing (consciousness observing without intervening). The [[witness-consciousness|witness]] articles add the *sakshi* register; the [[selection-only-channel|selection-only channel]] supplies the technical picture of selection as the act of choosing among brain-prepared candidates without contributing to the candidate set. Reading the four traditions against these registers yields a candidate pattern:

- **Buddhist equanimity** is trained primarily through open monitoring and the progressive *withdrawal* of selection. The *jhāna* sequence systematically disengages applied thought (*vitakka*), sustained thought (*vicāra*), and then even valenced feeling. Equanimity is what the affective register looks like when selection is maximally disengaged—the witness mode at its purest. Flat tone tracks disengaged selection.

- **Bhakti and Sufi ecstasy** train *intensified participation*: attention is not withdrawn but poured into a single beloved object, sustained and amplified. This is engagement at maximum, the selective interface running hot on a devotional target. High positive valence tracks maximal directed engagement.

- **Christian oscillation** trains a relationship that the practitioner does not control. Consolation and aridity arrive and withdraw; the discipline is to maintain intent across both. The affective swing tracks an interface mode in which engagement is *received* rather than *held*—the practitioner cannot simply select the felt state into being, and the alternation is the phenomenology of that non-control.

- **Daoist naturalness** trains the *suspension of forcing*—*wu wei* is precisely action without the friction of effortful selection, responsiveness without imposition. Ease tracks an interface mode in which selection neither runs hot (as in devotion) nor is fully withdrawn (as in deep equanimity), but is released from contrivance while remaining responsive.

The structural claim is that affective tone is not a free parameter but appears to *covary* with which register of the interface—selection, witness, participation, non-forcing—the tradition emphasises. Tone looks like a feature of the *engagement register*, not a readout of consciousness considered apart from any engagement.

## The Hypothesis: Tone Is a Feature of Engagement, Not of Consciousness-as-Such

The parent article's load-bearing sentence proposes that affective tone "might depend on which aspects of the interface are engaged or disengaged rather than on what consciousness itself 'feels like' at base." Developed, the hypothesis runs:

> The valence of an advanced contemplative state is a property of *how consciousness is engaging the brain's interface*—how much it is selecting, what it is selecting toward, whether selection is held or received—rather than an intrinsic affective character that consciousness possesses independent of any interface activity. Different traditions train different engagement profiles; the affective divergence is the downstream signature of that training difference.

If this were right, it would dissolve the apparent paradox in the reports. The Buddhist and the Bhakta are not disagreeing about a single thing called "the feel of pure consciousness." They are reporting the affective signatures of two different things consciousness can *do*: disengage selection, or pour it into a beloved object. The reports would then be consistent and complementary rather than contradictory—each tradition a reliable witness to the affective consequence of the mode it trains.

The hypothesis also makes a prediction that distinguishes it from the deflationary reading (that the divergence is merely cultural expectation or comparing different stages). It predicts that a single practitioner who can shift between engagement registers should be able to traverse the affective range—moving from devotional intensity to equanimous flatness to unforced ease—as a function of which mode they enter, independent of doctrinal commitment. The contemplative-neuroscience record is *suggestive* on this point but does not settle it. Studies distinguishing focused-attention from open-monitoring practice report differing neural and affective profiles within trained practitioners (Lutz et al., 2008, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*), and more recent within-subject work finds that focused-attention, open-monitoring, and loving-kindness practice recruit common *and* state-specific neural-coupling patterns in the same long-term meditators (Yordanova et al., 2020, *Scientific Reports*)—the cleanest available evidence that a single practitioner's brain differentiates the modes the hypothesis appeals to. Loving-kindness practice is associated with increased positive affect (Fredrickson et al., 2008, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*; Zeng et al., 2015, *Frontiers in Psychology*), but the more cautious recent meta-analysis qualifies this sharply: loving-kindness interventions improve positive affect relative to *passive* (no-treatment) controls yet show no significant advantage over *active* controls and alternative treatments (Petrovic et al., 2024, *Clinical Psychology Review*). That active-control gap matters for the present argument: it means the loving-kindness positive-affect signal is partly carried by expectancy, demand characteristics, and the top-down semantic generation of a warm intentional object, not solely by whatever engagement mode the practice trains. The signal is consistent with tone tracking trained mode; it is not a confirmation that the *mechanism* is interface-register modulation rather than some downstream affective-system difference. The neural-correlate data are compatible with both the interface reading and a purely intra-physical reading.

### Why the Hypothesis Must Not Be Upgraded

The empirical material in this article comes in two grades, and the [[evidential-status-discipline|discipline]] turns on keeping them separate.

*Reported in the textual record:* Selected authoritative sources in the four traditions foreground the affective profiles described above, across primary texts spanning more than a millennium. The divergence is well-documented and not an artefact of one tradition's idiosyncrasy. The "unconnected cultures" framing must be calibrated, however: the parent [[comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions|comparative phenomenology]] flags that the traditions did not develop in strict isolation—the Sino-Indian pairing in particular (Daoist *zuowang* met Buddhist meditative architecture along the Silk Road from the 2nd–4th centuries CE) is the weakest independent-pair claim, and the Neoplatonist channel into early Sufism is moderate-to-substantial. The pattern therefore leans most heavily on the cleanly isolated pairings. Two further parent caveats apply here as well: the profiles may be drawn from *different stages* of practice rather than commensurable end-states, and contemplative reports are theory-laden in Steven Katz's (1978) sense—there may be no "pure" affective datum beneath the tradition-specific vocabulary that frames it. A third calibration is that the affective range is not uniformly positive: population surveys find that a substantial minority of meditators report meditation-related adverse effects, with roughly a tenth reporting effects lasting a month or longer (Goldberg et al., 2022, *Psychotherapy Research*). The Christian "aridity" pole and the difficult passages the contemplative traditions themselves document are not anomalies to be explained away; they belong to the affective record the hypothesis must accommodate, and they caution against reading "trained affective tone" as a reliably benign attainment.

*This article's hypothesis:* That the divergence is *caused by* differences in trained interface mode, and that affective tone is therefore a feature of engagement rather than of consciousness-as-such. This is a serious possibility that organises the established data cleanly—but its explanatory tidiness is not evidence for it. A hypothesis that accounts for the data it was built to account for has earned the status "live and unrefuted," not "supported." In the language [[framework-stage-calibration|framework-stage calibration]] supplies, the affective profiles are Pre-Kepler-stage observations: empirically reported, not yet theoretically integrated. The interface-mode reading is one proposed integration. Promoting it on the strength of the data it explains would be exactly the possibility/probability slippage the discipline exists to catch.

Several serious rivals the hypothesis does not defeat sit alongside it, and the discipline requires naming them rather than the one neural-training rival alone.

*Neural training.* The affective divergence might track differences in the *brain's* affective architecture under different training regimes—loving-kindness practice strengthening reward circuitry, equanimity practice down-regulating limbic reactivity—with no need to invoke an interface-mode that consciousness occupies. On that reading the tone is downstream of neural training, full stop, and the "interface mode" is a redescription rather than an explanation.

*Stage mismatch.* The four profiles may not be reports of the same kind of attainment at all. A fourth-jhāna equanimity report and a peak devotional report could be drawn from different points on different trajectories, in which case the "divergence" is partly an artefact of comparing non-corresponding stages rather than a genuine branching of advanced states.

*Report construction.* In the Katzian theory-laden reading, the affective vocabulary each tradition supplies *shapes* what gets reported—there may be no tradition-neutral valence beneath "equanimity," "*prema*," "aridity," and "*ziran*" for the hypothesis to explain. The divergence would then live in the descriptive apparatus rather than in the states.

Two further rivals are not merely neural-training redescriptions but developed research programmes, and the article should engage them by name. The **Theory of Constructed Emotion** (Barrett 2025) holds that affect is not read off the body but *constructed* by the brain's conceptual system from interoceptive prediction; on this reading each tradition trains a different conceptual scheme for categorising interoceptive signal, and the divergent tones are doctrine-driven conceptual constructions rather than readouts of distinct engagement modes. **Predictive Processing / Active Inference** offers a parallel mechanism without the constructionist commitment: contemplative training differentially re-weights the precision of priors and prediction errors, and the affective signatures fall out of that precision-weighting profile. Both are intra-physical readings, and both account for the same textual record this article does. The Map's [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]] tenet declines them—but it declines them at the framework boundary, not by defeating them on their own terms. The affective data are equally available to the constructed-emotion theorist and the active-inference theorist, exactly as they are available to the identity theorist; what the interface-mode hypothesis offers over them is a framework-relative way of *organising* the divergence, not an argument that discriminates against them on shared evidence. The honest statement is that these readings and this one are not yet separated by available data, and that the Map's preference among them runs through its foundational commitments rather than through a decisive observation. This article's hypothesis is therefore not entitled to claim victory over any of them.

## Sibling To, or Distinct From, Cessation-Versus-Plenitude?

The parent article treats affective-tone divergence as a "sibling phenomenon at a less load-bearing register" to the [[cessation-versus-plenitude|cessation-versus-plenitude divergence]] (whether the ultimate state is the cessation of experience or its fullest expression). The relationship is worth making precise, because the two divergences operate on different axes.

Cessation-versus-plenitude is a disagreement about the *presence or absence of content* at the limit. The Theravāda *nirodha samāpatti* reports the stopping of perception and feeling; the Vedāntic *sat-chit-ānanda* reports awareness at maximal fullness. The axis is content-quantity: nothing versus everything.

Affective-tone divergence is a disagreement about *valence at the engagement register*. It is not primarily about how much content is present but about the emotional character of the engaging. A devotional state and an equanimous state can both be content-rich; they differ in valence, not in plenitude. A deep equanimity and a cessation approach can both be content-poor; they differ in whether valence has flattened or experience has stopped.

This makes affective tone *structurally distinct* from cessation-versus-plenitude rather than a smaller copy of it. They are siblings in that both are divergences the cross-traditional convergence argument must accommodate rather than explain away, and both are Pre-Kepler-stage observations. But cessation-versus-plenitude threatens the convergence argument more directly, because it concerns the end-state the traditions are aiming at—if advanced practice genuinely branches into stopping versus fullness, the convergence argument's reach is bounded at its most load-bearing point. Affective-tone divergence is less threatening precisely because the interface-mode hypothesis offers a way to read it as *consistent* variation: different valences from different trained modes need not imply different end-states of consciousness itself. Whether that reading holds is the open question; that it is *available* is what makes affective tone the lighter of the two divergences.

## Relation to Site Perspective

### Dualism

The interface-mode hypothesis presupposes [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]: it makes sense to ask "which mode is consciousness engaging the brain in?" only if consciousness is something other than the brain activity it engages. On a strict identity reading, there is no consciousness-occupying-a-mode over and above the neural state, and the hypothesis collapses into the rival redescription noted above. The Map's interest in the hypothesis is therefore framework-relative, and the article does not present the affective data as evidence *for* dualism—the data are equally available to the identity theorist, who reads "interface mode" as "neural configuration." What the hypothesis offers the dualist is a way of *organising* the divergence that the tenets make coherent, not a confirmation the tenets earn from the data.

### Bidirectional Interaction

The hypothesis connects to [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] at the correlation register, not the mechanism register. If sustained training in a given mode reliably produces a given affective profile—and the loving-kindness and equanimity literatures suggest it does—then conscious practice is systematically shaping affective experience and, by the training literature, the training-sensitive affective and functional-neural patterns that accompany it. This is the same direction-of-influence claim the parent article makes for contemplative training generally. The strengthening is real but narrowly scoped: the correlation between trained mode and affective outcome is well-supported; whether the causal route runs through a consciousness-occupied interface mode (genuine downward causation) or through ordinary neural self-modification (training reshaping affective circuitry) is exactly what the evidence does not yet discriminate. The Bidirectional Interaction reading is one branch of that undecided fork, held as a live option rather than asserted as the established mechanism.
