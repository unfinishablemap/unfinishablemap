Interventionist and counterfactual dualism names the Map's single most important live rival to its own preferred structure for mental causation. Where the Map favours the closure-preserving [[trumping-preemption|trumping]] route — one authoritative cause, no trajectory change — this rival family holds that a non-physical experience and its physical base are *both* genuine causes of behaviour, standing alongside one another rather than one being selected over the other by an authority law. Its members share a single structural commitment: **two causes, not one**. The exclusion worry that motivates trumping is answered not by decoupling authority from sufficiency, but by showing that causation is difference-making (or counterfactual dependence), which a systematic mind–body law can supply without the resulting overlap counting as the "bad", coincidental kind of overdetermination.

Stating this rival fairly does real work for the Map, and the [[#the-tenet-diagnostic|tenet-diagnostic]] developed below is the payoff. On a literal reading, the discriminating tenet — [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] (Tenet 2) — leans *toward* co-causation, not toward the Map's preferred trumping. Framed within the Map's own commitments, the Map's preference for trumping therefore rests on a methodological posture (minimise the detectable footprint), not on anything the five tenets strictly entail. Naming that sharpens the Map's position into a visible, defensible choice under underdetermination rather than an assumed one.

## The Two-Cause Family: A Taxonomy

The family has three branches that share the two-cause commitment but differ in what makes the experience a cause. A downstream reader should keep them distinct.

### Counterfactual Dualism (Kroedel 2015)

Thomas Kroedel's "Dualist Mental Causation and the Exclusion Problem" (2015) is explicitly a *dualist* account. Its move is to combine dualism with the assumption that psychophysical laws have a special status; given that assumption, some physical effects counterfactually depend on mental events, and so — on a difference-making semantics of causation — mental events cause them. As Kroedel puts the thought: if our minds had been different, the physical world would have been different, therefore the mind causes events in the physical world.

Kroedel then dissolves exclusion in one of two ways, and lets the dualist choose: either *deny* that the physical effect is overdetermined by mental and physical causes, or *accept* the overdetermination but argue it is benign, because the case is sufficiently dissimilar to the prototypical, coincidental overdetermination of a firing squad. No preemption is invoked; nothing is selected as authoritative.

This is the nearest dualist competitor to the trumping route, and the [[trumping-preemption]] article notes that Bradford Saad himself cites Kroedel in situating his own view. The two routes are close to mirror images. Where trumping secures *authority without counterfactual dependence* (the experience is the cause even though the base would have sufficed on its own), Kroedel secures *counterfactual dependence without authority-selection* (the effect depends on the experience, and that dependence is all that "being a cause" requires — no ranking law needed).

### Interventionist Co-Causation (Vaassen 2024)

Bram Vaassen's "Mental Causation for Standard Dualists" (2024) targets the "standard objection" to dualism: that dualism seemingly cannot account for mental phenomena causing behaviour, given that behaviour is physically necessitated by entirely physical phenomena. Vaassen argues that an interventionist account, in the Woodward difference-making tradition, provides mental causation *within* a standard dualist ontology. Mental states qualify as causes because they are **points of control** — difference-making variables — for behaviour, whatever energy the physical base transfers.

The crucial structural claim is that difference-making is not energy transfer, so causal closure read as a claim about energy or forces is simply untouched. Both the experience and its physical base come out as genuine causes; this is co-causation, and there is no preemption competition to resolve. As [[trumping-preemption]] records (following the author's own correction of an earlier Map framing), Vaassen's is a *both-are-causes interventionist* view, not a preemption view — which is precisely what makes it a rival to trumping rather than an instance of it.

### Lawful Co-Causation: the Older Lineage (Mills 1996; Lowe 2003)

Both Kroedel and Vaassen descend from an older invisible-causation lineage. Eugene Mills's "Interactionism and Overdetermination" (1996) argues the causal-closure argument against dualism fails because overdetermination is not ruled out, and defends **overdeterministic interactionism**: an arm's rising is overdetermined by both a non-physical mental cause and a physical cause. The structural insight the Map already borrows is that overdetermination is objectionable only when *coincidental* — when two independent processes happen to converge. A systematic mind–body law makes the convergence non-coincidental, stripping the overdetermination of its "bad" flavour.

E. J. Lowe's "Physical Causal Closure and the Invisibility of Mental Causation" (2003) adds the other half. Lowe develops models on which, *if* mental causation is real, it would nonetheless appear to physical scientists that all the causation in human behaviour is purely physical. He distinguishes what the physicalist is entitled to — *observational* closure, meaning no *detectable* trajectory violation — from closure full-stop. If lawful co-causation preserves observational closure, the dualist has not taken on the bad kind of overdetermination.

Lowe's route matters to the diagnostic below because it prizes exactly what the Map's trumping route prizes — observational invisibility — yet reaches it by *co-causation* rather than by authority-selection. The Map's own [[observational-closure]] concept leans on Lowe; the family that supplies it does not, on its own terms, need trumping.

### Two Contrast Points: the Exclusion-Analytic and Physicalist Mirrors

Two further sources sharpen the terrain without belonging to the dualist family. Vaassen's "Causal Exclusion without Causal Sufficiency" (2021) is *not* a pro-dualist paper: it is an analysis of the exclusion argument itself, arguing that objections claiming exclusion relies on a problematic notion of *causal sufficiency* are superficial, because exclusionists can reformulate in terms of *physical sufficiency* and the reformulated argument survives. It shows the exclusion pressure both routes must answer is robust — and it maps Vaassen's own trajectory: he tightens the exclusion argument in 2021, then argues in 2024 that standard dualists can nonetheless escape it. His "Halfway Proportionality" (2022) contributes to the same difference-making toolkit, arguing one half of the proportionality principle is overly demanding and the other unobjectionable.

Lei Zhong's "A causal argument for physicalism" (2023) is the physicalist mirror. Zhong runs causal/counterfactual machinery structurally similar to the dualist routes to argue for *non-reductive* physicalism — all mental stuff is metaphysically necessitated by *some* physical stuff. It conflicts with the Map's [[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]] tenet, and it is included precisely as a cautionary contrast: the same difference-making toolkit can be turned *against* dualism, so the Map cannot treat co-causation machinery as intrinsically dualist-friendly. (The higher-level-property version of this physicalist route is covered under [[causal-exclusion-argument]] and [[objections-to-interactionism]]; the gap this article fills is specifically the *dualist* variant.)

## One Cause or Two: the Axis That Divides the Field

The clean way to locate every position is the number of genuine causes it posits for a single behaviour.

- **Trumping / delegation** ([[delegatory-causation|the Map's preference]]): *one* authoritative cause. Both the experience and its physical base remain counterfactually sufficient, but a higher-order psychophysical law selects the experience as *the* cause. The base is not cut off and not epiphenomenal; it is simply not authoritative when a matching experience is present. Efficacy is relocated onto an authority axis with no trajectory signature.
- **Co-causation** (this family): *two* genuine causes. The experience makes a difference (Vaassen), or the effect depends on it (Kroedel), or the law makes their joint production non-coincidental (Mills/Lowe). No selection is needed because there is no competition to adjudicate — closure, read as an energy claim, was never in tension with a non-energetic difference-maker.

The [[causal-exclusion-argument|exclusion argument]] in its canonical Kimian form pushes the dualist toward epiphenomenalism or systematic overdetermination. Both routes claim a third option, but they take opposite exits. Trumping keeps *one* cause and pays for it with an authority law whose non-stipulative ground is an open problem. Co-causation accepts *two* causes and pays for it by arguing the overlap is benign. A dualist who accepts Kroedel's or Vaassen's dissolution on its own terms could drop trumping entirely — which is exactly why trumping may be "answering a question already closed," as the [[trumping-preemption]] article concedes.

## The Tenet-Diagnostic {#the-tenet-diagnostic}

Here the article turns from source exposition to Map integration. The question the taxonomy poses for the Map is whether its tenets favour the closure-preserving trumping route it currently prefers, or the difference-making co-causation route. Framed relative to the Map's own commitments, the landing is that the tenets are jointly *satisfiable* by both routes but do not pull symmetrically — and, non-obviously, the discriminating tenet leans toward the rival.

**Closure alone does not decide it.** Both routes preserve *observable* closure. Trumping does so by being difference-making-free: both processes complete, a law picks the authoritative one, and no trajectory changes. Vaassen/Lowe co-causation does so because difference-making is not energy transfer, so closure as an energy claim is untouched. The Map cannot appeal to observational-closure-preservation to break the tie, because both rivals deliver it.

**Tenet 3, read literally, favours co-causation.** [[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]] says consciousness *causally influences* the physical world. Co-causation makes the experience a genuine difference-maker, which is the most natural reading of "influence." Trumping's authority-without-any-difference is the very structure the Map itself flags as uncomfortably close to [[ensemble-level-epiphenomenalism|ensemble-level epiphenomenalism]]: efficacy relocated onto an authority axis with no trajectory signature. A difference-making reading of Tenet 3 is more securely satisfied by the rival.

**Tenet 2 is the discriminating tenet — and it too leans co-causation, on the axis that matters.** This is the sharp result, with one qualification the other two tenets do not need. [[tenets#^minimal-quantum-interaction|Minimal Quantum Interaction]] posits an *actual, minimal* non-physical influence on quantum outcomes. On its strict [[selection-only-channel|selection-only]] reading, that influence is *per-trial* difference-making — the mind fixes *which* single candidate is realised — yet *ensemble* difference-making-free: the long-run frequencies stay Born-exact, so it shifts no distribution and is statistically invisible. It thereby relaxes *strong* causal closure (physics alone no longer settles the token outcome) while preserving *observational* closure. On that strong-closure axis MQI is continuous with the co-causation family — a genuine per-trial difference-maker — and discontinuous with trumping, which is difference-making-free and strong-closure-preserving. The [[trumping-preemption]] article already concedes this seam, reading "the quantum-selection channel … as a distinct and potentially competing mechanism to the trumping route rather than an instance of it." The continuity is axis-restricted, though: the co-causation family (Vaassen, Kroedel, Mills/Lowe) turns on *ensemble*-level difference-making — a shift in the distribution of behaviour — so it is the *probability-bias* intermediate reading of Tenet 2, not the strict selection-only channel, that is continuous with the family there; the strict channel shares its ensemble-invisibility with trumping instead. A Map that takes MQI as a real per-trial selection has *already* relaxed strong closure at the quantum level; layering trumping on top at the psychophysical level introduces a second, structurally different channel, and the two sit in mild tension.

**What actually drives the trumping preference is methodological.** The trumping route's distinctive prize is *strong*-closure preservation plus zero trajectory footprint — maximal empirical conservatism. That is a posture: minimise the detectable metaphysical footprint. It is not one of the five tenets. The honest statement is that the Map's preference for trumping over co-causation is tenet-*coherent* but tenet-*underdetermined*, and it trades on closure-conservatism rather than on anything the tenets require. Tenet 5, [[tenets#^occams-limits|Occam's Razor Has Limits]], cuts symmetrically here and blocks the tempting shortcut: the Map cannot claim trumping is *simpler and therefore truer*, because its own fifth tenet denies that simplicity tracks truth under incomplete knowledge. The trumping route is cleaner, but cleanliness is not a tenet-level reason.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The interventionist/counterfactual co-causation family is the route most continuous with the Map's own difference-making commitments — Tenet 3 read literally, and Tenet 2's per-trial selection channel structurally (on the strong-closure axis; its strict selection-only reading is ensemble Born-preserving). It is not a weaker cousin of trumping to be dispatched in a clause. The Map still prefers trumping, for its stronger closure-preservation and cleaner invisibility, and it may reasonably do so. But the preference is a choice under underdetermination, and stating the rival fairly is what makes that choice visible and defensible rather than assumed.

Two honest caveats bound the diagnostic. First, "leans toward" is not "entails": all three positions are tenet-coherent, and nothing here forces the Map off trumping. Second, the co-causation family carries its own bill — the two-cause structure must still argue that lawful overlap is benign against critics who reply that overdetermination is overdetermination however systematic. The diagnostic's value is not that it settles the question but that it names where the real decision lies: not in the tenets, but in a methodological preference the Map should own explicitly.
