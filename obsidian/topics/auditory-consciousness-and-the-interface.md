Philosophy of perception is overwhelmingly a philosophy of vision. Colour, the inverted spectrum, blindsight, change blindness, the Molyneux problem—the canonical examples are visual, and the [[dualist-perception|Map's own treatment of perception]] concedes that "while vision dominates philosophical discussions, perception extends across modalities." This article develops the auditory case, because hearing supplies three kinds of evidence the visual case lacks or only weakly displays: experience that constitutively unfolds in time rather than presenting a frozen scene, a perceptual organisation problem (auditory scene analysis) that isolates binding from spatial layout, and qualities—pitch and timbre—that are vividly determinate yet appear to carry no intrinsic spatial character. None of these phenomena, on the reading offered here, *settles* dualism over physicalism, which has accounts of each. What they may do is sharpen what any theory must accommodate, and they suggest the Map's [[mind-matter-interface|interface]] reading extends cleanly beyond the visual paradigm that shaped it.

This piece is about audition *as a perceptual modality and interface case*. It is distinct from [[phenomenology-of-musical-understanding|the phenomenology of musical understanding]], which concerns aesthetic normativity and non-propositional meaning in music specifically; here the subject is ordinary hearing—voices, footsteps, a kettle, a single sustained note.

## The Temporal Unfolding of Heard Sound

Vision presents a scene that is, to a first approximation, available all at once. You can fixate a static image and the visual content persists; you can pause and inspect. Sound has no static configuration to inspect. A heard melody, a spoken sentence, even a single tone, exists only as it unfolds. The [[dualist-perception|perception article]] marks the contrast in one line—"an imagined melody can be paused where a heard one unfolds with perceptual authority"—and that line repays development.

The asymmetry it names is real. An imagined melody is under voluntary control: you can hold a note, skip ahead, replay a bar, or freeze the sequence at will. A *heard* melody arrives on the world's schedule. You cannot pause the trumpet; you cannot hold the third beat while you examine it; the sound is gone the instant it sounds and is replaced by the next. Hearing therefore presents its objects with what we might call temporal authority: the content is not yours to arrange. This is arguably one face of the broader [[temporal-consciousness|temporal structure of consciousness]]—the heard present seems to be a span, not an instant (William James's "specious present"), within which succession is itself experienced rather than, on this reading, merely inferred.

The physicalist has a ready account: auditory cortex implements temporal integration windows, echoic memory buffers the recent signal, and predictive models project the next event. Nothing here forces a dualist reading. What the auditory case *does* is make vivid that perceptual consciousness is not generically snapshot-like. A theory built entirely on the visual snapshot—consciousness as a rendered still image—will mis-describe hearing. The Map's interface framework, which treats conscious experience as constituted at the boundary between physical processing and phenomenal presentation, is not committed to snapshots; it can, on this reading, represent a presentation that is intrinsically durational. That is an accommodation, not a proof.

## Auditory Scene Analysis as a Binding Case

The [[the-binding-problem|binding problem]] is usually posed visually: how do separately processed features—colour here, motion there, shape elsewhere—come to be experienced as one bound object? Vision lets the spatial layout do much of the work; features at the same retinal location are plausibly the same object. Audition strips that crutch away. Sounds from different sources arrive superimposed in a single pressure wave at each ear, and the auditory system must decompose that one signal into distinct perceived sources without a spatial map to sort them by.

This is the problem Albert Bregman named **auditory scene analysis** (Bregman, 1990). His framework distinguishes two grouping operations: *sequential* integration, which connects sounds over time into a single ongoing stream representing one source (the recurring melody line, one speaker's voice), and *simultaneous* integration, which partitions the spectral content arriving at one instant into concurrent sounds, each with its own qualities. These can compete: a frequency component may be heard either as the continuation of an earlier stream or as part of a concurrent sound. The "cocktail party" achievement—following one voice in a noisy room—is sequential streaming succeeding against competition.

Auditory scene analysis is valuable for the binding discussion precisely because the grouping cannot be read off a spatial scene. Frequency proximity, harmonicity, common onset, and continuity do the organising work, and the result is a perceived sound *object* that no single point in the signal corresponds to. Bregman's own account is computational and broadly physicalist; he did not argue for any dualist conclusion, and the streaming phenomena have been modelled successfully without one. The point for the Map is structural: scene analysis suggests that phenomenal grouping is not parasitic on spatial co-location, which would mean a theory of binding cannot reduce binding to "features at the same place." On the [[capability-division-in-vision|capability-division]] reading the Map applies to vision, the brain-side capabilities (frequency analysis, onset detection, harmonic grouping) may be taken to deliver organised candidate streams, while the experienced unity of *hearing one sound* appears as the mind-side contribution. The auditory case generalises that division beyond the visual modality where it was first stated—again, as an accommodation the framework permits, not a phenomenon that compels it.

## Non-Spatial Pitch and Timbre Qualia

Visual qualia are saturated with space. Colour is colour *of a region*; shape is spatial by definition. Auditory qualities are different. Sounds have pitch, timbre, and loudness, but—on a standard view in the philosophy of perception—these qualities "lack intrinsic spatial characteristics" (O'Callaghan, *Auditory Perception*, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy). We do localise sound sources, but localisation is computed from interaural timing and level differences; it is not carried within the pitch or timbre itself the way extension is carried within a colour patch. A pitch can rise or fall, but "up" and "down" here are not literal spatial directions—they are a metaphor laid over an ordering that is, at root, a matter of periodicity.

Two features make auditory qualia especially clean test cases for the irreducibility of [[qualia]]. First, the **missing fundamental**: a complex tone lacking any spectral energy at its fundamental frequency is still heard at that fundamental's pitch. The perceived quality does not correspond to a frequency present in the stimulus—pitch is computed, not read off. Second, **timbre** is the quality by which two sounds of identical pitch and loudness still differ—a violin and a flute on the same note. Timbre is multidimensional and resists reduction to any single physical parameter, yet it is immediately and determinately present in experience.

The physicalist treats all of this as more perceptual computation: periodicity extraction yields the missing-fundamental pitch; spectral-envelope and temporal-fine-structure analysis yields timbre. That account is live and well supported, and nothing in the missing fundamental tells against it. What the non-spatial auditory qualities arguably do is press the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]] in a register vision cannot. It is no accident that the canonical statement of the gap—Nagel's question of what it is like to be a bat (Nagel, 1974)—reaches for an *auditory* creature whose world is built from echolocation; the felt character of a heard quality is where the "what it's like" pressure is most easily isolated from any external object-property to identify it with. With colour one can be tempted to locate the quality in the surface—to say yellowness just *is* a reflectance. Pitch and timbre seem to have no such external home: there is no "fundamental frequency" sitting in the world to identify the missing-fundamental pitch with, and no single physical magnitude that plausibly identifies timbre. The quality appears determinate in experience while being conspicuously non-identical to any one stimulus property. That may make audition a sharp illustration of why qualia resist reduction—an illustration, the Map holds, rather than a demonstration.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The auditory distinctives above are framed throughout as phenomena the dualist/interface reading *accommodates*, not as evidence that settles the dispute. The physicalist has a credible account of each—temporal integration windows, computational scene analysis, periodicity-based pitch extraction—and those accounts are taken seriously here. The Map's claim is the weaker, defensible one: a theory of consciousness modelled on the visual snapshot will systematically mis-describe hearing, and the interface framework, by contrast, extends to audition without strain.

**[[tenets#^dualism|Dualism]]**: Auditory qualities arguably sharpen the case that [[qualia]] are not identical with physical properties, because pitch and timbre appear to have no external object-property to be identified with—the missing fundamental has no fundamental in the signal. This may make audition a clean illustration of the [[explanatory-gap|explanatory gap]], while leaving the physicalist's computational reply standing. The Map reads perceptual qualities as constituted at the [[mind-matter-interface|interface]] between physical processing and conscious experience; the auditory case suggests that reading is not merely a visual artefact.

**[[tenets#^bidirectional-interaction|Bidirectional Interaction]]**: The active, directed character the [[dualist-perception|perception article]] attributes to seeing applies equally to hearing—we *listen to* a sound, attend selectively to one stream, foreground a voice. The Map interprets this attentional selection in auditory scene analysis as consonant with bidirectional interaction: phenomenal attention participates in which stream is foregrounded. This is an interpretive alignment, not a finding; the epiphenomenalist can re-describe the same selection as wholly neural, and the Map's commitment rests on its broader [[predictive-processing-and-dualism|interaction framework]] rather than on the auditory case alone.

The Map does not claim audition settles dualism. It claims audition is evidence any adequate theory must accommodate, and that a framework shaped by vision alone seems incomplete until it does.
