## Executive Summary

Eliminative materialism (EM) is the radical thesis that our ordinary, common-sense conception of mind ("folk psychology") is a deeply false theory whose ontology—beliefs, desires, qualia, the self—will be displaced rather than reduced by mature neuroscience. Distinct from reductive physicalism, which holds that mental states are identical to brain states, eliminativism denies that the mental categories pick out anything real. The position has two principal strands: classic eliminativism about propositional attitudes (Paul and Patricia Churchland) and contemporary illusionism about phenomenal consciousness (Frankish, Dennett). Eliminativism stands as the most direct philosophical antagonist of the Map's dualist commitments: where the Map argues that consciousness is irreducible and causally efficacious, eliminativism argues that the very categories we use to describe consciousness are theoretically bankrupt. An article on this topic must engage seriously with the strongest forms of the view rather than dismissing it.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Eliminative Materialism
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/materialism-eliminative/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - EM is a "radical claim that our ordinary, common-sense understanding of the mind is deeply wrong"
  - The term was first introduced by James Cornman in 1968; C.D. Broad's 1925 *The Mind and its Place in Nature* discussed a related "pure materialism"
  - Foundational thinkers: Wilfred Sellars (1956 "Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind"), W.V.O. Quine, Paul Feyerabend, Richard Rorty
  - Distinguished from reductionism by Lycan and Pappas (1972): true eliminativism requires claiming mental notions pick out nothing real
  - Two strategies against folk psychology: (a) general theoretical poverty, (b) specific problems with sentential structure and semantic content
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Dualism, Bidirectional Interaction, Minimal Quantum Interaction; weaponizes Occam's Razor in a way the Map argues against
- **Quote**: "Mental notions pick out nothing real... a mature science of the mind will surpass this limited framework entirely"

### Paul Churchland, "Eliminative Materialism and the Propositional Attitudes" (1981)
- **URL**: https://www.sfu.ca/~kathleea/docs/Eliminative%20materialism.pdf
- **Type**: Journal article (foundational paper)
- **Key points**:
  - Folk psychology (FP) is a stagnant theory: fails to explain dreams, mental illness, consciousness, memory, learning
  - Inductive argument: folk physics, folk biology, folk epidemiology all proved radically false; FP addresses far more complex phenomena and is therefore likely worse
  - FP's commitment to sentential, compositional structures conflicts with brain's actual neurocomputational architecture
  - Mental concepts are theoretical posits, not introspective reports—so introspection cannot vindicate them
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts directly with Dualism and the Map's view of phenomenology as evidence
- **Quote**: "FP suffers explanatory failures on an epic scale... it has been stagnant for at least twenty-five centuries"

### Patricia Churchland, *Neurophilosophy* (1986)
- **URL**: https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/1052850.Neurophilosophy
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Neuroscience portends "a bleak future for commonsense mental states"
  - Argues for vector-coding/neurocomputational replacement of linguistic-format mental representation
  - Established the methodological program of "neurophilosophy" — philosophy answerable to neuroscience
  - Pressed philosophers to consider neural plasticity, brain lesions, computational models for mental representation
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Dualism; exemplifies the "physicalism by promissory note" the Map critiques
- **Notable**: Patricia Churchland is UC President's Professor of Philosophy Emerita at UC San Diego

### Keith Frankish, "Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness" (2016)
- **URL**: https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_Illusionism%20as%20a%20theory%20of%20consciousness_eprint.pdf
- **Type**: Journal article (target paper, *JCS* special issue)
- **Key points**:
  - Phenomenal properties are illusory; introspection misrepresents experiential states as having irreducible phenomenal character
  - Distinguishes "classic qualia" (target of Dennett's attack), "zero qualia" (functional only), "diet qualia" (intermediate)
  - States possess only *quasi-phenomenal* properties—non-phenomenal features that are systematically misrepresented as phenomenal
  - In 2024, Frankish published "The Ethical Implications of Illusionism"
  - Illusionism is "entity eliminativism about phenomenal consciousness even if the label 'eliminativism' is avoided"
- **Tenet alignment**: Direct conflict with the Map's view that phenomenal evidence is real and constitutive
- **Quote**: "Where the realist sees a hard problem, the illusionist sees an illusion problem—why does it *seem* there is a hard problem?"

### Daniel Dennett, "Illusionism as the Obvious Default Theory of Consciousness" (2016)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/DENIAT-3
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - Endorses Frankish's illusionism as the front-runner among theories of consciousness
  - Builds on his "Quining Qualia" (1988): qualia concepts are "fundamentally confused, with properties pulling in incompatible directions"
  - 1978 pain argument: morphine subjects report excruciating pain without unpleasantness, contradicting essentialist assumptions about pain
- **Tenet alignment**: Direct conflict with Dualism and phenomenological evidence
- **Quote**: "Where consciousness is concerned, the existence of the appearance is the reality" (Searle's objection, which Dennett rejects)

### Ramsey, Stich, Garon, "Connectionism, Eliminativism, and the Future of Folk Psychology" (1990)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-94-011-2500-0_7
- **Type**: Book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - Distributed connectionist networks store information without causally discrete, semantically evaluable structures
  - "There do not appear to be any structures in these networks that might serve as candidates for beliefs"
  - If cognition is implemented this way, the propositional-attitude framework lacks an implementation base
  - Provides the first concrete cognitive-science model that vindicates eliminativist conclusions rather than just promising one
- **Tenet alignment**: Indirect conflict via undermining of mental ontology; ironically the Map can use distributed representation arguments against substrate-independence claims
- **Quote**: "There are no causally distinct structures that stand for specific states of affairs"

### Boghossian, "The Status of Content" (1990)
- **URL**: Referenced in SEP entry on EM
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - Sophisticated self-refutation argument: arguments for irrealism about propositional-attitude content generalize to all content, including linguistic content
  - This produces incoherence: claiming there are no truth conditions while asserting that certain sentences about content are false
  - Devitt and Rey respond that this begs the question by ascribing truth-conditional semantics to the eliminativist
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Map's view that eliminativism is self-undermining

### Hutto and Myin, *Radicalizing Enactivism* (2012) and *Evolving Enactivism* (2017)
- **URL**: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/319110376_Mental_representation_and_two_kinds_of_eliminativism
- **Type**: Books
- **Key points**:
  - "Hard problem of content": naturalistic theories of representation cannot explain how neurological states acquire genuine semantic properties
  - Radical enactivism rejects inner mental representations entirely
  - Distinct from Churchland-style eliminativism: targets *content* rather than *folk categories*
- **Tenet alignment**: Mixed—shares with the Map the rejection of representation-as-easy-naturalization; conflicts with mental ontology

## Major Positions

### Classical Eliminativism (Paul Churchland 1981)
- **Proponents**: Paul Churchland, Patricia Churchland, Stephen Stich (early), Feyerabend, Rorty
- **Core claim**: Folk psychology is a false empirical theory whose ontology will be displaced by neuroscience. Beliefs, desires, intentions, etc., do not exist.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. *Theoretical poverty*: FP fails to explain mental illness, dreams, learning, consciousness
  2. *Inductive argument*: Other folk theories (folk physics, folk biology) failed; FP is more ambitious and likely worse
  3. *Mismatch with neuroscience*: brain doesn't contain sentence-like structures
  4. *Stagnation*: FP has not advanced in 2500 years
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct conflict with Dualism (denies mental ontology), Bidirectional Interaction (no mental causation if mental states aren't real), Minimal Quantum Interaction (no role for consciousness in physics). The Map's response: treats failure-to-reduce not as evidence of folk-theory falsity but as evidence of consciousness's irreducibility (see [reductionism-and-consciousness](/topics/reductionism-and-consciousness/)).

### Illusionism / Eliminativism about Phenomenal Consciousness
- **Proponents**: Keith Frankish, Daniel Dennett, François Kammerer, Jay Garfield (some readings)
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal properties are illusory. There are no qualia in the rich sense; introspection misrepresents quasi-phenomenal properties as phenomenal.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. The "hard problem" dissolves into an "illusion problem": why does it *seem* there is a hard problem?
  2. Dennett's "Quining Qualia": qualia concepts are conceptually incoherent
  3. Evolutionary debunking: phenomenal-belief-formation mechanisms could exist without phenomenal properties
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct conflict with the Map's reliance on phenomenal evidence. Map's response: "where consciousness is concerned, the existence of the appearance is the reality" (Searle); the illusion theory needs to explain who is being deceived.

### Kind Dissolutionism (Griffiths, Machery)
- **Proponents**: Paul Griffiths (emotion), Edouard Machery (concepts)
- **Core claim**: Folk categories like *emotion* and *concept* heterogeneously lump distinct scientific kinds and should be abandoned scientifically—but this is taxonomy reform, not full eliminativism.
- **Key arguments**:
  - *Emotion* incorrectly lumps affect programs, higher cognitive emotions, and social constructions
  - *Concepts* encompass prototypes, exemplars, and theories—too heterogeneous
- **Relation to site tenets**: Mostly orthogonal; the Map can accept that some folk categories are messy without conceding eliminativism about consciousness itself.

### Mental Fictionalism (Toon 2023)
- **Proponents**: Adam Toon
- **Core claim**: Propositional-attitude ascriptions are make-believe rather than genuine theoretical posits.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction; legal/moral systems treat beliefs as causally efficacious.

## Key Debates

### The Self-Refutation Debate
- **Sides**:
  - *Standard objection*: To assert EM, eliminativist must believe it; if there are no beliefs, the assertion is incoherent
  - *Eliminativist response (Churchland, Devitt, Rey)*: Bare thesis "there are no beliefs" is not contradictory; the objection begs the question by assuming FP's truth
  - *Boghossian's sophisticated version*: Arguments against propositional-attitude content generalize to all linguistic content, producing genuine incoherence
- **Core disagreement**: Whether semantic notions are presupposed by the very act of theorizing
- **Current state**: Ongoing; most philosophers regard the standard self-refutation as question-begging but Boghossian-style arguments as more challenging

### Folk Psychology's Predictive Success
- **Sides**:
  - *Realists*: FP works—we successfully predict behavior with it daily; inference to best explanation supports realism
  - *Eliminativists*: Vitalism and phlogiston theory also seemed to work; predictive success is compatible with profound falsity
- **Core disagreement**: How much weight to put on practical success vs. theoretical integration with science
- **Current state**: Stalemate; depends on developments in cognitive neuroscience

### Connectionism vs. Classical Cognitive Architecture
- **Sides**:
  - *Eliminativists (Ramsey/Stich/Garon)*: Distributed networks lack discrete propositional structures
  - *Defenders (Fodor, Pylyshyn)*: Cognition requires systematic, productive structure that distributed networks cannot deliver without becoming classical
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the implementation level matters for the existence of beliefs
- **Current state**: Modern deep learning has revived this debate; LLMs raise new questions about whether language-using systems require beliefs

### The Status of Phenomenal Consciousness
- **Sides**:
  - *Illusionists (Frankish, Dennett)*: Phenomenal consciousness is illusory; only quasi-phenomenal properties exist
  - *Realists (Chalmers, Goff, Searle)*: Phenomenal consciousness is the one thing whose appearance and reality cannot come apart
  - *Mooreans*: I am more certain phenomenal consciousness exists than I am of any premise eliminativism uses against it
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the appearance/reality distinction applies to phenomenal experience
- **Current state**: Active and growing; 2024-2025 saw new "Defense of Moorean Argument Against Illusionism" papers (Frontiers in Psychology, 2024)

2. **Eliminativism as the limit case of physicalism**: Frame EM as showing what physicalism *commits to* if pursued consistently—not a fringe position but the natural endpoint of the physicalist project. Useful because it sharpens the choice: either accept eliminativism's bullets, or recognize that the failure to eliminate is evidence for irreducibility. Connects to [reductionism-and-consciousness](/topics/reductionism-and-consciousness/).

3. **The introspection-as-theory-laden challenge**: Eliminativism's deepest move is making introspection itself epistemically suspect. The Map's reliance on phenomenal evidence depends on rejecting this. An article could focus narrowly on this epistemological pivot. Connects to [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/) and [introspection](/concepts/introspection/).

4. **Self-refutation, properly understood**: A focused article on whether EM is incoherent. Useful because the simple version begs the question, but Boghossian's version has teeth. The Map can argue that EM is not strictly incoherent but is *self-undermining* in a weaker sense: the conditions for its assertibility are precisely the conditions it denies. Connects to [argument-from-reason](/topics/argument-from-reason/).

5. **Illusionism specifically (separate article)**: Treat Frankish-Dennett illusionism as a distinct topic given its 2020s prominence. The "illusion problem" framing deserves its own treatment. Could be a follow-on after this primary article.
