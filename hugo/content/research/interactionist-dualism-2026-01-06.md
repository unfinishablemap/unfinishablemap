## Executive Summary

Interactionist dualism holds that mind and body are distinct substances (or have distinct properties) that causally influence each other. The classical formulation from Descartes faced the "interaction problem": how can an immaterial mind cause physical changes in the brain? Contemporary responses fall into two main camps: (1) philosophical arguments that challenge the causal closure assumption, and (2) quantum mechanical proposals that identify specific physical mechanisms where mental causation could occur without violating conservation laws. The most developed quantum proposals come from Henry Stapp (quantum Zeno effect in synapses) and Penrose-Hameroff (orchestrated objective reduction in microtubules). These align closely with the site's Minimal Quantum Interaction and Bidirectional Interaction tenets, though both face empirical challenges related to decoherence timescales.

## Major Positions

### Classical Cartesian Interactionism
- **Proponents**: René Descartes (1596-1650)
- **Core claim**: Mind (res cogitans) and body (res extensa) are distinct substances that causally interact, possibly via the pineal gland
- **Key arguments**:
  - We have clear and distinct ideas of mind (thinking) and body (extension)
  - What we can clearly conceive as distinct can exist separately
  - Yet our experience shows mind and body influence each other
- **Relation to site tenets**: Historical foundation for Dualism tenet; Bidirectional Interaction updates mechanism

### Property Dualism / Naturalistic Dualism
- **Proponents**: David Chalmers, contemporary philosophers
- **Core claim**: Mental properties are ontologically distinct from physical properties but need not require separate substances
- **Key arguments**:
  - The conceivability of zombies shows consciousness isn't entailed by physics
  - Consciousness is fundamental, like mass or charge
  - Lawful but non-reductive relationship between mental and physical
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible with Dualism tenet; Chalmers now expresses preference for substance dualism

### Quantum Interactionism (Stapp)
- **Proponents**: Henry Stapp (Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory)
- **Core claim**: Consciousness influences brain via quantum Zeno effect at synaptic level
- **Key arguments**:
  - Von Neumann's orthodox QM assigns special role to observer
  - Attention holds quantum superpositions in place via rapid "observations"
  - Allows top-down mental causation without violating conservation laws
- **Mechanism**: Mental effort maintains "template for action" neural patterns by preventing decoherence
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly supports Minimal Quantum Interaction ("biasing otherwise indeterminate outcomes") and Bidirectional Interaction

### Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Penrose-Hameroff)
- **Proponents**: Roger Penrose (physicist), Stuart Hameroff (anesthesiologist)
- **Core claim**: Consciousness originates from quantum computations in microtubules within neurons
- **Key arguments**:
  - Classical computation cannot explain non-algorithmic aspects of understanding (Gödel)
  - Microtubules maintain quantum coherence long enough for computation
  - "Objective reduction" (gravitational collapse) generates moments of conscious experience
- **Relation to site tenets**: Alternative mechanism for quantum consciousness; compatible with Minimal Quantum Interaction but differs in specifics

### Emergentist Interactionism
- **Proponents**: Various contemporary philosophers
- **Core claim**: Mental properties emerge from but are not reducible to physical organization, and exert downward causation
- **Key arguments**:
  - Strong emergence is metaphysically possible
  - Emergent properties can have novel causal powers
  - Does not require separate substance
- **Relation to site tenets**: Weaker than site's commitment but potentially compatible

## Key Debates

### The Interaction Problem

**Sides**:
- Critics (from Princess Elizabeth to Kim): How can something non-spatial causally affect something spatial? What mechanism could bridge ontologically distinct realms?
- Defenders: Various responses (see below)

**Core disagreement**: Whether causal interaction between different kinds of substance/property is coherent

**Current state**: The problem shifted focus from "what's the mechanism?" to "does physics allow it?"

### The Causal Closure Argument

**Sides**:
- Physicalists (Kim, Papineau): Every physical event has sufficient physical causes; no room for mental causation
- Interactionists: Closure is assumed not proven; quantum mechanics provides exceptions

**Core disagreement**: Is the physical world causally closed?

**Responses to closure argument**:
1. **Challenge closure**: Physics doesn't actually support strict closure; it's a metaphysical assumption
2. **Energy redistribution**: Mental causation redistributes energy without adding it
3. **Overdetermination**: Physical events can be overdetermined by both physical and mental causes
4. **Quantum indeterminacy**: At quantum level, physics leaves outcomes undetermined; consciousness can influence which occurs

**Current state**: Ongoing debate; quantum responses increasingly sophisticated

### Decoherence Objection to Quantum Consciousness

**Sides**:
- Critics (Tegmark, others): Decoherence in warm, wet brains is too fast (~10^-13 seconds) for quantum effects to matter
- Proponents (Stapp, Hameroff): Various responses about timescales, protected subspaces, or different mechanisms

**Core disagreement**: Whether quantum coherence can be maintained long enough in neural tissue for consciousness-relevant effects

**Current state**: Active empirical research; 2024-2025 studies finding quantum effects in biological systems at room temperature (superradiance in tryptophan networks); debate continues

### Property Dualism vs. Substance Dualism

**Sides**:
- Property dualists (early Chalmers): Only need distinct properties, not substances
- Substance dualists (Swinburne, later Chalmers): Requires distinct mental substance for personal identity

**Core disagreement**: What ontological commitments does dualism require?

**Current state**: Chalmers (2016) expressed preference for substance dualism; distinction may matter less than shared commitment to irreducibility
