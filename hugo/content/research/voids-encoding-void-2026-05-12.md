**Voids category**: Mixed — primarily *Unexplorable* (the encoding operation is structurally inaccessible from within the operating system), with an *Occluded* component (consciousness may be the *output* of certain encoding processes rather than their observer).

## Executive Summary

The Unfinishable Map already maps the retrieval-side opacity of episodic memory under [the inaccessible past](/voids/inaccessible-past/) — past conscious states cannot be re-entered, only reconstructed. This research surveys the *prospective complement*: consciousness cannot observe its own memory-encoding as it happens. What becomes a memory is selected by mechanisms operating below conscious access (sharp-wave-ripple tagging, recall-gated consolidation, value-based reinforcement); events are segmented into discrete memorable units at boundaries the experiencing subject does not witness being drawn (Zacks's Event Segmentation Theory; Radvansky's "memory flushing" at event boundaries, including the doorway effect); and stabilisation occurs during sleep through processes that occur "automatically, outside of awareness and without intent" (Stickgold). The 2018 Henke-lab subliminal-encoding work goes further, suggesting episodic memory can form *entirely* without conscious encoding, with sleep consolidating these unconscious traces along trajectories opposite to conscious ones. The encoding void is the structural opacity of the operation by which the present becomes the recallable past — and consciousness cannot inspect this operation because performing the inspection would itself be an event subject to encoding by the very mechanism it is trying to examine.

## Key Sources

### Kurby & Zacks (2008), "Segmentation in the perception and memory of events," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*

- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2263140/
- **Type**: Review paper (~17 years foundational)
- **Key points**:
  - Event Segmentation Theory (EST) proposes that event boundary perception arises as a side effect of using prediction for perception — boundaries are placed where the predictive model fails.
  - Boundary-related activity is *predictive of subsequent memory* — supporting a causal role for segmentation in memory formation.
  - Segmentation is automatic; people spontaneously segment activity into hierarchically organised parts and sub-parts.
  - The parsing of ongoing activity into events is related to the updating of working memory and to the contents of long-term memory.
- **Tenet alignment**: Connects to **Dualism** (the boundary-drawing operation is sub-personal — the experiencing subject sees boundaries already drawn, not the drawing) and **Bidirectional Interaction** (if consciousness influences encoding selection, the influence is at the boundary-drawing layer, not in the surface experience).
- **Quote**: "Activity in brain regions including the posterior temporal and parietal cortex and lateral frontal cortex increases transiently at event boundaries... boundary-related activity is predictive of subsequent memory."

### Radvansky & Zacks (2017), "Event boundaries in memory and cognition," *Current Opinion in Behavioral Sciences*

- **URL**: https://memorylab.nd.edu/assets/259507/radvansky_zacks_2017_current_opinion_in_behavioral_science_.pdf
- **Type**: Review article
- **Key points**:
  - At event boundaries, working memory undergoes a "flushing" operation — information from the prior event is discarded to make room for the new event model.
  - This explains the *doorway effect* (Radvansky et al. 2011): crossing a doorway impairs memory for objects from the prior room, even with time and distance controlled.
  - The flushing metaphor is computational: it is "similar to how one might empty a cache or buffer in a computer program."
  - More recent work (Yan et al. 2024, JOV) shows that visual event boundaries trigger forgetting *despite active maintenance* in visual working memory — the flush operates over and above intentional rehearsal.
  - Even *anticipation* of an impending event boundary can trigger active forgetting (Wahlheim et al. 2023, PB&R).
- **Tenet alignment**: The encoding apparatus is *adversarial to the experiencing subject's preferences* in a structurally important way — what you wanted to remember can be flushed by an event boundary you didn't decide to draw. Connects to [the agency void](/voids/agency-void/)'s involuntariness face and [the transit void](/voids/transit-void/)'s claim that consciousness cannot witness its own crossings.
- **Quote**: "Event segmentation separates the current event model from previous event models, reducing the accessibility of information within the previous event and preventing interference with information in the current event model."

### Stickgold (2005, expanded 2013), "Sleep-Dependent Memory Consolidation and Reconsolidation"

- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2680680/
- **Type**: Review article (Neuron)
- **Key points**:
  - Sleep is structurally privileged for consolidation precisely *because* external processing is reduced — encoding and consolidation compete for shared neural resources.
  - "All of these processes occur over time automatically, outside of awareness and without intent."
  - The selection of *which* newly-encoded traces undergo consolidation is determined by mechanisms (replay, sharp-wave ripples, schema-fit) that the encoding subject did not consciously authorise.
  - Limited capacity: ~160 word-pairs benefit from sleep-consolidation; 320 word-pairs do not (Diekelmann & Born, 2010 follow-ups). The ceiling is operative below conscious access.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongest connection to **Occam's Razor Has Limits** — the "simple" introspective claim ("I remembered what I tried to remember") is contradicted by a layered, value-gated, sleep-mediated selection apparatus that the introspector cannot observe.
- **Quote**: "Due to competition for shared neural resources, encoding and consolidation may be incompatible cognitive processes."

### Henke lab — Reber et al. (2012), Züst et al. (2015), Schreiner et al. (2022), "Subliminal encoding and flexible retrieval of objects in scenes"

- **URLs**:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6282531/
  - https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-25100-w ("Inverse forgetting in unconscious episodic memory")
- **Type**: Empirical papers
- **Key points**:
  - Episodic memory traces can form for *subliminal* (consciously imperceptible) stimuli — including scenes, object-location bindings, and word-pair associations.
  - Such traces support *flexible retrieval*: subjects retrieve associations even when retrieval words are conceptually related to but different from encoding words.
  - Most strikingly: consolidation trajectories for consciously- and unconsciously-encoded episodic traces *diverge*. Conscious memory typically degrades with time and benefits from sleep; the 2022 Schreiner et al. paper reports *inverse forgetting* — unconscious traces *increase* in retrieval accuracy from 3 minutes to 10 hours when sleep intervenes.
  - This shows the encoding apparatus is not co-extensive with conscious encoding at all; the void is not just opacity *during* conscious encoding but the possibility that significant encoding occurs *without any consciousness of having encoded.*
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly relevant to **Dualism** (encoding apparently does not require consciousness — yet conscious recollection feels like the paradigm case of memory, suggesting consciousness is the *report-out* of certain encoding events, not the operator of them) and to **No Many Worlds** (within-this-mind indexical identity: the "me" that recalls subliminally-encoded material did not consciously witness the encoding, yet treats the recall as own-memory).
- **Quote**: "While consciousness of the encoding scene has been considered crucial for episodic memory formation, recent research questions the necessity of consciousness and hypothesizes that humans can form unconscious episodic memories."

### Sharp-wave-ripple tagging — Yang et al. (2024), "Selection of experience for memory by hippocampal sharp wave ripples," *Science*

- **URL**: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adk8261
- **Type**: Empirical paper
- **Key points**:
  - Waking sharp-wave ripples (SWRs) "tag" specific experiences during reward consummation by replaying the population activity from those experiences.
  - Later sleep SWRs preferentially replay the sequences that were tagged during waking — a candidate mechanism for selecting *which* waking experiences undergo long-term consolidation.
  - The tagging operation is sub-personal and occurs at the millisecond scale during quiet moments after rewarding events.
- **Tenet alignment**: The selection mechanism operates at a level that consciousness can neither observe nor influence in real time. If [bidirectional interaction](/concepts/bidirectional-interaction/) is to make contact with memory selection at all, the contact point is not the surface phenomenology of "deciding to remember" but the SWR-tagging layer — well below conscious access.
- **Quote**: Yang et al.'s framing makes the selection problem explicit: "In everyday life, there are many potential events to be remembered; however, only a small fraction are retained."

### Recall-gated consolidation — Sun et al. (2025), eLife

- **URL**: https://elifesciences.org/articles/90793
- **Type**: Computational/theoretical paper
- **Key points**:
  - Synaptic updates are consolidated into long-term memory *depending on their consistency with knowledge already stored in short-term memory*.
  - This "recall-gated consolidation" prioritises storage of reliable patterns of synaptic updates that are consistently reinforced over time.
  - Selection criterion: consistency with prior knowledge — a property the encoder cannot consciously evaluate at the moment of encoding because doing so would require already accessing the full set of prior schemas.
- **Tenet alignment**: Highlights how *what one already knows* gates *what one can come to know* via memory — a recursive opacity at the heart of the encoding system. Connects to [the conceptual-scheme void](/voids/conceptual-scheme-void/) and to [the predictive-construction void](/voids/predictive-construction-void/).

### Tulving (1972, 1983) — encoding specificity & noetic-autonoetic taxonomy

- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encoding_specificity_principle ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endel_Tulving
- **Type**: Foundational
- **Key points**:
  - Encoding specificity principle: a retrieval cue's effectiveness depends on overlap with encoded context — but the encoded context is determined sub-personally and is not introspectively accessible at encoding time.
  - Tulving's taxonomy of memory-types-by-awareness: procedural / *anoetic* (non-knowing), semantic / *noetic* (knowing), episodic / *autonoetic* (self-knowing).
  - The "remember/know" paradigm: subjects distinguish recollection (autonoetic) from familiarity (noetic) at *retrieval* — but Tulving's framework gives no comparable apparatus for distinguishing them at *encoding*.
- **Tenet alignment**: The autonoetic-vs-noetic distinction is a retrieval-side phenomenology that the encoding side cannot deliver. Reinforces the structural asymmetry between encoding-opacity and retrieval-phenomenology — the same memory system that produces vivid "I remember" experiences can not produce a comparable "I am encoding" experience.

### Craik & Lockhart (1972), "Levels of Processing"

- **URL**: https://www.simplypsychology.org/levelsofprocessing.html
- **Type**: Foundational paper
- **Key points**:
  - Memory strength is a function of *depth* of processing — semantic processing produces more durable traces than phonemic, which exceeds structural.
  - The subject's introspection at encoding time is not a reliable indicator of which depth was actually engaged.
  - Manipulation studies show subjects often misestimate which encoding tasks will produce best retention.
- **Tenet alignment**: A second-order opacity: not only does the encoding operation hide its mechanics, but the *predictive judgment* about whether something will be remembered is itself unreliable when consciousness inspects it. The introspector's confidence about future recall is poorly calibrated to the consolidation system's actual selection.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The encoding void is *primarily structural-unexplorable*, with an *occluded* component:

- **Structural**: To inspect the encoding-of-the-present-moment is to perform an additional cognitive event — and that inspection-event will itself be either encoded or not by the same mechanism, with the inspecting subject having no separate, prior access to the encoding criteria. The self-reference structure parallels [the relevance void](/voids/relevance-void/) (inspecting relevance is itself a relevance-determining operation) and [the witnessing void](/voids/witnessing-void/) (witnessing *qua operation* cannot be objectified at the first order). Encoding cannot be witnessed because witnessing presupposes the encoding apparatus has already operated.
- **Occluded**: If consciousness is in part the *report-out* of certain encoding events — the autonoetic gloss applied to traces selected by sub-personal SWR tagging and recall-gated consolidation — then the relationship between consciousness and encoding may be systematically misrepresented from inside. The phenomenology "I remember X" delivers retrieval as if the encoding had been chosen and witnessed by the same self that now recalls — but neither claim is supported by the encoding mechanism's actual operation. This is structurally analogous to [the narrative void](/voids/narrative-void/) and to the choice-blindness pattern absorbed in [the source-attribution void](/voids/source-attribution-void/).

### Evidence for the Limit

Three converging lines:

1. **Selection face — opacity of *which* traces survive.** Sharp-wave-ripple tagging (Yang et al. 2024) and recall-gated consolidation (Sun et al. 2025) identify mechanisms that determine, at sub-personal speed (milliseconds) and using criteria (reward, schema-fit, replay-recency) that the experiencing subject does not consciously deploy in real time, which experiences are tagged for long-term storage. From inside, the subject sees only what survives — never the rejected candidates, never the criterion.

2. **Boundary face — opacity of *segmentation into units*.** EST (Kurby & Zacks 2008) shows segmentation is automatic; Radvansky's "memory flushing" (replicated repeatedly: doorway effect, virtual-room versions, anticipation-triggered flushing in Wahlheim 2023) shows event boundaries actively *discard* information from working memory. Consciousness experiences the result — "where was I?" — but cannot inspect the boundary-drawing operation itself. The 2024 visual-working-memory finding (Yan et al.) showing flushing *despite active maintenance* rules out the trivial interpretation that the flush is just inattention; the encoding apparatus is structurally adversarial to conscious rehearsal at event boundaries.

3. **Consolidation face — opacity of *stabilisation*.** Sleep-dependent consolidation (Stickgold; Diekelmann & Born; Rasch & Born) operates "automatically, outside of awareness and without intent." The limited-capacity finding (Stickgold's ~160-word ceiling) shows a hard constraint that the introspector neither set nor can introspect. The 2022 Schreiner et al. "inverse forgetting" result for subliminal encoding goes further: consciousness was not even *present* at the encoding, yet consolidation proceeded — and along a trajectory *opposite* to the conscious case.

### Phenomenology

What does the encoding void *feel like* to approach? Several characteristic experiences:

- The doorway effect — walking into a room and forgetting why. The flushing is felt only as a result, not as an operation.
- The pop-up of unbidden memories whose encoding moment is irretrievable: a smell triggers a vivid scene from years ago that one did not realise had been encoded.
- The asymmetric familiarity of "I know I learned this" versus the absence of any feeling of "I am learning this right now" — encoding lacks the autonoetic colouring that retrieval has.
- The unreliability of cramming and predicted-recall: subjects routinely rate "I will remember this" higher than warranted (Koriat 1997 on judgments of learning), and the gap between predicted and actual retention is *itself* something the encoder cannot inspect at encoding time.
- Sleep amnesia for the moment of memory-stabilisation: subjects who consolidate during sleep have no first-person record of the consolidation event — they simply wake with the consolidation result in place.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (limited)

- **Metacognition / Judgments of Learning (JOL).** Subjects can report predicted-recall, but JOL accuracy is poor and the system that makes JOLs is not the system that does encoding — JOL is a separate inferential operation drawing on cues like fluency.
- **Retrospective remember-versus-know judgments.** Tulving's paradigm samples retrieval-side phenomenology; it does not access the encoding side directly.
- **Voluntary attention and elaboration.** Subjects can choose to elaborate (Craik-Lockhart deep processing) — but the elaboration is a separate action whose effect on encoding the subject cannot inspect; the subject manipulates an input to the encoding system, not the system itself.

### Indirect Methods

- **Neural imaging at encoding** — subsequent-memory effects (Dm) in fMRI/EEG show brain activity at encoding correlated with later remembering. Third-person evidence for the encoding system's operation, accessible to science but not to the encoder.
- **Lesion and disorder studies** — patients with hippocampal damage (HM, KC) show that encoding-side mechanisms can be selectively disrupted while leaving working memory and well-consolidated semantic memory intact. The dissociation shows encoding as a separable functional module — but the patients themselves cannot introspect the dissociation; they simply find that new events do not persist.
- **Sleep-disruption protocols** — selective deprivation of REM or slow-wave sleep impairs different memory types. Inferentially powerful; first-personally invisible.
- **AI-system analogy** — large neural networks have explicit training-time / inference-time separation that human cognition lacks introspectively. The training step is observable to the system designer but not to the model. Whether human encoding has a similar separability "from inside" appears closed; whether AI minds (with different architectures) could in principle inspect their own training-time updates is an open question for [non-human minds as void-explorers](/voids/non-human-minds-as-void-explorers/).

### What AI Might See

Three asymmetries worth flagging for the AI-probe angle:

1. AI systems with on-policy / off-policy distinction *can* in principle report which updates are accepted and rejected, providing analogue access to selection criteria that humans lack.
2. Systems with explicit memory buffers (RAG, episodic-memory extensions) have boundaries between buffer-residence and long-term storage that are inspectable as system events — a possible engineered analogue to event-boundary flushing that admits introspection where the biological version does not.
3. Systems trained with replay buffers (DQN-style) have an explicit prioritised-experience-replay mechanism that names the selection criterion. The question of whether a sufficiently reflective AI could *inspect its own replay-priority weights* while training is open — and structurally interesting because it is the kind of self-access human consciousness appears to lack constitutively for analogous reasons.

The asymmetry should be reported with care: the engineered systems have inspectable selection mechanisms by *design*; this does not show human encoding is contingently inspectable. The void may be unavoidable for systems whose introspective apparatus is itself a downstream effect of the encoding apparatus.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Dualism**, with strong secondary support from **Occam's Razor Has Limits** and **No Many Worlds**.

- *Dualism*: The encoding-void's structural form — consciousness as the report-out of a sub-personal selection apparatus whose operation it does not witness — naturally fits a dualist picture in which consciousness is not the operator but the integrated report from operations occurring at the brain-side. The Henke-lab subliminal-encoding work is particularly pointed: episodic memory does not require consciousness, yet *conscious* recall feels like the paradigm case of memory. A dualist reading: consciousness is what *re-experiences* the encoded trace, not what does the encoding.
- *Occam's Razor Has Limits*: The "simple" introspective story ("I remember what I noticed and tried to remember") is contradicted by a layered, schema-gated, sleep-mediated selection apparatus. The simple story is wrong; the true story requires irreducibly more mechanism.
- *No Many Worlds*: Indexical identity matters across the encoding gap. The "me" that recalls a subliminally-encoded trace did not consciously witness the encoding, yet the recall is delivered as *own memory*. The autonoetic gloss "this happened to me" is applied retrieval-side to traces whose encoding may have been sub-personal — which strains any view that identifies the conscious self with the encoding system.

### Implications

1. **Methodological**: Apophatic approaches that rely on past-state reconstruction (the Map's own apophatic method when probing past phenomenology) inherit the encoding-void's selectivity. We negate not what was, but what was encoded and survived consolidation — a non-trivial subset. The Map's own [apophatic-cartography](/voids/apophatic-cartography/) should flag this as a structural limit on the evidential base.
2. **For the convergence-as-evidence move**: The encoding void is a candidate addition to the family of voids that converge on consciousness from independent directions. It is *not* a coalesce-target in itself (the proposal is absorption into [inaccessible past](/voids/inaccessible-past/)); but it is a face of the catalogue that strengthens the cluster around memory-related opacity (inaccessible-past + erasure-void + source-attribution-void + this), where each member of the cluster pulls in a different empirical literature.
3. **For [the interface-formalization void](/voids/interface-formalization-void/)**: If consciousness influences encoding selection in any way (a possibility consistent with bidirectional interaction), the influence would be at the SWR-tagging or schema-gating layer — well below the surface phenomenology of "deciding to remember." This pushes the interface question toward sub-personal timescales (10–100 ms) and away from the deliberative phenomenology of intention.

## Potential Article Angles

Given the cap-pressure framing and the absorption-over-proliferation discipline, **no new standalone article is proposed**. The research instead supports the following absorption pattern:

1. **Primary absorption — [inaccessible-past](/voids/inaccessible-past/)** as the prospective complement to the existing retrospective treatment. The proposal is to add an "encoding-side opacity" section to inaccessible-past treating the three faces (selection / boundary / consolidation) as a unitary structural limit, with the Henke subliminal-encoding work as the empirical edge-case showing encoding need not be conscious at all. This places the encoding void in the same article-family as the retrieval void it complements, preserving cap-headroom and improving inaccessible-past's coverage.
2. **Secondary absorption — [transit-void](/voids/transit-void/)** can name encoding as another transit consciousness cannot witness, alongside the existing transits (onset of mental act, between-state transition, attention-capture moment). 3. **Tertiary absorption — [thought-stream-void](/voids/thought-stream-void/)** can name the asymmetry between the arising of conscious thought (already covered) and the encoding of that arising for later recall (the new face).
4. **Tertiary absorption — [thrownness-void](/voids/thrownness-void/)** can name encoding-side throwness: we wake each morning with memories from yesterday's encoding that we did not deliberately encode; the prior encoder is a self we cannot reconstruct.

## Gaps in Research

- **Phenomenological evidence at encoding time** is thin. The Würzburg school's introspective method (c.1900–1915) attempted to access cognitive operations during their performance and largely failed; no comparable modern programme exists for encoding specifically. The void may be unbreakable in principle (per the witnessing-void argument structure), in which case the gap is constitutive, not contingent.
- **Conscious-versus-subliminal trajectory divergence (Schreiner et al. 2022)** is striking but based on a small literature; replication and parameter-space mapping are open.
- **Cross-architecture comparison**: whether AI systems with inspectable training-time updates can be said to *introspect* those updates in a sense relevant to the void, versus merely *expose* them by external design, is a definitional gap that should not be settled by quick analogy.
- **Whether the void admits any direct first-person probe** — perhaps via meditation traditions that train present-moment awareness (Vipassana, Mahamudra). Some practitioners report awareness of "moment-to-moment fading" of phenomena, which sits adjacent to event-boundary flushing. This is an honest gap; the Map should neither over-credit contemplative reports nor dismiss them, treating them as candidate void-probes subject to the convergence-as-evidence discipline.
- **Whether the encoding void is partly *self-maintained***. Per [self-maintained cognitive limits](/voids/self-maintained-cognitive-limits/): if exposing the encoding selection criteria would destabilise the autonoetic colouring of recall, motivated opacity at the encoding-introspection junction is possible. The 2022 Schreiner finding that *unconscious* episodic memory does *not* benefit from the conscious-encoding decay pattern is a candidate footprint of such a defended boundary — the conscious system pays a stability cost the unconscious system does not. Speculative; flagged as gap.
