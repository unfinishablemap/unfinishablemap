# Research: Phenomenology of Volitional Control — Libet Experiments and Conscious Causation Evidence

**Date**: 2026-03-20
**Search queries used**: Libet experiments conscious volitional control, Schurger 2012 readiness potential stochastic neural noise, conscious veto power Libet free won't, Alfred Mele Libet free will criticism, phenomenology agency ownership action Gallagher Zahavi, Brass Haggard intentional inhibition veto brain imaging, Daniel Wegner illusion conscious will, dualism conscious causation quantum mechanics interactionism, Fried Mukamel 2011 single neuron volition

## Executive Summary

The Libet experiments (1983) launched a still-active debate about whether consciousness initiates voluntary action or merely accompanies brain processes already underway. The readiness potential (RP) — a slow negative EEG shift beginning ~550ms before movement — precedes reported conscious intention by ~350ms, which was widely interpreted as evidence against conscious causation. However, the evidential force of this finding has been substantially weakened: Schurger et al. (2012) reinterpreted the RP as stochastic neural noise rather than unconscious decision-making, Mele's philosophical critiques showed the experiments don't address real-world deliberative choice, Brass and Haggard (2007–2008) identified neural correlates of conscious veto that support Libet's own "free won't" proposal, and phenomenological analyses (Gallagher, Zahavi) distinguish the sense of agency from mere ownership in ways the experiments cannot capture. The research landscape has shifted markedly away from the early triumphalist claims that Libet disproved free will, though deep questions remain about the temporal relationship between consciousness and neural preparation.

### Schurger, Sitt & Dehaene (2012) — An Accumulator Model for Spontaneous Neural Activity Prior to Self-Initiated Movement
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3479453/
- **Type**: Paper (PNAS)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposed that the readiness potential reflects ongoing stochastic fluctuations in neural activity, not an unconscious decision
  - A leaky stochastic accumulator model accounts for the specific shape of the RP and the distribution of waiting times
  - In spontaneous movement tasks, premotor activity fluctuates near threshold due to implicit imperative; random fluctuations determine threshold-crossing time
  - "Libetus interruptus" variant validated the model: interrupted trials confirmed spontaneous fluctuations were already near threshold
  - "This interpretation of the readiness potential thus takes the sting out of the Libet argument against free will"
- **Tenet alignment**: **Strongly aligns** with Bidirectional Interaction — removes the primary neuroscientific objection to conscious causation
- **Quote**: "This interpretation of the readiness potential thus takes the sting out of the Libet argument against free will, as the latter was based on interpreting the readiness potential as reflecting an unconscious decision to act."

### Fried, Mukamel & Kreiman (2011) — Internally Generated Preactivation of Single Neurons Predicts Volition
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3052770/
- **Type**: Paper (Neuron)
- **Key points**:
  - Recorded 1019 neurons during self-initiated finger movements in 12 subjects
  - Progressive neuronal recruitment in supplementary motor area (SMA) over ~1500ms before reported decision
  - Population of 256 SMA neurons predicted decision to move with >80% accuracy 700ms before awareness
  - Proposed computational model: volition emerges when internally generated firing rate crosses a threshold
- **Tenet alignment**: **Complex** — demonstrates neural preparation precedes reported awareness (challenging for conscious initiation), but the threshold-crossing model is compatible with consciousness modulating the threshold or selecting among competing activations
- **Quote**: "Volition emerges once a change in internally generated firing rate of neuronal assemblies crosses a threshold"

### Brass & Haggard (2007, 2008) — Intentional Inhibition and the What-When-Whether Model
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6870995/ (2007), https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1073858408317417 (2008)
- **Type**: Papers (Human Brain Mapping; The Neuroscientist)
- **Key points**:
  - Identified dorsal fronto-median cortex (dFMC) activation during voluntary inhibition of prepared actions
  - Proposed "what, when, whether" model of intentional action with distinct neural substrates
  - The "whether" component — deciding to act or not — has specific neural correlates in dFMC
  - dFMC exerts top-down inhibition of premotor areas, providing mechanism for conscious veto
- **Tenet alignment**: **Aligns** with Bidirectional Interaction — provides neural evidence for Libet's veto proposal; the "whether" decision is a plausible locus for conscious causal influence
- **Quote**: "dFMC allows intentional inhibition of action through top-down inhibition of premotor areas"

### Mele — Effective Intentions and Libet Critiques
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/MELLOF-2
- **Type**: Philosophical analysis (multiple publications)
- **Key points**:
  - Reinterpreted Libet's data: what subjects become aware of may be an urge to flex, not a decision already made
  - The experimental paradigm (spontaneous wrist flicking) bears little resemblance to genuine voluntary action
  - Mele does not dispute the data but challenges the interpretive leap from RP timing to denial of conscious causation
  - Ecological validity objection: real decisions involve deliberation over minutes, hours, or days — not impulsive wrist flexing
- **Tenet alignment**: **Strongly aligns** with Dualism and Bidirectional Interaction — shows that Libet experiments do not warrant epiphenomenalist conclusions
- **Quote**: "Nothing justifies the claim that what a subject becomes aware of is a decision that has already been made, as opposed to an urge to flex that has already arisen"

### Wegner (2002) — The Illusion of Conscious Will
- **URL**: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262731621/the-illusion-of-conscious-will/
- **Type**: Book (MIT Press)
- **Key points**:
  - Claims conscious will is always imaginary — a post-hoc interpretation, not a cause
  - Proposes "modular epiphenomenalism": the brain system producing the experience of will is distinct from the system producing action
  - Draws on Libet data, automatisms, hypnosis, ideomotor effects, and dissociative phenomena
  - Despite the illusion claim, acknowledges conscious will serves a role in self-understanding and morality
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with Dualism, Bidirectional Interaction — the strongest available philosophical case for conscious will as epiphenomenal
- **Quote**: "The experience of conscious will is the feeling that we are doing things... the feeling may not be a true reading of what is happening"

### Nahmias (2002) — When Consciousness Matters (critique of Wegner)
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/rec/NAHWCM
- **Type**: Philosophical review (Philosophical Psychology)
- **Key points**:
  - Wegner's evidence is "inconclusive" for the strong illusionist claim
  - Wegner has not shown that conscious will plays no causal role in planning, intention formation, and action
  - The illusion claim may be "theoretically incoherent" — rooted in a category mistake presupposing neuronal activity causes conscious will
- **Tenet alignment**: **Aligns** — defends efficacy of consciousness against the strongest epiphenomenalist challenge

### Gallagher & Zahavi — The Phenomenological Mind
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/GALTPM-4
- **Type**: Book (Routledge, multiple editions)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguish sense of ownership ("my body is moving") from sense of agency ("I am initiating the action")
  - In normal voluntary action, ownership and agency coincide; in involuntary action, they dissociate
  - Ownership can persist without agency, suggesting ownership is more fundamental
  - Further distinguish motor-level agency (SAm) from intentional/goal-level agency (SAi)
  - Phenomenology provides resources for cognitive science that neuroscience alone cannot capture
- **Tenet alignment**: **Aligns** with Dualism — the phenomenological distinction between agency and ownership shows first-person structure that resists reductive explanation; the multi-level agency model is compatible with consciousness operating at the intentional level while neural systems handle motor preparation

## Major Positions

### Epiphenomenalism / Illusionism about Conscious Will
- **Proponents**: Daniel Wegner, some interpretations of Libet's data, Sam Harris
- **Core claim**: Conscious experience of willing is a post-hoc construction. The brain initiates actions, and consciousness confabulates authorship after the fact.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The readiness potential precedes conscious awareness of intention by ~350ms
  - Brain activity can predict decisions up to 10 seconds in advance (Soon et al. 2008)
  - Automatisms, alien hand syndrome, hypnotic action, and ideomotor effects show action without experienced will
  - Wegner's "modular epiphenomenalism" — separate brain systems for action and for the feeling of willing
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts** with Dualism and Bidirectional Interaction. This is the primary opponent position. The Map's response: the RP is noise (Schurger), the experiments don't test real decisions (Mele), and the reportability argument against epiphenomenalism applies here — if conscious will were illusory, our reports about willing would be causally disconnected from actual willing.

### Libertarian Agent Causation (the Map's position)
- **Proponents**: Timothy O'Connor, Robert Kane, Roderick Chisholm (classically); the Map's framework
- **Core claim**: Conscious agents are genuine causes of their actions, irreducible to event-causal chains. Consciousness causally contributes to action through mechanisms that exploit quantum indeterminacy.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Phenomenological reality of agency (Gallagher's sense of agency)
  - The reportability argument: discussing consciousness requires it to have causal power
  - Schurger's reinterpretation removes the main neuroscientific objection
  - Quantum mechanisms (Stapp, Penrose-Hameroff) provide plausible physical interface
  - Brass & Haggard's "whether" component shows neural substrates for conscious veto
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Direct alignment** with all five tenets

### Compatibilist Accounts
- **Proponents**: Daniel Dennett, John Martin Fischer, Michael Smith
- **Core claim**: Free will is compatible with determinism. What matters is not metaphysical libertarian freedom but whether actions flow from the agent's own deliberative processes.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Libet's experiments are irrelevant to real free will — they test only impulsive movements
  - Free will requires reasons-responsiveness, not contra-causal power
  - The phenomenology of agency is real but doesn't require non-physical causation
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Partially conflicts** — accepts conscious efficacy (aligning with Bidirectional Interaction) but typically assumes physicalism (conflicting with Dualism and Minimal Quantum Interaction)

### Libet's Own Position: "Free Won't"
- **Proponents**: Benjamin Libet
- **Core claim**: Consciousness does not initiate actions but retains veto power. The brain generates impulses unconsciously; consciousness can cancel them in the ~150ms window before movement.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Subjects could veto planned actions in the 100-200ms window before the pre-set time
  - Veto activates distinct brain regions from action initiation
  - Free will operates as conscious selection among unconsciously generated options
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Partially aligns** — preserves conscious causal power (Bidirectional Interaction) but limits it to inhibition rather than initiation. The Map may argue for a stronger role: consciousness selects among neural options, not merely vetoes.

## Key Debates

### Is the Readiness Potential Evidence of Unconscious Decision?
- **Sides**: Traditional interpretation (RP = unconscious decision) vs. Schurger et al. (RP = stochastic noise accumulation)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the RP reflects goal-directed preparation or random neural fluctuations reaching a threshold
- **Current state**: Shifting toward Schurger's interpretation. A 2021 meta-analysis found the evidence for RP-precedes-intention is "uncertain and based on only a small number of studies." The field increasingly treats Libet's original conclusion as overstated.

### Does Conscious Veto Require Its Own Unconscious Initiation?
- **Sides**: Critics argue the veto itself must be unconsciously initiated (infinite regress); defenders argue the veto is a distinct process
- **Core disagreement**: Whether conscious inhibition faces the same timing problem as conscious initiation
- **Current state**: Brass & Haggard's neuroimaging identified distinct neural substrates for the "whether" decision, suggesting it is not simply another motor initiation process. The regress objection remains philosophically live but lacks empirical support.

### Is Conscious Will an Illusion? (Wegner vs. critics)
- **Sides**: Wegner (always illusory) vs. Nahmias, Mele, and others (genuine causal role)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the evidence from automatisms and timing studies generalizes to all voluntary action
- **Current state**: Wegner's strong illusionism is widely regarded as overreach. His evidence demonstrates that the feeling of willing can be miscalibrated, but this does not entail it is never accurate. The consensus has moved toward a more nuanced view: conscious will is sometimes confabulated but often causally efficacious.

### Does Phenomenology Provide Evidence for Conscious Causation?
- **Sides**: Phenomenologists (first-person experience of agency is evidentially significant) vs. eliminativists (introspection is unreliable)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the felt distinction between voluntary and involuntary action carries evidential weight
- **Current state**: Gallagher and Zahavi's multi-level account of agency (motor-level vs. intentional-level) has gained traction. The dissociability of agency and ownership in clinical populations (schizophrenia, anarchic hand) provides additional evidence that the sense of agency tracks real causal structures rather than being a simple confabulation.
