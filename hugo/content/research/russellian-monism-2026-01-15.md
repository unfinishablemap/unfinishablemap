## Executive Summary

Russellian monism is an increasingly prominent theory of mind arguing that physics describes only the relational/dispositional structure of matter, leaving its intrinsic nature—quiddities—unexplained. These quiddities, the theory proposes, account for consciousness. The view attempts a middle path: avoiding dualism's interaction problem (by integrating consciousness into physical causation) while avoiding physicalism's hard problem (by denying that structure alone determines consciousness). Key variants include panpsychist Russellian monism (quiddities are phenomenal properties) and panprotopsychist Russellian monism (quiddities are non-phenomenal but constitute consciousness). The combination problem—how micro-level properties constitute macro-level consciousness—remains the principal objection. For the project's framework, Russellian monism shares key insights (physics is silent on intrinsic nature) but its monist commitment may conflict with the project's interactionist dualism; the combination problem and certain structural features make it arguably no better than dualism despite claiming to avoid it.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy - Russellian Monism
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/russellian-monism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia entry
- **Key points**:
  - Three core claims: structuralism about physics, realism about quiddities, quidditism about consciousness
  - Physics describes "spatiotemporal structure and dynamics" but remains "silent" about what fundamentally grounds that structure
  - Aims to combine virtues of physicalism and dualism while avoiding drawbacks of both
  - Major objection: combination problem (how quiddities combine to constitute macro-consciousness)
- **Tenet alignment**: Shares key insight with site—physics doesn't capture everything—but monist framework conflicts with Dualism tenet; combination problem mirrors issues site avoids with interactionist dualism
- **Quote**: "By contrast, Russellian monism seems to avoid those costs of both traditional physicalism and traditional dualism. On Russellian monism, the properties that constitute consciousness—quiddities and (perhaps) structural properties—play indispensable roles in physical causation."

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy - Panpsychism
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/panpsychism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia entry
- **Key points**:
  - Russellian monism "increasingly being seen as one of the most promising ways forward on the problem of consciousness"
  - Two forms: panpsychist (quiddities are phenomenal) and panprotopsychist (quiddities are non-phenomenal but ground phenomenality)
  - Historical figures: Russell, Eddington. Contemporary: Strawson, Chalmers, Goff
- **Tenet alignment**: Compatible with Dualism tenet's claim that consciousness is irreducible, but monist framework differs from site's interactionist dualism

### Galen Strawson - "Realistic Monism: Why Physicalism Entails Panpsychism"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/STRRMW
- **Type**: Philosophical paper (Journal of Consciousness Studies 2006)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues true physicalism (taking consciousness seriously as physical) entails panpsychism
  - Rejects "proto-experience" as a way to avoid panpsychism—either it is experience or we face "magic passage across the experiential/non-experiential divide"
  - Questions why we posit non-experiential concrete reality with "zero evidence"
  - Critics note Strawson's view may be "substance monism together with property dualism"—dualism in disguise
- **Tenet alignment**: Strawson's insistence on taking experience seriously aligns with Dualism tenet; but his monism conflicts with interactionist framework
- **Quote**: "For there to seem to be rich phenomenology or experience just is for there to be such phenomenology or experience."

### David Chalmers - "Panpsychism and Panprotopsychism"
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/panpsychism.pdf
- **Type**: Philosophical paper
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes panpsychism (micro-phenomenal quiddities) from panprotopsychism (non-phenomenal quiddities that ground phenomenality)
  - Argues Russellian monism responds to conceivability argument by denying physics captures all physical truths
  - Poses "revenge problem" for panprotopsychism: protophenomenal zombies may be conceivable
  - Tentatively favorable: "If we can find a reasonable solution to the combination problem...this view would immediately become the most promising solution to the mind-body problem"
- **Tenet alignment**: Chalmers's analysis aligns with site's Occam's Razor Has Limits tenet—incomplete knowledge justifies ontological caution
- **Quote**: "The conceivability argument refutes narrow physicalism but is compatible with broad physicalism."

### Philip Goff - "Consciousness and Fundamental Reality" (2017)
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/consciousness-and-fundamental-reality-9780190677015
- **Type**: Philosophical monograph
- **Key points**:
  - First half argues physicalism cannot account for consciousness; second half defends Russellian monism
  - Develops "phenomenal bonding hypothesis" as solution to combination problem (criticized as epistemically opaque)
  - Ultimately defends cosmopsychist form: all facts grounded in facts about the conscious universe
  - Argues against "constitutive" Russellian monism in favor of versions where consciousness is more fundamental
- **Tenet alignment**: Goff's anti-physicalism aligns with Dualism tenet; cosmopsychism is more radical than site's position but shares commitment to consciousness as fundamental

### Arthur Eddington - "The Nature of the Physical World" (1928)
- **URL**: https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/1231664-the-mind-stuff-of-the-world-is-of-course-something-more
- **Type**: Historical scientific-philosophical work
- **Key points**:
  - "The stuff of the world is mind-stuff"—though Eddington clarified he didn't mean mind or stuff in ordinary senses
  - Physics reveals only structure, not intrinsic nature
  - "Mind is the first and most direct thing in our experience, and all else is remote inference"
  - Some classify as panpsychism, others as neutral monism influenced by Russell
- **Tenet alignment**: Eddington's insight that physics reveals structure only aligns with site's framework; his idealism goes beyond site's dualism
- **Quote**: "It is difficult for the matter-of-fact physicist to accept the view that the substratum of everything is of mental character. But no one can deny that mind is the first and most direct thing in our experience."

### Adam Pautz - "A Dilemma for Russellian Monists About Consciousness"
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/archive/PAUCRM
- **Type**: Philosophical paper
- **Key points**:
  - Argues Russellian monism requires brute "grounding laws" connecting quiddities to consciousness
  - These laws are "no better than the brute psychophysical laws of dualism"
  - Conclusion: Russellian monism claims to avoid dualism's costs but actually inherits them
- **Tenet alignment**: This criticism supports the project's preference for explicit interactionist dualism over disguised dualism
- **Quote**: "Though called a physicalist view, it seems to have many of the vices of dualism. Where dualism requires psychophysical laws, this view requires exactly parallel special, brute 'grounding laws.'"

## Major Positions

### Russellian Panpsychism
- **Proponents**: Galen Strawson, Philip Goff (earlier work), Gregg Rosenberg
- **Core claim**: The quiddities (intrinsic properties) underlying physical structure are themselves phenomenal—experience is fundamental and ubiquitous
- **Key arguments**:
  - Physics only describes structure; intrinsic nature must be something, and consciousness is the only intrinsic nature we know directly
  - Avoids emergence by making consciousness fundamental rather than derivative
  - More parsimonious than positing two fundamentally different kinds of property
- **Relation to site tenets**: Agrees with Dualism that consciousness is irreducible, but makes it too ubiquitous—site's framework requires consciousness to be special, not everywhere. Combination problem severe.

### Russellian Panprotopsychism
- **Proponents**: David Chalmers (tentatively), Sam Coleman
- **Core claim**: Quiddities are non-phenomenal "protophenomenal" properties that jointly constitute phenomenal properties when appropriately organized
- **Key arguments**:
  - Avoids attributing experience to electrons while keeping consciousness grounded in fundamental reality
  - Responds to conceivability argument by positing properties physics doesn't capture
- **Relation to site tenets**: Faces "revenge problem"—protophenomenal zombies may be conceivable, so the view may not escape the hard problem. Site's explicit dualism is cleaner.

### Neutral Monism (Related View)
- **Proponents**: William James, Bertrand Russell (some periods), Ernst Mach
- **Core claim**: Reality is fundamentally neither mental nor physical; mind and matter are different arrangements of neutral elements
- **Key arguments**:
  - Dissolves mind-body problem by rejecting the distinction at fundamental level
  - Both mental and physical are constructed from same basic stuff
- **Relation to site tenets**: Incompatible with site's Dualism tenet, which maintains a genuine distinction. Neutral monism also struggles with subjective character of experience.

## Key Debates

### Russellian Monism vs. Standard Physicalism
- **Sides**: Russellian monists argue physics is incomplete; standard physicalists argue structure is sufficient
- **Core disagreement**: Whether quiddities exist and whether they're relevant to consciousness
- **Current state**: Growing acceptance that physics describes structure only; debate shifts to whether quiddities help with consciousness
- **Site position**: Site agrees physics is incomplete but prefers interactionist dualism to monist solutions

### Russellian Monism vs. Dualism
- **Sides**: Russellian monists claim to avoid interaction problem; dualists argue Russellian monism inherits dualism's costs covertly
- **Core disagreement**: Whether integrating quiddities into physical causation genuinely differs from positing psychophysical laws
- **Current state**: Critics like Pautz argue the "grounding laws" of Russellian monism are just psychophysical laws under another name
- **Site position**: Site's explicit interactionist dualism is preferable to disguised dualism

### The Combination Problem
- **Sides**: Panpsychists must explain how micro-experiences combine; critics argue no satisfactory solution exists
- **Core disagreement**: Whether phenomenal bonding, cosmopsychism, or other solutions work
- **Current state**: Chalmers calls this "the hard problem of panpsychism." Goff moved from constitutive to cosmopsychist views to avoid it. No consensus solution.
- **Site position**: Site's substance dualism avoids the problem by positing unified mental subjects rather than combinations of micro-subjects

### Panpsychism vs. Panprotopsychism
- **Sides**: Panpsychists say quiddities are phenomenal; panprotopsychists say they're not but ground phenomenality
- **Core disagreement**: Whether non-phenomenal properties can intelligibly constitute phenomenal properties
- **Current state**: Strawson argues panprotopsychism faces Descartes's problem of how non-experiential becomes experiential. Chalmers argues panprotopsychism faces "revenge" conceivability arguments.
- **Site position**: Both face problems the project's explicit dualism avoids
