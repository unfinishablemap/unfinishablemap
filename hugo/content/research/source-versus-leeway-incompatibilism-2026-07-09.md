## Executive Summary

"Source vs leeway incompatibilism" is the organizing distinction that carves the incompatibilist half of the free-will debate into two families. **Leeway incompatibilism** holds that free will and responsibility require *alternative possibilities* — the ability to do otherwise (the Principle of Alternate Possibilities, PAP) — so determinism threatens freedom by closing off the alternatives. **Source incompatibilism** holds that what matters is being the *ultimate source or origin* of one's action — an actual-sequence property — so determinism threatens responsibility even if Frankfurt cases succeed in showing PAP is false. The taxonomy is shared conceptual machinery of the whole debate (compatibilists split the same way): it is a distinction about *what free will requires*, and by itself carries no commitment to dualism, determinism, or any particular metaphysics. Two historical pressures moved much of the field from leeway toward source: Frankfurt's 1969 counterexamples to PAP, and Pereboom-style manipulation arguments that turn on causal history rather than on foreclosed alternatives. The Map's libertarian interface is best framed as *one* position locatable within this taxonomy, not as evidence for the taxonomy or for dualism.

## The Core Distinction

- **Leeway incompatibilism**: an agent acts freely / is responsible only if she has "the right kind of alternatives to her actual course of action" — the ability to do otherwise, "could have done otherwise," genuine open options ("garden of forking paths"). Determinism removes these alternatives, hence removes free will. This is the *alternative-sequences* or *alternative-possibilities* condition.
- **Source incompatibilism**: an agent acts freely / is responsible only if she is the *ultimate source* (origin, author) of her action — the action is "up to her" in the sense that its causal springs trace to her and not to factors ultimately beyond her control. This is an *actual-sequence* condition: it looks at how the action actually came about, not at what alternatives were available. Determinism is incompatible with ultimate sourcehood because it makes every action the consequence of prior causes stretching back before the agent existed.

Key framing point (from SEP *Arguments for Incompatibilism*): the two are not simply rivals — they can be interconnected. Lacking the ability to do otherwise *could* undermine the self-determining control sourcehood requires, so some theorists argue a kind of leeway is in any case required for sourcehood (Timpe's "wide" source incompatibilism, below). The distinction is thus a spectrum of emphasis, not a strict partition.

## Key Sources (web-verified at publisher of record)

### SEP: "Arguments for Incompatibilism" (Kadri Vihvelin et al.)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/incompatibilism-arguments/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (verified live)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes source incompatibilism ("we act freely and are morally responsible only if we are the ultimate source of our actions") from leeway incompatibilism (free will requires "a genuine choice about what actions we perform").
  - Consequence Argument (van Inwagen): "If determinism is true, then our acts are the consequence of laws of nature and events in the remote past. But it's not up to us what went on before we were born, and neither is it up to us what the laws of nature are." Notes van Inwagen later conceded the original Rule Beta formulation invalid and proposed repairs; Lewis's reductio reply.
  - Frankfurt cases challenge PAP; most compatibilists persuaded, most incompatibilists not — "flickers of freedom" reply (agent retains morally relevant alternatives despite the counterfactual intervener).
  - Pereboom's manipulation/Zygote argument runs from the manipulated agent's unfreedom to the determined agent's unfreedom.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (shared-ground exposition).

### SEP: "Compatibilism" (Michael McKenna & D. Justin Coates)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/compatibilism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (verified live)
- **Key points**: Frames the same leeway/source split on the compatibilist side; "State of the Art" supplement treats Pereboom's Four-Case argument as "a kind of litmus test for the credibility of a compatibilist theory." Demonstrates the taxonomy is cross-cutting (both compatibilists and incompatibilists come in leeway and source varieties).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral.

### Timpe, *Free Will: Sourcehood and Its Alternatives*
- **Citation**: Kevin Timpe, *Free Will: Sourcehood and Its Alternatives*, Continuum Studies in Philosophy, London/New York: Continuum, 2008 (ISBN 9780826496256); 2nd edn, Bloomsbury, 2013.
- **Type**: Book (verified: Continuum/Bloomsbury; NDPR review live)
- **Key points**:
  - Names and systematizes the leeway/sourcehood distinction; argues for a version of source incompatibilism.
  - Distinguishes **narrow** source incompatibilism (sourcehood alone, no alternative-possibilities requirement) from **wide** source incompatibilism (ultimate sourcehood *itself requires* morally relevant alternative possibilities). Wide source incompatibilism is Timpe's own position — a bridge showing source and leeway need not be exclusive.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral on dualism; libertarian-friendly framing.
- **Note**: A closely related PhilPapers record, Timpe, "Leeway vs. Sourcehood Conceptions of Free Will" (PhilPapers rec TIMLVS), gives the distinction its now-standard label. Cite the book as the primary; treat the shorter piece as a label source only (full pagination not independently verified here — do not fabricate).

### Fischer & Ravizza, *Responsibility and Control*
- **Citation**: John Martin Fischer & Mark Ravizza, *Responsibility and Control: A Theory of Moral Responsibility*, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1998.
- **Type**: Book (verified via CUP / Google Books)
- **Key points**:
  - The paradigm **actual-sequence** account. Responsibility requires **guidance control**, not **regulative control** (the dual power to do otherwise).
  - Guidance control = the action issues from the agent's *own*, appropriately **reasons-responsive** mechanism. Reasons-responsiveness is analyzed counterfactually (how the mechanism *would* respond across scenarios) but the responsibility-grounding fact is a property of the *actual* mechanism.
  - Fischer's **semicompatibilism**: moral responsibility is compatible with determinism (it needs only guidance control) even if freedom-to-do-otherwise is not. Fischer is officially agnostic on determinism/incompatibilism, but his actual-sequence framing is the template every source theorist uses. Source incompatibilism = an actual-sequence view that *adds* the claim that the actual sequence must not be deterministic.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; the compatibilist actual-sequence sibling of source incompatibilism.

### Pereboom, *Living Without Free Will*
- **Citation**: Derk Pereboom, *Living Without Free Will*, Cambridge Studies in Philosophy, Cambridge University Press, 2001 (ISBN 9780521791984).
- **Type**: Book (verified via CUP)
- **Key points**:
  - Home of the **Four-Case Manipulation Argument**. Case 1: a neuroscientist creates and manipulates the agent moment-to-moment; Cases 2–3 progressively relax the manipulation; Case 4 is ordinary causal determinism. Intuition: no responsibility in Case 1; no *relevant* difference across the series; therefore no responsibility under ordinary determinism.
  - The argument turns on **causal history / sourcehood**, not on alternatives: what defeats responsibility is that "his action results from a deterministic causal process that traces back to factors beyond his control." This is the canonical **source-incompatibilist** argument.
  - Pereboom is a **hard incompatibilist** (denies we have the responsibility-grounding free will whether or not determinism holds), not a libertarian — important not to conflate source incompatibilism with libertarianism.
- **Tenet alignment**: Source-style reasoning aligns structurally with the Map's emphasis on origination; Pereboom's *skeptical conclusion* conflicts with the Map's libertarianism.

### Pereboom, *Free Will, Agency, and Meaning in Life*
- **Citation**: Derk Pereboom, *Free Will, Agency, and Meaning in Life*, Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2014 (219 pp.).
- **Type**: Book (verified via OUP / NDPR)
- **Key points**: Refines the manipulation argument and the "basic desert" conception of responsibility that source incompatibilists target; the sense of "up to the agent" at stake is the desert-grounding one.
- **Tenet alignment**: as above.

### McKenna & Pereboom, *Free Will: A Contemporary Introduction*
- **Citation**: Michael McKenna & Derk Pereboom, *Free Will: A Contemporary Introduction*, Routledge Contemporary Introductions to Philosophy, New York: Routledge, 2016 (15 March 2016; 330 pp.; ISBN 9780415996877).
- **Type**: Book / textbook (verified via Routledge / Harvard Book Store)
- **Key points**: Standard textbook exposition of the leeway vs source models, co-authored by a compatibilist (McKenna) and an incompatibilist (Pereboom); good neutral source for the taxonomy's canonical statement.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral.

## Major Positions

### Leeway Incompatibilism
- **Proponents / associated**: Carl Ginet, Peter van Inwagen (the Consequence Argument is classically a leeway argument), Robert Kane in part (though Kane's "ultimate responsibility" adds a source element).
- **Core claim**: Free will requires the ability to do otherwise (PAP); determinism removes alternatives; therefore incompatible.
- **Key argument**: The **Consequence Argument** — cross-ref topic the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism. If determinism holds, our acts are consequences of the remote past + laws, neither of which is "up to us," so our acts are not up to us. This is leeway-flavored: it concludes we *cannot do otherwise*.
- **Chief threat**: Frankfurt cases (below), which aim to sever responsibility from alternative possibilities.

### Source Incompatibilism
- **Proponents / associated**: Derk Pereboom (hard incompatibilist), Kevin Timpe (wide source incompatibilist, libertarian), Eleonore Stump, Linda Zagzebski; Robert Kane's "ultimate responsibility" is a source condition even though Kane also wants indeterminist leeway.
- **Core claim**: Free will/responsibility requires being the *ultimate source* of one's action; determinism defeats ultimate sourcehood (the causal buck never stops with the agent). Holds *even if* PAP is false.
- **Key arguments**: The **Manipulation Argument** — cross-ref topic the-manipulation-argument-and-hard-incompatibilism — Pereboom's Four-Case / Mele's Zygote (Ernie). A determined agent is relevantly like a covertly manipulated agent; if manipulation defeats responsibility, so does determinism, and it does so by poisoning the *causal history*, not by removing alternatives.
- **Internal split (Timpe)**: *narrow* (sourcehood only) vs *wide* (sourcehood requires alternative possibilities too — reconciles with leeway).

### Semicompatibilism (the compatibilist actual-sequence sibling)
- **Proponents**: John Martin Fischer (with Ravizza).
- **Core claim**: Responsibility needs only *guidance control* (an actual-sequence, reasons-responsive property), which is compatible with determinism; freedom-to-do-otherwise may not be, but responsibility does not need it.
- **Relation**: Shows the source/actual-sequence framing is *neutral* between compatibilism and incompatibilism. Source incompatibilism is what you get by taking Fischer's actual-sequence emphasis and adding an anti-deterministic sourcehood requirement.

## Key Debates

### Did Frankfurt cases really move the field from leeway to source?
- **Sides**: Frankfurt (PAP is false; a counterfactual intervener shows responsibility without alternatives) vs "flicker of freedom" defenders (Widerker, Kane, Ginet: a residual alternative or "sign" remains, so PAP survives) and Fischer (even if a flicker remains it is not robust enough to ground responsibility — so drop PAP but keep an actual-sequence account).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether responsibility supervenes on the actual sequence alone.
- **Current state**: Ongoing, but the widely reported effect is a *shift of gravity* toward source/actual-sequence framings among both compatibilists and incompatibilists. Cross-ref topic frankfurt-cases-and-the-principle-of-alternate-possibilities.

### Is source incompatibilism really distinct from leeway?
- **Sides**: "Genuinely distinct" (Pereboom: causal-history defeaters need no reference to alternatives) vs "sourcehood covertly needs leeway" (Timpe's wide view; some argue ultimate origination is unintelligible without a branching point).
- **Current state**: Live; "wide source incompatibilism" is the main reconciling position.

### Does the manipulation argument beg the question?
- **Sides**: Pereboom (soft-line/hard-line manipulation cases) vs compatibilist "soft-line" responders (Mele, others: a principled difference between manipulation and determinism) and "hard-line" responders (bite the bullet: the manipulated agent *is* responsible).
- **Current state**: The Four-Case argument is "a litmus test" for compatibilist theories; unresolved.

## Calibration Notes for the Downstream Article (read before writing)

- **Do NOT lead with dualism or treat the taxonomy as Map-specific.** Source vs leeway is neutral, shared machinery of the *entire* free-will debate; it does not require or support dualism. Present it that way first, then locate the Map inside it. (This is the expand-topic-lead-overclaims-phenomenal-absence failure mode: leading with the Map's stake as if it were the point.)
- **Do not conflate source incompatibilism with libertarianism.** Pereboom is a *source incompatibilist who denies free will* (hard incompatibilist). Source incompatibilism is a claim about *what free will would require*, not a claim that we have it. Libertarians and hard incompatibilists share the source premise and disagree on whether it is satisfied.
