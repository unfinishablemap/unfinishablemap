## Executive Summary

Teleosemantics (also called biosemantics, after Millikan's coinage) is the leading *positive*
naturalistic theory of mental content. Where crude causal/informational theories fix content by
covariation ("state S means P because S reliably co-occurs with P"), teleosemantics adds
**biological proper function fixed by selection history**: a state means P because the mechanisms
that produce or consume it were *selected for* their role relative to P. This extra ingredient is
what lets the theory generate genuine *misrepresentation* and *correctness conditions* — the
normative dimension a bare covariation story cannot supply — and so it is the naturalist's
strongest attempt to cross from mere correlation to genuine *aboutness*. The major variants divide
on **where** function does the semantic work: **output/consumer-side** (Millikan, Papineau — content
is what a downstream consumer was selected to use the sign *for*) versus **input/producer-side**
(Dretske, Neander — content is the distal cause the sensory system was selected to be *responsive
to*). The theory faces a canonical battery of objections: the **disjunction problem** (which it is
designed to solve), the **frog/toad indeterminacy** cases (Neander vs Fodor), **Swampman**
(Davidson), the **indeterminacy of function** (Pietroski's kimu, Kingsbury), and the deeper
Hutto–Myin worry that proper function still cannot deliver *intensional* content. This note is the
positive rival's fair hearing; the Map's dissent (Tenet 1) is summarised at the end and left for the
article to develop, not pre-loaded here.

## Key Sources

### Teleological Theories of Mental Content (SEP)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/content-teleological/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (authoritative; entry authored by Peter Schulte & Karen Neander)
- **Key points**:
  - Teleosemantics attempts to solve *Brentano's problem*: explaining how mental states can be
    about things.
  - Proponents favour an **etiological theory of function**: a trait's function is what past
    tokens of that type were *selected for*.
  - Millikan's two core claims: (1) all representations are messages from a **producer** to one or
    more **consumers**; (2) representations must map onto (usually external) world-states by a
    **mapping rule** so the consumer can perform its proper functions "in the Normal way" — the way
    ancestral consumers succeeded.
  - Solving the disjunction problem: the crude causal theory says a belief caused by both cats and
    small foxes means *cat-or-small-fox*; teleosemantics blocks this by making content depend on
    *function*, so tokening in the absence of the selected-for condition is genuine **error**.
  - **Input vs output** split: Dretske/Neander tie content to what the system was selected to
    *detect / be caused by* (producer-side); Millikan/Papineau tie it to what a *consumer* was
    selected to *use the sign for* (output-side).
- **Tenet alignment**: The whole programme is a *rival* to Tenet 1 (dualism / irreducibility of the
  mental) — it is the most credible naturalist attempt to reduce aboutness to physical-biological
  facts. Steelman first; dissent second.

### A Mark of the Mental: In Defense of Informational Teleosemantics — Karen Neander (2017)
- **URL**: https://direct.mit.edu/books/monograph/3551/ (MIT Press, publisher of record)
- **Type**: Book (MIT Press, 2017). **Verified title/subtitle**: "In Defense of Informational
  Teleosemantics" (note: NOT "A Defence of" — correct the record).
- **Key points**:
  - Defends **informational teleosemantics** for sensory-perceptual representation: sensory
    systems have the function to produce inner-state changes that are both **caused by** and
    **analogs of** (second-order-similar to) their referents.
  - Three elements — **functions, causal-information relations, and second-order similarity** —
    are argued to complement rather than conflict.
  - Producer-side account: content is fixed by the distal *cause* the system was selected to
    respond to (Neander's **distality principle**: the system responds to proximal features
    *because* this enabled detecting distal food/prey, establishing an asymmetry).
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongest producer-side rival; the sophistication (analog/second-order
  similarity machinery) is exactly what must be steelmanned before the Map locates its dissent.

### Language, Thought, and Other Biological Categories — Ruth Millikan (1984)
- **URL**: https://mitpress.mit.edu/ (MIT Press). **Verified**: MIT Press 1984.
- **Type**: Book. The founding text of biosemantics; introduces **proper function** and the
  producer/consumer framework.

### White Queen Psychology and Other Essays for Alice — Ruth Millikan (1993)
- **URL**: https://direct.mit.edu/books/monograph/4750/ (MIT Press, Bradford Books). **Verified**:
  contains the essay **"Biosemantics"** (Ch. 4; the essay originally appeared in *Journal of
  Philosophy* 86(6), 1989, pp. 281–297).
- **Type**: Essay collection. The accessible statement of biosemantics; consumer-side emphasis.

### Explaining Behavior: Reasons in a World of Causes — Fred Dretske (1988)
- **URL**: https://direct.mit.edu/ (MIT Press). **Verified**: MIT Press 1988.
- **Type**: Book. The **indicator-function** account: an internal state acquires the *function of
  indicating* a condition when a consumer system is recruited (via learning or selection) to use it
  in guiding behaviour. Content = what the state has the function to indicate.

### Naturalizing the Mind — Fred Dretske (1995)
- **URL**: https://direct.mit.edu/books/monograph/4689/ (MIT Press, Jean Nicod Lectures).
  **Verified**: MIT Press 1995.
- **Type**: Book. Extends indicator-function representationalism to *phenomenal* qualia (the
  qualia = represented external properties thesis), giving a fully naturalistic representationalism
  of consciousness. Directly relevant to the Map because it is a naturalist attempt to reduce
  *experience* as well as content.

### Philosophical Naturalism — David Papineau (1993)
- **URL**: https://www.davidpapineau.co.uk/ ; Blackwell. **Verified**: Blackwell 1993.
- **Type**: Book. Output/consumer-side teleosemantics tied to **success semantics**: a desire's
  satisfaction condition is the effect it is the desire's biological purpose (selection history) to
  produce; a belief is true when it would, combined with desires, tend to produce success. (The
  reported gloss "that effect which it is the desire's biological purpose to produce," *Philosophical
  Naturalism* pp. 58–59, is paraphrased from secondary sources — verbatim wording NOT confirmed
  against the primary text; the article should treat it as a paraphrase or verify at source before
  quoting.)

### The Status of Teleosemantics, or How to Stop Worrying about Swampman — David Papineau
- **URL**: https://www.uh.edu/~garson/Papineau.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (open PDF). Papineau's mature Swampman reply: teleosemantics is a scientific
  *reduction of the actual-world* property of belief, so a merely possible Swampman is not a
  counterexample.

## Major Positions

### Millikan's Biosemantics (consumer/output-side)
- **Proponents**: Ruth Millikan.
- **Core claim**: A representation's content is fixed by what the **consumer** system downstream of
  it was selected to do — the world-condition onto which the sign must map for the consumer to
  perform its proper function "the Normal way." Producers matter for representational *status* but
  (on the standard reading) content is consumer-determined. (Note the live interpretive debate:
  Millikan herself resists the pure "consumer theory" label and later stresses complementary
  producer/consumer roles; some commentators posit "two Millikans" — a consumer theory in
  *LTOBC* and a hybrid theory in later work. Synthese 2025, "Millikan vs. Millikan," is the
  current locus.)
- **Key arguments**: Proper function is etiological (selection history), so it is a *natural,
  scientifically respectable* relation; yet it is *normative* — a state can fail to map correctly,
  which is misrepresentation. This is the crossing from covariation to aboutness.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The strongest reductive rival to Tenet 1. Its whole appeal is that
  it derives normativity (correctness/error) from mind-independent selection facts.

### Informational Teleosemantics (producer/input-side)
- **Proponents**: Karen Neander (paradigm); Fred Dretske (indicator-function ancestor).
- **Core claim**: Content is the distal condition the sensory-perceptual system was **selected to
  be caused by / to detect**. Adds causal-information + second-order similarity to function.
- **Key arguments**: The distality principle breaks ties between candidate contents (proximal
  retinal features vs distal prey) by asking what the system was selected to *track* — it responds
  to proximal features *for the sake of* detecting distal ones.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Rival to Tenet 1; its advantage is a clean answer to the frog case
  (below) without appeal to downstream benefit.

### Dretske's Indicator-Function Account
- **Proponents**: Fred Dretske (*Explaining Behavior* 1988; *Naturalizing the Mind* 1995).
- **Core claim**: A state means *that p* when it has acquired the **function of indicating p** —
  where indication is Dretske-style natural information (nomic dependence) and function comes from a
  consumer being recruited to *use* the indicator in behaviour control. In *Naturalizing the Mind*
  this is extended to qualia (phenomenal character = represented external properties).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The most ambitious — it targets both Tenet-1 content *and* the
  phenomenal, aiming to naturalise experience itself. The Map's explanatory-gap reasoning bears
  directly on the 1995 extension.

### Papineau's Success-Semantic Teleosemantics
- **Proponents**: David Papineau (*Philosophical Naturalism* 1993).
- **Core claim**: Output-side; truth conditions of beliefs and satisfaction conditions of desires
  are fixed by the biological purposes (selection history) of the behaviour-guiding states —
  success semantics grounded in teleology.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Rival to Tenet 1; notable for Papineau's later, unusually candid
  concession-and-reframe on Swampman.

## Key Debates

### The Disjunction Problem
- **Sides**: Fodor (poses it) vs teleosemanticists (claim to solve it).
- **Core disagreement**: A purely causal theory cannot say a state means *cat* rather than
  *cat-or-whatever-else-causes-it* (small fox on a dark night), so it cannot distinguish
  misrepresentation from correct representation of a disjunction.
- **Current state**: Teleosemantics's *raison d'être* — function specifies the selected-for
  condition, so off-condition tokenings are error, not part of a disjunctive content. Widely
  regarded as teleosemantics's strongest card; the debate has moved on to *which* condition
  function picks out (see indeterminacy).

### The Frog/Toad Indeterminacy (fly vs frog-food vs small-dark-moving-thing)
- **Sides**: Neander vs Fodor.
- **Core disagreement**: The frog's prey-detector was selected because it (a) responded to
  small-dark-moving-things, (b) enabled prey-catching, (c) yielded nutrition. So does the inner
  state represent *fly*, *frog food*, or *small dark moving thing*? Fodor: selection is silent
  between locally co-instantiated candidates, so content is indeterminate — teleosemantics fails.
  Neander: content is what the system was selected to be **causally sensitive** to; frog visual
  systems cannot be causally sensitive to *nutritional value* or to *Diptera membership*, only to
  visible motion/shape — so content is "small, dark, moving," determinately.
- **Current state**: Live; the flagship test case. Recent work (e.g. Biology & Philosophy 2024,
  "Frogs recognize prey: a causal-behavioral teleosemantics") continues to litigate it.

### Swampman
- **Sides**: Davidson (1987, poser) vs teleosemanticists.
- **Core disagreement**: A lightning-struck molecular duplicate of a person, with **no selection
  history**, has no proper functions — so teleosemantics entails Swampman has no contentful states,
  which many find intuitively false.
- **Reply strategies**:
  1. **Bite the bullet / deny the intuition** (Millikan 1996, Neander 1996/2006): "intentionality"
     rigidly designates a historical-selectional kind (cf. water = H₂O); the Swampman intuition is
     mistaken, like a pre-scientific intuition that water needn't be H₂O.
  2. **Actual-world reduction** (Papineau 2001/2006): teleosemantics reduces the *actual* property
     of belief; a merely-possible Swampman with content is no counterexample to an actual-world
     identity claim. (Papineau reports being moved off pure bullet-biting by the ethical worry that
     denying Swamppeople minds would license killing them.)
- **Current state**: Ongoing; recent papers (Synthese 2020 "why the Swampman is still worrisome";
  Synthese 2022 "kind essences") argue the modal/explanatory bite remains. The Map's interest: the
  intuition that a physical duplicate *would* think is exactly an anti-reductive intuition.

### Indeterminacy of Function (Pietroski's kimu; Kingsbury)
- **Sides**: Pietroski (1992), and indeterminacy critics generally, vs benefit-based
  teleosemantics.
- **Core disagreement**: Pietroski's **kimu** — colour-blind creatures whose brain-state B, tied to
  a wavelength, spread because it led them up-hill away from dawn predators (snorf), not because it
  tracked *red*. Benefit-based (consumer/output) teleosemantics seems forced to say B means
  *snorf-free space*, though intuitively B represents *red*. This targets specifically
  *benefit*-based versions; producer/input theories (Neander) that tie content to the *cause* avoid
  it. Kingsbury-style objections press the more general worry that selection is for *complexes* of
  co-implicated properties (Neander's own antelope-hemoglobin example: oxygen uptake AND higher
  pasture AND nutrition AND predator avoidance were all selected together), so function underdetermines
  content.
- **Millikan's response (2000)**: distinguish *represented* from *causing* properties — a state can
  represent an invisible property (nutrition, snorf-absence) caused by visible features.
- **Alternatives**: Martínez 2013 (content = natural-kind / homeostatic property cluster); Agar 1993
  (conjunctive content); Bergman 2021/2023 (accept a benign, vagueness-like indeterminacy that does
  not generalise to human propositional attitudes).
- **Current state**: Live; the sharpest internal pressure on output-side variants and a reason
  informational/producer teleosemantics has gained ground.

### The Hard-Problem-of-Content Objection (the Map's ally — cross-reference, do not duplicate)
- **Sides**: Hutto & Myin vs all teleosemantics.
- **Core disagreement**: Even granting proper function, the account reads content off causal +
  historical facts that are themselves *silent about meaning*; it can specify *when* a consumer
  should use a state but not the **guise/intension** under which the state represents (representing
  *as food* vs *as nutritionally-optimal-nearby-item*). Mann & Pain (2022) concede intensionality
  but argue it is a personal-level desideratum, not a requirement on sub-personal states.
- **Current state**: This is already the Map's `hard-problem-of-content` and
  `naturalisation-failure-for-content` material. The new page should **link** to it as the negative
  argument, not restate it.
