## Executive Summary

Epistemicism is the theory of vagueness on which vague predicates **do** draw sharp boundaries — there is a precise last hair before "bald," a precise last second of "noon" — but we cannot **know** where those boundaries fall. Timothy Williamson (*Vagueness*, 1994) and Roy Sorensen (*Blindspots*, 1988; *Vagueness and Contradiction*, 2001) are its principal defenders. Applied to the phenomenal-sorites problem, epistemicism says: string a continuum from a clearly conscious human to a clearly non-conscious object, and there **is** a determinate last conscious member of the series — a sharp on/off fact — but its location is hidden from us in principle. The reason is not squeamishness about metaphysics but a positive theory of knowledge: Williamson's **margin-for-error principle** plus the **safety condition** on knowledge together entail that a belief about the exact boundary, even if true, could not count as knowledge, because a marginally different case would have made the same belief false, so the belief would be true only by luck.

For the Map, epistemicism is the **most natural ally** on the phenomenal-sorites. It is the one locus of the ontic/semantic/epistemic trichotomy that keeps a determinate subject-fact — exactly what Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds; indexical identity matters) needs — while conceding what the vagueness camp is right about: we cannot point to the cutoff. This is a *motivation*, not a proof: the Map is drawn to epistemicism because of what it protects, and that pull must be stated as a stake rather than a verdict. The note also develops the central objection the Map must answer — the **truth-maker / fact-maker problem**: Williamson grounds ordinary sharp boundaries in facts about *use and meaning*, but a phenomenal boundary has no obvious use-facts to fix it, so epistemicism-about-consciousness owes an account of *what makes* the sharp fact true. This is where the treatment couples to the phenomenal-sorites resolution: the non-physical coupling's mere engagement can supply the on/off truth-maker that use-facts cannot.

## Key Sources

### Williamson, *Vagueness* (1994)
- **URL**: https://www.routledge.com/ (Routledge monograph; widely reviewed, e.g. Sainsbury's BJPS review at https://www.marksainsbury.net/uploads/9/7/2/9/97295048/1995williamsonvagueness.pdf)
- **Type**: Book — Routledge, 1994
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (author Timothy Williamson, title *Vagueness*, Routledge 1994 confirmed across multiple independent reviews and the SEP "Vagueness" and "Sorites Paradox" entries, which cite it as the canonical statement of epistemicism)
- **Key points**:
  - Vague predicates have **sharp boundaries** whose locations are unknowable; bivalence and classical logic are preserved.
  - The ignorance is explained by **margin-for-error principles**: roughly, if you know that an *n*-unit case is Φ, then an (*n*+1)-unit case must also be Φ, else your belief in the boundary would be right only by luck. Knowledge requires a margin; a belief hugging the exact cutoff has no margin.
  - Williamson locates the sharp boundary in an unknowable function of speakers' total dispositions of use — meaning supervenes on use, but the supervenience function is too complex and inaccessible for us to compute the threshold.
  - Distinctive claim: what makes vagueness a source of inexactness is that its margin-for-error principles "advert to small differences in *meaning*, not to small differences in the objects."
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong structural ally of Tenet 4 — preserves a determinate fact where the location is epistemically inaccessible. But the *grounding* story (use/meaning-facts) does not transfer cleanly to phenomenal facts (see fact-maker objection).

### Sorensen, *Blindspots* (1988)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/SORB
- **Type**: Book — Clarendon Press / Oxford University Press, 1988
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (author Roy A. Sorensen, title *Blindspots*, Clarendon Press Oxford 1988 confirmed; PhilPapers record SORB and Pagin's review PAGROR)
- **Key points**:
  - Early independent statement of epistemicism: vague ("blurry") predicates draw sharp divisions we cannot know; the sorites is treated as a paradox about the *word*.
  - Introduces "absolute borderline cases" where ignorance of the boundary is insurmountable.
- **Tenet alignment**: Same as Williamson — determinate fact, hidden location.

### Sorensen, *Vagueness and Contradiction* (2001)
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/vagueness-and-contradiction-9780199241309
- **Type**: Book — Oxford University Press, 2001 (ISBN 9780199241309)
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (author, title, publisher, year, ISBN confirmed at OUP; Weatherson review at brian.weatherson.org/Sorensen.html; NDPR review)
- **Key points**:
  - "Vagueness abounds in the way the world is divided; the divisions are sharp; yet we often do not know where they are."
  - Chapter 11 develops **truthmaker gaps**: indeterminate truths are indeterminate because *nothing in the world grounds them* — a proposition can have a truth-value without a truth-maker. This is **truthmaker-gap epistemicism**: the sharp fact is true, but *ungrounded*.
- **Tenet alignment**: Double-edged for the Map. Truthmaker-gap epistemicism would let the Map keep the sharp consciousness-fact *without* owing a truth-maker — but the Map arguably does NOT want an ungrounded phenomenal fact; it wants the non-physical coupling to be the truth-maker. So the Map is closer to a *grounded* epistemicism than to Sorensen's ungrounded version. Worth flagging as the fork within epistemicism.

### Jago, "The Problem with Truthmaker-Gap Epistemicism" (2012)
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/tht3.49 (open preprint: https://philarchive.org/archive/JAGTPW)
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Thought: A Journal of Philosophy*, 2012; DOI 10.1002/tht3.49
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (author Mark Jago, title, journal *Thought*, year 2012, DOI confirmed via Wiley listing and PhilArchive record JAGTPW; volume/issue not separately confirmed and deliberately omitted rather than fabricated)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that truthmaker-gap epistemicism (the Sorensen-descended view that indeterminate truths lack truth-makers yet keep a truth-value) is unstable: if truth-makers do the metaphysical work of fixing truth, an ungrounded-but-true proposition is problematic.
  - Useful *against* the ungrounded version and therefore *for* the Map's preferred grounded version: it shows the Map is right to insist on a truth-maker rather than resting on a gap.
- **Tenet alignment**: Instrumentally aligned — supports the Map's move to *ground* the sharp fact (in the coupling) rather than leave it ungrounded.

### SEP, "Sorites Paradox" — §3.2 The Epistemic Theory
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sorites-paradox/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy; substantively revised 2025-08-26)
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (genuine SEP entry; §3.2 confirmed by direct fetch)
- **Key points**:
  - "Vague terms have sharp boundaries whose locations are hidden from us." Proponents: Williamson, Sorensen, Delia Graff Fara, with antecedents in Chrysippus.
  - Margin-for-error principles stated as: if x and y differ incrementally on a decisive dimension and x is known to be Φ, then y is Φ — knowledge only in clear cases.
  - States the standard objection candidly: epistemicism "multiplies mysteries" by positing unknowable sharp boundaries fixed by an unknowable function of unknowable use-patterns; critics call it a "just so story"; defenders answer that it alone preserves classical logic and bivalence.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral reference; supplies the balanced framing (the Map should steelman the "just so story" charge, not hide it).

### SEP, "Vagueness" — epistemic theory section
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/vagueness/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (SEP)
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (genuine SEP entry confirmed)
- **Key points**:
  - "If nothing steps in to make a proposition true, then it is false" — the supervenience-of-meaning-on-use picture Williamson relies on; boundaries are set by the environment/use "by default rather than by decision."
  - Williamson's childhood-threshold example of margin-for-error: knowing an *n*-day-old is a child forces the (*n*+1)-day-old to be a child too, else the belief is right by luck.
  - Epistemicists are "methodologically committed to a partisan role for logic" — they solve the sorites with classical logic and bivalence intact.
- **Tenet alignment**: The "meaning supervenes on use" grounding is exactly what does NOT transfer to phenomenal facts — the pivot for the fact-maker objection.

## Major Positions

### Epistemicism (Williamson / Sorensen)
- **Proponents**: Williamson (1994), Sorensen (1988, 2001); antecedents in Chrysippus; Delia Graff Fara a further defender.
- **Core claim**: Vague predicates have sharp, bivalent boundaries; we cannot know their location.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. **Margin-for-error**: knowledge of Φ-status requires a margin of safety; a belief pinned to the exact cutoff lacks one, so cannot be knowledge even when true.
  2. **Safety condition**: a true belief is knowledge only if it could not easily have been false; boundary-beliefs could easily have been false (a marginally different case flips them), so they fail safety.
  3. **Preserves classical logic**: bivalence and excluded middle survive; no third truth-value, no truth-value gaps (except, for Sorensen, truthmaker gaps).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map's natural ally. Preserves the determinate subject-fact Tenet 4 requires while conceding the boundary is unfindable. Frame as *motivation* — the Map is drawn to epistemicism by what it protects, not because the epistemic theory has been proven against ontic or semantic rivals.

### Epistemicism applied to consciousness (the Map's construction)
- **Core claim**: In any conscious-to-non-conscious series there is a determinate last conscious member — a sharp on/off subject-fact — whose location is unknowable.
- **Status**: Not a position the literature defends *for consciousness by name* (confirmed gap; SEP and the sibling note both treat the phenomenal application as under-developed). The article should present it explicitly as the Map's own extension of Williamson/Sorensen, motivated by Tenet 4, not attribute it to an existing consciousness-epistemicist.
- **Two internal forks**:
  - **Grounded** (Map-preferred): the sharp fact has a truth-maker — the non-physical coupling's engagement — so it is not ungrounded.
  - **Ungrounded** (Sorensen truthmaker-gap): the sharp fact is true but has no truth-maker. The Map should note this and *decline* it (Jago's objection bites; and an ungrounded phenomenal fact is doubly mysterious).

### Ontic vagueness (Schwitzgebel, Hall, Tye) — the opponent
- Removes the determinate fact. Tenet-4-threatening. Epistemicism is precisely the reply that keeps the fact and relocates the indeterminacy into knowledge.

### Semantic vagueness (Papineau) — the near-rival to distinguish from
- Keeps a determinate physical reality but denies a determinate *phenomenal* fact (concepts fail to refer uniquely). Epistemicism differs sharply: the phenomenal fact IS determinate; only our access fails. The article must keep these apart — both say "no answer we can give," but only semantic vagueness denies there is an answer.

## Key Debates

### Is epistemicism about consciousness even coherent — can a *phenomenal* fact be unknowable?
- **The objection**: Phenomenal facts are paradigmatically self-intimating ("there is something it's like"). An *unknowable* phenomenal fact — whether a borderline system has any experience at all — sits uneasily with the first-person transparency of consciousness. Simon's Positive-Characterization argument sharpens this: if every borderline case must be positively characterizable and phenomenal borderline cases cannot be, then perhaps there are no borderline cases to be *ignorant of* either.
- **The Map's reply to develop**: The unknowability is *third-personal* (whether *another* system, or a system far down the series, is conscious), not a claim that a subject is ignorant of *its own* present experience. The self-intimation of one's own consciousness does not extend to knowing the boundary in an evolutionary or neuron-removal series. So the epistemicism is about the *location of the boundary across systems*, not about opacity of a subject to itself — this defuses much of the "odd unknowable phenomenal fact" worry.

### What grounds the sharp phenomenal fact? (the fact-maker / truth-maker objection — the central one)
- **The problem**: Williamson's ordinary sharp boundaries are grounded in an (unknowable) function of *use and meaning* — social-linguistic facts. A phenomenal on/off boundary has no such use-facts to fix it: whether a system *is conscious* is not settled by how we use the word "conscious." So epistemicism-about-consciousness owes a truth-maker the ordinary theory got from use.
- **Three responses**:
  1. **Sorensen's truthmaker-gap route**: there is no truth-maker; the fact is just true. Jago (2012) argues this is unstable, and the Map has independent reason to dislike an ungrounded phenomenal fact. *Decline.*
  2. **Physical-realizer grounding**: ground the boundary in a graded physical property (Φ, neural complexity). Blocked by **Antony's conditional** — sharpness-plus-correctness refutes complex-property theories, and a graded realizer would reintroduce the sorites at its own switch-on. *Blocked.*
  3. **Coupling-engagement grounding** (Map-preferred, links to the phenomenal-sorites resolution): the truth-maker is the *simple, non-graded engagement of the non-physical mind–brain coupling* — whether the coupling engages *at all*. This is not a sorites series (it is a relation that either holds or does not), so it supplies a sharp on/off truth-maker without a graded physical cutoff. Epistemicism-about-consciousness pairs naturally with this: the *fact* is fixed by the coupling; its *location in the physical series* is unknowable because we cannot observe where the coupling engages.
- **Residual open problem**: if the coupling's engagement is itself *triggered* by a graded physical variable, the sorites re-enters at the trigger's switch-on point. The article should concede this honestly (as the sorites article does) rather than claim closure.

### Epistemicism vs. its "just so story" charge
- SEP states the standard complaint: epistemicism multiplies mysteries (unknowable boundary, unknowable function, unknowable use). For consciousness the charge is *sharper* because the use-facts grounding is unavailable — which is exactly why the Map needs the coupling truth-maker to answer it. The article should carry this objection, not bury it.

## Map's Stake and Tenet Analysis

- **Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds / indexical identity matters)** is the load-bearing stake. Indexical identity presupposes a determinate fact about *whether there is an experiencer* and *whose* an experience is. Of the three loci, only epistemicism keeps that fact intact: ontic vagueness deletes it, semantic vagueness deletes the *phenomenal* fact while keeping the physical one. So epistemicism is the Map's single most natural ally on the phenomenal-sorites — it lets the Map concede the entire *epistemic* case the vagueness camp makes ("you cannot find the boundary") while denying the *metaphysical* conclusion ("therefore there is no boundary"). Frame framework-relative: the Map is *motivated toward* epistemicism because of what it protects, and this motivation is a reason to look hard at the theory, not a demonstration that Williamson beats Schwitzgebel.
- **Tenet 1 (Dualism)** does the constructive work. Antony's conditional bars grounding the sharp fact in a complex/graded physical property; interactionist dualism supplies an alternative truth-maker — the simple engagement of the non-physical coupling — that is not itself a sorites series. Epistemicism-about-consciousness is thus a *dualist* epistemicism: the fact is fixed by a non-physical relation, its location hidden because the relation is unobservable, so the physical continuity the vagueness camp points to never transmits to the phenomenal fact.
- **Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor has limits)** licenses resisting the "any sharp line in nature is arbitrary, hence unparsimonious" intuition that drives Schwitzgebel toward option (d). Once the boundary is fixed by a non-physical coupling rather than a physical realizer, a sharp line need not be an arbitrary physical cutoff — the parsimony intuition is exactly the kind of simplicity-appeal Tenet 5 warns against over-trusting.
- **Honest cost**: epistemicism buys Tenet 4 a determinate subject-fact at the price of a permanent unknowability, and it inherits the "just so story" and "odd unknowable phenomenal fact" charges. The article must pay these openly. The Map's distinctive contribution is not epistemicism itself but the *grounding* of its sharp fact in the coupling — which answers the fact-maker objection that generic epistemicism-about-consciousness cannot.
