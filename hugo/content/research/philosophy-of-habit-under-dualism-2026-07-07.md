## Executive Summary

Habit is the philosophical hinge between conscious activity and automatic process. Across a tradition running from Aristotle's *hexis* through Ravaisson, Bergson, Dewey, Merleau-Ponty, and Peirce, habit is characterized not as mere mechanism but as the *acquisition* of a "second nature" — a settled disposition that begins in conscious effort and sediments into unreflective execution, leaving a physical trace that persists after attention withdraws. Contemporary cognitive science (Graybiel; Wood & Rünger; Daw et al.) operationalizes this as cue-triggered, outcome-insensitive behavior implemented in basal-ganglia circuits, dissociable from goal-directed control. For the Map, habit is the clearest everyday instance of the interface's **delegation function**: the boundary at which the interactionist-dualist interface hands control to procedural systems, and — crucially — can re-engage when novelty or salience demands. This connects most directly to **Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)**: conscious training leaves durable structural residue in the physical substrate that keeps operating unattended, which is the bidirectional interface writing to the world and reading its own inscriptions back. It also touches **Tenet 1 (Dualism)**: the philosophical tradition consistently resists reducing habit to pure mechanism, holding that habit *descends from* activity (Ravaisson's law), preserving an asymmetry between spontaneous consciousness and settled disposition.

**Scope note (avoid duplication):** The Map already holds a rich skill cluster — [skill-delegation](/concepts/skill-delegation/), [consciousness-and-skill-acquisition](/topics/consciousness-and-skill-acquisition/), [delegatory-causation](/concepts/delegatory-causation/), [delegatory-dualism](/topics/delegatory-dualism/), [attention-as-interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/). This research targets **habit-as-such**, not skill. The distinction to preserve in any article: *skill* is a competence (a graded capacity to do a task well); *habit* is a disposition to initiate a specific response to a specific cue, often independent of whether the response still serves a goal. Skill answers "how well can you?"; habit answers "what will you do, unprompted, here?" Habit's philosophically distinctive features — outcome-insensitivity, cue-triggering, the ethical ambivalence of "second nature," and Ravaisson's double law — are absent from the skill articles and are the article's white space.

## Key Sources

### Félix Ravaisson, *Of Habit* (*De l'habitude*, 1838)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Ravaisson-Mollien ; English trans. Clare Carlisle & Mark Sinclair, Continuum, 2008 (https://www.bloomsbury.com / Amazon 9781847061980)
- **Type**: Book (foundational essay)
- **Key points**:
  - Habit is a "metaphysics" that steers between materialism and idealism — habit is neither pure mechanism nor pure freedom but the passage between them.
  - **The double law of habit**: with repetition, *receptivity/sensation* diminishes (we feel a repeated stimulus less) while *activity/movement* becomes easier and more spontaneous (we execute it more readily). Effort fades; facility grows.
  - Habit is where activity descends into nature — "habit is a second nature." Ravaisson reads this descent as continuous, not a break: the will's spontaneity does not vanish but becomes an obscure, sedimented tendency in the body.
  - Influenced Bergson, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, Deleuze; Proust responded to it.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly compatible with **Tenet 1** — Ravaisson refuses the reduction of habit to mechanism, treating the automatic as a *lowered* form of the same activity, not a different kind of thing. The "descent" model is a natural gloss on the Map's delegation: consciousness does not disappear from a habituated act; its contribution attenuates.
- **Quote (Aristotelian root Ravaisson develops)**: habit is "a second nature" that grows within the first.

### John Dewey, *Human Nature and Conduct* (1922)
- **URL**: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/41386/41386-h/41386-h.htm (full text); https://brocku.ca/MeadProject/Dewey/Dewey_1922/
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Habit is the organizing category of conduct: "an understanding of habit and of different types of habit is the key to social psychology."
  - Habits are **transactional** — like breathing and digestion, they require "the cooperation of organism and environment"; a habit is not stored inside the agent but is a functional relation between agent and world.
  - Habits are *projective and dynamic*, not passive repetition: they are "will," "demands for certain kinds of activity," and they constitute the self. Impulse supplies novelty; habit supplies organization; intelligence reconstructs habit when it fails.
  - Distinguishes routine (rigid) habit from **intelligent/flexible** habit that remains open to re-adjustment — anticipating the Map's "re-engagement" point.
- **Tenet alignment**: Compatible with **Tenet 3** — Dewey's insistence that habit lives in the organism-environment loop (not sealed in the head) is a pragmatist statement of the interface writing into the physical world and being conditioned by it. His "intelligence reconstructs habit" is the re-engagement of active control at the boundary of the automatic.

### Maurice Merleau-Ponty, *Phenomenology of Perception* (*Phénoménologie de la perception*, 1945)
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/merleaupontys-phenomenology-of-perception/
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Habit is acquired by the **body schema**, a pre-conscious, implicit map of bodily capacity — "to have a habit is to have a world" ready-to-hand.
  - Habit is not intellectual knowledge nor mechanical reflex but a **motor intentionality**: the body "understands" and "aims at" the world. Consciousness is primarily "I can," not "I think."
  - The organist who masters a new organ, the blind man's cane that becomes an extension of the body — habit *dilates our being in the world* by incorporating instruments into the schema (direct bridge to Malafouris).
  - The **Schneider case** (brain-injured soldier) shows what is lost when motor intentionality is damaged: concrete habitual action survives while abstract/novel movement fails — a natural experiment separating the automatic from the deliberate.
- **Tenet alignment**: Compatible with **Tenet 1 & 3**. Motor intentionality is a form of directedness that is neither reflective consciousness nor brute mechanism — a middle term the Map can read as the delegated interface operating "beneath" active attention while remaining a form of aiming, not blind causation.

### Lambros Malafouris, *How Things Shape the Mind: A Theory of Material Engagement* (MIT Press, 2013)
- **URL**: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262528924/how-things-shape-the-mind/
- **Type**: Book (Material Engagement Theory)
- **Key points**:
  - Cognition arises **through** interaction with materials, tools, and environments — not solely in the brain. Things become cognitive extensions of the body (the potter's wheel and clay, the knapper's stone).
  - Skill and habit are enacted at the **brain-body-thing** boundary; the material scaffold is constitutive of the cognitive act, not a mere external prop.
  - "Thinging" / the potter example: the potter does not fully represent the pot in advance and impose it; the form emerges in the ongoing, habituated engagement of hand, wheel, and clay.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)** — the strongest anchor of the note. Malafouris makes vivid that habit's traces are not only neural but *material*: the worn tool, the shaped clay, the grooved path are physical residues of past conscious engagement that then constrain and enable future action. This is the interface writing into durable matter and reading it back — habit as a loop through the world, not just through the brain. Use MET to make the "physical traces that persist after conscious attention withdraws" claim concrete without over-relying on neuroscience.

### Ann M. Graybiel, "Habits, Rituals, and the Evaluative Brain," *Annual Review of Neuroscience* 31 (2008): 359–387
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18558860/
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed review
- **Key points**:
  - Habits are structured, chunked action sequences (motor *or* cognitive) elicited by a context/cue; they become "relatively automatic and unconscious."
  - **Chunking**: the striatum/basal ganglia bind sub-actions into a single unit run as a whole ("action chunking"); task-bracketing activity marks the start and end of a learned routine.
  - Habits form via experience-dependent plasticity in basal-ganglia circuits and can outlast the goals that created them.
  - (Companion: A. M. Graybiel & S. T. Grafton, "The Striatum: Where Skills and Habits Meet," *Cold Spring Harb Perspect Biol* 7(8), 2015 — explicitly locates the skill/habit interface.)
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/empirical; supplies the **physical-trace** mechanism (**Tenet 3**). Use carefully — the article must not let the neural story *become* the metaphysics (that would concede Tenet 1). Frame: the basal-ganglia circuit is where the delegated procedure runs; it is the substrate written by conscious calibration, not the origin of the calibrating selection.

### Wendy Wood & Dennis Rünger, "Psychology of Habit," *Annual Review of Psychology* 67 (2016): 289–314
- **URL**: https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-psych-122414-033417 ; PDF: https://dornsife.usc.edu/wendy-wood/wp-content/uploads/sites/183/2023/10/wood.runger.2016.pdf
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed review
- **Key points**:
  - Habits form as people repeat responses in a stable context to pursue goals; over time the **cue** (not the goal) comes to trigger the response.
  - Habits are **outcome-insensitive**: a cue activates a single, well-learned response even when it conflicts with current intentions — the defining marker separating habit from goal-directed action.
  - Habit and deliberate goal pursuit operate **synergistically**; habit is the efficient default, overridden only when motivation *and* opportunity for deliberate control are present.
- **Tenet alignment**: Empirical; the "override only with motivation and means" finding is a near-perfect operationalization of the Map's **re-engagement** claim — the interface re-takes control when a novelty/salience threshold is crossed.

### Henri Bergson, *Matter and Memory* (*Matière et mémoire*, 1896)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_and_Memory
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Two irreducible kinds of memory: **habit-memory** (motor, embodied, built by repetition, oriented to present action, "inscribed within the body") and **pure/recollection memory** (representational, virtual, "spiritual," free, preserving the past *as* past).
  - Habit-memory "acts" the past rather than representing it; it is utilitarian and automatic. Confusing the two produces philosophical error.
- **Tenet alignment**: Compatible with **Tenet 1** — Bergson explicitly keeps a non-material, non-bodily memory alongside the embodied habit-memory, resisting reduction. The Map can adopt the *distinction* (bodily automatic vs. free/attentive) while being cautious about Bergson's fuller metaphysics of duration.

### Charles Sanders Peirce, "The Law of Mind" (*The Monist*, 1892) and related cosmological papers
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/peirce/
- **Type**: Journal essays / SEP overview
- **Key points**:
  - Habit is the master concept unifying Peirce's system: belief *is* a habit of action; laws of nature are cosmic habits.
  - **Tychism** (absolute chance) plus **habit-taking**: the universe evolves from chance toward regularity as mind's "tendency to take habits" hardens spontaneity into law.
  - "Habituescence" — occasional non-conformity is essential to habit-taking; a habit that could never be broken is not a living habit.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Handle with care.** Peirce's habit-taking cosmology is genuinely interesting for **Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)** — a universe where regularity crystallizes out of chance resonates loosely with a picture in which consciousness biases otherwise-chancy quantum outcomes. But Peirce's panpsychist/objective-idealist framing ("matter is effete mind") is *not* the Map's dualism. Cite Peirce for the idea that habit is the general form of settled-tendency-out-of-spontaneity; flag the metaphysical divergence explicitly rather than borrowing his monism.

### Clare Carlisle, *On Habit* (Routledge, "Thinking in Action," 2014)
- **URL**: https://www.routledge.com/ ; ISBN 978-0415619141
- **Type**: Book (contemporary synthesis; Carlisle is Ravaisson's English translator)
- **Key points**:
  - Habit involves both **receptivity and resistance to change** as basic principles of habit-formation — habit makes us plastic *and* rigid.
  - The philosophy of habit "anticipates the discoveries of recent neuroscience" and carries **ethical** significance (Aristotle: excellence is a habit; Hume: habit is "the great guide of life"; Proust: "if habit is a second nature, it prevents us from knowing our first").
  - Contrasts Spinoza vs. Hume on whether habitual thinking is reliable knowledge.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/framing; the best single secondary source for structuring the article's history section and its ethical dimension.

### Nathaniel Daw, Samuel Gershman, Ben Seymour, Peter Dayan & Raymond Dolan, "Model-Based Influences on Humans' Choices and Striatal Prediction Errors," *Neuron* 69(6) (2011): 1204–1215
- **URL**: https://www.princeton.edu/~ndaw/dsd12.pdf (related overview)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed empirical paper
- **Key points**:
  - Formalizes the habit/goal split as **model-free** (habitual; caches past action values, cheap, inflexible) vs. **model-based** (goal-directed; simulates consequences using a world-model, costly, flexible) reinforcement learning.
  - Human behavior blends both, and the two are neurally dissociable; arbitration between them shifts with cognitive load, stress, and training.
- **Tenet alignment**: Empirical; gives a computational vocabulary for delegation (model-free = delegated procedure) vs. re-engagement (model-based = interface resumes deliberation). Use to sharpen the *cost* of re-engagement — deliberation is expensive, which is *why* the interface delegates.

## Major Positions

### 1. Habit as "Second Nature" / Descent from Activity (Aristotle → Ravaisson)
- **Proponents**: Aristotle (*hexis*), Ravaisson, Carlisle.
- **Core claim**: Habit is acquired disposition that begins in conscious/voluntary activity and sinks into spontaneous, unreflective execution — a *second nature* grown within the first. Ravaisson's double law: sensation dims, action eases.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map's delegation function is essentially Ravaisson's "descent" in interface terms — conscious selection does not vanish but attenuates into an automatic procedure. Preserves **Tenet 1**'s asymmetry: the automatic is downstream of, and answerable to, the active.

### 2. Habit as Organism-Environment Transaction (Dewey / pragmatism)
- **Proponents**: Dewey; Peirce (belief-as-habit); Malafouris (materialized).
- **Core claim**: Habit is not stored inside the agent but is a functional loop through the environment; intelligent habit stays revisable.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Tenet 3** — habit as a loop the interface runs *through the world*, depositing physical traces (tools, paths, worn grooves) that condition future action.

### 3. Habit as Motor Intentionality (phenomenology)
- **Proponents**: Merleau-Ponty; Bergson (habit-memory).
- **Core claim**: Habit is a bodily, pre-reflective directedness — "I can" — distinct from both reflex and representation.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A middle term between conscious aiming and blind mechanism; models the delegated interface as still a *form of directedness*, not mere causation. Compatible with **Tenet 1**.

### 4. Habit as Cue-Triggered, Outcome-Insensitive Behavior (cognitive science)
- **Proponents**: Graybiel; Wood & Rünger; Daw et al.
- **Core claim**: Habit = context-cued, chunked, model-free response, dissociable from goal-directed control, running in basal-ganglia circuits, overridable only with motivation + means.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Supplies the physical-trace mechanism (**Tenet 3**) and an empirical signature of re-engagement (**Tenet 3/attention interface**). Must be framed as *substrate of the delegated procedure*, not the metaphysical ground of selection (guard **Tenet 1**).

### 5. Habit as Cosmic Tendency (Peirce)
- **Proponents**: Peirce.
- **Core claim**: Habit-taking is the general form by which spontaneity/chance settles into regularity, at every scale from mind to cosmos.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Suggestive for **Tenet 2** (chance settling toward law), but Peirce's monism diverges from Map dualism — cite the *form* of the idea, disown the metaphysics.

## Key Debates

### Is habit mechanical or a "lowered" form of activity?
- **Sides**: Reductive/mechanistic reading (habit is automatism, full stop) vs. the Ravaisson–Merleau-Ponty tradition (habit is spontaneity sedimented, retaining a trace of the activity that formed it).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the automatic is a *different kind* of process or the *same* process with attention withdrawn.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; the Map sides with the "lowered activity / delegation" reading — decisively relevant to Tenet 1.

### Does habit free us or imprison us?
- **Sides**: Habit as enabling (Aristotle's virtue, Hume's "guide of life," frees attention for novelty) vs. habit as ossifying (Proust's "second nature prevents us knowing our first"; addiction as pathological habit).
- **Core disagreement**: The ethical valence of delegation. Carlisle: habit is *both* receptivity and resistance to change.
- **Current state**: Live; gives the article an ethical/existential dimension the skill cluster lacks.

### One system or two?
- **Sides**: Dual-system (habit vs. goal-directed; model-free vs. model-based) vs. single-process accounts that treat the distinction as a continuum or artifact.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether habitual and deliberate control are architecturally distinct.
- **Current state**: Empirically active; the Map's delegation model presupposes a real (if graded) boundary that the interface can cross in both directions.

## Potential Article Angles

Target section: **topics/** or **concepts/** (habit-as-such). Suggested title: *"The Philosophy of Habit"* or *"Habit and the Delegation Function."*

1. **Habit as the interface's delegation function (primary angle).** Frame habit as the everyday boundary where conscious selection hands control to procedural systems and can re-engage for novelty. Spine: Ravaisson's descent → Merleau-Ponty's motor intentionality → Wood & Rünger's override conditions → the Map's interface re-engagement. Explicitly *contrast with* [skill-delegation](/concepts/skill-delegation/): skill = competence; habit = cue-triggered disposition, possibly outcome-insensitive. **Tenet 3** as the load-bearing connection (physical traces persisting after attention withdraws), with **Tenet 1** securing the non-reductive reading. Cross-link but do not duplicate [skill-delegation](/concepts/skill-delegation/), [delegatory-causation](/concepts/delegatory-causation/), [attention-as-interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/), [consciousness-and-skill-acquisition](/topics/consciousness-and-skill-acquisition/).

2. **The material trace of consciousness (Tenet 3 deep-dive).** Use Malafouris + Dewey to argue that habit inscribes conscious engagement into *durable matter* (tools, worn paths, shaped clay) and neural circuits alike — the interface writing to the world and reading it back. This is the freshest, least-covered material the harvested review flagged, and the cleanest Tenet-3 story.

3. **The ethics of the automatic.** Carlisle + Aristotle + Proust: delegation is double-edged — it frees attention for novelty (the interface's efficiency) but risks ossification and addiction (pathological habit where re-engagement fails). Connects to the Map's responsibility cluster ([responsibility-gradient-from-attentional-capacity](/topics/responsibility-gradient-from-attentional-capacity/)).

4. **When re-engagement fails (pathology angle).** Addiction, compulsion, and the Schneider case as boundary phenomena where the interface *cannot* resume control, or where only the habitual survives. Illuminates the delegation model by its breakdowns.

When writing, follow `obsidian/project/writing-style.md`: front-load the delegation-function thesis; use named-anchor summaries for forward references; include background only where framed for the Map's dualist reading (skip generic habit-101 that LLMs already have); include an explicit "Relation to Site Perspective" section keyed to Tenets 3 and 1; avoid the "not X, it is Y" construct and gratuitous "load-bearing."
