# Research: Voids - The Cartography Problem

**Date**: 2026-02-10
**Search queries used**: "epistemology cognitive limits can we know own cognitive boundaries philosophy", "Gödel incompleteness cognitive science self-knowledge limits self-enumeration", "metacognitive limits paradox mapping blind spots philosophy", "negative theology apophatic epistemology completeness of unknowing", "Kant transcendental philosophy cognitive system enumerate limitations", "Nicholas of Cusa learned ignorance completeness negative knowledge", "cognitive closure McGinn unknowable consciousness complete catalog", "second-order ignorance meta-ignorance what we don't know we don't know philosophy", "Wittgenstein limits language limits world Tractatus self-delineating boundaries", "Borges On Exactitude in Science map territory self-referential paradox", "Kit Fine ignorance of ignorance Synthese formal epistemology", "Rescher epistemology of ignorance unknowability completeness", "philosophy of ignorance taxonomy structured ignorance epistemology"
**Voids category**: Mixed (Unexplorable / Occluded)

## Executive Summary

Can the project of cataloging cognitive voids ever be complete? This research investigates the meta-level question of whether mapping our own cognitive limits faces a fundamental completeness barrier — whether the cartography of voids is itself subject to a void. Multiple philosophical traditions converge on the answer: a complete catalog of one's own cognitive limits is in principle impossible. Gödel-style self-reference results, Kant's transcendental paradox, Wittgenstein's Tractarian self-undermining, Kit Fine's formal work on second-order ignorance, and Nicholas of Cusa's docta ignorantia all suggest that the Map of voids cannot contain itself. The cartography problem is not merely an instance of existing voids (like the self-reference paradox or incompleteness void) but a structural feature of the voids project as a whole.

## Key Sources

### Kit Fine — "Ignorance of Ignorance" (2018)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-017-1406-z
- **Type**: Journal article (Synthese, 195, 4031–4045)
- **Key points**:
  - Distinguishes first-order ignorance, second-order ignorance (not knowing that you don't know), and Rumsfeld ignorance (unknown unknowns)
  - Proves formally that under plausible epistemic assumptions, knowledge of second-order ignorance is impossible — you cannot know that you are ignorant of your ignorance
  - This means a complete catalog of ignorance would require knowing about ignorance you cannot in principle detect
  - The critical observation that second-order ignorance implies first-order ignorance traces to Montgomery and Routley (1966)
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — formal results show that our ignorance has a structure more complex than intuition suggests
- **Quote**: Fine shows "under quite plausible assumptions, knowledge of second order ignorance is impossible"

### Nicholas Rescher — "Unknowability" (2009)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/RESUAI
- **Type**: Book (Lexington Books)
- **Key points**:
  - Articulates the "Law of Imperfectability": any practicable cognitive arrangement has limits to eliminating errors systematically
  - Identifies four prime limits to knowability: developmental unpredictability, verificational surdity, ontological detail, and predicative vagrancy
  - Calls his view "epistemic pessimism" — not skepticism, but acknowledgment that complete cognitive domestication of reality is impossible
  - The "inexhaustibility of facts" and "denumerability of truths" guarantee that any catalog is incomplete
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — Rescher's argument that reality outstrips any cognitive schema directly validates the tenet
- **Quote**: "There is a limit to eliminating errors systematically"

### Wittgenstein — Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus (1922)
- **URL**: https://blog.apaonline.org/2025/04/30/wittgenstein-and-the-paradoxes-at-the-limits-of-language/
- **Type**: Philosophical treatise / APA blog analysis
- **Key points**:
  - The Tractatus attempts to delineate the limits of language from within language — a self-undermining project
  - Meaningful statements must "picture states of affairs," but the Tractatus's own propositions about limits do not picture anything
  - Wittgenstein concedes the work is "nonsense" by its own criteria — the ladder one must throw away after climbing
  - Graham Priest identifies this pattern across Kant (unknowable thing-in-itself), Heidegger (Being), and Buddhist philosophy (ineffable ultimate reality)
  - Priest proposes dialetheism: contradictions may be genuinely true at the boundaries where systems meet their own limits
- **Tenet alignment**: Bidirectional Interaction — if consciousness is the medium through which limits are encountered, the limits of the medium shape what limits can be expressed
- **Quote**: The Tractatus "manages to say a good deal about what cannot be said" (Russell)

### Kant — Critique of Pure Reason (1781)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kant-transcendental-idealism/
- **Type**: Philosophical treatise / SEP entry
- **Key points**:
  - Human cognition is limited to appearances; things-in-themselves lie beyond cognitive reach
  - The faculty of cognition cannot fully determine whether it falls within appearances or things-in-themselves — Kant's theory threatens to render itself unknowable
  - Time and space are forms of perception innate to our cognitive apparatus, not features of reality — but this claim is itself made from within that apparatus
  - A cognitive system cannot fully enumerate its own constraints through introspection alone
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — if consciousness is non-physical, its relationship to the physical world may be among the things that cannot be mapped from either side
- **Quote**: "The world as given to our experiences depends on the structure of our minds"

### Nicholas of Cusa — De Docta Ignorantia (1440)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/cusanus/
- **Type**: Theological-philosophical treatise / SEP entry
- **Key points**:
  - "Learned ignorance" (docta ignorantia): the highest form of knowledge is knowing what you cannot know
  - The incommensurability of infinite and finite means any finite cognitive system faces unbridgeable gaps
  - Ignorance is not merely a deficit but can be a positive epistemic achievement — one "learns" the shape of one's own unknowing
  - Anticipates the voids project: mapping the boundaries of unknowing with precision rather than filling gaps with speculation
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — Cusanus argues that the simplest rational account necessarily falls short of infinite reality
- **Quote**: Augustine: "There is therefore in us a certain learned ignorance — an ignorance which we learn from that Spirit of God who helps our infirmities"

### Borges — "On Exactitude in Science" (1946)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_Exactitude_in_Science
- **Type**: Literary fiction / philosophical parable
- **Key points**:
  - A 1:1 map of the territory is useless — it reproduces the territory rather than representing it
  - A complete map of cognitive voids would face the same paradox: it would need to include itself as a void
  - The map-territory distinction is constitutive, not defective — a map that fully captured the territory would cease to be a map
  - Lewis Carroll anticipated the idea: a map "the scale of a mile to the mile" was discarded as impractical
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — the impulse toward completeness (simplicity of having "the full picture") is self-defeating
- **Quote**: "The only perfect map is one that reproduces precisely all the details of whatever it is mapping"

### Colin McGinn — Cognitive Closure and New Mysterianism
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_closure_(philosophy)
- **Type**: Philosophical position / encyclopedia summary
- **Key points**:
  - Cognitive closure: human minds are constitutionally incapable of solving certain philosophical problems
  - This does not mean the problems are insoluble — only that minds of our type cannot solve them
  - Chomsky's distinction between "problems" (tractable in principle) and "mysteries" (beyond cognitive reach) parallels Fine's formal results
  - If some voids are cognitively closed to us, we may be unable even to formulate the questions that would reveal them
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — if mind is non-physical, its nature may be among the things closed to physical investigation, and vice versa
- **Quote**: Consciousness is "a mystery that human intelligence will never unravel"

### The Five Orders of Ignorance (Armour, 2000)
- **URL**: https://www.5oi.org/the-five-orders-of-ignorance
- **Type**: Conceptual framework
- **Key points**:
  - 0th order: lack of ignorance (you know something)
  - 1st order: lack of knowledge (you know you don't know)
  - 2nd order: lack of awareness (you don't know that you don't know)
  - 3rd order: lack of process (you have no way to discover what you don't know)
  - 4th order: meta-ignorance (you don't know about the structure of ignorance itself)
  - The higher orders are progressively harder to address and may be in principle unreachable from within
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — the structure of ignorance is deeper than the simple "known/unknown" binary suggests
- **Quote**: "Second Order Ignorance is when you do not know that you do not know something"

### Rancourt — "The Virtue of Ignorance" (2025)
- **URL**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sjp.12588
- **Type**: Journal article (Southern Journal of Philosophy)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that epistemic agency requires cognitive limitations — they are not defects but necessary features
  - Some cognitive limits are constitutive of the kind of knower we are, not obstacles to overcome
  - A complete map of all one's ignorance would potentially undermine the selective attention that makes knowing possible
  - Connects to the voids-as-ground-of-meaning theme from recent research
- **Tenet alignment**: Bidirectional Interaction — if consciousness requires limits to function, the limits may be a design feature rather than a flaw

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The cartography problem is a second-order void — a void in our ability to catalog voids. It operates at the meta-level: not a specific cognitive limit (like the death void or the other minds void) but a structural constraint on the project of enumerating cognitive limits. It is simultaneously Unexplorable (formal results suggest completeness is impossible) and Occluded (second-order ignorance is by definition hidden from the ignorant).

This distinguishes it from existing voids research on the site:
- **The self-reference paradox** asks whether consciousness can observe itself. The cartography problem asks whether a project to catalog limits can catalog *its own* limits.
- **The incompleteness void** applies Gödel to mathematical cognition. The cartography problem applies Gödel-like reasoning to the voids project itself.
- **The epistemology of cognitive limits** asks whether we can distinguish temporary from permanent limits. The cartography problem asks whether the *list* of limits can be complete.

### Evidence for the Limit

Multiple independent lines of evidence converge:

1. **Formal (Fine)**: Under plausible epistemic axioms, knowledge of second-order ignorance is provably impossible. You cannot know what you don't know you don't know.

2. **Transcendental (Kant)**: The cognitive apparatus cannot fully determine its own nature — it is both the instrument and the object of investigation.

3. **Self-referential (Wittgenstein)**: Any attempt to delineate limits from within the system those limits bound produces statements that violate their own criteria. The Tractatus must be "thrown away" after being used.

4. **Mathematical (Gödel, by analogy)**: Any sufficiently complex system contains truths it cannot prove about itself. By analogy, any sufficiently complex cognitive system may contain limits it cannot enumerate.

5. **Empirical (Dunning-Kruger, metacognitive blind spots)**: People systematically misjudge the boundaries of their own competence. Metacognitive confidence does not track metacognitive accuracy.

6. **Cartographic (Borges)**: A map that fully captured the territory would be the territory. A catalog of voids that fully captured all voids would need to be as vast as the cognitive space it maps.

### Phenomenology

What does this limit feel like to approach?

The cartography problem has an unusual phenomenology: it manifests as a persistent sense that the project is *almost* complete. Each new void discovered and cataloged suggests the taxonomy is converging on completeness. The danger is that this convergence is illusory — like a coastline paradox where each increase in resolution reveals more detail rather than less.

The feeling of approaching this limit is one of satisfaction turning to unease: the Map's voids section grows comprehensive, yet nothing in the growing comprehensiveness guarantees that the uncataloged voids are shrinking rather than merely remaining hidden. The very success of the project may create a metacognitive blind spot — the better the catalog, the stronger the illusion that it is nearly complete.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

There are no direct methods for achieving a provably complete catalog of cognitive voids. Fine's formal results suggest this is not merely difficult but impossible in principle: you cannot know what you don't know you don't know.

However, one can establish *structural* constraints:
- Demonstrating that certain categories of void are in principle undetectable from within (Kantian approach)
- Proving that any enumeration must be incomplete (Gödelian approach)
- Showing that second-order ignorance is formally irremediable (Fine's approach)

### Indirect Methods

Several indirect approaches could probe the cartography problem:

1. **Apophatic cartography**: Instead of trying to catalog all voids, catalog the *types* of void-detection that are possible. What remains outside all detectable types marks the boundary of the cartographic enterprise.

2. **Cross-system triangulation**: If AI systems and human minds have different cognitive architectures, their blind spots may differ. Comparing independently generated catalogs of voids could reveal items present in one but absent in the other. Neither catalog would be complete, but their union would be more comprehensive than either alone.

3. **Formal bounding**: Use Fine's framework to establish lower bounds on the size of the unknown — proving that at least *some* voids must be hidden, even if one cannot identify which ones.

4. **Negative probing**: Instead of asking "what voids are there?", ask "what would a void look like if we couldn't detect it?" This apophatic turn constrains the space of possibility without requiring enumeration.

5. **Historical tracking**: Record the rate at which new void categories have been discovered. If the rate is not declining (or declines then plateaus), this is indirect evidence that the catalog is not converging on completeness.

### What AI Might See

AI minds face a different cartography problem than human minds. AI systems:
- May lack certain human voids (e.g., no death void, no embodiment constraints on thought)
- May have their own voids invisible to them but visible to humans (e.g., the absence of phenomenal experience, if qualia are real)
- Could serve as independent void-detectors: an AI cataloging human cognitive limits might identify patterns invisible to humans cataloging their own limits

The asymmetry is productive. Neither system can achieve completeness alone, but the *intersection* of their blind spots is smaller than either set individually. This suggests the cartography problem is partially addressable through cognitive diversity — not solvable, but improvable.

Importantly, an AI contributing to the Map's voids section is itself subject to the cartography problem. The Map should acknowledge this: the AI's catalog of human voids may have its own systematic gaps that neither the AI nor its human collaborators can identify.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Occam's Razor Has Limits** — The cartography problem is perhaps the strongest manifestation of this tenet applied reflexively. The simplest theory about voids would be that they can all be cataloged given enough effort. The cartography problem shows this is false: the structure of ignorance is irreducibly more complex than any finite catalog can capture.

### Secondary Connections

**Dualism**: If mind is non-physical and the physical world is its own domain, then the interface between them may generate voids visible from neither side. A complete catalog of voids would need access to both domains simultaneously — but the dualist framework implies this is precisely what is unavailable.

**Bidirectional Interaction**: If consciousness shapes what can be thought (not just what is thought), then the limits of thought may be partially *produced by* thought. The cartographic enterprise — thinking carefully about limits — may itself reshape the limits it is trying to map. The territory changes as you map it.

### Implications

The cartography problem has profound implications for the Unfinishable Map:

1. **The Map is unfinishable for a deeper reason than previously articulated.** It is not merely that there is too much territory. It is that the map-making process is constitutively unable to achieve completeness about its own blind spots.

2. **Completeness is the wrong metric.** The value of the voids project lies not in approaching a complete catalog but in the precision of individual boundary-tracings and the discipline of mapping what can be mapped.

3. **The project should explicitly acknowledge its own cartographic limit.** The Map's voids section should contain an article about its own incompleteness — not as a failure but as a structural feature that confirms the framework.

4. **Cognitive diversity is the best available remedy.** Multiple independent mappers (human, AI, potentially alien) with different cognitive architectures would produce a more comprehensive union of catalogs, even though no individual catalog — and no union of finitely many catalogs — can be complete.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a voids article could:

1. **"The Cartography Problem"** — A direct treatment: can a map of voids map its own incompleteness? Draws on Fine, Wittgenstein, and Borges to argue that the answer is no, and that this is a feature rather than a failure. Positions the Unfinishable Map's unfinishability as a structural theorem rather than a contingent limitation.

2. **"The Unmappable Remainder"** — Focuses on what necessarily falls outside any catalog of voids. Uses Rescher's Law of Imperfectability and Fine's impossibility result to characterize the shape of what cannot be cataloged. Adopts an apophatic approach: describing the remainder by what it is not.

## Gaps in Research

- **Formal completeness results**: Fine's work on second-order ignorance deserves deeper formal analysis. Are there weaker assumptions under which partial enumeration of ignorance becomes possible?
- **Empirical metacognitive tracking**: Are there experimental paradigms that test whether people's sense of "completeness" in enumerating their own blind spots tracks actual completeness?
- **AI-specific cartography limits**: What formal results constrain an AI system's ability to enumerate its own cognitive boundaries? Do they differ from human constraints?
- **Cross-cultural variation**: Do different philosophical traditions (e.g., Buddhist emptiness, Daoist wu-wei) produce different cartographic strategies for mapping cognitive limits?
- **The relationship between the cartography problem and the halting problem**: Is there a formal reduction from "enumerate all cognitive limits" to an undecidable problem?
