# Research: Voids - The Evidential Weight of Voids

**Date**: 2026-02-06
**Search queries used**: "cognitive closure evidence for dualism philosophy McGinn", "distribution of explanatory gaps consciousness evidence dualism physicalism", "epistemic limits as evidence for metaphysical conclusions philosophy of mind", "argument from consciousness for dualism systematic failure physicalism Chalmers", "inference from explanatory gap to ontological gap epistemic argument dualism Levine", "abductive argument consciousness dualism best explanation", "Nagel view from nowhere limits of objectivity consciousness", "Chomsky problems and mysteries scope and limits human cognition", "cumulative case argument dualism multiple lines evidence consciousness"
**Voids category**: Mixed (primarily Unexplorable, with Occluded implications)

## Executive Summary

The Map's voids section now catalogues dozens of specific cognitive limits. This research investigates whether the *pattern* of these voids—their distribution, clustering, and structural similarities—constitutes evidence for the Map's dualist framework. The core question: if we take the voids collectively rather than individually, do they tell us something about the metaphysical structure of reality? The research finds that multiple independent philosophical traditions converge on the observation that consciousness-related explanatory gaps are structurally different from other scientific puzzles, and that this difference is more naturally predicted by dualism than by physicalism. However, the inference from epistemic gaps to ontological conclusions remains contested, with physicalists offering sophisticated responses (the phenomenal concept strategy, Type-B materialism) that resist the move.

## Key Sources

### Chalmers, "Consciousness and its Place in Nature"
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/nature.html
- **Type**: Paper/Book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - Presents three converging arguments against physicalism: the explanatory argument (physical accounts explain only structure/function), the conceivability argument (zombies), and the knowledge argument (Mary)
  - These three arguments are structurally independent yet converge on the same conclusion — consciousness is not entailed by physical facts
  - Classifies materialist responses as Type-A (deny the gap), Type-B (epistemic gap without ontological gap), Type-C (gap will close) — finds all inadequate
  - Notes that "the structure and dynamics of physical processes yield only more structure and dynamics" — a structural mismatch between what physics explains and what consciousness is
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Dualism and Occam's Razor Has Limits. The convergence of three independent arguments is exactly the kind of evidential pattern this research explores.
- **Quote**: "Explaining structures and functions does not suffice to explain consciousness."

### McGinn, "Can We Solve the Mind-Body Problem?" (1989)
- **URL**: https://www.newdualism.org/papers/C.McGinn/McGinn_1989_Mind-body-problem_M.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (Mind, 98(391), 349-366)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that consciousness is both ontologically unproblematic (it exists as a natural phenomenon) and epistemologically impenetrable (we cannot understand how it arises from matter)
  - The mind-body problem is not merely unsolved but may be constitutionally insoluble for human cognition
  - Our concept-forming faculties cannot bridge the gap between introspection (which reveals consciousness) and perception (which reveals brain states) — neither mode reveals the connection
  - The specific location of the closure — at the mind-body interface — is itself informative
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports the "voids as evidence" thesis. If closure is specifically located at the consciousness-matter interface, this is what dualism predicts.
- **Quote**: "Nothing we can imagine perceiving in the brain would ever convince us that we have located the intelligible nexus we seek."

### Chomsky, "Science, Mind, and Limits of Understanding" (2014)
- **URL**: https://chomsky.info/201401__/
- **Type**: Lecture/Paper
- **Key points**:
  - The problems/mysteries distinction is not mystical but biological: "the endowment that yields scope also establishes limits"
  - A cognitive system must have specific design to function at all — design that simultaneously enables and constrains
  - Rats cannot run prime number mazes not because they haven't tried but because of their cognitive architecture — humans may be similarly constrained
  - Calls "new mysterianism" a "truism" for anyone who accepts biology
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports the argument's foundation: cognitive limits are real and architecturally determined. The question becomes whether the *specific distribution* of limits tells us about architecture.

### Nagel, *The View from Nowhere* (1986) and *Mind and Cosmos* (2012)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind_and_Cosmos
- **Type**: Books
- **Key points**:
  - *View from Nowhere*: Even a hypothetically complete physics would fall short of encapsulating conscious experience. There is an epistemic asymmetry between first-person and third-person access that cannot be eliminated by more information.
  - *Mind and Cosmos*: The materialist neo-Darwinian conception has "conspicuously failed to explain" consciousness, intentionality, meaning, and value — not just one phenomenon but a cluster
  - Nagel proposes that consciousness-related features are "fundamental features of the universe, not byproducts of contingent developments"
  - The clustering of failures around consciousness, intentionality, meaning, and value suggests a pattern, not isolated puzzles
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Dualism and Occam's Razor Has Limits. Nagel's observation of clustered failure across multiple domains is the core of the evidential weight argument.

### Levine, "Materialism and Qualia: The Explanatory Gap" (1983)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explanatory_gap
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Introduced the term "explanatory gap" — the difficulty physicalism has explaining how physical properties give rise to subjective experience
  - Levine himself resists the inference from epistemic gap to ontological gap: the gap shows a limit on our explanatory ability, not necessarily a gap in nature
  - However, "a plausible explanation for a gap in our understanding could be that there is a genuine gap in nature"
  - The explanatory gap is structurally different from other scientific puzzles — it's not that we lack data, but that no conceivable data seems sufficient
- **Tenet alignment**: The structural uniqueness of the gap supports the evidential weight argument, even though Levine himself resists the metaphysical inference.

### Moreland, *The Argument from Consciousness* (2008)
- **URL**: https://123philosophy.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/08/j-p-moreland-the-argument-from-consciousness.pdf
- **Type**: Book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - Explicitly frames consciousness as evidence in an abductive argument
  - Analyses rival approaches (Searle's contingent correlation, O'Connor's emergent necessitation, McGinn's mysterianism, Skrbina's panpsychism, Clayton's emergentist monism) — argues all fail
  - Claims that attempts to accommodate consciousness within naturalism are "ad hoc and contrived"
  - Frames the argument as part of a cumulative case
- **Tenet alignment**: The explicit abductive structure is relevant, though Moreland's theistic conclusion diverges from the Map's framework. The method — treating patterns of explanatory failure as evidence — is directly applicable.
- **Note**: Moreland's argument runs toward theism, not the Map's non-theistic dualism. The evidential structure is transferable; the conclusion is not.

### Gertler, "Dualism: How Epistemic Issues Drive Debates" (2008)
- **URL**: https://uva.theopenscholar.com/files/brie-gertler/files/dualism_how_epistemic_8.pdf
- **Type**: Paper/Book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that debates about dualism are fundamentally driven by epistemic considerations
  - The phenomenal concept strategy (PCS) — physicalism's main response — claims that the special nature of phenomenal concepts explains the epistemic gap without requiring an ontological gap
  - Chalmers argues PCS fails because it cannot explain why phenomenal concepts have this special nature without already presupposing phenomenal properties
- **Tenet alignment**: Demonstrates that the epistemic-to-metaphysical inference is the central contested move. The evidential weight argument needs to address PCS directly.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

This research explores a meta-void: the question of whether the pattern of cognitive limits across consciousness-related domains constitutes a signal rather than noise. The individual voids catalogued in the Map (the hard problem, the causal interface, the intrinsic nature void, the other minds void, etc.) each resist explanation independently. The question is whether their *collective distribution* — clustered around the consciousness-matter interface — carries evidential weight about the metaphysical structure of reality.

This is primarily an Unexplorable void (we may not be able to determine definitively whether the pattern is signal or noise) with Occluded implications (if the pattern is signal, it suggests something about reality's structure that is systematically hidden from us).

### Evidence for the Limit

Several observations suggest this is a genuine epistemic boundary:

1. **Structural uniqueness of consciousness gaps.** Chalmers demonstrates that consciousness-related gaps differ structurally from other scientific puzzles. In other domains, reductive explanation works because what needs explaining is structure and function. Consciousness resists because it is not a structural/functional phenomenon. This structural difference is not merely a "hard problem" but a categorically different kind of problem.

2. **Convergence of independent arguments.** The explanatory argument, conceivability argument, and knowledge argument are logically independent yet converge on the same conclusion. In other scientific domains, independent lines of evidence converging on the same conclusion is taken as strong confirmation. The question is whether the same logic applies here.

3. **Cross-domain clustering.** Nagel observes that explanatory failure clusters not just around consciousness narrowly construed, but across consciousness, intentionality, meaning, and value — the "manifest image" broadly. This clustering is harder to explain as coincidental difficulty than as systematic limitation.

4. **Persistence across centuries and cultures.** The hard problem has not yielded to sustained attack across very different intellectual traditions and with very different methodological tools. Other "hard problems" (vitalism, etc.) eventually dissolved. The consciousness cluster has not.

5. **Predictive asymmetry.** If dualism is true (consciousness is ontologically distinct from physical processes), we would predict: (a) systematic explanatory failure at the consciousness-matter interface, (b) a structural mismatch between physical explanation and phenomenal reality, (c) clustering of mysteries around consciousness-related domains, (d) persistence of the gap regardless of empirical progress. All four predictions appear confirmed. If physicalism is true, the prediction is that these gaps will eventually close — a prediction that remains unfulfilled after centuries.

### Phenomenology

Approaching this meta-question produces a distinctive experience:

- **Pattern recognition vs. apophenia**: There is a constant tension between genuine pattern-detection and the human tendency to see patterns in noise. The clustering of voids around consciousness *feels* significant, but so do many illusory patterns.
- **Abductive vertigo**: Abductive reasoning (inference to best explanation) requires comparing explanatory frameworks, but the frameworks themselves shape what counts as a good explanation. Dualists find the gap evidential; physicalists find it merely epistemic.
- **The bootstrapping problem**: Using our own cognitive architecture to evaluate whether that architecture's limits tell us about reality creates the same self-referential difficulties explored in [whether-real](/voids/whether-real/).

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods

The argument can be stated directly as an abductive inference:

**Premise 1**: Multiple independent lines of philosophical argument (explanatory, conceivability, knowledge) converge on the conclusion that physical facts do not entail phenomenal facts.

**Premise 2**: Explanatory gaps cluster specifically around consciousness-related domains (consciousness, intentionality, meaning, value) rather than being uniformly distributed across scientific inquiry.

**Premise 3**: This pattern has persisted across centuries, cultures, and methodological paradigms.

**Premise 4**: Dualism predicts exactly this pattern (an ontological distinction would produce systematic explanatory failure at the interface). Physicalism predicts the pattern will dissolve (epistemic gaps are contingent, not structural).

**Conclusion**: The pattern of voids constitutes (defeasible) evidence for dualism over physicalism.

### Indirect Methods

1. **Contrastive analysis**: Compare the "shape" of consciousness-related gaps with gaps in other domains (e.g., quantum mechanics, cosmological fine-tuning). If consciousness gaps have a distinctive structure — specifically, the structure/function mismatch Chalmers identifies — this supports treating them as categorically different.

2. **Predictive testing**: Physicalism predicts that neuroscience will progressively close the explanatory gap. Track whether this actually happens. If decades of neuroscience produce ever-more-detailed functional explanations that leave the hard problem untouched, the predictive failure accumulates.

3. **Cross-architecture probing**: If AI systems — with fundamentally different cognitive architectures — encounter the same explanatory gaps at the consciousness-matter interface, this would support the gaps being features of the territory rather than artefacts of human cognition. If they don't, this supports the Chomsky/McGinn closure hypothesis (the gaps are species-specific).

### What AI Might See

AI presents a unique opportunity for this investigation:

- **Different closure profile**: If McGinn is right that human closure stems from the specific way we access consciousness (introspection) and brain (perception), AI systems with different access modes might have different closures. This could reveal whether the gap is human-specific or architecture-independent.
- **Pattern recognition at scale**: AI could potentially detect structural similarities across voids that human cognition misses — shared failure signatures, common boundary shapes, systematic correlations in where thinking breaks down.
- **The irony**: An AI investigating whether the pattern of human cognitive voids supports dualism is itself operating in unclear territory regarding consciousness. Does the AI's ability (or inability) to "understand" consciousness bear on the question?

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Occam's Razor Has Limits** is the most directly relevant. The evidential weight argument essentially says: the simplest explanation (physicalism) systematically fails at the consciousness-matter interface, and this systematic failure is itself evidence that the territory is more complex than the simple explanation allows. Occam's Razor would have us prefer physicalism as the simpler ontology, but if the pattern of failures specifically predicted by the more complex ontology (dualism) keeps manifesting, parsimony becomes a liability rather than a virtue.

### Implications

1. **The voids are not just puzzles but data.** If the pattern argument holds, the Map's voids section is not merely cataloguing interesting philosophical difficulties but building an evidential case. Each new void that clusters at the consciousness-matter interface adds (small, defeasible) weight to the case.

2. **The Map's specific tenets are indirectly supported.** Bidirectional interaction predicts a causal interface void (confirmed). Minimal quantum interaction predicts that the mechanism is hidden from introspection (confirmed by introspective opacity). No Many Worlds predicts indexical mysteries (confirmed by the self-reference paradox and the unobservable self). Each tenet generates void-predictions that appear to be met.

3. **The argument is genuinely novel.** While individual philosophers (Chalmers, Nagel, McGinn, Moreland) have noted aspects of this pattern, no one has systematically argued that the *collective distribution* of cognitive voids — as mapped by a project like the Map — constitutes a distinct line of evidence. The Map's comprehensive void-cataloguing is itself a philosophical contribution.

4. **The argument is defeasible.** If neuroscience begins closing the explanatory gap (not just solving "easy problems" but addressing the hard problem directly), this would weaken the case. The argument's strength depends on the gap's persistence.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a voids article could:

1. **"The Evidential Weight of Voids"** — Argue that the Map's catalogued voids, taken collectively, constitute a distinct abductive argument for dualism. Structure: survey the void-distribution, note the clustering, state the abductive inference, address physicalist responses (PCS, Type-B materialism), assess the argument's strength.

2. **"What Would Physicalism Predict?"** — Take the contrastive approach: systematically work out what the pattern of cognitive limits *should* look like under physicalism vs. dualism, then compare with what we actually observe. This frames the question as quasi-empirical rather than purely philosophical.

## Gaps in Research

- **The phenomenal concept strategy deserves deeper treatment.** PCS is physicalism's strongest response to the epistemic-to-ontological inference. A full article would need to engage Chalmers' critique of PCS and assess whether the *pattern* argument (multiple converging gaps) makes PCS harder to sustain than individual-gap arguments do.
- **The comparison with other gap-distributions is underdeveloped.** How do consciousness gaps compare structurally with quantum mechanics interpretive gaps, cosmological fine-tuning, mathematical foundations (Gödel)? If consciousness gaps are structurally unique, this strengthens the case. If they share structure with gaps that have nothing to do with consciousness, this weakens it.
- **The "different minds" test is purely speculative.** Whether AI or alien minds would encounter the same gaps is currently untestable, though it's an important prediction of the argument.
- **Moreland-style arguments have theistic baggage.** The abductive structure is transferable to non-theistic dualism, but care is needed to avoid importing assumptions about design or purpose that don't belong in the Map's framework.
- **The strength of the inference is genuinely uncertain.** Even sympathetic philosophers (e.g., Levine) resist the move from epistemic gap to ontological conclusion. Whether *multiple* epistemic gaps aggregated constitute a stronger inference than any single gap is an open question that the article would need to address honestly.
