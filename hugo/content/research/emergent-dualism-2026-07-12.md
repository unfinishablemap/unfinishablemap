## Executive Summary

Emergent dualism is William Hasker's position that the conscious self is a genuinely new, non-composite mental *substance*—a single subject of experience with novel causal powers—that is *generated by* and *dependent on* the brain's functional organization once it reaches sufficient complexity. It occupies a deliberately narrow middle ground: unlike Cartesian substance dualism, the mind is not a pre-existing soul but is produced by brain function (so it is emergent and brain-dependent); unlike property dualism or emergent materialism, what emerges is a new individual thing, not merely a set of properties borne by the brain. Hasker's central argument is the unity-of-consciousness (UOC) argument: a unified conscious experience requires a single experiencer, and no physical composite—whose parts constantly change and are many rather than one—can be that single subject. His signature analogy is the magnetic (electromagnetic) field: the mind stands to the brain as a field to the magnet that generates it—qualitatively different from its physical source, spatially located where the source is, and able to act back on it. That spatial location is how emergent dualism claims to dissolve the pairing problem that dogs Cartesian souls. The position is a live rival/cousin to the Map's interactionist dualism: both reject reduction (Tenet 1), but emergent dualism makes consciousness *emerge from and depend on* the brain, which is precisely the dependence-vs-independence question the Map must sharpen for its quantum-interface account.

## Major Positions

### Emergent Dualism (Hasker)
- **Proponents**: William Hasker (originator); sympathetic engagement from Dean Zimmerman on emergent-individual metaphysics.
- **Core claim**: When a brain/nervous system reaches sufficient complexity, it *generates* a new non-physical substance—the conscious self—which is a single subject of experience with its own novel causal powers, spatially located where the brain is, and able to act back on the brain.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. **Unity-of-consciousness (UOC) argument** — the central argument against materialism. A unified conscious experience requires a single, genuinely unified experiencer; a physical composite is a manifold (many parts, constantly changing) and so cannot *be* the one subject. Therefore the subject is a non-composite substance.
  2. **Emergence, not pre-existence** — the mind is produced by brain function and depends on it, so no Cartesian pre-existing soul is posited; this keeps the view continuous with a broadly naturalistic, brain-respecting picture.
  3. **Substance, not mere property** — what emerges is a new individual (a bearer of experience), not just irreducible properties of the brain; this is what distinguishes it from property dualism / emergent materialism.
  4. **Field analogy** — mind : brain :: magnetic field : magnet. Qualitatively distinct from its generator, co-located with it, and causally efficacious on it.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong alignment with Tenet 1 (irreducibility) and Tenet 3 (downward/bidirectional causation). The friction point is *dependence*: emergent dualism makes consciousness ontologically posterior to and generated by the brain, whereas the Map's interactionist dualism treats non-physical consciousness as a distinct pole interacting at a minimal quantum interface (Tenet 2). Whether the Map's consciousness *emerges from* the brain or is *independent of* it is exactly the question emergent dualism forces.

### Contrast class 1: Cartesian / Thomistic substance dualism
- **Proponents**: Descartes (historical); contemporary substance dualists including Richard Swinburne, and (as critics of the emergent variant) Rickabaugh, Moreland.
- **Core claim**: The soul is a substance that does not depend on the brain for its existence (Cartesian: non-spatial, separately created/pre-existing).
- **Key contrast**: Shares "mind is a substance" with emergent dualism but rejects brain-generation and (for Cartesianism) spatial location. This is what leaves classical dualism exposed to the pairing problem that emergent dualism claims to solve.

### Contrast class 2: Property dualism / emergent materialism
- **Proponents**: e.g. non-reductive physicalists and property emergentists.
- **Core claim**: Consciousness is (or supervenes on) irreducible *properties* of the physical brain; there is no new *thing*, only new properties.
- **Key contrast**: Emergent dualism accepts emergence but insists a new *individual/substance* emerges—precisely because (per the UOC argument) properties borne by a composite still leave you without a single subject.

## Key Debates

### Does the unity of consciousness require a non-composite subject?
- **Sides**: Hasker (and Barnett, Swinburne) — yes; materialists and some dualists — no.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "unified experience needs a single experiencer" entails a *metaphysically simple* subject, or whether functional/integrative unity in a composite suffices.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Critics argue the materialist "has little to fear" and that unity may actually be *worse* for the emergent dualist—if the mind is *generated by* a composite brain, the burden of explaining how unity arises from multiplicity reappears at the emergence step (the combination worry).

### Is the emergence of a *substance* coherent / unprecedented?
- **Sides**: Hasker — a novel but intelligible kind of emergence; critics (Rickabaugh; also Kim-influenced physicalists) — emergence of new *substances* with new causal laws is unprecedented and mysterious.
- **Core disagreement**: Ordinary emergence (solidity, liquidity) is grounded in micro-parts obeying standard physics; a new conscious substance with new causal powers is "a far cry" from that (a concession Hasker himself makes). Is that a difference in degree or an unexplained ontological leap?
- **Current state**: Unresolved; central cost cited by critics.

### Causal exclusion / overdetermination (Kim)
- **Sides**: Kim-style exclusion argument vs. emergentist replies.
- **Core disagreement**: If every physical effect already has a sufficient physical cause, a distinct mental/emergent cause is either excluded or produces systematic overdetermination. Emergentists reply variously (e.g. denying that emergent properties are merely supervenient, or embracing genuine downward causation).
- **Current state**: Live across all interactionist views—shared with the Map's own interactionism, which is why it is a useful shared battleground rather than a distinctive objection to emergent dualism alone.

### Does emergent dualism actually solve the pairing problem?
- **Sides**: Hasker — yes (spatial location dissolves it); skeptics — the generation relation just relocates the mystery.
- **Core disagreement**: Because the emergent mind is *spatially located* where the generating brain is, "why is this soul paired with this brain?" is said to be no more puzzling than "why is this magnetic field paired with this magnet?"—the field is where its source is. Skeptics ask whether "generated by and located at" is itself well-understood for a non-physical substance.
- **Current state**: Treated by Hasker and the SEP as a comparative *advantage* over Cartesian dualism; critics regard it as promissory.

## Map Self-Positioning

- **Shared ground (Tenets 1 & 3)**: Both emergent dualism and the Map reject reduction of consciousness to physical processes, and both affirm that consciousness acts back on the physical (Hasker's field "acts on the brain that generates it"). Emergent dualism is therefore a *cousin*, not an opponent, at the level of anti-physicalism.
- **The decisive divergence — dependence vs. independence**: Emergent dualism makes consciousness *generated by and ontologically dependent on* the brain. The Map's interactionist dualism treats non-physical consciousness as a distinct pole that *interacts* with the physical at a minimal quantum interface (Tenet 2). The sharpening question the Map should answer explicitly: does the Map's non-physical consciousness *emerge from* neural complexity (moving it toward Hasker), or is it independent and merely *coupled* to the brain (moving it toward Cartesian/interactionist views)? Emergent dualism is the best-developed articulation of the "emerges from the brain" horn, so it is the ideal foil for stating the Map's answer precisely.
- **Leverage for the Map**: Hasker's field analogy and his pairing-problem solution are assets the Map can borrow *if* it wants spatial co-location of the interface; the combination/UOC worries against Hasker are also warnings for any view (including the Map's) that lets a unified subject arise from, or couple to, a physical manifold.
- **Corpus fit**: Hasker is already the most-cited dualist author across the corpus (unity-of-consciousness, pairing-problem, agent-causation, consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition) with no home page. agent-causation states "Hasker combines agent causation with emergent dualism" with nothing to link.
