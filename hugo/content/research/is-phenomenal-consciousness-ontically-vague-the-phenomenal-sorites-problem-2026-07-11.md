## Executive Summary

The question is whether phenomenal consciousness is *ontically* (metaphysically) vague: could there be a system for which there is **no fact of the matter whether it is phenomenally conscious at all** — not merely how much, but whether the lights are on in any degree? This is the phenomenal analogue of the sorites paradox: string a continuum from a clearly-conscious human to a clearly-non-conscious object (evolutionary series, gradual neuron removal, developmental gradient) and ask whether every adjacent pair must share consciousness-status, whether a sharp cutoff falls somewhere, or whether some middle cases are indeterminate.

The literature splits sharply and the task's shorthand attribution was **partly wrong**, which the verification below corrects. Two camps: (1) a **sharpness camp** denying any borderline cases — **Antony** (the concept CONSCIOUS STATE is plausibly sharp) and **Simon** (zombie conceivability entails no positive characterization of a borderline case, so 'phenomenally conscious' cannot have borderline cases); and (2) a **vagueness-accepting camp** — **Hall** (borderline consciousness is possible), **Schwitzgebel** (a naturalist quadrilemma forces indeterminacy), and — contrary to the task's shorthand — **Tye**, whose 2021 book argues consciousness *is* vague and resolves the resulting paradox by embracing panpsychism. **Papineau** is a special case: he locates the vagueness in phenomenal *concepts* (semantic/conceptual), not in a special ontic ingredient, but as a physicalist he accepts that borderline creatures may have no determinate consciousness-fact — so he does not sit cleanly in the "sharp lights on/off" camp, and Antony's work is in fact a direct reply to him.

For the Map, the stake is Tenet 4 (indexical identity matters / No Many Worlds): indexical identity presupposes a determinate fact about *whether there is a subject* and *whose* experience an experience is. Ontic vagueness of consciousness — especially Schwitzgebel's companion claim that the number of conscious subjects need not be a determinate whole number — would dissolve the sharp anchor Tenet 4 depends on.

### Simon, "Vagueness and Zombies: Why 'Phenomenally Conscious' has No Borderline Cases"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-016-0790-4
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Philosophical Studies* 174(8): 2105–2123 (2017)
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s11098-016-0790-4
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (author Jonathan A. Simon, title, journal, vol/issue/pages, DOI confirmed)
- **Key points**:
  - Defends a *Positive Characterization Thesis*: every borderline case of any predicate admits a positive characterization that shows a competent speaker what makes it borderline.
  - Argues that zombie *conceivability* entails there can be no positive characterization of a borderline case of 'phenomenally conscious'.
  - Therefore 'phenomenally conscious' has no borderline cases: for any creature at any time it cannot be vague whether it is phenomenally conscious.
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with the Map's need for a sharp consciousness-fact (Tenet 4). His dissertation is titled *The Sharp Contour of Consciousness*.

### Schwitzgebel, "Borderline consciousness, when it's neither determinately true nor determinately false that experience is present"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-023-02042-1
- **Type**: Journal paper — *Philosophical Studies* 180(12): 3415–3439 (2023)
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s11098-023-02042-1
- **Verify verdict**: CORRECT (author, title, journal, vol/issue/pages, DOI confirmed)
- **Key points**:
  - Presents a **quadrilemma**: either (a) nothing is conscious, or (b) everything is conscious, or (c) there is a sharp boundary across the apparent continuum, or (d) consciousness is a vague property admitting indeterminate cases.

## Major Positions

### Ontic (metaphysical) vagueness — "no fact of the matter"
- **Core claim**: The world itself is indeterminate at the boundary; there are systems for which it is objectively neither true nor false that experience is present.
- **Proponents**: Schwitzgebel (from the naturalist quadrilemma); Hall (defends its *possibility*); Tye (accepts consciousness is vague, then escapes via panpsychism rather than tolerating a borderline conscious/non-conscious case).
- **Key argument**: continuity + anti-arbitrariness. Any sharp cutoff across an evolutionary/developmental/anatomical continuum looks metaphysically arbitrary given naturalism; rejecting (a) nothing-conscious and (b) everything-conscious then forces indeterminacy.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct conflict with Tenet 4. If it is indeterminate whether there is a subject, the indexical "this experience is mine, now" has no determinate truth-maker.

### Conceptual / semantic vagueness — vagueness in the word, not the world
- **Core claim**: The underlying reality is determinate; 'conscious' / phenomenal concepts fail to pick out a unique property, so borderline verdicts reflect linguistic indeterminacy.
- **Proponents**: Papineau (phenomenal concepts are referentially indeterminate).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible with a determinate ontology (partial Tenet-4 friendliness) but still yields no-determinate-answer borderline cases, so it does not by itself secure the sharp subject-fact the Map wants.

### Epistemicism — sharp boundary, unknowable location
- **Core claim** (Williamson-style, applied here): there IS a precise fact about the last conscious member of the series; we simply cannot know where it falls.
- **Status in this literature**: usually the fallback for those who want sharpness without arbitrariness; less prominent in the phenomenal debate but the natural home for a dualist who wants a determinate on/off fact fixed by the (non-physical) interface rather than by a complex physical realizer.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Most Tenet-4-friendly: preserves a determinate fact about the subject even where it is epistemically inaccessible.

### Sharpness / no-borderline-cases
- **Core claim**: 'phenomenally conscious' admits no borderline cases; the concept is sharp.
- **Proponents**: Simon (zombie-conceivability + Positive Characterization Thesis); Antony (the concept is plausibly sharp).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong alignment with Tenet 4. But Antony's conditional warns that sharpness plus correctness sinks *complex-property* theories — including complex-property dualism — pushing the Map toward a *simple* interface relation (see below).

## Key Debates

### Can 'phenomenally conscious' have borderline cases at all?
- **Sides**: Simon/Antony (no) vs Hall/Schwitzgebel/Tye (yes, or must-be-vague).
- **Core disagreement**: whether the first-person "there's something it's like" self-intimates in a way that blocks a positive characterization of a borderline state (Simon), or whether naturalist continuity makes borderline cases unavoidable (Schwitzgebel).
- **Current state**: live and unresolved (2016–2023 exchange still active).

### If vague, is the vagueness ontic, semantic, or epistemic?
- **Sides**: ontic (Schwitzgebel, and Tye before his panpsychist exit) vs semantic (Papineau) vs epistemic (the sharpness-preserving fallback).
- **Core disagreement**: locus of indeterminacy — world, language, or knowledge.
- **Current state**: this taxonomy is the decisive one for the Map; the article should foreground it.

### The vagueness argument against physicalism
- **Structure**: (P1) If physicalism, consciousness is realized by complex physical properties that come in degrees, so borderline cases exist. (P2) 'Phenomenally conscious' has no borderline cases (Simon/Antony). (C) Physicalism is false.
- **Relevance**: a dualist-friendly argument; the Map can adopt it while noting it equally pressures *complex-property* dualism, motivating a simple interface relation.

## Map's Stake and Tenet Analysis

- **Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds / indexical identity matters)** is load-bearing here. Indexical identity presupposes a *determinate* fact about whether there is an experiencer and *whose* the experience is. Ontic vagueness — and especially Schwitzgebel's indeterminate subject-count — would leave borderline cases with no fact about whether any "I" exists, dissolving the anchor Tenet 4 relies on. The Map therefore has a real stake in the **sharpness/epistemicist** camp (Simon, Antony) over the ontic-vagueness camp (Schwitzgebel, Hall, Tye).
- **Tenet 1 (Dualism)** — Antony's conditional is a double-edged sword: sharpness plus a *correct* sharp concept refutes complex-property identity theory, functionalism, AND complex-property dualism. The Map's interface dualism can absorb this by grounding the on/off fact in a **simple, non-complex interface relation** (cf. interface-threshold) rather than a graded physical/functional realizer — so the sorites series never supplies a graded truth-maker for consciousness. This is the article's constructive move.
- **Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor has limits)** — the pro-vagueness arguments lean on the intuition that any sharp line "in nature" is arbitrary/unparsimonious. The Map's Tenet 5 licenses resisting that intuition: with consciousness a non-physical addition, a sharp boundary need not be an arbitrary physical cutoff.
