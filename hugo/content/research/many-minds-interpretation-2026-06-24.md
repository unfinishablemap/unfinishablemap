## Executive Summary

The Many-Minds Interpretation (MMI) is the one mainstream interpretation of quantum mechanics that puts *minds* explicitly into the formalism. Where the Many-Worlds Interpretation (MWI) splits the physical universe at each measurement, MMI keeps physical reality single and unbranching, and locates the multiplicity in the observer's mental life: each sentient being is associated with a continuous infinity of minds, and the minds—not the world—distribute themselves across measurement outcomes. The canonical version is Albert and Loewer (1988, *Synthese*); a physicalism-preserving rival is Lockwood (1996, *BJPS*); the decoherence-rooted ancestor is Zeh (1970, 1981, 2000). MMI is acutely relevant to the Unfinishable Map because (a) it is a direct competitor to Tenet 4 (No Many Worlds) that *retains a single physical world* while still multiplying experiencers; (b) Albert and Loewer's version is explicitly **non-supervenient and quasi-dualist**, which both echoes and pressures the Map's [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) tenet; and (c) its central failures—the "mindless hulks" problem, the collapse of diachronic personal identity, and an unusable notion of probability—map almost exactly onto the Map's own indexical objection to MWI, but turned against a mind-based theory. MMI is a sharp test case: it shows that simply adding minds to Everett does **not** rescue indexical identity, which strengthens the Map's case that you need *genuine collapse plus a determinate self*, not just minds-in-superposition.

## Key Sources

### Many-minds interpretation — Wikipedia
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Many-minds_interpretation
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - "Rather than the worlds that branch, it is the observer's mind that branches." Each observer has "an infinity of minds, whose prevalence correspond to the amplitude of the wavefunction."
  - First introduced by H. Dieter Zeh (1970) as an Everett variant tied to decoherence; named/developed by Albert & Loewer (1988); reformulated by Lockwood (1996).
  - Albert & Loewer's motivation: "the mind of an observer cannot be in an indefinite state because an observer must answer the question about which state of a system he has observed with complete certainty." Resolution: minds stay definite; only bodies are superposed.
  - Lists criticisms: non-locality / Bell, the "mindless hulks" objection, conservation-law worries, the quantum-suicide paradox (Peter J. Lewis 2000), and the need to either drop the strong Church–Turing thesis or supply a physical model of consciousness.
  - "There is currently no empirical evidence for the many-minds interpretation."
- **Tenet alignment**: Partial. Single unbranching physical world is friendlier to Tenet 4 than MWI; but minds remain *passive distributors over* unitary outcomes, not *causal selectors of* them — conflicts with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction).

### Everettian Interpretations of Quantum Mechanics — Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/everett/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (peer-reviewed)
- **Key points**:
  - **Single-mind view**: each observer has *one* mind whose mental state evolves *probabilistically* (50/50 on a fair spin), even though the brain stays superposed. Albert & Loewer concede this forces dualism: "one cannot tell from the state of a brain what its single mind believes" (Albert and Loewer 1988: 206).
  - **Mindless hulks** (Barrett 1999): in a multi-observer superposition, all terms but one are "mindless brains" — physically full observers with *no* associated mind. There is no fact about which branch carries the mind; introspection and memory become unreliable (Barrett 1999: 187–190).
  - **Many-minds view** fixes mindless hulks by populating *every* term with minds — at the cost of an infinity of minds per observer and continued dualism: mental states are "non-physical and non-supervenient on physical states except at the 'global' level."
  - A noted inconsistency: only the *global* mental state obeys QM; the *local* state an observer actually introspects does not.
  - Probability = "the proportion of minds associated with each term" (Albert and Loewer 1988: 208). Critics (Barrett; Loewer 1996) judge this "puzzling and insufficient" as a guide to rational prediction.
- **Tenet alignment**: The single-mind version's admitted dualism resonates with Tenet 1; but it is a *consequence the authors regret*, not a feature, and the minds are epiphenomenal riders, not causes.

### Many-Worlds Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics — Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-manyworlds/ (Vaidman, ongoing)
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Albert & Loewer posit "every sentient being has a continuum of minds"; on measurement "the minds of this sentient being evolve randomly and independently into mental states corresponding to these different perceptions (with probabilities equal to the quantum probabilities)."
  - "Each 'I' corresponds to a single mind, and it ends up in a state corresponding to a world with a particular outcome" — so each mind has a definite, single-outcome experience.
  - The cost, in Vaidman's framing: "this solution comes at the price of introducing additional structure into the theory, including a genuinely random process" — i.e. MMI reintroduces the fundamental randomness MWI was meant to eliminate.
  - Explicit terminological warning: "the MMI of Albert and Loewer (1988) … should not be confused with the MMI of Lockwood et al. (1996)"; the Lockwood version "resembles the approach of Zeh (2000)."
- **Tenet alignment**: The "genuinely random process" cost is exactly what the Map's interface model converts into *consciousness-biased* (non-random) selection — a clean contrast point.

### 'Many Minds' Interpretations of Quantum Mechanics — Michael Lockwood (1996, BJPS 47: 159–188)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/LOCMIO (+ "Replies to Replies", BJPS 47: 445–461)
- **Type**: Journal article (target of a full BJPS symposium with Brown, Butterfield, Deutsch, Loewer, Papineau, Saunders)
- **Key points**:
  - Lockwood's program "takes completely at face value the account which unitary quantum mechanics gives of the physical world" and insists on "a fundamental democracy of vector bases in Hilbert space" — *no objectively preferred basis*; both state reduction and the preferred basis are "illusion."
  - Rejects Albert–Loewer dualism: minds are grounded in physical brain processes; an observer has a "Mind" (a "multimind") comprising the minds across all superposition terms, each with a "maximal experience."
  - Concludes it is "senseless to ask which, of the many possible minds, are 'yours'" after a measurement — explicitly aligning with **Parfit** on the meaninglessness of "which person will I become?"
  - Probability as "a naturally preferred measure on sets of simultaneous maximal experiences" with "temporal length" and "superpositional width" — judged to fail as a decision guide because nothing tracks an observer across time (Loewer 1996; Barrett 1999).
- **Tenet alignment**: **Direct conflict with Tenet 4.** Lockwood's Parfitian "no fact about which mind is yours" is precisely the indexical deflation the Map rejects; it is the cleanest articulation of the view the Map argues *against*.

### Albert & Loewer, "Interpreting the Many-Worlds Interpretation" (1988, Synthese 77: 195–213)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/ALBITM
- **Type**: Foundational journal article
- **Key points**: Origin of the single-mind and many-minds proposals; introduces the probabilistic mental-evolution rule and the continuum-of-minds ontology; concedes the dualism and the mind-brain readability problem (p. 206) at the outset.
- **Tenet alignment**: The *acknowledged* non-physicalism is a notable concession from analytic philosophers usually hostile to dualism — citable evidence that taking the measurement problem seriously pushes even physicalists toward mind-involving ontologies (supports Tenet 1's plausibility, not its specifics).

### Self-locating Uncertainty and the Origin of Probability in Everettian QM — Sebens & Carroll (2016, BJPS / arXiv:1405.7577)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1405.7577
- **Type**: Journal article / preprint
- **Key points**: Modern Everettian attempt to ground the Born rule via *self-locating uncertainty* in the window between decoherence-splitting and outcome-registration; argues the Born rule is the uniquely rational credence. McQueen & Vaidman counter that the examples involve *absent* uncertainty, not genuine self-locating uncertainty.
- **Tenet alignment**: Shows the probability problem MMI was invented to solve is *still live* in 2010s–2020s Everettian work — MMI is not a settled fix. Contemporary anchor for the article.

### Personal Identity and Uncertainty in the Everett Interpretation (arXiv:2209.02639, 2022)

## Major Positions

### Single-Mind Interpretation (Albert & Loewer 1988)
- **Proponents**: David Albert, Barry Loewer (originating proposal).
- **Core claim**: One mind per observer; the brain evolves unitarily into superposition, but the *mind* hops stochastically to one definite mental state, with Born-rule probabilities. Physical world never branches.
- **Key arguments**: Preserves determinate experience and ordinary probability talk without collapse; localizes the "split" to the mental.
- **Fatal problem**: Mind no longer supervenes on brain (admitted dualism); in multi-observer cases produces "mindless hulks."
- **Relation to site tenets**: Resonates with **Tenet 1 (Dualism)** by conceding mental states don't reduce to brain states. But the mind is a *passive lottery winner*, not a cause — it violates **Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)**, under which consciousness *biases* outcomes rather than being randomly assigned one. And the world stays single (Tenet 4-friendly) only by smuggling randomness back in.

### Many-Minds Interpretation (Albert & Loewer 1988)
- **Proponents**: Albert, Loewer.
- **Core claim**: Each observer has a *continuous infinity* of minds; on measurement the minds distribute across outcomes in proportion to |amplitude|². Every superposition term gets minds, so no term is a "mindless hulk."
- **Key arguments**: Fixes mindless hulks; recovers Born statistics as a *measure over minds*; single physical world.
- **Problems**: Profligate (infinite minds per person); dualist (minds non-supervenient except "globally"); local introspected state violates QM; probability-as-mind-proportion is not a usable decision guide.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Direct rival to Tenet 4.** It is the strongest "no extra physical worlds, but still many experiencers" position the Map must answer. The Map's indexical objection transfers: even with minds in the formalism, *nothing picks out which mind is the actual continuant "I."* MMI multiplies the indexical problem rather than solving it — a key argumentative win for the Map.

### Lockwood's Many-Minds / "Multimind" (1996)
- **Proponents**: Michael Lockwood; akin to Zeh (2000).
- **Core claim**: No dualism — minds are physical-brain-grounded; one "Mind" spans all maximal experiences; no preferred basis (basis "democracy"); reduction is illusion.
- **Key arguments**: Physicalist-respectable Everettianism; explicitly Parfitian about identity.
- **Problems**: Probability measure (temporal length × superpositional width) fails as a decision guide; "which mind is yours" declared senseless.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **The cleanest statement of the view Tenet 4 rejects.** Lockwood's Parfitian deflation of "which mind is mine" is exactly the non-deflationary "I" the Map defends against. Use Lockwood as the named foil for the indexical argument.

### Zeh's Decoherence-Rooted Multi-Consciousness View (1970, 1981, 2000)
- **Proponents**: H. Dieter Zeh.
- **Core claim**: Consciousness "selects" a branch via entanglement/decoherence; the many-minds reading is "the most natural extension of standard psycho-physical parallelism" once you reduce the universal wave function to a local density operator.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Zeh's "consciousness selects a branch" language *sounds* interface-like, but the selection is epiphenomenal (decoherence does the work; consciousness rides along). Contrast with the Map's *causal* selection.

## Key Debates

### Does MMI require dualism?
- **Sides**: Albert & Loewer (yes — minds are non-supervenient, they accept it) vs. Lockwood/Zeh (no — physicalist many-minds via supervenience on the global state).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether mental states can supervene on a *superposed* brain. Albert–Loewer say a definite mind can't read off a superposed brain, so dualism. Lockwood denies a preferred basis and grounds a "Mind" physically.
- **Current state**: Unresolved; widely seen as MMI's central liability. **Directly load-bearing for the Map**: it shows that taking determinate experience seriously inside unitary QM pushes toward dualism — but a *weak, acausal* dualism the Map should distinguish itself from.

### The "mindless hulks" objection
- **Sides**: Barrett (1999) presses it against single-mind; many-minds is the defensive retreat.
- **Core disagreement**: In a superposition of observer-states, which physical term actually carries a mind? Single-mind says one; the rest are behaviorally-complete zombies ("hulks") with no way to tell from outside.
- **Current state**: Standard objection. **Strong Map parallel** to the [multi-mind-collapse-problem](/concepts/multi-mind-collapse-problem/) and the zombie/[problem-of-other-minds](/concepts/problem-of-other-minds/) literature: the Map's interface-locality answer (each consciousness modulates only its own brain's collapse) sidesteps hulks because collapse is *real and local*, so there is no superposed-observer term to leave un-minded.

### Does MMI solve the probability problem?
- **Sides**: Albert–Loewer/Lockwood (yes, via measure over minds) vs. Barrett, Loewer-1996, and the later Oxonian/decision-theoretic and self-locating-uncertainty camps (no — measure-over-minds isn't an *uncertainty* a rational agent can act on).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "half my future minds see up" is a *probability* an agent can use, given all outcomes occur.
- **Current state**: Live into the 2020s (Sebens–Carroll vs. McQueen–Vaidman). MMI did not close it.

### Diachronic personal identity / "which mind am I?"
- **Sides**: Lockwood + Parfit (senseless question) vs. the Map (a meaningful, non-deflationary "I").
- **Core disagreement**: Whether there is a *further fact* about which successor-mind is the genuine continuant.
- **Current state**: This is the crux. The Map's **indexical objection to MWI applies verbatim to MMI** — adding minds to the formalism doesn't supply the missing fact about *which* mind is mine.
