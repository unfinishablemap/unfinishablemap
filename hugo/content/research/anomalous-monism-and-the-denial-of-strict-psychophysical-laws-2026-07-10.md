## Executive Summary

Anomalous monism is Donald Davidson's attempt to be a physicalist about mental events (each mental event token IS a physical event token) while denying that there are any strict psychophysical laws linking the mental and the physical. Davidson reaches this conclusion by arguing that three plausible principles — that some mental events causally interact with physical events, that singular causal relations are backed by strict laws, and that there are no strict laws in which mental predicates figure ("the anomalism of the mental") — are jointly consistent only if mental events are token-identical to physical events falling under strict physical laws. The mental supervenes on the physical without being reducible to it. The position is a sharp, named rival to any law-based program that posits a strict psychophysical bridge law, yet a subtle one: Davidson AGREES that strict reductive psychophysical laws are unavailable, but concludes the mental is lawless-yet-physical rather than governed by a special interface law. The dominant objection since 1982 (Honderich, then Kim, Sosa, McLaughlin) is that anomalous monism makes mental PROPERTIES causally idle — an epiphenomenalism of the mental qua mental — because events cause only via their strict-law-instantiating physical properties.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Anomalous Monism" (Yalowitz)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/anomalous-monism/
- **Author**: Steven Yalowitz
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (peer-reviewed reference)
- **First published**: 2005-11-08; **substantive revision**: 2025-05-16 (verified on entry page)
- **Key points**:
  - States Davidson's three principles as the Principle of Causal Interaction, the Principle of the Nomological Character of Causality (the "cause-law principle"), and the Principle of the Anomalism of the Mental.
  - Token-identity is DERIVED, not assumed: if a mental event causally interacts, it must fall under a strict law; since no strict law contains mental predicates, it must instantiate a physical property and so be token-identical to a physical event.
  - Supervenience: no two events can differ mentally without differing physically, giving dependence without reduction or bridge laws.
  - Catalogs the "mental properties and causal relevance" objection and names Kim, Honderich, Sosa, McLaughlin as critics.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with the Map's Tenet 2/3 program (a strict/selection psychophysical law); aligns with the Map's skepticism about strict REDUCTIVE psychophysical laws.
- **Note**: The SEP entry has dedicated sub-pages ("Mental Properties and Causal Relevance", "Mental Causation and Supervenience") that a downstream expand-topic can mine for the objection dialectic.

### Davidson, "Mental Events" (1970)
- **Original venue (verified)**: In L. Foster & J. W. Swanson (eds.), *Experience and Theory*, Amherst: University of Massachusetts Press, 1970, pp. 79-101.
- **Canonical reprint (verified)**: In Davidson, *Essays on Actions and Events*, Oxford: Clarendon Press / Oxford University Press, 1980, ch. 11 (widely cited as pp. 207-224).
- **Type**: Primary paper / book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - Introduces all three principles and the reconciliation via token identity.
  - "The mental is anomalous" = no strict laws (psychophysical or purely psychological) permit precise prediction/explanation of the mental.
  - Grounds anomalism in the CONSTITUTIVE role of rationality/holism: ascribing beliefs and desires is governed by norms of coherence and charity that have "no echo" in physical theory, so the two vocabularies cannot be locked together by law.
- **Tenet alignment**: Direct rival on the existence of a strict psychophysical law; shares the anti-reductionist diagnosis.

### Kim, *Mind in a Physical World* (1998)
- **Citation (verified)**: Jaegwon Kim, *Mind in a Physical World: An Essay on the Mind-Body Problem and Mental Causation*, Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 1998. (Based on the 1996 Townsend Lectures.)

### Honderich, "The Argument for Anomalous Monism" (1982)
- **Citation (verified)**: Ted Honderich, "The Argument for Anomalous Monism," *Analysis*, Vol. 42, No. 1 (Jan. 1982), pp. 59-64. DOI: 10.1093/analys/42.1.59. (University College London.)
- **Key points**: First and most-cited statement of the "anomalous monism is epiphenomenalism in disguise" objection. Argues not all of an event's properties are causally relevant; since only the strict-law-instantiating (physical) properties are nomically efficacious, the mental properties do no causal work. Proposes reframing the cause-law principle as the "Principle of the Nomological Character of Causally Relevant Properties," which rules out the causal relevance of anomic mental properties.
- **Tenet alignment**: The pivotal objection; sharpens the Map's contrast (the Map WANTS mental properties to be causally relevant and locates that relevance at the quantum interface).

### Sosa, "Mind-Body Interaction and Supervenient Causation" (1984)
- **Citation (verified)**: Ernest Sosa, "Mind-Body Interaction and Supervenient Causation," *Midwest Studies in Philosophy*, Vol. 9, No. 1 (1984), pp. 271-281. DOI: 10.1111/j.1475-4975.1984.tb00064.x.
- **Key points**: Presses the causal-relevance worry via a bomb/shot example: an event causes an effect in virtue of some of its properties but not others; anomalous monism seems to make the mental property the causally irrelevant one.
- **Tenet alignment**: Reinforces Honderich; supplies the standard intuition pump for the objection.

### Davidson, "Thinking Causes" (1993)
- **Citation (verified)**: Donald Davidson, "Thinking Causes," in J. Heil & A. Mele (eds.), *Mental Causation*, Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1993, pp. 3-17. (Replies by Kim, McLaughlin, and Sosa appear in the same volume.)
- **Key points**: Davidson's rebuttal. He denies the critics' shared premise that events cause "in virtue of" their properties — for Davidson, causation is a relation between events, extensional and description-independent; properties enter at the level of EXPLANATION, not causation. He also argues mental efficacy is secured a priori because the interaction principle already asserts it.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/instructive — shows the escape route (deny property-causation) that the Map does not need, because the Map affirms a real property-level (indeed quantum-level) mental influence.

## Major Positions

### Anomalous Monism (Davidson)
- **Proponents**: Donald Davidson; sympathetic reconstructions by Yalowitz.
- **Core claim**: Ontological monism (every mental event token = a physical event token) PLUS conceptual/nomological dualism (mental predicates are not law-linked to physical predicates). The mental supervenes on but is irreducible to the physical.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. The three-principle reconciliation (interaction + nomological causality + anomalism → token identity).
  2. The constitutive-ideal-of-rationality argument for anomalism: mental ascription is holistic and norm-governed in a way physical description is not, so no strict bridge laws are possible.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A NAMED RIVAL to the Map's law program. The Map (Tenets 2 and 3) posits a real, minimal, bidirectional psychophysical influence — plausibly a probabilistic SELECTION LAW at the quantum measurement interface. Davidson denies any strict psychophysical law exists. Crucial nuance: they converge on the failure of strict REDUCTIVE bridge laws but diverge on the verdict — Davidson: mental is anomalous yet physical; Map: there is a non-strict/probabilistic interface law that is neither reductive nor absent. Davidson is a physicalist and rejects dualism (Tenet 1); the Map is dualist. So the note should present anomalous monism as the sophisticated physicalist alternative the Map must out-argue on causal-relevance grounds.

### The Epiphenomenalist Reading (Honderich, Kim, Sosa, McLaughlin)
- **Proponents**: Ted Honderich (1982, originator), Jaegwon Kim (1989, 1993, 1998), Ernest Sosa (1984), Brian McLaughlin (1989, "Type Epiphenomenalism...").
- **Core claim**: Anomalous monism secures the causal efficacy of mental EVENTS but not of mental PROPERTIES. Because only physical (strict-law-instantiating) properties are nomically efficacious, an event's mentality does no causal work — "type" or "property" epiphenomenalism.
- **Key arguments**: Not all properties of a cause are causally relevant; causal relevance tracks nomic/lawlike properties; mental properties are anomic; therefore mental properties are causally irrelevant. Kim generalizes this into the supervenience/exclusion argument.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly useful to the Map. The objection shows that a token-physicalism that denies psychophysical laws struggles to keep the mental causally relevant. The Map turns this into a positive motivation: to make consciousness genuinely causally relevant (Tenet 3, bidirectional interaction) you arguably NEED some psychophysical law-like link — precisely what Davidson forgoes. The Map's minimal-quantum-interaction proposal (Tenet 2) is one way to buy back property-level relevance without full reduction.

### Davidson's Defense (property-causation denial)
- **Proponent**: Davidson ("Thinking Causes," 1993).
- **Core claim**: The objection presupposes that causation is "in virtue of" properties; deny that, and epiphenomenalism does not follow — events cause simpliciter, and mental descriptions remain explanatorily good.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map should note this reply exists and is contested (many find it changes the subject from causal relevance to bare event-causation). The Map does not take this exit; it wants robust property-level mental influence.

## Key Debates

### Does anomalous monism entail epiphenomenalism of the mental?
- **Sides**: Critics (Honderich, Kim, Sosa, McLaughlin) say yes at the level of mental PROPERTIES; Davidson (and defenders) say the charge conflates causation with explanation and misreads his event-based causal relation.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether causal relevance requires properties to be law-linked, and whether "causation in virtue of properties" is even the right framework.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and canonical; widely regarded as the central pressure point on anomalous monism. No consensus; most take the objection to have real force absent Davidson's controversial extensionalism about causation.

### Is anomalism really established by the constitutive ideal of rationality?
- **Sides**: Davidson/Yalowitz defend the holism-and-normativity route to anomalism; critics question whether irreducible normativity really precludes strict laws.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the normative/holistic character of intentional ascription blocks lawlike psychophysical connections or merely makes them messy.
- **Current state**: Live; connects to broader debates on the autonomy of psychology.
