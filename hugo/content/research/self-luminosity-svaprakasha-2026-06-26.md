### 1. Advaita defense — the anti-regress argument for self-luminosity

Covered at argument-level depth, not in passing, in **three** places:

- `topics/essential-vs-contingent-consciousness.md` (§"Advaita: The Cleanest Essential View", l.52–56) states the regress argument *and* its dialectical weak point: "Self-luminosity therefore is not an empirical finding but the terminating condition of a regress — which is exactly why the contingentist can reject it without contradicting any datum: deny that the regress must terminate in self-luminosity (perhaps it terminates in a causal condition instead), and the argument loses its grip." That is the sharpest formulation of the defense *and* its rebuttal anywhere a dedicated page would aim for.
- `topics/indian-philosophy-of-mind.md` (l.100): "if a primary cognition required a second apperceptive cognition to be known, the second would require a third, generating an infinite regress. Self-luminosity terminates the regress."
- `topics/the-observer-witness-in-meditation.md` (l.139) and `concepts/witness-consciousness.md` (l.163, "The Regress Objection") give the lamp-illuminates-itself form with the phenomenological gloss.

### 2. Nyaya rejection — consciousness as adventitious, regress terminated by apperception

`topics/essential-vs-contingent-consciousness.md` (§"Nyaya: Consciousness as Adventitious Quality", l.58–64) is effectively the dedicated treatment: consciousness as *agantuka guna* (adventitious quality), arising on *manas–atman* contact; and crucially the **rival regress-terminator**: "Where Advaita's regress argument terminates in a self-revealing consciousness, Nyaya terminates the regress in a causal condition — apperception (*anuvyavasaya*), a distinct later cognition directed at the prior one… Consciousness of consciousness is a further event, not a built-in luminosity." It also gives the mirror-image dreamless-sleep reading. `indian-philosophy-of-mind.md` (l.92) states the rejection explicitly. This is the focused Nyaya-rejection the steering asked whether the corpus had — it does.

### 3. Buddhist reflexivity — *svasaṃvedana* and the Mādhyamaka rejection

Two articles carry this at dedicated depth (and it is the genuinely distinct large literature the steering flagged as the strongest candidate for warranting its own page — yet it is already present):

- `topics/the-hard-problem-in-non-western-philosophy.md` (l.82–86): Dignāga (c. 480–540 CE) and Dharmakīrti (c. 600–660 CE) on *svasaṃvedana* — "every cognitive event involves a pre-reflective awareness of its own occurrence" (Coseru cited verbatim) — **and** the intra-Buddhist contest: "The Madhyamaka school… rejects reflexive self-awareness, arguing that it creates an infinite regress and is incompatible with emptiness (*śūnyatā*)."

This is the precise content a dedicated svasamvedana page would carry.
