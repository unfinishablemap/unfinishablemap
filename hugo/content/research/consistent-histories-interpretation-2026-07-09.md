## Executive Summary

Consistent Histories (CH), also called Decoherent Histories, is a single-world, realist interpretation of quantum mechanics that removes measurement, observers, and wave-function collapse from the foundations. It treats quantum theory as a stochastic theory of *histories* — time-sequences of properties represented by projection operators — and assigns ordinary (interference-free) probabilities only to families of histories that satisfy a mathematical "consistency" (or "decoherence") condition. Its most contested feature is *framework-dependence*: many mutually incompatible consistent families exist for the same system, and the theory offers no law selecting a preferred one. Griffiths' "single-framework rule" forbids combining incompatible frameworks (blocking contradictions) but treats the choice among them as pragmatic, question-relative — a stance Dowker & Kent (1996) argue leaves the theory without unconditional predictive power. CH is a serious rival to consciousness-linked interpretations (it needs no observer) and simultaneously a single-world ally against Many-Worlds (it rejects the physical reality of the superposition).

## Key Sources

### The Consistent Histories Approach to Quantum Mechanics (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-consistent-histories/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Author**: Robert B. Griffiths (the interpretation's originator is also the SEP entry's author — note the self-authored status when leaning on it; it presents CH sympathetically). First published 2014-08-07; substantive revision 2024-06-19.
- **Key points**:
  - A quantum *history* is a temporal succession of events, each an eigenvalue/property represented by a projector; formally a product of projectors at successive times acting on a "history Hilbert space."
  - A *framework* is a projective decomposition of the identity (PDI) — a set of mutually orthogonal projectors summing to the identity — that supplies a probabilistic sample space.
  - Four principles: R1 Liberty (multiple frameworks may be used), R2 Equality (no framework is more fundamental), R3 Incompatibility (incompatible frameworks are *never* combined into one description), R4 Utility (choice is guided by the question asked). The article calls this "pluricity" vs classical "unicity."
  - Positions itself as "Copenhagen done right": measurement loses fundamental status, all time-evolution is stochastic via the Born rule, collapse is a "handy calculational tool" not a physical process.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with the Map's consciousness-collapse leanings (no observer role); aligns with the Map's rejection of Many-Worlds (single world, superposition denied physical reality).
- **Quote**: "There is no measurement problem, and under appropriate conditions one can discuss the microscopic properties revealed by measurements."

### Griffiths, R. B. (1984), "Consistent histories and the interpretation of quantum mechanics"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF01015734
- **Type**: Paper (Journal of Statistical Physics 36:219–272; DOI 10.1007/BF01015734)
- **Key points**: Origin paper. Generalizes transition-probability formula to conditional probabilities for selected sequences of events at several times ("consistent histories"), via a criterion guaranteeing classical probability rules hold. Applicable to closed systems, time-symmetric (past/future interchangeable), no collapse, no reference to measurement.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/conflicts — foundational apparatus is observer-free.
- **VERIFIED**: Publisher of record (Springer), pages 219–272, 1984.

### Griffiths, R. B. (2002), *Consistent Quantum Theory*
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/ (Cambridge University Press) — record at https://philpapers.org/rec/GRICQT
- **Type**: Book (Cambridge University Press, 2002)
- **Key points**: Book-length systematic development. The "single-framework rule" is presented as the precise mathematical statement of Bohr's complementarity: any prediction must be confined to a single framework; combining elements from different (incompatible) frameworks yields quantum-mechanically meaningless statements. Within a single framework ordinary classical logic holds; different frameworks can support different conclusions.
- **VERIFIED**: CUP 2002.

### Omnès, R. (1994), *The Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics*
- **URL**: https://press.princeton.edu/ — see https://philpapers.org/rec/OMNUQM (companion volume "Understanding Quantum Mechanics," 1999)
- **Type**: Book (Princeton University Press, 1994; Princeton Series in Physics, 576 pp.)
- **Key points**: Develops CH into a "consistent revision of the Copenhagen interpretation," adding logical rules for meaningful quantum statements ("true"/"reliable" properties within a framework) and integrating decoherence to recover classical logic in the macroscopic regime. Omnès' series of papers began 1988.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral — Copenhagen-descended but observer-free.
- **VERIFIED**: Princeton UP 1994.

### Gell-Mann, M. & Hartle, J. B. (1990), "Quantum Mechanics in the Light of Quantum Cosmology"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/GELQMI (in *Complexity, Entropy, and the Physics of Information*, ed. W. H. Zurek, Addison-Wesley, 1990)
- **Type**: Book chapter / conference paper (also in Proc. 3rd Int. Symp. Foundations of QM, Tokyo, 1990)
- **Key points**: Independent "decoherent histories" formulation. Sum-over-histories quantum cosmology in which the quasiclassical domain of everyday experience is *derived* from a decoherence process among alternative coarse-grained histories of a closed universe (ultimately, the universe as the only truly closed system). Introduces decoherence functional; "decoherence" and "consistency" conditions are closely related (medium decoherence implies consistency).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/conflicts — explicitly cosmological and observer-free; the universe needs no external observer.
- **VERIFIED**: Publisher/editor confirmed (Zurek, ed., Addison-Wesley 1990).

### Dowker, F. & Kent, A. (1996), "On the Consistent Histories Approach to Quantum Mechanics"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF02183396 (preprint arXiv:gr-qc/9412067)
- **Type**: Paper (Journal of Statistical Physics 82:1575–1646; DOI 10.1007/BF02183396)
- **Key points**: The canonical critique. Reviews the Griffiths / Omnès / Gell-Mann–Hartle formulations and classifies consistent sets. Argues: (i) the formalism treats all consistent families "democratically" and gives no criterion selecting a preferred one; (ii) with no law of nature fixing the framework there is no way to compute unconditional probabilities of specific histories (probabilities are always framework-conditional); (iii) Omnès' characterization of "true" statements is defective; (iv) Gell-Mann–Hartle's interpretation is critically examined. Conclusion: CH must be *supplemented* by some set-selection principle to become a fundamental theory capable of unconditional predictions — Kent's later "set-selection problem."
- **Tenet alignment**: Instrumentally useful to the Map — its "no rule selects the framework" gap is exactly the crack the Map's downstream angle probes (see Map relevance).
- **VERIFIED**: Publisher of record (Springer), JSP 82:1575–1646, 1996; arXiv gr-qc/9412067.

## Major Positions

### Consistent / Decoherent Histories (Griffiths, Omnès, Gell-Mann & Hartle)
- **Proponents**: Robert Griffiths (originator, 1984), Roland Omnès (logical development, from 1988), Murray Gell-Mann & James Hartle (decoherent histories / quantum cosmology, 1990).
- **Core claim**: Quantum mechanics is a stochastic theory of histories of a closed system. Probabilities attach to *consistent* families of histories — those whose interference cross-terms vanish (the consistency/decoherence condition), so the classical additivity rules for probability apply. No collapse, no fundamental measurement, no fundamental observer.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The measurement problem dissolves: "measurement" is just one kind of physical interaction analyzable within a suitable framework; apparatus and observer are ordinary quantum systems.
  - Decoherence (environmental entanglement) is what typically renders macroscopic, "quasiclassical" families consistent, explaining the emergence of the classical world without adding structure.
  - The single-framework rule blocks the standard no-go paradoxes (e.g., combining incompatible property assertions) by forbidding cross-framework reasoning.
- **Relation to site tenets**:
  - *Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)* and consciousness-collapse: **rival**. CH removes any fundamental role for consciousness; outcomes are fixed by the stochastic dynamics within a framework, not by a mind.
  - *Tenet 4 (No Many-Worlds)*: **ally**. CH is explicitly single-world; only one history in a family is realized, and superpositions are denied the status of physical coexistence. It rejects MWI without paying the collapse or hidden-variable price.
  - *Tenet 1 (Dualism)* / *Tenet 3 (Bidirectional)*: **no support and mild tension** — CH is a self-contained physicalist ontology with a closed causal story. The Map must engage it as a genuine competitor, not fold it into dualism.

### Framework Realism vs Framework Anti-Realism (internal debate about CH)
- **Proponents**: Griffiths reads CH realistically (frameworks are objectively available "views" of a single reality; noncontextual within a framework); critics (Dowker & Kent, and others) read the framework proliferation as sliding toward instrumentalism or arbitrariness.
- **Core claim (Griffiths' realist reading)**: There is one world with definite (framework-relative) properties; multiplicity of frameworks is like multiplicity of coordinate systems, not multiplicity of worlds. No framework is privileged, but that is a feature (quantum "pluricity"), not a defect.
- **Key arguments**: Griffiths answers the "which framework?" worry pragmatically — probabilities are *conditional* on a chosen framework the way classical probabilities are conditional on a chosen sample space; no "law of nature" need pick one, and demanding one imports a classical unicity assumption CH rejects.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The realism-vs-anti-realism seam is where the Map's angle lives (below). If frameworks are objectively real yet unselected by physics, *something* extra-physical could in principle do the selecting — but CH proponents deny anything need select at all, so the Map must argue *for* a selection need, not assume it.

## Key Debates

### The Framework / Set-Selection Problem
- **Sides**: Griffiths (and CH proponents) hold no preferred framework is needed — probabilities are legitimately framework-conditional. Dowker & Kent (and Kent's subsequent work) hold that without a set-selection principle CH cannot make unconditional predictions and so fails as a *fundamental* theory.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether a physical theory must deliver framework-independent, unconditional probabilities for events, or whether framework-conditional probabilities plus the single-framework rule are enough.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and unresolved. No consensus set-selection principle has been found; CH remains a live minority interpretation, and the critique is widely regarded as unanswered rather than refuted.

### "Copenhagen done right" vs residual instrumentalism
- **Sides**: CH claims to give Copenhagen a realist, observer-free reconstruction. Critics ask whether the reliance on question-relative frameworks smuggles the observer back in as the (unmodeled) agent who picks the framework.
- **Core disagreement**: Is framework choice a harmless pragmatic step (like coordinate choice) or a covert reintroduction of an agent/observer at the foundations?
- **Current state**: Unresolved; this is precisely the seam the Map can exploit without over-claiming.
