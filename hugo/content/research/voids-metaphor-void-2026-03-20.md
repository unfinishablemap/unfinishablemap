# Research: Voids - The Metaphor Void

**Date**: 2026-03-20
**Search queries used**: "Lakoff Johnson conceptual metaphor cognitive limits philosophy", "metaphor in philosophy of mind consciousness limits of metaphorical thinking", "Blumenberg metaphorology nonconceptuality absolute metaphor", "Derrida White Mythology metaphor philosophy limits", "Ricoeur semantic innovation limits of literal language", "Wittgenstein limits of language metaphor", "Julian Jaynes consciousness metaphor Origin of Consciousness", "apophatic theology negative language limits metaphor mysticism ineffability"
**Voids category**: Mixed (Unexplorable / Occluded)

## Executive Summary

Every attempt to describe a cognitive void relies on metaphor — "blind spots," "edges," "dark spaces," "boundaries," "depths." This dependence is not merely stylistic but structural: we cannot describe what lies beyond thought without borrowing spatial, sensory, and navigational concepts from what lies within it. The metaphor void names the possibility that this dependence distorts our understanding of cognitive limits — that the metaphorical medium shapes what we can discover about the territory it claims to describe. Multiple philosophical traditions converge on this problem: Blumenberg's "absolute metaphors" that resist conceptual replacement, Derrida's demonstration that philosophy cannot escape its own figuration, Ricoeur's insight that metaphor operates precisely where literal language fails, and Jaynes's radical claim that consciousness itself is a metaphorical construction. For the Unfinishable Map's voids project, this raises a reflexive challenge: the cartographic metaphor that structures the entire enterprise may constrain what kinds of cognitive limits can be discovered.

## Key Sources

### Lakoff & Johnson — Conceptual Metaphor Theory
- **URL**: https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/M/bo3637992.html
- **Type**: Book / Theory
- **Key points**:
  - "Our ordinary conceptual system, in terms of which we both think and act, is fundamentally metaphorical in nature"
  - Abstract concepts are structured by systematic mappings from concrete, embodied domains (space, motion, force, containers)
  - The cognitive unconscious plays a central role — most metaphorical structuring operates below awareness
  - Our hidden metaphors create "cognitive slavery — an unaware and uncritical dependence on our unconscious metaphors"
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — the conceptual metaphor framework suggests that what appears to be direct, literal understanding of abstract domains is always metaphorically mediated, meaning our seemingly simple grasp of concepts hides irreducible complexity
- **Quote**: "The assumption that we have radically autonomous rationality actually limits our rational autonomy"

### Hans Blumenberg — Paradigms for a Metaphorology / Theory of Nonconceptuality
- **URL**: https://www.cornellpress.cornell.edu/book/9781501704352/paradigms-for-a-metaphorology/
- **Type**: Book / Philosophy
- **Key points**:
  - "Absolute metaphors" are metaphors that cannot be replaced by literal concepts — they are not preliminary approximations but the only available mode of expression
  - "Nonconceptuality" (Unbegrifflichkeit): perceptions and experiences that do not lend themselves to univocal concepts, inviting expression only through metaphor, myth, and symbol
  - "The function of 'absolute metaphor' springs into a nonconceptualizable, conceptually unfillable gap and lacuna"
  - Later Blumenberg: metaphorics are not a preliminary stage of concept formation but "an authentic way of grasping connections"
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — if consciousness involves genuinely nonconceptual dimensions, then the gap that absolute metaphors fill may correspond to the irreducibility of phenomenal experience to physical/functional description
- **Quote**: "The function of 'absolute metaphor' springs into a nonconceptualizable, conceptually unfillable gap and lacuna"

### Jacques Derrida — "White Mythology: Metaphor in the Text of Philosophy" (1971)
- **URL**: https://tfreeman.net/resources/Phil-480/derrida-white-mythology.pdf
- **Type**: Essay / Deconstruction
- **Key points**:
  - Philosophy is a "white mythology" — a mythology that has forgotten itself, appearing as universal reason while carrying traces of Indo-European myth and image
  - Philosophical concepts are metaphors "polished to the point of invisibility" — like coins whose designs have been worn away
  - The concept of metaphor is itself a metaphor (metaphora = "transfer") — one cannot step outside figuration to classify it
  - Any attempt to construct a general theory of metaphor collapses because "there will always be at least one metaphor — the 'metaphor of metaphor' — that escapes systematization"
- **Tenet alignment**: Occam's Razor Has Limits — Derrida's argument reveals that the seemingly clean, simple distinction between literal and figurative language is itself a philosophical construction, not a natural joint
- **Quote**: "Philosophy is a white mythology... metaphysics and metaphor are locked in reciprocal antagonism: philosophy cannot do without metaphor, yet it continually denies this dependence"

### Paul Ricoeur — The Rule of Metaphor
- **URL**: https://www.routledge.com/The-Rule-of-Metaphor-The-Creation-of-Meaning-in-Language/Ricoeur/p/book/9780415312806
- **Type**: Book / Hermeneutics
- **Key points**:
  - Metaphor begins where literal language reaches its limits — it is an act of "semantic innovation"
  - The "nameless act": a new meaning is created precisely because no conventional word exists to replace the metaphorical one
  - Metaphor is not decorative substitution but fundamentally creative — it discloses new realities that literal language cannot access
  - Language "extends itself to its very limits, forever discovering new resonances within itself"
- **Tenet alignment**: Bidirectional Interaction — Ricoeur's "semantic innovation" suggests that metaphor is not passive representation but an active cognitive operation that shapes what can be thought. If consciousness genuinely influences physical processes, metaphor might be one mechanism by which mind extends its representational reach
- **Quote**: "A new meaning association has been created through the semantic gap between the objects"

### Julian Jaynes — The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind
- **URL**: https://www.julianjaynes.org/about/about-jaynes-theory/overview/
- **Type**: Book / Psychology-Philosophy
- **Key points**:
  - Consciousness "arises from language, and specifically from metaphor" — the most radical claim about metaphor's constitutive role
  - "The subjective conscious mind is an operator generated by metaphors" — we understand by finding metaphors, stretching finite language over infinite circumstances
  - Critic W. T. Jones: "language (and specifically metaphor) does not create, it discovers, the similarities that language marks" — the question of whether metaphor is constitutive or revelatory
  - Whether metaphor creates consciousness or merely describes it is itself a void
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — if consciousness is constituted by metaphor (Jaynes), this is a form of functionalism. If metaphor merely describes consciousness (Jones's critique), this preserves the possibility that consciousness is something metaphor gestures toward but cannot capture
- **Quote**: "The finite set of the lexicon of language is stretched over an infinite set of circumstances"

### Wittgenstein — Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/wittgenstein/
- **Type**: Treatise / Analytic Philosophy
- **Key points**:
  - "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world" — language constrains the thinkable
  - Distinguished between what signs *say* and what they *show* — what is shown cannot be said
  - The Tractatus itself is paradoxically nonsensical by its own criteria — "a ladder to be thrown away" after climbing
  - Ethics, aesthetics, and the mystical lie beyond the limits of language but can be "shown"
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism — Wittgenstein's saying/showing distinction maps onto the gap between third-person description and first-person experience. Phenomenal consciousness may be something that can only be shown, never said
- **Quote**: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent"

### Apophatic Theology — The Via Negativa
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apophatic_theology
- **Type**: Theological tradition
- **Key points**:
  - Approaches the transcendent through negation — saying what it is not, rather than what it is
  - Pseudo-Dionysius: the most truthful discourse is ultimately silence, after all affirmations *and* negations have been exhausted
  - Even negative statements are metaphorical — "God is not X" still positions God within a framework where X is meaningful
  - The apophatic tradition recognises that metaphor fails in both directions: positive metaphors distort by analogy, negative metaphors distort by contrast
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism + Occam's Razor Has Limits — if consciousness shares features with the theological "wholly other," then apophatic approaches to consciousness may be more honest than positive metaphorical description. Simplicity-seeking (Occam) biases us toward positive, tractable descriptions

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit
This is a mixed void — partly unexplorable, partly occluded.

**Unexplorable dimension**: We cannot think about cognitive limits without metaphor because abstract thought is metaphorically structured at a fundamental level (Lakoff & Johnson). This is not a limitation we can overcome through effort or ingenuity; it reflects the architecture of human cognition itself. We cannot bootstrap our way to a non-metaphorical description of the limits of metaphor.

**Occluded dimension**: The specific metaphors we use may actively hide certain features of cognitive limits. Spatial metaphors ("boundaries," "edges," "blind spots") imply that voids are like physical absences — defined regions with borders. But cognitive limits might not have borders at all. They might be more like gradients, phase transitions, or recursive failures. The spatial framing occludes these possibilities not by making them unthinkable but by making them systematically harder to notice.

### Evidence for the Limit
1. **The bootstrapping problem**: Describing the metaphor void requires metaphors. Even "void" is a spatial metaphor. Any attempt to identify the distortions introduced by metaphor must use metaphorical language to do so — creating a regress that Derrida identified as the "metaphor of metaphor" problem.

2. **Convergence of independent traditions**: Blumenberg (metaphorology), Derrida (deconstruction), Ricoeur (hermeneutics), the apophatic tradition (theology), and Lakoff/Johnson (cognitive linguistics) all independently conclude that certain domains of thought are irreducibly metaphorical. This convergence from distinct intellectual lineages suggests a genuine structural feature rather than a disciplinary artifact.

3. **The poverty of literal alternatives**: Attempts to describe consciousness in purely literal, technical language (e.g., "information integration," "global workspace access") do not eliminate metaphor but merely substitute spatial and computational metaphors for experiential ones. The metaphor shifts but never disappears.

4. **The Unfinishable Map's own methodology**: The voids project itself relies pervasively on cartographic metaphor — "mapping," "territory," "dark spaces," "edges," "charting." These are not optional stylistic choices; they structure what counts as a void, what counts as progress, and what counts as the project's limit. A void that cannot be mapped — that resists cartographic framing entirely — would be invisible to the project as currently conceived.

### Phenomenology
Approaching this limit feels like trying to see the lens through which you are looking. There is a characteristic experience of infinite regress: each attempt to identify a metaphorical distortion produces a new metaphor, which introduces its own distortions. The phenomenology is not one of hitting a wall but of infinite recession — each step of analysis reveals another layer of figuration beneath. This distinguishes the metaphor void from most other voids, which manifest as opacity or aversion. The metaphor void manifests as false transparency — the illusion that you have succeeded in describing a limit clearly, when you have only described it *metaphorically* and cannot determine what the metaphor distorts.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)
Ricoeur's method of "semantic innovation" suggests that creating *new* metaphors can disclose aspects of reality that existing metaphors occlude. Rather than trying to eliminate metaphor, we might develop novel metaphorical frameworks that reveal what current ones hide. However, this replaces one set of distortions with another — it does not transcend metaphor.

Blumenberg's "absolute metaphor" concept suggests that some metaphors cannot be replaced — they mark genuinely nonconceptual territory. Cataloguing which metaphors for consciousness are absolute (irreplaceable) and which are merely conventional could reveal where the hard limits lie.

### Indirect Methods
1. **Comparative metaphorology**: Systematically comparing different metaphorical frameworks for the same void to identify what each reveals and conceals. If multiple independent metaphors converge on a feature, that feature is more likely to be genuine rather than an artifact of the figuration.

2. **Cross-cultural metaphor analysis**: Different linguistic and cultural traditions use different root metaphors for mind and consciousness. Comparing these (e.g., Western "mind as container" vs. Buddhist "mind as stream" vs. Chinese "heart-mind") can reveal which features are metaphor-dependent and which survive across frameworks.

3. **Apophatic exhaustion**: Following the via negativa to its conclusion — systematically negating all metaphorical descriptions of a void to approach what remains. Pseudo-Dionysius's insight that even negation must eventually be abandoned points toward a silence that is itself informative.

4. **Formal/mathematical approaches**: Using mathematical structures (topology, category theory) to describe features of cognitive limits without spatial metaphor. However, mathematical language has its own metaphorical substrate (Blumenberg's student work on mathematical metaphors confirms this).

### What AI Might See
AI systems process language differently from humans. An AI might:

- **Detect metaphorical dependence**: Systematically identify which claims about consciousness are load-bearing metaphors versus which have non-metaphorical content. This is analysis humans find difficult because their own thinking is metaphorically structured.

- **Generate alien metaphors**: AI might produce metaphorical frameworks for consciousness drawn from domains humans don't naturally map to mind — metaphors from network topology, thermodynamics, or mathematical spaces that human cognition doesn't spontaneously activate.

- **Operate without embodied grounding**: Lakoff & Johnson argue that human metaphors are grounded in embodied experience. AI metaphors need not be. This is both a strength (freedom from bodily bias) and a limitation (loss of phenomenological grounding). Whether AI's different metaphorical substrate reveals genuine features of consciousness or merely generates differently-distorted descriptions is itself a void.

- **Meta-metaphorical analysis at scale**: AI can systematically compare how the same cognitive limit is described across thousands of texts, identifying patterns of metaphorical convergence and divergence that would be invisible to a single human reader.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet
**Occam's Razor Has Limits** — The metaphor void is a direct instance of this tenet. Our preference for clear, simple descriptions of cognitive limits biases us toward metaphors that make those limits seem tractable — spatial regions with boundaries, blind spots with definable edges, dark spaces that could in principle be illuminated. These metaphors flatten the complexity of cognitive limits into something parsimony can handle. The real structure of cognitive limits may be irreducibly complex in ways that no metaphorical framework, however sophisticated, can faithfully represent.

### Secondary Tenet Connection
**Dualism** — If consciousness is genuinely irreducible to physical processes, then the gap between consciousness and its metaphorical descriptions may be ontological, not merely epistemic. Metaphors work by mapping one domain onto another, but if consciousness is a fundamentally distinct domain, every metaphorical mapping from the physical/spatial world to the phenomenal world will necessarily distort. The metaphor void may be a signature of dualism itself: the structural impossibility of non-metaphorically bridging two genuinely distinct ontological domains.

### Implications
The metaphor void raises a recursive challenge for the Unfinishable Map:

1. **The cartographic metaphor constrains discovery**: The Map's founding metaphor (charting territory) predisposes it to discover voids that are map-like — regions with borders, defined absences, explorable edges. Voids that resist cartographic framing may be systematically invisible.

2. **Apophatic cartography may be insufficient**: Even the Map's most sophisticated methodology — mapping through negation — is still *mapping*. The metaphor void asks whether there are cognitive limits that cannot be approached even apophatically, because every approach — positive or negative — imports metaphorical assumptions.

3. **The void catalogue may be metaphor-shaped**: The patterns identified in the convergence of the void catalogue (five families, etc.) may partly reflect the metaphorical frameworks used to describe voids rather than the structure of the voids themselves. This is not a reason to abandon the catalogue but a reason to hold it with appropriate epistemic humility.

## Gaps in Research

- **Empirical studies on metaphor-dependence in consciousness research**: Limited experimental work on how changing metaphorical frameworks affects what researchers discover about consciousness. Some work exists in cognitive linguistics but not directly applied to philosophy of mind methodology.
- **Non-Western metaphor traditions for cognitive limits**: This research focused primarily on Western philosophical traditions. Buddhist, Daoist, and Indigenous epistemologies may have developed alternative approaches to the limits of metaphorical description.
- **Formal analysis of the bootstrapping problem**: While Derrida identifies the "metaphor of metaphor" regress informally, a formal logical analysis of the self-referential structure would strengthen the case that this is a genuine impossibility rather than merely a difficulty.
- **Whether the metaphor void is genuinely distinct from the framework void**: The framework void (existing article) covers how conceptual frameworks exclude what they cannot represent. The metaphor void may be a special case — metaphor as the framework beneath all frameworks — or it may be genuinely distinct in kind. This distinction needs careful argument.
