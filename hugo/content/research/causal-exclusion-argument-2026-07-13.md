## Executive Summary

Jaegwon Kim's causal exclusion argument (also called the "supervenience argument" or "the master argument") is the central contemporary challenge to non-reductive views of mental causation, including interactionist dualism. It claims that if every physical effect already has a sufficient physical cause (closure), and mental properties are distinct from physical ones (non-identity), then a distinct mental cause would either be redundant — "screened off" by the physical cause — or would make the effect systematically overdetermined, which is implausible. The forced conclusion: mental causes must either *be* physical (reductionism) or do nothing (epiphenomenalism). The argument generalizes beyond mind (the "causal drainage" worry) to threaten all special-science causation. Responses split into compatibilist (Bennett), interventionist/difference-making (List & Menzies, Woodward), eliminativist/reductionist (Kim's own preferred horn), and — the dualist's routes — denying closure, accepting overdetermination, or locating mental influence where physics is genuinely insufficient (the Map's quantum-selection joint).

## The Argument's Structure

Kim's argument is usually reconstructed as an **inconsistent set of individually plausible premises** — an "inconsistent tetrad" plus a supervenience/realization assumption that tightens the screw. The IEP reconstruction gives four jointly inconsistent principles:

1. **Mental Causation**: some events have mental causes ("some events have mental causes").
2. **Physical Causal Closure / Completeness**: "every physical event that has a cause has a sufficient physical cause" (at a time immediately prior).
3. **Irreducibility / Non-Identity**: mental causes are distinct from (not identical to) physical causes.
4. **Causal Exclusion Principle**: "No single event can have more than one sufficient cause occurring at any given time — unless it is a genuine case of causal overdetermination."

Add the **no-systematic-overdetermination** assumption — that it is implausible every bodily motion is caused *twice over*, by a distinct mental and physical cause, the way a firing squad's two bullets overdetermine a death — and the five commitments cannot all be true. Something must give.

The **supervenience premise** is what makes the mental horn so uncomfortable: because the mental supervenes on (is realized by / determined by) its physical base, the base both *causes* the behavioral effect and *necessitates* the mental event, leaving the mental event with "no work left to do." Kim's conclusion is a dilemma for the non-reductive physicalist (and, mutatis mutandis, the dualist): **reduce the mental to the physical (identity, saving efficacy) or accept epiphenomenalism (distinctness, losing efficacy).** There is no stable non-reductive middle.

### The three pillars (task framing)

The task's "three pillars" map onto premises 2, 4, and the no-overdetermination assumption:
- **Closure of the physical** (premise 2) — the physical domain is causally self-contained.
- **The exclusion principle** (premise 4) — no overdetermination-free double sufficient causation.
- **No systematic overdetermination** — the empirical/methodological implausibility of universal causal redundancy.

Non-identity (premise 3) is what makes the argument *bite against dualism specifically*: it is precisely the dualist's core commitment. So the dualist cannot escape via the reductionist horn that Kim himself ultimately (reluctantly) takes.

## Major Positions / Responses

### Reductionist / Eliminativist (Kim's own horn)
- **Proponent**: Kim.
- **Core claim**: Save mental causation by *identifying* mental properties with physical ones (functional reduction). Distinct-but-efficacious is not on the menu. Kim accepts that phenomenal qualia may not functionalize — hence "*or something near enough*": most of the mental reduces and stays efficacious; qualia may be the irreducible, epiphenomenal residue.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly opposed to Tenet 1 (Dualism) and Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). This is the position the Map must answer.

### Compatibilist / Autonomist — Karen Bennett
- **Core claim**: Deny the exclusion principle's application by adding a **counterfactual test for overdetermination**. Genuine overdetermination (firing squad) requires that *if one cause had occurred without the other, the effect would still have occurred* (the counterfactual `~C1 & C2 > E` is non-vacuously true). In the mental/physical case, because of supervenience, you *cannot* have the mental cause without the physical base — so that counterfactual is false or only vacuously true. Hence mental and physical are distinct sufficient causes **without** counting as overdetermination. Bennett calls this "tracting" the intractable exclusion problem.
- **Critique**: Objectors argue the reply presupposes the mental cause is *sufficient* in the first place — exactly what the physicalist picture denies — and that "dependent overdetermination" is a redescription rather than a dissolution.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Structurally parallel to the Map's overdetermination-dissolution move (both attack the exclusion principle via the special dependence between mental and physical), but Bennett stays within non-reductive *physicalism*. Worth flagging the parallel: the Map's selection-at-a-gap version claims the physical base is *not* sufficient, which is stronger than Bennett's "sufficient but not overdetermining."

### Interventionist / Difference-Making — List & Menzies, Woodward
- **Proponents**: Christian List and Peter Menzies (2009); James Woodward (2015).
- **Core claim**: Recast causation as **difference-making under interventions** (Woodward's interventionism), not as "sufficient causation." On this account the exclusion principle is **not a necessary truth but a contingent matter**. List & Menzies formulate a "new" exclusion principle with two directions: *upward exclusion* (realizer excludes the realized) and, more strikingly, **downward exclusion** (the higher-level property excludes its own realizer as the cause) — which can obtain when the higher-level property is the one whose presence/absence makes the difference and the specific realizer is not required (realization-insensitivity / proportionality). Result: some higher-level (including mental) properties are the genuine difference-makers, and their microphysical realizers are *not*.
- **Critique**: Bermúdez & Cahen and others argue the difference-making account does not actually help — proportionality can cut the other way, and interventionist causation may not deliver the metaphysical exclusion Kim needs to be denied.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A physicalist-friendly rescue of higher-level causal autonomy. Useful to the Map as *independent pressure on the exclusion principle* from within mainstream philosophy of mind — it shows Kim's premise 4 is not sacrosanct — without committing to dualism. Cite as "even physicalists reject the exclusion principle."

### The Generalization / Causal Drainage Worry — Ned Block
- **Proponent**: Ned Block, "Do Causal Powers Drain Away?"; Kim's reply, "Blocking Causal Drainage and Other Maintenance Chores with Mental Causation."
- **Core claim (Block)**: The exclusion reasoning does not stop at the mental. If neural causes exclude mental ones, chemical causes exclude neural, microphysical excludes chemical — causal power **drains down** the hierarchy. If there is **no bottom microphysical level** (infinite descent is at least possible), then causal power drains into "a bottomless pit" and there is **no causation anywhere** — a reductio. Block distinguishes an *epistemic* version (Exclusionists presumptuously assume a bottom level exists) and a *modal* version (they absurdly make causation contingent on a bottom level existing).
- **Kim's reply**: For drainage to occur at purely physical levels, the *reduction* option must be ruled out at those levels too, "and it is far from obvious that this can be done" — Kim thinks physical levels reduce, blocking the descent while leaving the mental non-reduction in place.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The single most powerful *neutral-ground* pressure point. If exclusion generalizes, it either kills all special-science causation (absurd) or the reasoning is flawed. `objections-to-interactionism.md:145` already gestures at this ("does temperature cause anything, or just molecular motion?"); the dedicated page should develop it fully with the Block/Kim exchange, because it is the response least dependent on the Map's own tenets.

### The Dualist's Three Options (Map-relevant)
Against the inconsistent set, a dualist committed to Tenet 3 must reject at least one premise:

1. **Deny closure** (reject premise 2). Straightforward interactionism: the physical is *not* causally complete; mind injects causal influence. Cost: apparent tension with conservation laws and the empirical success of physics. The Map's softer version is *observational* closure (Saad) — physics is complete for what experiments detect, not absolutely.
2. **Accept systematic overdetermination** (reject the no-overdetermination assumption). Some dualists (e.g., Mills, and on some readings the "dependent overdetermination" camp) bite this bullet, arguing the supervenience dependence makes the overdetermination benign, unlike the coincidental firing-squad case.
3. **Locate the mental cause where physics is genuinely insufficient** (reject the *applicability* of premise 4 by denying premise 2's *sufficiency* at the relevant joint). **The Map's route**: at quantum indeterminacies physics fixes probabilities but not outcomes, so there is *no sufficient physical cause* of the specific outcome for the mental to be redundant to. Consciousness selects among physically-undetermined possibilities. There is nothing to exclude because the "screening-off" premise's *sufficient* physical cause does not exist at that joint. This is developed in `overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism.md`; the delegatory-preemption reply (`delegatory-dualism.md`) is a complementary, quantum-independent option (only one cause operates over the preempted causal subset).

## Key Debates

### Does exclusion refute non-reductive physicalism or dualism *equally*?
- **Sides**: Kim aims it at non-reductive physicalism (mental supervenes but is irreducible); dualists inherit it because they too assert non-identity.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether closure is better challenged (dualist option) or whether the exclusion principle itself is contingent (interventionist) or misapplied (compatibilist).
- **Current state**: Ongoing. No consensus. Kim's own resolution (reduce-or-epiphenomenalism) is widely resisted; the interventionist and compatibilist replies are live but contested (Bermúdez & Cahen; Keaton & Polger "Exclusion, still not tracted").

### Is the closure premise empirically established or a methodological posit?
- Relevant to the Map's `causal-closure-debate-historical-survey.md` and `completeness-in-physics-under-dualism.md`. Papineau argues closure is empirically well-supported; the Map argues quantum indeterminism leaves a genuine gap, so closure-as-*determination* is not established.
