## Executive Summary

David Chalmers' **principle of organizational invariance** holds that any two systems with the *same fine-grained functional organization* have qualitatively identical conscious experiences. He argues *for* it with two thought experiments: **Fading Qualia** (gradual neuron-by-silicon replacement — if qualia faded while function held constant, the subject would be systematically and radically wrong about their own experience, taken as a *reductio*) and **Dancing Qualia** (a switchable silicon backup circuit — if qualia flipped while function held, the subject would notice nothing, a second and, on Chalmers' own view, stronger *reductio*). The arguments establish **nonreductive functionalism about the distribution of consciousness** — where consciousness is and is not, and of what character — *not* physicalism about its nature; Chalmers is himself a property dualist who accepts the hard problem. The principal objections all press on one hinge: that the arguments assume qualia cannot be *systematically mis-introspected*. **Critical for the Map**: organizational invariance is a direct rival to the interface model. If consciousness makes even a minimal quantum-level difference (Tenet 2), a silicon functional-duplicate lacking that quantum interface might *not* preserve qualia — exactly the fading/dancing scenario Chalmers deems absurd. Chalmers is thus a dualist **ally** on the hard problem but an **opponent** on substrate-independence.

## Primary Sources (publisher of record)

### Chalmers, "Absent Qualia, Fading Qualia, Dancing Qualia" (1995)
- **Venue**: In Thomas Metzinger (ed.), *Conscious Experience*, Imprint Academic (UK) / Ferdinand Schöningh (DE), 1995.
- **Online (author's own site, publisher-of-record online copy)**: https://consc.net/papers/qualia.html
- **PhilPapers record**: https://philpapers.org/rec/CHAAQF
- **Type**: Book chapter (primary text for both thought experiments).
- **Key points**:
  - States the **principle of organizational invariance**: any system with the same fine-grained functional organization as a conscious system has qualitatively identical experiences.
  - **Fading Qualia** setup: replace neurons one at a time with functionally identical silicon chips, preserving overall functional organization throughout. An intermediate part-silicon system ("Joe") that had *faded* experience while functionally isomorphic would be systematically wrong about its own phenomenology — sincerely reporting vivid red while (by hypothesis) experiencing tepid pink — despite fully intact, rational cognitive mechanisms. Chalmers takes this radical dissociation between experience and judgment as a *reductio* of absent/faded qualia.
  - **Dancing Qualia** setup: install a functionally identical silicon **backup circuit** for a neural region, with a switch that redirects processing between neural and silicon versions without altering overall functional organization. If experience differed between the two, flipping the switch would make qualia *change* (e.g., red↔blue) while the subject, whose functional state is unchanged, notices nothing. Chalmers calls this the *stronger* of the two arguments.
  - **What it establishes**: consciousness's *distribution and character* supervene (with natural/nomological necessity) on fine-grained functional organization. This is **not** a claim that consciousness is *nothing over and above* function — Chalmers keeps the hard problem and property dualism. Invariance is about where/what, not what-it-is.
- **Candidate quotes (MUST be verbatim-checked against the primary text before any use in the article — WebFetch summarizer word-order is NOT reliable; see citation discipline note below)**:
  - Principle (approximate, verify): "Given any system that has conscious experiences, then any system that has the same functional organization at a fine enough grain will have qualitatively identical conscious experiences."
  - Dancing reductio (approximate, verify): "My experiences will change before my eyes ... but I do not notice any change."

### Chalmers, *The Conscious Mind* (1996), Chapter 7
- **Venue**: Oxford University Press, 1996. Ch. 7 ("Absent Qualia, Fading Qualia, Dancing Qualia") develops the principle of organizational invariance at book length.
- **Précis (author's site)**: https://consc.net/papers/precis.html
- **Type**: Monograph chapter.
- **Key points**:
  - Logical spine: *if* absent qualia are possible, *then* fading qualia are possible; *if* absent or inverted qualia are possible, *then* dancing qualia are possible. But fading qualia are implausible and dancing qualia extremely implausible; therefore we have good reason to accept organizational invariance — functional organization fully determines conscious experience (of natural necessity).
  - Explicitly frames the target as **empirical/nomological** possibility, not logical possibility. Chalmers grants that absent/inverted qualia are *logically* possible (that is what the zombie argument trades on); the fading/dancing arguments claim they are ruled out in *our* world by the way experience actually depends on organization.

## Major Positions

### Organizational Invariance (Chalmers)
- **Proponent**: David Chalmers.
- **Core claim**: Fine-grained functional organization fixes phenomenal character with natural necessity; substrate (carbon vs silicon) is irrelevant so long as organization is preserved.
- **Key arguments**: Fading Qualia and Dancing Qualia reductios (above), both turning on the alleged absurdity of a large experience/judgment dissociation in a fully rational system.
- **Relation to site tenets**:
  - **Tenet 1 (Dualism)** — *partial ally*. Chalmers is a property dualist; invariance is compatible with dualism about the *nature* of consciousness. The Map and Chalmers agree consciousness is not reductively explained by function.
  - **Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)** — *direct opponent*. Invariance assumes a silicon isomorph can reproduce *all* consciousness-relevant organization. If the Map's minimal quantum interface is consciousness-relevant and not classically-functionally replicable, a silicon duplicate is *not* a true fine-grained isomorph — it omits the interface. Then fading/dancing qualia are not absurd; they are what the Map predicts. The Map's reply is not "introspection is unreliable" but "the silicon system was never a genuine organizational duplicate."
  - **Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)** — *tension*. Because Chalmers pairs invariance with property dualism, critics charge the resulting consciousness is epiphenomenal — a "meaningless spectator" (see Activitas Nervosa Superior 2019 below). The Map, by contrast, requires consciousness to *make a physical difference*, which is precisely what would break exact functional invariance.

### The Map's Rival Principle (interface-model reading)
- **Core claim**: Substrate matters *because* the quantum interface is part of the consciousness-relevant organization. Two systems can be classical-functionally identical yet differ at the quantum-interface grain, so they are not isomorphic "at a fine enough grain" in the sense the invariance principle needs.
- **Dialectical move**: This does not refute Chalmers head-on; it exploits the "fine enough grain" clause. Chalmers concedes organization must be specified at *some* grain; the Map argues the correct grain includes quantum-interface structure that silicon lacks. The downstream article should make this the central engagement — a **grain dispute**, not a flat denial.

## Key Debates / Objections

### Objection 1 — Introspective reliability (the shared hinge)
- **Sources**: Eric Schwitzgebel, "Chalmers's Fading/Dancing Qualia and Self-Knowledge" (Splintered Mind, 2010) and "An Objection to Chalmers's Fading Qualia Argument" (2024, blog + Substack); Andreas Mogensen, "How to Resist the Fading Qualia Argument" (Global Priorities Institute working paper).
- **Core disagreement**: Both reductios assume a rational system *cannot* be radically wrong about its own current experience. If introspection can be systematically unreliable — and Schwitzgebel argues at length that it is even in ordinary humans — then "faded/danced qualia the subject fails to notice" is not absurd but merely surprising, and the reductio dissolves. Schwitzgebel's twist: our ordinary introspection stays reliable partly because problem cases are *rare*; we are not entitled to assume fading/dancing robots are correspondingly rare.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; the introspective-reliability premise is the most-attacked and most-defended joint of the arguments. **Directly load-bearing for the Map**: the Map does *not* need to deny introspective reliability — its wedge is the grain/interface point, which leaves introspection intact.

### Objection 2 — Equivocation in the Dancing Qualia argument
- **Source**: B. van Heuveln, E. Dietrich & M. Oshima, "Let's Dance! The Equivocation in Chalmers' Dancing Qualia Argument," *Minds and Machines* 8(2):237–249 (1998). DOI: 10.1023/A:1008273402702. PhilArchive: https://philarchive.org/archive/VANLDT
- **Core disagreement**: The argument allegedly equivocates between (a) a *difference in experience between two systems* and (b) a *single system's experience of change*. Once separated, they argue, the reductio's force evaporates — the gap "cannot be closed," so dancing qualia fails to establish that qualia are organizational invariants.
- **Current state**: A standard cited critique specifically of the *dancing* (stronger) argument.

### Objection 3 — Biology-based / substrate critique
- **Source**: "The Limits of Artificial Consciousness: A Biology-Based Critique of Chalmers' Fading Qualia Argument" (EA Forum post; author/date to confirm at drafting). URL: https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/6CKGX7p7QiSsSEsJx/
- **Core disagreement**: Challenges whether silicon can *actually* preserve fine-grained functional organization — biological neurons may realize organization at a grain (electrochemical, possibly sub-cellular) that no silicon chip reproduces, so the replacement scenario is not the like-for-like isomorphism the argument assumes.
- **Relevance to Map**: This is the **closest external ally** to the interface-model reply and the natural cross-reference. Same structural move (deny genuine isomorphism) without the quantum commitment. The Map's version is stronger/more specific: the missing organization is the *quantum interface*, not merely unspecified wet biology.

### Objection 4 — Invariance ⇒ epiphenomenal "spectator"
- **Source**: "Chalmers' Principle of Organizational Invariance Makes Consciousness Fundamental but Meaningless Spectator of Its Own Drama," *Activitas Nervosa Superior* (Springer), 2019. DOI: 10.1007/s41470-019-00062-z.
- **Core disagreement**: If function fixes everything experiential and function is causally closed, consciousness does no work — it becomes an epiphenomenal spectator. Presses the epiphenomenalism worry that also motivates the Map's Tenet 3.

### Objection 5 — Further formal objections
- **Sources**: William M. Greenberg, "On Chalmers' principle of organizational invariance and his 'dancing qualia' and 'fading qualia' thought experiments" (PhilPapers: https://philpapers.org/rec/GREOCP-2); Nir Aides, "Yet Another Objection to Fading and Dancing Qualia" (PhilPapers: https://philpapers.org/rec/AIDYAO).
