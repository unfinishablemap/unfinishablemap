# Research: Completeness in Physics — EPR, Bell, and "Partial but Complete" (Supplement)

**Date**: 2026-03-17
**Supplements**: [completeness-in-physics-epr-bell-2026-03-14](/research/completeness-in-physics-epr-bell-2026-03-14/)
**Search queries used**: QBism completeness participatory realism Fuchs, Hardy theorem 1992 nonlocality without inequalities, Bell's theorem allows local theories Nature Physics 2022, Barrett quantum mechanics dualism measurement problem, von Neumann Wigner consciousness causes collapse formalization, Bohr reply EPR complementarity, notions of completeness EPR different senses, Stapp quantum interactive dualism

## Executive Summary

This supplement fills five gaps identified in the 2026-03-14 research: (1) QBism's rival account of completeness, (2) Hardy's nonlocality-without-inequalities theorem, (3) consciousness-collapse formalisations connecting to the "partial but complete" framing, (4) the Hance-Hossenfelder argument that Bell's theorem permits local theories under superdeterminism/retrocausality, and (5) Barrett's argument that the measurement problem pushes toward strong mind-body dualism regardless of interpretation. Together, these deepen the case that quantum mechanics is structurally complete yet ontologically partial — and that the gap is specifically consciousness-shaped.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Quantum-Bayesian and Pragmatist Views of Quantum Theory"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/quantum-bayesian/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - QBism (Caves, Fuchs, Schack; later Mermin) treats quantum states as encoding an agent's credences about future experiences, not as descriptions of observer-independent reality
  - Quantum theory is "complete" in QBism's sense: it is the best possible guide for an agent's expectations. There is nothing to add — no hidden variables, no deeper theory
  - But this completeness is explicitly agent-relative: "reality consists of more than can be captured by any putative third-person account of it" (Fuchs's "participatory realism")
  - Measurement is not passive observation but "a little act of creation" — each measurement gives rise to something genuinely new
  - Fuchs locates QBism in the tradition of William James's pragmatism: an open, pluralistic universe where agents participate in shaping reality
  - Caves later distanced himself from QBism; Mermin joined as a prominent advocate
- **Tenet alignment**: Complex. QBism shares the Map's rejection of the "view from nowhere" and agrees reality exceeds physical description. But QBism's agent-centrism is anti-realist about the wavefunction, conflicting with PBR-based ψ-ontic positions the Map endorses. QBism dissolves the measurement problem rather than solving it — which the Map rejects as evasion.
- **Quote**: Fuchs: "When an experimentalist reaches out and touches a quantum system — the process usually called quantum 'measurement' — that process gives rise to a birth."

### Hardy — "Nonlocality for Two Particles Without Inequalities" (1992-93)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardy%27s_paradox
- **Type**: Paper / overview
- **Key points**:
  - Hardy demonstrated quantum nonlocality without statistical inequalities — using only logical reasoning about definite measurement outcomes
  - Any pure entangled two-qubit state (except maximally entangled states) exhibits Hardy-type nonlocality
  - Extended to n-particle systems by Pagonis and Clifton (1992); inequality forms by Mermin (1994) and Cereceda (2004)
  - Called "the best version of Bell's theorem" due to its simplicity: it shows that for certain quantum states, local realism leads to a logical contradiction, not merely a statistical one
  - Almost all entangled multipartite states admit Hardy-type proofs (except tensor products of single-qubit and maximally entangled bipartite states)
- **Tenet alignment**: Strengthens the case from the 2026-03-14 notes. Hardy makes the impossibility of local hidden variables more vivid — it is not just that statistics disagree but that individual cases produce logical contradictions under local realism. This further closes the "incomplete physics" escape route and supports the Map's claim that what's missing from QM is not more physics but the role of consciousness.

### Hance & Hossenfelder — "Bell's theorem allows local theories of quantum mechanics" (2022)
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-022-01831-5
- **Type**: Journal article (Nature Physics)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that Bell's theorem is commonly misunderstood: it does not rule out all local hidden-variable theories, only those satisfying statistical independence (measurement independence)
  - Theories violating statistical independence — superdeterminism, retrocausality, or "supermeasured" models — can be local and still reproduce quantum predictions
  - Superdeterminism: hidden variables and measurement settings share a common cause in the initial conditions of the universe
  - Retrocausality: measurement settings influence hidden variables backward in time
  - These alternatives are widely regarded as philosophically costly (superdeterminism undermines experimental science; retrocausality challenges temporal causation)
- **Tenet alignment**: Relevant but ultimately not favourable. Superdeterminism eliminates free will entirely, conflicting with Bidirectional Interaction (consciousness must be causally efficacious). Retrocausality is potentially compatible with the Map (see retrocausal research notes) but does not obviously require consciousness. The Map's position — that nonlocality is real and consciousness operates at measurement — remains the cleaner option.

### Barrett — "A Quantum-Mechanical Argument for Mind-Body Dualism" (2007)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10670-006-9016-z
- **Type**: Journal article (Erkenntnis)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that a strong mind-body dualism is required of any formulation of quantum mechanics satisfying a relatively weak set of explanatory constraints
  - The preferred basis problem is the key driver: both collapse and no-collapse interpretations face it, and dualism provides a natural resolution in both cases
  - In collapse theories: dualism provides a criterion for when collapse occurs (conscious observation)
  - In no-collapse theories (e.g. Everett): dualism explains why observers have determinate experiences despite being in superposition
  - Dropping the explanatory constraints avoids dualism but may require a "physical-physical dualism" that is equally objectionable
  - This is not a proof of dualism but an argument that quantum mechanics structurally favours it
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Dualism and the "partial but complete" framing. Barrett's argument shows the measurement problem is not just a puzzle about physics — it is structurally entangled with questions about mind. The gap in QM (from superposition to definite outcome) is precisely where dualism finds its foothold. This directly supports the Map's claim that QM is complete about physics but partial about reality.

### Barrett — "Quantum Mechanics and Dualism" (2005)
- **URL**: https://sites.socsci.uci.edu/~jabarret/bio/publications/BC10.pdf
- **Type**: Book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - Extends the above argument: the measurement problem has led physicists and philosophers to speculate about mind-matter relations because quantum mechanics leaves a structural opening
  - The degree of freedom that consciousness provides is "tempting to use" in addressing the measurement problem — not as ad hoc patch but because the problem's structure points toward it
  - Reviews how Wigner, von Neumann, and Stapp each exploited this structural opening differently
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly supports the Map's "partial but complete" thesis — QM's incompleteness is consciousness-shaped.

### Stapp — "Quantum Interactive Dualism: An Alternative to Materialism" (2005)
- **URL**: https://www-physics.lbl.gov/~stapp/QID.pdf
- **Type**: Journal article (Zygon)
- **Key points**:
  - Uses orthodox (von Neumann) quantum mechanics to argue for interactive dualism: conscious choices (Process 1 interventions) are not determined by any known physical law
  - Consciousness exploits the quantum Zeno effect: sustained mental attention holds desired neural patterns by repeated "observation"
  - Neither the content nor the timing of Process 1 events is determined, even statistically, by quantum mechanics — this is the structural gap
  - Stapp's dualism is "interactive" in Descartes' sense but governed by quantum rules rather than pineal gland mechanics
  - Nonlocality (per Bell) supports the "holistic character of the stream of consciousness" — entanglement as model for unified experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with all five tenets. Stapp's framework is essentially the Map's Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet made technically precise. The structural gap in QM (Process 1 not determined by physics) is the "partial but complete" gap: QM is complete about Process 2 (unitary evolution) and Process 3 (collapse statistics) but silent on Process 1 (what triggers collapse and with what content).

### Von Neumann/Wigner — Consciousness Causes Collapse (Historical)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consciousness_causes_collapse
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Von Neumann (1932) showed the mathematical consistency of placing the "cut" (between quantum system and classical observer) anywhere, including at consciousness — but did not explicitly advocate consciousness-collapse. Recent scholarship (2025) challenges the folk attribution.
  - Wigner (1961) explicitly proposed consciousness as the collapse trigger in "Remarks on the mind-body question"
  - Wigner's friend scenario: if the friend is in superposition until Wigner observes, consciousness is the demarcation line
  - Wigner later abandoned this view (1970s-80s) after Zeh's decoherence work
  - Currently largely rejected by mainstream physics but remains a live option in philosophy of mind / quantum foundations
- **Tenet alignment**: Historical precursor to the Map's position. The Map's "partial but complete" framing improves on Wigner by: (1) grounding in post-Bell, post-PBR, post-KS results that were unavailable to Wigner; (2) invoking minimal interaction rather than strong consciousness-collapse; (3) allowing objective reduction mechanisms (per No Many Worlds tenet) alongside consciousness modulation.

### Bohr's Reply to EPR — Complementarity as Completeness Defence
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-epr/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (section on Bohr's response)
- **Key points**:
  - Bohr used the same title as EPR ("Can Quantum-Mechanical Description...") to argue QM is complete
  - His defence rests on complementarity: position and momentum measurements are mutually exclusive experimental arrangements, so demanding simultaneous reality for both is illegitimate
  - Bohr redefines "disturbance": measuring particle A changes "the conditions which define the possible types of predictions" for particle B — not a physical disturbance but an epistemological one
  - Halvorson (2004) argued Bohr's reply is more rigorous than commonly credited, but scholars remain divided
  - A gap persists: Bohr does not adequately address Einstein's concern about locality and separability
- **Tenet alignment**: Bohr's complementarity is a sophisticated rival to the Map's position. If complementarity is accepted, QM is complete in a strong sense and there is no gap for consciousness. The Map must argue that complementarity dissolves rather than solves the measurement problem — it tells us which questions are legitimate without explaining why we get definite answers.

## Major Positions (Supplement)

### QBism: "Complete for Agents"
- **Proponents**: Fuchs, Schack, Mermin
- **Core claim**: QM is complete as a user's manual for navigating experience. It is not a description of mind-independent reality but a normative framework for an agent's beliefs. The measurement problem dissolves because "measurement" is just an agent's experience of acting on the world.
- **Key arguments**: De Finetti-style subjective probability; participatory realism; rejection of the "view from nowhere"
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partially aligns (rejects reductive physicalism, grants agents irreducible role). Conflicts with the Map's realism about the wavefunction (PBR) and with the Map's claim that consciousness has a specific causal role — QBism makes the agent constitutive of quantum theory's meaning, not a cause within it.
- **Map's response**: QBism correctly identifies that QM cannot be understood without reference to agents/observers, but incorrectly concludes that there is nothing more to say. The Map says the agent is not just an epistemic framework but a causal participant — consciousness does not merely interpret outcomes but selects them.

### Superdeterminism/Retrocausality: "Complete and Local"
- **Proponents**: 't Hooft (superdeterminism); Price, Wharton (retrocausality); Hance & Hossenfelder (synthesis)
- **Core claim**: Bell's theorem can be evaded by abandoning statistical independence rather than locality. If hidden variables correlate with measurement settings (via initial conditions or backward causation), local models can reproduce quantum predictions.
- **Key arguments**: Hance & Hossenfelder (2022) showed Bell's theorem requires statistical independence as an explicit assumption; models violating it remain logically consistent
- **Relation to site tenets**: Superdeterminism directly conflicts with Bidirectional Interaction — if everything including measurement choices is predetermined, there is no room for conscious agency. Retrocausality is potentially compatible but does not require consciousness; see retrocausal research notes [retrocausal-selection-consciousness-physics-2026-03-14](/research/retrocausal-selection-consciousness-physics-2026-03-14/) and [retrocausal-selection-consciousness-physics-2026-03-16](/research/retrocausal-selection-consciousness-physics-2026-03-16/).

## Key Debates (Supplement)

### QBism vs. Realist Completeness
- **Sides**: QBists (complete-for-agents) vs. realists including the Map (structurally complete, ontologically partial)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "completeness" should be understood relative to an agent's decision-making or relative to mind-independent reality. QBism says QM is the final word on rational expectation; the Map says QM is the final word on physical structure but not on actuality.
- **Current state**: Active debate. QBism has gained philosophical sophistication (Fuchs's "participatory realism") but faces criticism for anti-realism about the wavefunction (tension with PBR).

### Is the Measurement Problem Consciousness-Shaped?
- **Sides**: Barrett (yes — dualism is structurally favoured), decoherence advocates (no — decoherence resolves it), many-worlds proponents (no — all outcomes happen)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the gap between superposition and definite outcome requires non-physical explanation or has a purely physical resolution
- **Current state**: Barrett's argument stands largely unrefuted on its own terms. Decoherence selects preferred bases but does not explain definite outcomes. Many-worlds eliminates the problem by denying definite outcomes — which the Map rejects (No Many Worlds tenet).

## Connections to "Partial but Complete"

The supplementary research strengthens the "partial but complete" framing in several ways:

1. **QBism as contrasting case**: QBism offers a rival "completeness" that is agent-relative rather than structural. The Map's position is sharper by contrast: QM is complete about physical structure (not just about expectations) and partial about ontology (not just about the agent's perspective). The difference is that the Map takes the wavefunction as real (per PBR) while QBism does not.

2. **Hardy's theorem as strengthening result**: Hardy shows that local realism produces not just statistical disagreement but logical contradiction. This closes the "incomplete physics" interpretation more firmly than Bell alone — the missing element cannot be local physical facts.

3. **Barrett's dualism argument as structural support**: Barrett shows the measurement problem structurally favours dualism across interpretation families. This is independent of the Map's other commitments — it follows from the physics alone. The gap QM leaves is not an accident or deficiency but a structural feature pointing toward mind.

4. **Stapp's Process 1 as the precise gap**: Von Neumann's three processes provide the technical framework: Process 2 (evolution) and Process 3 (Born rule statistics) are fully determined by physics. Process 1 (what question to ask / what measurement to perform) is not. This is the exact location of "partial but complete" — QM is complete about Processes 2 and 3, silent about Process 1.

5. **Superdeterminism as the cost of avoiding the gap**: The only way to maintain locality AND completeness AND determinism is superdeterminism — which eliminates free will and conscious agency entirely. This is the price of refusing "partial but complete."

## Gaps in Research

- **Chalmers-McQueen consciousness-collapse formalism**: Chalmers and McQueen (2022) have developed a more rigorous formalization of consciousness-collapse theories. Not covered here; would strengthen the technical bridge between "partial but complete" and specific dualist proposals.
- **Frauchiger-Renner (2018)**: Extended Wigner's friend scenario showing internal contradictions in quantum mechanics when applied to observers. Relevant to whether QM can be complete even about physical systems that include observers. Partially covered in [godel-measurement-problem-analogy-2026-03-17](/research/godel-measurement-problem-analogy-2026-03-17/).
- **Objective collapse theories** (GRW, CSL): These attempt to fill the measurement gap with physics rather than consciousness. The Map should engage more thoroughly with why these are insufficient (they may solve the measurement problem without consciousness, potentially undermining the "consciousness-shaped gap" thesis).
