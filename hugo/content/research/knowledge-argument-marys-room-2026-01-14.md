# Research: The Knowledge Argument (Mary's Room)

**Date**: 2026-01-14
**Search queries used**: "Stanford Encyclopedia Philosophy knowledge argument Frank Jackson Mary's Room", "Internet Encyclopedia Philosophy knowledge argument qualia consciousness", "Frank Jackson knowledge argument 2023 2024 2025 recent developments", "Chalmers two-dimensional semantics knowledge argument phenomenal concepts", "ability hypothesis Lewis Nemirow knowledge argument"

## Executive Summary

The knowledge argument, formulated by Frank Jackson (1982, 1986), is one of the most influential arguments against physicalism. Using the "Mary's Room" thought experiment, it argues that complete physical knowledge is insufficient for knowing what experiences are like—therefore non-physical facts exist. While Jackson himself later rejected the argument, it remains actively debated. Major physicalist responses include Lewis's ability hypothesis (Mary gains know-how, not propositional knowledge) and the phenomenal concepts strategy (Mary gains new concepts for old facts). The argument strongly supports The Unfinishable Map's Dualism tenet and connects directly to the explanatory gap literature.

## Major Positions

### Frank Jackson's Original Formulation (Anti-Physicalism)
- **Proponents**: Jackson (1982, 1986), Chalmers, Robinson, Nida-Rümelin
- **Core claim**: Mary, a brilliant neuroscientist confined to a black-and-white room, learns all physical facts about color vision. Upon release and seeing colors for the first time, she learns something new—what it's like to see red. Therefore, complete physical knowledge doesn't constitute complete knowledge; non-physical facts exist.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Complete physical knowledge leaves phenomenal knowledge unaddressed
  - The learning claim is intuitively compelling
  - If physicalism were true, Mary would already know everything about color experience
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly supports Dualism tenet. The argument directly establishes that physical facts cannot capture what the Map calls qualia—the qualitative character of experience that the Map holds to be irreducible.

### The Ability Hypothesis (Physicalist Response)
- **Proponents**: David Lewis (1983, 1988), Laurence Nemirow (1980, 1990)
- **Core claim**: Mary gains only practical abilities (know-how), not propositional knowledge (know-that). She acquires the ability to imagine, recognize, and remember colors—skills that don't require non-physical facts.
- **Key arguments**:
  - "Knowing what it is like is the possession of abilities" (Lewis)
  - Abilities can be physicalist: they're dispositions of a physical system
  - The learning claim is preserved but reinterpreted
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Dualism tenet. If successful, would show the explanatory gap is merely epistemic (about abilities) not ontological (about facts). However, critics argue abilities are neither necessary nor sufficient for phenomenal knowledge.

### Phenomenal Concepts Strategy (Physicalist Response)
- **Proponents**: Brian Loar, Michael Tye, David Papineau, John Perry
- **Core claim**: Mary gains new knowledge about old (physical) facts through acquiring new phenomenal concepts. The epistemic gap is real but reflects our mode of access to physical properties, not non-physical properties themselves.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Phenomenal concepts are recognitional/demonstrative, requiring experience for acquisition
  - Same fact, different conceptualization (like "water" and "H₂O")
  - Explains the cognitive significance of Mary's discovery without dualism
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Dualism but represents the "most sophisticated physicalist response." the Map's treatment of phenomenal-concepts-strategy engages this through Chalmers's master argument dilemma.

### Chalmers's Two-Dimensional Defense
- **Proponents**: David Chalmers (1996, 2003, 2009)
- **Core claim**: Uses two-dimensional semantics to show that Mary's phenomenal concepts have different primary intensions than any physical concepts—revealing genuine metaphysical possibility of zombies/inverted qualia.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Primary intensions of phenomenal and physical concepts differ
  - If (P & ¬Q) is conceivable, zombies are metaphysically possible
  - The conceivability-possibility link establishes dualism
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly supports Dualism. Chalmers's framework provides the formal apparatus connecting the knowledge argument to the zombie argument and conceivability arguments the Map already endorses.

### Jackson's Later Physicalism (Self-Rejection)
- **Proponents**: Jackson (1995 postscript, 2003, 2007, 2019, 2023)
- **Core claim**: Jackson now endorses physicalism, combining representationalism with the ability hypothesis. He argues phenomenal properties are representational properties that are physically explicable.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Epiphenomenalism (which his original argument implied) makes knowledge of qualia impossible
  - Strong representationalism explains phenomenal character in physical terms
  - No suitable referent exists for "like this" in phenomenal reports
- **Relation to site tenets**: Jackson's rejection conflicts with Dualism, but the Map notes his self-refutation doesn't undermine the argument's force—others (Chalmers, Nida-Rümelin) continue to defend it. In 2023 Jackson still acknowledged "the argument should be addressed really seriously if you are a physicalist."

## Key Debates

### Is Mary's Learning Propositional or Ability-Based?
- **Sides**: Lewis/Nemirow (ability) vs. Conee/Alter/Kind (propositional)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether phenomenal knowledge is a kind of knowing-how or knowing-that
- **Current state**: Ability hypothesis widely considered inadequate. Critics show imaginative abilities are neither necessary (people with no imagination can have phenomenal knowledge) nor sufficient (one could have the abilities without the knowledge).

### New Knowledge or New Concepts?
- **Sides**: Dualists (new non-physical facts) vs. Phenomenal concepts theorists (old facts, new representations)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the epistemic gap indicates an ontological gap
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Chalmers's "master argument" challenges phenomenal concepts strategy: either the special features of phenomenal concepts are themselves physical (in which case zombies could have them) or they're not (conceding non-physical aspects).

### Does Jackson's Rejection Undermine the Argument?
- **Sides**: Some claim the originator's rejection is significant vs. others argue arguments stand independently
- **Core disagreement**: The weight of authorial intent in evaluating philosophical arguments
- **Current state**: Consensus is that Jackson's rejection is philosophically interesting but doesn't settle the debate. The argument's validity depends on its structure, not its author's endorsement.

## Tenet Analysis

**Dualism**: Directly supports. The knowledge argument establishes that physical facts cannot capture phenomenal facts, which is precisely what the Dualism tenet asserts—consciousness is not reducible to physical processes.

**Bidirectional Interaction**: Indirectly supports. Jackson's original argument led to epiphenomenalism, which he later rejected because it makes knowledge of qualia impossible. This supports the tenet: consciousness must causally influence our beliefs about it.

**Minimal Quantum Interaction**: Neutral. The argument doesn't address mechanism, only establishes non-physical facts exist.

**No Many Worlds**: Neutral. No direct connection.

**Occam's Razor Has Limits**: Supports. Physicalism appears simpler but fails to capture phenomenal facts. The argument exemplifies how apparent parsimony misleads when conceptual resources are inadequate.
