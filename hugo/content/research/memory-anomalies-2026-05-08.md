## Executive Summary

Three closely related memory anomalies—déjà vu (false familiarity), jamais vu (false unfamiliarity), and cryptomnesia (forgotten source mistaken for original idea)—offer a natural triad for examining how memory systems can dissociate from accurate world-tracking. Contemporary cognitive science treats all three as failures or quirks of the recollection/familiarity dual-process architecture, with strong neural evidence (especially temporal-lobe epilepsy work) localizing déjà vu to perirhinal/parahippocampal regions. Philosophically, the trio bears on the constructive nature of memory, the reliability of introspective monitoring, the source-monitoring problem, and—via Bergson's "memory of the present"—on whether memory and perception are temporally separable at all. For the Map, these anomalies are useful precisely because they expose the *gap* between phenomenal experience and underlying causal machinery: a person feels certain they have lived this moment before, or feels a familiar word as alien, or feels a remembered idea as original—and that felt certainty is the explanandum, not the evidence.

## Key Sources

### Bergson, "Memory of the Present and False Recognition" (1908)
- **URL**: https://www.samwoolfe.com/2025/10/bergsons-theory-of-deja-vu.html ; https://philpapers.org/archive/PERBPO.pdf
- **Type**: Primary philosophical text (1908) with secondary commentary
- **Key points**:
  - Memory and perception are formed *simultaneously*, not sequentially. Every perception casts a "shadow" memory.
  - Déjà vu is not a glitch in retrieval but a momentary surfacing of the normally-suppressed virtual/memory aspect of present experience.
  - The interesting question for Bergson is not why déjà vu happens but why it is so rare given that memory is contemporaneous with perception.
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Dualism — Bergson's view requires a non-physical "virtual" register of consciousness alongside the actual. Aligns with Bidirectional Interaction insofar as Bergson's mind is not reducible to brain process. The Bergsonian framing resists pure neurological reduction without endorsing the paranormal.
- **Quote**: Bergson advanced "a view—later accepted by Aldous Huxley—that the mind acts as a reducing valve, only letting information enter conscious awareness that benefits survival."

### Brown (2003), "A Review of the Déjà Vu Experience"
- **URL**: https://www.marshmemorylab.com/s/Brown-Marsh-2010-4kfs.pdf (Brown & Marsh 2010 update); https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/full/10.1176/jnp.14.1.6
- **Type**: Review article (cognitive psychology)
- **Key points**:
  - Approximately two-thirds of healthy adults report at least one déjà vu experience.
  - Two source literatures—individual-differences and cognitive/neuroscientific—converged on the "single experience, multiple cause" picture.
  - Frames déjà vu as overlap between current perception and stored representation when the source memory is unrecallable.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. The cognitive account is metaphysically silent; it can sit beneath dualist or physicalist frames.
- **Quote**: "Déjà vu represents a strong sense of subjective familiarity in the absence of any objective evidence."

### Cleary (2008, 2012), Gestalt Familiarity Hypothesis
- **URL**: https://people.uncw.edu/tothj/PSY510/Cleary-Deja%20Vu-CDPS-2008.pdf ; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0028393212003260
- **Type**: Empirical research program
- **Key points**:
  - Déjà vu arises when the *configuration* of elements in a scene matches a previously-seen configuration whose source the observer cannot retrieve.
  - Lab paradigms using virtual environments successfully induce déjà vu by exposing participants to scenes that share configural layout with earlier ones.
  - Predicts feelings of premonition (because the gestalt match also produces a sense of "knowing what comes next") without any genuine future-knowledge.
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Occam's Razor Has Limits in this sense — the simple "memory match" story is empirically powerful but does not exhaust the phenomenology; sense of premonition without actual premonition is a striking finding the Map can use.
- **Quote**: "Cleary proposes a similarity hypothesis of déjà vu arising due to familiarity with a scene caused by an overlap with a previous experience which is undetected by the person experiencing it."

### Moulin et al. (2020/2021), Jamais Vu in the Laboratory
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09658211.2020.1727519 ; https://research-repository.st-andrews.ac.uk/bitstream/handle/10023/21466/Moulin_et_al_2020_Jamais_vu_Memory_AuthorVersion.pdf
- **Type**: Empirical paper (won 2023 Ig Nobel Prize for Literature)
- **Key points**:
  - Jamais vu reliably induced via word-repetition / semantic-satiation tasks.
  - About two-thirds of participants reported the "peculiar" subjective state in roughly one-third of trials, after ~30 repetitions or one minute.
  - Participant's *propositional knowledge* ("this is a real word") remains intact while the *feeling of familiarity* drops out.
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Dualism in a soft sense — the dissociation between knowing-that and feeling-familiar is a textbook case of how phenomenal qualities come apart from cognitive content. Useful exhibit for showing that subjective acquaintance is a separate ingredient.
- **Quote**: "Your awareness of that word being a proper word remains unchanged, whilst your subjective experience of encountering that word starts to go a little 'off'."

### Moulin (2018), *The Cognitive Neuropsychology of Déjà Vu*; TLE studies
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3420423/ ; https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09658211.2021.1911197
- **Type**: Monograph + clinical literature
- **Key points**:
  - Temporal-lobe epilepsy (TLE), especially right-side mesial TLE, reliably produces déjà vu as ictal aura.
  - In TLE, recollection is impaired but familiarity is largely spared—consistent with "déjà vu = pathological familiarity signal without recollection check."
  - Distinguishes pathological *déjà vécu* ("I have lived this exact life-episode before") from healthy déjà vu; déjà vécu involves erroneous recollection rather than mere familiarity.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral / instructive. Localization to perirhinal cortex is good evidence that the *signal* has a brain substrate, but the question of why this signal *feels* a particular way remains the hard problem the Map flags.
- **Quote**: "Whereas there is impaired recollection in TLE, familiarity is largely spared… these impairments spared their ability to engage recollective processes so as to counteract familiarity."

### Jung (1902, 1905), *On the Psychology and Pathology of So-Called Occult Phenomena*; "Cryptomnesia"
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptomnesia ; https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/09/26/cryptomnesia-psychology-of-writing/
- **Type**: Foundational psychiatric/philosophical texts
- **Key points**:
  - Term coined by Théodore Flournoy (1900) for medium Hélène Smith.
  - Jung diagnosed Nietzsche's *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* as containing cryptomnesic reproduction of a Justinus Kerner passage Nietzsche read at age 11.
  - Jung framed cryptomnesia *positively* as well: the unconscious's ability to translate forgotten material into novel synthesis is, in his view, near the heart of genius.
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Dualism in its broad sense — Jung's framework supposes mental content and processes that are not reducible to immediate neural states. The Helen Keller and Nietzsche cases are durable examples of "the source is gone but the content remains."
- **Quote** (Jung): cryptomnesia is when "the ability to unconsciously access and translate a 'rich vein' of forgotten, external material into philosophical, literary, or scientific insight" passes for novelty.

### Brown & Murphy (1989), Marsh & Bower (1993)
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8501434/ ; https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/sci/psych/people/fsch/fsch/studentresources/3rdyearprojects/projects/plag2.pdf
- **Type**: Empirical psychology
- **Key points**:
  - First experimental induction of cryptomnesia: in word-puzzle and category-generation tasks, participants reliably claim previously-heard items as their own ideas.
  - Effect sensitive to cognitive load: dividing attention away from source-monitoring increases cryptomnesia.
  - Frames cryptomnesia as a *source-monitoring failure*, not a content-retrieval failure.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Useful evidence that the same memory architecture that supports normal creativity systematically misattributes sources; supports the Map's general posture that introspection is unreliable about its own ingredients.
- **Quote**: "Cryptomnesia stems from a failure to simultaneously engage in creative thinking and monitor where incoming ideas are coming from."

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Memory" (Sutton & Cassam)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/memory/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Treats memory as constructive rather than reproductive; the SEP entry surveys the philosophical literature on the metaphysics of memory, the relationship between memory and personal identity, and epistemology of memory beliefs.
  - Memory anomalies are relevant to the question of whether *episodic* memory has a distinctive phenomenology that grounds claims about the past, or whether it is merely a confidence-bearing representation that can come apart from accuracy.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral background; the constructive view of memory underpins all three anomalies.

### Perrin & Sant'Anna (2024), "Déjà vécu is not déjà vu: An ability view"
- **URL**: https://andre-santanna.com/papers/dejavu-final.pdf ; https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09515089.2022.2161357
- **Type**: Philosophical paper, *Philosophical Psychology*
- **Key points**:
  - Argues that déjà vécu (the pathological "I have lived this whole episode before" experience) and déjà vu (the everyday flash of false familiarity) are categorically different phenomena, requiring different theoretical accounts.
  - Endorses an "ability view" of episodic memory: remembering is a competence, not a passive replay; déjà vécu is best understood as a malfunction of that competence rather than mere noise in the familiarity signal.
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with the Map's interest in non-reductive but disciplined accounts of mental capacities; the ability view is congenial to a dualist framework because it treats remembering as something a *subject* does.

## Major Positions

### Dual-Process Memory Theory (mainstream cognitive)
- **Proponents**: Mandler, Yonelinas, Tulving (in part), Brown, Cleary
- **Core claim**: Recognition memory has two dissociable components — *familiarity* (fast, automatic, contextless) and *recollection* (slower, episodic, source-bound). Memory anomalies arise when these decouple.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Déjà vu = familiarity signal without supporting recollection (Brown, Cleary).
  - Jamais vu = collapse of familiarity signal despite intact recollection / propositional knowledge (Moulin).
  - Cryptomnesia = recollection of *content* without recollection of *source* (Marsh, Bower).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The dual-process picture is metaphysically neutral and the Map can adopt it as the operating cognitive model. It does *not* by itself answer why these states feel any particular way; that gap is where the Map's dualism does work.

### Bergsonian "Memory of the Present"
- **Proponents**: Bergson; Deleuze (sympathetic reading); Ansell-Pearson
- **Core claim**: Perception and memory of that perception form simultaneously; déjà vu is the fleeting awareness of the normally-suppressed memory-aspect of the present moment.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The memory-of-the-present hypothesis explains the *qualitative* character of déjà vu (the pastness-without-content) better than retrieval-failure stories.
  - The mind as "reducing valve" framework (Bergson → Huxley → Stace) explains why these states are rare.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns strongly with Dualism and with Bidirectional Interaction in spirit. The reducing-valve picture is also relevant to the Map's interest in mystical and altered states. Risk: Bergson's metaphysics is unfashionable and easily caricatured as paranormal-friendly; care needed.

### Neurological / Pathological Account
- **Proponents**: Bartolomei, Spatt, Illman et al.; clinical neurology
- **Core claim**: Memory anomalies (especially déjà vu) are produced by aberrant electrical activity in mesial temporal-lobe structures (perirhinal cortex, hippocampus), often in the context of TLE.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Direct stimulation of human temporal cortex can produce déjà-like experiences.
  - TLE patients show selective familiarity impairments and characteristic ictal déjà vu/déjà vécu.
  - Pharmacological manipulation (e.g., cannabis, hypnogogic states) can elicit similar reports.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns with the Map's commitment that there are robust *physical correlates* of conscious states. Does not by itself entail reductionism — the correlates are real but the felt character remains unexplained.

### Skeptical / Constructivist (memory as confabulation)
- **Proponents**: Loftus, Schacter (Seven Sins of Memory), constructivist memory researchers
- **Core claim**: All memory is constructive; memory anomalies sit on a continuum with everyday confabulation, false memory, and source-monitoring error.
- **Key arguments**:
  - "Precognitive dream" reports are best explained by selective recall plus retrofitting (a known cryptomnesia-adjacent effect).
  - The frequency of déjà vu correlates with imaginative/dissociative tendencies in healthy populations.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns with Occam's Razor Has Limits *both ways*. The constructivist is right that we should not multiply paranormal entities to explain déjà vu. The Map can endorse the deflationary explanation of paranormal claims while still flagging that the deflationary story does not, by itself, explain the phenomenology.

### Paranormal / Past-Life / Precognitive Account
- **Proponents**: spiritualist, theosophical, and some Jungian-archetypal traditions; not mainstream
- **Core claim**: At least some déjà vu episodes are genuine memories of past lives, ancestral experience, or dream-precognition; cryptomnesia is sometimes channeled material.
- **Key arguments**: Largely anecdotal; the Stainton Moses and Hélène Smith cases are foundational examples that early researchers (Flournoy, Jung) explicitly used cryptomnesia to *deflate*.
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts** with the Map's general posture. The Map's dualism is not the spiritualist's dualism; we accept non-physical influence on the brain (Tenet 3) but not unbounded paranormal channels. These accounts are useful as *foils* — to show that the Map can take the phenomenology seriously without endorsing the paranormal explanation.

## Key Debates

### Is déjà vu one phenomenon or many?
- **Sides**: Brown (2003) treats it as a unitary phenomenon with multiple causes; Perrin & Sant'Anna (2024) argue déjà vécu is categorically different from déjà vu; some clinicians distinguish ictal, drug-induced, and healthy variants.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the felt quality of "having been here before" supervenes on a single neural/cognitive process or on a family of dissociable processes that share surface phenomenology.
- **Current state**: Active. The split between déjà vu (familiarity error) and déjà vécu (recollection error) has substantial traction.

### Is the recollection/familiarity dichotomy real?
- **Sides**: Yonelinas (yes, dual process) vs. signal-detection / single-process advocates.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether two qualitatively distinct memory processes really exist or whether "recollection" and "familiarity" are points on a continuum of memory strength.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Dual-process is the working assumption in the déjà vu literature.

### Is cryptomnesia a defect or a feature?
- **Sides**: Cognitive psychologists (Marsh, Bower, Brown) treat it as a source-monitoring failure to be guarded against; Jung and some literary theorists treat it as the engine of creative synthesis.
- **Core disagreement**: Normative — should we want to eliminate cryptomnesia, or does much creative work depend on something like it?
- **Current state**: Underdeveloped philosophically. The Map could contribute by framing cryptomnesia as a window onto the necessary opacity of one's own cognitive sources.

### Are these anomalies evidence for or against introspective reliability?
- **Sides**: Higher-order theorists who treat introspection as accurate vs. heterophenomenologists (Dennett) and constructivists who treat it as a confabulating story.
- **Core disagreement**: What memory anomalies *show* about the link between subjective certainty and objective fact.
- **Current state**: Foundational; the Map's standing position is that subjective certainty is not evidence for the corresponding metaphysical claim, which these anomalies illustrate beautifully.

## Potential Article Angles

The target article is `topics/memory-anomalies.md`. Possible framings, in rough order of fit with the Map:

1. **"The phenomenology gap" angle** — Use the three anomalies as a diagnostic showing that cognitive science can describe the *signal* (familiarity, recollection, source-monitoring) but not the *felt quality* of having been here before, of finding a known word alien, of mistaking a memory for an idea. Lead with this; it's the clearest path to tenet alignment via the hard problem.
2. **"Source opacity" angle** — Frame cryptomnesia, déjà vu, and jamais vu as three faces of one fact: the conscious subject does not have direct access to the provenance of its own contents. Connect to source-monitoring research and to the broader Map theme that introspection is unreliable.
3. **"Bergsonian dualist" angle** — Foreground Bergson's "memory of the present" and the reducing-valve picture; treat the anomalies as glimpses of normally-occluded structure. Risk: pulls toward speculative metaphysics; should be balanced with the cognitive evidence to avoid sliding into paranormal territory.
4. **"Pathology vs. health continuum" angle** — Use TLE déjà vu and Moulin's induced jamais vu to show that anomalies are normal-mechanism states pushed past threshold, not categorically distinct dysfunctions. Connect to the Map's interest in altered states and mystical experience.
5. **"What the anomalies are NOT evidence for" angle** — Briefly engage with paranormal interpretations (past lives, precognition) to show that the Map's dualism is principled rather than credulous; déjà vu does not prove past lives, even if it tells us something interesting about consciousness.

## Gaps in Research

- **Direct philosophy of mind treatment of jamais vu**: Almost none exists; nearly all coverage is empirical (Moulin's group). An article-length piece could note this gap and contribute its first conceptual analysis.
- **Cross-cultural data**: How robust are the prevalence and phenomenology of these anomalies across cultures? Most data is from WEIRD samples; this should be noted as an honest limitation.
- **Relationship to dreaming**: There are scattered claims that déjà vu often pairs with a sense that the prior "experience" was a dream, but the empirical literature is thin and inconclusive. Worth acknowledging without overclaiming.
- **Apex-level synthesis with other Map articles on memory**: Need to integrate with `episodic-memory-consciousness-2026-01-16`, `implicit-memory-consciousness-2026-01-18`, and `memory-dual-domain-capability-2026-03-09`. The article should cross-reference and the apex layer should connect the threads.
- **Computational / predictive-processing accounts**: Recent work frames déjà vu as a prediction-error misattribution under predictive coding; not deeply explored here, would benefit from a follow-up search if the article needs more cognitive-science depth.
