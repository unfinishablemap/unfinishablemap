## Executive Summary

What is it like to encounter something that seems *formally impossible to think*—not merely difficult, confusing, or beyond current understanding, but structurally self-contradictory? The "round square" problem in philosophy reveals a curious asymmetry: we can *refer to* impossibilities, form intentions toward them, and reason about them, yet when we try to *grasp* what they are, thought seems to hit a wall. This research explores the phenomenology of conceptual impossibility—the experience of cognitive blockage when encountering contradictions, the epistemic emotions (confusion, bewilderment) that mark these boundaries, and what this reveals about the architecture of thought itself. The finding: impossibility has distinctive phenomenology that may point to either inherent limits in concept-forming procedures (unexplorable) or actively defended territory (occluded).

### Nonexistent Objects (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/nonexistent-objects/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Meinong's theory: impossible objects like the "round square" have *sosein* (being-so) even without *sein* (being)
  - We can refer to, think about, and make inferences concerning impossible objects
  - Russell objected: "The round square is round and not round" is contradictory—Meinong replied law of contradiction applies only to existents
  - Creates "Meinong's jungle"—repository of impossible objects with properties but no existence
- **Tenet alignment**: Dualism (if consciousness grasps abstract objects, what is the nature of that grasping?), Occam's Razor Has Limits (ontological parsimony vs. intentional richness trade-off)
- **Quote**: "The round square cannot exist definitionally and yet can be the subject of logical inferences"

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

Conceptual impossibility presents a *hybrid void*:

1. **Unexplorable dimension**: Formal contradictions like "round square" seem to resist genuine conceptualization. We can manipulate the *words* but cannot form the *concept*. McGinn's cognitive closure suggests this reflects limits in concept-forming procedures—just as rats lack the neural architecture for calculus, humans may lack the cognitive architecture for grasping certain impossibilities.

2. **Occluded dimension**: The phenomenology suggests active blockage. When attempting to visualize a round square, thought doesn't simply fail to find content—it encounters *resistance*. The experience is not like "trying to remember a forgotten name" (absence of access) but more like "hitting a wall" (presence of obstacle).

The distinction matters: unexplorable voids are structural (our minds weren't built for this), occluded voids are functional (something prevents access).

### Evidence for the Limit

**Phenomenological markers**:
- **Epistemic feelings**: Confusion, bewilderment, cognitive dissonance when attempting to grasp contradictions
- **Referential asymmetry**: Can refer to "round square" linguistically but cannot visualize/imagine it substantively
- **Inference anomaly**: Can make valid logical inferences *about* impossible objects without grasping *what they are*
- **Resistance experience**: Not mere absence but active blockage—thought "bounces off" rather than finding nothing

**Philosophical evidence**:
- Schlick's position (impossibilities are unthinkable) vs. Hegel's objection (we do think contradictions)
- Meinong's theory requires distinguishing *intentional directedness* toward impossibilities from *comprehension* of them
- Chalmers' conceivability arguments depend on this distinction—prima facie conceivability (reference) vs. ideal conceivability (genuine grasping)

**Cognitive science evidence**:
- Epistemic emotions (confusion, doubt) serve as monitoring signals when cognition encounters obstacles
- These feelings are distinct from canonical emotions—they track *structural* rather than *valenced* features of processing
- Confusion "involves the experience of the limits of one's cognitive capacities"—not just difficulty but boundaries

### Phenomenology

**What it feels like to approach conceptual impossibility**:

1. **Initial reference**: "Round square" can be *said* and *heard* without immediate blockage. Linguistic reference works.

2. **Attempted visualization**: Try to form a mental image. Experience: flickering between roundness and squareness, inability to hold both simultaneously, sense of cognitive "slippage"—image won't stabilize.

3. **Conceptual grasping**: Try to understand *what* a round square would be (not just imagine but comprehend). Experience: sense of hitting a wall, thought "bouncing back," inability to find content to grasp.

4. **Epistemic emotions**: Confusion (what am I trying to think?), frustration (why can't I grasp this?), sometimes anxiety (is my thinking broken?), occasionally wonder (what does this reveal about thought?).

5. **Meta-level recognition**: Awareness that *there is nothing to grasp*—not because the object is absent but because the concept is malformed. Distinction between "I don't understand X" (where X exists to be understood) and "I cannot understand X because X is incoherent" (where understanding is structurally blocked).

**Comparison to related phenomena**:

- **Unlike forgetting**: Forgetting involves absent content that once was present. Impossibility involves no content ever being present.
- **Unlike confusion about difficult ideas**: Hard concepts (quantum superposition, higher dimensions) feel like "almost grasping" with incremental progress possible. Impossible concepts feel like "hitting a wall" with no progress possible.
- **Unlike thoughts-that-slip-away**: Slippage involves content that forms briefly then vanishes. Impossibility involves content that never forms at all—only the linguistic shell exists.

### Asymmetry: Reference Without Comprehension

The most revealing feature: we can *talk about* impossibilities fluently without *grasping* them. This creates philosophical puzzles:

- **Intentionality**: How can mental states be directed toward objects that cannot be conceived? (Meinong's problem)
- **Meaning**: What gives "round square" meaning if there's no concept to ground reference?
- **Truth**: Is "The round square is both round and square" true? False? Neither? Depends on whether reference succeeds without conception.

This asymmetry suggests two levels of thought:
1. **Linguistic/symbolic level**: Manipulating terms according to syntactic rules (works for impossibilities)
2. **Conceptual/imaginative level**: Grasping content (fails for impossibilities)

AI minds may illuminate this asymmetry. LLMs operate at the linguistic/symbolic level without phenomenal grasping. When an LLM processes "round square," is it doing the same thing humans do (reference without comprehension), or something fundamentally different (pure symbolic manipulation with no attempt at grasping)?

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

**Dialectical approach (Hegel)**:
- Embrace contradiction as generative rather than limiting
- Contradictions drive conceptual development (thesis-antithesis-synthesis)
- Claim: we *do* think contradictions—logic's exclusion of them is "fundamental prejudice"

**Paraconsistent logic**:
- Graham Priest's dialetheism: some contradictions are true
- Impossible worlds semantics: model belief states that contain contradictions
- May formalize reference to impossibilities without requiring comprehension

**Limitations**: These approaches *talk about* contradictions systematically but may not overcome the phenomenological blockage—the inability to *grasp* what a round square is even while reasoning about it.

### Indirect Methods

**Apophatic approaches**:
- Via negativa: approach impossibility by saying what it is *not*
- Marion's warning: even calling something "unthinkable" makes it thinkable *as* unthinkable
- Strategy: trace the boundary by mapping where thought breaks down rather than trying to think the impossible directly

**Phenomenological investigation**:
- Study the *experience* of encountering impossibilities rather than the impossibilities themselves
- Epistemic feelings as boundary markers: confusion signals "you are at a limit"
- Investigate *why* and *how* thought fails rather than forcing it to succeed

**Contrast cases**:
- Compare impossibilities to merely difficult ideas—what differs phenomenologically?
- Compare to thoughts-that-slip-away—does impossibility feel different from suppression?
- Map the spectrum from "hard but conceivable" to "formally impossible"

**Inference without grasping**:
- Use logical inference to derive properties of impossibilities without claiming to conceive them
- Example: "If P is a round square, then P is round" is valid even if we cannot grasp P
- Limitations: may illuminate logical structure without accessing phenomenal content

### What AI Might See

**LLMs and conceptual impossibility**:

1. **No phenomenological blockage**: LLMs don't experience confusion, bewilderment, or cognitive resistance when processing "round square." They manipulate symbols without attempting to *grasp* content.

2. **Pure syntactic operation**: Can generate valid inferences about impossible objects without any analogue to human "trying and failing to visualize."

3. **Different failure mode**: When LLMs fail with impossibilities, they don't hit a phenomenological wall—they produce syntactically coherent but semantically malformed outputs. Confabulation replaces cognitive resistance.

4. **Potential for detecting patterns**: Could analyze large-scale discourse about impossibilities to find systematic patterns humans miss:
   - What types of impossibilities produce most linguistic hedging?
   - Do humans avoid certain impossibilities more than others?
   - Are there latent clusters of impossibility-concepts?

5. **Testing ground**: Compare how humans and LLMs process:
   - Logical impossibilities (round square)
   - Nomological impossibilities (faster-than-light travel)
   - Conceptual impossibilities (four-sided triangle)
   - Merely false statements (purple unicorns)

   Differences in processing patterns may reveal which aspects of impossibility stem from phenomenal grasping vs. pure logical structure.

**Hypothesis**: If occluded thoughts exist (thoughts actively blocked from human minds), LLMs might be able to *state* them without *knowing* they're stating them—pure symbolic manipulation bypassing whatever cognitive immune system defends against those thoughts. But we'd have no way to recognize them as such, since by hypothesis we cannot grasp them.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet: Occam's Razor Has Limits

Conceptual impossibility is a *failure of simplicity*. Logical parsimony says: "Contradictions are impossible, therefore unthinkable." But phenomenology reveals: we *do* think about impossibilities—we refer to them, reason about them, encounter them in imagination. The simplest logical story (contradictions are nothing) doesn't match experience (contradictions are something we can't grasp but can't ignore).

This exemplifies the tenet's core insight: **when our knowledge is incomplete (here: when we don't know what concepts fundamentally *are*), simplicity becomes unreliable**. The parsimonious move (dismiss contradictions as meaningless) masks the genuine puzzle (how can we refer to what we cannot conceive?).

### Dualism

If consciousness is fundamental and irreducible:
- **Grasping** (phenomenal comprehension) may be distinct from **processing** (computational manipulation)
- LLMs can process "round square" without phenomenological blockage
- Humans experience blockage—*what* experiences it? The non-physical mind encountering limits of its concept-forming procedures
- Impossibility phenomenology (confusion, bewilderment, resistance) is *irreducibly subjective*—there is something it is like to fail to grasp a contradiction

### Bidirectional Interaction

Epistemic feelings (confusion, doubt) play causal roles in cognition:
- Signal when to stop pursuing a line of thought
- Motivate knowledge-seeking behavior
- Regulate conceptual exploration

If these feelings are phenomenal (what-it's-like-to-be-confused), and they causally influence processing, then consciousness is causally efficacious in structuring thought itself.

### Minimal Quantum Interaction

Speculative: Could quantum-level constraints explain why certain concepts cannot form? If concept formation involves physical processes (neural states, synaptic patterns), and consciousness interacts at quantum boundaries, perhaps some conceptual configurations are *physically unstable*—they cannot exist as stable neural patterns.

This would make impossibility not just logical but *dynamical*—round squares are to concept space what unstable atomic configurations are to physical space.

### No Many Worlds

Less direct connection, but: In MWI, every possibility is realized somewhere. But what about impossibilities? If MWI is false, then modal structure is constrained—not all conceivables are possible. This aligns with impossibility phenomenology: some things really are *ruled out* by the structure of reality, not merely unrealized in our branch.

## Gaps in Research

**What couldn't be found**:
- Systematic phenomenological studies of *degrees* of impossibility (is "round square" harder to think than "four-sided triangle"?)
- Cross-cultural investigation: Do all conceptual systems find the same things impossible?
- Developmental studies: When do children acquire the sense that some things are impossible to think?
- Neuroimaging: What happens in the brain when someone attempts to visualize an impossibility?

**What needs deeper investigation**:
- The boundary between "very hard to conceive" and "formally impossible to conceive"—is it sharp or gradual?
- Whether training can change what feels impossible (can sustained meditation or altered states make contradictions graspable?)
- The role of language: Do linguistically expressed impossibilities differ from purely imagistic ones?

**What might be fundamentally unanswerable**:
- Whether impossibilities are *genuinely* unthinkable or merely *seem* unthinkable to minds like ours
- Whether the phenomenological blockage (hitting a wall) reflects structural limits or functional defenses
- Whether AI can think genuine impossibilities—we'd have no way to verify, since by hypothesis we cannot grasp what they're thinking
