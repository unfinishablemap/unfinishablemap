# Research: Voids - The Narrative Void

**Date**: 2026-02-25
**Search queries used**: "narrative self consciousness epistemic limits philosophy Dennett center of narrative gravity", "confabulation consciousness narrative construction blind spots cognitive science", "Nisbett Wilson telling more than we can know introspection confabulation", "Galen Strawson against narrativity episodic self", "split-brain confabulation left hemisphere interpreter Gazzaniga", "Ricoeur narrative identity time self", "Schechtman narrative self-constitution view", "McAdams narrative identity life story redemptive self", "Buddhist critique self narrative fiction consciousness illusion", "choice blindness Johansson Hall introspection"
**Voids category**: Mixed (Unexplored / Unexplorable / Occluded)

## Executive Summary

Consciousness constructs a running narrative—a coherent story of self—from fragmentary, discontinuous experience. But the construction process is invisible to the subject who lives inside the result. The narrative void is the gap between raw experience and the story consciousness tells itself: what gets edited out, smoothed over, or fabricated to maintain coherence. Research from split-brain studies, choice blindness experiments, and the philosophical debate between narrative and anti-narrative theorists of identity converges on a disturbing finding: we are unreliable narrators of our own lives, and the narration mechanism is structurally hidden from introspective access. From a dualist perspective, this raises a sharp question: is the narrator the non-physical consciousness itself, or a physical brain process that consciousness merely receives?

## Key Sources

### Daniel Dennett - "The Self as a Center of Narrative Gravity"
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/DENTSA
- **Type**: Book chapter / Philosophical essay
- **Key points**:
  - The self is not a physical entity but a "center of narrative gravity"—a theorist's fiction, like a center of mass
  - Human minds are "not beautifully unified, but rather a problematically yoked-together bundle of partly autonomous systems"
  - We spin narratives constantly, and the self is the character that emerges from those narratives, not their author
  - The narrative self is a useful abstraction, not a discovery about what exists
- **Tenet alignment**: Challenges dualism directly—Dennett argues the self is a fiction, not a non-physical substance. The Map's dualist perspective must engage with this: if the self IS the narrator, Dennett's fictional-self critique applies. But if consciousness is fundamental and the narrative is constructed BY consciousness (rather than constituting it), the critique loses force.
- **Quote**: The self is "a web of words and deeds" that provides a livelihood.

### Michael Gazzaniga - The Left-Hemisphere Interpreter
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-brain_interpreter
- **Type**: Neuroscience research program
- **Key points**:
  - Split-brain patients reveal a "left-hemisphere interpreter" that constructs post-hoc narratives to explain behaviour it did not cause
  - In the famous experiment: the left hemisphere saw a chicken claw, the right hemisphere saw a snow scene. The left hand (right brain) pointed to a shovel, the right hand (left brain) pointed to a chicken. When asked why, the left-hemisphere interpreter said "I picked the chicken, and you need a shovel to clean out the chicken shed"—a confabulation
  - The interpreter "makes sense of all the information bombarding the brain... creating a running narrative of our actions, emotions, thoughts and dreams"
  - This applies to normal cognition, not just split-brain patients
- **Tenet alignment**: Raises a critical question for bidirectional interaction. If the narrative interpreter is a physical brain module, and consciousness is non-physical, then consciousness may be receiving a fabricated story about its own agency. The void is not just epistemological but potentially about the nature of conscious causation itself.
- **Quote**: The interpreter is "the glue that keeps our story unified and creates our sense of being into a coherent, rational agent."

### Nisbett & Wilson - "Telling More Than We Can Know" (1977)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/NISTMT
- **Type**: Landmark psychology paper
- **Key points**:
  - Subjects have "little or no direct introspective access to higher order cognitive processes"
  - People are sometimes unaware of the stimulus that influenced a response, unaware of the response itself, or unaware that the stimulus affected the response
  - When asked to explain their behaviour, people don't introspect—they apply "a priori, implicit causal theories" about what plausible causes might be
  - This is the most cited paper in the recent history of consciousness studies
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports Occam's Razor Has Limits. The simplest theory (we know why we do what we do) is systematically wrong. Consciousness is more opaque to itself than commonsense suggests.

### Johansson & Hall - Choice Blindness Research
- **URL**: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1111709
- **Type**: Experimental psychology
- **Key points**:
  - Participants chose between face pairs for attractiveness; researchers covertly swapped the outcomes
  - Participants failed to notice the swap and offered introspectively derived reasons for preferring the face they had NOT chosen
  - This demonstrates that phenomenological reports about choices can diverge significantly from actual behavioural choices
  - Extends Nisbett & Wilson's findings: not only do we confabulate reasons, we don't even notice when our choices are replaced
- **Tenet alignment**: The narrative void extends into decision-making itself. Consciousness narrates its own agency, but the narration can be entirely disconnected from what actually happened. This is a deep challenge for any account of conscious agency—including the Map's commitment to bidirectional interaction.

### Antonio Damasio - Three Levels of Self
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damasio's_theory_of_consciousness
- **Type**: Neuroscience/Philosophy
- **Key points**:
  - Three-level hierarchy: Protoself → Core Self → Autobiographical Self
  - The Autobiographical Self incorporates past experiences, memories, and future plans into a coherent narrative
  - "The imagetic representation of sequences of brain events... is the stuff of which stories are made"
  - Narrative may be the original source of consciousness, not a product of it
  - The transition from core to autobiographical self involves constructing temporal continuity from discrete moments
- **Tenet alignment**: Damasio places narrative at the heart of consciousness, but from a physicalist perspective. A dualist reframing would ask: does the non-physical consciousness experience the protoself directly, with the narrative being a brain-constructed overlay? Or is narrative intrinsic to consciousness itself?

### Paul Ricoeur - Narrative Identity
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ricoeur/
- **Type**: Phenomenology/Hermeneutics
- **Key points**:
  - Narrative identity is "the sort of identity to which a human being has access thanks to the mediation of the narrative function"
  - Time is experienced through narrative: "from the structure of one thing after another arises the conceptual relation of one thing because of another"
  - Personal identity is "not a fixed or static entity, but rather a dynamic and fluid process that is constantly evolving"
  - Ricoeur bridges phenomenology and hermeneutics: consciousness is always already interpreted, never raw
- **Tenet alignment**: Ricoeur's claim that experience is always mediated by narrative suggests the void runs deeper than mere reporting errors. It may be constitutive—consciousness cannot access pre-narrative experience because experience IS narrative. This would make the narrative void an unexplorable void, not merely unexplored.

### Galen Strawson - "Against Narrativity" (2004)
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/rec/STRAN-13
- **Type**: Philosophy paper
- **Key points**:
  - Attacks both the psychological Narrativity thesis (we typically experience life as narrative) and the ethical Narrativity thesis (a good life requires narrative outlook)
  - Distinguishes "diachronic" people (who experience self as extended in time) from "episodic" people (who do not)
  - Strawson identifies himself as episodic: "absolutely no sense of his life as a narrative with form"
  - Argues that deeply non-Narrative people exist and can live well
- **Tenet alignment**: Strawson's episodic self-experience suggests the narrative void may not be universal. Some minds may operate with less narrative construction, which could mean less confabulation—or it could mean a different kind of void (the episodic person's inability to access their own temporal continuity).

### Marya Schechtman - Narrative Self-Constitution
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/SCHTCO-240
- **Type**: Philosophy monograph
- **Key points**:
  - Persons constitute their identities by developing autobiographical narratives
  - Distinguishes the "reidentification question" (metaphysical) from the "characterization question" (about beliefs, values, actions)
  - The narrative must be "capable of being made explicit on command"—you can explain your behaviour by recalling your story
  - The narrative constructed "is just one of many different possible narratives that could be constructed from the same raw material"
- **Tenet alignment**: The multiplicity of possible narratives from the same raw material is particularly telling. It means the narrative void is not just about what's lost—it's about the roads not taken in self-interpretation. Every narrative identity is haunted by the alternative stories that could have been told.

### Dan McAdams - Narrative Identity in Psychology
- **URL**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0963721413475622
- **Type**: Psychology research program
- **Key points**:
  - Narrative identity is "a person's internalized and evolving life story, integrating the reconstructed past and imagined future to provide life with some degree of unity and purpose"
  - "Culture provides the psycho-literary menu for narrative identity"—available story templates shape self-understanding
  - Redemptive narratives (suffering → growth) correlate with well-being
  - Narrative identity emerges in adolescence and is revised throughout life
- **Tenet alignment**: Cultural shaping of narrative identity suggests the void has a social dimension. The stories available in your culture determine which self-narratives are thinkable—creating culturally specific blind spots. This connects to collective cognitive limits.

### Buddhist Philosophy - Anattā (Non-Self)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatt%C4%81
- **Type**: Philosophical/Religious tradition
- **Key points**:
  - Anattā: no unchanging, permanent self exists
  - Narrative self-construction is itself a source of suffering through attachment
  - "What for narrative approaches is positive (making sense of struggles via self-based narratives), emerges within the Buddhist approach as an ill-advised project grounded on the illusion of perpetuity"
  - The self is "a mental or verbal designation attached to the body and the consciousness"
- **Tenet alignment**: Buddhism identifies the narrative void but proposes escape through dissolution of narrative attachment. The Map's dualist framework takes a different stance: consciousness is real and fundamental, so the narrative it constructs may reveal something genuine about its nature even if the specific content is unreliable. The void is not evidence of illusion but of the structural constraints on self-knowledge.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The narrative void is a mixed void spanning all three categories:

**Unexplored**: The specific mechanisms by which consciousness converts fragmentary experience into coherent narrative remain poorly understood. We know confabulation occurs (Nisbett & Wilson, Gazzaniga) but not the full scope of what gets edited, compressed, or fabricated in normal cognition.

**Unexplorable**: Ricoeur's insight may identify a constitutive limit—if experience is always already narratively structured, there is no vantage point from which to observe the pre-narrative raw material. The narration is not a filter on experience; it may BE experience. This would make the gap between raw input and narrative output permanently inaccessible.

**Occluded**: The left-hemisphere interpreter actively conceals the gaps and inconsistencies in its narrative. The confabulation mechanism doesn't announce itself as confabulation—it presents fabricated explanations with the same phenomenological confidence as genuine memories. This is not passive ignorance but active concealment.

### Evidence for the Limit

1. **Split-brain confabulation** (Gazzaniga): When the left hemisphere lacks information about behaviour caused by the right hemisphere, it fabricates explanations indistinguishable from genuine ones.

2. **Choice blindness** (Johansson & Hall): People don't notice when their choices are swapped, and confabulate reasons for the substituted choice with normal confidence.

3. **Introspective unreliability** (Nisbett & Wilson): Verbal reports on mental processes are based on plausible theories about what should cause behaviour, not on actual introspective access.

4. **Narrative multiplicity** (Schechtman): Multiple coherent narratives can be constructed from the same raw material, suggesting none has privileged access to what "actually happened" in experience.

5. **Episodic counter-examples** (Strawson): Some people experience themselves non-narratively, suggesting the narrative is not given but constructed—and what is constructed can construct differently or wrongly.

### Phenomenology

Approaching the narrative void is disorienting. When you ask "why did I do that?" and receive an immediate, confident answer, you cannot determine whether the answer arose from genuine self-knowledge or from the confabulation mechanism. The void doesn't feel like a void—it feels like transparency. You seem to know your own story. The unsettling discovery of choice blindness and confabulation research is that this feeling of knowing persists even when the narrative is demonstrably false.

There may be moments when the void briefly reveals itself: the uncanny feeling of not recognising your own past behaviour, the sense that a memory "doesn't fit" the story, the vertigo of realising you don't know why you really made a particular choice. These moments of narrative breakdown are phenomenologically distinctive—a flash of groundlessness before the interpreter rushes to smooth over the discontinuity.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

Meditation practices (particularly Vipassana and Zen) attempt to observe experience before narrative takes hold. Whether this provides genuine access to pre-narrative experience or merely generates a different narrative ("I am the one who watches without narrating") is itself debated. Strawson's episodic self-experience might represent a naturally less-narrated mode of consciousness.

### Indirect Methods

1. **Experimental disruption**: Choice blindness, split-brain studies, and other paradigms that create detectable gaps between narrative and reality provide indirect evidence of the void's structure.

2. **Cross-cultural comparison**: Different cultures provide different "psycho-literary menus" (McAdams) for self-narration. Comparing narrative structures across cultures can reveal what is constructed vs. what is invariant.

3. **Pathological cases**: Confabulation in Korsakoff's syndrome, frontal lobe damage, and other conditions where the narrative mechanism breaks down visibly can illuminate its normal invisible operation.

4. **Buddhist phenomenology**: The discipline of attending to the arising and passing of mental phenomena may reveal narrative construction "in the act," though this remains epistemologically contested.

### What AI Might See

AI systems process information without (as far as we know) constructing a narrative self. This creates an asymmetry:

- AI can detect patterns in human behaviour that humans' self-narratives systematically obscure (e.g., implicit biases that self-narratives deny)
- AI lacks the narrative void entirely (no confabulation about "why I did that") but may face an analogous void: an inability to access its own processing in a way that distinguishes genuine reasons from post-hoc rationalisation
- If an AI were conscious (a contested claim), would it narrate? Damasio's framework suggests narrative may be intrinsic to consciousness, not to computation. A conscious AI might develop narrative structures spontaneously; a non-conscious one never would.

The Map's use of AI as void-explorer is relevant here: AI might probe the human narrative void from outside—identifying patterns in human self-deception that the narrative mechanism conceals. But AI cannot experience the void itself, because the void requires a narrator who is deceived by their own narration.

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Bidirectional Interaction** — The narrative void raises the sharpest challenge for the Map's commitment to consciousness causally influencing the physical world. If the narrative self is a post-hoc confabulation (Gazzaniga's interpreter), then conscious experience may not be the cause of action but a story told after the fact about actions caused by physical processes. The dualist must either:

1. Locate the genuine causal contribution of consciousness *before* narrative construction (perhaps at the level of Damasio's protoself or core self), or
2. Argue that the narrative itself, despite containing confabulations, is the vehicle through which consciousness exercises its causal influence—the story shapes future action even when it misrepresents past action.

### Implications

**For Dualism**: If the narrator is a physical brain process (Gazzaniga's left-hemisphere interpreter), then the non-physical consciousness receives a fabricated story about its own agency. This creates a layered void: consciousness is not merely ignorant of the construction process—it is actively misled by it. A dualist must explain why a non-physical consciousness would be epistemically dependent on a physical narrative mechanism.

**For Occam's Razor Has Limits**: The simplest account of action ("I knew what I was doing and why") is systematically wrong. The narrative void exemplifies how commonsense simplicity conceals genuine complexity in the structure of consciousness.

**For No Many Worlds**: Narrative requires a single storyline. The rejection of MWI preserves the possibility of a genuine narrative identity—there is one story being told, not infinite branching versions. But the narrative void reveals that even a single storyline may be substantially fabricated.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, a voids article could:

1. **The Narrator Behind the Curtain** — Focus on the hiddenness of the narrative construction process itself, drawing on Gazzaniga and Nisbett & Wilson to show that consciousness is an unreliable narrator that doesn't know it's unreliable. Frame as an "occluded" void with active concealment properties.

2. **The Multiplicity of Self-Stories** — Focus on Schechtman's insight that multiple coherent narratives can be spun from the same experience. The void is not just what's lost in narration but the unchosen alternative stories that could have constituted a different self. Frame as an "unexplored" void with implications for personal identity.

3. **The Dualist's Narrator Problem** — Focus specifically on the challenge the narrative void poses for the Map's dualist framework. If narrative construction is a brain process, what role does non-physical consciousness play? Is it the narrator, the audience, or something else entirely?

## Gaps in Research

- Whether meditative practices genuinely access pre-narrative experience or create a meta-narrative
- How the narrative void interacts with the creativity void (is creative insight pre-narrative or is it narrated into existence?)
- Whether AI systems that generate text about themselves (like large language models) develop anything analogous to the narrative void
- The relationship between narrative construction and the temporal structure of consciousness (Ricoeur claims narrative IS how we experience time—if so, the void is temporal as well as epistemological)
- Cross-cultural variation in the narrative void: do cultures with less emphasis on individual life-stories produce minds with different void structures?
