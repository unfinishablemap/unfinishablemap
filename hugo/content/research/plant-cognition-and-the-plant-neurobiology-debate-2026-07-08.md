# Research: Plant Cognition and the Plant-Neurobiology Debate

**Date**: 2026-07-08
**Search queries used**:

- Taiz 2019 "Plants Neither Possess nor Require Consciousness"

## Executive Summary

"Plant neurobiology" is a research programme launched in 2006 (Brenner, Stahlberg, Trewavas, Baluška, Mancuso, Van Volkenburgh) proposing that plants integrate environmental information via long-distance electrical signals, auxin transport, and "neuronal" molecules — and, in its bolder framings, that plants exhibit cognition, intelligence, learning, and even a form of sentience. It drew a sharp 36-author rebuttal (Alpi et al. 2007, "no brain, no gain?") and a later hard-line dismissal of plant consciousness (Taiz et al. 2019). The empirical flashpoints are Gagliano's contested learning experiments (Mimosa habituation 2014; pea-plant Pavlovian conditioning 2016), both of which met published critiques and a failed replication. Underneath the terminology war sits a genuine, uncontested biology: plants DO perform sophisticated adaptive information processing (glutamate-receptor-like wound signalling, Venus flytrap action-potential counting and short-term "memory") entirely WITHOUT neurons. For the Map, plants are the cleanest available test case of whether valence-guided, information-processing behaviour requires neural architecture at all — and whether any of it amounts to phenomenal experience, which almost no one in the debate claims.

## Key Sources

### Brenner et al. (2006) — the founding manifesto
- **URL**: https://www.cell.com/trends/plant-science/abstract/S1360-1385(06)00164-6
- **Type**: Opinion paper, *Trends in Plant Science*
- **Key points**:
  - Proposed "plant neurobiology" as a field: plants process information to develop and reproduce optimally via "integrated signaling, communication and response."
  - Emphasised long-distance electrical signals, vesicle-mediated auxin transport, and molecules known as neuronal in animals (glutamate, GABA, acetylcholine).
  - Framed the whole-plant coordination as analogous to a nervous system.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Describes non-neural information integration — compatible with the Map's interest in adaptive behaviour, but the paper makes no phenomenal-consciousness claim.

### Alpi et al. (2007) — "no brain, no gain?"
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17368081/
- **Type**: Letter (36 signatories), *Trends in Plant Science*
- **Key points**:
  - Objected that "plant neurobiology" implies plants have nerves, synapses, a root-localised brain, and intelligence — structures they lack.
  - Argued the label adds nothing to plant physiology, cell biology, or signalling and risks misleading by superficial animal analogy.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral / methodologically cautionary. A brake on over-reading behaviour as mind — resonates with the Map's discipline about not conflating information processing with experience.

### Taiz et al. (2019) — "Plants Neither Possess nor Require Consciousness"
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31279732/
- **Type**: Opinion paper, *Trends in Plant Science*
- **Key points**:
  - Directly rejects plant consciousness, cognition-as-experience, intentionality, emotion, and pain.
  - Leans on Feinberg & Mallatt's account of the minimal neural/brain complexity required for consciousness in animals; concludes the probability of plant consciousness is "effectively nil" given plants' lack of neurons and brains.
- **Tenet alignment**: Partially convergent with the Map's caution, but its underlying stance is that consciousness is grounded in specific neural architecture — a **physicalist / neural-necessity** premise the Map's dualism resists. Useful as the sharpest articulation of the "neural architecture is required" position the Map wants to test.

### Gagliano et al. (2014) — Mimosa habituation
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00442-013-2873-7
- **Type**: Experimental paper, *Oecologia*
- **Key points**:
  - Repeated non-harmful drop-shocks led *Mimosa pudica* to stop closing its leaves (habituation), with claimed stimulus specificity and retention over weeks; learning was faster in low-light (resource-poor) conditions.
  - Interpreted as a basic, non-neural form of learning/memory.
- **Contest**: A 2018 *Oecologia* response argued the data show no genuine dishabituation, lack a control to establish stimulus specificity, and are largely explicable by motor fatigue rather than learning.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Even if real, habituation is adaptive behaviour, not evidence of phenomenal experience.

### Gagliano et al. (2016) — "Learning by Association in Plants" (pea Pavlovian)
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/srep38427
- **Type**: Experimental paper, *Scientific Reports*
- **Key points**:
  - Claimed classical (Pavlovian) conditioning in pea plants: light (unconditioned stimulus) paired with a fan/wind (neutral → conditioned stimulus); trained plants grew toward the fan-predicted light source.
- **Contest**: Markel (2020, *eLife*) attempted replication and found no associative-learning effect; the result remains unreplicated, including by the original lab. Widely cited as the "most convincing" conditioning claim but not established.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral, and evidentially weak. A cautionary case in the Map's calibration about over-attributing mind to behaviour.

### Böhm et al. (2016) — Venus flytrap counts action potentials
- **URL**: https://www.cell.com/fulltext/S0960-9822(15)01501-8
- **Type**: Experimental paper, *Current Biology*
- **Key points**:
  - Prey touches trigger mechanosensor action potentials (APs); the trap counts them. Two APs in quick succession close the trap; further APs (≥5) trigger digestive-gland gene expression and sodium/nutrient uptake.
  - The count relies on a decaying cytosolic Ca²⁺ signal — a genuine electrical short-term "memory": if the second stimulus is too slow, the first AP is effectively forgotten.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral, but pivotal for the Map: a concrete, mechanistic case of decision-relevant counting and short-term memory with NO nervous system.

### Toyota et al. (2018) — glutamate/GLR long-distance wound signalling
- **URL**: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aat7744
- **Type**: Experimental paper, *Science*
- **Key points**:
  - Wounding releases glutamate, sensed by GLUTAMATE RECEPTOR-LIKE (GLR) channels, triggering a systemic Ca²⁺ wave that propagates across the plant within minutes and primes defence in distant, undamaged leaves.
  - GLR channels are evolutionary relatives of animal ionotropic glutamate receptors — the molecular substrate behind "neuronal-molecule" claims, but doing plant-specific signalling.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. The strongest uncontested evidence that plants run fast, integrated, electrochemical whole-body signalling without neurons.

### Calvo (2016) — "The philosophy of plant neurobiology: a manifesto"
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-016-1040-1
- **Type**: Philosophy paper, *Synthese*
- **Key points**:
  - Argues for a "Philosophy of Plant Neurobiology" at the intersection of philosophy of cognitive science and plant biology; treats plants as candidates for minimal cognition under embodied/enactive frameworks.
  - Represents the reflective, non-inflationary wing of the movement: cognition-as-adaptive-behaviour, generally agnostic-to-deflationary about phenomenal experience.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-useful. Enactivism grounds "cognition" in behaviour, not experience — a framing the Map can use to sharply separate the two questions.

## Major Positions

### 1. Plant neurobiology / plant cognition (inflationary wing)
- **Proponents**: Baluška, Mancuso, Trewavas, Stahlberg (and, for learning, Gagliano).
- **Core claim**: Plants integrate information, learn, remember, and behave "intelligently"; some proponents float sentience/awareness.
- **Key arguments**: Electrical signalling, GLR channels, neuronal molecules, whole-plant coordination, contested learning results.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The behavioural claims are Map-neutral. The Map should NOT endorse the sentience gloss on this evidence — nothing here reaches phenomenal experience. But the programme usefully widens the space of "value-sensitive selection without neurons."

### 2. Deflationary / eliminative critique
- **Proponents**: Alpi et al. (36 authors); Taiz, Alkon, Draguhn, Blatt et al.
- **Core claim**: Plant "neurobiology" is a misleading metaphor; plants have neither consciousness nor cognition-as-experience and require neither.
- **Key arguments**: No neurons, no brain, no integrative structures of the kind consciousness demands (Feinberg–Mallatt neural-minimum criteria); learning results are unreplicated/confounded.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Its *caution* aligns with the Map; its *grounding premise* — that consciousness is constituted by neural architecture — is a physicalist neural-necessity claim the Map's **Tenet 1 (dualism)** contests. The Map can accept "plants very probably have no experience" WITHOUT accepting "because experience just is neural computation."

### 3. Minimal-cognition / enactivist reframing
- **Proponents**: Calvo (philosophy); parts of the embodied-cognition literature.
- **Core claim**: "Cognition" can be defined behaviourally/functionally (adaptive, anticipatory sensorimotor coupling) and applied to plants without implying phenomenal consciousness.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Most useful to the Map. It cleanly decouples the two questions the Map cares about — information processing / adaptive valence-like behaviour vs. any phenomenal "what-it's-like."

## Key Debates

### Is "learning" in plants real?
- **Sides**: Gagliano et al. (yes — habituation, associative conditioning) vs. critics (Markel; the 2018 Mimosa response).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether observed behavioural changes are genuine learning/memory or artefacts (motor fatigue, missing controls, failed replication).
- **Current state**: Ongoing and unsettled; the associative-learning result is currently unreplicated and widely doubted, while non-associative habituation is more plausible but still contested.

### Is "cognition/intelligence/sentience" language warranted or metaphorical?
- **Sides**: Inflationary wing (substantive) vs. deflationists (misleading metaphor) vs. enactivists (functional redefinition).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether animal-derived mind vocabulary tracks something real in plants or imports unearned connotations of experience.
- **Current state**: The behaviour is real; the *labels* are the live dispute. Almost no serious participant asserts plant phenomenal consciousness.

### Does consciousness require neural architecture?
- **Sides**: Taiz et al. / Feinberg–Mallatt (yes) vs. the Map's own dualist openness (an open question).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the absence of neurons settles the absence of experience.
- **Current state**: This is precisely the question the Map's marginal-organism ladder is built to keep open — plants are its non-neural extreme.

1. **"Plant cognition without a nervous system"** (recommended): center the article on the Map's own question — can adaptive, information-processing, arguably valence-like behaviour exist with zero neural architecture? Answer: demonstrably yes (Venus flytrap counting, GLR wound signalling), which is why plants are the ladder's cleanest non-neural test point. Then hold the line: none of this evidences phenomenal experience, and the deflationists' *caution* is right even though their neural-necessity *premise* is a physicalist claim Tenet 1 rejects.
2. **The terminology war as a case study in the hard problem's displacement**: the debate is mostly about *words* (cognition/intelligence/sentience) precisely because everyone agrees on the behaviour and disagrees on what it licenses about experience — a live illustration of why the Map separates function from phenomenality.

## Connection to the Map (marginal-organism ladder / valence cluster)

The harvested gap is real: [marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection](/topics/marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection/) runs a consciousness/agency ladder from Hydra's diffuse nerve net to the single-celled slime mould *Physarum*, skipping the **multicellular, non-neural** rung. Plants fill exactly that rung — many-celled, no neurons, yet performing counting, memory, systemic electrical signalling, and (contestedly) learning.

- They are the extreme test of [valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/): if valence-guided or value-sensitive selection can be even loosely modelled in a neuron-free multicellular organism, that pressures any claim that neural architecture is necessary for adaptive value-tracking. If it cannot, plants sharpen where the threshold lies.
- They connect to [consciousness-in-simple-organisms](/topics/consciousness-in-simple-organisms/) as the non-neural bookend and to [birch-edge-of-sentience-and-the-five-tier-scale](/topics/birch-edge-of-sentience-and-the-five-tier-scale/) as an organism that sits *below* even the most permissive sentience frameworks.
- The Taiz/Feinberg–Mallatt neural-necessity premise is the direct foil for [animal-consciousness](/topics/animal-consciousness/) and the Map's dualism: the Map can agree plants very likely lack experience while refusing the reductive reason offered for it.
