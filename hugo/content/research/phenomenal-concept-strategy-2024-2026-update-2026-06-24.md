## Executive Summary

The phenomenal concept strategy (PCS) — the type-B physicalist move of locating the explanatory gap in our *concepts* rather than in reality — remains an active battleground in 2024-2026, but the debate has shifted from "does PCS work?" toward two more specific fronts. First, **the master-argument exchange has matured**: Chalmers's 2007 dilemma (phenomenal concepts are either too thin to explain the gap or too thick to be physically explicable) drew the Carruthers-Veillet (2017) equivocation reply, and 2025 sees fresh master-argument engagement (Sasaki 2025). Second, **the "new challenge" front** (Stoljar; powerful-qualities ontologies) plus the a-posteriori-physicalism literature (Zhou 2025, "indicating without revealing") asks whether phenomenal concepts can *indicate* their referents without *revealing* their natures — a reframing of the gap that sidesteps the older mode-of-presentation machinery. Crucially for the Map, two threads cut against PCS from inside the type-B camp: Derek Ball's externalist argument that **there are no phenomenal concepts at all** (which, if sound, removes PCS's explanans), and Martina Fürst's encapsulation account, which concludes the best explanation of phenomenal concepts **reinforces dualism**. The illusionist wing (Frankish, Kammerer) leans on a PCS-like introspection story while simultaneously straining it. Net assessment: nothing in the recent literature rescues PCS from the dilemma the Map already documents; the new material mostly sharpens *where* PCS fails and adds dualism-friendly variants.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Qualia: The Knowledge Argument"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qualia-knowledge/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - The "New Knowledge / Old Fact" view is explicitly framed as a specific instance of the broader Phenomenal Concept Strategy: Mary gains new *knowledge* under phenomenal concepts but learns no new *fact*.
  - Surveys the recognitional, quotational, and constitutional variants and their shared commitment to conceptual (not ontological) independence.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism) — preserves physicalism while conceding the epistemic gap.

### Wikipedia / Grokipedia — "Phenomenal concept strategy"
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenal_concept_strategy ; https://grokipedia.com/page/phenomenal_concept_strategy
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (tertiary, good for proponent map)
- **Key points**:
  - Confirms the proponent roster the Map already uses (Loar 1990/97, Papineau quotational, Balog constitutional, Carruthers-Veillet recognitional-isolation, Hill 1997 distinct-conceptual-roles).
  - Names Papineau's "antipathetic fallacy" as the diagnostic that phenomenal concepts make physicalism *feel* false even when true.
  - PCS advocates self-identify as type-B materialists: epistemic gap, no ontological gap; "our concepts are dualistic, but reality is monistic."

### Chalmers, D.J. — "Phenomenal Concepts and the Explanatory Gap" (2006/2007)
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/pceg.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (in Alter & Walter, eds., *Phenomenal Concepts and Phenomenal Knowledge*, OUP 2007)
- **Key points**:
  - The **master argument** dilemma. Let C be whatever feature of phenomenal concepts is supposed to explain our epistemic situation. Either C is *physically explicable* — in which case zombies share C and so C cannot explain why *we* have an explanatory gap — or C is *not physically explicable* — in which case C is itself a non-physical fact and physicalism has already lost.
  - Chalmers *concedes* that we possess phenomenal concepts with conceptual isolation; he denies that appeal to those concepts alone explains the gap, zombie-conceivability, or Mary's learning.
- **Tenet alignment**: The argument is dualism-supporting; aligns with Tenet 1.

### Carruthers, P. & Veillet, B. — "The Phenomenal Concept Strategy" (2007; reply collected 2017)
- **URL**: https://faculty.philosophy.umd.edu/pcarruthers/Phenomenal%20Concepts%20(Chalmers).pdf
- **Type**: Paper (J. of Consciousness Studies / reprinted)
- **Key points**:
  - Charges Chalmers's master argument with a **fallacy of equivocation** between *first-person* and *third-person* phenomenal concepts.
  - Argues a zombie and its conscious twin can share the *same epistemic situation* (same conceptual structure, same isolation) even though the *contents* differ — so possessing physically explicable phenomenal concepts is enough to generate the appearance of a gap.
  - Defines a *purely recognitional concept* as one both applied directly to instances and conceptually isolated from other concepts; isolation is the load-bearing feature.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1; this is the strongest live physicalist rebuttal to the master argument.
- **Map gap**: Neither existing article names the first-person/third-person equivocation charge explicitly. This is the single most useful addition for refining `phenomenal-concepts-as-materialist-response.md`.

### Ball, Derek — "There Are No Phenomenal Concepts" (*Mind* 118, 2009, pp. 935-962)
- **URL**: https://www.st-andrews.ac.uk/~db71/ball_no_pcs.pdf ; https://philpapers.org/rec/BALTAN-2
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Adapts **Burge-style social externalism** to phenomenal concepts: one can possess a concept partly in virtue of relations to one's linguistic community, not solely intrinsic properties.
  - If externalism extends to colour-experience concepts, then there are **no phenomenal concepts** of the intrinsic, individualistic kind PCS requires — which *simultaneously* undermines the knowledge argument (it needs phenomenal concepts to state the "new way of knowing") *and* PCS (it needs them as its explanans).
  - A genuinely double-edged result: bad for PCS, but also bad for the dualist who leans on the knowledge argument.
- **Tenet alignment**: Ambiguous / cuts both ways. Threatens Tenet 1's reliance on the knowledge argument while also dissolving the chief physicalist defence.
- **Map gap**: Ball is named in the March research note's query list but is **not developed** in either article. Worth a paragraph, precisely because it is double-edged — the Map should not pretend Ball only hurts the physicalist.

### Fürst, Martina — "A Dualist Account of Phenomenal Concepts"
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/archive/FRSADA ; https://www.newdualism.org/papers-Jul2020/Fuerst-A_Dualist_Account_of_Phenomenal_Concepts.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (in Liljenberg et al. / contemporary dualism collections)
- **Key points**:
  - Physicalist accounts fail to explain the *cognitive role* of phenomenal concepts. Fürst's **encapsulation account** holds that phenomenal concepts are constituted by *self-representing experiences* — the experience represents itself within the concept.
  - The best-explaining version of the encapsulation idea **implies non-physical referents**: only self-representing experiences can constitute a phenomenal concept, and such experiences are not physically reducible. So the account that best explains phenomenal concepts *reinforces* dualism rather than explaining the dualist intuition away.
  - This is the "PCS reversal": the physicalist's own explanatory apparatus, taken to its best form, becomes an argument *for* dualism.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1; already the centrepiece of the March 2026 research note.

### Zhou, Peng — "Indicating without revealing: a posteriori physicalism and the new challenge" (*Synthese*, 2025, vol. 206 no. 2)
- **URL**: (Synthese, 2025) — via PhilPapers index
- **Type**: Journal paper (recent)
- **Key points**:
  - Frames the a-posteriori-physicalist position as: phenomenal concepts *indicate* their referents (lock onto the physical state) without *revealing* the referent's nature (without a priori entailing the phenomenal character).
  - "Indicating without revealing" is a deliberate retreat from the older mode-of-presentation framing — it tries to keep the referential link while abandoning the claim that phenomenal concepts give substantive a priori access to their objects.
  - Engages the "new challenge" (Stoljar; powerful-qualities). The dualist diagnosis: an indication-only concept that reveals nothing of the referent's nature simply *restates* the explanatory gap as a feature of every phenomenal concept, rather than dissolving it.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (a-posteriori physicalist project), but the framing concedes ground the Map can exploit.

### Sasaki, Kento — "The Phenomenal Concept Strategy and the Master Argument" (2025)
- **URL**: (2025) — via PhilPapers index ; cf. earlier "The Phenomenal Concept Strategy and a Master Argument" https://philarchive.org/archive/MABTPC
- **Type**: Journal/working paper (recent)
- **Key points**:
  - Continues the master-argument exchange into 2025, examining whether the thin/thick characterisation of "our epistemic situation" admits a stable physicalist reading.
  - Connects to the prior move (Mabaquiao and others) that "our epistemic situation" can be given *either* a phenomenal *or* a topic-neutral characterisation, and that neither cleanly supports Chalmers's dilemma — the dialectic the Map's "master argument" section should flag as still open rather than settled.
- **Tenet alignment**: Contested terrain; relevant to how confidently the Map asserts the master argument "defeats" PCS.

### Morris, Kevin — "Acquaintance and Mary's Revelation: A Response to Alter" (*Philosophia*, 2025)
- **URL**: (Philosophia, 2025) — via PhilPapers index
- **Type**: Journal paper (recent)
- **Key points**:
  - Engages the **acquaintance** route into the knowledge argument and PCS — whether Mary's new knowledge is best understood as acquaintance rather than propositional/conceptual knowledge.
  - Relevant because the acquaintance reply is an *alternative* to PCS that some physicalists prefer; it bears on the Map's `phenomenal-acquaintance.md` concept and the boundary between "ability/acquaintance" replies and the concept-based reply.
- **Tenet alignment**: Mixed; acquaintance framings are partly congenial to dualist phenomenal-authority claims.

### Kammerer, F. & Frankish, K. — "The Landscape of Introspection: What Forms Could Introspective Systems Take?" (2024); Kammerer, "The Illusion of Conscious Experience" (*Synthese*, 2019)
- **URL**: https://www.francoiskammerer.com/publications/ ; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-018-02071-y
- **Type**: Papers
- **Key points**:
  - Illusionists *use* a PCS-style introspection story: phenomenal concepts (or quasi-phenomenal representations) explain why we are systematically disposed to *judge* that there is phenomenality, even if there is none.
  - But illusionism also pressures PCS: if a fully physical introspective system would deploy the same phenomenal concepts whether or not phenomenality is real, then those concepts cannot be evidence *for* phenomenal properties — which is exactly the leverage Chalmers's master argument exploits ("zombies share C").
  - Kammerer's "illusion meta-problem" (why does illusionism seem so obviously false?) is the illusionist mirror of the explanatory gap and leans on the same conceptual-isolation feature.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (illusionism denies phenomenal reality), but the coupling is dialectically useful: PCS and illusionism rise or fall partly together.

### Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews — Alter & Walter (eds.), *Phenomenal Concepts and Phenomenal Knowledge* (OUP 2007)
- **URL**: https://ndpr.nd.edu/reviews/phenomenal-concepts-and-phenomenal-knowledge-new-essays-on-consciousness-and-physicalism/
- **Type**: Book review (anchor volume)
- **Key points**: Still the canonical anthology framing the debate; useful for confirming the proponent/objector roster and primary-source citations.

## Major Positions (Updated Map)

### Type-B Physicalism / PCS (the defended view)
- **Proponents**: Loar, Papineau, Balog, Carruthers & Veillet, Hill, Tye (at times), Stoljar (as expositor/critic).
- **Core claim**: Epistemic gap, no ontological gap. Phenomenal concepts are conceptually isolated from physical concepts, which predicts the appearance of a gap on physicalist premises.
- **2024-2026 state**: The most defended live form is the **recognitional-isolation** account (Carruthers-Veillet) plus the **a-posteriori "indicate-not-reveal"** reframing (Zhou 2025). The mode-of-presentation/quotational machinery is comparatively quiescent.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 1. The Map's standing verdict (PCS relocates rather than solves the hard problem) survives the recent literature.

### Eliminativism about phenomenal concepts (Ball)
- **Proponent**: Derek Ball.
- **Core claim**: Social/semantic externalism implies there are no intrinsic phenomenal concepts; both the knowledge argument and PCS presuppose them and so both fail.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Double-edged. The Map should treat Ball as a genuine threat to the *knowledge-argument route* to Tenet 1, not merely as a physicalist self-inflicted wound. The Map's dualism does not stand or fall on the knowledge argument (it has the conceivability/zombie route, the explanatory gap, and the quantum-interface positive case), so Ball is survivable — but the Map should say so rather than ignore him.

### Dualist encapsulation (Fürst)
- **Proponent**: Martina Fürst.
- **Core claim**: The best account of phenomenal concepts (self-representing experiences) implies non-physical referents; PCS's apparatus, perfected, supports dualism.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1. This is the Map's preferred "judo" line and is already centred in the March research note.

### Illusionism-with-PCS (Frankish, Kammerer)
- **Proponents**: Keith Frankish, François Kammerer.
- **Core claim**: There is no phenomenality; phenomenal/quasi-phenomenal concepts explain the universal *illusion* of phenomenality.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 1. Useful as a foil: if the same concepts would be deployed by a zombie/illusionist system, they cannot be evidence for phenomenality (master-argument leverage), and illusionism's "meta-problem" mirrors the gap rather than dissolving it.

## Key Debates

### The Master Argument: settled or open?
- **Sides**: Chalmers (dilemma defeats all PCS) vs. Carruthers-Veillet (equivocation between first/third-person phenomenal concepts; zombies and twins share epistemic situation) vs. continued 2025 engagement (Sasaki).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "our epistemic situation" / feature C can be given a single stable characterisation that is both physically explicable *and* gap-explaining.
- **Current state**: **Open**, not closed. The Map's two articles currently present the master argument as decisive. Recommendation: soften from "shows why this fails" toward "exposes the central dilemma PCS has not stably escaped," and *name* the Carruthers-Veillet reply as the live counter. (The descriptions of both articles assert PCS-fails as flatly settled; this is the chief calibration risk in the existing corpus.)

### Indicating vs. revealing (the a-posteriori retreat)
- **Sides**: a-posteriori physicalists (Zhou 2025) vs. dualists.
- **Core disagreement**: Can a concept refer to / lock onto a physical state while revealing nothing of why that state feels as it does, *without* this concession just being the explanatory gap restated for every phenomenal concept?
- **Current state**: Ongoing; the dualist diagnosis (it restates the gap) is strong and worth recording.

### Do phenomenal concepts even exist?
- **Sides**: Ball (no) vs. essentially everyone in the PCS and knowledge-argument literatures (yes).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether externalism about content reaches all the way to phenomenal concepts.
- **Current state**: Minority but live; matters because it is the one objection that threatens dualist and physicalist uses alike.
