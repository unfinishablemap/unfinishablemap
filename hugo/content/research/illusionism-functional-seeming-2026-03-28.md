# Research: Illusionism and Frankish's Functional-Seeming Response

**Date**: 2026-03-28
**Search queries used**: "Frankish illusionism functional seeming qualia", "Keith Frankish illusionism response anti-illusionism arguments", "Frankish illusion problem functional seeming phenomenal seeming circularity objection", "Chalmers debunking arguments illusionism consciousness", "Kammerer illusionism phenomenal consciousness explaining illusion", "Galen Strawson anti-illusionism phenomenal realism", "Frankish quasi-phenomenal properties introspection misrepresentation", "Graziano attention schema theory illusionism"

## Executive Summary

This research focuses on Keith Frankish's specific response to the regress objection against illusionism—the argument that an illusion of phenomenal consciousness would itself require phenomenal consciousness. Frankish's central move is distinguishing functional seeming from phenomenal seeming: when illusionists say consciousness "seems" phenomenal, they mean this functionally (having the right beliefs, dispositions, and reactions) rather than phenomenally. This blocks the regress by denying that illusions require phenomenal experience. The research also covers the broader illusionism debate landscape, including Chalmers's Moorean and debunking arguments, Kammerer's illusion meta-problem, Graziano's attention schema theory, and Strawson's no-gap objection. This supplements earlier research (illusionism-consciousness-2026-01-14.md) with deeper focus on the functional-seeming mechanism and how the Map's dualist position should engage with it.

## Key Sources

### "Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness" (Frankish, 2016)
- **URL**: https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_Illusionism%20as%20a%20theory%20of%20consciousness_eprint.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (in Journal of Consciousness Studies)
- **Key points**:
  - Defines illusionism as the view that phenomenal properties are an introspective illusion
  - Introduces the "illusion problem" as replacement for the hard problem
  - Distinguishes weak illusionism (phenomenal properties exist but are mischaracterized) from strong illusionism (phenomenal properties don't exist at all)
  - Defines quasi-phenomenal properties: the non-phenomenal, physical properties of inner states that introspection detects and misrepresents as phenomenal
  - The central move: "seeming" is functional, not phenomenal—having the right beliefs, dispositions, memories, associations, and reactions
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism) and rules-out clause ("eliminative materialism" and "any view that treats consciousness as illusory")
- **Quote**: "To be under the illusion of being aware of mental qualities is to undergo all the psychological effects that awareness of mental qualities would cause"

### "What is Illusionism?" (Frankish, 2021)
- **URL**: https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_What%20is%20illusionism_eprint.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (Revue de Philosophie Klesis)
- **Key points**:
  - Clarifies that illusionism does not deny consciousness exists, only that it has phenomenal properties
  - Distinguishes three conceptions of qualia: "classic qualia" (Dennett's target—ineffable, intrinsic, private), "zero qualia" (functional conception with minimal theoretical commitments), and "diet qualia" (in-between, retaining some mystery)
  - Illusionists accept zero qualia but deny classic and diet qualia
  - The functional/phenomenal seeming distinction is explicitly defended against circularity charges
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1. The "zero qualia" vs "classic qualia" distinction attempts to defuse dualist arguments by redefining what's at stake
- **Quote**: "Seeming need not be phenomenal seeming, and there is thus no circularity in claiming that phenomenal properties seem to exist"

### "Debunking Arguments for Illusionism about Consciousness" (Chalmers, 2018/2022)
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/debunking.pdf
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**:
  - Chalmers presents a Moorean argument against illusionism: (1) People sometimes feel pain; (2) If illusionism is correct, no one feels pain; (3) Therefore illusionism is not true
  - Also examines debunking arguments *for* illusionism—the argument that if a correct explanation of our beliefs about consciousness is independent of consciousness, those beliefs are not justified
  - In 2022, Chalmers revised his position, arguing Moore's original argument was itself flawed
- **Tenet alignment**: Chalmers's anti-illusionism aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism). His Moorean argument parallels the Map's commitment to phenomenal reality
- **Quote**: The first premise of the Moorean argument "seems obviously true"

### "Illusionism, Moore, and Chalmers" (Loginov, 2024)
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1449314/full
- **Type**: Paper (Frontiers in Psychology)
- **Key points**:
  - Defends the Moorean argument against Chalmers's own revision
  - Argues Chalmers's Moorean argument against illusionism cannot ultimately refute illusionism
  - Explores whether "phenomenal consciousness" is too technical a term for Moorean common-sense argumentation
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral—explores the argumentative structure rather than taking a definitive side

### "How Can You Be So Sure?" (Kammerer, 2022)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-022-01804-7
- **Type**: Paper (Philosophical Studies)
- **Key points**:
  - Addresses the "obviousness" of phenomenal consciousness and why illusionism seems inconceivable
  - Poses the "illusion meta-problem": why does illusionism seem especially counterintuitive?
  - Proposes that the illusion is "rich"—it persists regardless of what one comes to believe about its reality
  - Introduces three hypotheses: introspective opacity, the infallibility intuition, and the justification constraint
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1. Kammerer's explanation of why illusionism seems absurd is precisely the kind of explaining-away that the Map's tenets reject

### "Illusionism about Phenomenal Consciousness: Explaining the Illusion" (Pereboom & Frankish, 2021)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13164-021-00537-6
- **Type**: Paper (Review of Philosophy and Psychology)
- **Key points**:
  - The illusion of a phenomenal property necessarily involves an introspective representation of it
  - Proposes the "distortion thesis": introspective mechanisms systematically misrepresent neural processes
  - Introspective access vs. introspective interpretation: we access real mental states but systematically misinterpret what we access
  - The conceptual framework we use to make sense of experience is what generates the illusion, not experience itself
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 and Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). If introspective reports are systematic misrepresentation, this undermines the Map's argument that discussing consciousness proves it causally influences the physical world

### Attention Schema Theory (Graziano)
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4407481/
- **Type**: Neuroscience framework
- **Key points**:
  - The brain constructs an internal model of attention (the "attention schema") analogous to the body schema
  - This model, being simplified and schematic, portrays attention as having a non-physical essence—creating the impression of subjective awareness
  - AST is the only cognitive neuroscience theory explicitly illusionist in orientation
  - Does not explain how we have subjective experience—explains how a machine *claims* to have subjective experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly conflicts with all five tenets. AST is a thoroughgoing physicalist, mechanistic account
- **Quote**: The theory "explains how a machine claims to have a subjective experience, and how it is that the machine cannot tell the difference"

### "The Consciousness Deniers" (Strawson, 2018)
- **URL**: https://web.ics.purdue.edu/~drkelly/StrawsonDennettNYRBExchangeConsciousness2018.pdf
- **Type**: Essay/Exchange (New York Review of Books)
- **Key points**:
  - Strawson calls illusionism "the silliest claim ever made" and compares it to Flat Eartherism
  - Presents the no-gap argument: "to seem to feel pain is to be in pain"—there is no appearance-reality gap for phenomenal experience
  - An illusion of phenomenal experience would itself constitute phenomenal experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1 (Dualism). Strawson's no-gap argument resonates with the Map's position that phenomenal consciousness is irreducible

## Major Positions

### Strong Illusionism (Frankish, Dennett)
- **Proponents**: Keith Frankish, Daniel Dennett, François Kammerer, Georges Rey, Amber Ross
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal properties do not exist at all. What exists are quasi-phenomenal properties—non-phenomenal physical properties that introspection systematically misrepresents as phenomenal. The "hard problem" is replaced by the "illusion problem."
- **Key arguments**:
  1. Functional seeming blocks the regress: "seeming" is understood functionally (having appropriate beliefs, dispositions, reactions), not phenomenally. No circularity in denying phenomenal properties while acknowledging they "seem" to exist.
  2. Introspective access vs. interpretation: We have real access to mental states but apply a flawed internal theory that posits phenomenal properties. The error is conceptual/theoretical, not experiential.
  3. The user illusion analogy: Just as a computer's desktop interface misrepresents underlying processes with simplified icons, introspection creates a simplified, schematic representation of neural activity that misrepresents it as having phenomenal character.
  4. Parsimony: Illusionism explains consciousness reports without positing anything beyond the physical.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly contradicts Tenet 1 (Dualism), Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction), and is motivated by the kind of parsimony reasoning that Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits) warns against.

### Weak Illusionism
- **Proponents**: Various; occupies middle ground
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal properties exist but are mischaracterized by introspection. They are not ineffable, intrinsic, or private in the way commonly supposed. This is closer to a deflationary realism than true illusionism.
- **Key arguments**: Preserves the reality of qualia in some form while explaining why philosophical puzzles about consciousness arise from mischaracterization.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Less directly conflicting—preserves phenomenal reality while deflating it. The Map could potentially engage with weak illusionism more constructively, though it still conflicts with the irreducibility commitment of Tenet 1.

### Phenomenal Realism / Anti-Illusionism
- **Proponents**: David Chalmers, Galen Strawson, Jesse Prinz
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal consciousness is real, not illusory. There is something it is like to have experiences, and this fact cannot be deflated or explained away.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. The Moorean argument: That we feel pain is more certain than any philosophical theory denying it
  2. The no-gap argument (Strawson): Seeming to feel pain just *is* feeling pain—no appearance-reality distinction is possible for phenomenal states
  3. Prinz's dilemma: Illusionism either collapses into realism (if it accepts any form of experiential quality) or faces a problem as hard as the hard problem (explaining how the illusion arises)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly aligned with Tenet 1. The Map is committed to phenomenal realism.

## Key Debates

### The Regress / Circularity Objection
- **Sides**: Anti-illusionists (Strawson, Chalmers) argue that an illusion of phenomenal consciousness requires phenomenal consciousness. Illusionists (Frankish) respond that "seeming" is functional, not phenomenal—blocking the regress.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "seeming" can be fully cashed out in functional terms, or whether any adequate account of seeming smuggles phenomenal consciousness back in.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. This is the central fault line. Frankish insists the functional account is complete; anti-illusionists insist something is left out. Neither side has produced a decisive argument.
- **Map's stake**: The Map's quantum-mechanism articles currently use the regress argument without engaging Frankish's functional-seeming response. This leaves the Map vulnerable to the charge of strawmanning illusionism.

### The Illusion Problem vs. The Hard Problem
- **Sides**: Frankish argues the illusion problem (explaining why phenomenal consciousness seems to exist) is tractable, unlike the hard problem. Anti-illusionists argue the illusion problem is just as hard—or harder—because explaining a systematic, universal misrepresentation of one's own mental states is deeply puzzling.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether replacing the hard problem with the illusion problem represents progress or lateral movement.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Kammerer's illusion meta-problem adds another layer: why does illusionism seem so obviously wrong?

### The Moorean Argument
- **Sides**: Chalmers (2018) deployed a Moorean argument against illusionism. Chalmers (2022) then argued the original Moorean argument was flawed. Loginov (2024) attempted to rehabilitate the Moorean argument.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether common-sense certainty about phenomenal consciousness can serve as a philosophical premise, and whether "phenomenal consciousness" is too technical a term for Moorean argumentation.
- **Current state**: Unresolved. The debate has become increasingly meta-philosophical—about what kinds of premises can ground what kinds of arguments.

### Functional Seeming and the Zombie Problem
- **Sides**: Frankish accepts that a philosophical zombie would have exactly the same introspective representations as a conscious being—both would "functionally seem" to be phenomenally conscious. Anti-illusionists argue this admission undermines illusionism: if zombies and conscious beings are functionally identical, then functional seeming cannot be the whole story.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether functional identity between zombies and conscious beings is a feature (illusionism says consciousness adds nothing) or a bug (it shows functional description is incomplete).
- **Current state**: This connects to the broader zombie argument for dualism, which the Map already endorses (Tenet 1 rationale cites conceivability arguments).
