## Executive Summary

The Map grounds value in a two-step chain: value requires *mattering*, and mattering requires a *subject* — a "someone" for whom things matter. The no-self objection attacks the second step. Buddhist *anātman* (no-self) plus *kṣaṇikavāda* (momentariness), and Parfit's reductionism about personal identity, both deny there is any "further fact" of a persisting, unified experiential locus beyond the causally-linked stream of momentary psychophysical events. If there is no persisting subject, the objection runs, then the mattering-recipient the value chain presupposes is a fiction — and either value collapses, or it must be re-grounded in something that does not require a persisting self.

The strongest reply available to the Map does not require defeating no-self wholesale. It requires distinguishing two things the objection runs together: a *persisting, substantial* self (which Buddhism and Parfit deny, and which the Map need not assert) versus a *minimal / momentary experiential locus* — the bare "for-me-ness" of a token experience (Zahavi, Strawson's "thin subject," Metzinger's minimal phenomenal experience). The Map's value chain plausibly needs only the latter: a mattering-recipient can be a momentary experiential locus, not a diachronically-persisting soul. This reply has a direct cost for Tenet 1: it commits the Map to saying the experiencing locus is neither "more mechanism" nor a persisting Cartesian substance, but something thinner — which needs positive characterisation.

## Key Sources

### Mind in Indian Buddhist Philosophy (SEP)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mind-indian-buddhism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - *Anātman* denies a permanent, substantive self; the "not-self" analysis extends beyond subjective experience to characterise all phenomena. Its primary motivation is soteriological (release from grasping), though it carries ontological weight.
  - *Kṣaṇikavāda* (momentariness): all conditioned phenomena exist only momentarily. This generates the coherence puzzle — if each moment of consciousness dissolves immediately, how does experience cohere and how do karmic effects arise from causes no longer present?
  - The person is analysed into five *skandhas* (aggregates): form (*rūpa*), sensation/feeling (*vedanā*), apperception (*saṃjñā*), volitions (*saṃskāra*), consciousness (*vijñāna*). The person is "nothing more than merely conventional designations established on the basis of the five aggregates."
  - Continuity without a subject is handled by mechanisms: mind-stream (*citta-santāna*), life-continuum mind (*bhavaṅga-citta*, Theravāda), repository consciousness (*ālaya-vijñāna*, Yogācāra — Asaṅga, Vasubandhu), and seeds (*bīja*, Sautrāntika).
  - Two-tiered ontology: only partless atoms and momentary mental events are ultimately real (*dravyasat*); persons and composites are only conventionally real (*prajñaptisat*). Buddhist reductionists are NOT eliminativists — person-discourse is retained as conventionally true.
  - Vasubandhu (c. 320–380 CE), *Abhidharmakośa* and the appended *Treatise on the Negation of the Person* (Pudgala-viniścaya), is the systematiser. The Pudgalavādins (Personalists) dissented, holding persons non-reductively supervene on aggregates — rejected by mainstream orthodoxy.
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly pressures Tenet 1's framing. Buddhism denies a *substantial* self but does NOT deny experience/consciousness — it relocates the target. Useful because it forces the Map to say precisely which notion of "subject" its value chain needs.
- **Note**: Whether *anātman* is best read as ontological or purely soteriological is itself contested; do not over-claim it as flat metaphysical denial of all subjecthood.

### Personal Identity and Ethics (SEP)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/identity-ethics/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Parfit's Reductionism: a person's existence "just consists in" the existence of a brain and body and a series of interrelated physical and mental events; there is no separately-existing "further fact."
  - The "argument from below": if fact X consists in facts Y, then only Y can carry rational/moral importance. So personal *identity* is not itself what rationally or morally matters — what matters is Relation R (psychological connectedness and/or continuity).
  - Ethical upshots: the special self-concern we have for our own future is not grounded the way we suppose; and aggregative ethical theories that sum harms/benefits across persons face "one fewer obstacle" once the deep further fact of identity is dropped.
- **Tenet alignment**: Parfit is a physicalist-friendly reductionist; his conclusion (identity is not what matters) is a Western analytic parallel to *anātman*. Relevant because it shows the no-self pressure is not parochially Buddhist — a mainstream secular metaphysics reaches the same "no persisting further-fact subject" verdict.

### Miri Albahari — *Analytical Buddhism: The Two-Tiered Illusion of Self* (2006); "Witness-Consciousness"
- **URL**: https://philpeople.org/profiles/miri-albahari/publications ; https://philpapers.org/rec/ALBWID
- **Type**: Book / paper
- **Key points**:
  - The self-illusion has two tiers: (1) a non-illusory *ownerless* witnessing consciousness, and (2) a constructed content-tier that yields a personalised identity ("the person").
  - Crucially for the Map: Albahari holds that *consciousness / witnessing subjectivity* is real and perspectival even though the *personal self* is illusory. There is a "perspectival owner of experience" without a substantial ego.
  - This is a live counter-resource: it separates "there is a subject-pole / for-whom-ness" (retained) from "there is a persisting personal self" (denied). Exactly the distinction the Map's reply needs.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly usable. Albahari, from *within* a no-self framework, preserves a subject-pole that is not "more mechanism" — congenial to Tenet 1 while conceding the Parfitian/Buddhist point about the personal self.
- **Caveat**: Albahari's further claim that witnessing awareness is "unconditioned" / persists unchanged is a substantive metaphysical thesis the Map need not (and probably should not) inherit.

### Dan Zahavi — minimal self / experiential for-me-ness
- **URL**: (representative) https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/self-consciousness-phenomenological/ ; academia.edu "Consciousness and (minimal) selfhood: Getting clearer on for-me-ness and mineness"
- **Type**: Book / papers (*Subjectivity and Selfhood*, 2005; *Self and Other*, 2014)
- **Key points**:
  - The "minimal self" is not a separately-existing entity but the *first-person givenness* of experience itself — its "for-me-ness" (*Für-mich-heit*) or mineness. Every conscious episode has a subjective character: it is *for* someone.
  - This subject is identified WITH the experiential dimension, not posited behind it. So it is thin, and does not require diachronic persistence to be present in a given experience.
  - Zahavi explicitly engages Buddhist no-self (with Albahari) — the debate is precisely whether the phenomenological for-me-ness survives the deconstruction of the personal self. Zahavi says yes; radical no-self theorists say even for-me-ness is constructed.
- **Tenet alignment**: This is the Map's best counter-resource. If mattering needs only for-me-ness (a subject-pole intrinsic to any experience), then momentariness/no-persisting-self does not defeat the value chain — each experience already has its "someone."
- **Note**: There is real opposition — e.g. critiques arguing self-consciousness "without an 'I'" is possible (Brill, *Research in Phenomenology* 52:1, 2022), and no-ownership readings. Present as contested, not settled.

### Thomas Metzinger — Minimal Phenomenal Experience (MPE) / Minimal Phenomenal Selfhood (MPS)
- **URL**: https://www.philosophie.fb05.uni-mainz.de/files/2020/03/Metzinger_MPE1_PMS_2020.pdf
- **Type**: Paper (*Philosophy and the Mind Sciences*, 2020); earlier *Being No One* (2003)
- **Key points**:
  - MPS is "the most basic possible kind of self-consciousness," arising from *pre-reflective self-modelling* — the phenomenal self is a *model* (the "phenomenal self-model"), not a substance. Metzinger's slogan: "no one ever was or had a self."
  - MPE work (meditation, "pure consciousness," tonic alertness) probes whether there is phenomenal experience with *no* self-model at all — a for-no-one experience.
  - This is a double-edged source: it supplies a *deflationary* self (self = model, no substantial subject → congenial to no-self) BUT the MPE research program raises the sharp question of whether experience can be genuinely subject-less. If MPE is real ownerless experience, the "every experience has a for-me-ness" reply is threatened.
- **Tenet alignment**: CONFLICTS with Tenet 1's spirit at face value (self as brain-built model = "more mechanism"). The Map should engage Metzinger as the strongest opposing account and either (a) accept the self-*model* is mechanism while insisting the *phenomenal locus that hosts the model* is not, or (b) contest that MPE is genuinely subject-less rather than merely selfless-in-the-personal-sense.

### Mark Siderits — *Personal Identity and Buddhist Philosophy: Empty Persons*; *Buddhist reductionism*
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/SIDPIA-3 ; https://philpapers.org/rec/SIDBR
- **Type**: Book / paper
- **Key points**:
  - Reads Abhidharma as reductionist and structurally akin to Parfit, but going further (all composites, not just persons, are conventionally real). Uses the Buddhist "two truths" to clarify Parfit: partless entities are ultimately real; persons are conventional constructions dependent on "our wants, needs and interests."
  - The person-concept is retained as pragmatically valuable shorthand — "empty persons" are conventionally real, so ethical and value discourse is not eliminated, just re-based.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-useful. Siderits shows that dropping the persisting subject need NOT eliminate value talk — it can be re-grounded at the conventional level. This is a template for a Map reply: value can attach to conventionally-real experiential streams without a further-fact self.
- **Cross-reference**: See also "Parfitian or Buddhist reductionism? Revisiting a debate about personal identity," *Asian Journal of Philosophy* (2024), https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s44204-024-00166-7 — recent literature distinguishing the two reductionisms.

### Galen Strawson — *The Subject of Experience* (OUP, 2017)
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/archive/MEATSO-8 (review) ; book: OUP 2017, ISBN 9780198777885
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Introduces a "thin" / "live" subject: a subject of experience *cannot exist unless there is an experience for it to be the subject of.* Subjects are short-lived, "gappy," serialised — arising and passing with experiences (the SESMET: Subject-of-Experience-that-is-a-Single-MEntal-Thing).
  - On the thinnest reading, for a token experience the subject, content, and experiencing are not three separate things: there is a tight identity ([E = S = C]).
- **Tenet alignment**: This is the single most useful source for the Map's reply. Strawson gives a *realist* subject (against no-ownership views) that is nonetheless *momentary* — exactly what is needed to say "value needs a subject" while conceding "there is no persisting self." A momentary subject can be a genuine mattering-recipient.
- **Note**: Strawson is a physicalist (of a panpsychist-leaning stripe). The Map can borrow his thin-subject structure without his physicalism.

## Major Positions

### Radical No-Self (eliminative/deconstructive reading of anātman + kṣaṇikavāda)
- **Proponents**: Abhidharma orthodoxy as sometimes read; Metzinger (secular version — "no one ever had a self").
- **Core claim**: There is no subject-pole at all beyond causally-linked momentary events; even the sense of a "for-whom" is a constructed representation.
- **Bearing on the objection**: This is the *strong* form of the no-self objection. If true, the Map's mattering-recipient is a fiction top-to-bottom, and value must be re-grounded impersonally (in the events/states themselves) or abandoned.
- **Relation to tenets**: Directly threatens Tenet 1's "experiencing locus that is not mechanism." The Map must either deny that experience can be genuinely for-no-one, or relocate value's ground.

### Thin-Subject Realism (minimal self / for-me-ness / thin subject)
- **Proponents**: Zahavi (for-me-ness), G. Strawson (thin/live subject), Albahari (ownerless-but-perspectival witnessing).
- **Core claim**: The *personal, persisting* self is illusory/constructed, BUT every experience has an intrinsic subject-pole (a for-whom-ness / experiential locus), which may be momentary.
- **Bearing on the objection**: Defuses the objection by conceding its target (persisting self) while preserving what the value chain actually needs (a mattering-recipient per experience). Value requires a subject; a subject need only be a momentary experiential locus.
- **Relation to tenets**: Most congenial to Tenet 1 — the locus is not "more mechanism," it is the for-me-ness intrinsic to experience — but it obliges the Map to characterise this locus positively and to resist collapsing it into either a Cartesian substance or a brain-built model.

### Parfitian Reductionism (secular, Western)
- **Proponents**: Parfit (*Reasons and Persons*, 1984); Siderits (bridging to Buddhism).
- **Core claim**: Persons reduce to interrelated physical/mental events; identity is "not what matters" — Relation R does. No deep further fact.
- **Bearing on the objection**: Supplies the objection's secular backbone and shows value-talk can be re-based on conventionally-real streams. Value need not track a further-fact identity; it can track experiential states and their connections.
- **Relation to tenets**: Parfit himself is broadly physicalist (conflicts with Tenet 1's dualism), but his *structural* point — identity is not the bearer of what matters — is separable from physicalism and can be absorbed by the Map.

## Key Debates

### Does phenomenal value require a *persisting* subject, or only a *momentary* experiential locus?
- **Sides**: The objection assumes the Map needs a persisting subject (and no-self denies one). Thin-subject realists (Strawson, Zahavi, Albahari) say only a momentary for-me-ness is needed.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "mattering-to" is a diachronic relation (requiring someone who persists to be benefited/harmed over time) or can be fully instantiated within a single experiential moment (this pain is bad *for* this experiential locus, now).
- **Current state**: Live and unresolved. The Map's most defensible line: hedonic/phenomenal badness is bad *for the experiencing locus of that very experience*; persistence is needed for prudence and narrative value, not for the base fact of mattering.

### Can there be genuinely subject-less (for-no-one) experience?
- **Sides**: Metzinger's MPE program and no-ownership readings say yes; Zahavi and Strawson say no — for-me-ness / a thin subject is constitutive of experience.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "pure consciousness" episodes lack a self-*model* only (compatible with a thin subject) or lack a subject-pole entirely.
- **Current state**: Empirically and conceptually open; central to whether the strong no-self objection succeeds.

### Is anātman ontological or soteriological?
- **Sides**: Metaphysical-denial readings vs. skill-in-relating (soteriological) readings.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether Buddhism asserts "no subject exists" or "do not grasp at a self."
- **Current state**: Contested in Buddhist studies; matters because only the strong ontological reading directly contradicts the Map's value chain.

- **Primary Buddhist texts not consulted directly** — relied on SEP + secondary summaries. Before any article quotes the *Milindapañha* (chariot analogy) or Vasubandhu's *Pudgala-viniścaya*, quotations must be verified at a scholarly translation, not paraphrased from encyclopedia summaries.
