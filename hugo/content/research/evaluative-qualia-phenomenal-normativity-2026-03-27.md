# Research: Evaluative Qualia and Phenomenal Normativity Across Traditions

**Date**: 2026-03-27
**Search queries used**: "evaluative qualia phenomenal normativity philosophy", "phenomenal normativity qualia Stanford Encyclopedia", "evaluative qualia Buddhist philosophy vedanta traditions", "phenomenal consciousness normativity dualism PhilPapers", "Sharon Hewitt normative qualia moral realism", "vedana Buddhist phenomenology evaluative feeling tone", "David Sosa two forms of dualism normativity consciousness", "phenomenal value intrinsic goodness badness pain pleasure", "Christian Onof property dualism epistemic normativity", "Confucian phenomenology moral feeling li qi consciousness", "direct acquaintance intrinsic value suffering phenomenal character", "Islamic philosophy consciousness qualia Avicenna Mulla Sadra", "phenomenal normativity Kriegel consciousness value", "rasa Indian aesthetics consciousness evaluative Abhinavagupta"

## Executive Summary

Evaluative qualia—the idea that phenomenal experiences carry intrinsic normative weight (felt goodness, felt badness)—represent a growing area where philosophy of mind, metaethics, and cross-cultural philosophy converge. Western analytic philosophy has recently produced explicit theories of "normative qualia" (Rawlette) and "direct acquaintance with intrinsic value" (Dimitrov 2025), while older traditions—Buddhist vedana theory, Confucian moral phenomenology, Indian rasa aesthetics, and Islamic knowledge-as-existence frameworks—have long treated the evaluative dimension of experience as foundational. For a dualist site, this topic is significant: if phenomenal experience carries irreducible evaluative properties, this strengthens the case that consciousness is not epiphenomenal and that qualia do real normative work that physicalism cannot capture.

## Key Sources

### "Direct Acquaintance with Intrinsic Value" — Martin Dimitrov (2025)
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/pq/article/75/2/428/7644691
- **Type**: Journal article (The Philosophical Quarterly)
- **Key points**:
  - Introspection of suffering's phenomenal character directly acquaints us with intrinsic badness
  - There is no appearance-reality gap for simple, nonrepresentational phenomenal states like felt badness
  - Value realism is entailed by the nature of introspective judgments about felt badness
  - Suffering seems bad because it *is* bad; direct acquaintance with badness justifies value realism
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Dualism tenet—intrinsic evaluative properties of experience are irreducible to physical description
- **Quote**: "Suffering seems bad because it is, and what makes it true is our direct acquaintance with badness."

### "Normative Qualia and a Robust Moral Realism" — Sharon Hewitt Rawlette
- **URL**: https://www.stafforini.com/docs/Hewitt%20-%20Normative%20qualia%20and%20a%20robust%20moral%20realism.pdf
- **Type**: Paper / Book precursor
- **Key points**:
  - Intrinsic goodness is an experiential property—a quality of conscious experience
  - Pleasure instantiates positive qualia; pain instantiates negative qualia
  - These are not *signs* of value—they *are* value and disvalue
  - Defends analytic descriptivism: our concept of intrinsic value derives from phenomenal experience
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Dualism—evaluative qualia are irreducible phenomenal properties. Also supports Bidirectional Interaction—if consciousness carries intrinsic value, its causal role matters morally
- **Quote**: "Experiences of pleasure and pain, joy and sorrow, ecstasy and despair are not signs of value or disvalue—they are instantiations of value and disvalue."

- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/SOSTFO-2
- **Type**: Journal article (Dialogue: Canadian Philosophical Review)
- **Key points**:

  - Asks whether rationality is an even "harder" problem than consciousness
  - Finds these two dualist currents are relatively independent
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly relevant—treats normativity as independent grounds for dualism beyond the usual phenomenal arguments
- **Quote**: "Is rationality an even harder problem than consciousness?"

### "Property Dualism, Epistemic Normativity, and the Limits of Naturalism" — Christian Onof (2008)
- **URL**: https://philarchive.org/rec/ONOPDE-2
- **Type**: Journal article (Philosophy and Phenomenological Research)
- **Key points**:
  - Examines quasi-epiphenomenalism implied by property dualism
  - Shows incompatibility between epistemic norms and awareness of phenomenal experience in certain scenarios
  - This provides epistemic grounds for postulating bridging laws between physical and phenomenal domains
  - Stronger claim: property dualism's ontology cannot properly account for the certainty of being phenomenally conscious
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Dualism; tensions identified may push toward stronger forms of dualism consistent with Bidirectional Interaction

### "Phenomenal Grounds of Epistemic Value" — Uriah Kriegel (2022)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/KRIPGO
- **Type**: Journal article (Philosophy Compass)
- **Key points**:
  - Explores how phenomenal consciousness grounds epistemic value
  - Distinguishes valence sentientism (hedonic tone is key) from broad sentientism (consciousness generally is key)
  - Any epistemic value requiring consciousness cannot exist in a zombie world
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Dualism—zombie arguments show phenomenal consciousness does irreducible normative work

### "The Value of Consciousness" — Uriah Kriegel (2019)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/archive/KRITVO-6.pdf
- **Type**: Journal article (Analysis)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues phenomenal consciousness is morally significant
  - Explores multiple routes: intrinsic value, well-being, moral status
  - Hedonism links well-being directly to phenomenological valence
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral to aligning—supports the importance of consciousness but doesn't commit to dualism specifically

### Vedana in Buddhist Philosophy
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedan%C4%81 ; https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/14639947.2018.1450966
- **Type**: Encyclopedia / Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - Vedana = feeling tone or hedonic valence: pleasant, unpleasant, neutral
  - Arises upon sensory contact; more fundamental than emotions
  - Second foundation of mindfulness (Satipatthana Sutta)
  - Buddhist framework treats evaluative phenomenal character as a basic feature of experience, not a higher-order judgment
  - Vedana is not qualia in the Western sense—Buddhists resist reifying subjective qualities
- **Tenet alignment**: Mixed. Buddhist no-self (anatta) conflicts with dualism's requirement for a conscious subject. But the analysis of evaluative phenomenal character as irreducible to sensation is structurally parallel to the normative qualia thesis

### Confucian Moral Phenomenology (Qing 情, Li 理, Qi 氣)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/emotions-chinese/ ; https://iep.utm.edu/neo-confucian-philosophy/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia entries
- **Key points**:
  - Neo-Confucianism develops a dualism of li (cosmic pattern/principle) and qi (vital force/material energy)
  - Moral feelings (qing) are not mere emotions but constitutive of ethical life
  - Zhu Xi: evaluative moral awareness emerges where li and qi interact in human nature
  - Emotion is taken as a constitutive structural condition of human praxis, not accidental
  - Wang Yangming: moral knowledge is innate and phenomenally immediate (liangzhi)
- **Tenet alignment**: Surprisingly strong alignment. Neo-Confucian li/qi dualism parallels mind/matter dualism. Treating moral feeling as irreducible and phenomenally immediate aligns with evaluative qualia thesis. However, Confucian framework is typically monist at the ultimate level

### Indian Rasa Theory — Abhinavagupta (c. 1000 CE)
- **URL**: https://philosophy.institute/aesthetics/abhinavagupta-rasa-theory-aesthetics/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia / Academic summary
- **Key points**:
  - Rasa = aesthetic "flavor" or evaluative consciousness experienced by spectators
  - Abhinavagupta shifted focus from objective artistic qualities to subjective phenomenal experience
  - Aesthetic experience (rasana) is a form of unified consciousness (ekaghanata) that transcends ordinary emotion
  - Connects to Kashmir Shaivism: aesthetic consciousness is a path to spiritual realization (ananda/bliss)
  - Rasa is "nothing but aesthetic enjoyment, and this enjoyment consists exclusively in a kind of knowledge or consciousness"
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Dualism—rasa theory treats evaluative consciousness as irreducible and transcendent. The Kashmir Shaivite metaphysics is idealist rather than dualist, but the phenomenological analysis of evaluative experience supports the irreducibility of qualia

### Islamic Philosophy of Consciousness — Avicenna and Mulla Sadra
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mulla-sadra/ ; https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/selfawareness-in-islamic-philosophy/CA8C4D24647D871DC54301BCB1DD4053
- **Type**: Encyclopedia / Academic book
- **Key points**:
  - Avicenna's flying man argument: self-awareness is pure first-personality, independent of bodily sensation
  - Mulla Sadra (17th c.): knowledge is a mode of existence, not mere representation
  - In knowing, the intellect becomes ontologically unified with the intelligible form—a transformation of the knowing subject
  - Sadra's "Transcendent Wisdom" synthesizes revelation, demonstration, and intuitive knowledge
  - Evaluative dimension: knowledge of value is not abstraction from experience but participation in modes of being
- **Tenet alignment**: Avicenna's dualism of soul and body aligns with Dualism tenet. Sadra's framework is more complex (existential monism with functional dualism). Both treat consciousness as irreducible and causally active, supporting Bidirectional Interaction

## Major Positions

### Normative Qualia Realism (Rawlette, Dimitrov)
- **Proponents**: Sharon Hewitt Rawlette, Martin Dimitrov, influenced by Thomas Nagel
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal experiences of pleasure and pain literally instantiate intrinsic goodness and badness. Value is not projected onto experience—it is constitutive of certain phenomenal qualities
- **Key arguments**: Direct acquaintance with felt badness yields veridical beliefs about intrinsic disvalue; no appearance-reality gap for simple nonrepresentational phenomenal states; this grounds moral realism without mysterious non-natural properties
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly aligned. If evaluative properties are irreducible features of phenomenal experience, then consciousness does something that physical description alone cannot capture. This strengthens both Dualism (irreducibility) and Bidirectional Interaction (phenomenal value is not epiphenomenal—it does real normative work)

### Phenomenal-Normative Dualism (Sosa)
- **Proponents**: David Sosa
- **Core claim**: There are two independent motivations for dualism—the phenomenal (what-it's-like-ness) and the normative (rationality, reasons). Normativity may resist naturalization even more stubbornly than consciousness
- **Key arguments**: Even if we could explain phenomenal consciousness physically, the normativity of reason might remain irreducible. These are distinct problems requiring potentially distinct solutions
- **Relation to site tenets**: Extends the Dualism tenet—suggests consciousness and normativity are two faces of the same resistance to physicalist reduction, though potentially independent

### Phenomenal Grounds of Epistemic Value (Kriegel)
- **Proponents**: Uriah Kriegel
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal consciousness grounds various forms of epistemic and moral value. Without phenomenal experience, certain normative properties cannot be instantiated
- **Key arguments**: Zombie worlds lack epistemic value; phenomenal consciousness is necessary for well-being; valenced experience specifically (or consciousness broadly) is necessary for moral status
- **Relation to site tenets**: Supports Dualism indirectly—if phenomenal consciousness is necessary for value, and value is real, then phenomenal consciousness is not eliminable or reducible

### Buddhist Vedana Analysis
- **Proponents**: Early Buddhist texts (Satipatthana Sutta), Abhidharma tradition
- **Core claim**: Evaluative feeling tone (pleasant/unpleasant/neutral) is a fundamental constituent of experience, arising at sensory contact before conceptual elaboration
- **Key arguments**: Vedana is not emotion, not judgment, not preference—it is pre-reflective evaluative phenomenology. Mindfulness of vedana reveals the evaluative structure of raw experience
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partial alignment. The phenomenological analysis supports evaluative qualia as irreducible. However, Buddhist metaphysics (anatta, dependent origination) rejects the kind of substantial self or consciousness that the Map's dualism requires. The structural insight is valuable even if the metaphysical framework differs

### Neo-Confucian Moral Phenomenology
- **Proponents**: Zhu Xi, Wang Yangming, Mencius (foundational)
- **Core claim**: Moral consciousness is phenomenally immediate—humans have innate evaluative awareness (liangzhi) that manifests through genuine affects (qing). The cosmic order (li) is not merely known but felt
- **Key arguments**: Moral feelings like compassion, shame, and reverence are not learned responses but spontaneous manifestations of human nature's participation in cosmic principle. Evaluative experience is constitutive of ethical life, not accidental
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns structurally with evaluative qualia thesis. The li/qi framework provides a cross-cultural parallel to mind/matter dualism. However, Neo-Confucianism is not strictly dualist—li and qi are interdependent aspects of one reality

### Kashmir Shaivite Aesthetics (Abhinavagupta)
- **Proponents**: Abhinavagupta, Bharata (Natyashastra), broader Indian aesthetic tradition
- **Core claim**: Aesthetic experience (rasa) is a form of evaluative consciousness that transcends ordinary emotional response. It is unified (ekaghanata), self-luminous, and reveals the nature of consciousness itself
- **Key arguments**: Rasa is not reaction to stimuli but a mode of knowing through feeling. Aesthetic consciousness reveals consciousness's inherent evaluative structure. The spectator's experience is not merely subjective—it participates in universal consciousness
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong alignment with Dualism's emphasis on irreducibility of consciousness. The evaluative dimension of rasa supports the claim that phenomenal experience carries normative weight. Idealist metaphysics differs from the Map's dualism but shares the commitment to consciousness as fundamental

### Islamic Existential Epistemology (Avicenna, Mulla Sadra)
- **Proponents**: Avicenna (Ibn Sina), Mulla Sadra, Suhrawardi
- **Core claim**: Knowledge is not representation but a mode of existence. Self-awareness is irreducible (Avicenna's flying man). In knowing value, the knower participates in the mode of being that constitutes that value (Sadra)
- **Key arguments**: The flying man establishes pure self-awareness without sensory input. Sadra's "knowledge by presence" (ilm al-huduri) means evaluative knowledge is not abstracted from experience but constituted by ontological participation
- **Relation to site tenets**: Avicenna's dualism directly supports the Dualism tenet. Sadra's framework—where consciousness transforms the knowing subject—supports Bidirectional Interaction. Both traditions treat consciousness as causally and ontologically irreducible

## Key Debates

### Is Felt Badness Intrinsic or Relational?
- **Sides**: Rawlette/Dimitrov (intrinsic—badness is a phenomenal quality) vs. functionalists/representationalists (badness is relational—depends on what the experience represents or what role it plays)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether evaluative properties are simple, nonrepresentational features of experience or whether they depend on representational content or functional role
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Dimitrov's 2025 paper is the most recent major defense of the intrinsic view

### Can Evaluative Qualia Ground Moral Realism?
- **Sides**: Rawlette (yes—normative qualia provide the missing foundation for moral realism) vs. critics who argue this collapses into subjectivism or faces the naturalistic fallacy
- **Core disagreement**: Whether phenomenal properties can be genuinely normative, or whether the move from "feels bad" to "is bad" requires additional philosophical machinery
- **Current state**: Active debate, with Effective Altruism community engaging practically with the implications

### Are Buddhist Vedana and Western Qualia the Same Thing?
- **Sides**: Cross-cultural philosophers who see structural parallels vs. those who insist the metaphysical contexts are too different for meaningful comparison
- **Core disagreement**: Whether vedana (feeling tone without a self) and qualia (phenomenal properties of a conscious subject) refer to the same phenomenological feature or are fundamentally different concepts embedded in incompatible frameworks
- **Current state**: Growing interest in comparative phenomenology; no consensus

### Does Normativity Require a Separate Dualist Argument from Consciousness?
- **Sides**: Sosa (yes—normativity and phenomenality are independent problems for physicalism) vs. those who think phenomenal consciousness subsumes or explains normativity
- **Core disagreement**: Whether solving the hard problem of consciousness would automatically solve the normative problem, or whether irreducible normativity persists even in a world where consciousness is explained
- **Current state**: Relatively unexplored; Sosa notes the lack of a dramatic resolution

## Gaps in Research

- No direct scholarly treatment found that systematically compares evaluative qualia across all these traditions in a single work
- Limited engagement between the normative qualia literature (Rawlette, Dimitrov) and the cross-cultural phenomenology literature
- Sosa's "two forms of dualism" thesis is underdeveloped—few others have picked up the thread
- The relationship between evaluative qualia and quantum-level interaction (the Map's Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet) is unexplored: if consciousness carries intrinsic value at the phenomenal level, does this constrain what kinds of physical interaction are plausible?
- African philosophical traditions on consciousness and value were not found in this search and would benefit from dedicated research
- Daoist and indigenous perspectives on evaluative experience also absent
