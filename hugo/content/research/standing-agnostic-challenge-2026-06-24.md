## Executive Summary

The "Standing Agnostic Challenge" is the Map's house name for the argument in Yoram Gutfreund's 2024 paper *Neuroscience of animal consciousness: still agnostic after all* (Frontiers in Psychology). The argument: third-person methods (behaviour and neural correlates) can establish that an organism makes perceptual decisions and instantiates sophisticated cognition, but they **cannot in principle distinguish cognition accompanied by felt experience from cognition without it**. Gutfreund frames this as a "fundamental, unsolvable problem" rather than a deficiency of immature science. The Map already invokes this by named anchor in at least four articles (animal-consciousness, consciousness-in-simple-organisms, cetacean-and-corvid-consciousness, interface-efficacy-and-the-cognitive-gap), reconstructing it each time; it needs a single canonical concept page those articles can wikilink. The challenge is distinct from but downstream of the hard problem and the problem of other minds, and it is the epistemic complement to Birch's precautionary "edge of sentience" framework. **The load-bearing Gutfreund 2024 citation is VERIFIED at the publisher of record** (DOI, volume, article number, and the exact quoted sentence all confirmed — see Citations).

- **Key points**:
  - The "distribution question" — *which animal species share with us humans the enigmatic capability for conscious awareness* — is a philosophical question stemming from the **problem of other minds**.
  - Two dominant scientific approaches both fail to settle it: (1) finding neural structures/activity akin to human neural correlates of consciousness (NCC); (2) recognising animal behaviours that resemble conscious human behaviours.
  - The limitation is "**not a problem of premature science but a fundamental, unsolvable problem**. We neuroscientists are unable to provide answers that transcend our personal beliefs because we can only observe cognitive behaviors and/or their underlying neural mechanisms, and the relationship between cognition and consciousness remains elusive."
  - Sharp conceptual cut: "**Perception does not equal sensory consciousness. The former is the detection of the stimulus, whereas the latter is the subjective experience/feeling of the stimulus (awareness).**"
  - Anthropomorphism is the diagnosed bias: the default preference for the "felt experience" hypothesis "stems from anthropomorphism… While anthropomorphism is natural, sensible, and important for guiding decisions about animal welfare, it cannot replace rigorous scientific reasoning."
  - Worked example — the Nieder et al. crow study (carrion crows, delayed near-threshold visual detection; nidopallium caudolaterale [NCL] activity predicts behavioural choice; authors claimed a "neural correlate of visual consciousness"). Gutfreund's critique: the crows "do not report their subjective experiences, rather they make a behavioral decision"; the study cannot separate neural correlates of *perceptual decision* from NCC; and by that logic any active organism making stimulus-dependent choices would qualify as conscious, making the criterion unfalsifiable. "The extra-step of inferring sensory consciousness is not a direct outcome of the evidence."
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns with Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits) — agnosticism *is* the principled refusal to let parsimony settle an underdetermined question. Compatible with Tenet 1 (Dualism) at the epistemic level — Gutfreund treats consciousness as a first-person phenomenon resistant to third-person reduction — but Gutfreund himself takes no positive dualist stance; he is methodologically agnostic, not metaphysically dualist. The Map's tenets remove a *metaphysical defeater* (no substrate barrier) but, as the existing articles correctly state, **do not absorb or dissolve Gutfreund's epistemic challenge.**

### Birch, J. (2024) — The Edge of Sentience: Risk and Precaution in Humans, Other Animals, and AI
- **URL (OUP)**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-edge-of-sentience-9780192870421
- **ISBN**: 9780192870421 (Oxford University Press, 2024; open-access)
- **Type**: Monograph
- **Key points**:
  - Defines **sentience** as the capacity for valenced experience (pain, pleasure, etc.).
  - Introduces the **"sentience candidate"**: a system there is a *realistic possibility* is sentient, given the best available evidence.
  - **Precautionary principle**: when there is a realistic possibility of sentience, it is irresponsible to ignore it in decisions affecting the being; a duty not to cause gratuitous suffering.
  - **Run-ahead principle** (for AI): regulation of sentient-AI development should run ahead of what current technology's risks would proportionately warrant, accounting for credible future trajectories.
  - Markers of sentience drawn from functionalist theories (global workspace, perceptual reality monitoring); "deep computational markers" possible.
- **Relation to the Standing Agnostic Challenge**: Birch is the *practical/ethical complement* to Gutfreund's *epistemic* point. Both accept that felt experience is not directly observable. Gutfreund concludes "remain agnostic"; Birch concludes "act precautionarily under that agnosticism" — you do not need to *settle* sentience to incur a duty, only to establish a realistic possibility. The two are not in conflict: Gutfreund bounds what evidence can show; Birch tells you what to do given that bound. The Map already has a topic [birch-edge-of-sentience-and-the-five-tier-scale](/topics/birch-edge-of-sentience-and-the-five-tier-scale/) — the new concept should wikilink it as the "what to do about the standing challenge" sibling.
- **Tenet alignment**: Birch's precaution-under-uncertainty is Occam-limits-friendly (Tenet 5) — refuses to let simplicity-driven dismissal ("probably just a reflex") override a live possibility.

### New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness (2024)
- **URL**: https://sites.google.com/nyu.edu/nydeclaration/declaration
- **Type**: Public scientific declaration (launched 19 April 2024, NYU; initiated by Jeff Sebo, Kristin Andrews, Jonathan Birch; 40 initial signatories, hundreds since)
- **Key claims**: "**strong scientific support**" for conscious experience in other mammals and birds; "**at least a realistic possibility**" of conscious experience in all vertebrates (reptiles, amphibians, fishes) and many invertebrates (at minimum cephalopods, decapods, insects). Core action principle: "When there is a realistic possibility of conscious experience in an animal, it is irresponsible to ignore that possibility."
- **Role in the dialectic**: This is the **foil** Gutfreund argues against. Where the Declaration reads convergent markers as "strong support," Gutfreund reads the same data as failing to cross the cognition→experience gap. The contrast (Declaration = positive marker confidence; Gutfreund = standing agnosticism) is the cleanest way to *position* the challenge for readers, and the interface-efficacy article already uses exactly this contrast.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/aligns — the Map can hold (as it does) that the Declaration supplies "structural permission" to entertain animal consciousness while Gutfreund supplies "the discipline of not over-interpreting comparative data."

### Background: Hard Problem & Problem of Other Minds (for the differentiation section)
- **Hard problem (Chalmers)** — SEP/IEP/Wikipedia: the problem of *experience* — why does any physical process give rise to "what it is like"? A constitutive/explanatory question.
- **Problem of other minds** — the *epistemic* question of how one can be justified in attributing mental states to anything outside one's own case.
- **URLs**: https://iep.utm.edu/hard-problem-of-conciousness/ ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_problem_of_consciousness
- **Distinction relevant to the new concept**: The hard problem is about *why/how* experience arises at all (metaphysical/explanatory). The other-minds problem is about *whether I can know* another system has it (general skepticism). **The Standing Agnostic Challenge is a specific, empirically-grounded restriction of the other-minds problem to the cognition–consciousness inference, sharpened by neuroscience's actual toolkit.** It says: even granting the other-minds problem can be pragmatically managed for fellow humans (shared report + shared architecture), the moment you remove verbal report (animals, AI), every behavioural or neural marker you have left underdetermines presence-vs-absence of felt experience — because the markers were calibrated on the human case where cognition and consciousness travel together, and the data cannot pry them apart elsewhere. It presupposes the hard problem (if experience reduced cleanly to function, the inference would go through) without being identical to it.

## Major Positions

### Standing Agnosticism (Gutfreund)
- **Proponents**: Yoram Gutfreund (2024); a tradition of agnosticism about animal consciousness.
- **Core claim**: The presence vs. absence of felt experience in non-reporting systems is underdetermined by all available third-person evidence (behavioural + neural); this underdetermination is in-principle, not a temporary instrument gap.
- **Key arguments**: (1) perception ≠ sensory consciousness; (2) NCC and behavioural-resemblance approaches both presuppose what they aim to show; (3) the anthropomorphism bias inflates the felt-experience prior; (4) the falsifiability collapse (everything that decides becomes conscious).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Tenet 5 (principled agnosticism where parsimony cannot adjudicate); epistemically compatible with Tenet 1 but not a dualist claim.

### Marker-Based Confidence (New York Declaration; Birch et al. marker framework; Andrews, Birch & Sebo)
- **Proponents**: Sebo, Andrews, Birch; the 2024 Declaration's signatories.
- **Core claim**: Convergent behavioural/neural markers warrant "strong support" (mammals, birds) or "realistic possibility" (other vertebrates, many invertebrates).
- **Key arguments**: convergent markers across taxa; evolutionary continuity; functionalist theories supply principled markers.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map treats this as supplying *structural permission* (no defeater) without elevating evidence grade past what Gutfreund's challenge allows.

### Precautionary Action Under Agnosticism (Birch, Edge of Sentience)
- **Core claim**: You needn't resolve sentience to incur duties; a realistic possibility suffices (sentience candidate → precaution; run-ahead for AI).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Tenet 5 — refuses simplicity-driven dismissal of live possibilities.

## Key Debates

### Does convergent marker evidence cross the cognition→experience gap?
- **Sides**: Declaration camp (yes, to "strong support"/"realistic possibility") vs. Gutfreund (no — markers underdetermine).
- **Core disagreement**: whether functional/behavioural sufficiency is evidence *of* accompanying experience or merely evidence of the functional profile that, in humans, co-occurs with experience.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; the Map sides with neither on grade — it adopts the standing challenge as a permanent epistemic ceiling while removing the metaphysical defeater.

### Is the agnosticism in-principle or merely current?
- **Sides**: Gutfreund (in-principle, "fundamental, unsolvable") vs. optimists who expect future "consciousness-meters."
- **Core disagreement**: whether the cognition–consciousness relationship is the kind of thing third-person science could ever operationalise.
- **Map relevance**: The Map's [the-interface-problem](/topics/the-interface-problem/) catalogues the missing joint phenomenal-physical instrumentation; the standing challenge is the epistemic statement of why that instrument is missing and may stay missing.
