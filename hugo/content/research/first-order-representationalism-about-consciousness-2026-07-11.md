## Executive Summary

First-order representationalism (also "first-order intentionalism" or "strong representationalism") is the reductive-physicalist thesis that the phenomenal character of an experience — its "what it is like" — just *is*, or is exhausted by, that experience's first-order representational content: what the experience represents the world (or the body) as being like. Its paradigm defenders are Michael Tye (the PANIC theory) and Fred Dretske (teleosemantic representational naturalism); Gilbert Harman supplies the influential transparency argument that motivates it. It is distinguished from *higher-order* representationalism (William Lycan's inner-sense theory; David Rosenthal's HOT theory), on which a state is conscious in virtue of being represented by a further mental state. The standard battlefield is a cluster of objections — inverted spectrum, Ned Block's Inverted Earth, and Block's "mental paint" — alleging phenomenal differences without representational differences (or vice versa). For the Map, first-order representationalism is the central reductive rival to Tenet 1 (dualism): it is the paradigm attempt to naturalize phenomenal character as a tracking relation and thereby dissolve the hard problem. The Map's resistance is that representationalism *relocates* but does not close the explanatory gap — it never explains why representing-thus should feel like anything — and that the transparency datum it leans on is fully compatible with a dualist reading.

## Key Sources

### Representational Theories of Consciousness (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consciousness-representational/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Draws the first-order vs. higher-order distinction: first-order representationalism holds sensory qualities are the representational contents of external (or bodily) properties; higher-order theories explain consciousness as one mental state representing another.
  - Catalogues the main first-order versions: Tye (nonconceptual, "poised" content), Dretske (teleofunctional/informational representation), Harman (transparency), plus non-reductive variants (Crane, Chalmers).
  - Frames the transparency argument (Harman) as the primary positive case for representationalism.
  - Lists the standard objections: inverted spectrum, Inverted Earth (Block 1990), mental paint (Block 1996), and "phenomenal" hard cases (blur, attention shifts, aspect perception / duck-rabbit).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (survey); the first-order reductive versions *conflict* with Tenet 1.

### Michael Tye, *Ten Problems of Consciousness: A Representational Theory of the Phenomenal Mind* (1995, MIT Press)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/TYETPO (verified: PhilPapers record + Google Books)
- **Type**: Book (monograph)
- **Key points**:
  - Introduces the PANIC theory: phenomenal character is "one and the same as" **P**oised, **A**bstract, **N**onconceptual **I**ntentional **C**ontent. "Poised" = content standing ready to make a direct impact on the belief/desire system; "abstract" = no particular object need exist; "nonconceptual" = content the subject need not possess concepts for.
  - The book is organized around ten problems Tye claims any adequate theory of consciousness must solve.
  - Strong (reductive) representationalism: no phenomenal residue beyond represented content; qualia in the intrinsic-non-representational sense are denied.
  - (Note: Tye later moved away from PANIC toward other views in his more recent work — the 1995 statement is the canonical first-order representationalist text.)
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (reductive physicalism about phenomenal character).

### Michael Tye, *Consciousness, Color, and Content* (2000, MIT Press)
- **URL**: https://www.amazon.com/Consciousness-Color-Content-Representation-Mind/dp/0262700883 (ISBN 9780262700887, verified)
- **Type**: Book (monograph)
- **Key points**:
  - Develops and defends representationalism against the objections that accumulated after *Ten Problems*, including inverted-spectrum and Inverted-Earth style cases.
  - Extends the account to color experience and to the metaphysics of represented color properties.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1.

### Fred Dretske, *Naturalizing the Mind* (1995, MIT Press; the Jean Nicod Lectures)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/DRENTM (verified: MIT Press / Cambridge Core review record)
- **Type**: Book (monograph)
- **Key points**:
  - Representational Thesis: (1) all mental facts are representational facts, and (2) all representational facts are facts about informational functions.
  - The phenomenal (qualitative) aspects of a perceptual experience are identical to the external, real-world properties the experience *represents* objects as having — combined with an evolutionary/teleological account of what gives a state its representational function.
  - Result advertised as a "completely naturalistic" account of phenomenal consciousness; treats introspection, qualia, inverted spectra, the biological function of experience, and knowing alien minds.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (the paradigm naturalizing move).

### William G. Lycan, *Consciousness and Experience* (1996, MIT Press)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/LYCCAE (verified: PhilPapers record)
- **Type**: Book (monograph)
- **Key points**:
  - **Distinct from Tye/Dretske**: Lycan pairs a *representational* theory of qualia (sensory qualities are the intentional objects of sensory representations) with a *higher-order* "inner sense" theory of state-consciousness (attention mechanisms monitor lower-level processes).
  - "Hegemony of representation": there is nothing to mind/consciousness beyond intentionality, functional organization, and second-order representation.
  - Important for the note's core distinction: Lycan is representationalist about *qualia* but higher-order about *awareness* — so he is the natural bridge to the existing higher-order-theories page and should not be filed as a pure first-order theorist.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1.

### Gilbert Harman, "The Intrinsic Quality of Experience" (1990, *Philosophical Perspectives* 4: 31–52)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consciousness-representational/ (position summarized in SEP; canonical venue Philosophical Perspectives vol. 4, ed. Tomberlin)
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - The transparency (diaphanousness) argument: when you try to attend to your visual experience of a tree, "the only features there to turn your attention to will be features of the presented tree." You look *through* the experience to its object; you never encounter intrinsic non-representational features of the experience itself.
  - Best explanation, per Harman/Tye: phenomenal character consists *entirely* in representational content.
- **Tenet alignment**: The argument is used *against* qualia realism / dualism; but the datum (transparency) is contested — see Map framing below.

### Ned Block, "Inverted Earth" (1990, *Philosophical Perspectives* 4: 53–79) and "Mental Paint" (2003)
- **URL**: http://fitelson.org/prosem/block.pdf ("Mental Paint"); Inverted Earth in Philosophical Perspectives vol. 4
- **Type**: Journal articles / book chapter
- **Key points**:
  - **Inverted Earth**: a planet where every color is the complementary of Earth's *and* the color vocabulary is inversely swapped, so speech remains "correct." A person transported there (with color-inverting lenses) eventually comes to *represent* (wide, externalist content) grass as red, sky as yellow, etc., while — Block argues — their phenomenal character stays put. Different representational content, same phenomenal character → phenomenal character is not (wide) representational content.
  - **Mental paint / mental latex**: introspectable, intrinsic, non-representational features of experience that persist across cases of identical represented content. Block's positive view is a qualia-realist "phenomenism."
  - Also invoked: phosphene / afterimage and blurry-vision cases where phenomenal features seem to lack a matching represented-property difference.
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns *in effect* with the Map's resistance (preserves a non-representational phenomenal residue) — though Block is himself a physicalist, not a dualist. Useful as an internal-to-physicalism pressure point, not as a dualist ally.

## Major Positions

### First-Order (Strong / Reductive) Representationalism — Tye, Dretske, Harman
- **Proponents**: Michael Tye, Fred Dretske, Gilbert Harman (Daniel Dennett is often grouped here).
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal character = first-order representational content of the experience (what it represents the world/body as being like). There is no further intrinsic, non-representational phenomenal property. Content is typically cashed out via *tracking* / informational-teleological relations (Dretske) or "poised nonconceptual" content (Tye).
- **Key arguments**:
  1. Transparency (Harman/Tye): introspection only ever finds represented worldly properties, never intrinsic features of the experience.
  2. Naturalistic economy: if phenomenal character reduces to tracking-relations, the hard problem dissolves — there is nothing left over to explain.
  3. Explains the intentionality and world-directedness of perception directly.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct denial of Tenet 1. This is the paradigm reductive-physicalist rival: it aims to *dissolve* rather than answer the hard problem by identifying feel with content.

### Higher-Order Representationalism — Lycan, Rosenthal (contrast class)
- **Proponents**: William Lycan (inner-sense / "inner monitoring"), David Rosenthal (higher-order thought, HOT).
- **Core claim**: A mental state is *conscious* in virtue of being represented by a suitable further (higher-order) mental state. Lycan additionally treats qualia representationally at the first order but locates consciousness *of* a state in second-order monitoring.
- **Key arguments**: The "simple argument" (a state we are in no way aware of is not a conscious state); functional divide-and-conquer against physicalism's challengers.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Also physicalist, also conflicts with Tenet 1, but via a different mechanism — see the existing higher-order-theories concept page. The key taxonomic point for the downstream article: *first*-order representationalism denies that any higher-order state is *needed* for phenomenal character; the feel is already there in the first-order content.

### Non-Reductive Representationalism (boundary case)
- **Proponents**: Tim Crane, David Chalmers (Fregean-content representationalism).
- **Core claim**: Phenomenal character covaries with / supervenes on representational content, but the content-property is not thereby *physically* reduced (Chalmers pairs it with property dualism / panprotopsychism).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Materially closer to the Map — representational structure without reductive physicalism. Worth flagging in the article to keep "representationalism" from being conflated with "physicalism"; the Map opposes the *reductive* first-order version specifically.

## Key Debates

### Does the inverted spectrum / Inverted Earth refute representationalism?
- **Sides**: Block (yes — phenomenal difference without representational difference, or vice versa) vs. Tye/Lycan (no — bite the bullet: content also shifts, or the case is incoherent / the phenomenal character does track the newly represented properties).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether phenomenal character can come apart from (wide) representational content.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and unresolved; a defining fault line of the literature. Representationalists typically go "wide-content" or deny the intuition; opponents insist on the residual "mental paint."

### Is the transparency of experience good evidence for representationalism?
- **Sides**: Harman/Tye/Dennett (transparency shows there is nothing but represented properties) vs. Block, Kind, and others (transparency is real but under-determined — it shows we *attend through* experience, not that experience *has* no intrinsic character; the inference smuggles in controversial assumptions).
- **Core disagreement**: What the introspective datum licenses.
- **Current state**: Live; the datum is widely accepted, its *interpretation* is not.

### Does representationalism close the explanatory gap?
- **Sides**: Representationalists (it dissolves the gap — once feel = content, the residual "why does it feel like anything" is confused) vs. gap defenders (Levine 1983; Chalmers) (relocating feel into content just re-poses the question at the level of content — *why does representing red feel like this?*).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether reduction of feel to tracking-content is explanatory or merely re-labels the mystery.
- **Current state**: Unresolved — this is exactly where the Map plants its flag.

Lead should be framework-relative and *not* a bald phenomenal-absence claim (per the expand-topic lead-over-claim pattern): state the view precisely and note it is the leading reductive rival the Map engages and resists, not a proof of anything.
2. **The transparency hinge**: Use transparency as the pivot. The Map can *accept* the transparency datum and read it dualistically — experience is transparent *to* its objects precisely because phenomenal binding is a non-physical coupling *to* the represented world, not an identity *with* representational content. Transparency undercuts naive qualia-as-inner-objects without establishing reduction.
3. **The relocation objection** (the Map's core move): Argue representationalism *relocates* the explanatory gap rather than closing it — replacing "why does C-fiber firing feel like this?" with "why does representing-thus feel like anything?" No tracking relation, however teleologically refined, entails a felt character; the gap survives the translation into content-talk.
4. **Taxonomy service**: Clearly separate first-order (Tye/Dretske) from higher-order (Lycan/Rosenthal) and from non-reductive (Crane/Chalmers) representationalism, so the corpus stops conflating "representationalism" with "physicalism." ### Map-relative framing (required for the article)

- **Tenet 1 (Dualism)**: First-order representationalism is a *direct denial* — phenomenal character is nothing over and above physical/functional tracking content. The article must present the dialectic and where the Map locates its resistance, **not** bald-assert refutation.
- **The hard problem**: The Map's line is that reduction of feel to content is not explanation but re-description; the "why anything at all" residue is exactly what Tenet-1 dualism takes to be irreducible.
- **Transparency compatibility**: The transparency intuition is a genuine phenomenological fact the Map can own; it is compatible with (arguably even suggestive of) a dualism on which experience is a non-physical coupling *directed at* worldly properties. Present as an interpretive option, not a knockdown.
- **Honest counterweight**: Give the representationalist their best case (naturalistic economy, transparency, world-directedness) before the resistance. The strongest internal-to-physicalism pressure on strong representationalism (Block's mental paint) comes from a *physicalist*, so cite it as dialectical pressure, not as a dualist ally.
