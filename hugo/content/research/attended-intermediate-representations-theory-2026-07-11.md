## Executive Summary

Jesse Prinz's AIR theory (Attended Intermediate-level Representation), developed most fully in *The Conscious Brain: How Attention Engenders Experience* (Oxford University Press, 2012), holds that a mental state is conscious **when and only when** an *intermediate-level* perceptual representation is *modulated by attention*, where attention is functionally defined as the process that makes information available to working memory. Two theses do the work: (1) consciousness always arises at one particular stage of perceptual processing — the intermediate level, roughly Marr's 2.5-D sketch, sitting between low-level feature detectors (edges, orientations) and high-level viewpoint-invariant object categories; and (2) attention is the necessary-and-sufficient gate that turns such a representation into a conscious experience, with a proposed neural signature in gamma-band synchrony of the vectors realizing those representations. AIR is a naturalistic, reductive neurofunctional theory: it aims to specify the psychological and neural correlates of consciousness, not to dissolve the explanatory gap. For The Unfinishable Map (Tenet 1) it is a rival NCC/theory-of-consciousness to engage as we do global-workspace, HOT, and IIT accounts — congenial as an empirical correlate proposal, but its attention-gating story is a *functional* account that leaves the hard problem untouched. Standard objections cluster around Block's overflow argument (rich phenomenology appears to outstrip attention/access), unattended-but-conscious cases (gist, periphery), conscious non-perceptual states (intentions), and whether the intermediate level is the right grain.

### Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews — review of *The Conscious Brain*
- **URL**: https://ndpr.nd.edu/reviews/the-conscious-brain-how-attention-engenders-experience/
- **Type**: Scholarly book review
- **Key points**:
  - Intermediate level in vision ≈ Marr's 2.5-D sketch: "surfaces and shapes oriented in specific ways at various distances from us," rather than low-level edge detection or high-level abstract categories.
  - Identifies a tension between a *modest* reading (intermediateness merely identifies which representations become conscious) and an *ambitious* reading (it explains *why* they become conscious).
  - Characterizes Prinz's attention as "processes that allow information to be encoded in working memory," and the neural proposal as intermediate-level representations firing "in the gamma range" and becoming available to working memory.
  - Raises objections: fragmented (non-intermediate) contents reported in schizophrenia; blindsight (patient GY) as possible attention-without-consciousness; and whether Prinz's strong neural-identity claims exceed what an explanatory theory needs.
- **Note on quoting**: the phrases "processes that allow information to be encoded in working memory" and "vectorwaves … fire in the gamma range" are the *reviewer's* characterizations/partial quotations, not confirmed verbatim from Prinz's text. Treated below as characterizations, not verified author quotes.

### Mylopoulos, "Conscious intention: a challenge for AIR theory"
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4438162/
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed article — Mylopoulos, M. (2015), *Frontiers in Psychology*, 6:675
- **Key points**:
  - Cites AIR's two core commitments with page references to the book: "Consciousness arises when and only when intermediate-level representations are modulated by attention" (Prinz 2012, p. 89) and "all consciousness is perceptual" (p. 150).
  - Objection: conscious intentions pose a dilemma — either they are attended intermediate-level *sensory* representations (but people form conscious intentions without any sensory imagery, and imagery cannot distinguish intending from desiring/predicting the same act), or intentions are never genuinely conscious (a reconstructive/post-hoc hypothesis that lacks empirical support).
  - Conclusion: AIR cannot account for all conscious mental states, specifically conscious intention/agency.
- **Tenet alignment**: A sympathetic-internal critique showing AIR's perceptualism is contestable; useful for the Map's engagement.

### Block, "Perceptual consciousness overflows cognitive access" (the overflow challenge)
- **URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1364661311002221 (Block 2011, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 15(12):567–575)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed article (target of the objection)
- **Key points**:
  - Sperling (1960) partial-report paradigm: subjects can report any cued row from a briefly-flashed array, suggesting a rich phenomenal representation of the whole array in iconic memory that outstrips the sparse capacity of what is attended/accessed.
  - Grounds Block's phenomenal-vs-access distinction: if phenomenal consciousness is richer than access, then an attention/access-based identity like AIR is false — some experience is conscious without being attended.
  - Prinz's camp (e.g., De Brigard & Prinz 2010) replies that the "overflow" contents are not genuinely conscious prior to attention (the "refrigerator light" worry cuts both ways) — a live, unresolved dispute.

## Major Positions

### AIR Theory (Prinz)
- **Proponents**: Jesse Prinz; sympathetic to Michael Cohen and to attention-necessity ("equivalency") views generally.
- **Core claim**: Consciousness = attended intermediate-level perceptual representation. Intermediate-level representations become conscious *iff* attention makes them available to working memory; neurofunctionally realized (proposed) by gamma-band synchronization of the neural vectors carrying those representations in sensory cortex.
- **Why intermediate (not low or high)**: Low-level states (edges, local orientation) are too fine-grained and not what introspection reports; high-level states are viewpoint-invariant categorical/conceptual and can be unconscious (e.g., masked-priming category activation). The intermediate level (surfaces, contours, boundaries from a viewpoint — Marr's 2.5-D sketch) matches the grain of what we consciously see. Prinz generalizes the intermediate-level thesis across modalities.
- **Why attention (necessary and sufficient)**: Attention is the process that changes information flow so perceptual content can be encoded in working memory. Prinz argues attention is both necessary (no consciousness without it) and sufficient (attending an intermediate representation suffices for experience).
- **Metaphysics**: "Neurofunctionalism" — consciousness is identified with a functionally-characterized neural process. Reductive and physicalist.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism). AIR is a well-specified correlate/mechanism story that the Map can accept *as* an empirical NCC candidate while denying it explains *why* attended intermediate representations feel like anything — the functional gating is orthogonal to the hard problem (Tenet 5, Occam's-razor-has-limits: a simple functional identity is not thereby the true story of phenomenal character).

### Dissociationist / Overflow view (Block; Koch & Tsuchiya; Lamme)
- **Proponents**: Ned Block, Christof Koch, Naotsugu Tsuchiya, Victor Lamme.
- **Core claim**: Consciousness and attention are dissociable. Phenomenal consciousness can overflow attention/access (Sperling iconic memory) and attention can occur without consciousness. Hence attention is not necessary-and-sufficient for consciousness.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The overflow view is the leading empirical challenge to AIR. Neutral-to-congenial to the Map: it undercuts a reductive attention-identity and preserves conceptual space for phenomenality that is not exhausted by functional access — consonant with Tenet 1's insistence that experience is not reducible to information-processing.

### Global Workspace Theory (contrast, not duplication)
- **Proponents**: Baars; Dehaene, Changeux (Global Neuronal Workspace).
- **Contrast with AIR**: GWT locates consciousness in *global broadcast* to a fronto-parietal workspace (availability to many consumer systems, "ignition"); AIR locates it earlier, in *sensory-cortex* intermediate representations made available to working memory via attention. AIR is more perceptual and more posterior; GWT is more access/broadcast and more frontal. Both are attention-adjacent access theories but differ on the *locus* and *level*. (Home page: [global-workspace-theory](/concepts/global-workspace-theory/).)

### Higher-Order Theories (contrast)
- **Contrast**: HOT makes a state conscious in virtue of a higher-order representation *of* that state. AIR is first-order-ish and perceptual: no meta-representation required, only attention to an intermediate perceptual state. Prinz explicitly rejects that higher-order thought is required.

### Integrated Information Theory (contrast)
- **Contrast**: IIT identifies consciousness with integrated information (Φ) intrinsic to a system's cause-effect structure, in principle substrate-general and not tied to attention or perception. AIR is mechanism- and process-specific (attention + intermediate perceptual level + gamma synchrony) and perceptualist. IIT is the sharpest contrast on the "is consciousness essentially perceptual?" axis.

## Key Debates

### Overflow: does phenomenal consciousness outstrip attention?
- **Sides**: Block/Koch/Lamme (yes — Sperling overflow, rich phenomenology) vs. Prinz/Cohen/De Brigard (no — unattended "overflow" is not genuinely conscious).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the partial-report evidence shows conscious-but-unaccessed content, or merely a large *preconscious* iconic buffer that becomes conscious only when attended.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and methodologically fraught (the "refrigerator-light" problem: any probe of whether unattended content was conscious itself deploys attention). No consensus.

### Is attention sufficient for consciousness?
- **Sides**: Prinz (yes) vs. critics who cite attention-without-report cases and cueing/priming effects that modulate behavior without acknowledged experience.
- **Current state**: Contested; hinges on operationalizing "attention" and "consciousness" non-circularly (NDPR flags a circularity worry: executive control may presuppose consciousness).

### Is consciousness essentially perceptual? (conscious intention)
- **Sides**: Prinz ("all consciousness is perceptual", p. 150) vs. Mylopoulos and defenders of cognitive phenomenology (conscious intentions, thoughts, and agency lack sensory imagery yet seem conscious).
- **Current state**: A standing objection; connects to the broader cognitive-phenomenology debate.

### Is the intermediate level the right grain?
- **Sides**: Prinz (intermediate ≈ 2.5-D sketch) vs. reports of non-intermediate conscious contents (e.g., fragmented percepts in schizophrenia) and questions about non-visual modalities where the "intermediate level" is less clearly defined.
- **Current state**: Open; the modest-vs-ambitious reading (NDPR) means the thesis's force depends on whether intermediateness *explains* or merely *labels* what becomes conscious.
