## Executive Summary

The "capability division problem" is not an established term in the literature but names a genuine and underexplored challenge for interactionist dualism: when the non-physical mind and the physical brain both contribute to vision, which capabilities belong to which domain? Neuroscience has mapped extensive unconscious visual processing (feature extraction, motion detection, spatial orientation, even emotional categorisation) that proceeds without phenomenal awareness. Blindsight demonstrates that the brain alone can guide visually informed behaviour. The two-streams hypothesis shows dorsal-pathway action guidance operates largely without conscious awareness. Yet conscious vision adds something: unified phenomenal experience, flexible reasoning about visual content, perceptual ownership, and the capacity to report and deliberate on what is seen. For interactionist dualism, this empirical landscape creates a specific problem — specifying the division of labour between brain and mind in vision without either (a) rendering consciousness epiphenomenal or (b) overloading it with capabilities the brain demonstrably handles alone.

### Two-Streams Hypothesis (Milner & Goodale, 1992)
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-streams_hypothesis

## Major Positions

### Physicalist Position: Brain Does Everything
- **Proponents**: Dennett, Churchland, Dehaene (Global Neuronal Workspace)
- **Core claim**: All visual capabilities — from low-level feature detection to unified conscious experience — are products of neural processing. Consciousness is what information processing "looks like from the inside."
- **Key arguments**:
  - Neural correlates exist for every aspect of visual experience
  - Lesion studies show specific brain areas produce specific experience deficits
  - Unconscious processing demonstrates brain self-sufficiency for many visual tasks
  - Causal closure of the physical leaves no room for non-physical contribution
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly conflicts with all five tenets. The Map's response: correlation is not identity; neural correlates may be the physical side of an interface rather than the complete story. The existence of unconscious processing doesn't show consciousness adds nothing — it shows the brain handles some tasks independently, which is exactly what capability division predicts.

### Epiphenomenalist Position: Consciousness Watches But Doesn't Act
- **Proponents**: Huxley (historically), Jackson (early career), some contemporary naturalists
- **Core claim**: Visual consciousness is a byproduct of neural processing. The brain generates both behaviour and experience, but experience doesn't causally contribute to behaviour.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Visual behaviour can be explained by neural processing alone
  - Blindsight shows action guidance without consciousness
  - Causal closure makes non-physical causation unnecessary
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly conflicts with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). The Map argues that if consciousness were epiphenomenal, our reliable reporting of visual experience would be accidentally correct — we'd talk about redness without redness causally influencing our speech. The evolutionary argument also applies: visual consciousness is metabolically expensive; if it contributes nothing, natural selection should have eliminated it.

### Interactionist Dualist Position: Brain and Mind Divide the Labour
- **Proponents**: Eccles, Popper, Stapp; the Map's framework
- **Core claim**: The brain handles computational visual processing (feature extraction, spatial mapping, motion detection, action guidance). Consciousness contributes phenomenal unification, perceptual ownership, flexible reasoning about visual content, and the capacity to use visual information in novel contexts.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Blindsight shows what vision looks like without conscious contribution — capable but limited
  - The ventral/dorsal dissociation maps onto conscious/unconscious visual capabilities
  - Consciousness appears at specific processing stages, consistent with an interface model
  - Quantum indeterminacy provides a candidate mechanism for mind-brain interaction
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the Map's position. The capability division problem is internal to this view — it asks interactionists to be precise about the boundary.

### Property Dualism / Naturalistic Dualism
- **Proponents**: Chalmers
- **Core claim**: Physical processing generates information states; consciousness is a fundamental property that accompanies certain information structures. The hard problem is real but consciousness may not be causally efficacious in the way interactionism requires.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Explanatory gap is real and unbridgeable by reductive methods
  - Consciousness may be law-governed (psychophysical laws) without being causally productive
  - Avoids interaction problem by not requiring non-physical causation
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partially aligns (Tenet 1 — dualism) but potentially conflicts with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction) if consciousness is merely law-correlated rather than causally active.

## Key Debates

### What Can Vision Do Without Consciousness?
- **Sides**: Extensive unconscious processing (Dehaene, blindsight researchers) vs. residual consciousness claims (Phillips)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "unconscious" visual processing is truly unconscious or merely degraded conscious processing
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The weight of evidence supports genuine unconscious visual capabilities (feature extraction, categorisation, action guidance), but Phillips's challenge to blindsight interpretation remains active. If Phillips is right, the capability division shifts — less goes to the brain alone.

### Does Conscious Vision Add Causal Power?
- **Sides**: Epiphenomenalists (consciousness contributes nothing causally) vs. interactionists (consciousness enables flexible reasoning, reporting, novel application)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the additional capabilities of conscious vs. unconscious vision are explained by more complex neural processing (still physical) or by genuine mental causation
- **Current state**: Unresolved. The baseline cognition pattern (Map's existing framework) provides the strongest interactionist argument: conscious vision enables capabilities that unconscious processing systematically cannot achieve.

### Where in the Visual Hierarchy Does Consciousness Enter?
- **Sides**: Early-stage theorists (recurrent processing in V1) vs. late-stage theorists (prefrontal involvement required) vs. mid-level (perceptual organisation in extrastriate cortex)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether consciousness requires global broadcasting (Global Workspace Theory) or arises from local recurrent processing
- **Current state**: Converging evidence suggests consciousness correlates with recurrent processing in visual areas plus frontal involvement, but the exact stage and mechanism remain debated. Recent work points to lateral occipital complex as key for conscious visual processing.

### The Two-Streams Division and Consciousness
- **Sides**: Milner & Goodale (dorsal for action, ventral for perception) vs. integrationists (streams interact substantially)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the dorsal stream truly operates without consciousness or merely with reduced consciousness
- **Current state**: The original strong dissociation has been moderated — streams interact more than originally thought. But the basic pattern holds: action guidance (dorsal) is less dependent on conscious awareness than object recognition (ventral).
