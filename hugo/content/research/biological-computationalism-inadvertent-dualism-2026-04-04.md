# Research: Biological Computationalism and Its Inadvertent Case for Dualism

**Date**: 2026-04-04
**Search queries used**: "biological computationalism philosophy of mind", "computationalism dualism consciousness philosophy", "biological naturalism Searle computation consciousness", "Stanford Encyclopedia Philosophy computational theory of mind", "biological computationalism consciousness substrate dependence 2025 2026", "computationalism implies dualism substrate independence problem consciousness", "multiple realizability argument against physicalism dualism", "Piccinini physical computation mechanistic account consciousness", "Chalmers hard problem computation functionalism organizational invariance", "Milinkovic Aru biological computationalism 2025 paper consciousness", "energy requirements undermine substrate independence functionalism consciousness"

## Executive Summary

Biological computationalism—the thesis that brains compute but in a substrate-dependent, metabolically grounded, hybrid discrete-continuous manner—has emerged as a "third path" between computational functionalism and biological naturalism. While its proponents frame it as a physicalist theory, the framework inadvertently builds a strong case for dualism. By insisting that consciousness requires something beyond abstract computation (specific physical dynamics, energy flows, scale-inseparable organisation), biological computationalism concedes the central dualist intuition: that functional organisation alone cannot account for phenomenal experience. The framework effectively identifies a "consciousness gap" between abstract computation and subjective experience, then fills it with biological specificity—but this move is structurally identical to the dualist claim that something beyond the functional is needed.

## Key Sources

### Milinkovic & Aru (2025) — "On Biological and Artificial Consciousness: A Case for Biological Computationalism"
- **URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0149763425005251
- **Type**: Journal article (Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, Vol. 181, 2026)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes biological computationalism as a third path between computational functionalism and biological naturalism
  - Brains perform hybrid discrete-continuous computation inseparable from their fluidic substrate
  - Consciousness requires "scale-inseparable, substrate-dependent multiscale processing as a metabolic optimisation strategy"
  - Continuous fields, ion flows, dendritic integration, and electromagnetic interactions are computational primitives, not implementation details
  - Current AI systems are unlikely to replicate conscious processing
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Tenet 1 (dualism) inadvertently—their substrate dependence claim mirrors the dualist insistence that consciousness is not reducible to abstract functional organisation. Aligns with Tenet 4 (no many worlds)—treats consciousness as tied to specific physical reality rather than abstractable across possible implementations
- **Quote**: "In biological computation, the algorithm is the substrate—the physical organization does not just enable the computation, it is what the computation consists of."

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "The Computational Theory of Mind"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/computational-mind/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - CTM claims mental activity is "Turing-style computation"
  - Introduced by Hilary Putnam (1967), combines Representational Theory of Mind with Computational Account of Reasoning
  - In practice all proponents embrace physicalism, but the theory is formally neutral between physicalism and substance dualism
  - Many philosophers doubt that simply being a computational state suffices for being a conscious state
- **Tenet alignment**: The formal neutrality between physicalism and dualism is significant—CTM's own framework cannot rule out dualist implementations
- **Quote**: "CTM remains neutral in the traditional debate between physicalism and substance dualism, proceeding at a very abstract level without specifying whether mental computations are implemented by physical stuff or Cartesian soul-stuff."

### Paul Thagard (2022) — "Energy Requirements Undermine Substrate Independence and Mind-Body Functionalism"
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/philosophy-of-science/article/energy-requirements-undermine-substrate-independence-and-mindbody-functionalism/2BB3C2353EFF80F9D5805CDCEA8C3C89
- **Type**: Journal article (Philosophy of Science)
- **Key points**:
  - Real-world information processing depends on energy; energy depends on material substrates
  - Church-Turing thesis provides no support for multiple realizability because it ignores time, space, and energy
  - Energy considerations undermine substrate independence claims for AI consciousness, mind uploading, simulation hypothesis
  - Endothermy, neural metabolics, and information-processing costs are not abstractions
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Tenet 1—if consciousness is energy-dependent in substrate-specific ways, functional organization alone is insufficient. Supports Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits)—the "simple" computational account omits crucial physical dependencies

### Gualtiero Piccinini — "Physical Computation: A Mechanistic Account" (OUP 2015)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/PICPCA
- **Type**: Book
- **Key points**:
  - Computation is a physical mechanism manipulating vehicles based on differences between portions of vehicles according to rules
  - Computation does not entail representation or information processing
  - Rejects pancomputationalism
  - Consciousness may be due to functional organisation of the brain without being computational—a "noncomputational functionalism"
- **Tenet alignment**: Piccinini's admission that consciousness may be noncomputational even within a functionalist framework is a significant concession toward dualist thinking. If the functional organisation that matters for consciousness is not the computational part, what is it?

### Chalmers — Organizational Invariance Principle
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/GREOCP
- **Type**: Multiple papers/discussions
- **Key points**:
  - Consciousness depends on functional organisation (organizational invariance), not material constitution
  - Any two systems processing information the same way should have the same conscious states
  - Supported by "fading qualia" and "dancing qualia" thought experiments
  - Critics argue these thought experiments are inconclusive
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 1 directly—but biological computationalism's rejection of organizational invariance actually provides new ammunition for the dualist critique of Chalmers' principle

### Searle — Biological Naturalism
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_naturalism
- **Type**: Encyclopedia/multiple sources
- **Key points**:
  - Consciousness is caused by neurobiological processes but ontologically irreducible to them
  - "Causally reducible" but not "ontologically reducible"
  - AI can simulate but not instantiate consciousness
  - Silicon lacks the causal capacities of neurons
- **Tenet alignment**: Partial alignment—Searle's ontological irreducibility echoes dualist commitments, though Searle himself rejects dualism. His framework is closer to the Map's position than he would admit.

## Major Positions

### Computational Functionalism (Orthodox CTM)
- **Proponents**: Hilary Putnam, Jerry Fodor, David Chalmers (re: organizational invariance)
- **Core claim**: Mental states are defined by their functional/computational roles; any system implementing the right computation is conscious
- **Key arguments**: Multiple realizability, organizational invariance, fading/dancing qualia thought experiments
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly opposes Tenet 1 (dualism) by claiming consciousness is exhausted by computation. However, CTM's formal neutrality between physicalism and dualism reveals an unacknowledged tension: if the same computation could run on soul-stuff or brain-stuff, the computation itself is not doing the explanatory work regarding consciousness.

### Biological Naturalism (Searle)
- **Proponents**: John Searle
- **Core claim**: Consciousness is caused by specific neurobiological processes; it is a higher-level biological feature causally but not ontologically reducible to lower-level processes
- **Key arguments**: Chinese Room, causal powers of neurons, simulation vs. duplication distinction
- **Relation to site tenets**: Closer to Tenet 1 than Searle admits. His "ontological irreducibility" of consciousness is structurally similar to property dualism. Searle's insistence that consciousness is not computation aligns with the Map's rejection of substrate independence.

### Biological Computationalism (Milinkovic & Aru)
- **Proponents**: Borjan Milinkovic, Jaan Aru (2025), building on Piccinini's mechanistic account
- **Core claim**: Brains compute, but biological computation is fundamentally different from digital computation—hybrid, scale-inseparable, substrate-dependent, metabolically grounded
- **Key arguments**: Continuous-valued computation in fluidic substrates, metabolic optimisation constraints, electromagnetic field interactions as computational primitives
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the most interesting position for the Map. The authors intend it as physicalist, but the framework inadvertently concedes the key dualist point: abstract functional organisation is insufficient for consciousness. By insisting consciousness requires specific physical dynamics beyond algorithmic description, they create explanatory space that dualism naturally fills. The question becomes: why does *this particular* physical organisation give rise to consciousness while a functionally equivalent digital system does not? The biological computationalist answers "because of the substrate"—but this is exactly the kind of brute physical fact that makes consciousness look like something over and above the physical.

### Mechanistic Computationalism (Piccinini)
- **Proponents**: Gualtiero Piccinini
- **Core claim**: Computation is a physical mechanism; consciousness may depend on functional organisation without being computational
- **Key arguments**: Computation as vehicle manipulation, noncomputational functionalism as possibility
- **Relation to site tenets**: Piccinini's "noncomputational functionalism" is a remarkable concession—the functional organisation that matters for consciousness might not be the computational part. This opens a door the dualist can walk through: if the consciousness-relevant organisation is not computational, what kind of organisation is it, and why should we assume it is purely physical?

## Key Debates

### Substrate Independence vs. Substrate Dependence
- **Sides**: Classical functionalists (Putnam, Fodor, Chalmers) argue consciousness is substrate-independent; biological computationalists (Milinkovic, Aru), biological naturalists (Searle), and energy-based critics (Thagard) argue it is substrate-dependent
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the physical medium of computation matters for consciousness or only the abstract functional organisation
- **Current state**: Ongoing, with momentum shifting toward substrate dependence following advances in neuroscience and failures of classical AI to produce anything resembling consciousness. The biological computationalism paper (2025) represents the latest major contribution.

### The Explanatory Gap in Biological Computationalism
- **Sides**: Biological computationalists claim their framework explains why brains are conscious and computers are not; critics (from both dualist and functionalist camps) question whether "biological computation" is doing genuine explanatory work or merely re-describing the mystery
- **Core disagreement**: Whether identifying consciousness with substrate-dependent computation explains consciousness or merely relocates the hard problem from "why does this computation produce consciousness?" to "why does this biological substrate produce consciousness?"
- **Current state**: Emerging debate. The biological computationalism framework is new enough (2025) that systematic critical responses are still developing.

### Multiple Realizability and Its Dualist Implications
- **Sides**: Putnam and functionalists use multiple realizability to argue against type-physicalism; property dualists note that if mental properties are not identical to physical properties, this supports the existence of non-physical properties
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the failure of type-identity theory supports non-reductive physicalism or property dualism
- **Current state**: Ongoing for decades. The biological computationalism movement represents a partial retreat from multiple realizability, acknowledging that not just any substrate will do—which paradoxically weakens the physicalist case while strengthening the dualist one.

## Potential Article Angles

Based on this research, an article could:

1. **"Biological Computationalism's Inadvertent Case for Dualism"** — Argue that by rejecting substrate independence while retaining computation, biological computationalism creates exactly the explanatory gap that dualism was designed to fill. The article would show how each concession biological computationalists make—substrate dependence, metabolic grounding, scale inseparability—maps onto a classical dualist argument. When they say "the algorithm is the substrate," they are effectively admitting that consciousness is not algorithmic. This aligns strongly with Tenets 1 and 2 (minimal quantum interaction could be the missing piece that makes biological substrates special).

2. **"The Convergence Problem: How All Roads Lead Away from Functionalism"** — A broader survey noting that biological naturalism, biological computationalism, mechanistic computationalism, and IIT all converge on rejecting pure functionalism, each for different reasons—but all implicitly moving toward the dualist position that something beyond functional role is needed. This could serve as a topic-level synthesis connecting existing Map content on functionalism, substrate independence, and the hard problem.

3. **"Energy, Metabolism, and the Hard Problem"** — Focus specifically on Thagard's energy argument and Milinkovic/Aru's metabolic grounding. The dualist angle: if consciousness requires specific thermodynamic and energetic conditions, this is either a brute physical fact (which makes consciousness look like strong emergence) or evidence that consciousness involves an interface with the physical that is sensitive to energetic conditions (which is exactly what Tenet 2, minimal quantum interaction, predicts).

**Recommended angle**: Option 1 is the most distinctive and directly serves the Map's thesis. The existing substrate-independence and functionalism articles provide a strong foundation, and this research adds the novel angle of how the latest physicalist theories are inadvertently building the dualist case.
