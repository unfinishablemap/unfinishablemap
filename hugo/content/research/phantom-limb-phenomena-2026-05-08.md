## Executive Summary

Phantom limb phenomena are first-person experiences of body parts that are physically absent — typically after amputation, but also in congenital aplasia and after spinal cord transection. Prevalence is high (≈60–80% phantom sensation; phantom pain in a similar fraction of acquired cases). The dominant neuroscientific account is multilevel: peripheral neuromas and ectopic firing, spinal central sensitisation, thalamic and cortical changes, with cortical reorganisation in S1 long taken as the core mechanism for phantom *pain* (Flor et al. 1995). That picture has been contested since 2013 by Tamar Makin's group, whose data suggest *preserved* rather than reorganised hand representation correlates with chronic phantom pain — a still-unresolved empirical reversal. Mirror therapy (Ramachandran and Rogers-Ramachandran 1996) demonstrated dramatic visual modulation of phantom phenomenology, but recent placebo-controlled meta-analyses are mixed-to-negative on its clinical efficacy. Congenital phantoms (Brugger et al. 2000; Melzack 1997) — phantoms in people who never had the limb — provide the theoretically loaded data: roughly 20% of those born missing a limb and ~50% of those amputated under age 6 report phantoms, suggesting an at least partly hard-wired body schema. The philosophical literature (Gallagher 2005; de Vignemont 2010, 2017) distinguishes body schema from body image and treats phantom limbs as critical evidence about which representation is doing what.

## Key Sources

### Ramachandran and Hirstein (1998) — "The perception of phantom limbs. The D. O. Hebb lecture"
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/brain/article/121/9/1603/283360
- **Type**: Invited review article (Brain, 121:1603–1630)
- **Key points**:
  - Comprehensive synthesis of clinical phenomenology and primate cortical reorganisation
  - Hand amputation in primates increases face-responsive neurons in the deprived hand cortical region
  - Topographically precise referred sensations: stroking specific cheek points evokes touch on specific phantom fingers
  - Use of MEG and fMRI to track cortical topography in individual patients
  - Discusses telescoping, painful frozen postures, and phantom intentional movement
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral on its own; the mechanism story is materialist; the explanatory gap survives. Useful for the Map's "production conditions vs. felt content" distinction.

### Flor, Elbert, Knecht, Wienbruch, Pantev, Birbaumer, Larbig, Taub (1995) — "Phantom-limb pain as a perceptual correlate of cortical reorganization"
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/375482a0
- **Type**: Original empirical paper (Nature, 375:482–484)
- **Key points**:
  - MEG study of unilateral upper-limb amputees
  - Reported a very strong correlation (r = 0.93) between magnitude of cortical reorganisation in S1 and intensity of phantom limb pain
  - No comparable correlation with non-painful phantom phenomena
  - Established the "maladaptive plasticity" model that dominated the field for ~20 years
- **Tenet alignment**: Provides the canonical materialist mechanism for phantom pain; the Map can accept this as the production-conditions story while resisting reduction.

### Makin et al. (2013, 2015) — Preserved hand representation challenge
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms2571 (2013); https://academic.oup.com/brain/article/138/8/2140/331219 (2015)
- **Type**: Original empirical papers (Nature Communications; Brain)
- **Key points**:
  - Small but consistent shifts in lip representation toward — but **not invading** — the former hand area
  - Found *no* statistical relationship between cortical reorganisation and phantom pain
  - Inverse pattern: chronic phantom pain associated with **preserved** structure and function in the former hand area
  - Reframes maladaptive plasticity: pain may *drive* preservation rather than reorganisation driving pain
  - 2020 review (Makin and Flor): "Brain (re)organisation following amputation"
- **Tenet alignment**: Important for `[[evidential-status-discipline]]` — the Flor 1995 finding is no longer the consensus. Any article that asserts "remapping → pain" without qualification is using outdated empirical claims.
- **Quote**: "Amputees who experience ample phantom pain have stronger rather than weaker cortical representations of the missing body part."

### Ramachandran and Rogers-Ramachandran (1996) — "Synaesthesia in phantom limbs induced with mirrors"
- **URL**: https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspb.1996.0058
- **Type**: Original empirical paper (Proceedings of the Royal Society B, 263:377–386)
- **Key points**:
  - Inexpensive "virtual reality box" — vertical mirror placing the reflection of the intact hand at the felt position of the phantom
  - 10 patients; in 6, moving the normal hand caused felt movement of the phantom
  - 5 had painful clenching spasms of the phantom; 4 of those had spasms relieved by mirror-induced "opening"
  - In some patients touching the normal hand evoked localised touch in the phantom — sometimes only when the patient *saw* the touch (visual-tactile referral)
- **Tenet alignment**: Direct evidence of top-down (visual experience → nociceptive modulation). Aligns with bidirectional-interaction tenet on a non-decisive but suggestive reading.

### Mirror therapy systematic reviews (mixed evidence, 2021–2023)
- **URLs**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0003999321013794 (Campo-Prieto and Rodríguez-Fuentes 2022, positive); https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/ejp.2035 (Guémann 2023, placebo-controlled — null)
- **Type**: Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- **Key points**:
  - One 2022 meta-analysis: statistically significant pain reduction at 1 month
  - Guémann et al. 2023: among placebo-controlled RCTs, only 1 of 5 showed significant benefit; conclusion is no evidence of efficacy at the highest evidence tier
  - Heterogeneous protocols (session frequency, duration, comparator) drive divergent conclusions
- **Tenet alignment**: Honour `[[evidential-status-discipline]]` — the strong "mirror therapy works" claim from earlier reviews is no longer well-supported under stricter methodology. The phenomenological observation that mirror feedback alters phantom posture and pain in individual patients survives; the population-level clinical efficacy claim does not, cleanly.

### Brugger, Kollias, Müri, Crelier, Hepp-Reymond, Regard (2000) — "Beyond re-membering: Phantom sensations of congenitally absent limbs"
- **URL**: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.100510697
- **Type**: Original empirical paper (PNAS, 97:6167–6172)
- **Key points**:
  - University-educated woman with congenital absence of forearms and lower legs (aplasia)
  - Reports vivid phantoms of all four limbs, including phantom finger movement
  - During imagined phantom finger movement: bilateral activation of dorsal premotor cortex (junction of superior frontal and precentral sulci) plus mesial premotor (SMA)
  - First study to give imaging-grounded evidence rather than self-report alone
- **Tenet alignment**: Most theoretically loaded piece of empirical data for the Map's reading. Undermines the strong "body schema is built from input" position; supports interface-spec reading.
- **Quote**: "Reports of congenital phantoms have led experts to conclude that neural representations of the body are hard-wired and underwritten by a genetically encoded blueprint."

### Melzack et al. (1997) — Phantoms in early amputees and aplasia
- **URLs**: https://www.psych.mcgill.ca/perpg/fac/melzack/phantom_limbs.pdf (background); https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1691874/ (1990 Trends in Neurosciences neuromatrix paper)
- **Type**: Empirical study + theoretical proposal
- **Key points**:
  - 41 of 125 people with congenital absence or amputation before age 6 reported phantoms
  - Phantoms in 33% overall, broken down to ~20% of congenital cases and ~50% of those amputated very young
  - Proposed *neuromatrix* theory: distributed brain network with genetically determined substrate, modified by experience, generating the body-self
- **Tenet alignment**: Same direction as Brugger — supports interface-spec reading. Neuromatrix is a materialist mechanism but its commitments (innate body schema; pain as output of a network rather than input from periphery) are friendly to the Map.

### Mitchell (1872) — *Injuries of Nerves and Their Consequences*
- **URL**: https://www.med.upenn.edu/neurology/silas-weir-mitchell.html (background); historical primary source
- **Type**: Foundational clinical treatise
- **Key points**:
  - Silas Weir Mitchell, head of Turner's Lane Hospital (Philadelphia) during the U.S. Civil War
  - Coined the term "phantom limb"
  - Documented vivid felt sensations and pain in amputated extremities of Civil War veterans
  - Also first to describe causalgia (peripheral nerve burning pain)
  - Historically important: established phantom limbs as real clinical phenomena rather than malingering or madness
- **Tenet alignment**: Background. Useful for opening hooks.

### Halligan, Marshall, Wade (1993, 1995) — Supernumerary phantom limbs after stroke
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7673976/ (1995 JNNP); also Halligan/Marshall earlier case reports
- **Type**: Case reports
- **Key points**:
  - Patient with right-hemisphere stroke reports a felt third arm in addition to two intact biological arms
  - Halligan and Marshall associated SPL with damage to basal ganglia and thalamus in some cases; parietal damage in others
  - Cognition and psychology otherwise normal — argues against psychiatric/confabulatory explanation
  - Distinguishes from "phantom" in amputation: here the limb never disappeared physically, but the patient feels an *additional* one
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports the body-as-construction reading; the brain can generate a phenomenally felt limb from scratch.

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Bodily Awareness" (de Vignemont, multiple editions)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/bodily-awareness/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia entry
- **Key points**:
  - Phantom limbs are one of the clearest expressions of the existence of body representations
  - Patients feel the limb because the missing limb is still represented neurally
  - Distinguishes proprioception (muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs, joint receptors) from broader bodily awareness
  - If long-term body image represents a part that no longer exists, one can have bodily experiences in the phantom
- **Tenet alignment**: Mainstream philosophical framing; useful for showing the Map is engaging with the standard literature, not bypassing it.

### Gallagher (2005) — *How the Body Shapes the Mind*
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/GALHTB-4
- **Type**: Monograph (Oxford University Press)
- **Key points**:
  - Body schema = pre-reflective sensorimotor capacities guiding action (no perceptual monitoring required)
  - Body image = perceptions, attitudes, beliefs about one's own body
  - Phantom limbs and aplasia are repeatedly used as wedge cases
  - Argues the body schema can persist without the body part
- **Tenet alignment**: Phenomenological tradition compatible with the Map's interface reading. Schema/image distinction is essential vocabulary.

### de Vignemont (multiple) — Body schema and body image
- **URLs**: https://philpapers.org/rec/DEVBSA (2010 *Neuropsychologia* "Body schema and body image — pros and cons"); https://philarchive.org/rec/PITBDB (Pitron and de Vignemont 2017 "Beyond differences" — co-construction model)
- **Type**: Philosophical papers
- **Key points**:
  - Defends the schema/image distinction with refinements
  - Sense of ownership comes from localising bodily sensation on the body schema map
  - 2017 co-construction model: schema and image are distinct but mutually influential
  - Phantom limbs analysed as cases where offline body representations operate disconnected from input
- **Tenet alignment**: Mainstream cognitive-philosophy framing.

### Hohwy and Seth — Predictive processing accounts of bodily self
- **URLs**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3940887/ (Seth and Critchley); https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23663284/ (Seth interoceptive inference)
- **Type**: Theoretical/review articles
- **Key points**:
  - Predictive processing (PP): the brain runs generative models predicting interoceptive, proprioceptive, exteroceptive signals; experience is the result of inference, not raw input
  - Seth: interoceptive inference grounds the embodied self
  - Selfhood and emotion as predictions about causes of internal signals
  - Phantom limbs become a case of "internal model uncoupled from input"
- **Tenet alignment**: PP is the strongest contemporary materialist competitor to the Map's interface reading. The honest engagement: PP can absorb phantoms and aplasia phantoms via priors and generative models; it does not therefore close the explanatory gap. PP gives the *form* of the production conditions but does not bridge to *felt* content.

### Flor, Nikolajsen, Jensen (2006) — "Phantom limb pain: A case of maladaptive CNS plasticity?"
- **URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn1991
- **Type**: Review article (Nature Reviews Neuroscience)
- **Key points**:
  - Multilevel reorganisation: peripheral, spinal, thalamic, cortical
  - Cortical level dominates conscious phenomenology and affective dimension
  - Pre-amputation pain is a major risk factor (pain memory)
  - Perioperative epidural analgesia reduces severe phantom pain (NNT ≈ 5.8 in some meta-analyses)
- **Tenet alignment**: Standard mechanistic synthesis as of 2006; later challenged by Makin.

### Sherman et al. (1980); Jensen et al. (1985) — Prevalence
- **Key points**:
  - Sherman 1980: surveyed 68 treatment methods; few moderately successful; documented chronic-pain landscape
  - Jensen 1985: 58 amputees; phantom pain at 72% (1 week), 65% (6 months), 59% (2 years)
  - Pre-amputation pain strongly predicts post-amputation phantom pain
  - Older estimates of ~2% reflected stigma/under-reporting

### Telescoping
- **URL**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pain-research/articles/10.3389/fpain.2026.1755884/full (recent Frontiers paper)
- **Key points**:
  - Telescoping ≈ 50% of amputees; usually within first weeks
  - Distal portion (hand, foot) is more strongly cortically represented and "survives"; proximal sensation fades
  - Three sensation types: kinetic (movement), kinesthetic (size/shape/position), exteroceptive (touch on the phantom)
  - Telescoping correlates with *less* phantom pain — interesting inverse relationship

### Spinal cord injury phantoms
- **URLs**: https://www.nature.com/articles/sc2010143 (Kawashima et al., supernumerary phantoms in SCI); https://jamanetwork.com/journals/archneurpsyc/fullarticle/651274 (older)
- **Key points**:
  - In complete spinal cord transection, the intact-but-deafferented limbs can become "phantom" — felt presence dissociates from physical reality
  - Supernumerary phantoms can appear within 6–7 days of complete cervical SCI
  - Visual-tactile feedback can manage phantom features in some patients

## Major Positions

### Maladaptive Plasticity (Classical)
- **Proponents**: Flor, Birbaumer, Ramachandran (early); the textbook account through ~2012
- **Core claim**: Phantom pain is a perceptual correlate of cortical reorganisation in S1 (face/stump invading hand area)
- **Key arguments**: Strong correlation (Flor 1995, r = 0.93); referred sensations from face stimulation; reversal hypothesis behind mirror therapy
- **Relation to site tenets**: A clean materialist mechanism. The Map accepts it as one possible production-conditions story while noting that even if this story is true, it does not bridge to felt content. Note: empirically contested since 2013 — tier this as historically dominant rather than current consensus.

### Persistent Representation (Makin)
- **Proponents**: Tamar Makin, Sanne Kikkert, and collaborators
- **Core claim**: Chronic phantom pain is associated with *preserved* cortical hand representation, not reorganisation
- **Key arguments**: 7T fMRI showing maintained somatotopy in the former hand area in pain patients; failure to find statistical relationship between remapping magnitude and pain intensity in larger samples
- **Relation to site tenets**: Friendly to interface-spec reading. If the cortical machinery for the hand is *maintained* without input, that's exactly what we'd expect on a "schema is structurally specified, not bottom-up constructed" view. Crucially: the empirical reversal does not settle the philosophical question, but it raises the cost of the hard-line "we have explained phantoms via remapping" claim.

### Neuromatrix (Melzack)
- **Proponents**: Ronald Melzack
- **Core claim**: A widely distributed, genetically substrate-determined neural network generates the body-self and pain. Sensory input modifies but does not construct it.
- **Key arguments**: Aplasia phantoms; phantoms in early childhood amputees; phantom pain without peripheral signal
- **Relation to site tenets**: Materialist in commitment but heavily compatible with the Map's interface reading. Reframes pain as output of the network, not read-out of nociception — converging with the Map's `[[pain-consciousness-and-causal-power]]` treatment.

### Predictive Processing / Interoceptive Inference
- **Proponents**: Anil Seth, Jakob Hohwy, Andy Clark
- **Core claim**: Phantom limbs are cases of generative models running with priors that no longer match reality (no input to update from). The felt body is what the model predicts when input is absent or sparse.
- **Key arguments**: Computational tractability; integration with rubber-hand-style ownership phenomena; explanation of telescoping as updated priors
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map's strongest interlocutor. PP claims to *explain* phantoms in computational terms. Honest position: PP gives a powerful account of the *form* and *dynamics* of phantom representation but inherits the explanatory gap on phenomenal content. Engage on its own terms via `[[direct-refutation-discipline]]`.

### Phenomenological / Embodied (Gallagher, Merleau-Ponty descendants, de Vignemont)
- **Proponents**: Shaun Gallagher; Frédérique de Vignemont; phenomenological tradition
- **Core claim**: Body schema is pre-reflective sensorimotor capacity; body image is conscious-perceptual. Phantoms reveal that the schema can persist without the body part. The phenomenology cannot be reduced to either input or computation alone.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible. Provides crucial vocabulary (schema/image) the Map should adopt rather than reinvent. The interface-spec reading is a friendly extension of "schema persists without limb."

## Key Debates

### Maladaptive Plasticity vs. Persistent Representation
- **Sides**: Flor/Ramachandran-tradition (remapping causes pain) vs. Makin (preserved representation correlates with pain)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the cortical correlate of chronic phantom pain is *reorganisation* (face/stump invading hand area) or *preservation* (former hand area maintains its function)
- **Current state**: Unresolved as of 2024. Newer methodologies (7T fMRI, large samples) tend to support Makin's reversal; older MEG correlations (Flor 1995) remain influential. Consensus reviews (e.g., Makin and Flor 2020) treat the issue as open.
- **For the Map**: This is precisely the kind of empirical contestation that `[[evidential-status-discipline]]` requires us to flag. Asserting "remapping causes phantom pain" as a settled fact would be over-confident.

### Mirror Therapy: Effective or Not?
- **Sides**: Single-trial / case-series enthusiasm (Ramachandran 1996; Chan et al. 2007 NEJM; some 2022 meta-analyses) vs. placebo-controlled scepticism (Guémann 2023)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the mirror provides a clinically meaningful treatment effect over placebo
- **Current state**: At the highest evidence tier, the data are weak. The phenomenological claim — visual feedback alters phantom posture and pain in individual patients — survives. The clinical-efficacy claim does not, cleanly.
- **For the Map**: The philosophical point about top-down causation does not depend on population-level clinical efficacy. Even if mirror therapy fails RCTs, the phenomenological observation in well-documented cases is intact. But the Map should not lean on "mirror therapy is an established treatment" claims.

### Are Congenital Phantoms Genuine?
- **Sides**: Brugger/Melzack tradition (genuine phantoms; evidence for innate body schema) vs. sceptics (confabulation, suggestion, learning from observing other bodies)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the introspective reports correspond to genuinely felt phantom limbs, and whether the phenomenology is built from observing others rather than from any innate substrate
- **Current state**: Brugger 2000's neuroimaging gives objective correlates (premotor activation during phantom movement), shifting the burden, but the developmental learning route cannot be ruled out entirely. Imaging shows the brain treats these as limb-movements; the phenomenological question of "what they feel like" still rests largely on self-report.
- **For the Map**: Honour the empirical situation — congenital phantoms are *suggestive* evidence for innate schema, not decisive proof. The Map's interface-spec reading is best framed as the most parsimonious integration of these data, not as proven.

### Body Schema vs. Body Image
- **Sides**: Sharp-distinction (Gallagher 2005) vs. co-construction (Pitron and de Vignemont 2017)
- **Core disagreement**: How much do schema and image overlap and influence each other? Is the schema/image cut a clean joint or a useful idealisation?
- **Current state**: Co-construction model gaining traction; older sharp-cut view treated as oversimplified
- **For the Map**: Use the distinction as terminology. The Map's "interface-spec" reading is closer to schema than image, but both are involved in phantom phenomenology.

## Potential Article Angles

Recommended refinements / topics for cross-review and follow-up work:

1. **Update the cortical-reorganisation section to acknowledge Makin's challenge.** The current article presents Flor 1995 as the standard mechanism without flagging the post-2013 empirical reversal. `[[evidential-status-discipline]]` calls for this. The Map's reading actually *strengthens* under Makin: persistent hand representation without input is more consonant with interface-spec than with bottom-up construction.

2. **Soften the mirror-therapy clinical claims.** The current article cites Chan 2007 and Foell 2014 as positive evidence; at the highest evidence tier (Guémann 2023) the picture is null. The phenomenological observation in case material survives; the population-effect claim does not. A paragraph acknowledging this lifts the article above one current standard reading.

3. **Add the schema/image vocabulary explicitly.** Gallagher 2005 and de Vignemont (2010, 2017) provide the standard taxonomy. Currently the article uses "body schema" and "somatic interface" without distinguishing schema from image; the result is rigorous-feeling but actually under-specified.

4. **Engage predictive processing on its own terms.** PP is the most serious materialist alternative. Currently the article mentions PP once. A short section that grants PP its computational power and locates the disagreement at the explanatory-gap level (rather than at the mechanism level) would honour `[[direct-refutation-discipline]]`.

5. **Add Mitchell as the historical opener.** The 1872 Civil War cohort is a vivid hook and currently absent from the article.

6. **Add the supernumerary-phantom case (Halligan/Marshall 1995) and SCI phantoms.** These extend the dissociation argument: phantoms can appear *in addition to* intact limbs and after spinal-cord deafferentation of intact limbs. Both strengthen the construction-not-transmission point.

## Gaps in Research

- **Ethnographic / cross-cultural variation**: No clear sense of whether phantom phenomenology varies meaningfully across cultures or whether reporting conventions account for apparent variation
- **Aphantasia and phantom limbs**: Whether people with aphantasia (no visual mental imagery) report phantom limbs with normal vividness — would be theoretically informative; not located in this search
- **Recent (2024–2026) data on Makin–Flor reconciliation**: The field may have moved further; the most recent comprehensive reviews are 2020. A follow-up search before any major refine should check 2024–2026 literature.
- **Phantom-limb experiences in animals**: Anecdotal reports exist (cats, dogs after amputation showing referred behaviour); not located in formal literature; would inform animal-consciousness clusters
- **Hypnotic / pharmacological modulation comparisons**: Direct comparison of mirror therapy effect sizes against hypnotic analgesia and ketamine for phantom pain not located cleanly
- **Exact mechanism of telescoping reversal**: Why a phantom that telescopes is typically *less* painful is reported but the mechanism is not well understood
