## Executive Summary

This note surveys the three historical dualist theories that accept a real mind–body *distinction* but *deny direct causal interaction* between mind and body: **occasionalism** (Malebranche, with an Islamic antecedent in al-Ghazālī), **psychophysical parallelism** (Leibniz's label, with Spinoza's dual-aspect reading and later Fechner/Wundt), and **pre-established harmony** (Leibniz's own positive doctrine). All three are responses to the same pressure that afflicts Cartesian interactionism — how can an unextended mind push an extended body, and what pairs a given mind to a given body (the interaction / pairing problem)? Each buys freedom from that problem by relocating or eliminating the causal link: God is the only genuine cause (occasionalism), or the two series merely run in lockstep with no cause between them (parallelism), or God pre-programmed the correspondence at creation (pre-established harmony). The standard objections are shared: theological extravagance / God-of-the-gaps, a slide toward epiphenomenalism (mind becomes causally idle with respect to body), and violation of parsimony. **These are precisely the positions The Unfinishable Map rejects.** The Map is an *interactionist* dualism (Tenet 3, Bidirectional Interaction); its Minimal Quantum Interaction proposal is designed to secure *real* bidirectional mind-to-matter causation while avoiding both the pairing-problem pressure that motivated these theories *and* their extravagance. Epiphenomenalism, the fourth non-interactionist option, has its own article and is cross-referenced but not duplicated here.

## Key Sources

### Occasionalism (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/occasionalism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Nicolas Malebranche is the most famous occasionalist of the Western tradition; his occasionalism appears in his first major work, *The Search After Truth* (*De la recherche de la vérité*, 1674–75), with a long defense in the *Elucidations* appended to it.
  - Central thesis: "there is only one true cause because there is only one true God." Finite minds and bodies are not genuine causes of anything; they are merely the **occasions** on which God, the sole efficacious cause, acts.
  - Four argument families reconstructed by SEP: (1) **Passive Nature** — bodies are just spatial relations and cannot possess causal powers, so only God supplies motive force; (2) **No Knowledge** — genuine causation requires knowing how to produce the effect, which creatures lack; (3) **No Necessary Connection (NNC)** — Malebranche's central argument: a true cause requires a *necessary* connection between cause and effect, and only God's will is necessarily efficacious; (4) **Conservation is Continuous Creation (CCC)** — God's conserving of the world is a continuous re-creation, leaving no causal work for creatures.
  - Al-Ghazālī (c. 1055–1111) developed the NNC-style argument earlier; his critique influenced medieval Christian thinkers (e.g. Nicholas of Autrecourt) and forms an antecedent to the European occasionalist tradition.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction) — occasionalism denies *any* creaturely mind-to-body causation. A foil for the Map.
- **Quote (flagged — reproduced via SEP, not verified against the print edition)**: SEP renders Malebranche's definition of a true cause as *"A true cause as I understand it is one such that the mind perceives a necessary connection between it and its effect"* (attributed to the *Search*, p. 450; standard English translation is Lennon & Olscamp, Cambridge). Treat as a reported quotation; confirm verbatim against the Lennon/Olscamp translation before quoting in an article.

### al-Ghazālī, *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* (Tahāfut al-falāsifa), Seventeenth Discussion
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/al-ghazali/ ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Incoherence_of_the_Philosophers
- **Type**: Primary work (via encyclopedia / secondary summary)
- **Key points**:
  - The Seventeenth Discussion is the *locus classicus* for the occasionalist critique of causality: "the connection between what is habitually believed to be a cause and what is habitually believed to be an effect is not necessary."
  - The fire-and-cotton example: when fire contacts cotton, God (directly or through the mediation of angels) creates the burning; the fire is not the true agent.
  - Scholarly caveat (Marmura and others): the chapter's internal structure offers *more than one* solution — thoroughgoing occasionalism is one reading, but a constrained secondary-causality reading is also present. Do not present al-Ghazālī as unambiguously occasionalist.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 3. Historical antecedent showing the "God is the only cause" move predates Malebranche by ~six centuries.
- **Quote (flagged — reproduced via secondary source; Marmura translation)**: the fire/cotton passage is often quoted as God being "the one who enacts the burning by creating blackness in the cotton … and making it cinder or ashes." Treat as reported; verify against Michael Marmura's translation (Brigham Young University Press) before quoting.

### Leibniz's Philosophy of Mind (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/leibniz-mind/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - **Pre-established harmony (PEH)**: "there is no mind-body interaction strictly speaking, but only a non-causal relationship of harmony, parallelism, or correspondence between mind and body." God programs each substance at creation so that "no state of a created substance has as a real cause some state of another created substance."
  - Important correction: the popular gloss ("Leibniz is a non-interactionist Cartesian dualist") is *wrong*. Leibniz rejected substance dualism itself; PEH holds among *all* substances (monads), not just between one mind and one body. His metaphysics is a monadology, not a two-substance dualism. **The article should state this carefully** — PEH is a non-interactionist *solution* borrowed by the dualist tradition, but Leibniz's own ontology is not Cartesian dualism.
  - Efficient vs. final causes: bodies operate by the laws of *efficient* (mechanical) causation; souls/minds by the laws of *final* causation (pursuit of the apparent good). The two realms never clash because their harmony is pre-established — "the kingdom of efficient causes and that of final causes are parallel to each other."
  - Key works: *Discourse on Metaphysics* (1686), *New System of Nature* (1695), *Principles of Nature and Grace* (1714), *Monadology* (1714).
  - SEP notes Leibniz's *arguments* for denying inter-substantial causation are contested: the complete-concept theory does not by itself rule out interaction, and the "monads have no windows" thesis awkwardly seems to exclude even God's causal action.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 3 (no creaturely mind→body causation) and is in tension with Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction posits a real, if minimal, causal channel that PEH denies outright). A primary foil.

### Psychophysical parallelism (Britannica / Wikipedia / secondary)
- **URL**: https://www.britannica.com/science/psychophysical-parallelism ; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychophysical_parallelism
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Parallelism: mental and physical events form two perfectly correlated series with no causal interaction; the stock image is two synchronized clocks keeping perfect time.
  - Three metaphysical homes for the parallel: (a) **Leibniz** grounds the correlation in God's pre-established programming (PEH is one species of parallelism); (b) **Spinoza's** double-aspect monism reads thought and extension as two attributes of one substance (God/Nature), each mode of one having an identical expression in the other — parallel *because* they are one thing under two descriptions, not two things synchronized; (c) **Fechner** (and later Wundt in experimental psychology) advances an empirical/psychophysical parallelism that rejects theological grounding: mental and physical states are not causally dependent but functionally correlated.
  - Terminological care needed: "parallelism" covers both the *dualist* (two-series, Leibnizian) and the *monist / dual-aspect* (Spinozist) readings. The Map's own [bi-aspectual-ontology](/concepts/bi-aspectual-ontology/) uses dual-aspect vocabulary but, crucially, affirms interaction between the aspects — so it must be distinguished sharply from Spinozist parallelism, which denies it.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 3. The Spinozist dual-aspect version is the most important to distinguish because it shares vocabulary with the Map's bi-aspectual ontology while denying causation.

### Leibniz's two-clocks passage (Third Explanation of the New System, 1696)
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/leibniz/ (context); primary source is Leibniz's letter/explanation to Basnage de Beauval, 1696
- **Type**: Primary work (via secondary compilation)
- **Key points**:
  - Leibniz's canonical illustration: two clocks in perfect agreement can be explained three ways — (1) **mutual natural influence** (one drives the other; = interactionism / "the way of influence"); (2) **a skilled craftsman continually adjusting them** (= occasionalism / "the way of assistance," which Leibniz mocks as requiring God's perpetual intervention, a *Deus ex machina*); (3) **initial perfect construction** so each runs by its own laws yet stays in agreement (= pre-established harmony, "the way of pre-established agreement"). Leibniz argues (3) is the only one worthy of God because it needs neither ongoing miracle nor unexplained influence.
  - **Verbatim status FLAGGED**: the Yale primary-passage PDF would not extract cleanly, so the exact wording above is *paraphrased*, not quoted. Do not present the three-ways passage as a verbatim quotation in the article without first confirming against a print translation (Ariew & Garber, *Leibniz: Philosophical Essays*, Hackett 1989, or Loemker). The three-way structure itself is well-attested across sources.

## Major Positions

### Occasionalism
- **Proponents**: Nicolas Malebranche (canonical); Louis de la Forge, Géraud de Cordemoy, Arnold Geulincx (Cartesian occasionalists); al-Ghazālī and the Ashʿarite kalām tradition (Islamic antecedent).
- **Core claim**: Finite minds and bodies never genuinely cause anything. Mental and physical events are the *occasions* on which God — the one true, necessarily efficacious cause — produces the corresponding effect. When my willing raises my arm, God raises the arm on the occasion of my willing.
- **Key arguments**: No Necessary Connection (only God's will is necessarily connected to its effect); Passive Nature of bodies; No Knowledge (creatures don't know how to produce their effects); Conservation as Continuous Creation.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct denial of Tenet 3. The Map keeps occasionalism's honest recognition that Cartesian interaction is problematic, but rejects the cure: instead of removing creaturely causation, the Map *locates* a minimal, physically-lawful causal channel at quantum indeterminacy (Tenet 2).

### Psychophysical Parallelism
- **Proponents**: Leibniz (theistic, PEH-grounded); Spinoza (dual-aspect monist reading); Gustav Fechner and Wilhelm Wundt (empirical psychophysics).
- **Core claim**: Mind and body form two perfectly correlated series with no causal traffic between them; the correlation is explained by God (Leibniz), by identity-under-two-attributes (Spinoza), or left as a functional/empirical posit (Fechner).
- **Key arguments**: Avoids the interaction problem by denying interaction; preserves the completeness/mechanism of the physical series (a selling point for later scientific psychology); Spinoza adds that one substance under two attributes *must* have parallel modal orders ("the order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things," *Ethics* IIp7 — flagged: verify wording/translation, Curley, before quoting).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 3. The Spinozist version is the sharpest foil for the Map's [bi-aspectual-ontology](/concepts/bi-aspectual-ontology/): same two-aspect vocabulary, opposite verdict on interaction.

### Pre-Established Harmony
- **Proponents**: Leibniz (sole originator).
- **Core claim**: God created each substance so that its internal states unfold by its own law yet correspond exactly to every other substance's states — the appearance of mind–body interaction is real *correspondence* without real *causation*. Two clocks synchronized at manufacture, never again adjusted.
- **Key arguments**: The two-clocks argument (PEH is the only "way" not requiring either brute influence or perpetual miracle); the completeness of each monad's concept; the parallel kingdoms of efficient and final causes.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with Tenet 3; in tension with Tenet 2 and Tenet 5. PEH is maximally *front-loaded* — all coordination is packed into the initial divine act — which the Map reads as trading an interaction problem for a far larger creation-design problem (a parsimony cost, cf. Tenet 5, Occam's Razor Has Limits, cuts both ways).

## Key Debates

### Does denying interaction really solve the mind–body problem, or just relocate the mystery?
- **Sides**: Occasionalists/PEH theorists say yes — no unextended-pushing-extended problem remains. Critics (from Leibniz against occasionalism, and moderns against both) say the coordination itself is now unexplained and must be dumped onto God.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether appealing to God's will/design *explains* the correlation or merely *names* it (the God-of-the-gaps charge).
- **Current state**: Ongoing as history-of-philosophy scholarship; as live metaphysics, all three positions are near-universally regarded as superseded, but they remain the canonical foils that define interactionism by contrast.

### Do these positions collapse into epiphenomenalism?
- **Sides**: Critics argue that if the mind never causally affects the body, mental states are causally idle with respect to behavior — the defining feature of epiphenomenalism. Defenders reply that the mind is not *inert* (it has real final-causal / internal life), it simply doesn't act *transitively* on matter.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "causally idle with respect to body" is enough to count as epiphenomenalism, or whether intra-mental causation rescues the mind's efficacy.
- **Current state**: The Map treats this as the decisive vulnerability shared with [epiphenomenalism](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) — see that article; do not duplicate its evolutionary/self-stultification arguments here, cross-reference them.

### Is Leibniz a dualist at all?
- **Sides**: The textbook tradition files PEH under "non-interactionist dualism"; Leibniz scholars insist his monadology is not substance dualism.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether PEH is a *dualist* solution or a *monist/idealist* one wearing dualist clothing.
- **Current state**: Settled among specialists (not a Cartesian dualist), routinely blurred in surveys. The article must state the correction to stay accurate.
