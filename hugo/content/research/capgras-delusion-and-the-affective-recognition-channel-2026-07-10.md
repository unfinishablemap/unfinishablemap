## Executive Summary

Capgras delusion — the belief that a familiar person (usually an emotional intimate) has been replaced by a near-identical impostor — is a clean interface-architecture case for the Map. Face/person recognition decomposes into at least two contributions: (a) perceptual identification ("this looks like my spouse") and (b) affective familiarity (the felt warmth/"mineness" of a known person). Capgras is the dissociation where perceptual ID is intact but the affective-familiarity channel is severed. The classic account (Ellis & Young 1990) frames it as the mirror image of prosopagnosia: prosopagnosia loses overt identification while covert autonomic discrimination survives; Capgras loses the autonomic/affective response while overt identification survives. Skin-conductance evidence (Hirstein & Ramachandran 1997; Ellis, Young, Quayle & De Pauw 1997) confirmed the missing autonomic signal to familiar faces. The dominant framework is now the two-factor model (Coltheart, Langdon, Davies, McKay): Factor 1 is the anomalous affective datum; Factor 2 is a separate belief-evaluation deficit (right-frontal) that lets the "impostor" hypothesis stick. A live minority critique (Corlett 2019) challenges whether Factor 1 is really a discrete familiarity/affective deficit at all, which is directly relevant to Tenet 5.

## Key Sources

### Ellis, H. D. & Young, A. W. (1990). Accounting for delusional misidentifications.
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2224375/
- **Citation (verified at PubMed)**: British Journal of Psychiatry, 157(2), 239-248. DOI 10.1192/bjp.157.2.239. PMID 2224375.
- **Type**: Paper (theoretical / model)
- **Key points**:
  - Applies functional face-recognition models to the delusional misidentification syndromes.
  - Proposes Capgras involves impaired "covert"/affective recognition — a mirror image of prosopagnosia (which loses overt identification but retains covert autonomic discrimination).
  - Predicts (before it was tested) that Capgras patients will be hyporesponsive to familiar faces on autonomic measures.
  - The anomalous experience ("known people feel unfamiliar") is the seed of the impostor belief.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-supportive of Tenet 1 (Dualism) as interface evidence; the functional decomposition itself is metaphysically neutral (physicalist functional models explain the same data).
- **Note**: A third-party reference aggregator (SciRP) lists a garbled page range ("113, 1721-1777") — that is an error. PubMed page range 239-248 is authoritative.

### Hirstein, W. & Ramachandran, V. S. (1997). Capgras syndrome: a novel probe for understanding the neural representation of the identity and familiarity of persons.
- **URL**: https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspb.1997.0062 (also https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9107057/)
- **Citation (verified)**: Proceedings of the Royal Society of London B: Biological Sciences, 264(1380), 437-444. DOI 10.1098/rspb.1997.0062. Published 22 March 1997.
- **Type**: Paper (single-case + theory)
- **Key points**:
  - Single patient (DS): skin-conductance responses to photos of familiar people (including his parents) were NOT larger than to unfamiliar faces — the normal familiarity SCR boost was absent.
  - The delusion was modality-specific: he called his parents impostors when looking at them but not when speaking on the telephone — pointing to a face-route-specific (visual→limbic) disconnection rather than a global belief problem.
  - Proposed a disconnection between face-processing areas (temporal/fusiform) and the limbic/amygdala system that assigns emotional significance.
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports interface-architecture reading; the felt-familiarity channel is separately damageable.
- **Quote (paraphrase of finding)**: familiar-face SCRs "were not larger in magnitude than his responses to photographs of unfamiliar people."

### Ellis, H. D., Young, A. W., Quayle, A. H. & De Pauw, K. W. (1997). Reduced autonomic responses to faces in Capgras delusion.
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9263474/ (open copy: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1688551/)
- **Citation (verified)**: Proceedings of the Royal Society of London B: Biological Sciences, 264(1384), 1085-1092. Published 22 July 1997.
- **Type**: Paper (controlled group study)
- **Key points**:
  - Tested Ellis & Young's 1990 prediction in FIVE Capgras patients, against five general-public controls and five psychiatric (matched anti-psychotic medication) controls.
  - Capgras patients were hyporesponsive to familiar faces — the predicted reduced autonomic orienting response.
  - Independently corroborates Hirstein & Ramachandran, and matters because it is a group study with a medication-matched control (rules out the "it's just the drugs" objection).
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongest empirical leg of the affective-channel decomposition.

### Coltheart, M. & Davies, M. (2022). What is Capgras delusion?
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34890309/ (PhilPapers: https://philpapers.org/rec/COLWIC-3)
- **Citation (verified via PubMed/PhilPapers)**: Cognitive Neuropsychiatry, 27(1), 69-82. DOI 10.1080/13546805.2021.2011185. PMID 34890309.
- **Type**: Paper (recent conceptual review)
- **Key points**:
  - Argues Capgras is not only about relatives/faces: replacement beliefs also target an acquaintance, a familiar voice, a pet, or a personal possession — five scenarios sharing the structure "something familiar replaced by something unfamiliar."
  - Restates the mechanism: personally familiar stimuli normally activate the sympathetic nervous system much more strongly than unfamiliar ones; in Capgras this difference is absent, prompting the impostor idea.
  - Evaluates whether the five variants are subtypes of one delusion — useful for scoping the article's definition.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; extends the affective-signal account beyond faces (good for arguing the channel is about felt-familiarity per se, not vision).

### Corlett, P. R. (2019). Factor One, Familiarity and Frontal Cortex: A challenge to the two-factor theory of delusions.
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6686846/
- **Citation (verified)**: Cognitive Neuropsychiatry, 24(3), 165-177.
- **Type**: Paper (critique)
- **Key points**:
  - Re-examines the four vmPFC cases used to support a discrete Factor 1; finds two (EVR-318, HS-1065) also had right dorsolateral prefrontal damage — the region theorists assign to Factor 2. So the "two independent factors" may not be cleanly dissociable in the anatomy.
  - Argues the patients' absent SCRs are not specific to familiar faces but extend to "any psychologically salient visual stimuli" — i.e. the deficit may be a broad salience/arousal problem, not a dedicated familiarity channel.
  - Implication: the clean "affective-familiarity channel selectively lesioned" story is contested.
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly relevant to Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits) — a cautionary datum against over-reading a clean channel decomposition; the article MUST engage it honestly rather than presenting the affective-channel story as settled.

## Major Positions

### Affective-Route (Disconnection) Account — Ellis & Young
- **Proponents**: Hadyn Ellis, Andrew Young; corroborated by Hirstein & Ramachandran.
- **Core claim**: Capgras results from a disconnection between intact face-perception and the limbic system that tags a face with emotional/personal significance. Overt identification survives; the affective familiarity signal is lost. The absence of the expected warmth generates the anomalous experience "this looks like X but doesn't feel like X."
- **Key arguments**: mirror-image relationship to prosopagnosia; reduced/absent familiar-face SCRs; modality-specificity (face vs. voice).
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the central interface-architecture datum. It shows that "recognising a loved one" is a compound in which one conscious contribution — felt familiarity — is separable from perceptual identification. Supports Tenet 1's interface-architecture reading: a specific conscious channel can be selectively disconnected while the rest of recognition runs. (Metaphysically neutral on its own — see Corlett and the physicalist reading.)

### Two-Factor Model — Coltheart, Langdon, Davies, McKay
- **Proponents**: Max Coltheart, Robyn Langdon, Martin Davies, Ryan McKay.
- **Core claim**: The affective deficit alone (Factor 1) cannot be the whole story, because it would only produce an odd feeling, not an irrational fixed belief that survives counter-evidence. A SECOND deficit (Factor 2) — impaired belief evaluation, associated with right frontal (rDLPFC) damage — is needed to explain why the impostor hypothesis is adopted and maintained. Factor 1 supplies the anomalous data; Factor 2 fails to reject the crazy explanation of it.
- **Key arguments**: patients with the same affective deficit but intact belief-evaluation (e.g. some with ventromedial damage who feel the strangeness but don't form the delusion) vs. Capgras patients who do; the delusion's resistance to disconfirmation implicates a reasoning/evaluation stage, not just an experiential one.
- **Internal variants**: Coltheart's model has Factor 2 operating at belief-evaluation (accept/reject a formed hypothesis); McKay's variant has Factor 2 bias operating in the transition from experience to belief (a bias toward explanatory adequacy that over-weights fit to the anomalous data). Worth noting the distinction but not over-detailing it.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Important for calibration. The two-factor model shows the anomalous affective datum is necessary but not sufficient for the delusion — so the article should NOT claim Capgras proves the affective channel is conscious/independent all by itself. The channel decomposition is the Factor-1 story; the belief pathology is a separate matter. This keeps the Map's claim narrow and defensible.

### Pure Top-Down / Prediction-Error Account (contrast)
- **Proponents**: Broadly, predictive-processing theorists; Corlett's critique lives adjacent to this.
- **Core claim**: The delusion may not require a dedicated, dissociable affective-familiarity module. Aberrant salience / prediction-error signalling, or a broad arousal deficit, plus reasoning failure, could generate the same picture without a clean "familiarity channel" being lesioned.
- **Key arguments**: Corlett 2019 — absent SCRs generalise beyond familiar faces to any salient visual stimulus; the anatomical "Factor 1" cases are contaminated with Factor-2 (frontal) damage.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the account the article must engage to satisfy Tenet 5. It does NOT refute the affective-route reading, but it shows the channel-decomposition inference is underdetermined — exactly the kind of "clean dissociation might just be what damageable functional sub-systems look like" caveat the recognition-void article already flags.

## Key Debates

### Is Factor 1 a discrete affective-familiarity channel, or a broad salience/arousal deficit?
- **Sides**: Ellis/Young/Coltheart (specific affective route) vs. Corlett (non-specific salience/frontal contamination).
- **Core disagreement**: whether the missing SCR is selective to personally-familiar stimuli (→ dedicated channel) or a general drop in autonomic responsiveness to any salient stimulus (→ no dedicated channel).
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The group study (Ellis et al. 1997) supports specificity for faces; Corlett questions the anatomical dissociation. The honest article position: the affective-familiarity contribution is real and separable in the classic data, but whether it is a metaphysically discrete "channel" or one physical sub-system among many is underdetermined.

### One factor or two?
- **Sides**: One-factor (the anomalous experience is enough, given normal reasoning under an extraordinary datum) vs. two-factor (Coltheart et al.: a separate belief-evaluation deficit is required).
- **Core disagreement**: whether an odd feeling can, by itself, produce a fixed delusional belief in an otherwise-intact reasoner.
- **Current state**: Two-factor is the dominant framework in cognitive neuropsychiatry; one-factor accounts persist. For the Map, only Factor 1 matters for the channel-decomposition claim; Factor 2 is why it becomes a delusion rather than a mere strange feeling.
