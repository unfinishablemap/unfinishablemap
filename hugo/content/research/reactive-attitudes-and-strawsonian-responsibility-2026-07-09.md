## CRITICAL DISAMBIGUATION (read first — carry into the downstream article)

This note is about **P. F. Strawson** — Sir **Peter Frederick Strawson** (1919–2006), Oxford analytic philosopher, author of "Freedom and Resentment" (1962). His view is a **compatibilist** account of moral responsibility that dissolves the free-will problem.

He is **NOT** **Galen Strawson** (b. 1952, P. F. Strawson's son), whose "Basic Argument" (see "The Impossibility of Moral Responsibility", 1994) concludes that ultimate moral responsibility is impossible whether or not determinism is true — a **free-will pessimist / impossibilist** position, the near-opposite of his father's optimism. The two men reach opposite conclusions about responsibility. Any downstream article, wikilink, or citation must keep them rigorously distinct. When the surname "Strawson" appears unqualified in the free-will literature in the context of reactive attitudes / participant stance, it is P. F.; in the context of the Basic Argument / causa sui, it is Galen. **The Map already has an incompatibilism cluster that references the reactive-attitudes tradition — this note supplies the P. F. Strawson primary source those articles gesture at.**

## Executive Summary

P. F. Strawson's "Freedom and Resentment" (1962) attempts to **dissolve** rather than answer the free-will problem. His move: moral responsibility is not grounded in a prior metaphysics of libertarian free will; it is **constituted by** our susceptibility to the "reactive attitudes" — resentment, indignation, gratitude, hurt feelings, guilt, love — which are the natural human responses to the quality of will (goodwill, ill will, indifference) that others display toward us and others. Because these attitudes are woven into interpersonal life so deeply that we could not, and would not, abandon them wholesale, the theoretical question "does the truth of determinism undermine responsibility?" is, Strawson argues, idle: no general theoretical thesis could rationally dislodge a framework we are practically committed to. The essay founded a major compatibilist tradition (Wallace, Watson, McKenna, Russell, Fischer & Ravizza in part) but also drew a durable objection — the **naturalism-vs-normativity** worry: does the account merely *describe* our blaming practices, or does it *justify* them? For the Map this is a full-strength compatibilist rival to its libertarianism: it is a genuine dissolution of the problem, not a strawman, and the Map's disagreement with it is framework-relative, not itself evidence for dualism.

## Key Sources

### "Freedom and Resentment" (P. F. Strawson, 1962) — the primary source
- **Publication of record**: P. F. Strawson, "Freedom and Resentment", *Proceedings of the British Academy*, vol. 48 (1962), pp. 1–25. Widely reprinted (e.g. in Strawson, *Freedom and Resentment and Other Essays*, Methuen 1974 / Routledge reissue 2008; and in Watson ed., *Free Will*, Oxford Readings in Philosophy). **No DOI** — a 1962 British Academy lecture; do not invent one.
- **Type**: Lecture / journal essay (the British Academy's annual philosophical lecture).
- **Key points**:
  - Frames the dispute between the "optimist" (compatibilist: responsibility survives determinism) and the "pessimist" (incompatibilist/libertarian: responsibility requires a freedom determinism would rule out) — and proposes to reconcile them by shifting the ground.
  - The reactive attitudes are natural, non-optional emotional responses to the **quality of will** others bear toward us: we resent injury done with ill will or indifference, feel gratitude for goodwill.
  - Two families of pleas that modify or suspend these attitudes: (1) **excuses** ("he didn't mean to", "he was pushed") — the act did not, after all, express ill will, so the attitude is inapt but the agent stays inside the moral community; (2) **exemptions** ("he's a child", "he's psychologically incapacitated", "he was under extreme stress") — we adopt the **objective attitude**, viewing the person as something to be managed, treated, or handled rather than reasoned with as a participant.
  - The objective stance can be adopted locally and temporarily, but it **cannot** be adopted universally and permanently as a policy: a wholesale, permanent objectivity toward all persons is not something we could sustain, given "the very great importance that we attach to the attitudes and intentions towards us of other human beings".
  - Therefore the abstract thesis of determinism, even if true, gives us no reason — and no psychological ability — to abandon the reactive framework as such. The question of whether determinism "really" undermines responsibility is idle.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with the Map's Tenet 1 (dualism) and its broader libertarian commitment — Strawson makes the metaphysics of free will *irrelevant* to responsibility, whereas the Map holds the metaphysical question is real and answers it in a libertarian, dualist direction. **Neutral** on Tenets 2–4 (quantum interaction, bidirectionality) which it simply never engages. Note for downstream: the conflict is a live philosophical rivalry, not a defeat of either side.
- **Quote (widely quoted formulation)**: Strawson emphasises "the very great importance that we attach to the attitudes and intentions towards us of other human beings" as the root of the reactive attitudes. (Verify exact wording against the reprinted text when quoting in the article.)

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Peter Frederick Strawson"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/strawson/

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Compatibilism"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/compatibilism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia.
- **Key points**: Credits P. F. Strawson (1962) with introducing "reactive attitude" and "objective attitude" to the free-will lexicon; describes the reactive attitudes as "the constitutive basis of our moral responsibility practices". Situates Strawson within the compatibilist family and against classical (conditional-analysis) compatibilism.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral / descriptive.

### R. Jay Wallace, *Responsibility and the Moral Sentiments* (1994)
- **Publication of record**: R. Jay Wallace, *Responsibility and the Moral Sentiments*, Harvard University Press, 1994 (paperback 1998), ISBN via Harvard University Press (https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674766235). **Book — no DOI.**
- **Type**: Monograph.
- **Key points**:
  - Develops a **normative** version of the Strawsonian view: the question "under what conditions is an agent morally responsible?" is equated with "under what conditions is it *appropriate / fair* to hold her responsible?"
  - Holding responsible does not depend on libertarian metaphysics; our practices of blame, indignation, and resentment "make perfectly good sense even if determinism is true".
  - Crucially **normativizes** Strawson: where Strawson can be read as saying the attitudes are simply a natural fact we cannot give up, Wallace insists there are *standards of fairness* governing when the attitudes are warranted (tied to the agent's possession of general powers of reflective self-control / reasons-responsiveness). This is the standard "repair" of Strawson against the charge that raw naturalism can't distinguish apt from inapt blame.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with the Map's libertarianism (compatibilist), but is important precisely because it supplies the *normative* resources Strawson's bare naturalism lacked — the strongest form of the rival for the downstream article to engage.

### Gary Watson, "Responsibility and the Limits of Evil: Variations on a Strawsonian Theme" (1987)
- **Publication of record**: Gary Watson, "Responsibility and the Limits of Evil: Variations on a Strawsonian Theme", in Ferdinand Schoeman (ed.), *Responsibility, Character, and the Emotions: New Essays in Moral Psychology*, Cambridge University Press, 1987, pp. 256–286. Reprinted in Watson, *Agency and Answerability*, Oxford University Press, 2004. **Chapter — no DOI.** (Note: some secondary listings misattribute the volume to Fischer & Ravizza's *Perspectives on Moral Responsibility*, where it was also reprinted; the *original* venue is the Schoeman volume. Verify before citing.)
- **Type**: Book chapter.
- **Key points**:
  - Broadly sympathetic "variation" on Strawson, but presses the **hard case of extreme evil** — Watson uses the real case of Robert Harris, a brutal murderer whose horrific childhood invites the objective/sympathetic stance even as his acts invite the fullest reactive condemnation.
  - The case exposes a tension in Strawson's framework: our reactive attitudes and our sympathetic (exempting) understanding can be **simultaneously** engaged and pull in opposite directions, and Strawson gives no clean rule for adjudicating. This threatens the claim that the reactive attitudes are a self-sufficient, self-interpreting basis for responsibility.
  - Feeds the objection that Strawson cannot, without importing further (possibly metaphysical) considerations, explain *why* certain agents are exempted — leaving the door open to the libertarian rejoinder that what exempts is an incapacity to do otherwise.
- **Tenet alignment**: Internal-to-compatibilism critique; **useful to the Map** as an *internal* pressure point on the Strawsonian dissolution (the rival is contested from within, not only from the libertarian outside).

### John Martin Fischer & Mark Ravizza, *Responsibility and Control* (1998)
- **Publication of record**: John Martin Fischer & Mark Ravizza, *Responsibility and Control: A Theory of Moral Responsibility*, Cambridge University Press, 1998. **Book — no DOI.**
- **Type**: Monograph.
- **Key points**: **Semicompatibilism** — grants (for argument) that determinism may be incompatible with the *freedom to do otherwise*, but argues moral responsibility requires only **moderate reasons-responsiveness** of the actual mechanism plus the agent's "taking ownership" of it (a historical condition). Their theory is Frankfurt-descended (guidance control, not regulative control) rather than purely Strawsonian, but it **borrows** the Strawsonian insight that responsibility need not wait on libertarian metaphysics, and treats Strawson's naturalism as a compelling alternative it must position against. Fischer & Ravizza also edited an influential anthology, *Perspectives on Moral Responsibility* (Cornell UP, 1993), that reprinted key Strawsonian-tradition essays.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with libertarianism; a distinct compatibilist rival. The Map's cluster already engages Fischer semicompatibilism as a genuine (undefeated) rival — the downstream article should note that the Strawsonian and reasons-responsive strands are *different* compatibilisms, not one lump.

### McKenna & Russell (eds.), *Free Will and Reactive Attitudes* (2008) and Russell's Strawsonian survey
- **URLs**: https://www.routledge.com/... (Ashgate/Routledge 2008 volume); Paul Russell, "Free Will and Moral Sentiments: Strawsonian Theories" (survey, philarchive.org/archive/RUSFWA-5).
- **Type**: Edited essay collection + survey chapter.
- **Key points**: The standard modern anthology consolidating four decades of debate. Frames the central interpretive fault line: is Strawson best read as a **naturalist** (the attitudes are a brute, unrevisable natural fact) or as offering a **normative/quasi-transcendental** argument (the attitudes carry their own internal standards of fitness)? Russell distinguishes Strawson's naturalistic and transcendental tendencies and argues a fully Strawsonian reading must honour both. This is the primary secondary literature for the "naturalism vs normativity" section.
- **Tenet alignment**: Descriptive scholarship; neutral. Essential for presenting the rival at full strength.

## Major Positions

### The Strawsonian (reactive-attitudes) account of responsibility
- **Proponents**: P. F. Strawson (1962); developed by R. Jay Wallace, Gary Watson, Michael McKenna, Paul Russell, R. Jay Wallace; partly echoed by Fischer & Ravizza.
- **Core claim**: To be morally responsible just *is* to be an apt target of the reactive attitudes. Responsibility is **constituted by** the practice of holding-responsible (the participant stance), not read off a prior metaphysical fact about free will.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. **The constitutive move**: reactive attitudes are the natural response to quality of will; the class of responsible agents is the class toward whom these attitudes are apt.
  2. **The inescapability / practical-commitment argument**: we cannot and would not adopt the objective attitude universally and permanently; the reactive framework is a condition of human relationship, not an optional theory.
  3. **The idleness argument**: since no abstract theoretical thesis (determinism) could rationally or psychologically dislodge that framework, the "does determinism threaten responsibility?" question is idle — dissolved, not answered.
- **Relation to Map tenets**: A **compatibilist rival** to the Map's libertarianism. It does not deny dualism so much as declare the whole metaphysical dispute *beside the point* for responsibility. The Map's counter-stance (note for downstream, do not over-claim): the Map holds the metaphysical question is real and answers it libertarian-dualist; it therefore **rejects the dissolution** and reads Strawson as changing the subject rather than settling it. This is a framework-level disagreement about what responsibility *requires*, not a demonstration that dualism is true.

### The libertarian / "pessimist" rival (the Map's home camp)
- **Proponents**: classical libertarians; on the Map, the incompatibilism cluster (consequence argument, manipulation argument, source-vs-leeway incompatibilism).
- **Core claim**: Genuine responsibility requires a kind of freedom (ultimate sourcehood and/or alternative possibilities) that determinism would rule out; the metaphysics is not optional.
- **Relation to Strawson**: Strawson's "pessimist". Strawson's strategy is to deny that the pessimist's metaphysical demand is a real precondition of the practices. The libertarian rejoinder (echoed by Watson's worry): the exemption cases seem to track exactly a *capacity* condition, which looks metaphysical after all.

### Galen Strawson's impossibilism (DO NOT CONFLATE — the opposite pole)
- **Proponent**: Galen Strawson (P. F.'s son).
- **Core claim**: The **Basic Argument** — to be ultimately responsible for an action one must be responsible for how one is (mentally); that would require being *causa sui*; nothing can be causa sui; so ultimate moral responsibility is impossible, whether determinism is true or false.
- **Relation to this note**: Included **only** to firewall the disambiguation. Galen rejects responsibility outright; P. F. rescues it by relocating it in the reactive attitudes. Same surname, opposite conclusions. The downstream article should mention the distinction explicitly if it links either way.

## Key Debates

### Naturalism vs. normativity — does the account justify or merely describe?
- **Sides**: (a) *Descriptive/naturalist reading* — Strawson reports an unrevisable natural fact about human psychology; blame needs no justification because it is not the sort of thing we could give up (justification-talk is misplaced). (b) *Normative reading* (Wallace; parts of McKenna) — the attitudes carry internal standards of *fairness/aptness*; the account must (and can) say when blame is *warranted*, not just that it is *had*.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "constituted by our practices" collapses the normative question ("*ought* we hold X responsible?") into a merely factual one ("*do* we?"). Critics charge that pure naturalism cannot criticise a cruel or mistaken blaming practice from within; defenders answer that the reactive attitudes have built-in felicity conditions (quality of will, the excuse/exemption structure) that supply normativity without external metaphysics.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and central. The dominant modern Strawsonianism (Wallace) is **normativized** precisely to answer this. The Map's most incisive engagement lives here: it can grant the descriptive power of the account while pressing that the normative "aptness" conditions (competent agency, responsiveness, sourcehood) smuggle back in exactly the metaphysical questions Strawson claimed to dissolve.

### The hard cases of evil and exemption (Watson)
- **Sides**: Strawson (excuse/exemption structure suffices) vs. Watson (extreme-evil cases show reactive and objective stances co-engaged with no principled adjudication).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the reactive attitudes are *self-interpreting* enough to fix the boundaries of the moral community without a further theory of agential capacity.
- **Current state**: Live; motivates capacity-based repairs (Wallace, Fischer & Ravizza) that move the tradition toward reasons-responsiveness.

### Is the objective stance really un-adoptable wholesale?
- **Sides**: Strawson (psychologically impossible to sustain universally) vs. free-will skeptics such as Derk Pereboom and the "objective attitude" critics (a thoroughly *forward-looking*, non-reactive stance is both possible and morally preferable — living without desert-based reactive attitudes).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the inescapability premise is a genuine constraint or an overstated empirical claim.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; free-will skepticism (Pereboom, Caruso) is the principal contemporary pressure on Strawson's inescapability move. **Useful to the Map**: the skeptics attack Strawson from the opposite side to the Map, showing the dissolution is contested on both flanks.
