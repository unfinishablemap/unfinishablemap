## Executive Summary

Alexithymia is a normally-distributed personality trait (elevated in ~10% of the general population) marked by difficulty **identifying** feelings, difficulty **describing** feelings, and an **externally-oriented thinking** style. Coined by Peter Sifneos in the early 1970s from Greek roots meaning "no words for emotion," it is a construct, not a DSM disorder. For the Map it is a genuine third member of the **phenomenal-divergence cluster** alongside aphantasia (absent/reduced visual imagery) and synaesthesia (anomalous cross-modal binding): a trait where introspective access to one phenomenal domain — the emotional/interoceptive — diverges systematically from the typical case. The single most Map-relevant finding is that the naive "alexithymia = an interoceptive/emotional *deficit*" story has weakened: recent meta-analytic work finds **no reliable association between alexithymia and objective interoceptive accuracy**, only a weak negative association with self-reported interoceptive *sensibility*. That relocates the divergence toward the **access/report layer** rather than the phenomenal layer — which sharpens, rather than blunts, the Map's introspection-architecture reading and its use as a common-cause-null test.

## Key Sources

### Sifneos (1973) — coinage of "alexithymia"
- **Citation**: Sifneos, P. E. (1973). "The prevalence of 'alexithymic' characteristics in psychosomatic patients." *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics*, 22(2), 255–262.
- **Type**: Foundational paper
- **Key points**:
  - Coined "alexithymia" (Greek: *a-* "without," *lexis* "words," *thymos* "emotion") for a cognitive style seen in psychosomatic patients.
  - Grew out of mid-1960s work with John Nemiah on the cognitive style of psychosomatic patients: impoverished fantasy life, utilitarian/externally-oriented thinking, marked difficulty describing subjective feelings.
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED (title/author/year; Semantic Scholar record + multiple secondary histories). Standard secondary sources sometimes cite the term's first appearance as a 1972/1973 conference/paper — the 1973 *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics* article is the canonical citation.

### Bagby, Parker & Taylor (1994) — the TAS-20
- **Citation**: Bagby, R. M., Parker, J. D. A., & Taylor, G. J. (1994). "The twenty-item Toronto Alexithymia Scale—I. Item selection and cross-validation of the factor structure." *Journal of Psychosomatic Research*, 38(1), 23–32. (Companion paper —II on convergent/discriminant/concurrent validity, same year, same authors.)
- **Type**: Psychometric instrument paper
- **Key points**:
  - The standard measure. Three factor-analytically derived subscales: **Difficulty Identifying Feelings (DIF)**, **Difficulty Describing Feelings (DDF)**, **Externally-Oriented Thinking (EOT)**.
  - Good internal consistency (α ≈ .81) and test–retest reliability (≈ .77).
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED (title/authors/year/venue/pagination; Wikipedia + PubMed 8126688 for the —II companion). The three-factor DIF/DDF/EOT structure is well-established.

### Garfinkel, Seth, Barrett, Suzuki & Critchley (2015) — three dimensions of interoception
- **Citation**: Garfinkel, S. N., Seth, A. K., Barrett, A. B., Suzuki, K., & Critchley, H. D. (2015). "Knowing your own heart: distinguishing interoceptive accuracy from interoceptive awareness." *Biological Psychology*, 104, 65–74.
- **Type**: Empirical / model paper
- **Key points**:
  - Interoception is not monolithic: **accuracy** (objective performance, e.g. heartbeat-detection), **sensibility** (self-reported dispositional interoceptive focus, questionnaire), and **awareness** (metacognitive insight — correspondence between confidence and accuracy).
  - These dimensions are dissociable, so a person can have intact accuracy but poor sensibility/awareness, or vice versa.
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED (title/authors/venue/volume/pages; PubMed 25451381, publisher PDF). Note "Barrett" here is **Adam B. Barrett** (Sussex), NOT Lisa Feldman Barrett — a name-collision worth guarding against when the article also cites the constructed-emotion theory below.
- **Cross-reference**: This is the same accuracy/sensibility/awareness partition already cited in `concepts/somatic-interface.md` (as "Garfinkel et al. 2015"). A new note should reuse, not re-derive, that citation.

### Herbert, Herbert & Pollatos (2011) — the original interoception–alexithymia link
- **Citation**: Herbert, B. M., Herbert, C., & Pollatos, O. (2011). "On the relationship between interoceptive awareness and alexithymia: is interoceptive awareness related to emotional awareness?" *Journal of Personality*, 79(5), 1149–1175.
- **Type**: Empirical paper
- **Key points**:
  - Reported that heartbeat-perception performance was negatively associated with alexithymia — i.e. poorer interoceptive accuracy in higher-alexithymia individuals. This seeded the "alexithymia = interoceptive deficit" hypothesis.
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED (title/authors/year; ResearchGate record). BUT the **finding is now CONTESTED** — see meta-analysis below. Treat as a historically-important claim that later work failed to replicate cleanly, NOT as settled fact.

### Contemporary meta-analytic correction — interoception–alexithymia is weaker/subjective-only
- **Sources**:
  - Trevisan, D. A., et al. / and a PLOS One systematic review & meta-analysis: "A systematic review and meta-analysis of the relationship between subjective interoception and alexithymia" (PLOS ONE, journals.plos.org).
  - Meta-analysis of ~10 studies (total N ≈ 998): **weak negative correlation** between alexithymia and interoceptive *sensibility* (self-report); **no reliable correlation** between alexithymia and interoceptive *accuracy* (heartbeat task). Some studies find the accuracy relationship is better described as **quadratic** (high alexithymia at either high or low accuracy).
- **Type**: Meta-analysis / systematic review
- **Key points**:
  - High-alexithymia individuals tend to *underestimate* their interoceptive ability while showing **no objective interoceptive-accuracy deficit** — a metacognitive/report divergence, not necessarily a sensory one.
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED as a real body of current work (multiple independent hits: PLOS One review; Springer "Disentangling interoceptive abilities in alexithymia" 2021; PMC7076086 quadratic-relationship paper). Exact author/year of the headline meta-analysis should be pinned at article-writing time — the *finding pattern* is robust across sources, the single canonical citation is not yet nailed to one paper here.

### Constructed-emotion / active-inference framing (contemporary theory)
- **Citation**: Barrett, L. F. (2017). "The theory of constructed emotion: an active inference account of interoception and categorization." *Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience*, 12(1), 1–23.
- **Type**: Theory paper
- **Key points**:
  - Emotions are constructed predictively by categorising interoceptive/bodily signals under learned concepts. On this view alexithymia is imprecise **categorisation of bodily signals** (low "emotional granularity") rather than absent feeling.
  - Motivates a reading where the divergence is at conceptual/categorisation machinery, not raw phenomenal presence.
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED (title/author/year/venue; PubMed 27798257, PMC5390700). This is a *physicalist-leaning* constructivist theory — include it as the leading opposing frame, not as the Map's own.

### Skin-conductance / arousal-discordance evidence (present-but-inaccessible question)
- **Sources**: e.g. "Skin Conductance as an Index of Alexithymic Traits in the General Population" (PMC9136468, Oxford); "Alexithymia predicts arousal-based processing deficits and discordance between emotion response systems during emotional imagery" (PubMed 26461248).
- **Key points**:
  - Mixed: some studies show high-alexithymia individuals report high subjective intensity with **low physiological arousal** (discordance → emotion present but poorly reported); others show genuinely **reduced** autonomic reactivity (arousal itself blunted → not merely an access problem).
  - A single-case study: high alexithymia + low emotional awareness but **good performance on emotion-recognition tasks**, with atypically low arousal ratings — illustrating dissociability of the sub-processes.
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED as a genuinely mixed literature. The evidence does NOT cleanly settle present-but-inaccessible vs absent — and that unresolved status is itself the Map-relevant point.

## Major Positions

### "Access-layer divergence" reading (Map-congenial)
- **Core claim**: In alexithymia the phenomenal affect is present but introspective *access/report* machinery cannot reliably identify, categorise, or describe it. The "no objective interoceptive-accuracy deficit" meta-analytic pattern supports locating the divergence at the reporting/metacognitive layer.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Congenial to **Tenet 1 (Dualism)**. If emotions carry irreducible phenomenal character, then *knowing* an emotion is a bridging operation between the phenomenal domain and the conceptual domain; alexithymia is a principled difficulty in that crossing rather than a mere machine fault. This is exactly the framing already in `emotion-and-dualism.md` ("the bridge connects ontologically distinct realms"). Maps directly onto the confabulation / source-attribution distinctions in the introspection-architecture programme: the deficit looks like a **report/attribution** failure, not necessarily an absence of the phenomenal state.

### "Absent/blunted feeling" reading (deflationary)
- **Core claim**: Some arousal-discordance studies show genuinely reduced autonomic reactivity — the feeling itself may be attenuated, not merely unreported. On the strong version there is less phenomenal affect to access.
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the honest rival. If the phenomenal state is genuinely *reduced*, alexithymia says less about a two-domain bridge and more about upstream affect generation. The Map should NOT hide this; the literature is mixed and the note flags it as unresolved.

### Constructed-emotion / active-inference reading (physicalist)
- **Proponents**: Lisa Feldman Barrett and the predictive-processing tradition.
- **Core claim**: Emotions are predictive categorisations of interoceptive signal; alexithymia = low emotional granularity / imprecise conceptual categorisation. No irreducible phenomenal residue needed.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with **Tenet 1**. This is the leading reductive account and must be represented as the main opposing frame. The Map's response: constructivism describes the categorisation *machinery* but is silent on why there is something-it-is-like to the affect being categorised — the categorisation story and the phenomenal-presence story are orthogonal, and alexithymia's dissociations (intact recognition, blunted felt arousal, poor report) are consistent with either.

## Key Debates

### Is the interoception–alexithymia link real?
- **Sides**: Herbert et al. (2011) and early work → yes, poorer heartbeat accuracy. Recent meta-analyses → no objective-accuracy link, only weak subjective-sensibility link, possibly quadratic.
- **Current state**: Ongoing / trending toward "the objective deficit is weak-to-absent; the divergence is metacognitive/report-level." Important for the Map because it RELOCATES alexithymia from sensory-deficit to access-architecture — the more interesting cell.

### Present-but-inaccessible vs absent
- **Sides**: Arousal-discordance evidence (present, poorly reported) vs hypo-reactivity evidence (blunted at source).
- **Current state**: Genuinely unresolved; likely heterogeneous (alexithymia is not one mechanism — a "three-process model" is discussed in the literature). This heterogeneity is the honest headline.

## Assessment: Does alexithymia actually bear on the common-cause null?

The claim in `common-cause-null.md` and `cross-traditional-convergence...` is that anomalous-introspective-architecture populations (aphantasia, alexithymia, synaesthesia, neglect, blindsight) test whether the cross-tradition convergence signature is a neurotypical-introspection artefact. Honest assessment:

- **The test is real but partial for alexithymia specifically.** Alexithymia is a graded *trait* in the general population, not a lesion population — so it is a cleaner "dial" than blindsight but a weaker "different-architecture" case. Its value is that it dissociates identify/describe/externally-oriented sub-capacities, letting one ask which specific introspective sub-function the convergence depends on.
- **What the current data actually shows**: the meta-analytic "no objective interoceptive-accuracy deficit" result means alexithymics are NOT straightforwardly missing the phenomenal signal — they diverge at report/metacognition. That *strengthens* the common-cause-null discharge: if the felt affect is present but the reporting architecture differs, and the irreducibility intuitions still track the felt affect, then the convergence is not an artefact of a shared *reporting* architecture.
- **Caveat to state plainly**: there is no direct study asking whether high-alexithymia individuals report the *irreducibility signature* itself (i.e. that emotion overflows conceptualisation). The Map's use of alexithymia is currently a *predicted* exhibit, not a *confirmed* one. The note should say so — the divergence-cluster test is a research programme, not a settled result.
