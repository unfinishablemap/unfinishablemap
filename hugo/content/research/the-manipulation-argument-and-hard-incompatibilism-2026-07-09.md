## Executive Summary

Derk Pereboom's **four-case manipulation argument** is the leading manipulation argument for incompatibilism about moral responsibility. It presents four cases of one agent, "Professor Plum," who commits the same murder while satisfying all standard compatibilist conditions (reasons-responsiveness, acting on second-order desires, etc.), differing only in the causal *route* to his decision: (1) real-time neuroscientist manipulation, (2) covert programming at creation, (3) rigorous deterministic upbringing, and (4) ordinary determinism with no manipulator. Pereboom argues that Plum is intuitively not responsible in Case 1, that there is no *responsibility-relevant* difference between adjacent cases, and therefore that he is not responsible in Case 4 either — so compatibilism fails. The argument is the negative half of Pereboom's positive view, **hard incompatibilism**: we lack the free will required for *basic-desert* moral responsibility whether determinism is true OR the relevant indeterminism obtains. Crucially for the Map, hard incompatibilism does NOT stop at determinism — it argues the libertarian escape *also* fails: event-causal libertarianism only adds luck (the "disappearing agent" objection), and agent-causal libertarianism, while it *could* in principle secure responsibility, is empirically dubious (irreconcilable with physics without "wild coincidences"). Compatibilist replies split into **soft-line** (find a responsibility-relevant difference between the cases, usually at the 3→4 seam) and **hard-line** (bite the bullet: manipulated agents in the earlier cases CAN be responsible, so the argument's opening intuition is denied). **Calibration carried forward for expand-topic (see the "expand-topic lead over-claims phenomenal-absence" failure mode): the Map must NOT claim it defeats Pereboom. Pereboom's luck/control objection to libertarianism is forceful and unresolved; the Map's interface-as-selection-mechanism reply is ONE contested answer, not a refutation.**

- **Type**: Journal article

### Living Without Free Will (Pereboom 2001) — canonical monograph
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/9780521791984 (Cambridge); https://philpapers.org/rec/PERLWF-3
- **Type**: Book (monograph), Cambridge Studies in Philosophy
- **Key points**:
  - Systematic defense of **hard incompatibilism**: we lack the free will required for moral responsibility whether or not determinism is true. Argues against BOTH compatibilism (via the four-case argument) AND libertarianism.
  - Against event-causal libertarianism: indeterminism located in the agent's decision means the agent's states/events "leave it open" which decision occurs, so the agent does not *settle* it — luck, not enhanced control.
  - Against agent-causal libertarianism: it *could* satisfy the origination condition, but we have no empirical reason to believe we are agent-causes, and reconciling agent-causation with physics requires implausible coincidences.
  - Develops the constructive side: life, morality, meaning, and reformed attitudes to wrongdoing survive without basic-desert responsibility (forward-looking, not retributive).
- **Tenet alignment**: The single most direct CONFLICT source for the Map's libertarian program — it accepts incompatibilism (Map-aligned) but argues the libertarian exit is blocked (Map-threatening).
- **Citation status**: **VERIFIED** — Derk Pereboom, *Living Without Free Will*, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2001. ISBN 9780521791984 (hbk); paperback reissue 9780521029964. No DOI for the print monograph (not invented).

### Free Will, Agency, and Meaning in Life (Pereboom 2014) — restatement + basic-desert focus
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/book/6037 (OUP); https://philpapers.org/rec/PERFWA-5
- **Type**: Book (monograph)
- **Key points**:
  - Refines the target to **basic-desert moral responsibility**: an agent is responsible in the basic-desert sense if she deserves blame or credit *just in virtue of* having performed the action, given that she understood its moral status — not by appeal to consequences, contracts, or forward-looking aims.
  - Restates the four-case argument with an updated "best-explanation" framing: the BEST explanation of the shared non-responsibility intuition across the cases is that Plum is determined by factors beyond his control, present equally in Case 4.
  - Adds "wild coincidences" development of the empirical objection to agent-causation (the physical regularities would have to line up with agent-causal interventions with no explanation).
- **Tenet alignment**: Same conflict profile as the 2001 book; the basic-desert framing sharpens what the Map must claim to secure (desert-entailing agency, not merely counterfactual control).

## Major Positions

### Hard Incompatibilism (Pereboom)
- **Proponents**: Derk Pereboom (also broadly aligned: Galen Strawson's Basic Argument, though distinct).
- **Core claim**: We lack the free will required for basic-desert moral responsibility. Determinism rules it out (four-case argument); and the relevant indeterminism does not restore it (luck/disappearing-agent objection to event-causal libertarianism; empirical implausibility of agent-causal libertarianism). So responsibility-grounding free will is unlikely to exist EITHER way.
- **Key arguments**: (a) four-case manipulation argument against compatibilism; (b) disappearing-agent / luck objection against event-causal libertarianism; (c) "wild coincidences"/empirical objection against agent-causal libertarianism.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map's MOST DANGEROUS free-will opponent. It shares the Map's incompatibilism but denies the Map's positive libertarianism. The Map's Tenets 2–3 (minimal quantum interaction; bidirectional consciousness→physical causation) posit exactly the kind of non-physical, agent-harnessable influence Pereboom's empirical objection targets — the Map must argue its interface is a genuine SELECTION mechanism (settling which quantum outcome occurs), not a mere randomizer, and must accept that this is a substantive, contested empirical-metaphysical bet.

### Compatibilism — Soft-Line Reply
- **Proponents**: e.g. defenders who locate a difference at the Case 3→4 seam (and various "the manipulated agent is relevantly abnormal" arguments).
- **Core claim**: There IS a responsibility-relevant difference between the manipulation cases and ordinary determinism — e.g. the manipulated agent's capacities were bypassed, implanted, or are the product of an intentional agent's design in a way ordinary causal history is not. So non-responsibility does not transfer to Case 4.
- **Weakness Pereboom presses**: any candidate difference (another agent's involvement; the timing; the "local vs global" origin) can be shown morally irrelevant by tuning the intermediate cases — the continuum is deliberately smooth.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Not the Map's route (the Map is incompatibilist), but instructive: the Map effectively runs a soft-line-*like* move at the metaphysical level — it claims determinism (Case 4) is FALSE for genuine agents because the interface introduces agent-controlled indeterminism, so the Map does not owe a difference between manipulation and *determinism*; it owes an answer to the luck objection instead.

### Compatibilism — Hard-Line Reply
- **Proponents**: Michael McKenna; Sofia Jeppsson (agential perspective); others.
- **Core claim**: Bite the bullet — deny the opening intuition. A manipulated agent who satisfies the compatibilist conditions CAN be morally responsible; at minimum, judgment about the normal Case 4 should transfer *backwards* to unsettle the Case 1 intuition, rather than the reverse.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Not the Map's route, but relevant: shows the argument's force rests on a defeasible intuition-pump, which the Map can note when calibrating how "decisive" to call Pereboom.

## Key Debates

### Does the four-case continuum have a responsibility-relevant seam?
- **Sides**: Pereboom (no seam — smooth continuum) vs soft-liners (seam exists, usually 3→4).
- **Core disagreement**: whether the involvement of another *manipulating agent*, or the *time/locality* of the determining cause, is morally relevant to responsibility.
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Pereboom's "best explanation" reformulation (2014) shifts the burden from case-by-case intuition to the claim that determination-by-factors-beyond-control is the common explainer.

### Direction of intuition transfer (hard-line)
- **Sides**: Pereboom (Case 1 non-responsibility → Case 4) vs McKenna (Case 4 responsibility → Case 1).
- **Core disagreement**: which end of the continuum carries the more secure intuition.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; a live standoff over the argument's methodology (intuition-based theorizing).

### Can indeterminism provide *enhanced* control (the Map's crux)?
- **Sides**: Pereboom (no — event-causal indeterminism = luck; agent-causation = empirically dubious) vs libertarians (event-causal: Kane; agent-causal: O'Connor, Clarke).
- **Core disagreement**: whether locating indeterminism in decision-making adds control or only noise.
- **Current state**: **Unresolved and forceful for Pereboom's side.** This is precisely where the Map must intervene, and it should present its interface answer as a contested reply, not a settled refutation.
