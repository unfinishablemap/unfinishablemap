# Research: Probability and Decision-Theory Objections to Many Worlds

**Date**: 2026-03-04
**Search queries used**: probability problem Many Worlds Born rule objection; decision theory Many Worlds Deutsch Wallace criticism; Kent "One world versus many"; Albert "Probability in the Everett picture"; Lewis "Uncertainty and probability for branching selves"; Zurek envariance Born rule circularity; self-locating uncertainty Many Worlds Sebens Carroll criticism; Huw Price Everettian probability circularity; Baker measurement outcomes Everettian; incoherence problem probability deterministic Many Worlds

## Executive Summary

The Many-Worlds interpretation faces a deep and unresolved problem with probability. Since all quantum outcomes occur in different branches, it is unclear how probability can be meaningful at all (the incoherence problem) or why it should follow the Born rule (the quantitative problem). Three major strategies have been proposed to address this—decision theory (Deutsch-Wallace), self-locating uncertainty (Vaidman, Sebens-Carroll), and envariance (Zurek)—but each faces serious objections involving circularity, question-begging assumptions, or inability to explain empirical statistics. These objections strongly support the Map's No Many Worlds tenet.

### Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Everettian Interpretations
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/everett/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Everett's original solution was circular—assumed the Born rule measure rather than deriving it
  - Graham showed that branch-counting yields the wrong statistics: worlds displaying proper quantum statistics are "in a numerical minority"
  - Wallace's decision-theoretic approach derives that agents should act "as if" Born rule holds, but critics question whether this explains empirical frequencies
  - Baker identified that decoherence proofs depend implicitly on the Born rule, creating a vicious circle
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds

### Kent — "One World Versus Many" (2010)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/0905.0624
- **Type**: Paper (in Oxford UP volume *Many Worlds?*)
- **Key points**:
  - No known version of purely unitary Everettian quantum theory can account for the appearance of probabilities
  - Cannot explain why Copenhagen QM appears confirmed or why our evolutionary history appears Born-rule typical
  - Wallace's strategy of axiomatizing precise decision theory within fuzzy Everettian quasiclassical ontology is incoherent
  - Wallace's axioms are not constitutive of rationality—they are substantive claims
  - Proposes one-world randomness account that has no many-worlds analogue
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds
- **Quote**: Wallace's axioms "are not constitutive of rationality"

### Albert — "Probability in the Everett Picture" (2010)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/ALBPIT-3
- **Type**: Paper (in *Many Worlds?*)
- **Key points**:
  - Decision-theoretic program addresses the wrong question: it explains betting behaviour rather than actual empirical frequencies
  - Tongue-in-cheek argument that an agent should care about branches where they are "fatter" because "there is more of them" on that branch—challenges the assumption that amplitude determines rational caring
  - Betting behaviour cannot be explanatory of observed statistics
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds

### Price — "Decisions, Decisions, Decisions: Can Savage Salvage Everettian Probability?" (2010)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/0802.1390
- **Type**: Paper (in *Many Worlds?*)
- **Key points**:
  - Everettian decision theory depends on circular reasoning
  - Decoherence proofs depend implicitly on the Born rule
  - Without justifying the probability rule first, Everettians cannot establish the preferred basis or branch structure
  - Without branches, no probability assignment is possible
  - This creates a vicious circle: probability → decoherence → branches → probability
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds

### Lewis — "Uncertainty and Probability for Branching Selves" (2007)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/LEWUAP-3
- **Type**: Paper (*Studies in History and Philosophy of Modern Physics*)
- **Key points**:
  - All variants of the subjective uncertainty strategy fail
  - Either the uncertainty is spurious, or it is in the wrong place to yield probabilistic predictions
  - Arguments from personal identity fail to locate genuine uncertainty
  - Branching does not have sufficient structure to ground self-location probabilities
  - Alternative rules could satisfy the same rationality constraints Deutsch proposed
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds

### Baker — "Measurement Outcomes and Probability in Everettian Quantum Mechanics" (2007)
- **URL**: https://philsci-archive.pitt.edu/2717/
- **Type**: Paper (*Studies in History and Philosophy of Modern Physics*)
- **Key points**:
  - Everettians must define what constitutes a measurement outcome before speaking of probability
  - Identifying outcomes with post-branching persons (as Wallace and Greaves do) makes amplitude not a measure over measurement outcomes
  - Identifying outcomes with branches reduces to the preferred basis problem
  - Circularity: decoherence proofs depend on Born rule → cannot establish preferred basis without Born rule → cannot assign probabilities without preferred basis
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds

### Sebens & Carroll — "Self-Locating Uncertainty and the Origin of Probability in Everettian QM" (2018)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/1405.7577
- **Type**: Paper (*British Journal for the Philosophy of Science*)
- **Key points**:
  - Proposes epistemic separability principle (ESP-QM): an agent's self-locating credences should depend only on the quantum state of the relevant part of the multiverse
  - Claims Born rule is uniquely rational way of apportioning credence
  - Criticized by Friederich and Dawid: the derivation's central principle is implausible given its own motivating assumptions
  - Presupposes ability to count branches, which are ill-defined or lead to wrong predictions
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (attempt to solve MWI's problem, but its failure supports No Many Worlds)

### Zurek — Envariance and the Born Rule
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0401180 (Mohrhoff critique)
- **Type**: Paper series
- **Key points**:
  - Zurek claims to derive Born rule from environment-assisted invariance (envariance) without circularity
  - Critics argue the derivation assumes probabilities are associated with quantum states—then merely shows they take the Born rule form
  - This is insufficient for non-circular derivation
  - Assuming what needs to be proven: that quantum states relate to probabilities at all
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (attempt to solve MWI's problem)

### Saunders — "Branch-counting in the Everett Interpretation" (2021)
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2201.06087
- **Type**: Paper (*Proceedings of the Royal Society A*)
- **Key points**:
  - Branch-counting is the natural alternative probability measure in MWI
  - When a world splits into two branches with different amplitudes, branch counting assigns equal probability regardless of amplitude
  - This generically contradicts the Born rule
  - Defenders must explain why amplitude-weighted measure is preferred over branch counting
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports No Many Worlds (exposes the arbitrariness of probability assignment)

## Major Positions

### The Incoherence Problem
- **Proponents**: Wallace (named the problem), Albert, Kent
- **Core claim**: In a deterministic theory where all outcomes occur, probability is conceptually incoherent. An observer will experience every outcome across branches, so asking "what is the probability of outcome A?" has no clear meaning.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Probability requires genuine uncertainty about outcomes, but MWI guarantees all outcomes occur
  - Redefining probability as "degree of caring about branches" changes the subject—it explains betting behaviour, not why we observe Born-rule frequencies
  - If probability is subjective self-location uncertainty, it is unclear where the uncertainty exists or what grounds it
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds. The Map's commitment to real collapse (Minimal Quantum Interaction) means outcomes are genuinely indeterminate, making standard probability well-defined without philosophical gymnastics.

### The Decision-Theoretic Program (Deutsch-Wallace)
- **Proponents**: Deutsch (1999), Wallace (2003, 2007, 2010), Saunders (2004), Greaves (2004)
- **Core claim**: Rational agents in an Everettian multiverse, following certain decision-theoretic axioms, must weight outcomes by the Born rule when making decisions.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Axioms of rational preference constrain how agents should bet on quantum experiments
  - The only consistent weighting that satisfies all axioms is the Born rule
  - This operationalizes probability through rational action rather than metaphysical chance
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with No Many Worlds (assumes MWI is true). However, the failure of this program supports the tenet. The axioms are contested as non-rational, and the approach explains betting rather than empirical frequencies.

### The Circularity Objection
- **Proponents**: Baker (2007), Price (2010), Kastner, Barnum et al. (2000)
- **Core claim**: All attempts to derive the Born rule in MWI are viciously circular because decoherence (needed to define branches) presupposes the Born rule.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Decoherence suppresses off-diagonal elements, but this process is described using the Born rule
  - Without decoherence, there is no preferred basis and no well-defined branches
  - Without branches, probability cannot be assigned to outcomes
  - The circle: Born rule → decoherence → branches → probability → Born rule
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds. The circularity cannot be broken within MWI. Collapse interpretations avoid this entirely—collapse is a fundamental postulate, not derived from something else.

### The Branch-Counting Problem
- **Proponents**: Graham (1973), Albert (2010), Saunders (2021)
- **Core claim**: The most natural probability measure in MWI is branch counting (equal weight to each branch), which contradicts the Born rule.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Branch counting is the naive and natural way to assign probabilities when all branches are equally real
  - Graham showed that Born-rule-typical branches are in a numerical minority under branch counting
  - Amplitude weighting requires additional justification beyond the theory itself
  - Albert's "fatness" argument: caring more about high-amplitude branches because "there is more of you" is not clearly rational
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly supports No Many Worlds. The fact that MWI's natural probability measure gives wrong answers—and the correct measure requires external justification—is evidence that the framework is deficient.

### Self-Locating Uncertainty Approach
- **Proponents**: Vaidman, Sebens & Carroll (2018), Saunders
- **Core claim**: Between decoherence and observation, an observer genuinely doesn't know which branch they occupy. This grounds probability as rational credence about self-location.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The "sleeping pill" thought experiment: an observer asleep during measurement can rationally assign probabilities to being in each branch
  - ESP-QM principle constrains credences to match the Born rule
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with No Many Worlds (assumes MWI). Its failure supports the tenet: Lewis showed the uncertainty is either spurious or wrongly placed, and Friederich & Dawid showed the central principle is implausible on its own terms.

## Key Debates

### Can Decision Theory Ground Probability in MWI?
- **Sides**: Deutsch-Wallace-Saunders (yes) vs. Kent-Albert-Price-Lewis (no)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether rational decision-theoretic axioms can non-circularly derive the Born rule and whether this constitutes genuine probability
- **Current state**: Ongoing but increasingly recognized as unresolved. The 2010 Oxford volume *Many Worlds?* presented the debate in sharp form. No consensus has emerged. The decision-theoretic program has not convincingly answered the circularity or empirical frequency objections.

### Is the Incoherence Problem Fatal?
- **Sides**: Critics (Albert, Kent) who say probability is meaningless in MWI vs. Defenders (Wallace, Saunders) who redefine probability as rational caring or self-location credence
- **Core disagreement**: Whether redefined "probability" is genuine probability or merely a rational decision rule that fails to explain empirical observations
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Albert's objection that decision theory addresses betting behaviour rather than empirical frequencies remains a serious challenge.

### Does Decoherence Presuppose the Born Rule?
- **Sides**: Baker, Price, Kastner (yes, fatally circular) vs. Wallace, Zurek (no, or the circularity can be broken)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the mathematical formalism of decoherence can be established without probability concepts that depend on the Born rule
- **Current state**: The circularity charge remains influential. Wallace's response—that decoherence is a structural feature of the formalism, not probabilistic—has not convinced all critics.
