## Assess-First Verdict: Worth Covering (Yes)

Hemispherectomy — the removal (anatomical) or functional disconnection (functional hemispherectomy / hemispherotomy) of an entire cerebral hemisphere — is mentioned across ~8 Map files but has no dedicated home. It deserves a `topics/` article because it is arguably a **stronger** resilience datum than split-brain (callosotomy) for the fragmentation flagship. In callosotomy the "two streams" reading is at least *available*: there is a second, intact hemisphere that could in principle host a rival conscious stream, and the literature genuinely debates whether it does. In hemispherectomy there is no second hemisphere. You cannot even *ask* "one mind or two?" — there is only one candidate substrate — yet unified consciousness, a single personality, and (especially in children) near-normal cognition survive the loss of roughly half the cortex. The resilience of unity across massive substrate loss is exactly the kind of datum the Map's filter/interface framework wants to interpret, and the physicalist reorganization reading is a live competitor. High-value, non-duplicative.

## Executive Summary

Hemispherectomy removes or disconnects an entire cerebral hemisphere, most often to stop catastrophic drug-resistant epilepsy (Rasmussen encephalitis, hemimegalencephaly / cortical dysplasia, Sturge-Weber, perinatal stroke). Long-term series (Johns Hopkins: Vining et al. 1997, n=58; Pulsifer et al. 2004, n=71) show that most survivors are seizure-free or greatly improved, that IQ typically changes little, and that personality and unified selfhood are preserved. When surgery is early, the remaining hemisphere can take over functions — including language after left-hemisphere removal — normally lateralized to the excised side (Curtiss & de Bode). Modern connectome work (Kliemann et al. 2019, Cell Reports, six adults; adult case reports) shows the surviving hemisphere reorganizes, with *increased between-network connectivity* relative to controls. The philosophical payload: unity of consciousness is robust to the loss of half the physical substrate, but this is **compatible with both** a filter/production reading and a physicalist plasticity reading. Hemispherectomy sharpens the discriminating question; it does not by itself settle it. Apply possibility-not-proof discipline.

## Key Sources

### Vining et al. (1997), "Why Would You Remove Half a Brain? The Outcome of 58 Children After Hemispherectomy—The Johns Hopkins Experience: 1968 to 1996"
- **URL**: https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article-abstract/100/2/163/38963/
- **Citation**: Vining EPG, Freeman JM, Pillas DJ, Uematsu S, Carson BS, Brandt J, Boatman D, Pulsifer MB, Zuckerberg A. *Pediatrics*. 1997;100(2):163-171.
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed clinical outcome series (published journal article)
- **Key points**:
  - 58 children operated on 1968-1996; 4 perioperative deaths (of 58), 54 survivors.
  - Of 54 survivors: 54% (29/54) seizure-free, 24% (13/54) non-handicapping seizures, 23% (12/54) residual interfering seizures.
  - Etiologies: 27 Rasmussen syndrome, 24 cortical dysplasia / hemimegalencephaly, 7 Sturge-Weber / congenital vascular.
  - Famous for the observation that removing a hemisphere does *not* destroy the person: memory, personality, humor, sense of self persist.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral as data; the persistence of a unified self despite gross substrate loss is congenial framing for dualism/filter but does not by itself establish it.
- **Note**: The frequently cited "we are awed by the apparent retention of memory... and by the retention of the child's personality and sense of humor" observation traces to this Hopkins group. Verify any *verbatim* quote at the publisher before quoting in the article (quote-fidelity discipline).

### Pulsifer et al. (2004), "The Cognitive Outcome of Hemispherectomy in 71 Children"
- **URL**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15009226/
- **Citation**: Pulsifer MB, Brandt J, Salorio CF, Vining EPG, Carson BS, Freeman JM. *Epilepsia*. 2004;45(3):243-254.
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed neuropsychological outcome series
- **Key points**:
  - 71 patients, mean age at surgery 7.2 years; mean follow-up ~5.4 years.
  - IQ change < 15 points for 34 of 53 tested; 11 declined, 8 improved — i.e. cognition mostly *stable*, sometimes improved (seizure relief helps).
  - 65% seizure-free, 49% medication-free at follow-up; no respondent rated quality of life as worse.
  - Etiologies: 27 cortical dysplasia, 37 Rasmussen, 7 vascular/stroke.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral data; supports the resilience claim (half the cortex removed, cognition largely conserved).

### Curtiss & de Bode (2003), "Spoken Language Outcomes After Hemispherectomy: Factoring in Etiology"
- **URL**: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/a1f72ddfd4f3049cb96a610570ddbd5c2264a8d6 (also linguistics.ucla.edu/people/curtiss/HemiPub.doc)
- **Citation**: Curtiss S, de Bode S. *Brain and Language*. 2003;86(2):193-206. (Analysis of 43 UCLA hemispherectomy patients; spoken language rated 0-6.)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed developmental-linguistics study
- **Key points**:
  - After *left* hemispherectomy in childhood the *right* hemisphere can take over language; receptive and (to a lesser extent) expressive functions are often spared.
  - Right-hemisphere plasticity for language appears to persist *beyond* the classically proposed critical period.
  - Outcome depends on age at surgery/onset, etiology, and seizure control — plasticity is real but bounded, not unlimited.
- **Tenet alignment**: Reads naturally as *reorganization* (physicalist plasticity). The Map must engage this honestly: it is the strongest single argument for the production/reorganization interpretation.

### Kliemann et al. (2019), "Intrinsic Functional Connectivity of the Brain in Adults with a Single Cerebral Hemisphere"
- **URL**: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6914265/ (open access)
- **Citation**: Kliemann D, Adolphs R, Tyszka JM, Fischl B, Yeo BTT, Nair R, Dubois J, Paul LK. *Cell Reports*. 2019;29(8):2398-2407.e4.
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed resting-state fMRI connectome study (published)
- **Key points**:
  - Six adults who had undergone childhood hemispherectomy vs. controls (plus a normative sample n=1,482).
  - *Within*-network connectivity in the single hemisphere was strong and equivalent to controls.
  - *Between*-network connectivity was markedly **increased** in hemispherectomy participants across nearly all networks — a signature of reorganization supporting normal cognition on one hemisphere.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral mechanism data; the "increased cross-network integration" is exactly what a physicalist expects and what the Map must not overclaim against.

### Granovetter, Robert, Ettensohn & Behrmann (2022), "With Childhood Hemispherectomy, One Hemisphere Can Support—But Is Suboptimal for—Word and Face Recognition"
- **URL**: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2212936119
- **Citation**: Granovetter MC, Robert S, Ettensohn L, Behrmann M. *PNAS*. 2022;119(44):e2212936119.
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed behavioral study (published; supersedes the 2020 bioRxiv preprint — cite the PNAS version, not the preprint)
- **Key points**:
  - Patients (median age ~16.7 y) with one hemisphere: word AND face recognition both above 80% accuracy but **significantly below** matched controls.
  - Both left- and right-hemispherectomy patients could do both tasks — a single hemisphere supports functions that in intact brains are hemispherically specialized — but at a measurable cost.
  - Honest bidirectional datum: resilience is real *and* bounded ("support but suboptimal").
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; a good antidote to over-rosy resilience framing.

### Kim et al. (2022), "Case Report: Resting-State Brain-Networks After Near-Complete Hemispherectomy in Adulthood"
- **URL**: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9226565/ (Frontiers in Neurology, open access)
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed single-case report
- **Key points**:
  - High-functioning middle-aged man, 6 years after functional hemispherectomy following malignant MCA infarction.
  - Increased between-network connectivity across all seven networks in the surviving hemisphere — corroborates Kliemann in an *adult-onset* case.
  - Demonstrates preserved consciousness and functioning on one hemisphere even when the loss occurred in adulthood (weaker plasticity than childhood, yet unity holds).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral; valuable because adult-onset cases weaken the "it only works because of a child's plastic brain" deflation of the unity datum.

## Major Positions

### Physicalist / Reorganization Reading
- **Proponents (implicit)**: mainstream cognitive neuroscience (Behrmann, Kliemann/Adolphs, Curtiss/de Bode read this way).
- **Core claim**: Unity survives because the remaining tissue *reorganizes* to implement the functions the lost hemisphere used to run. Consciousness is a property of the functioning network; shrink the network, it re-wires, the property persists. Increased between-network integration (Kliemann, Kim) is the mechanism.
- **Key arguments**: Measurable connectome changes; bounded outcomes (Granovetter "suboptimal"); strong age-at-surgery dependence (plasticity).
- **Relation to site tenets**: This is the serious rival to the Map's reading. It does not require dualism. The Map must present it fairly and locate the *discriminating* question rather than claim hemispherectomy refutes physicalism.

### Filter / Transmission (Map-aligned) Reading
- **Core claim**: If consciousness is *received/filtered* through the brain rather than *produced* by it, then unity is a property of the receiver (the non-physical mind), and the brain is the *interface*. Losing half the interface degrades bandwidth and specific channels (hence "suboptimal") but does not fragment the *self*, because the self was never identical to the hemispheres. Unity is expected to be robust to substrate loss — which is what we observe.
- **Key arguments**: The self/personality persists intact; there is never a hint of "half a person"; unity does not track cortical mass.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Aligns with filter-theory and bidirectional interaction. But — crucially — it is *compatible* with the same data as the physicalist reading. Both predict preserved unity. Honesty requires stating this.

### The Split-Brain Contrast (why hemispherectomy is the sharper case)
- In callosotomy, two hemispheres remain; the "two streams" interpretation (Sperry/Gazzaniga classic picture) is at least *available*, and recent work (Pinto et al. 2017, *Brain*, "divided perception but undivided consciousness"; Volz & Gazzaniga 2017) contests whether there are really two conscious subjects.
- In hemispherectomy there is only one hemisphere, so the "two streams" question **cannot even be posed**. Unity is not the *survival of one stream over a rival* — there is no rival. This removes the split-brain escape hatch and isolates the datum: unity persists through massive substrate loss per se.

## Key Debates

### Does preserved unity favor filter over production?
- **Sides**: Map/filter reading vs. physicalist reorganization reading.
- **Core disagreement**: whether robustness-of-unity is *evidence for* a non-physical receiver, or merely what a plastic network does.
- **Current state**: Unresolved and — on current data — *underdetermined*. Both readings predict preserved unity. Hemispherectomy is possibility-consistent-with-the-Map, not proof of it.

### What is the discriminating question? (state this, not a proof)
- A genuinely discriminating test would need a case where the two readings make *different* predictions. Candidate framing for the downstream article: does the *quality/structure* of unified experience degrade in proportion to lost substrate (production predicts graded fragmentation of the *self*), or does the self stay whole while only *channels/contents* degrade (filter predicts intact unity + degraded bandwidth)? Granovetter's "intact-but-suboptimal" profile is *weakly* more congenial to the channels-degrade picture, but is not decisive — a physicalist reorganization account predicts the same "capable but costly" profile. Flag as an open discriminating question, not a settled result.
