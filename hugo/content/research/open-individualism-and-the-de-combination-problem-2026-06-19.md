## Executive Summary

Two distinct-but-mirrored ideas were surveyed. (1) **Open individualism** (Daniel Kolak's term; Arnold Zuboff's "universalism") is the thesis that there is numerically only *one* subject of experience, who is everyone — the radical rival to the commonsense **closed individualism** (many distinct, persisting subjects — the Map's "manyism" in spirit) and to **empty individualism** (no persisting subject; subject-boundaries are conventional, à la Parfit). (2) The **de-combination problem** (Gregory Miller 2018; also called the *derivation problem* or, by Albahari, the *decombination problem*) is the structural inverse of panpsychism's combination problem: if one cosmic subject is fundamental (**cosmopsychism**), in virtue of what does it ground or fragment into the many "little" subjects we observe? Miller argues this is *no easier* than combination — both are the single puzzle of whether subjects can be proper parts of subjects, approached from opposite ends. **All citations below were web-verified at publisher of record or PhilPapers/SEP; several pre-supplied metadata items needed correction (see Citations).** The two ideas connect at the Map: open individualism is a coherent *rival the Map declines* (the Map's No-Many-Worlds / haecceity commitments require numerically distinct subjects), and the de-combination problem is a *cost cosmopsychism and idealism pay* that the Map's interactionist dualism structurally avoids (it denies micro-experience and a single cosmic field alike, so there is nothing to combine, share, *or* de-combine).

## Major Positions

### Open Individualism (Kolak; Zuboff's "universalism")
- **Proponents**: Daniel Kolak (*I Am You*, 2004); Arnold Zuboff ("One Self: The Logic of Experience," 1990 — his label is *universalism*); precursors named in the literature: Erwin Schrödinger ("consciousness is a singular of which the plural is unknown"), Schopenhauer, the Upanishadic *tat tvam asi*, Spinoza, Alan Watts, Freeman Dyson.
- **Core claim**: There exists only one numerically identical subject who is everyone, at all times. The apparent separation between you and me is an illusion of perspective; subject-boundaries carry none of the metaphysical weight commonsense assigns them.
- **Kolak's taxonomy** (the load-bearing piece for the Map — already used in `consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation`): three *exhaustive* positions on personal identity / individuation —
  - **Closed individualism**: the commonsense view — personal identities are particular to individuals and persist over time. (The Map sits closest here; this is the spirit of its "manyism" of distinct subjects.)
  - **Empty individualism**: there is no persisting self; identity corresponds to a momentary pattern that vanishes with time's passage. Kolak files Parfit's reductionism here.
  - **Open individualism**: one subject, appearing as many.
- **Kolak's argument structure**: the standard criteria for personal identity (bodily continuity, psychological continuity, Lockean memory) all fail to ground determinate closed-individualist boundaries; once they fail, Kolak argues open individualism is the *best competing explanation* of who we are, and grounds a global ethics (harming another is harming oneself). The book's subtitle — *The Metaphysical Foundations for Global Ethics* — signals the ethical payoff is the motivation.
- **Zuboff's variant**: derives the one-subject conclusion from the *logic of first-person immediacy* — the "I am the one having this experience" character is the same wherever consciousness occurs, so there is no principled fact dividing the immediacy into separate owners. (Note: a forthcoming Zuboff book, *Finding Myself*, with a Nagel foreword, was mentioned in secondary sources — not verified at a publisher and should NOT be cited as established.)
- **Relation to site tenets**: **CONFLICTS** with the Map. Open individualism collapses exactly the distinct-subjects fact the Map's `haecceity` and `No Many Worlds` tenet presuppose ("which branch am I in?" requires a haecceitistic *this-one* answer). The Map is a *closed-individualist* framework. Open individualism is therefore a rival the Map declines — engaged honestly, not refuted.

### Cosmopsychism (the home of the de-combination problem)
- **Proponents (verified via SEP roster)**: Freya Mathews (2011, 2020); Jaskolla & Buck (2012); Itay Shani (2015); Yujin Nagasawa & Khai Wager (*priority cosmopsychism*, 2016); Philip Goff (2017, 2020 — *grounding by subsumption*); Shani & Keppler (2018, the SED/zero-point-field "filtering" model); Miri Albahari (*perennial idealism*, a panpsychist-idealist variant).
- **Core claim**: invert panpsychism's direction — the cosmos as a whole is the one fundamental conscious subject; individual minds are not built up from micro-subjects but are *grounded in*, *derived from*, or *dissociated from* the cosmic subject.
- **Motivation**: cosmopsychism was proposed largely *because* it appeared to dodge the subject-summing combination problem (no micro-subjects to sum).
- **Relation to site tenets**: **CONFLICTS** — it is a monist grounding programme; the Map's `Dualism` tenet denies that ordinary consciousness is grounded in any prior consciousness (cosmic or micro). But cosmopsychism is the Map's natural *foil* for showing the de-combination cost.

### The Map's position (for the article's "Relation to Site Perspective")
- The Map denies BOTH directions of composition: matter has no micro-experience (so no combination), and there is no single cosmic conscious field (so no de-combination/derivation). Consciousness is a distinct ontological category that *interacts* with matter (`interactionist-dualism`, `minimal-quantum-interaction`). The de-combination problem is thus a cost the rivals pay and the Map sidesteps — at the price of the *interaction* problem instead (the Map's standard, honest trade; already articulated in `panpsychisms-combination-problem`).

## Key Debates

### Is the de-combination problem genuinely as hard as combination?
- **Sides**: Gregory Miller (2018) — YES, equivalent; both are the problem of subjects-as-proper-parts-of-subjects. David Chalmers (2017) — the analogous "decomposition problem ... seems just as hard as the combination problem." vs. cosmopsychists who claim an asymmetry favours the cosmic direction (Goff's *grounding by subsumption*; Shani's *partial grounding* via the cosmos's perspectival/experiential capacities; Shani & Keppler's *filtering/resonance* model that denies subjects are literally "fractured from" the cosmic subject; Nagasawa & Wager's *priority* framing that the whole is prior without literal parthood).
- **Core disagreement**: whether grounding "little" subjects in a "big" one requires intelligible subject-subject parthood (Miller's hard nut) or can be cashed out by a non-constitutive relation (filtering, dissociation, perspectival partition) that sidesteps parthood.
- **Albahari's twist**: she argues the decombination problem *exposes* a perspectival assumption baked into modern panpsychism; her *perennial idealism* posits an *aperspectival*, non-dual cosmic consciousness, so there is no perspectival cosmic subject to de-combine in the first place. (This is a "dissolve, don't solve" move.)
- **Current state**: ONGOING and active (2015–present). The consensus in the critical literature (Miller, Chalmers) is that cosmopsychism has *not* clearly escaped — it has relocated the combination problem rather than solved it. This is precisely the Map's reading.

### The conceivability mirror
- SEP states the parallel crisply: *"just as we can conceive of micro-level subjects existing in the absence of macro-level subjects, so it seems that we can conceive of a conscious universe existing without having conscious parts."* This is the de-combination analogue of Goff's micro-experiential zombie / Coleman's dilemma — directly reusable to show the symmetry.

### Open individualism vs the Map's haecceity commitment
- **Sides**: open individualism (boundaries illusory) vs the Map (boundaries real but their *ground* is a void — see `consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation`, `vertiginous-question`).
- **Core disagreement**: whether the felt this-ness of being *this* subject is a genuine metaphysical fact (Map: yes, possibly primitive/haecceitistic) or a perspectival illusion (open individualism: no).
- **Note**: open individualism and the de-combination problem are *cousins, not the same thing*. Open individualism is a thesis about *persons/identity*; the de-combination problem is a structural problem for *cosmopsychism's grounding*. They meet because a one-subject metaphysics (open individualism, cosmopsychism, idealism) must explain the *appearance* of many — which just IS the de-combination/individuation question. The article should make this connection explicit and resist conflating them.
