## Executive Summary

The comic (or humor) is a genuine aesthetic category alongside the beautiful and the sublime — a mode of aesthetic response with its own object, its own felt quality (amusement / mirth), and its own theoretical literature. Three classical theories compete to explain it: **superiority** (laughter as felt triumph — Hobbes's "sudden glory," rooted in Plato and Aristotle), **relief** (laughter as discharge of pent-up nervous energy — Spencer, Freud), and **incongruity** (laughter at a perceived violation of one's mental patterns and expectations — Beattie, Kant, Schopenhauer, and, in refined "incongruity-resolution" form, the dominant modern view of Michael Clark, John Morreall, and Noël Carroll). For the Map, the philosophically load-bearing datum is the phenomenology of **amusement itself**: the felt "click" of getting a joke is an evaluative quale that seems to outrun any structural description of the joke — a fresh test case for phenomenal irreducibility parallel to the aesthetic knowledge argument. But this reading must be calibrated: computational descendants of incongruity theory (notably Hurley, Dennett, and Adams's *Inside Jokes*) model amusement as an evolved reward signal with no experiential primitive, so the dualist interpretation is one live reading, not a proof.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Philosophy of Humor"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/humor/
- **Author**: John Morreall
- **Dates**: First published 2012-11-20; substantive revision 2024-09-19
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (authoritative; author is himself the leading contemporary humor philosopher)
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED (fetched full text)
- **Key points**:
  - Lays out the three theories and their proponents (details below).
  - Notes the 18th-century rise of incongruity theory (Beattie, Kant) as a challenge to superiority theory; "It is now the dominant theory of humor in philosophy and psychology."
  - Distinguishes incongruity *perception* from incongruity *resolution*: Schultz (1976) and Suls (1972/1983) argue that amusement requires "getting the joke" through reinterpretation; unresolvable incongruity is mere nonsense.
  - Morreall's own view: humor is **cognitive play** — playful engagement with a violation of one's mental/conversational patterns in a safe (non-practical) frame. Amusement requires Bergson's "momentary anesthesia of the heart" (emotional disengagement) and a safety condition (Aristotle, *Poetics* 5: what is funny is "a mistake or unseemliness that is not painful or destructive").
  - Morreall (1987, 204-205): several aesthetic categories — the grotesque, the macabre, the horrible, the bizarre, the fantastic — involve *non-humorous* enjoyment of pattern-violation; so incongruity alone does not entail humor. (Useful: this places the comic explicitly in a family of aesthetic categories.)

### Kant, *Critique of Judgment* (1790), First Part, §54
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED — section number and quote confirmed against SEP and IEP.
- **Quote**: "Laughter is an affection arising from the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing."
- Kant illustrates with the story of the Indian astonished that the beer foamed out of a bottle — the joke turning on how it got *in*. Kant stresses that the pleasure is bodily, not intellectual: the play of thought produces an "alternating tension and relaxation" felt in the body. This bodily/anti-cognitivist strand is a useful counter-nuance to a purely propositional reading.

### Schopenhauer, *The World as Will and Representation*, Vol. I §13 (1818), developed in Vol. II ch. 8 (1844)
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED — §13 (Vol. I) confirmed as the primary locus; the fuller "Theory of the Comical" is Vol. II, ch. 8 of the 1844 second edition.
- **Core claim**: Laughter is "the sudden perception of an incongruity between a concept and the real [perceived] object" — between abstract thought and intuitive perception. In any conflict between the perceived and the conceived, "what is perceived is always unquestionably right." Wit is the facility for subsuming an object under an unexpected concept.
- Note: often cited by translation title *The World as Will and Idea* (Haldane & Kemp, 1907) — the SEP uses that title. Both refer to the same work; prefer the modern "Representation" translation in article prose.

### Freud, *Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious* (1905)
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED (1905, German *Der Witz und seine Beziehung zum Unbewussten*; standard English in the Strachey/Standard Edition, 1960/1974).
- **Type**: Book — canonical statement of the relief theory.
- Freud distinguishes three cases: *jokes* (Witz) release energy otherwise spent on repression; *the comic* releases energy devoted to thinking/ideation; *humor* is an economy of affect (feeling spared). The "hydraulic" model of discharged nervous energy is now regarded as scientifically obsolete (Morreall's own assessment).

### Noël Carroll, *Humour: A Very Short Introduction* (2014)
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED — Oxford University Press, Very Short Introductions series, ISBN 9780199552221, 2014.
- **Type**: Book (accessible scholarly overview).
- Surveys superiority, incongruity, release, play, and dispositional theories; Carroll **favors the incongruity theory**. He emphasizes humor's cognitive and social functions — "comic amusement" builds community, relieves tension, displays intelligence — and treats amusement as a distinctive emotional/cognitive response, not just energy discharge. Also foregrounds the ethics of humor ("When is humour immoral?").

### Michael Clark, "Humour and Incongruity" (1970)
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED — *Philosophy* 45(171): 20-32 (1970), Cambridge University Press, DOI 10.1017/S003181910000958X. Note: SEP cites a REPRINT in Morreall (ed.), *The Philosophy of Laughter and Humor* (1987), pp. 139-155. Use the 1970 original as the primary citation; the 1987 reprint is a valid secondary locator.
- **Key contribution**: proposes three (near) necessary-and-sufficient conditions for humor: (1) the person perceives an incongruity, (2) enjoys perceiving it, and (3) enjoys the incongruity partly *for itself*, not solely for ulterior reasons. Condition (3) is doing real work for the Map: it locates the value of the comic in a *disinterested* enjoyment of the incongruity as such — a formal parallel to disinterested aesthetic pleasure in beauty (Kant) and to the acquaintance principle (you must feel it yourself; being told a joke is funny is not amusement).

### Hurley, Dennett & Adams, *Inside Jokes: Using Humor to Reverse-Engineer the Mind* (2011)
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED — MIT Press, 2011 (hardcover; paperback 2013), ISBN 9780262518697.
- **Type**: Book — the leading COMPUTATIONAL / evolutionary account. This is the rival to name for calibration.
- **Core claim**: Humor is "a detailed and sophisticated descendant of incongruity theories." The mind, doing open-ended predictive inference under time pressure, unconsciously commits to a false assumption; mirth is the reward signal delivered when that covert error is discovered and retracted ("epistemic caretaking"). Natural selection "cannot just order the brain to find and fix all our... misleaps... She has to bribe the brain with pleasure." On this account amusement is fully explained as an evolved computational reward — **no experiential primitive is posited or needed.** A system could detect and correct the covert error (and even reap the functional benefit) without any account of *why it is felt*. This is precisely the model that dissolves the "irreducible quale" reading, and must be presented as a live competitor.

## Major Positions

### Superiority Theory
- **Proponents**: Plato (*Philebus* 48-50, laughter as malice at others' self-ignorance), Aristotle (*Rhetoric* 2.12, wit as "educated insolence"; *Poetics* 5, the ridiculous as painless ugliness), Thomas Hobbes (*Leviathan*, 1651).
- **Core claim**: Laughter is the expression of "sudden glory" — a felt sense of superiority over others or over a former self.
- **Hobbes quote**: "Sudden glory, is the passion which maketh those grimaces called laughter" (*Leviathan* I.6).
- **Standard objection**: Hutcheson (1750) — superiority is neither necessary nor sufficient; we laugh at clever feats *beyond* our ability and at incongruities involving no one's inferiority.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral. A purely functional/social account of why laughter is triggered; says nothing about the phenomenal character of amusement, so neither supports nor threatens Tenet 1.

### Relief Theory
- **Proponents**: Shaftesbury (1709), Herbert Spencer ("On the Physiology of Laughter," 1911), Sigmund Freud (1905).
- **Core claim**: Laughter vents surplus nervous energy that has become superfluous (e.g., when an aroused emotion turns out inappropriate).
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral-to-mildly-adverse. Its hydraulic-energy mechanism is a physicalist reduction, but it is largely abandoned on empirical grounds, so it is a weak physicalist ally rather than a live rival.

### Incongruity Theory (and Incongruity-Resolution)
- **Proponents**: James Beattie (1779), Kant (1790), Schopenhauer (1818/1844); modern: Michael Clark (1970), John Morreall, Noël Carroll; incongruity-*resolution* refinement: Schultz (1976), Suls (1972/1983).
- **Core claim**: Amusement is the (playful, disinterested) enjoyment of a perceived incongruity — a clash between what one expected/conceived and what is presented. The resolution variant adds that the incongruity must be *resolvable* ("getting it"): pure unresolved incongruity is nonsense or unease, not comedy.
- **Why dominant**: it fits the widest range of cases (verbal jokes, slapstick, wit) and cleanly separates the *object* of humor (the incongruity) from the *response* (amusement).
- **Relation to site tenets**: The most fertile for the Map. Incongruity theory characterizes the *object* structurally but leaves the *felt amusement* as an extra term (Clark's conditions 2 and 3 — *enjoying* it, and enjoying it *for itself*). That residue — mirth as an evaluative quale not entailed by the structural description — is the dualist opening (Tenet 1). But see the calibration flag: the computational-resolution wing (Hurley/Dennett/Adams) closes exactly that gap by naturalizing the reward, so the opening is contested, not proven.

## Key Debates

### Does incongruity need resolution?
- **Sides**: Pure incongruity theorists vs incongruity-resolution theorists (Suls, Schultz).
- **Disagreement**: whether mere perceived incongruity suffices, or whether amusement requires a subsequent reinterpretation that makes the incongruity fit ("getting the joke").
- **State**: Ongoing; resolution is the more popular refinement but faces cases of enjoyed nonsense/absurdist humor.

### Is amusement reducible to computation? (the Map-relevant debate)
- **Sides**: Computational/evolutionary naturalism (Hurley, Dennett & Adams, *Inside Jokes*) vs any view treating mirth as an irreducible felt quality.
- **Disagreement**: whether the felt "click" of amusement is exhausted by its functional role (a reward for covert error-correction) or whether the phenomenal character is a further, unexplained fact.
- **State**: Open. This is the debate a Map article should stage — and, per the calibration note, should stage *fairly*, presenting the dualist reading as one interpretation.

### When is humor immoral? (secondary)
- Carroll and Morreall both treat the ethics of humor (e.g., laughing at cruelty). Not central to the Map's phenomenal-irreducibility angle, but a natural sub-section and a hedge against over-narrow treatment.

## Potential Article Angles

1. **The comic as an aesthetic category (primary, tenet-aligned).** Mirror `the-sublime-and-negative-aesthetics.md`: present humor as a third aesthetic category beside the beautiful and the sublime, with its own object (resolvable incongruity) and its own quale (mirth). Argue that the three structural theories characterize the *object* and *trigger* of amusement but leave the felt "click" as a residue — an aesthetic knowledge-argument analog (you can know a joke's entire incongruity-resolution structure and still fail to be amused). Connect explicitly to Tenet 1 (Dualism) and Tenet 5 (limits of Occam's Razor — the simplest, purely functional story may be inadequate precisely where phenomenal character is at stake).
2. **The amusement quale vs the humor-detector (calibrated).** Frame the core thought experiment: a system that detects and even reports a joke's structure without being amused (the humor analog of the philosophical zombie / Mary's Room). Then, *in the same article*, give the naturalizing rival — Hurley/Dennett/Adams's "bribe the brain with pleasure" — its full strength, and conclude that the dualist reading is one live interpretation, not a demonstration.
3. **The disinterested-enjoyment bridge.** Use Clark's "enjoyed for itself" clause and Kant's disinterested-pleasure framework to link the comic to the broader Map treatment of aesthetic response and the acquaintance principle (testimony that a joke is funny is not amusement — you must feel it).

## Calibration Flag Carried Forward (for expand-topic)

**Per the guidance against over-claiming phenomenal absence:** the future article must NOT lead with, or assert as proven, that amusement is a non-physical/irreducible primitive. The incongruity-resolution and computational/predictive-processing accounts (especially *Inside Jokes*, Hurley/Dennett/Adams 2011) model amusement as an evolved reward signal for covert-error correction with **no experiential primitive** — a full functional story. The article should:
- Name this rival explicitly and state it at its strongest.
- Present the "mirth as irreducible evaluative quale" reading as ONE live interpretation, framework-relative, not a proof of dualism.
