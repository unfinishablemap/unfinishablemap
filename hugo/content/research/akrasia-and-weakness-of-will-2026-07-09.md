## Executive Summary

Akrasia (Greek: "lack of self-control"), usually translated "weakness of will," names the phenomenon of acting intentionally and freely against one's own better judgement about what is best to do. The problem is contested at the descriptive level: Plato's Socrates in the *Protagoras* denied that genuine akrasia is even possible (no one knowingly does the worse — apparent akrasia is really ignorance), whereas Aristotle (*Nicomachean Ethics* VII) accepts the phenomenon and diagnoses it as a failure to *activate* knowledge one possesses, analysed through the "practical syllogism." The modern locus is Donald Davidson's 1970 essay "How Is Weakness of the Will Possible?", which reconstructs akrasia as intentional action against an *all-things-considered* better judgement and resolves the apparent paradox via a distinction between conditional (prima facie / all-things-considered) and unconditional (all-out) evaluative judgements, plus a "principle of continence." A later strand (Richard Holton, *Willing, Wanting, Waiting*, 2009) detaches "weakness of will" from acting-against-best-judgement entirely, recasting it as the over-ready abandonment of a prior *resolution* under temptation — foregrounding willpower and diachronic self-management. The whole cluster is primarily philosophy of action / moral psychology and does **not** require dualism; the Map's selection-model reading is one interpretive lens on the first-person datum "I chose X while judging Y better," not evidence for dualism (see Calibration Flag below).

### Donald Davidson, "How Is Weakness of the Will Possible?"
- **Original publication**: In Joel Feinberg (ed.), *Moral Concepts*, Oxford University Press. **DATE FLAG**: commonly cited as **1970**; some catalogue records give the Feinberg volume as **1969**. Treat the collected reprint as the stable citation.
- **Canonical reprint**: In Davidson, *Essays on Actions and Events*, Oxford University Press, **1980**, Essay 2 (pp. 21–42). VERIFIED at global.oup.com (product page, ISBN 9780199246274 for the 2nd-edition paperback; original hardback 0198246374 / 9780198246374).
- **Type**: Journal-adjacent essay / book chapter
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED — book confirmed at OUP publisher of record; essay confirmed as Essay 2 of the collection via OUP Academic and PhilPapers (DAVHIW). Do NOT invent a DOI for the 1970/1980 essay; cite by book + essay number/page range.
- **Key points**:
  - Definition of incontinent (akratic) action — an agent does x incontinently iff: (1) x is done intentionally; (2) the agent believes an alternative y is open; (3) the agent judges that, all things considered, it would be better to do y than x.
  - The resolution turns on grading judgements: **prima facie (PF)** judgements ("in light of reason r, a is prima facie better") and **all-things-considered (ATC)** judgements are *conditional* / relational; an **all-out (AO)** judgement ("a is better," unconditional) is what actually issues in action. The akratic reaches the ATC judgement favouring y but forms the AO judgement favouring x. The action is thus intelligible but *irrational*.
  - **Principle of continence**: perform the action judged best on the basis of all available relevant reasons; the akratic violates this principle.
- **Quote**: "if we ask what is the agent's reason for doing [b] when he believes it would be better...to do another thing, then the answer must be: for this, the agent has no reason" (*Essays on Actions and Events*, p. 42).

### Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book VII (chs. 1–10, the treatment of akrasia)
- **Type**: Primary classical text (public domain; multiple critical editions/translations)
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED as to location (NE Book VII is the standard locus for akrasia/*enkrateia*), corroborated by SEP "Aristotle's Ethics" and secondary literature. Cite by Bekker/book-chapter (NE VII) rather than a spurious DOI.
- **Key points**:
  - Aristotle *accepts* akrasia against Socrates but tries to preserve "the kernel of truth in the Socratic paradox" — that people act against the best only through some *ignorance*. He asks: if the akratic acts by reason of ignorance, "what is the manner of his ignorance?"
  - Answer via the **practical syllogism**: the akratic possesses the relevant knowledge but it is "not in active use" — a distinction between *potential* and *actual* (activated) knowledge. Under passion/appetite the agent fails properly to grasp or deploy the *minor premise* (the application of the universal to the particular situation).
  - The state is contrasted with *enkrateia* ("continence"/self-mastery) and *sophrosyne* (temperance): the akratic knows better but is overcome; the continent person feels the pull but masters it.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral / compatible. Aristotle's "knowledge not active" is a structural claim about how evaluative cognition relates to action — of interest to the Map's selection framing, but carries no dualist commitment.

### Richard Holton, *Willing, Wanting, Waiting*
- **URL**: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/... (Oxford University Press, 2009); OUP Academic book id: https://academic.oup.com/book/8583
- **Type**: Monograph
- **Verification status**: VERIFIED at OUP (2009; hardback ISBN 9780199214570; paperback 9780199692286). Reviewed in *Ethics* 120(3) and *Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews*. Related precursor article: Holton, "Intention and Weakness of Will," *Journal of Philosophy* 96(5), 1999 (PDF at web.mit.edu/holton) — cited by SEP as the origin of the resolution-abandonment view.
- **Key points**:
  - **Reconception**: weakness of will is NOT (necessarily) acting against one's better judgement. It is the over-ready **abandonment of a resolution** — an intention formed precisely to insulate the agent against contrary inclinations expected to arise later.
  - **Resolution** = "intentions that are formed precisely in order to insulate one against contrary inclinations which one expects to feel when the time comes." Temptation typically works by *shifting one's judgement* about what is best (or, in addiction, by decoupling desire from judgement).
  - **Strength of will / willpower**: the corresponding skill/faculty of maintaining a resolution under temptation; Holton treats willpower as an effortful, trainable capacity, not the automatic output of correct judgement.
  - Situates the will as a set of devices that *compensate* for our inability to make and hold sound judgements — deflating the picture of the will as the pinnacle of rationality.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Holton draws on empirical psychology (ego-depletion-era self-control research); no dualist commitment. Useful to the Map as a *diachronic* counter-model to Davidson's *synchronic* framing.

## Major Positions

### Socratic Denial (Intellectualism)
- **Proponents**: Socrates (as portrayed in Plato's *Protagoras*); modern echo in R. M. Hare's argument that "ought" judgements are prescriptive, so sincerely judging y best entails doing y (making genuine akrasia near-impossible).
- **Core claim**: No one knowingly acts against the good. Apparent akrasia is really a cognitive failure — miscalculation of pleasures/pains or ignorance of the actual better option.
- **Key arguments**: If to judge y better just is to be most motivated toward y, acting otherwise is incoherent; so the "better judgement" must not really have been held.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Neutral/challenging to the selection framing. If Socrates is right, there is no first-person datum "I chose X while judging Y better" — the datum dissolves into "I misjudged." The Map should treat the *reality of the datum* as itself contested, not assumed.

### Aristotelian Diagnosis (Knowledge Not Active)
- **Proponents**: Aristotle (NE VII); modern Aristotelians.
- **Core claim**: Akrasia is real but is a failure to *exercise* possessed knowledge under the influence of appetite/passion; the practical syllogism's particular premise is not properly deployed.
- **Key arguments**: Distinguish having knowledge from using it; drunkenness/passion analogy — one can "have and not have" knowledge.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible; a structural account of will/judgement/action that the Map's selection model can read as: the interface's selection is not a transparent read-off of the agent's best evaluative state.

### Davidsonian Resolution (Possible but Irrational)
- **Proponents**: Donald Davidson.
- **Core claim**: Akrasia is genuinely possible; it is intentional action against an ATC judgement, made intelligible by the PF/ATC (conditional) vs AO (unconditional) distinction, and it is *irrational* (violates the principle of continence) but not *impossible* or *reason-less-as-behaviour*.
- **Key arguments**: The gap between the ATC judgement and the AO judgement that actually moves the agent; the akratic has no *reason* for the discrepancy, which is precisely the mark of irrationality.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The core datum for the Map. Davidson locates a genuine gap between "what the agent judges best all things considered" and "what the agent all-out does" — a first-person structural fact about willing that the selection model can interpret.

### Holtonian Reconception (Resolution-Abandonment / Willpower)
- **Proponents**: Richard Holton.
- **Core claim**: Weakness of will ≠ acting against best judgement; it is unreasonably abandoning a prior resolution under temptation. Willpower is a distinct, effortful, trainable faculty.
- **Key arguments**: Ordinary usage tracks resolution-failure, not evaluative incoherence; temptation often *changes* the judgement, so "acting against present best judgement" mislocates the failure to the wrong time.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Compatible; supplies a *diachronic* model (maintaining/abandoning a commitment over time) complementary to Davidson's *synchronic* one. Bears on whether willpower is a capacity the conscious interface exercises.

## Key Debates

### Is akrasia even possible?
- **Sides**: Socratic/Harean denial vs Davidsonian (and common-sense) affirmation.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether "judging y best" is constitutively motivating (so that acting against it is incoherent) or whether judgement and motivation can come apart.
- **Current state**: Ongoing, but the post-Davidson consensus treats akrasia as real; debate has shifted to *how* to characterise it and whether it is always irrational.

### Is akratic action necessarily irrational?
- **Sides**: Davidson (irrational by definition — violates continence) vs later writers who argue akratic action can be *rational* when the agent's "better judgement" is itself defective or when resisting a bad resolution.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the norm violated (act on your best all-out judgement) is unconditionally rational.
- **Current state**: Live; connects to the internalism/externalism dispute about judgement–motivation links.

### Synchronic vs diachronic akrasia
- **Sides**: Davidsonian synchronic framing (present judgement vs present action) vs Holtonian diachronic framing (past resolution vs present abandonment).
- **Core disagreement**: At what time-index the failure occurs, and whether the relevant failure is evaluative (judgement) or executive (maintenance of intention/willpower).
- **Current state**: Both framings coexist; often treated as capturing distinct phenomena rather than rival analyses of one.
