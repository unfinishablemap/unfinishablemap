## Executive Summary

Reductionism is the philosophical thesis that complex phenomena can be explained by—and ultimately reduced to—their simpler constituents. Three types must be distinguished: ontological (claims about what exists), methodological (scientific strategy), and epistemic/explanatory (claims about understanding). The Unfinishable Map's anti-reductionist stance concerns primarily ontological and explanatory reductionism about consciousness. Key arguments against reducing consciousness to physical processes include: Levine's explanatory gap (physical descriptions don't explain why there is experience), Chalmers' hard problem (functional explanations leave qualia unexplained), and Putnam/Fodor's multiple realizability (same mental state realized by different physical states). Kim's exclusion argument challenges non-reductive views, but the Map's quantum framework provides a response by locating consciousness at genuine physical incompleteness.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy - Scientific Reduction
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/scientific-reduction/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Reduction is a term philosophers use for relations of particular importance in philosophy of science, mind, and metaphysics
  - Most contemporary reductionists are committed to physicalism, but reductionism per se is ontologically neutral
  - For reductions to be explanatory, they must not leave out any aspect of the phenomenon
  - If qualia-concepts pick out mental states in terms of features that cannot be fully explained in terms of reducing science vocabulary, the reduction fails
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports Dualism tenet—the explanatory gap shows reduction fails for consciousness
- **Quote**: "If qualia-concepts or concepts of conscious states pick out mental states in terms of features that cannot be fully explained in terms of the vocabulary of the reducing science, the explanation and hence the reduction is not successful."

### Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy - Reductionism
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/red-ism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Three types: ontological, methodological, epistemic
  - Ontological reductionism claims reality consists of minimal parts
  - Methodological reductionism is scientific strategy of studying wholes through parts
  - Arguments of Nagel (1974), Jackson (1982), and Chalmers (1996) against physical reducibility of consciousness
- **Tenet alignment**: Aligns—distinguishing types clarifies site's position (ontological anti-reductionism)
- **Quote**: "There are many less general arguments that have been advanced to show that one particular kind of science cannot be reduced, for example, the arguments of Thomas Nagel (1979), Frank Jackson (1982), and David Chalmers' (1996) against the physical reducibility of consciousness."

### Stanford Encyclopedia - Multiple Realizability
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/multiple-realizability/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Putnam (1960s): if mental kind is multiply realizable by distinct physical kinds, cannot be identical to any specific physical kind
  - Pain is not identical to C-fibre firing because alien beings, robots, silicon life might share psychological states
  - Fodor extended to all special sciences—psychology, economics not reducible to physics
  - Bridge laws between psychology and neuroscience not achieved
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports site's anti-reductionism about consciousness
- **Quote**: "If a given mental kind is multiply realizable by distinct physical kinds, then it cannot be identical to any specific physical kind."

### Joseph Levine - The Explanatory Gap
- **URL**: https://www.informationphilosopher.com/solutions/philosophers/levine/Explanatory_Gap.pdf
- **Type**: Original paper
- **Key points**:
  - "Pain is the firing of C fibers" is valid physiologically but doesn't explain how pain feels
  - Explanatory gap: even if consciousness is physical, it cannot be *explained* in physical terms
  - The gap is in our understanding, not necessarily in nature (Levine remains agnostic on ontology)
  - Chalmers extends: the gap shows consciousness is not physical
- **Tenet alignment**: Central to Dualism tenet—explanatory gap supports irreducibility
- **Quote**: "The explanatory gap argument doesn't demonstrate a gap in nature, but a gap in our understanding of nature."

### Ernest Nagel - Theory Reduction Model
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/scientific-reduction/ (discussed within)
- **Type**: Classic model
- **Key points**:
  - One theory reduced to another when axioms derivable with "bridge principles"
  - Bridge laws connect vocabulary of reduced theory to reducing theory
  - Example: thermodynamics reduced to statistical mechanics via temperature = mean kinetic energy
  - Bridge laws must be identities for genuine reduction (not mere correlations)
  - Attempts to find bridge laws between psychology and neuroscience have failed
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports site—no psycho-physical bridge laws found
- **Quote**: "A reduction is effected when the experimental laws of the secondary science are shown to be the logical consequences of the theoretical assumptions of the primary science."

### Jaegwon Kim - Exclusion Argument
- **URL**: https://books.google.com/books/about/Physicalism_or_Something_Near_Enough.html
- **Type**: Philosophical monograph
- **Key points**:
  - Exclusion argument: physical causes are sufficient → mental causes overdetermine → must reduce mental to physical
  - Non-reductive physicalism is unstable—forced to accept overdetermination or epiphenomenalism
  - Downward causation is inconsistent with causal closure
  - Kim argues mental causation preserved only via reduction
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts—but site responds by denying causal closure at quantum level
- **Quote**: "Mental causation can be preserved only by embracing a reductionist view, whereby mental properties are considered causally efficacious by being reduced to physical properties."

### Daniel Dennett - Greedy Reductionism
- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greedy_reductionism
- **Type**: Concept from *Darwin's Dangerous Idea*
- **Key points**:
  - "Greedy reductionism" = skipping explanatory levels in rush to foundational physics
  - Good reductionism uses "cranes" (legitimate mechanisms); greedy reductionism wants no cranes
  - Middle ground between abandoning reductionism and greedy reductionism
  - Even Dennett, a reductionist, acknowledges limits
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports caution about over-aggressive reduction; site goes further (ontological anti-reductionism)
- **Quote**: "Good reductionists suppose that all Design can be explained without skyhooks; greedy reductionists suppose it can all be explained without cranes."

### IEP - Hard Problem of Consciousness
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/hard-problem-of-conciousness/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Hard problem: why and how do organisms have qualia, phenomenal consciousness?
  - Easy problems (discrimination, integration, behavioral functions) amenable to functional explanation
  - Chalmers: any reductive explanation in purely physical terms must fail
  - Zombie argument: conceivable micro-physical duplicates without consciousness
  - Reductionists (Dennett) argue hard problem reduces to easy problems or derives from misconceptions
- **Tenet alignment**: Central to Dualism tenet
- **Quote**: "Chalmers argues that any reductive explanation of consciousness in purely physical terms must fail."

### Stanford Encyclopedia - Emergent Properties
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/properties-emergent/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Weak emergence: compatible with reduction and deducibility
  - Strong emergence: fundamentally different type of stuff; non-redundant causal contributor
  - Strong emergentists deny Physical Causal Closure
  - Crane: non-reductive physicalism requires in-principle explanatory reduction; emergentism denies this
- **Tenet alignment**: Site is strong emergentist—consciousness has novel causal powers
- **Quote**: "Strong emergence occurs when the emergent phenomenon is of a fundamentally different type of stuff than is the stuff it emerges from."

## Major Positions

### Position 1: Reductive Physicalism (Type Identity Theory)
- **Proponents**: Place, Smart, Armstrong; contemporary: Kim (partially)
- **Core claim**: Mental states are identical to brain states. Pain = C-fibre firing.
- **Key arguments**: Parsimony, causal closure, scientific success
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts with Dualism tenet.** If mental = physical, consciousness is reducible.

### Position 2: Non-Reductive Physicalism
- **Proponents**: Davidson, Fodor, Putnam (early), most contemporary philosophers of mind
- **Core claim**: Mental supervenes on physical but is not reducible to it. Multiple realizability blocks reduction.
- **Key arguments**: Same mental state can be realized by different physical substrates
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Partially aligns but insufficient.** Accepts irreducibility but struggles with causal efficacy (Kim's exclusion argument). Site requires causal powers, not just supervenience.

### Position 3: Eliminative Materialism
- **Proponents**: Churchlands, Dennett (sometimes classified here)
- **Core claim**: Folk psychology (beliefs, desires, qualia) will be eliminated by completed neuroscience
- **Key arguments**: Folk theories often get eliminated (phlogiston, vital force)
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Conflicts with Dualism tenet.** Denies qualia exist as irreducible phenomena.

### Position 4: Methodological Reductionism Only
- **Proponents**: Many working scientists, pragmatist philosophers
- **Core claim**: Reductionist methods useful for science; agnostic on ontology
- **Key arguments**: Studying parts illuminates wholes; no commitment to ontological claims
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Compatible.** Site can accept methodological reductionism while rejecting ontological reduction.

### Position 5: Strong Emergentism / Property Dualism
- **Proponents**: Chalmers, O'Connor, Wong; historically Broad, Alexander
- **Core claim**: Consciousness genuinely emerges with novel causal powers not deducible from physics
- **Key arguments**: Explanatory gap, conceivability of zombies, hard problem
- **Relation to site tenets**: **Aligns closely.** Site's position is emergentist dualism with quantum mechanism.

## Key Debates

### Debate 1: Does the Explanatory Gap Indicate an Ontological Gap?
- **Sides**: Levine (epistemological gap only, ontology open) vs Chalmers (gap shows consciousness not physical)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether our inability to explain consciousness physically reflects limits of understanding or limits of physics
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Site sides with Chalmers—the gap is ontological.

### Debate 2: Does Multiple Realizability Block Reduction?
- **Sides**: Putnam/Fodor (yes) vs Kim (disjunctive reductions possible)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether mental predicates can be reduced to disjunctions of physical predicates
- **Current state**: Multiple realizability widely accepted as challenge; Kim's response controversial.

### Debate 3: Kim's Exclusion Argument
- **Sides**: Kim (non-reductive physicalism collapses to reduction or epiphenomenalism) vs defenders (various responses)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether mental causation is possible without reduction
- **Current state**: Central challenge to non-reductive views. Site responds: mental causation at quantum incompleteness.

### Debate 4: Greedy vs Legitimate Reductionism
- **Sides**: Dennett (middle ground—cranes all the way) vs anti-reductionists (some levels irreducible)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether consciousness requires "skyhooks" or just better cranes
- **Current state**: Dennett's position influential among naturalists; site holds consciousness requires more than cranes.
