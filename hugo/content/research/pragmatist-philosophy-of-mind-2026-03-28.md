## Executive Summary

Classical pragmatism (Peirce, James, Dewey) offers unexpectedly rich resources for the Map's dualist project. While most pragmatists rejected substance dualism, their core commitments — irreducibility of experience, indeterminism, reflexive self-undermining of eliminativism — provide independent lines of argument that converge with the Map's tenets. The site already has strong pragmatism coverage (concepts/pragmatism, topics/pragmatisms-path-to-dualism, topics/william-james-consciousness), but several other articles could be enhanced with pragmatist connections. Key opportunities: Peirce's tychism as historical precedent for the quantum indeterminism tenet, James's panpsychist sympathies as context for the prebiotic collapse discussion, the pragmatist-to-enactivist pipeline as background for embodied cognition articles, and Putnam's trajectory away from functionalism as evidence against that framework.

### Stanford Encyclopedia — Neutral Monism
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/neutral-monism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - James's radical empiricism as paradigm of neutral monism — "pure experience" as neither mental nor physical
  - In "Does Consciousness Exist?" (1904), James argued consciousness is not a substance but a function within pure experience
  - This is not eliminativism: James preserved the reality of experience while questioning the mental/physical dichotomy
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with substance dualism but shares the Map's insistence that experience is real and irreducible. James's framework is a sophisticated alternative that the Map should engage with more explicitly
- **Quote**: "There is one fundamental 'stuff' that is neither material nor mental"

### Stanford Encyclopedia — William James
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/james/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Stream of consciousness: consciousness is continuous, personal, selective — not discrete atomic mental states
  - James's "Will to Believe" doctrine permits holding metaphysical beliefs when evidence is genuinely ambiguous
  - His pragmatic criterion of truth evaluates beliefs by their practical consequences, including for the believer's capacity for inquiry
- **Tenet alignment**: Strong alignment with Tenet 5 (Occam's limits) — James's arguments against premature closure parallel the Map's case. Also supports Tenet 3 (bidirectional interaction) through voluntary attention doctrine

### Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy — John Dewey
- **URL**: https://iep.utm.edu/john-dewey/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - Dewey rejected dualism between being and experience, treating mind as "part and parcel of nature"
  - Inquiry begins with obstacles to action, not passive observation — knowledge is active adaptation
  - Dewey's naturalism eliminates the spectator theory of knowledge
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with dualism directly. However, Dewey's emphasis on active engagement (vs. passive observation) parallels the Map's bidirectional interaction tenet. The brain-as-organ-of-action view resonates with the Map's rejection of epiphenomenalism

### Stanford Encyclopedia — Charles Sanders Peirce
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/peirce/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia
- **Key points**:
  - **Tychism**: absolute chance is a real factor in the universe — indeterminism is ontological, not merely epistemic
  - **Synechism**: continuity is fundamental — denies mind-matter dualism but also denies pure materialism
  - "The Law of Mind" (1892): ideas spread, affect other ideas, lose intensity but gain generality
  - Peirce's cosmological evolution: law develops out of chance, consciousness is a factor reinstated through tychism
- **Tenet alignment**: Tychism strongly aligns with Tenet 2 (minimal quantum interaction) as a philosophical predecessor. Peirce's claim that "consciousness is not to be reinstated without tychism" directly parallels the Map's argument that quantum indeterminism opens space for mental causation
- **Quote**: "The positive part of the argument shows what can be done to reinstate consciousness as a factor of the universe when once tychism is admitted"

- **URLs**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neopragmatism, https://iep.utm.edu/rorty/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia/Reference
- **Key points**:
  - Rorty: neither mind nor language "mirrors" nature — eliminativism about mental representation
  - Rorty's holism refuses one-to-one identity between brain states and mental states

- **Tenet alignment**: Rorty's anti-representationalism conflicts with the Map's realism about consciousness. However, his rejection of reductive identity theory between brain and mind indirectly supports dualism's critique of type-identity physicalism

### Britannica — Hilary Putnam / IEP — Functionalism

## Major Positions

### Classical Pragmatist Philosophy of Mind (James, Peirce, Dewey)
- **Proponents**: William James, Charles Sanders Peirce, John Dewey
- **Core claim**: Mental phenomena must be understood through their practical consequences and functional roles in organism-environment interaction. Experience is the fundamental category — not matter, not mind as substance, but lived experience
- **Key arguments**:
  - James: stream of consciousness, radical empiricism, will to believe
  - Peirce: tychism (real chance), synechism (continuity), evolutionary cosmology
  - Dewey: organism-environment transaction, rejection of spectator theory, inquiry as active problem-solving
- **Relation to site tenets**: Mixed. Classical pragmatists rejected substance dualism but insisted on the irreducibility of experience. Their methods, reflexively applied, support dualism (as the Map already argues in topics/pragmatisms-path-to-dualism). Peirce's tychism is a direct philosophical predecessor of Tenet 2

### James's Radical Empiricism / Neutral Monism
- **Proponents**: William James, later Bertrand Russell, Ernst Mach
- **Core claim**: "Pure experience" is the fundamental stuff — neither mental nor physical. Mind and matter are functional distinctions within a single experiential stream
- **Key arguments**:
  - "Does Consciousness Exist?" (1904) — consciousness is a function, not a substance
  - Relations are directly experienced, not inferred
  - The mental/physical distinction is derivative, not fundamental
- **Relation to site tenets**: Conflicts with substance dualism (Tenet 1). However, shares the Map's core commitment to irreducibility of experience. The Map's existing coverage in topics/william-james-consciousness handles this well. Could be referenced more in the convergence argument discussion — even James's anti-dualist metaphysics preserves what matters most to the Map (irreducible experience)

### Neopragmatist Anti-Representationalism (Rorty)
- **Proponents**: Richard Rorty, later Huw Price
- **Core claim**: The "mirror of nature" metaphor — that mind represents external reality — should be abandoned. Philosophy should stop trying to ground knowledge in mental representation
- **Key arguments**:
  - Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature (1979): anti-foundationalism
  - Neither mind nor language "mirrors" external reality
  - Vocabularies are tools, not truth-pictures
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly conflicts with the Map's realism about consciousness. However, Rorty's destruction of reductive identity theory (no one-to-one brain-mind mapping) inadvertently supports the Map's case against type-identity physicalism

### Putnam's Pragmatic Realism
- **Proponents**: Hilary Putnam
- **Core claim**: Moving from metaphysical realism through internal realism to pragmatic direct realism. Mental states are real, externally constituted, and not reducible to functional roles
- **Key arguments**:
  - Multiple realizability (functionalism phase) — mental states are not type-identical to brain states
  - Semantic externalism — "meanings ain't in the head"
  - Self-refutation of functionalism — Putnam abandoned his own creation
  - Pragmatic realism — direct engagement with reality, influenced by James
- **Relation to site tenets**: Putnam's trajectory is highly useful for the Map. His functionalism provided the most powerful argument against type-identity (supporting Tenet 1), then his abandonment of functionalism itself shows the limits of any purely structural account of mind

### Pragmatist-Enactivist Synthesis
- **Proponents**: Evan Thompson, Francisco Varela, Alva Noë; with pragmatist lineage through Dewey and Merleau-Ponty
- **Core claim**: Cognition is embodied, embedded, enacted, extended (4E). The pragmatist emphasis on organism-environment transaction is the historical root of this program
- **Key arguments**:
  - Dewey's organism-environment interaction → autopoietic enactivism
  - Habits of mind → embodied cognition
  - Non-representational cognition → radical enactivism
- **Relation to site tenets**: Enactivism typically opposes dualism, but the Map's existing article (topics/enactivism-challenge-to-interactionist-dualism) argues enactivism's own irreducibility commitments support dualist conclusions. Tracing enactivism's pragmatist roots strengthens this argument: if pragmatism's own reflexive logic supports dualism, then enactivism inherits that support

## Key Debates

### Pragmatism and the Mind-Body Problem: Dissolution vs. Solution
- **Sides**: Dewey/Rorty (dissolve the problem — it rests on bad assumptions) vs. James/Peirce (transform the problem — experience and indeterminism reframe it)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the mind-body problem is a genuine metaphysical puzzle or an artifact of Cartesian framing
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The dissolution strategy dominates contemporary neopragmatism, but the Map argues (correctly, given its tenets) that the hard problem survives dissolution attempts

### Functionalism: Creation and Abandonment
- **Sides**: Early Putnam (for) vs. Later Putnam, Searle, Block, Chalmers (against, on different grounds)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether functional organization is sufficient for mental states
- **Current state**: Functionalism remains the default in cognitive science but is under sustained philosophical attack. Putnam's own defection is the most dramatic development — the architect found the building uninhabitable

### Tychism and Quantum Indeterminacy
- **Sides**: Peirce (indeterminism is ontological) vs. determinists (apparent randomness is epistemic)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether chance is real or merely reflects ignorance
- **Current state**: Quantum mechanics vindicated Peirce's tychism decades after he proposed it. This is now the majority physics position (Copenhagen, QBism) though deterministic interpretations (Bohm, Everett) persist

### Pragmatism's Relationship to Panpsychism
- **Sides**: James (sympathetic but raised combination problem) vs. Peirce (psycho-physical monism — mind suffuses universe) vs. Dewey (naturalist, no panpsychism)
- **Core disagreement**: Whether experience is universal or emergent
- **Current state**: Revival of interest due to hard problem persistence. James's combination problem objection remains the central challenge
