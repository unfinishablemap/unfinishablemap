## Key Sources

### Occasionalism (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy) — deepened extraction
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/occasionalism/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia. Author: Sukjae Lee (Seoul National University) — verified via SEP's author/citation page and PhilPapers record. First published 2008-10-20, substantive revision 2019-10-17.
- **Key points beyond the 2026-07-12 note**:
  - **Global vs local occasionalism**: the entry distinguishes *global* occasionalism (no creaturely causation anywhere; Malebranche's mature position) from *local* occasionalism (divine causation replaces creaturely causation only in restricted domains — e.g. body-body or body-mind causation — while genuine creaturely causes persist elsewhere). This distinction is essential for the article: several "occasionalists" were local occasionalists.
  - **La Forge**: first to employ the term "occasional cause"; argued from conservation — when God conserves a body He must conserve it *somewhere*, so God's conservation activity already includes producing its spatial modes, leaving no motive work for bodies. Notably he **preserved mental causation** (local, not global, occasionalism) — matches the Map's existing survey wording.
  - **Cordemoy**: argued bodies are not real causes via the premise that motion initiated by an agent can only be continued by that same agent; since bodies manifestly do not sustain their own motion, God alone moves bodies.
  - **Geulincx**: the epistemic axiom — *quod nescis quomodo fiat, id non facis* ("what you do not know how to bring about, you do not do"). SEP renders the Metaphysica vera statement as "it is impossible to bring about something when one does not know how it is brought about". Since we do not know how our volitions produce arm-raisings (we don't know the neurophysiology), we are not their causes. This is the "No Knowledge" argument in its purest form; the entry notes Geulincx and some Islamic occasionalists pressed it as a fully general condition on efficacy.
  - **Malebranche's true-cause definition — quote status upgraded**: the 2026-07-12 note flagged this quote as unverified. This session re-verified it against the live SEP entry: "A true cause as I understand it is one such that the mind perceives a necessary connection between it and its effect" — cited by SEP to *Search* 450 (OCM II 316). Status: verbatim per SEP's rendering with a precise page citation; still SEP's English (Lennon & Olscamp-based), so an article should cite it as "Search After Truth, trans. Lennon & Olscamp, p. 450, as quoted in the SEP entry" or paraphrase.
  - **al-Ghazali caution reconfirmed**: recent scholarship reads the Seventeenth Discussion as defending the *possibility* of miracles rather than endorsing global occasionalism; "some kind of genuine causality in creatures" is left open. Do not present al-Ghazali or the Ash'arite school as unambiguously global occasionalists.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction). The global/local distinction gives the article a precise way to state the Map's contrast: the Map is at the opposite pole from global occasionalism, but even *local* occasionalism about mind-body causation concedes exactly what Tenet 3 refuses to concede.

### Nadler, *Occasionalism: Causation Among the Cartesians* (OUP 2011)
- **URL**: https://philpapers.org/rec/NADOCA ; review of record: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1467-9213.2012.00054.x
- **Type**: Book (the standard modern scholarly treatment). Publisher-verified: Oxford University Press, 2011, ISBN 9780198250081; the Philosophical Quarterly review (Smith 2012) records pp. xii + 207.
- **Key points**:
  - Collects Nadler's 1993-2004 articles on occasional causation in Arnauld, Cordemoy, La Forge, Descartes, Geulincx, and Malebranche, plus occasionalism's influence on Leibniz and Hume.
  - Frames occasionalism as a serious philosophical response to genuine problems in Cartesian metaphysics — not (per the older caricature) an ad hoc "Deus ex machina" patch for the mind-body problem. Nadler's revisionist point: occasionalism was primarily a doctrine about *causation in general* (especially body-body causation), with mind-body traffic as one application. An article that presents occasionalism as merely a mind-body dodge repeats the caricature Nadler corrects.
- **Related**: Nadler (ed.), *Causation in Early Modern Philosophy: Cartesianism, Occasionalism, and Preestablished Harmony* (Penn State Press, 1993) — https://philpapers.org/rec/NADCIE — useful anthology framing all three rivals together.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral (scholarship). Supplies the historiographical guardrail for the article.

- **Type**: Primary text, modern edition. Publisher-verified: eds. Nicholas Jolley & David Scott, Cambridge Texts in the History of Philosophy, Cambridge University Press, 1997 (ISBN 9780521574020 hb / 9780521574358 pb). Original French: *Entretiens sur la métaphysique et sur la religion*, 1688.

### Harding, "Causality Then and Now: Al Ghazali and Quantum Theory" (1993)
- **URL**: https://www.ajis.org/index.php/ajiss/article/view/2505 (journal of record); PDF mirror: https://www.ghazali.org/articles/harding-V10N2-Summer-93.pdf
- **Type**: Journal article. Publisher-verified: *American Journal of Islamic Social Sciences* (now titled *American Journal of Islam and Society*) 10(2), 1993, pp. 165-177.
- **Key points**: The earliest widely-cited "quantum occasionalism" comparison: parallels between al-Ghazali's denial of necessary natural causation (causal regularities as God's habits) and the Copenhagen interpretation's break with classical causality. An analogy paper, not a mechanism proposal.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral/illustrative. Credibility sorting done this session: beyond Harding, the "quantum occasionalism" search space is dominated by blogs and devotional sites (not citable). The citable contemporary line is the divine-action literature below.

### Quantum divine action: Plantinga's divine collapse-causation and Russell's NIODA
- **URL**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/14746700.2022.2155914 (critical engagement with Russell, *Theology and Science*, 2022); https://philarchive.org/archive/SILACA-5 (Silva, European Journal for Philosophy of Religion 7/4, 2015, on Plantinga)
- **Type**: Books/papers. Alvin Plantinga, *Where the Conflict Really Lies: Science, Religion, and Naturalism* (Oxford University Press, 2011) — "divine collapse-causation" (DCC): whenever a quantum collapse occurs, God decides its outcome; Plantinga favors spontaneous-collapse (GRW-style) framings over Copenhagen. Robert John Russell's NIODA (non-interventionist objective divine action): God objectively acts by determining otherwise-underdetermined quantum events, without violating any physical law.
- **Key points**:
  - These are the *serious* modern descendants of occasionalism's causal structure: an immaterial agent supplies the determination that physics leaves open, at the quantum joint, without energy injection or law violation.
  - **This is the sharpest dialectical material for the Map.** DCC/NIODA use the *same causal joint* as Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction) but assign the selecting to God rather than to creaturely minds. Occasionalism's modern form thus concedes the Map's key physical claim (quantum indeterminacy is a real, law-respecting opening for non-physical causation) while re-running the classical occasionalist move of relocating agency to God. The Map's reply mirrors Arnauld's classical objection and adds the modern cost: routing every determination through God explains too much (and so nothing), erases creaturely mental causation (Tenet 3), and pays parsimony costs Tenet 5 warns about — an omni-determining agent at every collapse versus minimal creaturely selection at few.
  - Also useful in reverse: critics of the Map's mechanism sometimes deploy anti-DCC arguments (e.g. episodicity, the problem of evil for an all-determining God, measurement-problem dependence); the article can show which transfer to creaturely selection and which do not.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 3 (divine, not creaturely, agency) while structurally *agreeing* with Tenet 2's physics. That asymmetry is the article's core payoff.

### New position-level material this session
- **Global occasionalism** (mature Malebranche): no creaturely causation at all. The pure foil for Tenet 3.
- **Local occasionalism** (La Forge on body-body causation while preserving mental causation; arguably al-Ghazali on natural causation): divine causation in restricted domains. Shows the position space is graded — and that the *mind-body* application was historically separable from the general doctrine.
- **Quantum occasionalism / divine action** (Plantinga DCC, Russell NIODA): God as the selector of quantum outcomes. Same joint as the Map's mechanism, different agent; the living version of the rival.

## Key Debates (additions only)

### Is "quantum occasionalism" credible philosophy or apologetics?
- **Sides**: Harding 1993 and the divine-action literature (Russell, Plantinga, the Vatican/CTNS "Scientific Perspectives on Divine Action" project) treat the quantum joint seriously in peer-reviewed venues; a large popular/blog literature overextends the analogy (entanglement, many-worlds, simulation).
- **Core disagreement**: whether quantum indeterminacy is a metaphysically real opening for non-physical determination (the Map and DCC/NIODA both say yes) or a temporary artifact of an unsolved measurement problem.
- **Current state**: Active in philosophy of religion / theology-and-science. For the article: cite Plantinga 2011, Russell (e.g. the 2022 Theology and Science engagement), Harding 1993; do not cite blogs.

### Was occasionalism ever primarily about the mind-body problem?
- **Sides**: The textbook framing (occasionalism = mind-body dodge) vs Nadler's correction (a general theory of causation, with body-body causation the central case for several Cartesians).
- **Current state**: Nadler's reading is standard among specialists. The article should present occasionalism as a general causal doctrine that *entails* a mind-body position, and flag the caricature.
