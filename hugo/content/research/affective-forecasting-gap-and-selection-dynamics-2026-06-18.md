## Executive Summary

The **affective-forecasting gap** is the systematic divergence between *anticipated* valence (how good or bad we predict an option will feel) and *experienced* valence (how it actually feels). Three decades of work by Timothy Wilson and Daniel Gilbert document a robust **impact bias**: people overestimate the intensity and especially the *duration* of their future emotional reactions, driven by **immune neglect** (failing to anticipate the psychological immune system that dampens negative affect) and **focalism** (over-attending to the focal event, ignoring everything else that will also bear on mood). Kahneman's parallel programme distinguishes **decision utility** (the weight an outcome carries in choice), **experienced utility** (real-time hedonic quality), and **remembered utility** (the retrospective evaluation that actually feeds the next decision), and shows the last is governed by a **peak-end rule** with **duration neglect** rather than by the integral of moment-by-moment experience. Decision neuroscience supplies a mechanistic correlate: the midbrain dopamine signal is a **reward-prediction error** — a teaching/prediction signal, not a hedonic "pleasure" signal — and Berridge's **wanting/liking dissociation** shows the motivational pull that *guides choice* ("wanting") is neurally separable from the hedonic impact that *is the felt good* ("liking").

For the Map this is article-worthy for one precise reason. The corpus's value-sensitive horn ([valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/)) holds that *felt value guides choice*; but selection must run on valence assessed *before* an outcome is actualised — i.e., on **anticipated** valence. The forecasting literature shows anticipated valence systematically diverges from experienced valence. So "felt value guides selection" is true, if at all, in a falsifiable and uncomfortable form: the valence that is *causally load-bearing in selection* is the predicted kind, which is exactly the kind that is least reliable and most mechanism-like. The value-blind steelman ([the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection](/topics/the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection/)) can cite this as evidence that selection runs on a *predictive mechanism* decoupled from actual felt value. The Map's reply must specify **which valence** it claims is causally load-bearing, and own the cost of either answer. This note assembles the verified literature and lays out the argument structure; it does not write the article.

## Key Sources

### Wilson & Gilbert (2003) — "Affective Forecasting" (foundational review)
- **Citation**: Wilson, T. D., & Gilbert, D. T. (2003). Affective forecasting. *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 35, 345–411.
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — volume 35, pp. 345–411 corroborated across Google Scholar lookup, Harvard (dtg.sites.fas.harvard.edu) author-hosted PDF, and the Harvard DASH publication record.
- **Type**: Full-length review chapter (the canonical statement of the programme)
- **Key points**:
  - Coins/consolidates the **impact bias**: forecasters overestimate the intensity and duration of future affect.
  - Catalogues sources: immune neglect, focalism, misconstrual, inaccurate theories, motivated distortion.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral as data; the *interpretation* (predictive valence ≠ felt valence) is what bears on the value-sensitive/value-blind fork.

### Wilson & Gilbert (2005) — "Affective Forecasting: Knowing What to Want"
- **Citation**: Wilson, T. D., & Gilbert, D. T. (2005). Affective forecasting: Knowing what to want. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 14(3), 131–134. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.0963-7214.2005.00355.x
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 14, pp. 131–134, DOI confirmed via SAGE journals listing and SciRP reference index; author PDF on dtg.sites.fas.harvard.edu.
- **Type**: Short review (the accessible précis)
- **Key points**:
  - Decisions rest on affective forecasts; the impact bias means we routinely mispredict.
  - Names **focalism** as a primary cause: underestimating how much *other* events will shape our feelings.
- **Quote (paraphrase of abstract)**: people "display an impact bias, overestimating the intensity and duration of their emotional reactions" to future events.

### Gilbert, Pinel, Wilson, Blumberg & Wheatley (1998) — "Immune neglect"
- **Citation**: Gilbert, D. T., Pinel, E. C., Wilson, T. D., Blumberg, S. J., & Wheatley, T. P. (1998). Immune neglect: A source of durability bias in affective forecasting. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 75(3), 617–638.
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 75, pp. 617–638 corroborated via Harvard author-hosted PDF, Semantic Scholar, and the coglab/dtg Harvard publications page.
- **Type**: Empirical (6 studies)
- **Key points**:
  - **Durability bias**: people overestimate how *long* negative affect will last.
  - Cause is **immune neglect** — forecasters fail to anticipate the "psychological immune system" (rationalisation, reframing) that ameliorates negative affect. Across 6 events (breakup, tenure denial, electoral loss, negative feedback, reading of a child's death, job rejection) participants over-predicted duration.
- **Bearing on the Map**: a concrete mechanism by which *anticipated* negative valence overshoots *experienced* negative valence — the gap is not noise but a structured, predictable bias.

### Wilson, Wheatley, Meyers, Gilbert & Axsom (2000) — "Focalism"
- **Citation**: Wilson, T. D., Wheatley, T. P., Meyers, J. M., Gilbert, D. T., & Axsom, D. (2000). Focalism: A source of durability bias in affective forecasting. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 78(5), 821–836.
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 78, pp. 821–836 corroborated via Harvard (scholar.harvard.edu/danielgilbert) PDF and ResearchGate record.
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Key points**:
  - **Focalism**: forecasters fixate on the focal event and neglect that many other things will also influence mood, inflating the predicted impact of the focal event.

### Gilbert & Wilson (2007) — "Prospection: Experiencing the Future"
- **Citation**: Gilbert, D. T., & Wilson, T. D. (2007). Prospection: Experiencing the future. *Science*, 317(5843), 1351–1354. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1144161 (PMID 17823345)
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 317, pp. 1351–1354, DOI and PMID confirmed via Science.org listing, PubMed, and Harvard author PDF.
- **Type**: Review (Science)
- **Key points**:
  - Frames forecasting as **prospection**: simulating ("previewing") future events to anticipate their affect. The *preview* is a mental representation that is systematically unrepresentative (truncated, decontextualised, present-biased), which is *why* the forecast diverges from the experience.
- **Bearing on the Map**: gives the anticipatory-valence mechanism a name and a failure-mode story. If selection runs on prospected valence, it inherits prospection's distortions.

### Kahneman, Wakker & Sarin (1997) — "Back to Bentham?"
- **Citation**: Kahneman, D., Wakker, P. P., & Sarin, R. (1997). Back to Bentham? Explorations of experienced utility. *The Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 112(2), 375–405. (JSTOR 2951240)
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 112(2), pp. 375–405 corroborated via Oxford Academic QJE listing, JSTOR, and Wakker's author-hosted PDF.
- **Type**: Theoretical (economics/psychology)
- **Key points**:
  - The load-bearing tripartite distinction: **decision utility** (weight in choice) vs **experienced utility** (real-time hedonic quality; "instant utility" integrated over time) vs **remembered utility** (retrospective evaluation).
  - Documents that retrospective evaluation systematically departs from the integral of instant utility — and that *remembered* utility, not experienced utility, drives subsequent decisions, which "can induce a preference for dominated options."
- **Bearing on the Map**: this is the cleanest formal map of *which* valence is doing *which* job. The Map's "felt value guides choice" is, in Kahneman's terms, a claim that *experienced* utility should be load-bearing — but choice empirically runs on *decision* utility shaped by *remembered* utility.

### Redelmeier & Kahneman (1996) — peak-end / duration neglect
- **Citation**: Redelmeier, D. A., & Kahneman, D. (1996). Patients' memories of painful medical treatments: real-time and retrospective evaluations of two minimally invasive procedures. *Pain*, 66(1), 3–8. https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-3959(96)02994-6 (PMID 8857625)
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 66(1), pp. 3–8, DOI and PMID confirmed via PubMed, ScienceDirect, and journals.lww.com (Pain).
- **Type**: Empirical (colonoscopy n=154, lithotripsy n=133)
- **Key points**:
  - Retrospective total-pain judgments track the **peak** and the **end** intensity, not the integral.
  - **Duration neglect**: total length of the episode has almost no effect on the remembered evaluation.
- **Bearing on the Map**: the remembered-utility signal that calibrates *future* anticipated valence is itself a non-faithful summary of experienced valence. The gap compounds: experience → (peak-end distorted) memory → (impact-biased) forecast → selection.

### Schultz (2016) — dopamine reward-prediction error
- **Citation**: Schultz, W. (2016). Dopamine reward prediction error coding. *Dialogues in Clinical Neuroscience*, 18(1), 23–32. https://doi.org/10.31887/DCNS.2016.18.1/wschultz (PMID 27069377)
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 18(1), pp. 23–32, DOI and PMID confirmed via PubMed, PMC, and Taylor & Francis.
- **Type**: Review (decision neuroscience)
- **Key points**:
  - Most midbrain dopamine neurons code a **reward-prediction error** (received minus predicted reward): activated by better-than-predicted, baseline for fully-predicted, depressed for worse-than-predicted.
  - The signal codes the **predictive** component (and an economic-utility-like value), not the hedonic/"pleasure" aspect.
- **Bearing on the Map**: the neural quantity that most plausibly *biases action selection* is explicitly a *prediction* signal, not an *experienced-pleasure* signal — the neuroscientific form of the value-blind steelman's "selection runs on a predictive mechanism."
- **Note**: the foundational Schultz, Dayan & Montague (1997) *Science* 275:1593–1599 paper is already verified and cited in [the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection](/topics/the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection/); the 2016 review is the cleaner statement of the prediction-not-pleasure point.

### Berridge & Robinson (2016) — wanting/liking
- **Citation**: Berridge, K. C., & Robinson, T. E. (2016). Liking, wanting, and the incentive-sensitization theory of addiction. *American Psychologist*, 71(8), 670–679. https://doi.org/10.1037/amp0000059
- **Verification**: VERIFIED — vol 71(8), pp. 670–679, DOI confirmed via Berridge Lab author-hosted PDF, PMC, and the AP listing. **Title corrected**: it is incentive-*sensitization*, not incentive-*salience* (a recurring mis-title).
- **Type**: Review
- **Key points**:
  - **"Wanting"** (incentive salience, mesolimbic dopamine) and **"liking"** (hedonic impact, opioid hotspots) are neurally dissociable; they can come apart (addiction: intense wanting of substances no longer liked).
- **Bearing on the Map**: "wanting" is the *anticipatory/predictive* pull that guides choice; "liking" is the *experienced* hedonic good. The dissociation *is* the affective-forecasting gap at the motivational level — what drives selection (wanting) is separable from what is actually felt as good (liking). The Berridge (2009) *Inquiry* 52(4):378–398 paper already cited in [valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/) makes the same dissociation point and need not be re-verified.
- **Honest flag**: Berridge is a physicalist; "liking" for him is an objective sub-personal reaction. Do not enlist him as a value-realist *or* an eliminativist; the dissociation is empirical, the metaphysics is not his to settle (this discipline is already modelled in the steelman article).

### The contested-robustness sources (honesty requires these)
- **Levine, L. J., Lench, H. C., Kaplan, R. L., & Safer, M. A. (2012).** Accuracy and artifact: Reexamining the intensity bias in affective forecasting. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 103(4), 584–605. (PMID 22889075) — VERIFIED via PubMed. Argues the *intensity* component of the impact bias is partly a procedural artifact; the *duration* (durability) bias survives.
- **Wilson, T. D., & Gilbert, D. T. (2013).** The impact bias is alive and well. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 105(5), 740–748. (PMID 24219785) — VERIFIED via PubMed and Harvard author PDF. Rebuts Levine et al.'s meta-analysis.
- **Bearing on the Map**: the gap is real and well-replicated for *duration*; the *intensity* magnitude is contested. An honest article must not overstate the size of the gap — the load-bearing claim (anticipated ≠ experienced valence in a structured way) survives the debate even on Levine et al.'s reading, because *duration* misforecasting is conceded by both sides.

## Major Positions

### Anticipated-valence selection (the Map's value-sensitive horn, sharpened)
- **Core claim**: consciousness selects the outcome whose *anticipated* valence is highest (the formal sketch in [valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/): bias selection toward outcome with highest V(Oᵢ)).
- **The problem this note surfaces**: V(Oᵢ) must be an *anticipated* quantity, and anticipation is impact-biased, immune-neglecting, focalistic, and calibrated against peak-end-distorted memory. So a value-sensitive selection law denominated in anticipated valence will, predictably, sometimes select outcomes that feel *worse* than the alternative.
- **Relation to site tenets**: preserves [valence](/concepts/valence/) as real and load-bearing, but at the cost of conceding that the load-bearing valence is the *unreliable predictive* kind, not the *authoritative experienced* kind. This is a real concession, not a refutation.

### Value-blind / predictive-mechanism selection (the steelman, strengthened by this literature)
- **Core claim**: selection runs on a predictive/control signal (RPE, "wanting") that is decoupled from experienced hedonic value; "felt value" does no selective work.
- **The forecasting gap as evidence**: if the valence that guides choice diverges systematically from the valence that is felt, the parsimonious reading is that choice is governed by a *predictor* (a teaching signal / incentive-salience pull) and the *feeling* is a downstream or separate fact — exactly Schultz's "prediction not pleasure" and Berridge's wanting/liking.
- **Relation to site tenets**: the Map declines this on prior commitments (rejecting illusionism; holding [phenomenal-value-realism](/topics/phenomenal-value-realism/)), but per the steelman's discipline must *argue* against it. The forecasting gap is a genuine new exhibit *for* the rival, not a strawman.

### The disambiguation move (a candidate Map reply, not yet a position)
- **Core claim**: the value-sensitive horn should specify that the *experienced* valence is metaphysically authoritative (it constitutes value) while the *anticipated* valence is what is *causally* consulted in selection. The two are different roles for the same dimension; their divergence is a *calibration failure*, not evidence that value is value-blind.
- **Cost**: this concedes that selection is causally driven by a *fallible predictor of* value rather than by value itself — which is uncomfortably close to the value-blind reading at the level of the selection *law*, even if it differs at the level of *what value is*. The article must decide whether this distinction does real work or merely relocates the problem.

## Key Debates

### Which utility is load-bearing in choice?
- **Sides**: Kahneman's framework says *decision* utility (shaped by *remembered* utility) drives choice, and it diverges from *experienced* utility. The Map's value-sensitive horn wants *experienced* (felt) value to be load-bearing.
- **Core disagreement**: whether the valence that *guides* selection is the felt one or a predicted/remembered proxy for it.
- **Current state**: empirically, the proxy wins (people choose dominated options when remembered utility misleads). Philosophically open whether this *refutes* value-sensitivity or merely shows the currency is poorly calibrated.

### Is the impact bias real / how big?
- **Sides**: Wilson & Gilbert (robust, intensity + duration) vs Levine et al. (duration robust; intensity partly artifactual).
- **Current state**: ongoing; *duration* misforecasting conceded by both. The Map's argument should lean on the duration result, which is uncontested, rather than on contested intensity magnitudes.

### Is the dopamine RPE a value signal at all, or just a learning signal?
- **Sides**: Schultz (RPE codes an economic-utility-like *predictive* value) vs more deflationary "pure teaching signal" readings (already noted in the steelman via Dayan & Niv 2008).
- **Bearing**: either reading supports "predictive, not experienced-hedonic," which is the point the gap needs.

## Potential Article Angles

The natural home is a **concepts/** article (headroom confirmed: 264/300). Recommended title direction: *"The Affective-Forecasting Gap and the Selection Currency"* or *"Which Valence Selects? Anticipated vs Experienced Value."*

1. **The disambiguation article (recommended).** Thesis: the value-sensitive horn is under-specified until it names *which* valence is causally load-bearing; the forecasting literature forces the choice; the Map should say *anticipated* valence drives selection while *experienced* valence constitutes value, and own the resulting "fallible-predictor" cost. Aligns with [valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/) by closing its explicitly-flagged "anticipated vs experienced valence" open question (its §"Limits and Open Questions"). Connects to the [the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection](/topics/the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection/) by giving the steelman a new, fair exhibit (RPE/wanting as predictive mechanism) and then stating the Map's calibrated reply.
2. **The "calibration, not blindness" article.** Thesis: the gap shows the currency is *poorly calibrated*, not *absent* — value-sensitivity survives as a claim about the selection *criterion* even if the criterion is consulted via a fallible predictor. Risk: collapses toward the value-blind reading at the level of the selection law; the article must defend the distinction's load-bearingness.
3. **A testability-focused angle.** Convert the gap into discriminating predictions: if selection is value-sensitive-on-experienced-valence, manipulations that *correct* forecasts (e.g., defocusing/"de-biasing" interventions) should change which outcome is selected; if value-blind-on-prediction, debiasing the *feeling forecast* should not move selection that is driven by the RPE/wanting channel. This dovetails with the existing testability hooks in [valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/) and the discriminating battery referenced there.

## Relation to Site Perspective

- **[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: if consciousness causally selects, and selection is denominated in *anticipated* valence, then the interaction is real but routed through a fallible predictor. The article should not let this quietly narrow the tenet into near-epiphenomenalism of the *experienced* evaluative dimension — the same trap [valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/) already tracks for its value-blind horn.
- **[Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)**: a selection law reading off *anticipated* valence is no less minimal than one reading off attention; but if anticipated valence reduces to an RPE/wanting computation, the value-blind steelman's parsimony advantage strengthens. The Map's reply must be the prior-commitments reply (reject illusionism, hold phenomenal value realism), not a parsimony contest it would lose.
- **[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: this is the tenet that licenses declining the more-parsimonious predictive-mechanism reading despite the forecasting evidence — but only if the decline is *argued*. The forecasting gap is a real cost the value-sensitive horn must pay, not wave away.
- **[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)**: under MWI the gap is moot (all outcomes occur); the Map's commitment to genuine selection is what makes "which valence selects" a live, answerable question.

## Gaps in Research

- **No direct neuroscience of "anticipated phenomenal valence as a selection input."** The RPE and wanting literatures concern *behavioural/motivational* selection, not the Map's *quantum-underdetermined-outcome* selection. The mapping is interpretive; the article must flag that it borrows the *structure* (predictive ≠ experienced) not a ready-made mechanism.
- **The metaphysical status of predicted valence is unsettled** (already flagged as an open problem in [valence-and-conscious-selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/): "Does predicted valence have the same metaphysical status as experienced valence?"). This note sharpens but does not resolve it.
- **Cross-species scope** of the forecasting gap (does it apply to the marginal organisms of [marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection](/topics/marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection/)?) is not addressed here.
- **Did not independently re-read** the ScienceDirect/Elsevier full texts behind paywalls (standard 403 blocks); metadata triangulated across ≥2 independent sources per citation, including author-hosted PDFs and PubMed/DOI registries.

## Decline Assessment

**Verdict: worth a dedicated article; do not fold.** The subject is not a duplicate. The Berridge research note (2026-06-03) covers the *wanting/liking* dissociation; the steelman research note (2026-06-18) covers the *general* value-blind case (RPE, affordance competition, valence-as-inference, illusionism). Neither treats the **affective-forecasting gap proper** — impact bias, immune neglect, focalism, predicted/experienced/remembered utility, peak-end/duration neglect — and neither closes the *specific* open question both live articles explicitly flag ("anticipated vs experienced valence"). The subject (a) names a falsifiable structure (anticipated ≠ experienced valence), (b) supplies the steelman a fair new exhibit, and (c) forces the Map's value-sensitive horn to disambiguate which valence is causally load-bearing. That is a self-contained conceptual contribution, not padding.
