## Executive Summary

Recent research (2024-2025) reveals a striking dissociation between dopamine's roles in movement and reward that illuminates the attention-motor-quantum interface. A landmark October 2024 *Nature* study demonstrates that **tonic (slow) dopamine supports spontaneous movement** while **phasic (rapid) dopamine drives motivation and reward-seeking behavior**. Dopamine-deficient mice "can move" but "won't move"—they retain motor capacity but lose goal-directed initiative. This suggests dopamine functions not as motor fuel but as a **salience signal** that tags what matters, bridging the gap between wanting (motivation) and doing (action initiation).

The "wanting vs. liking" distinction (Berridge & Robinson) shows dopamine mediates *incentive salience*—making stimuli seem worth pursuing—rather than hedonic pleasure itself. This reconceptualizes dopamine's evolutionary role: connecting the perception of value with the initiation of action. For the quantum interface model, dopamine may modulate the **decision threshold** at which competing motor programs resolve, effectively setting the "stakes" for selection while consciousness determines which option crosses threshold.

## The "Wanting vs. Liking" Distinction

### Core Finding
Berridge and Robinson's work establishes that dopamine mediates "wanting" (incentive salience, motivation to pursue) rather than "liking" (hedonic pleasure, consummatory enjoyment):

- **Wanting**: Dopamine-dependent, anticipatory, approach-oriented
- **Liking**: Opioid-dependent, consummatory, in-the-moment pleasure

### Evidence from Parkinson's Disease
- Parkinson's patients with extensive dopamine depletion give normal hedonic ratings to sweet tastes
- Their "liking" is intact; their "wanting" is impaired
- Anhedonia in PD reflects anticipatory (wanting) deficits, not consummatory (liking) deficits
- Approximately 30-40% of PD patients have depression; anhedonia occurs at 7-45% rates
- Some medicated PD patients develop behavioral addictions (gambling, shopping)—dopamine excess creates pathological wanting

### Implications for Consciousness Framework
The wanting/liking distinction suggests dopamine tags stimuli as **worth pursuing** without determining whether pursuit succeeds. This matches the selection model: dopamine marks options as salient/valuable, while another mechanism (potentially consciousness via quantum selection) determines which salient option becomes action.

## Tonic vs. Phasic Dopamine

### Two Modes of Signaling
1. **Tonic dopamine**: Slow, irregular pacemaker activity; establishes background levels; may last seconds to minutes
2. **Phasic dopamine**: Short bursts of high-frequency spikes; rapid rise and fall; encodes reward prediction errors

### Functional Dissociation
- **Movement**: Requires tonic dopamine (baseline levels); phasic dopamine dispensable
- **Reward-guided behavior**: Requires phasic dopamine; motivation diminished without rapid bursts
- **Learning**: Phasic dopamine encodes prediction errors; tonic may modulate exploitation vs. exploration

### Motor Control Specifics
- Tonic dopamine in ventral striatum: determines response vigor over time
- Tonic dopamine in dorsal striatum: determines movement speed according to energetic costs
- The tonic/phasic distinction maps onto general activation vs. specific selection

## Dopamine and Salience

### Definition
Salience = the neural process by which certain stimuli or internal states gain prominence and preferentially capture cognitive and behavioral resources.

### Dopamine's Role
- Dopamine mediates the conversion of a neutral stimulus into an attractive or aversive entity
- Novelty, valence, and prediction errors all modulate dopaminergic response "on behalf of salience"
- The salience network (anterior cingulate, ventral anterior insula, amygdala, ventral striatum) shows reduced connectivity after dopamine depletion

### Connection to Attention
- Salience initiates tagging of relevant items (often unconsciously) to guide subsequent attentional deployment
- Improving salience only enhances attention when the object is also task-relevant (2024 research)
- This suggests a two-stage process: salience tagging (dopamine) → attentional selection (consciousness?)

## Connection to Quantum Interface Model

### The Selection Locus Problem
The attention-motor-quantum-interface model posits that consciousness selects among competing motor programs via quantum Zeno effects. Dopamine research illuminates WHERE and HOW this selection operates:

1. **Threshold Modulation**: Dopamine sets the decision threshold; consciousness determines which option crosses first
2. **Salience Marking**: Dopamine tags options as worth considering; consciousness chooses among marked options
3. **Timing Substrate**: Dopamine facilitates prokinetic theta rhythms that may provide the timing window for quantum selection

### The "Can Move / Won't Move" Distinction
Dopamine-deficient mice demonstrate that movement CAPACITY is separate from movement INITIATION:
- Without dopamine: motor circuitry intact, but no initiative to engage it
- This suggests the "will to act" is not reducible to motor computation

For the dualist framework:
- Neural computation generates motor options (unaffected by dopamine loss)
- Dopamine tags options as worth pursuing (salience)
- Consciousness selects which salient option to actualize (quantum interface)

### Joy and Movement: Why the Same Neurotransmitter?

The evolutionary question: why would one neurotransmitter mediate both reward/pleasure and movement initiation?

**Answer**: Because in natural environments, wanting and pursuing are inseparable. Dopamine connects VALUE PERCEPTION with ACTION INITIATION—it's the bridge between "this matters" and "go get it."

This supports the unified attention-motor interface: if attention is motor planning (PMTA), and dopamine marks salience for both perception and action, then a single dopaminergic system appropriately serves the unified interface.

### Phenomenological Connection
The research notes in [attention-motor-quantum-interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/) observe that willed attention and willed action share phenomenology (both feel effortful). Dopamine research adds: both also share salience—what we attend to and what we act toward are unified by what matters to us.

The "effort" may be the conscious engagement with the selection process, experienced across both domains because the underlying mechanism is shared.

## Implications for Site Tenets

### Dualism
The "can move / won't move" distinction suggests something beyond motor computation initiates action. Dopamine marks salience but doesn't select—something else chooses among salient options. The dualist framework positions consciousness as that selector.

### Minimal Quantum Interaction
Dopamine doesn't inject energy or generate movement—it modulates thresholds and marks salience. Consciousness operating through quantum selection would similarly modulate rather than generate, biasing which option crosses threshold rather than creating force.

### Bidirectional Interaction
Dopamine demonstrates clear brain-to-consciousness effects (no dopamine → behavioral unconsciousness). The framework predicts consciousness-to-brain effects via selection among dopamine-marked options.

### No Many Worlds
Under MWI, all salient options actualize in some branch. Dopamine's salience-marking would be causally inert—mere branch-discovery. The phenomenology of "wanting" (some things mattering more than others) would be illusion. Single-world interpretation preserves the face value: salience actually influences which singular outcome occurs.

### Occam's Razor Has Limits
A purely computational account of dopamine's dual role might seem simpler, but it leaves unexplained:
- Why movement capacity is preserved when initiative is lost
- Why behavioral "consciousness" tracks dopamine levels
- Why the same molecule serves perception and action

The quantum selection framework provides explanatory purchase on these puzzles.
