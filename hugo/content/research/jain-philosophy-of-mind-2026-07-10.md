## Executive Summary

Jainism is a classical Indian tradition (with Mahavira, the last Jina, c. 599–527 BCE) whose metaphysics is an explicit, thoroughgoing **substance dualism**: the world is built from two everlasting, uncreated, mutually independent categories — **jiva** (living, conscious substance, the soul) and **ajiva** (non-living, including **pudgala**, matter). Consciousness (**chetana**; more technically **upayoga**, experience, dividing into cognition *jnana* and perception *darshana*) is the *defining, intrinsic* mark of every jiva. Unlike Advaita Vedanta's single Atman/Brahman, Jainism is firmly **pluralist**: infinitely many souls, each eternal, beginningless, individually distinct, with a fixed cosmic quantity. Karma is conceived not as abstract moral desert but as **subtle physical matter** that literally sticks to and weighs down the soul (a strikingly literal mind-matter *interaction* model, mediated by the "glue" of the passions). Jivas are graded by sensory capacity — from one-sensed (**ekendriya**: plants, earth/water/fire/air bodies) up to five-sensed (**panchendriya**: humans, higher animals, gods) — a graded sentience map directly relevant to the Map's edge-of-sentience threads. Epistemically, **anekantavada** (many-sidedness), its linguistic method **syadvada** (sevenfold conditional predication), and **nayavada** (doctrine of standpoints) hold that reality is many-faceted and no single viewpoint captures the whole — resonant with Tenet 5.

For the Map, Jainism supplies a **fourth** independent classical-Indian tradition (beside Samkhya, Nyaya, Vedanta) converging on consciousness-irreducibility, but from a *substance-pluralist* premise. This strengthens the cross-traditional convergence argument the corpus already makes — WHILE the corpus's own **common-cause caveat** still fully applies (shared introspective/first-person method is not independent confirmation). Honor that caveat; do not cheerlead.

## Key Sources

### Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — "Jaina Philosophy"
- **URL**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/jaina-philosophy/
- **Type**: Encyclopedia (peer-reviewed)
- **Author / date**: Marie-Hélène Gorisse; first published 13 February 2023. VERIFIED (author confirmed via PhilPapers linked bibliography and Birmingham research listing).
- **Key points**:
  - Jaina metaphysics is "an atomist and dualist conception of the world" resting on two everlasting, uncreated, coexisting, independent categories: jiva (self) and ajiva (non-self).
  - "The defining characteristic of the self is experience (upayoga)," dividing into cognition (jnana) and indeterminate perception (darshana). The self is "unobstructed consciousness that concretely experiences cognition and perception."
  - "There are as many selves as there are living beings"; each self "is eternal … has no beginning nor end," has persisting self-identity even in liberation, and there is "a fixed amount of selves in the universe." Explicitly rejects both Buddhist no-self and Brahmanical monism.
  - Karma "is conceived as a subtle type of matter that fills all cosmic space"; "due to passions which act as a glue, karmic matter sticks to the self and obstructs its potency." Eight karma types, four harming (ghatiya) and four non-harming (aghatiya).
  - Jivas classified by senses one through five; only five-sensed humans can attain liberation ("a reasoned anthropocentrism").
  - Anekantavada: entities are at once persistent (as substance) and changing (as modes) — "Existence is endowed with production, decay and duration." Syadvada gives seven conditional predications (exists / does not exist / both / indescribable / and the three combinations). Nayavada is "a technical apparatus … like a meta-epistemology," with rival schools treated as radicalized *partial* perspectives rather than simple errors.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly aligns with Tenet 1 (dualism) and Tenet 5 (limits of single-framework parsimony). The karma-as-matter model is a thematic parallel to Tenet 3 (bidirectional interaction), NOT a mechanistic anticipation of it.
- **Quote**: "The defining characteristic of the self is experience (upayoga)."

### Tattvartha Sutra (Tattvarthasutra) — Umasvati / Umasvamin
- **Type**: Primary Jain text (systematic treatise)
- **Attribution / date**: Umasvati (a.k.a. Umasvamin), c. 150–400 CE; SEP calls it "the oldest extant Jaina treatise in Sanskrit" and "the first work and main representative of classical Jaina philosophy." Accepted across both Svetambara and Digambara sects.
- **Key points**: Canonical systematization of the seven/nine tattvas (reals), the jiva/ajiva division, and the karma-influx (asrava) → bondage (bandha) → stoppage (samvara) → liberation (moksha) sequence.
- **Note for writers**: This is the primary source; keep it clearly distinct from secondary scholarship below.

## Major Positions

### Jiva/Ajiva Substance Dualism (Jain)
- **Proponents**: Umasvati (Tattvartha Sutra); classical Jain tradition; expositors Jaini, Johnson, Gorisse.
- **Core claim**: Reality divides into two independent, eternal substance-categories. Jiva is *intrinsically* conscious (consciousness is not an emergent add-on but the very definition of the soul-substance); ajiva/pudgala is intrinsically insentient.
- **Key arguments**: Consciousness (upayoga) is a per-se attribute with no material analogue; matter can obstruct but never *produce* it. Souls are known through the immediacy of cognition/perception.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct fit with Tenet 1 (consciousness not reducible to the physical). Stronger than Western *property* dualism because jiva is a distinct *substance*, and distinct from Samkhya because in Jainism the soul is genuinely an *agent/knower* embroiled in matter, not the passive witness (purusha) of Samkhya. (Cross-ref existing cluster: indian-philosophy-of-mind, samkhya-three-way-distinction, interaction-problem-across-traditions.)

### Plurality of Eternal Souls (vs monistic Atman)
- **Proponents**: Classical Jainism.
- **Core claim**: Infinitely many numerically distinct, beginningless, eternal jivas; individuality is preserved even at liberation (siddhas remain distinct). Contrast with Advaita's one Atman = Brahman.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Consonant with Tenet 4's emphasis that indexical/individual identity matters (the Map rejects identity-dissolving monism/MWI-style views). A substance-pluralist metaphysic is a natural conversation partner for the Map's insistence that *this* subject is not interchangeable. (Handle as thematic resonance, not a derivation.)

### Karma as Subtle Matter (literal mind-matter interaction)
- **Proponents**: Tattvartha tradition; analyzed by Johnson (*Harmless Souls*).
- **Core claim**: Karma is pudgala — actual subtle material particles pervading space that adhere to the soul when the "passions" (kashaya) make it sticky, obscuring its innate omniscience and driving rebirth. Liberation is the *physical shedding* (nirjara) of accumulated karmic matter.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A historical *thematic* parallel to Tenet 3 (bidirectional causal interaction between the mental and physical) — soul and matter genuinely act on each other. IMPORTANT SCOPING: present as a pre-modern interaction *model*, NOT as anticipating quantum interaction or any mechanism. Do not overstate.

### Anekantavada / Syadvada / Nayavada (epistemic pluralism)
- **Proponents**: Jain logicians (Siddhasena, Akalanka); modern readings by Balcerowicz ("relativity"), Matilal ("synthesis"), Ganeri ("syncretism"), Mookerjee ("non-absolutism").
- **Core claim**: Reality is many-sided; every assertion is perspective-indexed and must be prefixed by *syat* ("in some respect"). The blind-men-and-the-elephant parable is the stock illustration. No single naya (standpoint) captures the whole.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strong resonance with Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits; methodological pluralism — a single simple framework is not guaranteed to capture a complex reality). Useful as a *historical precedent* for the Map's own multi-framework stance. Caveat: anekantavada is a claim about representation/predication, not license for contradiction (syadvada is a disciplined seven-valued scheme, not "anything goes") — writers should not flatten it into relativism.

## Key Debates

### Does cross-traditional convergence *confirm* consciousness-irreducibility?
- **Sides**: The convergence argument (four+ independent classical traditions — Samkhya, Nyaya, Vedanta, now Jainism — all resist reducing consciousness) vs. the common-cause objection.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether agreement across traditions is independent evidence, or an artifact of a *shared method* (first-person introspection) and shared cultural-soteriological aims (moksha/liberation).
- **Current state**: The Map's own convergence articles already register the common-cause caveat. Jainism *adds a data point* but does NOT dissolve the objection — indeed Jainism shares the introspection-plus-liberation method, so it is not fully "independent." The eventual article must state this explicitly. (Cross-ref: cross-traditional-convergence-on-consciousness-irreducibility, the-hard-problem-in-non-western-philosophy.)

### Is anekantavada logically coherent?
- **Sides**: Critics (historically Dharmakirti and other Buddhist logicians) charge that asserting many-sidedness of everything is self-undermining; defenders (Balcerowicz, Matilal, Ganeri) read syadvada as a disciplined conditional/perspectival logic, not a contradiction-tolerating one.
- **Current state**: Ongoing in modern scholarship; relevant if the article leans on anekantavada for Tenet-5 support (must not overclaim it as a formal system that "solves" pluralism).
