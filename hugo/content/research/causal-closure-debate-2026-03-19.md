# Research: The Causal Closure Debate — Historical Evolution and Contemporary Challenges

**Date**: 2026-03-19
**Search queries used**: "causal closure of the physical philosophy Stanford Encyclopedia", "causal closure principle debate history philosophy of mind", "causal closure physical domain challenges dualism mental causation", "causal closure principle contemporary challenges quantum mechanics", "Jaegwon Kim causal exclusion argument", "conservation laws causal closure Papineau", "Barbara Montero causal closure criticism", "Lowe dualism quantum mechanics causal closure", "Henry Stapp quantum mechanics consciousness causal closure", "no good arguments for causal closure", "Descartes Princess Elisabeth mental causation"

## Executive Summary

The causal closure of the physical — the thesis that every physical event has a sufficient physical cause — is the central premise driving most contemporary arguments against interactionist dualism. Its historical evolution runs from Descartes' confrontation with Princess Elisabeth through Leibniz's conservation principles, the rise of 19th-century energy conservation, and Jaegwon Kim's 20th-century causal exclusion argument. Contemporary challenges come from multiple directions: philosophers questioning whether the principle is empirically supported or merely assumed (Buhler, Montero, Lowe), physicists noting that quantum mechanics leaves a causal gap where consciousness could act (Stapp), and arguments that conservation laws do not actually entail causal closure (Gibb). The debate remains very much alive, with strong implications for the Map's tenets on dualism and bidirectional interaction.

## Major Positions

### Causal Closure Defenders (Physicalism)
- **Proponents**: Jaegwon Kim, David Papineau, David Lewis, Daniel Stoljar
- **Core claim**: Every physical event has a sufficient physical cause. Combined with no-overdetermination, this excludes non-physical mental causes.
- **Key arguments**:
  - The empirical success of neuroscience in mapping neural causes of behavior
  - Conservation of energy and momentum (physical quantities stay constant)
  - The causal exclusion argument: if physical causes suffice, mental causes are excluded
  - The inductive argument from the history of science closing gaps
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly conflicts with Tenets 1, 2, and 3. The Map's response: quantum indeterminacy means physics is *not* complete at every point; consciousness acts where physics leaves outcomes undetermined.

### Causal Closure Critics — Philosophical Arguments
- **Proponents**: E.J. Lowe, Keith Buhler, Barbara Montero, Tim Crane
- **Core claim**: The arguments for causal closure are weaker than commonly assumed. They either beg the question, commit inductive fallacies, or depend on formulations that are actually compatible with dualist interaction.
- **Key arguments**:
  - A priori arguments for causal closure are circular (Buhler)
  - Standard formulations are consistent with interactionist dualism (Lowe)
  - The explanatory gap undermines the motivation for accepting causal closure (Montero)
  - Conservation laws do not entail causal closure (Gibb, Lowe)
  - The principle is treated as an axiom rather than a conclusion
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly aligns with all five tenets. Provides philosophical cover for the Map's rejection of causal closure.

### Causal Closure Critics — Physics-Based Arguments
- **Proponents**: Henry Stapp, Roger Penrose, Stuart Hameroff, David Bohm (partially)
- **Core claim**: Contemporary physics — particularly quantum mechanics — is not causally closed. The measurement problem, quantum indeterminacy, and the absence of a physical account of Process 1 (measurement choice) leave genuine causal gaps.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Von Neumann's Process 1 has no physical specification (Stapp)
  - Quantum indeterminacy means physical laws determine probabilities, not outcomes
  - The measurement problem remains unsolved — decoherence does not explain definite outcomes
  - Energy is not strictly conserved in quantum theory or general relativity (Lowe)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly supports Tenets 2, 3, and 4. This is the Map's primary response to causal closure.

### Compatibilist Positions
- **Proponents**: Some nonreductive physicalists, emergentists
- **Core claim**: Mental causation can be preserved even with causal closure through supervenience, functionalism, or weak emergence.
- **Key arguments**:
  - Mental properties supervene on physical properties but are not identical to them
  - Higher-level explanations are autonomous from lower-level ones
  - Downward causation operates through constitutive rather than causal relations
- **Relation to site tenets**: Partially conflicts — the Map holds that genuine (not merely explanatory) causal contribution is required by Tenet 3. Supervenience-based accounts tend toward epiphenomenalism in practice.

## Key Debates

### The Exclusion Problem (Kim vs. Critics)
- **Sides**: Kim argues mental causes are excluded by sufficient physical causes; critics (Baker, Crane, Loewer) dispute the no-overdetermination premise or challenge the notion of sufficiency
- **Core disagreement**: Whether physical causal sufficiency genuinely excludes non-physical causes, or whether the exclusion principle is too strong (it would also exclude macro-physical causes in favor of micro-physical ones)
- **Current state**: Ongoing. Kim's argument remains influential but is widely contested. The "generalization problem" (exclusion applies to all higher-level causation, not just mental) is seen as a serious difficulty.

### Conservation Laws and Mental Causation
- **Sides**: Papineau and others argue conservation laws support causal closure; Gibb, Lowe, and Stapp argue they do not
- **Core disagreement**: Whether conservation of energy and momentum entails that no non-physical influences can act on physical systems
- **Current state**: Growing consensus that conservation laws alone do not establish causal closure. Even Papineau concedes Newtonian conservation does not strictly block mental forces. Quantum indeterminacy and general relativistic considerations further weaken the argument.

### Quantum Mechanics and Causal Openness
- **Sides**: Physicalists argue quantum indeterminacy is irrelevant (decoherence, or randomness ≠ freedom); dualists argue it provides a genuine causal gap
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the indeterminacy in quantum mechanics is merely epistemic or represents a genuine opening for non-physical causation
- **Current state**: Ongoing. The measurement problem remains unsolved. Stapp's framework provides the most developed account of how consciousness could act through quantum mechanics without violating conservation laws. Critics question whether quantum effects are relevant at neural scales, though biological quantum effects (avian magnetoreception) demonstrate that evolution can exploit quantum coherence.

### The Definitional Challenge
- **Sides**: Montero, Crane, and others question what "physical" means; defenders of closure offer various definitions
- **Core disagreement**: If "physical" is defined by current physics, causal closure is false (current physics is incomplete). If defined by a future completed physics, the claim is empty (we don't know what that physics includes).
- **Current state**: This "Hempel's dilemma" remains a significant challenge. No consensus definition of "physical" supports causal closure without circularity or vacuity.
