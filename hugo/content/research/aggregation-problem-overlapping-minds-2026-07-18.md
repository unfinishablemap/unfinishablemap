## Executive Summary

"The aggregation problem for overlapping minds" names two intertwined puzzles that arise once you allow a single token experience to belong to more than one subject at once (experience-sharing / overlapping subjects). **Metaphysically**, it is a variant of panpsychism's combination problem: if micro-subjects aggregate into macro-subjects without vanishing, then subjects overlap and nest indefinitely, and there is no principled count of how many minds occupy a given region. **Morally**, it is a live problem for welfare aggregation (utilitarian and otherwise): if two subjects genuinely share one token pleasure or pain, do we count its value once, twice, or somewhere in between? Luke Roelofs is the central figure — his *Combining Minds* (2019) defends "manyism" (overlapping, experience-sharing subjects), his "No Such Thing as Too Many Minds" (AJP) argues the resulting overpopulation is tolerable, and Roelofs & Sebo's "Overlapping minds and the hedonic calculus" (*Philosophical Studies*, 2024) develops the moral-aggregation horn. The Map already covers the metaphysical side well ([manyism](/concepts/manyism/), [combination-problem](/concepts/combination-problem/), [open-individualism-and-the-de-combination-problem](/topics/open-individualism-and-the-de-combination-problem/)); the **unfilled gap is the moral/hedonic aggregation dimension** and its collision with the Map's tenets.

## Key Sources

### Overlapping minds and the hedonic calculus (Roelofs & Sebo, 2024)
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11098-024-02167-x
- **PhilPapers**: https://philpapers.org/rec/ROEOMA
- **Type**: Journal article (*Philosophical Studies*, 2024)
- **Key points**:
  - Near-future neurotechnology could let two subjects share a single token mental state (one feeling of pleasure or pain), not merely two qualitatively identical states.
  - This breaks welfare aggregation: **if two subjects share very few mental states, intuition says count the shared value twice; if they share very many, intuition says count it once.** Neither extreme scales to the middle.
  - Proposed reconciliation: the conflicting intuitions can be squared **if the mental states that matter for welfare have a holistic character** (welfare-bearing states are individuated holistically, so how much value a shared state contributes depends on the whole it belongs to).
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral-to-conflicting. Presupposes experience-sharing across subjects, which sits closer to constitutive panpsychism than to the Map's interactionist dualism, but the *aggregation puzzle itself* is a stress test any theory of subjects must answer.
- **Quote (paraphrase of the core tension)**: "If two subjects share very few mental states, it seems we should count the value of those states twice; if they share very many, it seems we should count it once."

### No Such Thing as Too Many Minds (Roelofs)
- **URL**: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361894887_No_Such_Thing_as_Too_Many_Minds (Australasian Journal of Philosophy)
- **Type**: Journal article
- **Key points**:
  - Responds to the "overpopulation" objection: manyism implies indefinitely many overlapping conscious subjects per head (from ~2 up to trillions via the Problem of the Many).
  - Argues **many overlapping minds are no more problematic than many overlapping physical objects** — the Problem of the Many already forces us to tolerate indefinitely many overlapping physical objects (the cat and its many nearly-coincident aggregates), so overlapping minds inherit an already-accepted metaphysical cost.
- **Tenet alignment**: Conflicts with Tenet 4 (indexical identity matters). If "you" are indefinitely many overlapping subjects, indexical singularity is denied.

### Combining Minds: How to Think about Composite Subjectivity (Roelofs, 2019)
- **URL (NDPR review)**: https://ndpr.nd.edu/reviews/combining-minds-how-to-think-about-composite-subjectivity/
- **URL (author copy)**: https://eprints.whiterose.ac.uk/id/eprint/195898/1/Luke%20Roelofs.edit_%20(1).pdf
- **Type**: Book (OUP) + review
- **Key points**:
  - Challenges the principle of **privacy** — the intuition that no two subjects can share a single token experience — and argues it "is no longer as compelling" once we seriously consider overlapping subjects sharing a brain region.
  - **Experience Inheritance** (p. 79): "whenever a part of aggregate x undergoes an experience … x undergoes that same experience." This is the aggregation rule that generates nesting: parts and wholes share experiences wherever there is physical interaction.
  - **Nesting Problem**: if consciousness meets criterion C, why don't subsystems, brain regions, and larger collectives (companies, nations) also qualify? Roelofs' answer: consciousness combines like mass — no exclusion across levels.
  - Distinguishes weak vs. strong privacy and weak vs. strong independence; subjectivity entails only the *weak* versions, so overlap and sharing are permitted.
- **Tenet alignment**: Rival monist framework; the Map engages it as the strongest constitutive-panpsychist competitor (see [manyism](/concepts/manyism/)).

### The Combination Problem for Panpsychism (Chalmers)
- **URL**: https://consc.net/papers/combination.pdf
- **Type**: Paper
- **Key points**: Canonical taxonomy — subject-summing, quality, and structural combination problems. The "subject-summing" problem (traceable to William James, *Principles of Psychology* 1890) is the ancestor of the aggregation problem: why/how do micro-subjects sum into a macro-subject rather than remaining "shut in its own skin, windowless."
- **Tenet alignment**: Supports the Map's diagnostic use — the aggregation problem is a cost panpsychism pays that interactionist dualism avoids.

### Quantum panprotopsychism and the subject-summing combination problem
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2409.01368
- **Type**: Preprint (2024)
- **Key points**: Recent attempt to use quantum structure (indistinguishability / entanglement) to address subject-summing. Relevant as a physicalist-adjacent rival route to aggregation; worth flagging for the Map's quantum-interaction tenets but not central to the moral aggregation angle.

### Fuzzy, Nested Minds Problematize Utilitarian Aggregation (reducing-suffering.org)
- **URL**: https://reducing-suffering.org/fuzzy-nested-minds-problematize-utilitarian-aggregation/
- **Type**: Long-form essay (Brian Tomasik)
- **Key points**: Minds are both **fuzzy** (no sharp boundary) and **nested** (little minds combine into bigger minds), so there is no canonical unit to aggregate over. Independent convergence on the same worry from the effective-altruism/utilitarian side.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral. Useful as non-panpsychist corroboration that the aggregation problem is real for anyone doing welfare arithmetic.

## Major Positions

### Manyism / Experience-Sharing (Roelofs)
- **Proponents**: Luke Roelofs (with Jeff Sebo on the ethics)
- **Core claim**: A single token experience can belong to many overlapping subjects; parts and wholes coexist and share experiences (Experience Inheritance) rather than one replacing the other.
- **Key arguments**: dissolves subject-summing (no fusion required); privacy is only weak; overpopulation is tolerable by parity with the Problem of the Many.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongest monist rival to [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/). Conflicts with Tenet 4 (indexical identity) by multiplying overlapping "you"s. The Map engages, does not endorse.

### Holistic Welfare-State Individuation (Roelofs & Sebo)
- **Core claim**: The moral aggregation problem is solved by individuating welfare-bearing states holistically, so a shared state's contribution depends on the whole(s) it figures in — reconciling "count once" (heavy overlap) with "count twice" (light overlap).
- **Key arguments**: The extremes are intuitive; a holistic individuation of value interpolates between them.
- **Relation to site tenets**: A moral-theory patch that leaves the underlying overlapping-subjects metaphysics (and its Tenet-4 conflict) intact.

### Dualist Dissolution (The Map's likely stance)
- **Core claim**: [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) posits genuinely distinct, non-composite subjects; token experiences are not shared across subjects, so neither the metaphysical aggregation problem nor the moral double-counting problem arises in this form.
- **Key arguments**: If the subject is a simple (non-aggregate) locus of consciousness, there is no summing to explain and no overlap to count. The aggregation problem is a cost of *constitutive* views, not of interactionism.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Directly supports Tenets 1 (dualism) and 4 (indexical identity). This is the diagnostic payoff the article should foreground.

## Key Debates

### Metaphysical vs. moral aggregation — one problem or two?
- **Sides**: Roelofs treats them as continuous (the same overlapping-subjects metaphysics drives both); a critic could hold the moral puzzle is downstream and separable.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether fixing welfare-value individuation (holism) can proceed without settling how many subjects there are.
- **Current state**: Ongoing; the 2024 *Phil Studies* paper is the state of the art on the moral horn.

### Count once, twice, or in between?
- **Sides**: "Count once" (a shared pleasure is one pleasure); "count twice" (two subjects each have a reason-giving experience); interpolation (holistic individuation).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the moral weight of an experience tracks the *token state* or the *number of subjects* it belongs to.
- **Current state**: Unresolved; conflicting intuitions across cases is the paper's central datum.

### Is overpopulation a reductio?
- **Sides**: Roelofs (no — parity with Problem of the Many); critics (yes — trillions of minds per head is a cost).
- **Current state**: Live.
