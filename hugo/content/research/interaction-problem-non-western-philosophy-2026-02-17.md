## Executive Summary

The interaction problem -- how can mind and body causally interact if they are ontologically distinct? -- is not a uniquely Western concern. Long before Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia posed it to Descartes in 1643, Indian philosophers wrestled with the same puzzle in the Samkhya tradition's Purusha-Prakriti dualism. Islamic thinkers developed three radically different solutions (emanationism, occasionalism, and processual unity). Buddhist philosophy dissolved the problem by rejecting permanent substances altogether. Chinese Neo-Confucianism proposed a pattern-matter (li-qi) framework that avoids sharp mind-body division. African traditions posit ontologies where force, rather than substance, is the fundamental category, making the interaction problem structurally different.

These traditions offer resources that Western philosophy of mind has largely ignored: non-causal interaction models (Samkhya reflection), God-mediated causation (Ash'ari occasionalism), processual ontologies that dissolve the gap between mind and matter (Mulla Sadra), event-ontologies without enduring substances (Buddhism), and force-based monisms that never generate the interaction problem in the first place (Bantu philosophy). Several of these align with or challenge The Unfinishable Map's dualist commitments in illuminating ways.

## 1. Indian Philosophy

### 1a. Samkhya: The Original Interaction Problem

**The Problem**: Samkhya is one of the oldest systematic dualisms, positing two co-eternal, independent realities: Purusha (pure consciousness, passive, plural) and Prakriti (matter/nature, active, singular but containing the three gunas). The interaction problem is explicit in the tradition: "How can the inactive soul influence matter, and how could an unintelligent substance, nature, serve anybody's purpose?"

**Proposed Mechanisms**:

1. **The Lame Man and Blind Man Analogy** (Samkhyakarika, verse 21, Isvarakrishna, c. 350 CE): "Just as a lame man and a blind man can move about when they cooperate, so also Purusha and Prakriti can create the world when they are in union." Purusha provides awareness (sight) but cannot act; Prakriti provides activity (locomotion) but cannot perceive. Their union produces experience and evolution. The analogy explains mutual benefit but does not specify a causal mechanism -- it is more a statement of complementarity than an explanation of interaction.

2. **The Magnet Analogy**: Purusha's relation to Prakriti is compared to a magnet and iron: "Purusha itself does not come into contact with Prakriti. But it influences Prakriti." The mere proximity or presence of Purusha disturbs the equilibrium of the three gunas in Prakriti, initiating the evolution of the manifest world. This is a form of non-contact causation -- influence through proximity rather than efficient causation.

3. **The Reflection Model** (developed by Vacaspati Misra, c. 9th century, and Vijnanabhikshu, c. 16th century): The most philosophically sophisticated Samkhya answer. As an image in a mirror has no effect on the reflected object and the mirror itself remains unchanged, so the unchanging Purusha can "reflect" the external world while the material psyche reacts to this reflection. According to Vacaspati Misra, the self (Purusha) is reflected in the buddhi (intellect), making the unconscious mental complex appear conscious. Vijnanabhikshu developed this into a "mutual reflection" theory: consciousness reflects the intellect, and the intellect reflects consciousness, creating a bidirectional informational relationship without either substance being altered.

**Strengths**:
- Avoids the need for a physical mechanism (no "pineal gland" needed)
- The reflection model is an early form of supervenience or informational coupling without efficient causation
- Preserves the immutability of consciousness (important for Samkhya soteriology)
- The mutual reflection model anticipates aspects of contemporary "structural correspondence" theories

**Weaknesses**:
- The reflection metaphor may replace the interaction problem rather than solve it -- what makes Prakriti "reflective" in the first place?
- The magnet analogy still requires explaining what kind of "influence" proximity exerts
- The lame-and-blind analogy describes the result of interaction but not its mechanism
- Critics (especially from Advaita) argued that Samkhya never adequately explains why a passive Purusha's mere presence should disturb Prakriti's equilibrium

**Comparison to Western approaches**: The reflection model is strikingly unlike Descartes' pineal gland (a physical mechanism for non-physical causation). It is closer to pre-established harmony (Leibniz) or to certain supervenience accounts where mental states track physical states without causing them. However, unlike Leibnizian parallelism, Samkhya insists the reflection is genuinely informative -- Purusha "learns" about the world through it -- making it a form of bidirectional interaction without efficient causation.

**Key scholarly source**: *Mirror of Nature, Mirror of Self: Models of Consciousness in Samkhya, Yoga, and Advaita Vedanta* (Oxford Academic, 2023) provides the most thorough treatment of the reflection models. The IEP entry on Sankhya (https://iep.utm.edu/sankhya/) covers the interaction problem systematically.

### 1b. Nyaya-Vaisheshika: Mind as Atomic Intermediary

**The Problem**: Nyaya-Vaisheshika is a realist, pluralist system that accepts both the existence of a permanent self (atman) and the reality of the external world. The atman is eternal, omnipresent (in later Nyaya), and the substrate of consciousness -- but consciousness is not intrinsic to the atman. It arises only when the atman contacts the mind (manas), which contacts the sense organs, which contact external objects.

**Proposed Mechanism**:

The mind (manas) is an atomic, material, internal organ that serves as an intermediary between the non-physical atman and the physical sense organs. Consciousness arises as a "conditional attribute" (guna) of the atman only when the atman-manas-sense organ chain is complete. The mind can attend to only one sensory input at a time (because it is atomic, it cannot be in multiple places at once), explaining the serial nature of attention.

Causal interaction is explained through two types of relations:
- **Samyoga (conjunction)**: A temporary contact between substances, such as the mind contacting a sense organ
- **Samavaya (inherence)**: A permanent, inseparable relation between entities, such as a quality inhering in its substrate

The atman-manas connection is a form of samyoga -- a real, causal conjunction between substances. The mind literally "touches" both the soul and the senses, bridging the ontological gap.

**Strengths**:
- Provides a concrete mechanism (manas as intermediary)
- Makes consciousness conditional rather than essential, explaining unconscious states (deep sleep, liberation)
- The atomic mind explains the serial nature of attention (a genuine empirical observation)
- The inherence (samavaya) relation provides a non-reductive way of relating properties to substances

**Weaknesses**:
- If the mind is material and atomic, the problem merely shifts: how does a material atom interact with a non-material atman?
- The claim that the mind is both "atomic" and "material" yet capable of contacting something immaterial invites the same objection Elisabeth raised against Descartes
- The system requires accepting the category of samavaya (inherence) as a real, irreducible relation -- which rival schools (especially Buddhist) rejected as metaphysically inflated

**Comparison to Western approaches**: The Nyaya model is structurally similar to Descartes' pineal gland solution: positing a specific entity that bridges the mind-body gap. Both face the same regress: the intermediary itself must interact with both realms, requiring an explanation of that interaction in turn. However, Nyaya's richer categorical scheme (multiple types of relations, multiple types of causation) gives it more resources to address the regress than Descartes had.

**Key scholarly sources**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Nyaya (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/nyaya/); IEP entry on Nyaya (https://iep.utm.edu/nyaya/); Vaisheshika entry on Britannica (https://www.britannica.com/topic/Vaisheshika).

### 1c. Yoga (Patanjali): Theistic Modification of Samkhya

**The Problem**: Yoga shares Samkhya's dualism of Purusha and Prakriti but introduces Ishvara (a "special Purusha" or personal God) as a twenty-sixth tattva (principle). The interaction problem is inherited from Samkhya.

**Proposed Mechanism**: Ishvara is a special Purusha that has never been entangled with Prakriti and is therefore "essentially inactive" yet all-knowing. While Yoga does not explicitly solve the interaction problem through Ishvara, the introduction of a divine Purusha provides a possible teleological ground for the Purusha-Prakriti relationship: the universe evolves for the purpose of liberating individual Purushas, and Ishvara's presence may serve as the ultimate reason the reflection/proximity mechanism works.

Yoga also emphasizes the practical dimension: through disciplined practice (the eight limbs of yoga), the practitioner can experientially distinguish Purusha from Prakriti, achieving kaivalya (isolation/liberation). The interaction problem is ultimately dissolved experientially rather than solved theoretically.

**Strengths**:
- Introduces a teleological framework that explains *why* interaction occurs (for liberation)
- Provides a practical program for testing the dualist hypothesis through first-person experience

**Weaknesses**:
- Ishvara does not actually explain the mechanism of interaction -- the problem is pushed up one level
- The relationship between Ishvara and ordinary Purushas is not well specified

**Comparison to Western approaches**: The introduction of God to solve the interaction problem parallels Malebranche's occasionalism and Leibniz's pre-established harmony -- both of which invoke God to bridge the mind-body gap.

### 1d. Advaita Vedanta: Dissolving the Problem Through Non-Dualism

**The Problem**: Advaita Vedanta, systematized by Shankara (c. 8th century), rejects Samkhya's dualism. There is ultimately only one reality: Brahman (which is identical with Atman, the self). The apparent distinction between mind and body, and between self and world, is the product of avidya (ignorance/illusion/maya). If there are not genuinely two substances, there is no interaction problem.

**Proposed Mechanism**: There is no interaction mechanism because there is no genuine interaction between two substances. The phenomenal world, including the body, is an "illusory appearance" (vivarta) of Brahman, or more precisely, an appearance due to maya/avidya. The mind-body relationship is like a dream: real within the dream, but lacking ultimate ontological standing.

**Critique of Samkhya**: Advaita provides one of the earliest explicit critiques of dualist interaction. Shankara and his followers argued that Samkhya never adequately explains how a purely passive Purusha can causally influence Prakriti, even by proximity. If Purusha is truly changeless and actionless, the very idea that its "presence" disturbs Prakriti's equilibrium is incoherent.

**Strengths**:
- Eliminates the interaction problem entirely by denying the premises
- Philosophically elegant: one substance, no bridging needed

**Weaknesses**:
- The concept of avidya/maya faces its own "interaction problem": if Brahman is the sole reality, where does ignorance come from? Shankara left this as the most crucial unresolved problem for his followers
- Risks trivializing the phenomenal world, including the apparent reality of mental causation that is part of everyday experience

**Comparison to Western approaches**: Closest to idealist monisms (Berkeley, Hegel) and to eliminativism about the interaction problem. The unresolved problem of maya's origin parallels difficulties in Berkeley's system about explaining error and illusion within a God-centered idealism.

**Key scholarly source**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Shankara (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/shankara/).

## 2. Islamic Philosophy

### 2a. Avicenna (Ibn Sina, 980-1037 CE): Emanation and the Dual-Aspect Soul

**The Problem**: Avicenna argued that the soul is an immaterial, self-subsistent substance (demonstrated by his famous "Floating Man" thought experiment: a person suspended in a void, deprived of all sensation, would still know they exist, proving the soul's independence from the body). Yet each soul needs a body "in order to be distinguished from other souls" and to act in the material world. The question is how an immaterial soul causally affects and is affected by a material body.

**Proposed Mechanism**:

Avicenna's solution has several components:

1. **Emanation from the Active Intellect**: The soul is "generated by the separate intelligences of the heavens and emanated by them upon the body." The body is the "accidental cause" of the soul -- it provides the occasion for the soul's emanation, but the soul's source is the Active Intellect (the lowest celestial intelligence). This cosmological framework means the soul-body connection is grounded in a top-down metaphysical hierarchy rather than in bottom-up physical causation.

2. **Dual-Aspect Intellect**: The soul has two functional aspects:
   - The **practical intellect** faces downward toward matter, governing bodily actions
   - The **theoretical (intellectual) intellect** faces upward toward the Active Intellect, receiving intelligible forms

   The mechanism of brain-to-mind influence is "bottom-up preparing influence" -- bodily states prepare the soul to receive forms from the Active Intellect. The mechanism of mind-to-brain influence is "top-down actualizing effect" -- once the soul receives intelligible forms from the Active Intellect, it determines brain states and initiates action through the practical intellect.

3. **Third-Entity Interface**: In his main work *al-Shifa*, Avicenna described "the mechanism of the causal effect of mind on the brain via a third identity, which works as an interface between them." The soul's action on the body "cannot be at a specific space-time point, but it must affect the whole dynamics and the motion of matter from the initial point to the final point (i.e., the space-time extended flowing existence of motion)."

**Strengths**:
- Provides a rich, multi-level account of bidirectional interaction
- The emanationist framework avoids the problem of two utterly disparate substances needing to interact: both soul and body ultimately derive from the same source (the chain of emanation from the One)
- The "preparation/actualization" model (body prepares, intellect actualizes) anticipates modern "enabling condition" theories
- The insight that mental causation affects dynamics globally rather than at a point anticipates some features of quantum approaches (compare to Stapp's model)

**Weaknesses**:
- Relies on a specific Neoplatonic cosmology (emanation from celestial intellects) that most contemporary thinkers reject
- The "third identity" interface is underspecified -- it might just push the problem back one step
- The Active Intellect as "giver of forms" to both minds and nature is metaphysically extravagant
- Does not explain why the soul, if truly immaterial, should need a body for individuation

**Comparison to Western approaches**: Avicenna's model is richer than Descartes' pineal gland answer. The emanationist framework has some structural similarity to contemporary top-down causation models and to field theories of consciousness. His dual-aspect intellect is comparable to the Kantian distinction between theoretical and practical reason, though metaphysically grounded rather than epistemological.

**Key scholarly sources**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Ibn Sina (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ibn-sina/); IEP entry on Avicenna (https://iep.utm.edu/avicenna-ibn-sina/); Stanford Encyclopedia on Arabic and Islamic Psychology and Philosophy of Mind (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/arabic-islamic-mind/).

### 2b. Al-Ghazali (1058-1111 CE): Occasionalism

**The Problem**: Al-Ghazali, the most famous proponent of Ash'ari theology applied to philosophy, radicalized the interaction problem. One motivation for his theory "is the dualist belief that mind and matter are so utterly different in their essences that one cannot affect the other. Thus, a person's mind cannot be the true cause of his hand's moving, nor can a physical wound be the true cause of mental anguish."

**Proposed Mechanism**: Occasionalism -- God is the sole true cause of everything. What appears as causal regularity in nature (fire burns cotton, mental decisions move limbs) is actually "a kind of constant and continual regularity with no independent necessitation of change and becoming, other than what God has ordained." Created things have no genuine causal power. On each occasion when we observe what looks like causation, God directly produces both the "cause" event and the "effect" event.

**Important nuance**: Scholars disagree about al-Ghazali's actual commitment to full occasionalism. "Al-Ghazali does not deny the existence of a connection between a cause and its effect; rather he denies the necessary character of this connection." Some Ash'ari theologians endorsed God acting through secondary causality, and al-Ghazali may have endorsed this position in some works (the *Madnun*). The question is whether God creates regularities that involve genuine secondary causal powers in creatures, or whether each event is individually willed.

**Strengths**:
- Radically solves the interaction problem: if only God causes, there is no problem of how mind causes body or vice versa
- Preserves divine omnipotence (theologically motivated)
- Anticipates Malebranche's occasionalism in Europe by several centuries
- The weaker version (denying necessary connection while accepting regular connection) anticipates Hume's critique of necessary causation

**Weaknesses**:
- Makes all of science problematic -- if causal regularities are not necessary, scientific laws are merely descriptions of God's habits
- Does not actually explain interaction; it eliminates it by making God do everything
- Relies on a specific theological commitment (Islamic monotheism with radical divine sovereignty)
- The weaker interpretation may not be an interaction theory at all, but a theory about the epistemology of causation

**Comparison to Western approaches**: Directly parallels Malebranche's occasionalism (1674-1715), which was independently developed to solve the same Cartesian interaction problem. Also parallels Berkeley's view that God is the true cause of our ideas. The key difference is that al-Ghazali's version is motivated primarily by theological concerns (preserving God's sovereignty) rather than by the Cartesian framework.

**Key scholarly sources**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on al-Ghazali (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/al-ghazali/); Stanford Encyclopedia entry on Occasionalism (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/occasionalism/); IEP entry on Occasionalism (https://iep.utm.edu/occasion/).

### 2c. Mulla Sadra (c. 1572-1640 CE): Substantial Motion and Processual Unity

**The Problem**: Mulla Sadra's "Transcendent Theosophy" (al-hikmat al-muta'aliyah) represents the most sophisticated Islamic approach to the soul-body problem. He rejected both the Peripatetic view (soul as a pre-existing entity that enters the body) and the theological view (soul as created ex nihilo at a moment). His key insight was that the interaction problem arises from treating soul and body as static substances; if existence is fundamentally dynamic, the problem dissolves.

**Proposed Mechanism**: Two interconnected doctrines:

1. **Substantial Motion (al-harakat al-jawhariyyah)**: Everything in nature undergoes continuous substantial transformation. Existence is not static being but perpetual becoming. The soul is not a fixed substance that must somehow interact with another fixed substance (body); both soul and body are stages in a continuous process of intensification (tashkik) of being.

2. **Bodily Origination, Spiritual Subsistence (jismani al-huduth, ruhani al-baqa)**: The soul originates as a material, bodily reality and progressively transforms into an immaterial, spiritual reality. The soul "is corporeal in its origination and spiritual in its subsistence." It begins as "a corporeal faculty (quwwah jismaniyyah), then a natural form (surah tabi'iyyah), then a sensible soul (nafs hassasah)" and eventually becomes "a rational soul (natiqah) possessing theoretical and practical reason, and eventually becomes active intellect."

The body is therefore not a separate substance that interacts with the soul; it is "a lower, dynamic level of the soul's existence." The soul-body relationship is one of ontological continuity across different grades of being, not interaction between distinct substances.

**Strengths**:
- Genuinely dissolves the interaction problem rather than just relocating it -- if soul and body are grades on a continuum, there is no gap to bridge
- The processual ontology anticipates aspects of Whitehead's process philosophy
- Avoids the static substance framework that generates the problem in the first place
- Accommodates the empirical observation that mental capacities develop gradually (from embryo to adult)
- The idea of being as dynamic and intensifiable is philosophically rich and avoids many standard objections to dualism

**Weaknesses**:
- If the soul begins as material, it is unclear how it becomes genuinely immaterial -- the "substantial motion" from material to immaterial needs explanation
- The ontology of "grades of being" (tashkik al-wujud) is metaphysically demanding and not easy to integrate with modern physics
- May dissolve the interaction problem only by denying the dualist premise -- if soul and body are continuous, this may not count as a dualist view at all
- Relies on a complex metaphysical framework (primacy of existence, modulation of being) that is not easily separable from its Islamic theological context

**Comparison to Western approaches**: Closest to Whitehead's process philosophy, where mind and matter are both aspects of "occasions of experience." Also has affinities with Aristotelian hylomorphism (soul as form of the body) but goes beyond it by making the form itself dynamic and evolving. Unlike Descartes' sharp substance divide, Mulla Sadra's ontology is fundamentally gradualist.

**Key scholarly sources**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Mulla Sadra (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mulla-sadra/); IEP entry on Mulla Sadra (https://iep.utm.edu/sadra/); Abbas Ali Shameli, *The Soul-Body Problem in the Philosophical Psychology of Mulla Sadra and Ibn Sina* (https://al-islam.org/soul-body-problem-philosophical-psychology-mulla-sadra-and-ibn-sina-abbas-ali-shameli/).

## 3. Buddhist Philosophy

### 3a. Abhidharma: Event Ontology Without Substances

**The Problem**: Buddhism rejects the existence of a permanent self (anatta/anatman) and analyzes all experience into momentary events (dharmas). There is no enduring mind-substance and no enduring body-substance. The question then becomes: how do mental events causally influence physical events (and vice versa) if there are no enduring substances for them to belong to?

**Proposed Mechanism**:

1. **Dharma Theory**: All experience is analyzed into elementary events (dharmas) classified into categories: form/matter (rupa), consciousness (citta), mental factors (caitta), and others. These dharmas arise and pass away moment-to-moment according to causal conditions. Mental and physical dharmas interact through conditioned arising -- a mental dharma can be a condition for a physical dharma and vice versa, without needing any permanent substance to mediate.

2. **The 24 Conditional Relations (Patthana)**: The Theravada Abhidhamma enumerates 24 types of conditional relations describing how dharmas influence each other. These include root condition, object condition, predominance condition, proximity condition, contiguity condition, and many others. This provides "a comprehensive map of the intricate web of causality that underpins the dynamic processes of experience." Mental-physical causation is just one instance of conditioned arising among many.

3. **Dependent Origination (Pratityasamutpada)**: The most fundamental Buddhist causal principle: "If this exists, that exists; if this ceases to exist, that also ceases to exist." The twelve links of dependent origination describe how mental states (ignorance, volition, consciousness) condition physical states (name-and-form, sense bases) and vice versa in a continuous chain. Volition (cetana) is the key mental factor driving karma and physical action.

**The Problem of Momentariness**: "If causes, conditions, and their results are all momentary events, how can an event that has ended have a result?" Buddhist philosophers developed several responses: the Sarvastivada school held that dharmas exist in all three times (past, present, future); the Sautrantika school developed a "seed" (bija) theory where causal influence is transmitted through a series of momentary states; the Yogacara school posited the alaya-vijnana (storehouse consciousness) as a substrate for causal continuity.

**Strengths**:
- Dissolves the substance-interaction problem entirely: no enduring mind-substance, no enduring body-substance, hence no interaction problem in the Cartesian form
- The fine-grained event ontology is arguably more compatible with physics than substance ontologies
- The 24 conditional relations provide a detailed, empirically-informed taxonomy of mental-physical causation
- Avoids postulating mysterious substances or intermediaries
- The dependent origination framework treats mental-physical causation as just one instance of a universal causal principle

**Weaknesses**:
- The problem of momentariness creates its own version of the interaction problem: how do momentary events influence future events?
- The denial of self (anatta) makes it difficult to account for personal identity and unified conscious experience
- The various schools' solutions (three-time existence, seeds, storehouse consciousness) may reintroduce substance-like entities through the back door
- Does not address the "hard problem" -- why mental events are conscious rather than merely functional

**Comparison to Western approaches**: The Buddhist event ontology is closest to Hume's bundle theory of the self and to Russell's neutral monism. The Abhidharma analysis anticipates functionalist accounts where mental states are defined by their causal roles. However, unlike Western functionalism, Abhidharma does not reduce mental events to physical ones -- mental dharmas have their own ontological status.

**Key scholarly sources**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Abhidharma (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/abhidharma/); Stanford Encyclopedia entry on Mind in Indian Buddhist Philosophy (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mind-indian-buddhism/).

### 3b. Yogacara: Consciousness-Only

**The Problem**: The Yogacara (Vijnanavada) school, founded by Asanga and Vasubandhu (4th-5th century CE), addresses the interaction problem by arguing that everything we take to be "external" and "physical" is actually a construction of consciousness. If there is no genuinely mind-independent physical world, there is no mind-body interaction problem.

**Proposed Mechanism**:

1. **Vijnaptimatrata (Consciousness-Only)**: What we call the physical world is actually a manifestation of consciousness. "From the mind-only position, it is easier to posit a mind-to-mind causation than to have to explain mind-to-body causation, which the realist must do." Yogacara "rejected the possibility of establishing a causal connection between an external object and a form in cognition."

2. **Eight Consciousnesses**: Yogacara posits eight types of consciousness:
   - Five sense consciousnesses (visual, auditory, etc.)
   - Mental consciousness (manovijnana)
   - Afflicted mind (manas/klistamanas) -- the basis of ego-clinging
   - Storehouse consciousness (alaya-vijnana) -- "the most fundamental layer of perception," which stores karmic seeds (bija) that ripen into experience

   All eight consciousnesses "exist in a mutual relation of cause and effect, each exerting an influence on the others." The physical world as experienced is a projection from karmic seeds stored in the alaya-vijnana.

3. **Karmic Seeds (Bija)**: The alaya-vijnana stores "seeds" planted by past mental and physical actions. These seeds mature and manifest as the entire range of experience, including the appearance of external objects and one's own body. "Each lifetime, or 'moment of consciousness,' is shaped by the mental imprints stored in this storehouse."

**Strengths**:
- Eliminates the interaction problem at its root: if everything is consciousness, there is no non-conscious realm to interact with
- The alaya-vijnana provides a mechanism for causal continuity without requiring a permanent self
- Sophisticated phenomenological analysis of how experience is structured
- Compatible with contemporary arguments about the theory-ladenness of perception and the impossibility of proving mind-independent reality

**Weaknesses**:
- If "consciousness-only" means external reality is denied, it faces the problems of idealism (solipsism, other minds, coordination of experience)
- Scholars debate whether Yogacara is truly idealist or merely phenomenological (analyzing experience without denying external reality)
- The alaya-vijnana looks suspiciously like the permanent self (atman) that Buddhism rejects
- The seed metaphor does not explain the mechanism of karmic storage and maturation

**Comparison to Western approaches**: Closest to Berkeley's idealism and to contemporary analytic idealism (Kastrup). The alaya-vijnana has structural similarities to Leibniz's monads (self-contained units of experience) and to the Jungian collective unconscious. The critique that it smuggles in a self resembles Western critiques of Humean bundle theory (who or what does the bundling?).

**Key scholarly sources**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Yogacara (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/yogacara/); William S. Waldron, "A Buddhist Theory of Unconscious Mind (alaya-vijnana)" (https://www.middlebury.edu/college/sites/default/files/2023-03/waldron-a_buddhist_theory_of_unconscious_mind0.pdf).

## 4. Chinese Philosophy

### 4a. Zhu Xi's Neo-Confucianism: Li and Qi

**The Problem**: Neo-Confucianism does not frame the interaction problem in Cartesian terms because it never posits two radically distinct substances. Instead, Zhu Xi (1130-1200 CE) developed a framework of li (principle/pattern) and qi (vital force/material energy) that structures all reality. The question becomes: how do immaterial principles (li) relate to material qi?

**Proposed Mechanism**:

1. **Li-Qi Complementarity**: Li refers to "patterns of coherence" and qi is "the stuff in which those patterns inhere." They are conceptually distinct but never found separately: "There is no qi without li, and no li without qi." Li provides the rational structure; qi provides the dynamic material. Zhu Xi maintained a certain "metaphysical dualism of qi (vital stuff) and li (Pattern), which he then used to explain everything from human nature to the natural inclinations of plants to the acquisition of moral knowledge."

2. **Li as Nature, Qi as Disposition**: "In the neo-Confucian formulation, li in itself is pure and almost-perfect, but with the addition of qi, base emotions and conflicts arise." Human nature (xing) is originally good (being identical with li) but the particular endowment of qi in each person can be clear or turbid, leading to moral differences. "The task of moral cultivation is to clear our qi."

3. **Heart-Mind (Xin)**: The heart-mind (xin, a single Chinese concept encompassing both cognition and feeling) is the point where li and qi meet. "Zhu Xi argued that li is the nature found in the heart." The heart-mind is simultaneously a li-reality (having inherent moral principle) and a qi-reality (being an active, material process).

**Strengths**:
- Avoids the Cartesian interaction problem by never fully separating mind and matter
- Li and qi are always co-present, making their "interaction" a matter of internal dynamics rather than cross-substance causation
- The framework accommodates both the rational structure of mind (li) and its dynamic, embodied nature (qi)
- The heart-mind concept avoids the Western dichotomy between reason and emotion

**Weaknesses**:
- If li and qi are never separate, it is unclear whether this is a form of dualism at all -- it might be a structured monism
- Zhu Xi's system struggles to explain evil: if li is perfect and qi merely obscures it, why is qi ever turbid?
- The relationship between li and qi remains somewhat mysterious: does li cause qi to take certain forms, or does it merely describe patterns qi already exhibits?
- Li lacks causal efficacy in most interpretations -- it does not "do" anything, it merely "is" the pattern

**Comparison to Western approaches**: Li-qi has structural similarities to Aristotelian hylomorphism (form and matter) and to contemporary structural realism (patterns as fundamental). Unlike Cartesian dualism, the li-qi framework does not generate an interaction problem because it never posits causally inert, fully separable substances. This is arguably a strength -- but it may not be relevant to traditions that accept a stronger form of dualism.

### 4b. Wang Yangming (1472-1529 CE): Unity of Knowing and Acting

**The Problem**: Wang Yangming rejected Zhu Xi's residual dualism between li (found "out there" in things) and the mind. For Wang, "the human mind is both the locus and the standard of sagehood." Li is not in the world waiting to be discovered; it is identical with the heart-mind.

**Proposed Mechanism**:

1. **Unity of Knowing and Acting (zhi xing he yi)**: Wang denied any separation between moral knowledge and moral action. "When Wang speaks of a unity of knowing and acting, it is precisely because he wants people to understand that the generation of a single thought is already action." Seeing a beautiful color and loving it are not two events (first perception, then response) but a single event. Similarly, knowing the good and doing it are not separable.

2. **Liangzhi (Innate Knowing)**: All people have an innate moral consciousness that is simultaneously cognitive and active. Mind and body are unified because the mind's knowing is inherently an active engagement with the world, and bodily action is inherently an expression of knowing.

**Strengths**:
- Dissolves mind-body interaction by denying the separation
- The unity of knowing-and-acting is phenomenologically compelling (our experience is often pre-reflective engagement, not a two-step process of thinking then acting)
- Avoids the intellectualism that Zhu Xi's system tends toward

**Weaknesses**:
- If knowing and acting are truly unified, it becomes difficult to account for weakness of will (knowing something is wrong but doing it anyway -- which Wang must deny is genuine knowledge)
- This is a monism, not a dualism -- it does not solve the interaction problem so much as deny its premises

**Comparison to Western approaches**: Closest to Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of embodied cognition, pragmatist accounts of knowing-as-doing (Dewey), and enactivism in cognitive science. Also parallels aspects of Ryle's critique of the "ghost in the machine."

**Key scholarly sources**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Wang Yangming (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/wang-yangming/); Stanford Encyclopedia entry on Zhu Xi (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/zhu-xi/); IEP entry on Neo-Confucian Philosophy (https://iep.utm.edu/neo-confucian-philosophy/).

## 5. African Philosophy

### 5a. Bantu Philosophy: Vital Force (Ntu)

**The Problem**: Bantu philosophy, as described by Placide Tempels (1945) and refined by Alexis Kagame and subsequent African philosophers, operates with a fundamentally different ontological framework than Western philosophy. Where Western thought takes "being" or "substance" as the fundamental category, Bantu thought takes "force" (ntu) as fundamental. The interaction problem as posed in the West presupposes a substance ontology; in a force ontology, the problem takes a different form (or may not arise at all).

**Proposed Framework**:

1. **Force Ontology**: "In Bantu philosophy, 'force' takes the place of the notion 'being' in Western philosophy." All reality is constituted by forces of different kinds. The material and spiritual are not two separate substances but two aspects of a single force-reality.

2. **Four Categories of Force (Kagame)**: The cosmos is "constituted by a play of forces" of four kinds:
   - **Muntu**: Human force (includes living, dead, and spiritual beings with intelligence)
   - **Kintu**: Thing-force (animals, plants, minerals -- forces without intelligence)
   - **Hantu**: Place-time force
   - **Kuntu**: Modality force (beauty, manner, style)

3. **Interaction as Force Dynamics**: In this framework, "the interaction between vital human force and inferior forces (animals, plants, minerals), and the significant relationship between the living and the living-dead (ancestors)" are all expressions of a single type of ontological relationship: force influencing force. The "spiritual" and "material" realms are not separate domains requiring a bridge; they are continuous dimensions of the same force-reality.

4. **Vital Force as Unifying Concept**: "In African Philosophy, Vital Force is often seen as a unifying concept that bridges the gap between the spiritual and material realms. It is believed to be a manifestation of the divine, connecting humans to their ancestors, the natural world, and the cosmos as a whole."

**Strengths**:
- The force ontology avoids generating the interaction problem in the first place -- there is no gap between inert matter and immaterial mind
- Accommodates the full range of human experience (physical, mental, social, spiritual) within a single framework
- The relational nature of force (forces interact by strengthening or weakening each other) provides a natural model for mental-physical causation
- Avoids the Western tendency to treat consciousness as a special, anomalous feature of an otherwise mechanical universe

**Weaknesses**:
- Tempels' account has been criticized as a European reconstruction of African thought, potentially imposing systematicity that the original traditions lack
- The force concept is vague: it is unclear what distinguishes "force" from "substance" beyond the label
- Does not provide a detailed mechanism for specific kinds of interaction (how exactly does mental force affect physical force?)
- May be too holistic to generate specific empirical predictions

**Comparison to Western approaches**: The force ontology has affinities with Leibniz's monadology (reality as constituted by active forces rather than inert matter), Whitehead's process philosophy, and contemporary panpsychism (consciousness as a fundamental feature of reality rather than an anomaly). Unlike these Western systems, however, Bantu philosophy is not primarily concerned with the mind-body problem as an academic question -- the practical implications for ethics, community, and ancestor relations are primary.

**Key scholarly sources**: Tempels, P. (1945). *Bantu Philosophy* (https://cyrho.com/data/Placide%20Tempels%20Bantu%20Philosophy%201959.pdf); Blog of the APA: "Colonial Heritage or Philosophical Source? Update on the Heritage of Bantu Philosophy" (https://blog.apaonline.org/2024/02/13/colonial-heritage-or-philosophical-source-update-on-the-heritage-of-bantu-philosophy/).

### 5b. Yoruba Philosophy: The Ori Complex

**The Problem**: Yoruba philosophical anthropology, centered on the concept of ori, provides a tripartite account of the human person that integrates physical and non-physical dimensions in a way that differs from Western substance dualism.

**Proposed Framework**:

1. **Tripartite Person**: Human personality consists of three components:
   - **Ara**: The physical body
   - **Emi**: The soul/life force -- "invisible and intangible -- the life force breathed into each human by Olodumare [the supreme deity]. Emi is what gives life to the body."
   - **Ori**: The "inner head" -- which has two aspects:
     - **Ori-ode**: The "outer head" or physical head, the "mere vessel or physical container"
     - **Ori-inu**: The "inner or spiritual head," the "nucleus of a person's being and the embodiment of their unique destiny"

2. **The Ori Complex**: "At the individual level, the concept is expressed in the Ori complex (or soul complex) -- an extensionist construct that explains the interaction between tangible and intangible existences." The three parts are: Ori (consciousness), Ori Inu (inner self), and Iponri (higher self).

3. **Integration Rather Than Interaction**: The Yoruba concept "stands in contrast to Western Cartesian mind-body dualism by presenting a unified framework where body, soul, and spirit interact as an integrated whole directed toward fulfilling one's destiny." The person is not composed of two alien substances forced together; the physical and spiritual are inherently integrated, both oriented toward the fulfillment of the person's chosen destiny (which is selected before birth).

**Strengths**:
- The tripartite model is richer than Western dualism's binary (mind/body), providing a more nuanced account of personhood
- The integration of destiny (purpose) into the ontology connects the mind-body question to questions of meaning and value
- Avoids the sharp dichotomy that generates the Cartesian interaction problem
- The concept of ori-inu (inner head) as the seat of destiny provides a non-reductive account of personal identity

**Weaknesses**:
- The framework is embedded in a specific religious/cultural tradition, making it difficult to evaluate philosophically in isolation
- The "integration" claim needs more specification: how exactly do ara, emi, and ori relate causally?
- The pre-birth choice of destiny raises questions about freedom and determinism
- Limited development as a systematic philosophical position (much of the philosophical articulation has been done by scholars reconstructing oral tradition)

**Comparison to Western approaches**: The tripartite structure (body, soul, inner head) is reminiscent of Plato's tripartite soul (appetitive, spirited, rational) and of Aristotelian hylomorphism (body informed by soul). The integration of destiny into personal ontology has no close Western parallel but resonates with existentialist themes about authenticity and self-realization.

**Key scholarly sources**: "Integrating Body, Mind, and Spirit Through the Yoruba Concept of Ori" (https://read.aupress.ca/read/sharing-breath/section/c53bd62e-abaf-4bd3-859d-20cf02db467a); Adebowale, "Soul as the Sole Determinant of Human Personality in Plato and Yoruba Traditional Thought" (http://www.cscanada.net/index.php/ccc/article/view/5406).

## Comparative Analysis

### Taxonomy of Approaches to the Interaction Problem

| Tradition | Approach | Dualist? | Mechanism |
|-----------|----------|----------|-----------|
| Samkhya | Non-causal reflection | Yes (strong) | Informational coupling without efficient causation |
| Nyaya-Vaisheshika | Atomic intermediary | Yes | Mind (manas) as material bridge between soul and senses |
| Yoga | Theistic teleology | Yes (strong) | Inherited from Samkhya; Ishvara as ground of interaction |
| Advaita Vedanta | Dissolution (non-dualism) | No | No interaction; appearance is illusory |
| Avicenna | Emanation + dual-aspect | Yes (moderate) | Soul emanated upon body; bidirectional via practical/theoretical intellect |
| Al-Ghazali | Occasionalism | Irrelevant | God is sole cause; no genuine creaturely causation |
| Mulla Sadra | Processual continuity | Transcended | Soul-body as grades of one being in substantial motion |
| Abhidharma | Conditioned arising | Neither | Mental and physical events co-condition each other |
| Yogacara | Consciousness-only | Idealist | No external reality; all is consciousness |
| Zhu Xi | Li-qi complementarity | Weak | Pattern and matter always co-present; heart-mind is their meeting |
| Wang Yangming | Unity of knowing-acting | No | Mind and action are identical |
| Bantu | Force ontology | No | All reality is force; no gap between mental and physical |
| Yoruba | Integrated whole | Tripartite | Body, soul, and inner head are inherently integrated |

### Key Insights for The Unfinishable Map

1. **Samkhya's reflection model** is the most directly relevant to a dualist project. It shows that interaction need not be efficient causation -- informational or structural correspondence can suffice. This is compatible with the Map's Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet: consciousness need not push particles; it might "select" among possibilities, analogous to reflection selecting what appears in the mirror.

2. **Avicenna's dual-aspect model** provides the richest non-Western account of genuine bidirectional interaction. His insight that mental causation "cannot be at a specific space-time point, but must affect the whole dynamics" resonates with quantum approaches to consciousness (where mental causation operates through probability distributions rather than point-forces).

3. **Mulla Sadra's processual ontology** challenges the Map to consider whether the interaction problem might be an artifact of static substance thinking. If consciousness and matter are grades on a continuum, the "gap" that needs bridging may be illusory. This does not refute dualism but suggests that dualism might need a more dynamic formulation.

4. **Buddhist conditioned arising** shows that mental-physical causation can be explained without enduring substances. The 24 conditional relations of the Patthana provide a far more detailed taxonomy of mental-physical influence than anything in Western philosophy.

5. **African force ontology** suggests that the interaction problem may be an artifact of Western substance metaphysics. In a framework where force (rather than inert substance) is fundamental, consciousness and matter are both active, and their interaction is natural rather than anomalous.

6. **Advaita, Yogacara, and Wang Yangming** show that the interaction problem can be dissolved by denying one of its premises (that mind and body are genuinely distinct). The Map, committed to dualism, cannot take this route -- but understanding why these dissolutions are attractive helps clarify what the dualist is committed to defending.
