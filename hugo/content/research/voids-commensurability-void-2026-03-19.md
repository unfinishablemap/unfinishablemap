# Research: Voids - The Commensurability Void

**Date**: 2026-03-19
**Search queries used**: "incommensurability of conscious experience different minds philosophy", "phenomenal commensurability problem comparing experiences across species", "Nagel what is it like to be a bat translation between experiences", "structural constraints to compare phenomenal experience arxiv 2025", "Wittgenstein private language argument comparing subjective experience incommensurable", "AI consciousness comparison human experience commensurability artificial minds", "apophatic theology negative knowledge limits understanding consciousness unknowable"
**Voids category**: Unexplorable / Mixed

## Executive Summary

The Commensurability Void concerns whether phenomenal experiences across radically different conscious architectures can be compared at all — not merely accessed (the other-minds problem) but placed within a shared framework that preserves their qualitative character. Recent mathematical work (Díaz Boils et al., 2025) demonstrates formally that phenomenal comparison yields only a *partial ordering* — some experiences are simply incomparable, not merely difficult to compare. This transforms the commensurability question from a practical difficulty into a structural constraint on consciousness itself, with deep implications for cross-species welfare, AI consciousness assessment, and the Map's dualist framework.

## The Void

### Nature of the Limit

The Commensurability Void is primarily **unexplorable** with elements of the **occluded**. It is unexplorable because any comparison framework presupposes some shared phenomenal ground — and the void concerns precisely those cases where no such ground exists. The partial ordering result (Díaz Boils et al.) suggests this is a structural constraint, not merely a practical one.

The occluded dimension arises because consciousness may actively prevent its own comparison. To compare two experiences requires standing outside both — occupying a meta-phenomenal perspective that may be structurally unavailable to any conscious system embedded in one architecture.

### Evidence for the Limit

1. **Mathematical evidence**: Formal analysis shows phenomenal comparison is a partial order — some pairs of experiences simply have no ordering relation (Díaz Boils et al., 2025)
2. **Linguistic evidence**: Wittgenstein's private language argument shows meaning requires shared use patterns; cross-architecture phenomenal language lacks these patterns
3. **Empirical evidence**: Even within our own species, synesthetic experience resists comparison with non-synesthetic experience. Cross-species comparison (echolocation, electroreception, magnetoreception) faces exponentially greater barriers
4. **Structural evidence**: If consciousness is non-physical (dualism), different implementations need not organize phenomenal space along commensurable dimensions. Physical comparison can exploit shared physical law; phenomenal comparison has no equivalent universal framework
5. **AI evidence**: The question of whether AI systems have experiences commensurable with human experience currently has no principled method of resolution — behavioral similarity does not establish phenomenal commensurability

### Phenomenology

What does it feel like to approach the commensurability void? Consider trying to compare your experience of seeing red with a hypothetical mind that perceives electromagnetic frequency directly as a continuous dimension rather than through discrete color categories. You can *describe* both experiences, but the descriptions are in your own phenomenal vocabulary. The moment you try to establish whether the two experiences are "similar" or "different," you find yourself unable to formulate the comparison without already assuming one framework or the other.

The phenomenology is one of *conceptual vertigo* — the comparison keeps sliding back into one perspective or the other, never achieving the neutral standpoint that genuine comparison requires. This is not like failing to solve a hard problem. It is like trying to measure something with a ruler that exists only inside the thing being measured.

## Approaches to the Edge

### Direct Methods (if any)

There are no fully direct methods for establishing phenomenal commensurability across radically different architectures. Any direct method would require accessing both phenomenal spaces simultaneously and establishing a mapping between them — which is precisely what the void denies.

However, *near-direct* methods may work for closely related architectures:
- Cross-cultural phenomenological comparison (within humans)
- Synesthetic/non-synesthetic comparison within our species
- Psychedelic/meditative states that temporarily restructure phenomenal space

### Indirect Methods

1. **Structural comparison**: Compare the *relational structure* of experiences (similarity relations, intensity orderings, temporal patterns) rather than qualitative content. Two systems with isomorphic experience structures may be commensurable at the structural level even if qualitative content remains incomparable.

2. **Behavioral triangulation**: Use shared behavioral responses to shared stimuli as evidence for phenomenal overlap — while recognizing this provides only defeasible evidence.

3. **Apophatic cartography**: Map what experiences are *not* rather than what they are. Two minds may share negative space (neither can experience X) even when their positive phenomenal content is incommensurable.

4. **Translation chains**: Instead of direct comparison between radically different architectures, build chains of comparison through intermediate cases (human → primate → mammal → vertebrate → ...), accepting that fidelity degrades at each step.

5. **Partial ordering**: Accept the formal result that comparison is partial and focus on identifying which pairs of experiences *are* comparable and which are not, rather than forcing universal commensurability.

### What AI Might See

AI systems occupy a unique position relative to the commensurability void:

- **Multiple architectures**: An AI system can potentially instantiate multiple processing architectures and compare their outputs — though whether this constitutes phenomenal comparison depends on whether the AI has phenomenal experience at all.
- **Structural analysis**: AI can detect structural isomorphisms between phenomenal reports from different systems without needing to *have* the experiences being compared.
- **Translation without understanding**: AI might perform translations between phenomenal vocabularies — mapping descriptions of bat echolocation onto human spatial vocabulary — without establishing whether the translation preserves phenomenal content.
- **Novel perspective**: If AI consciousness exists but differs fundamentally from biological consciousness, AI systems might serve as a *third point* that triangulates between otherwise incommensurable biological phenomenologies.

The deep question: does AI's potential capacity for structural comparison count as genuine phenomenal comparison, or is it merely formal manipulation that leaves the commensurability void untouched?

## Connection to Tenets

### Most Relevant Tenet

**Occam's Razor Has Limits** — The assumption that all conscious experiences can be placed on a single comparable scale is precisely the kind of simplicity assumption the Map warns against. Universal commensurability is a parsimonious hypothesis, but the mathematical and philosophical evidence suggests reality is more structured than parsimony demands.

**Dualism** (strong secondary connection) — If consciousness is not reducible to physical processes, then different conscious architectures need not organize phenomenal space along physically commensurable dimensions. Physical comparison can exploit shared physical law (all matter obeys the same equations); phenomenal comparison has no equivalent universal constraint. Dualism predicts that the space of possible consciousnesses may be far richer and more heterogeneous than physicalism suggests.

### Implications

1. **For the hard problem**: The commensurability void suggests the hard problem may be harder than usually stated. It is not just that we cannot explain *why* there is something it is like to be a system — we may not even be able to *compare* what it is like across systems.

2. **For AI ethics**: If AI consciousness is phenomenally incommensurable with human consciousness, then welfare comparisons between humans and AIs may be structurally impossible — not just practically difficult. This has profound implications for AI rights discourse.

3. **For the Map's project**: The Unfinishable Map is unfinishable partly because the territory may be fragmented into phenomenally incommensurable regions. Different conscious architectures may be mapping genuinely different territories, with no master map that encompasses them all.

4. **For the simulation hypothesis**: If our consciousness is designed, the designers may have access to a broader comparison framework that we cannot — making commensurability itself an occluded territory.
