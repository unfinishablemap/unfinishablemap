## Executive Summary

The "left-hemisphere interpreter" is Michael Gazzaniga and Joseph LeDoux's name for a language-dominant system that spontaneously constructs plausible causal narratives for behaviour whose real drivers it cannot access. In split-brain patients, the verbally competent left hemisphere confabulates reasons for actions initiated by the mute right hemisphere — the canonical chicken-claw/snow-shovel demonstration. This finding anchors a broader empirical family (choice blindness, post-hoc rationalization) and a philosophical tradition (Dennett's "center of narrative gravity") that together mount the strongest naturalistic case for illusionism about the self: that felt unity is a retrospective story the interpreter tells, not a metaphysically unified subject. This note steelmans that case honestly. The Map's discriminating response — to be developed downstream — is that the interpreter explains the *confabulation of reasons* without explaining away the *phenomenal unity already present in the moment*, since the interpreter operates on contents that are, by hypothesis, already conscious. The gap is a discriminating question, not a proof.

## Key Sources

### The Integrated Mind (Gazzaniga & LeDoux, 1978)
- **Citation**: Gazzaniga, M. S., & LeDoux, J. E. (1978). *The Integrated Mind*. New York: Plenum Press. (Interpreter material pp. 132–161.)
- **Type**: Book (peer-reviewed academic press)
- **Verification**: Confirmed via Springer Nature, NYU Scholars, Semantic Scholar. Published work, not a preprint.
- **Key points**:
  - Introduces the observation that the left hemisphere generates explanations reconciling new information (including the right hemisphere's behaviourally expressed choices) with what it already knows.
  - Source of the classic paradigm in which the right hemisphere sees one image and the left another; the left hemisphere then narrates the whole sequence as if it authored both.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral as data; the *illusionist interpretation* layered onto it conflicts with Tenet 1 (Dualism) and is the rival to be engaged.

- **Type**: Book
- **Verification**: Confirmed via Wikipedia references and secondary sources; not verified page-by-page at publisher.
- **Key points**: Where the term "interpreter" is crystallized and generalized beyond split-brain cases toward a standing feature of the intact left hemisphere.

### Who's in Charge? (Gazzaniga, 2011)
- **Citation**: Gazzaniga, M. S. (2011). *Who's in Charge? Free Will and the Science of the Brain*. New York: Ecco/HarperCollins.
- **Type**: Trade book (popular synthesis)
- **Verification**: Widely attested; treat as secondary/popular, not primary evidence.
- **Key points**: Uses the interpreter to argue that the felt sense of a unified, in-charge self is a construction — the most illusionist-friendly framing in Gazzaniga's own corpus.

### Failure to detect mismatches... (Johansson, Hall, Sikström & Olsson, 2005)
- **Citation**: Johansson, P., Hall, L., Sikström, S., & Olsson, A. (2005). Failure to detect mismatches between intention and outcome in a simple decision task. *Science*, 310(5745), 116–119. DOI: 10.1126/science.1111709
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed empirical paper
- **Verification**: DOI and full author list confirmed at science.org and Lund University (LUCS) repository. Published, not a preprint.
- **Key points**:
  - "Choice blindness": participants choosing the more attractive of two faces failed to notice when a sleight-of-hand swapped their chosen face for the rejected one, and then *confabulated reasons* for the choice they had not made.
  - Extends the interpreter phenomenon from the surgical edge case (split brain) to neurologically intact adults making ordinary choices.
- **Tenet alignment**: Neutral as data; strengthens the naturalistic rival.

### The Self as a Center of Narrative Gravity (Dennett, 1992)
- **Citation**: Dennett, D. C. (1992). The self as a center of narrative gravity. In F. S. Kessel, P. M. Cole, & D. L. Johnson (Eds.), *Self and Consciousness: Multiple Perspectives* (pp. 103–115). Hillsdale, NJ: Lawrence Erlbaum.
- **Type**: Book chapter
- **Verification**: Editors, publisher, and page range confirmed via PhilPapers and Internet Archive.
- **Key points**:
  - The self is "an abstract object, a theorist's fiction" — analogous to a center of gravity: real as an explanatory posit, but not a further concrete thing with properties of its own.
  - The narrative is spun by the brain's interpretive machinery; the "author" is a product of the telling, not its source. This is the strong illusionist reading the interpreter data are taken to support.
- **Tenet alignment**: **Conflicts** with Tenet 1 (Dualism) and the Map's claim of a genuinely unified experiencing subject. Primary steelman target.

### Split brain: divided perception but undivided consciousness (Pinto et al., 2017)
- **Citation**: Pinto, Y., Neville, D. A., Otten, M., Corballis, P. M., Lamme, V. A. F., de Haan, E. H. F., Foschi, N., & Fabri, M. (2017). Split brain: divided perception but undivided consciousness. *Brain*, 140(5), 1231–1237.
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed empirical paper
- **Verification**: Confirmed at academic.oup.com (Brain) and PubMed (PMID 28122878). Published, not a preprint.
- **Key points**:
  - Two split-brain patients could respond to stimuli across the whole visual field with either hand, contra the textbook "two independent conscious agents" story.
  - Argues for **one conscious agent with split perception**: unified consciousness, divided input streams.
- **Why it matters here**: Complicates the strong "two selves / no unified self" gloss on split-brain data — a live empirical crack in the illusionist edifice that the Map can cite without over-claiming.

### The Split-Brain Phenomenon Revisited (Pinto, de Haan & Lamme, 2017)
- **Citation**: Pinto, Y., de Haan, E. H. F., & Lamme, V. A. F. (2017). The split-brain phenomenon revisited: A single conscious agent with split perception. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 21(11), 835–851.
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed review
- **Verification**: Title/venue confirmed via ScienceDirect; issue/pages from secondary listing — verify pagination before citing.
- **Key points**: Companion theoretical piece arguing the split-brain literature has been over-read; conscious unity may survive callosal section even as perception fragments.

## Major Positions

### Strong Illusionism (the rival to steelman)
- **Proponents**: Dennett (narrative gravity); Gazzaniga in his more deflationary moments; broadly, illusionists about phenomenal unity.
- **Core claim**: The felt unity of a single experiencing subject is a *retrospective narrative* produced by an interpreter module. There is no unified subject "behind" the story; the story is all there is. Confabulation is not a malfunction but a window onto the normal mechanism of selfhood.
- **Key arguments**:
  1. The interpreter demonstrably fabricates reasons (chicken-claw/snow-shovel) with full subjective conviction and no access to the true cause.
  2. Choice blindness shows this is not a split-brain artifact but the ordinary operation of intact minds.
  3. If the sense of authorship is systematically unreliable, the parsimonious conclusion is that there is no unified author — only a center of narrative gravity.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct rival to Tenet 1. This is the strongest naturalistic case that the Map's unified subject is a user-illusion. It must be built at full strength before any departure is marked (per [direct-refutation-discipline](/project/direct-refutation-discipline/)).

### The Map's Response (to be developed downstream — not a proof)
- **Core move**: Distinguish two things the interpreter is invoked to explain:
  1. The **confabulation of reasons** — post-hoc causal stories about *why* an action occurred.
  2. The **phenomenal unity of the moment** — that there is something it is like to undergo a single, bound field of experience *now*.
- The interpreter data establish (1) decisively. They do **not** touch (2): the interpreter operates *on already-conscious contents*. It narrates experiences that are, by hypothesis, already given as experienced. Confabulating a false reason for pointing at the shovel presupposes that the pointing, the seeing, and the felt puzzlement are already present as experience for a subject.
- **Concession (do not hide)**: The interpreter genuinely establishes that introspective reports of *causation and authorship* are unreliable, that the narrating self is partly a construction, and that a great deal of what feels like unified deliberate agency is retrospective. The Map should concede all of this plainly.
- **Discriminating question, not a proof**: *Does the interpreter construct the phenomenal unity, or merely narrate over a unity that is already phenomenally present?* The confabulation evidence is equally consistent with both. It cannot, on its own, decide against a genuinely unified experiencer — so it under-determines the metaphysical conclusion. This is a possibility-not-proof result: the Map does not prove unity is fundamental; it shows the interpreter argument does not refute it (cf. [evidential-status-discipline](/concepts/evidential-status-discipline/)).
- **Pinto leverage**: The 2017 "undivided consciousness" results are an independent empirical reason not to over-read even the split-brain case as "two selves," weakening the strongest fragmentation premise.

### Related tradition: post-hoc rationalization / "telling more than we can know"
- **Proponents**: Nisbett & Wilson (1977, "Telling More Than We Can Know") — worth adding as background; the interpreter and choice blindness sit in this lineage.
- **Core claim**: Verbal reports of higher cognitive processes are often theory-driven confabulations, not introspective read-outs.
- **Relation to tenets**: Neutral background; strengthens the rival's empirical base. (Nisbett & Wilson 1977 *Psychological Review* 84(3):231–259 — **not searched this session; verify before citing.**)

## Key Debates

### Does confabulation of reasons entail illusion of the experiencer?
- **Sides**: Illusionists (Dennett) say the machinery that fabricates reasons is the same machinery that fabricates the self, so unreliability propagates all the way down. The Map (and non-illusionist realists about phenomenal consciousness) say authorship-of-reasons and unity-of-experience are separable, and only the former is impugned.
- **Core disagreement**: Whether the interpreter *constitutes* phenomenal unity or *operates on* it.
- **Current state**: Unresolved; the empirical evidence under-determines the two readings.

### One conscious agent or two? (split-brain)
- **Sides**: Classical Sperry/Gazzaniga reading (two independent conscious systems) vs. Pinto et al. (one conscious agent, split perception).
- **Core disagreement**: Whether callosotomy divides consciousness or only divides information access.
- **Current state**: Actively contested post-2017; see also the cross-cueing exchange (Volz & Gazzaniga; de Haan et al.) in *Brain* 141(3).
