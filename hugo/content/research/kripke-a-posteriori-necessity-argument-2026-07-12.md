## Executive Summary

Saul Kripke's modal argument (Lecture III of *Naming and Necessity*) is the fourth co-equal pillar of the standard anti-materialist convergence case, alongside the knowledge argument, philosophical zombies, the explanatory gap, and Nagel's bat — yet it is the only pillar without a dedicated Map page (currently 2 sub-sections across the corpus). The argument turns Kripke's own apparatus — rigid designators, the necessity of identity, and a posteriori necessity — against the mind-brain (type-)identity theory. If "pain = C-fibre firing" is an identity between rigid designators, it must (if true) be *necessarily* true. Yet it seems contingent: we can conceive pain without C-fibres and C-fibres without pain. Kripke concedes the parallel "heat = molecular motion" also seems contingent but explains that illusion away (we are really imagining the *sensation* heat produces, not heat itself, being absent). The move that rescues heat fails for pain, because pain has no appearance/reality gap — pain is individuated by how it feels, so there is no contingent mode of presentation to reassign the intuition to. Hence the apparent contingency is genuine, the identity is false, and type-identity materialism is false. The live materialist reply is the **phenomenal concepts strategy** / a posteriori physicalism, which the Map already covers in a dedicated page. The argument is congenial to the Map's Tenet 1 (dualism) but framework-relative, not a knockdown.

- **Key points**:
  - The phenomenal concept strategy (PCS) is the physicalist response that "locates the gap in the relationship between our *concepts* of physical processes and our *concepts* of consciousness, rather than in the relationship between physical processes and consciousness themselves" — concepts are dualistic, reality monistic, analogous to "heat"/"molecular motion" being two concepts of one property.

## Major Positions

### Kripke's modal argument against type-identity (the target thesis)
- **Proponent**: Saul Kripke (*Naming and Necessity*, Lecture III).
- **The machinery**:
  1. **Rigid designator** — a term that picks out the same object (or, for kind/property terms, the same property) in every possible world in which it exists. Proper names and natural-kind terms are rigid; most definite descriptions ("the inventor of bifocals") are not.
  2. **Necessity of identity** — if 'a' and 'b' are rigid designators and 'a = b' is true, then it is *necessarily* true. There are no contingent identities between rigid designators; if they co-refer in one world they co-refer in all.
  3. **A posteriori necessity** — some necessary truths are known only empirically: "Hesperus = Phosphorus", "water = H2O", "heat = molecular motion". Necessity (a modal/metaphysical status) comes apart from apriority (an epistemic status).
- **The argument proper**:
  1. Type-identity theory asserts "pain = C-fibre firing" as a theoretical identity. Both 'pain' and 'C-fibre firing' are plausibly rigid designators of properties/kinds.
  2. So if the identity is true, it is *necessary* — true in all possible worlds.
  3. But the identity *seems contingent*: it is conceivable that pain occur without C-fibre firing (disembodied/differently-embodied pain) and that C-fibres fire without pain (a zombie-like case).
  4. **The heat manoeuvre**: For "heat = molecular motion" the same apparent contingency arises, but Kripke explains it away. 'Heat' is rigid, but we *fix its reference* via a contingent mode of presentation — the sensation heat causes in us. When we think we are imagining "heat without molecular motion", we are really imagining a world where that *sensation* occurs without molecular motion (or molecular motion without a creature to feel it). The rigidly-designated property (heat = molecular motion) is untouched; only the contingent reference-fixer is being varied. So the identity stays necessary despite seeming contingent.
  5. **Why the manoeuvre fails for pain**: Pain has **no appearance/reality distinction**. There is no gap between the pain and how the pain seems, because pain *just is* the way it feels — the phenomenal quality is its essence, not a contingent mode of presentation of some hidden underlying property. So there is no distinct "sensation of pain, as opposed to pain itself" to which the contingency intuition could be reassigned. (Kripke's paraphrased formulation: to be in the same epistemic situation that would obtain if one had a pain *is* to have a pain, whereas to be in the epistemic situation of "feeling heat" need not be for heat to be present. **UNVERIFIED verbatim** — see Candidate Quotes below.)
  6. Therefore the apparent contingency of "pain = C-fibre firing" is *genuine possibility*, not illusion. By the necessity of identity, a genuinely contingent identity between rigid designators is no identity at all.
  7. **Conclusion**: "pain = C-fibre firing" is false; **type-identity materialism is false.**
- **Scope note**: The argument targets **type-identity** specifically (property/kind identities). It is standardly read as leaving *token*-identity and some functionalist positions less directly hit — though the underlying conceivability point generalises to the broader anti-physicalist family. (Natural companion to the `type-identity-theory` research note produced this same session.)
- **Relation to site tenets**: Strongly congenial to **Tenet 1 (dualism / irreducibility of consciousness)** — it is a modal route to the same conclusion as the knowledge argument and zombies. But framework-relative: its force depends on (a) treating 'pain' as rigid, (b) the conceivability→possibility bridge (Tenet-consistent caution: see the Map's `conceivability-possibility-inference`), and (c) the denial that pain has any mode of presentation — exactly what the phenomenal-concepts reply contests.

### Type-B materialism / a posteriori physicalism (the main reply) — the phenomenal concepts strategy
- **Proponents**: Brian Loar, Christopher Hill, David Papineau, Ned Block, Katalin Balog (a posteriori / type-B physicalists).
- **Core claim**: Kripke is wrong that pain lacks a contingent mode of presentation. **Phenomenal concepts** *are* the mode of presentation. We conceptualise one and the same brain state under two independent concepts — a physical/theoretical concept ('C-fibre firing') and a phenomenal concept (the recognitional "this feeling"). The appearance of contingency arises from the conceptual independence of these two concepts, not from any metaphysical distinctness of the properties. So "pain = C-fibre firing" can be a necessary a posteriori identity after all — the psychological explanation of the illusion that Kripke says is unavailable for pain *is* available, located in the concepts rather than in a distinct property.
- **Relation to site tenets**: Direct challenge to the Kripkean route to Tenet 1. **Already covered by the Map's `phenomenal-concepts-strategy` page**, which notes Chalmers's "master argument" dilemma against PCS — cross-reference, don't restate.

### Disputing the conceivability → possibility inference (second reply)
- **Proponents**: various type-B physicalists; also relevant: the a posteriori-necessity framework itself.
- **Core claim**: Even granting pain has no appearance/reality gap, the step from "we can conceive pain without C-fibres" to "it is metaphysically possible" is exactly what a posteriori necessity was supposed to block for water/heat. The materialist argues the conceivability here is ideal-negative at best and does not license a possibility claim.
- **Relation to site tenets**: The Map's own `conceivability-possibility-inference` page treats when this bridge holds; the Map is cautious about treating any single conceivability argument as decisive, consistent with Tenet 5 (Occam's razor has limits — no cheap victories).

## Key Debates

### Does pain really lack a mode of presentation?
- **Sides**: Kripke (yes — pain's essence is its feel, no reference-fixing gap) vs. phenomenal-concept theorists (no — the phenomenal concept is the mode of presentation).
- **Core disagreement**: whether the "appearance = reality" feature of pain is a metaphysical fact about the property or an artefact of how we conceptualise it.
- **Current state**: Ongoing and central. Chalmers's master argument (already on the Map) presses PCS with a dilemma; type-B physicalists have not, on the Map's current reading, stably escaped it.

### Is 'pain' actually a rigid designator?
- **Sides**: Kripke's argument presupposes it; some functionalists deny that mental-state terms rigidly designate a single property (they may designate role-fillers that vary by world).
- **Core disagreement**: the semantics of mental predicates.
- **Current state**: Contested; often folded into the type-identity vs. functionalism dispute.

### Does the argument refute all materialism or only type-identity?
- **Sides**: Read narrowly it targets type-identity; read via its conceivability core it feeds the broader zombie/knowledge-argument case against physicalism generally.
- **Current state**: Standard teaching treats Lecture III as an argument against *identity* theory specifically; its generalisation is the province of the 2D conceivability argument (Chalmers).
