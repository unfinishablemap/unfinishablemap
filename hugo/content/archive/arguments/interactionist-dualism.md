Interactionist dualism holds that consciousness is irreducible to physical processes and causally influences the physical world. This is not merely the denial of materialism—it is a positive claim about the nature of mind and its relationship to matter. This page presents the cumulative case.

## The Core Position

Interactionist dualism makes three claims:

1. **Irreducibility**: Consciousness cannot be identified with or reduced to physical properties
2. **Causal efficacy**: Consciousness genuinely causes physical effects in the brain
3. **Bidirectional interaction**: Physical brain states influence conscious experience, and conscious states influence physical brain states

The first claim is shared with all forms of dualism, including epiphenomenalism. The second distinguishes interactionism from epiphenomenal views. The third specifies the nature of the mind-body relation: not mere correlation, not one-way causation, but genuine two-way interaction.

## Argument 1: The Explanatory Gap

**Premise 1**: Physical explanations describe structure, function, and dynamics—how parts relate and systems evolve.

**Premise 2**: Conscious experience has qualitative, intrinsic character—what it is *like* to see red, feel pain, taste coffee.

**Premise 3**: No description of structure, function, or dynamics entails or explains qualitative character.

**Conclusion**: Consciousness has properties that physical explanation cannot reach.

This is Joseph Levine's [explanatory-gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/), strengthened by David Chalmers into the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/). The gap is not epistemic—not a matter of insufficient data. It is structural. Physics describes relations between quantities; qualia are intrinsic qualities. No amount of relational information yields intrinsic character.

The standard materialist responses—eliminativism, illusionism, identity theory—all fail to close this gap. Eliminativism denies what is most evident. Illusionism posits an illusion without an experiencer. Identity theory asserts consciousness is neural activity without explaining why that activity is experienced.

## Argument 2: Conceivability and Zombies

**Premise 1**: We can coherently conceive of beings physically identical to us but lacking all conscious experience ([philosophical-zombies](/concepts/philosophical-zombies/)).

**Premise 2**: What is coherently conceivable is metaphysically possible.

**Premise 3**: If zombies are possible, consciousness is not necessitated by physical facts.

**Conclusion**: Consciousness is something over and above the physical.

The crucial premise is 2. Unlike scientific identities (water = H2O), phenomenal concepts give us direct access to their referents. We don't merely describe consciousness from outside—we instantiate it. When we conceive of zombies, we positively grasp a scenario where all physical facts obtain and phenomenal facts do not. The coherence is not due to ignorance.

Chalmers: "Conceivability is not a perfect guide to possibility, but when we can positively conceive of a scenario—when we can coherently describe a possible world where it obtains—that is strong evidence of possibility."

## Argument 3: Mary's Room

**Premise 1**: Mary knows all physical facts about colour vision while living in a black-and-white room.

**Premise 2**: When Mary sees red for the first time, she learns something new—what red looks like.

**Premise 3**: What Mary learns is not deducible from physical facts.

**Conclusion**: There are facts about conscious experience beyond physical facts.

This is Frank Jackson's [knowledge-argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/). Critics offer "ability hypothesis" responses: Mary gains a new ability (recognizing, imagining red), not new knowledge. But the intuition persists—Mary learns what red *is like*, a fact about phenomenal character that complete physical knowledge did not include.

The knowledge argument targets physicalism specifically. If all facts are physical facts, complete physical knowledge should include all facts. Mary's surprise upon seeing red suggests it doesn't.

## Argument 4: The Unity of Consciousness

**Premise 1**: Conscious experience is unified—there is a single subject experiencing multiple contents.

**Premise 2**: Physical systems are composed of discrete parts (neurons, molecules, atoms).

**Premise 3**: No physical mechanism explains how discrete parts compose a single unified subject.

**Conclusion**: The subject of experience is not composed of parts—it is a simple substance.

William Hasker develops this argument for substance dualism. The [binding-problem](/concepts/binding-problem/) illustrates: color, shape, and motion are processed in different brain regions, yet we experience one unified object. Neural synchrony and global workspace theories propose mechanisms for *integration*, but integration of information is not identity of subject. Synchronized neurons remain many neurons. A single subject experiencing them remains unexplained.

Hasker's "emergent dualism" accepts that the soul emerges from brain function while being non-physical—generated by the brain but ontologically distinct, with spatial location but not material composition.

## Argument 5: Intentionality

**Premise 1**: Mental states are about things—beliefs are about facts, desires are about outcomes, fears are directed at objects.

**Premise 2**: Physical states are not intrinsically about anything—a rock is not about mountains.

**Premise 3**: No physical account of intentionality (causal, informational, teleological) successfully reduces aboutness to physical relations.

**Conclusion**: Intentionality is a non-physical feature of mind.

Franz Brentano called [intentionality](/concepts/intentionality/) "the mark of the mental." John Searle's Chinese Room shows that syntax (symbol manipulation) doesn't yield semantics (meaning). A computational system following rules doesn't thereby understand what the symbols mean. Aboutness resists naturalization.

Phenomenal Intentionality Theory deepens the point: intentionality may *depend on* phenomenal consciousness. If so, explaining intentionality requires first explaining experience. The two problems are linked.

## Argument 6: Self-Stultification of Epiphenomenalism

**Premise 1**: We discuss consciousness—we write about qualia, debate the hard problem, report our experiences.

**Premise 2**: If consciousness is epiphenomenal (causally inert), our discussions are not caused by consciousness.

**Premise 3**: If our discussions are not caused by consciousness, we have no reason to trust them as accurate reports about consciousness.

**Conclusion**: [Epiphenomenalism](/arguments/epiphenomenalism/) undermines itself; consciousness must be causally relevant.

This argument is often overlooked but decisive against epiphenomenal dualism. The very fact that we discuss consciousness proves it is not epiphenomenal. An epiphenomenon—causally inert by definition—could not influence the neurons that move our mouths or type our words. Yet here we are, arguing about qualia.

If consciousness doesn't cause behavior, then when you say "I see red," your utterance is caused entirely by neural states that are not themselves caused by your seeing red. Your report is not about your experience—it's about nothing, or about neural states that happen to co-occur with experience. But then why should we trust introspective reports at all?

Interactionist dualism avoids this problem. Consciousness causes our reports *about* consciousness, making those reports genuine evidence about the nature of experience.

## Argument 7: Quantum Indeterminacy

**Premise 1**: Standard quantum mechanics describes probabilities, not certainties, for measurement outcomes.

**Premise 2**: The "measurement problem" shows that quantum mechanics does not specify what triggers collapse.

**Premise 3**: This leaves conceptual room for consciousness to influence which outcome actualizes.

**Conclusion**: Physics does not exclude [mental-causation](/concepts/mental-causation/); the [causal-closure](/concepts/causal-closure/) objection fails.

This is the key to interactionism. Classical physics was deterministic—every event had sufficient physical causes, leaving no room for mental causation. But quantum mechanics is indeterministic. The Schrödinger equation governs continuous evolution; collapse into definite outcomes remains unexplained.

Henry Stapp proposes that consciousness affects which neural pattern actualizes through the quantum Zeno effect—rapid attention holds quantum states in place, biasing probabilities toward intended outcomes. Whether this specific mechanism is correct matters less than what it shows: physics has a gap, and consciousness might fill it.

No energy injection is required. Consciousness selects among outcomes physics leaves undetermined, all of which conserve energy. This addresses the conservation law objection directly.

## The Cumulative Case

Each argument addresses a different aspect:

| Argument | What It Shows |
|----------|---------------|
| Explanatory Gap | Physical explanation cannot reach qualia |
| Zombie Conceivability | Consciousness is not necessitated by physics |
| Mary's Room | There are non-physical phenomenal facts |
| Unity of Consciousness | The subject is simple, not composite |
| Intentionality | Aboutness is non-physical |
| Self-Stultification | Consciousness must be causally relevant |
| Quantum Indeterminacy | Physics permits mental causation |

Arguments 1-5 establish dualism: consciousness has properties that physics cannot capture. Argument 6 rules out epiphenomenalism: consciousness must cause behavior. Argument 7 shows how interaction is possible: quantum indeterminacy provides the opening.

Together, they support the full interactionist position: consciousness is irreducible *and* causally efficacious *and* interacts bidirectionally with the brain.

## Objections and Responses

### The Pairing Problem

**Objection** (Kim): If minds are non-spatial, what pairs *this* mind with *this* body? Physical causation depends on spatial relations.

**Response**: Minds have spatial location—in the brain regions where they causally interact. the project's framework locates consciousness where quantum effects occur. This preserves dualism (minds are ontologically distinct) while granting spatial presence (minds occupy brain space).

### Conservation Laws

**Objection**: Mental causation would violate energy conservation.

**Response**: Consciousness selects among outcomes physics leaves undetermined—outcomes with equal energy. No energy is added; conservation equations are satisfied. Quantum selection is not energy injection.

### The Exclusion Argument

**Objection** (Kim): Physical properties "screen off" mental ones. If physical state P suffices for effect E, mental property M is redundant.

**Response**: At quantum indeterminacies, P does not suffice for E. Physics determines probabilities, not outcomes. Mental properties are causally relevant precisely where physics is insufficient.

### Parsimony

**Objection**: Dualism posits two kinds of stuff when one might suffice.

**Response**: A theory that explains nothing is not parsimonious—it's empty. Materialism explains mechanisms but not experience. Dualism explains both, and the additional ontology is justified by the additional explananda. See [Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits).

### Decoherence

**Objection** (Tegmark): Quantum coherence in the brain decoheres in femtoseconds—too fast for neural timescales.

**Response**: Decoherence explains why macroscopic superpositions don't persist; it doesn't explain why specific outcomes occur. Consciousness may influence which classical state emerges from decoherence, not maintain coherence across neural timescales.
