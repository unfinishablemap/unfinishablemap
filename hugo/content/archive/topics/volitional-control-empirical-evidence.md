The phenomenology of willing — the first-person experience of initiating, sustaining, and vetoing actions — is sometimes dismissed as an unreliable guide to what the brain actually does. The Unfinishable Map argues otherwise. Multiple independent empirical lines now confirm that the felt structure of volition tracks real causal distinctions in the nervous system. Clinical dissociations, brain stimulation experiments, implicit timing measures, and neuroplasticity studies converge on a single conclusion: the experience of agency is not a post-hoc story the brain tells itself. It reflects — imperfectly but genuinely — the causal architecture of voluntary action. This article assembles that evidence, distinguishes what each line establishes from what it merely suggests, and explains why the pattern favours [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) over [epiphenomenalist](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) alternatives.

For the philosophical debate over the Libet experiments and phenomenological structure, see [Phenomenology of Volitional Control](/topics/volitional-control/). For the broader empirical case that consciousness causally selects among neural outcomes, see [Empirical Evidence for Consciousness-Selecting](/topics/empirical-evidence-for-consciousness-selecting/).

## Clinical Dissociations: Agency and Ownership Come Apart

The strongest empirical evidence that the sense of agency tracks real causal structure comes from clinical populations where agency and ownership dissociate.

**Schizophrenic delusions of control.** Patients with first-rank symptoms report that their bodies move but that someone or something else is causing the movements. Gallagher (2000) analysed this phenomenology carefully: ownership remains intact — "this is my arm" — while agency vanishes — "I am not the one moving it." The comparator model developed by Frith (1992) and refined by Blakemore, Wolpert, and Frith (2002) explains why. In normal voluntary action, the motor system generates an efference copy — a prediction of the sensory consequences of the intended movement. When the actual sensory feedback matches the prediction, the movement is experienced as self-generated. In schizophrenia, the comparator mechanism malfunctions: predicted and actual feedback diverge, and the movement is attributed to an external source.

The comparator model makes testable predictions. Blakemore et al. (1998) demonstrated that healthy subjects cannot tickle themselves because the efference copy predicts the sensory consequences of self-generated touch, attenuating the tickle response. Schizophrenic patients *can* tickle themselves — their comparator fails to cancel self-generated sensation, consistent with the same mechanism that produces delusions of control.

**Anarchic hand syndrome.** Patients with medial frontal or callosal lesions experience a hand that performs goal-directed actions — reaching for objects, unbuttoning clothes — that the patient does not intend and cannot stop. Ownership is preserved: "that is my hand." Agency is absent: "I am not doing this." Della Sala, Marchetti, and Spinnler (1991) documented cases where the anarchic hand actively opposed the patient's voluntary actions with the other hand, producing inter-manual conflict. The hand's behaviour is not random — it responds to affordances in the environment — but it operates outside the patient's volitional control.

**Anosognosia for hemiplegia.** After right-hemisphere stroke, some patients deny that their paralysed limb is impaired, insisting they have just moved it when they have not (Berti et al., 2005). Here agency is falsely present while execution is absent — the mirror image of anarchic hand. Motor intention circuitry generates the experience of willing, but damage to monitoring systems prevents the mismatch between intention and outcome from being detected.

These three dissociations triangulate a common architecture:

| Condition | Ownership | Agency | Movement |
|-----------|-----------|--------|----------|
| Normal voluntary action | Present | Present | Present |
| Schizophrenic delusion of control | Present | Absent | Present |
| Anarchic hand | Present | Absent | Present |
| Anosognosia | Present | Falsely present | Absent |

The pattern is systematic. If agency were mere confabulation — a story generated after every movement regardless of its actual cause — these selective dissociations would be unexplained. A confabulation mechanism would either always attribute agency (failing in schizophrenia and anarchic hand) or always deny it (failing in normal action). The fact that agency tracks the integrity of specific neural circuits — the comparator system, medial frontal cortex, right-hemisphere monitoring networks — demonstrates that the phenomenology reflects causal architecture, not narrative convenience.

## Brain Stimulation: Intention Without Execution

Desmurget et al. (2009) provided direct evidence that conscious intention and motor execution occupy distinct neural territory. During awake neurosurgery, electrical stimulation of the posterior parietal cortex produced conscious intentions to move — and at higher intensities, the false belief of having moved — without any muscle activity. Stimulation of premotor cortex produced actual movements that patients denied initiating.

This double dissociation is significant. If the experience of willing were simply the brain's interpretation of detecting its own motor output, stimulating the motor system should produce the experience of willing. It does not — it produces movement without intention. Conversely, stimulating parietal regions produces intention without movement. The two come apart cleanly.

Sjöberg (2024) extended this logic with a different methodology. Patients who had the supplementary motor area (SMA) resected — the region generating the readiness potential that Libet measured — retained their subjective sense of willing. They reported maximal effort to move despite being unable to execute voluntary actions. Automatic movements like walking remained intact. The neural machinery of movement initiation was gone, but the phenomenology of willing persisted.

Together, these findings establish that the experience of agency is generated by neural systems distinct from those that execute movement. The phenomenology of willing is not a readout of motor output. It is produced independently, by circuits that can be selectively damaged, stimulated, or preserved.

## Intentional Binding: An Implicit Measure of Agency

The sense of agency is not only available through introspective report. Haggard, Clark, and Kalogeras (2002) discovered intentional binding — a temporal compression between a voluntary action and its outcome. When subjects press a button voluntarily and hear a tone 250 milliseconds later, they perceive the action as occurring later and the tone as occurring earlier than their actual times. The perceived interval shrinks. This compression does not occur for involuntary movements produced by transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS).

Intentional binding provides an implicit, non-introspective measure of the sense of agency. Subjects do not report whether they feel in control; instead, their temporal perception shifts in ways that correlate with voluntary action. Moore and Obhi (2012) reviewed a decade of binding research and concluded that the effect tracks the operation of agency mechanisms — particularly the predictive (efference-copy) component — rather than post-hoc inference about authorship.

The binding effect responds to manipulations in principled ways. It is reduced when outcomes are unpredictable (Wolpe et al., 2013), consistent with the comparator model's emphasis on prediction matching. It is altered in schizophrenic patients (Voss et al., 2010), consistent with the same comparator dysfunction that produces delusions of control. These convergences between an implicit timing measure and the comparator model's predictions strengthen the case that agency phenomenology tracks real computational processes.

## Directed Effort and Neuroplasticity

If conscious effort is epiphenomenal — if the felt strain of concentrating against distracting impulses accompanies but does not cause neural changes — then it should be possible to produce the same neural changes without the phenomenology of effort. The evidence points the other way.

Schwartz et al. (1996) demonstrated that OCD patients who learn to cognitively reframe their intrusive thoughts produce measurable decreases in caudate nucleus activity, visible on PET scans. The treatment — mindfulness-based cognitive behavioural therapy — requires patients to recognise obsessive urges as brain-generated and redirect attention away from them. Patients consistently report this as effortful, requiring sustained concentration against compelling impulses. The phenomenology of effort and the neural changes track each other.

The Schwartz finding has been replicated across therapeutic contexts. Paquette et al. (2003) showed that CBT for spider phobia produced changes in dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and parahippocampal gyrus activity. Goldapple et al. (2004) found that CBT for depression altered prefrontal and limbic metabolism in patterns distinct from pharmacological treatment — same symptom improvement, different neural signatures, suggesting the cognitive intervention operates through a different causal pathway than the drug.

Rajan et al. (2019) identified neural signatures specific to willed attention. Frontal theta oscillations and bidirectional fronto-parietal coherence appear during effortful, self-directed attention but are absent during instructed or automatic attention. The distinction emerges approximately 300 milliseconds into the attentional process — willed processing leaves a neural fingerprint that automatic processing does not.

The neuroplasticity evidence does not prove that consciousness operates through non-physical mechanisms. A physicalist could argue that the felt effort is identical to the neural process that drives change. But the evidence does establish that the phenomenology of effort — the subjective sense of straining to direct attention — correlates with distinct neural effects that differ from those produced by automatic processing or pharmacological intervention. This is what [consciousness-selecting](/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/) predicts: conscious effort contributes something causally distinct from the unconscious baseline.

## The Readiness Potential Reconsidered

The Libet experiments (1983) — where the readiness potential precedes reported awareness of intention by approximately 350 milliseconds — were long treated as the definitive empirical challenge to volitional phenomenology. [The philosophical reinterpretation](/topics/volitional-control/) of these experiments is treated in detail elsewhere. The empirical developments that undermined the original interpretation deserve emphasis here.

Schurger, Sitt, and Dehaene (2012) showed that the readiness potential's shape and timing match a stochastic accumulator model — random neural noise drifting toward a motor threshold — rather than an unconscious decision process. Their "Libetus interruptus" variant confirmed the prediction: when subjects were unexpectedly interrupted, their neural activity was already near threshold on trials where they would have moved soon, exactly as the noise model predicts.

Braun, Wessler, and Friese's 2021 meta-analysis found the evidence base for the RP-preceding-intention claim is "uncertain and based on only a small number of studies." The field has moved substantially from the early triumphalism.

Fried, Mukamel, and Kreiman (2011) recorded 1019 individual neurons during self-initiated finger movements. Progressive recruitment of 256 SMA neurons could predict the decision to move with over 80% accuracy 700 milliseconds before reported awareness. Their threshold-crossing model is compatible with consciousness modulating the threshold or selecting among competing activations — the brain prepares multiple options, and something determines which crosses the line.

The empirical picture no longer supports the claim that neuroscience has disproved volitional phenomenology. What the timing evidence shows is that neural preparation precedes reported awareness — which is compatible with consciousness selecting among prepared options rather than initiating activity from nothing.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map's [tenets](/tenets/) predict the empirical pattern documented here. The **Dualism** tenet holds that consciousness is irreducible to physical processes. The clinical dissociations demonstrate that the phenomenology of agency has a structure — ownership versus agency, motor versus intentional levels — that maps onto but is not identical to neural circuitry. Desmurget's stimulation studies show that intention and execution occupy different neural real estate, and Sjöberg's resection cases show that the phenomenology survives the destruction of the neural machinery it was supposedly identical to.

The **Bidirectional Interaction** tenet holds that consciousness causally influences the physical world. The neuroplasticity evidence is the most direct support. Schwartz's OCD patients produce measurable brain changes through directed mental effort — changes that track the phenomenology of straining to redirect attention. If the effort were epiphenomenal, the neural changes would occur regardless of the subjective experience. Instead, the changes correlate specifically with the effortful, conscious component of the intervention.

The **Occam's Razor Has Limits** tenet is relevant to the history of the Libet debate. For decades, the parsimonious interpretation — that the readiness potential proves consciousness is epiphenomenal — was treated as settled science. Schurger's stochastic model, the meta-analytic uncertainty, and Sjöberg's clinical evidence have collectively dismantled that conclusion. The simpler story was wrong, or at minimum, radically underdetermined by the evidence.

The Map interprets these converging empirical lines as evidence that the phenomenology of volitional control — the felt distinction between willing and merely happening, between effortful control and automatic execution — reflects genuine causal architecture. Consciousness does not merely accompany volition. The empirical evidence increasingly suggests it contributes to it.
