Dan Zahavi's **minimal self** is the pre-reflective first-personal givenness that every conscious experience already carries — the "for-me-ness" that makes an experience this subject's rather than anyone's or no one's. On Zahavi's phenomenological account, this minimal self is the structural form consciousness takes rather than something consciousness *has* alongside its contents. The Unfinishable Map adopts this Zahavian position in preference to Thomas Metzinger's self-model theory, which treats selfhood as the content of a transparent representation that the brain generates and could in principle dismantle entirely. The distinction is load-bearing: arguments about ego-dissolution, meditative witnessing, indexical identity, and agency require a subject that the Metzinger framework does not supply, and that Zahavi's framework does.

This article explains what the Zahavian minimal self *is* in Zahavi's own terms, distinguishes it from three neighbouring positions (Metzinger's self-model eliminativism, Galen Strawson's thin-subject no-self, and Hume's bundle), and sets out why the Map's filter theory of consciousness and its ego-dissolution evidence specifically require the Zahavian version.

## Zahavi's Position in His Own Terms

In *Subjectivity and Selfhood* (2005) and *Self and Other* (2014), Zahavi develops a phenomenological account inherited from Husserl. Every conscious experience is characterised by **first-personal givenness**: experience is never simply *present* but present *for* a subject, from a perspective, as *mine*. This is its mode of appearance, not an additional property layered onto it. Zahavi: "Experiential phenomena ... are characterized by a quality of mineness, the fact that the experiences are given (at least tacitly) as *my* experiences" (2005, p. 124).

Four features matter:

**Pre-reflective.** First-personal givenness is not produced by introspection or higher-order monitoring. You do not experience a red apple and then *add* "I am experiencing this." Reflective self-consciousness presupposes pre-reflective self-consciousness and cannot generate it.

**Minimal, not substantial.** Not a homunculus behind the scenes, nor an unchanging soul-substance — simply the subjective character of experience itself, what Zahavi calls its "mineness" (*Jemeinigkeit*).

**Constitutive in kind, not in degree.** Against eliminativists, no coherent phenomenology lacks first-personal givenness; an experience belonging to no one would not be an experience. But the claim is a kind-claim — first-personal givenness admits of degrees (see "Constitutive as Kind, Not as Degree" below). A scalar feature can still be structurally necessary if every value above zero counts as instantiation.

**Synchronic only.** Zahavi's minimal self does not entail diachronic identity. Each conscious moment has first-personal givenness; whether this grounds numerical identity across time is a further question Zahavi treats with caution. On this Zahavi converges with some Buddhist critics rather than opposing them (see [buddhism-and-dualism](/concepts/buddhism-and-dualism/)).

## Metzinger's Alternative and Why It Differs

Thomas Metzinger's *Being No One* (2003) defends **Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity** (SMT). The self is the content of a phenomenally transparent self-model — a brain-generated representation treated as a non-representational presentation of a real self. The transparency gives rise to the illusion of a self "having" experiences: we mistake the model for its (non-existent) target. Metzinger's programme is explicitly *eliminativist*: "No such things as selves exist in the world" (2003, p. 1). What exist are self-models running on brains. When the self-model is disrupted — through meditation, psychedelics, neurological damage — no deeper subject is left over to "witness" the disruption.

His later work on **Minimal Phenomenal Experience** (MPE) characterises deep meditative states as potentially exhibiting a **"zero-person perspective"** — phenomenal consciousness without subject-structure, without for-me-ness (Metzinger 2020).

Three contrasts with Zahavi:

1. **Status.** Zahavi: first-personal givenness is constitutive of phenomenality. Metzinger: it is a contingent feature of most phenomenal states, in principle absent in MPE.

2. **What survives ego-dissolution.** Zahavi predicts the minimal self persists as bare for-me-ness while narrative and bodily self-models dissolve. Metzinger predicts dissolution of the self-model removes the subject entirely, leaving impersonal phenomenology.

3. **Epistemic implications.** For Zahavi, ego-dissolution reports are reports *by* the surviving minimal self *about* loss of richer self-structures. For Metzinger, reports are produced by a reconstituted self-model after the fact and may not reflect what was present during the state.

The Map takes the Zahavian reading to better fit first-person reports: subjects describe loss of "me-as-character" alongside persistence of bare experiential presence — not loss of experience altogether.

## Distinguishing Zahavi from Strawson and Hume

Two further positions need separating from Zahavi's.

**Galen Strawson's "thin subject" / Pearl view.** Strawson (2009) holds that subjects of experience exist but are thin and short-lived — each conscious moment has its own distinct subject, with no persistence across the gaps in consciousness (sleep, anaesthesia, attention breaks). This is closer to Zahavi than Metzinger is, but Strawson commits to a specific negative answer to the diachronic question that Zahavi treats with caution.

**Hume's bundle theory.** Hume famously reported that when he introspected for the self, he found only perceptions, "never anything but the perception" (Hume 1739, I.IV.VI). This is often cited against the minimal self, but Zahavi's reply is diagnostic: Hume was looking for the self as a distinct *object* of introspection. The minimal self is not an object; it is the *subjective pole* of the very introspection Hume performed. Hume could not *find* the self for the same reason an eye cannot see itself seeing. This parallels the [structural unobservability](/voids/self-opacity/) of the subject.

## Why the Map Requires the Zahavian Version

Three of the Map's core commitments require the Zahavian minimal self specifically, and fail on the Metzinger-eliminativist alternative.

**Ego-dissolution as evidence.** The Map's treatment of [psychedelics](/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/), [meditation](/topics/the-observer-witness-in-meditation/), and [anaesthesia](/topics/anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface/) depends on a subject whose relationship to the brain's self-model can change. If the self is nothing but the self-model (Metzinger), ego-dissolution *is* local extinction of the subject and "witness" reports are post-hoc confabulation. If the minimal self persists through self-model disruption (Zahavi), filter-theoretic interpretations of altered states become intelligible. The Map's case for [non-brain-produced consciousness](/concepts/mind-brain-separation/) trades on the persistence of the subject across filter-relaxation events.

**Indexical identity.** The [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) commitment rests on *this* experience being *mine* and not some duplicate's. A Metzingerian zero-person phenomenology, if genuinely attainable, would have no indexical anchor — a consciousness had by anyone or no one. The Map's indexical arguments presuppose the Zahavian picture.

**Bidirectional interaction.** The [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) tenet requires a subject whose influence on physical outcomes is real. Cognitive science treats representations as causally implicated, but *implicated in* is not the same role as *the agent of*. On the dualist picture, the bearer of consciousness does the influencing; representations are part of the structure through which influence operates. A self-model alone does not supply that bearer; the Zahavian minimal self does. Zahavi is a phenomenologist, not a metaphysician of interaction, but his posit gives the Map's metaphysics the subject it needs.

## Choice Under Metaphysical Commitment

The Zahavi/Metzinger question cannot be decided by ego-dissolution reports alone. Husserl's evidence taxonomy (developed in [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/#husserls-evidence-taxonomy)) clarifies the structure: apodictic weight attaches to the *existence* of for-me-ness; the choice of interpretive framework sits at assertoric and presumptive weight. Metzinger's reading — that ego-dissolution reports are post-hoc reconstructions produced by a reconstituted self-model — is internally consistent and not refutable by introspective data, since by hypothesis no introspective access to a putative zero-person phase is possible.

The Map's preference for Zahavi is therefore conditional, resting on three further commitments: (i) the indexical structure invoked by [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds), which presupposes *someone* whose experience is *this* one; (ii) [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction), which requires a subject who does the influencing; and (iii) [dualism](/tenets/#dualism), that consciousness is not exhausted by representational structure. If these are sound, Zahavi coheres with them and Metzinger does not. If not, the parsimony advantage runs to Metzinger. The choice is one of metaphysical fit, not empirical compulsion.

## The Madhyamaka Challenge

The most serious objection to the Zahavian framework comes from the Mādhyamaka Buddhist tradition. If it succeeds, the article's connection to the Map's [haecceity](/concepts/haecceity/) commitment comes apart.

**The challenge.** Mādhyamaka, following Nāgārjuna, grants what Zahavi notices but contests what he infers. The phenomenological observation that experience presents itself as *for-someone* is real; the mistake is to treat for-someone-ness as a structural feature implying a self-standing subject. On sustained analytical and contemplative investigation, "for-me-ness" does not bottom out in any bearer; it decomposes into conditioned moments, each arising in dependence on the previous, none belonging to a subject standing outside the sequence. The grammatical "for-me" is not evidence of a *me* the experience is *for* — it is the residual habit of self-grasping (*ātma-graha*) that Buddhist analytical practice is designed to expose. Zahavi has correctly described how experience *appears* and incorrectly inferred a structural feature from the appearance.

The objection sharpens when the Map moves from the synchronic minimal self to the diachronic [haecceity](/concepts/haecceity/) commitment. Zahavi's minimal self is synchronic and agnostic on persistence across time. The Map adds the diachronic claim that *this* subject is the same indexical bearer across phenomenal moments. From a Mādhyamaka standpoint, that move reinstates an *ātman* — the deepest locus of cognitive distortion the analysis is designed to undo. Readers who would let the synchronic claim through are likely to refuse the diachronic step.

**Thompson's middle path.** Evan Thompson (*Self, No Self?* 2011; *Waking, Dreaming, Being* 2014) refuses both poles. He takes pre-reflective self-awareness seriously but resists treating it as licensing a substantive subject. Reflexive awareness — experience knowing itself as it occurs — is how experience constitutes its own appearing, not evidence of an additional witnessing self. This converges with Mādhyamaka at the load-bearing point: the inference from "experience has reflexive structure" to "there is a self this experience belongs to" is not licensed by phenomenology alone. The Map's haecceity claim is precisely that unlicensed addition.

**Three replies.** First, the *conventional/ultimate* distinction Mādhyamaka itself supplies: Nāgārjuna's two-truths doctrine permits conventional claims even where ultimate inherent existence is denied. The Map's haecceity could be read as conventional individuation without asserting ultimate *svabhāva*. But this weakens [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) correspondingly: if "this experience is mine" is true only conventionally, indexical resistance to MWI's branching duplicates loses much of its force.

Second, denying the symmetry of Mādhyamaka analysis — that what *investigates* emptiness cannot itself be empty without infinite regress. Yogācāra-influenced readings move this way, but adopting Yogācāra refuses Mādhyamaka's terms rather than answering the objection.

Third, the candid reply: the haecceity-across-time commitment cannot survive sustained Mādhyamaka analysis in full force. The Map's position is conditional on holding that contemplative selflessness is interpretable as attenuated, denarrativised experience by a still-individuated subject, not as a vantage where individuation has genuinely dissolved. That reading is a philosophical choice, not evidence-forced. If sustained analysis really dissolves the indexical bearer rather than thinning the narrative around it, the [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) tenet faces a tenet-level reply, not an article-level refinement. The Map proceeds with the second reading because haecceity, No Many Worlds, and Bidirectional Interaction form a mutually supporting cluster the first reading would dissolve simultaneously.

## Constitutive as Kind, Not as Degree

Sass and Parnas's clinical work on schizophrenic ipseity disturbance (see [clinical-phenomenology-and-altered-experience](/topics/clinical-phenomenology-and-altered-experience/)) presses the constitutive thesis from a non-contemplative angle. Patients describe first-personal givenness as attenuated or fragmented: thoughts experienced as alien, the sense of being the one undergoing the experience wavering. The depersonalisation literature documents a parallel scalar disruption — the self felt as unreal while experience continues. Both appear to threaten the constitutive claim. If the asymmetry can be partially breached, in what sense is it structurally necessary?

The claim is about *kind*, not uniformity of intensity. Clinical variation shows the asymmetry can attenuate, distort, or destabilise — never that it fully dissolves while phenomenal life continues. Schizophrenic reports describe felt encounters with the breakdown of for-me-ness; the breakdown is encountered *from* a position. Depersonalisation patients report the unreality of the self *to* themselves. Every reported case is reported from somewhere. A scalar feature is structurally necessary if every value above zero counts as instantiation.

The falsifying scenario is specifiable: not attenuation but clean elimination — phenomenal life continuing fully *without* the asymmetry, no residue of for-me-ness, no position from which the lack is registered. Evidence supplies attenuation, distortion, and felt unreality — not elimination.

This methodological move generalises. The [self-opacity void](/voids/self-opacity/#constitutive-as-kind-not-as-degree) applies the same kind/degree distinction. Wherever the Map advances a constitutive claim, clinical-evidence pressure can be answered with the same reply: distinguish kind-claim from degree-claim, name the falsifying scenario as elimination rather than attenuation, and concede scalar variation without conceding structural contingency. Parsimony at the kind/degree level erases architectural detail clinical phenomenology demands ([Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)).

## Acknowledged Limits

Zahavi's synchronic account leaves diachronic identity open, requiring the Map to supplement with haecceity-based commitments — precisely the supplement that comes under Mādhyamaka fire above. Meditative evidence on attenuation of for-me-ness in deep contemplative states adds a second source of scalar pressure on the constitutive thesis; the same kind-vs-degree response applies, but the meditative case is harder to underwrite because the reports are first-person rather than third-person.

The Map does not treat Zahavi as the last word, only as the best current framing — the one that coheres with the Map's broader commitments in a way Metzinger's cannot.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: First-personal givenness is the structural feature [reductive programmes](/topics/reductionism-and-consciousness/) cannot accommodate; its constitutive status rules out eliminating subjectivity in favour of a self-model.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: The minimal self is a more perspicuous locus of agency than a self-model. Representations can be causally implicated, but "implicated in" is not "the bearer of" — and only the latter is what the tenet asks for.

**[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)**: The indexical character of first-personal givenness — that this experience is *mine*, unrepeatably — is what the tenet protects against MWI's proliferation of qualitatively identical subjects.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: Metzinger is more parsimonious. But parsimony misleads when the dispensed-with entity is what the evidence (ego-dissolution reports, ipseity disturbance, the structure of introspection) actually requires.
