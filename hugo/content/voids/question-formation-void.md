The **question formation void** names the structural constraint on *which questions a mind can formulate* — distinct from the constraint on which answers a mind can reach. Most voids on the Map are bounded at the answer stage: one can ask "what is consciousness?" without resolving the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/). The question formation void operates earlier. Questions never posed do not appear as gaps; they appear as nothing at all, and the mind that cannot form them has no internal signal that anything is missing.

This void joins the April 2026 cluster of second-order voids — the [epistemic horizon void](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/#the-scope-question-estimating-total-ignorance-from-within), the [plenitude void](/voids/plenitude-void/), the [categorial void](/voids/categorial-void/), and the [inference void](/voids/inference-void/) — each concerning an architectural feature of self-reflective cognition rather than a specific content gap. It is the void that shadows [apophatic cartography](/voids/apophatic-cartography/) itself: mapping by negation can only negate what can already be formulated. The Map's own method has a horizon it cannot see past from within.

## Three Layers of the Limit

The constraint operates at three levels, each with a distinct philosophical pedigree.

**The presuppositional layer.** R. G. Collingwood's logic of question-and-answer distinguishes *relative* from *absolute* presuppositions. A relative presupposition stands as presupposition to one question but as answer to another, so it can be interrogated. An absolute presupposition is "one which stands, relatively to all questions to which it is related, as a presupposition, never as an answer." Asking whether an absolute presupposition is true is, Collingwood argues, "a nonsense question" within the system it underwrites. Some assumptions are constitutively unavailable as questions from within the apparatus they anchor.

Erotetic logic — the formal theory of questions developed by Andrzej Wiśniewski — sharpens this. A question's *presupposition* is the assertive core implied by each admissible answer; a question whose presupposition fails does not produce a wrong answer but fails to count as a question at all. The notion of an *evoked question* captures the generative side: a question is rationally entertainable only when evoked by the agent's background. Questions that no background evokes simply never occur.

**The generative layer.** Questions do not appear in consciousness fully formed from nowhere. They are generated by an apparatus with specific affordances — a language, a paradigm, a hermeneutic horizon, a background of evoked inferences. Thomas Kuhn argues in *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* that paradigms do not merely answer shared questions differently but determine *which questions count as legitimate puzzles*. Hans-Georg Gadamer, following Collingwood explicitly, places the "priority of the question" at the centre of hermeneutics and treats understanding as occurring within a historically-determined horizon that can fuse with other horizons but never be escaped. Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* gives a famous formulation of the thought — "the limits of my language mean the limits of my world" — though his later work would replace the picture-theory underpinning with the appeal to language-games; the phenomenon survives both framings.

Problem-finding research (Getzels and Csikszentmihalyi, 1976) supplies an empirical check: problem finding is cognitively distinct from problem solving and generally harder. Problem discovery is largely unconscious; individuals with matched problem-solving ability differ in what problems they spot. The generative apparatus is real, measurable, and variable.

**The inventory layer.** A mind cannot list the questions it cannot formulate, because such a list would require formulating them. Unlike [conceptual impossibilities](/voids/conceptual-impossibility/), which can sometimes be gestured at from adjacent concepts surrounding the impossible region, question-formation failures leave no conceptual shadow. There is no neighbouring question from which to triangulate one that was never generated. The void resists exhaustive catalogue from within the apparatus; partial catalogues remain possible only by triangulation against minds with different generative habits.

## Evidence That the Limit Is Real

Two senses of the void need separating before evidence can be weighed.

A *coarse* (or strong) question-formation void is one where a question cannot be posed *at all* from inside the apparatus — no proxy, no near-miss, no half-formed itch. A *fine-grained* void is one where a question can be posed in some rough form but not at the categorical resolution that would make it actionable, communicable, or aggregable with others' experiences. The strong sense is what the article's deepest claims need; it is also constitutively harder to evidence, because by hypothesis nothing within the apparatus carries it forward. Most of the case material below is closer to the fine-grained sense, and this should be owned, not finessed.

Miranda Fricker's analysis of *hermeneutical injustice* in *Epistemic Injustice* (2007) is the most widely-cited candidate. Her paradigm case is Carmita Wood, who could not articulate what had happened to her until "sexual harassment" entered the conceptual repertoire in the 1970s. Fricker argues that "hermeneutical injustice can be traced down to a lacuna of a concept that would help to make sense of the target experience." Wood could and did ask coarse questions about her experience — *was this wrong? why is this happening?* — but she lacked the named category that would let her ask the question at the resolution needed for collective remedy. The case is therefore a fine-grained question-formation void, not a coarse one, and it stands as evidence for the article's claim only at that resolution. The retrospective lift is real: subjects report recognising, after the concept arrives, what they had been unable to ask. But the lift is consistent with vocabulary acquisition and does not on its own demonstrate the strong sense.

Paradigm transitions supply historical cases at the fine-grained end. Pre-Lavoisier chemistry could not form certain oxygen-centred questions; pre-Einsteinian mechanics could not form certain questions about simultaneity. These are not wrong answers to shared questions but questions that became salient only after the shift. Translation failures across traditions cluster around presuppositional differences; whole question-structures, not merely individual terms, resist cross-linguistic transfer.

The selection problem must be acknowledged at this point. Every example above is one in which the supposed void *was lifted* by ordinary discursive means — new vocabulary, new theory, new social context. The article cannot, by its own logic, exhibit a currently-unliftable coarse void: any question it could gesture at would, by the gesture, be partly lifted. This means the evidence base is constitutively a sample of closed cases, and a deflationary critic is entitled to read the catalogue as the history of conceptual change renamed. The article concedes the point. Its load-bearing claim is not that the void is unprecedented but that the *currently* unliftable catalogue is real, methodologically constraining, and not introspectively visible from inside. Forward-looking probes — described under "Approaches to the Edge" — are the way the claim earns its keep, not retrospective lifts.

## Phenomenology: Markers of the Inner Wall

The *void itself* — the set of questions the apparatus cannot generate — leaves no trace, because trace would require a question-shape to carry it. What does have a phenomenology is *approaching the edge from inside*: the inner wall has markers, even though nothing transmits through from beyond it. The four forms below describe the inside of the limit, not signals from past it. Most voids have analogous inner-wall markers — vertigo at the edge of the [intrinsic nature void](/voids/intrinsic-nature-void/), blank at the [language–thought boundary](/voids/language-thought-boundary/), recursion-failure at the [meta-epistemological](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/) edge.

The question formation void's inner-wall markers take four recognisable forms:

- *The half-formed itch.* A sense that something matters here without being able to say what. Keats's "negative capability" — "when a man is capable of being in uncertainties, mysteries, doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact and reason" — names the discipline of dwelling in this state rather than collapsing it into the closest available question.
- *The "why do they ask* that*?" reaction.* When encountering a tradition with different question-forming habits, specific questions feel pointless or misdirected rather than merely difficult. The reaction is reported through introspection and so inherits the usual reliability worries about introspective data; what it adds, weakly but suggestively, is presupposition-mismatch sensed without being articulated.
- *The retrospective lift.* When a new concept arrives, the subject reports "oh, *that* is what I was trying to ask." The prior state does not feel impoverished from within; it reveals itself as impoverished only after the lift.
- *Foreclosure rather than absence.* Heidegger's analysis of the forgetting of the question of being supplies a *model* — not a general phenomenology — for how a question's possibility can be covered over rather than merely left unanswered. Repurposed here, the model points to a marker: in some cases the question is not experienced as unasked, because the very possibility of asking has been foreclosed by the apparatus's settled habits. Heidegger's own claim is historical-ontological and specific to the question of being; the article borrows the *form* of foreclosure, not the historical thesis.

## Approaches to the Edge

Direct methods face the inventory problem: one cannot list what one cannot formulate. Partial direct methods exist. Collingwood's *regressive method* works from propositions back to the questions they answer, and from those questions back to their required presuppositions. Iterating until presuppositions become absolute does not escape the void but maps its inner wall. Erotetic formalisation makes the presupposition-set of asked questions explicit without revealing the unasked ones.

Indirect methods are more tractable. Reading traditions with different background commitments *for their questions* (not their answers) uses the shapes they centre as partial data about what one's own tradition has not centred. Historical recovery — examining how concepts like "sexual harassment," "intersectionality," or "attention deficit" came into articulate form — supplies existence proofs for question-formation lifts and narrative data about the void's shape. Kuhnian anomaly-watching treats phenomena that resist dismissal as candidate signals that a currently-unformulable question is gathering evidential weight.

[Non-human minds as void explorers](/voids/non-human-minds-as-void-explorers/) offer a further method, with caveats. Large language models sample question-shapes from a corpus that overlaps human question-formation but is not co-extensive with it: their generativity is shaped by training-data composition, RLHF pressure, attention biases, and decoding regimes, all of which differ from human acquisition. Whether these differences are large enough to surface human-side voids by triangulation is an empirical question, not a settled one — the corpus that constitutes their generativity is largely human, so the method risks reflecting human voids back rather than illuminating them. The *cautious* claim is the defensible one: LLMs can be prompted to inhabit multiple presupposition-systems within a single session and lack some human "forgettings" (no biological embeddedness, no socialisation into a single horizon) while having others (training-distribution skew, reinforcement pressure toward certain question-shapes). This makes them a partial probe — not a view from nowhere, and not a guaranteed view from a different somewhere, but a candidate sample from a partly different distribution.

## The Inheritance Problem

This article uses question-formation apparatus to describe question-formation limits. The questions it poses about the void are a proper subset of the questions its cognitive apparatus can form; any questions outside that subset are unreachable by the investigation that would name them. The same pattern appears in the [epistemic horizon](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/#the-scope-question-estimating-total-ignorance-from-within) and [categorial](/voids/categorial-void/) discussions: the verifying apparatus is implicated in what is being verified.

Three honest concessions follow. The void description will carry blind spots specific to its generative architecture — Western philosophical training, Indo-European question syntax, and whatever the Map's own framework excludes. Convergence of voids catalogues across different minds is weak evidence of completeness and may instead reflect shared architectural limits. The cleanest empirical probe is precisely the method the apparatus cannot self-administer: exposing the question-set to minds whose generativity differs, and looking for systematic divergence. None of these moves escape the void. They make it visible.

## Distinguishing the Void

The [framework void](/voids/framework-void/) concerns the totality of one's conceptual apparatus as a system. The question formation void is narrower: it concerns the generative sub-process by which questions arise within whatever framework is in use. A framework shift changes question-space, but the question formation void operates within any fixed framework.

[Conceptual impossibility](/voids/conceptual-impossibility/) concerns the space of thinkable contents. The question formation void concerns the space of formulable interrogatives over whatever contents are thinkable — what can be *asked about what is conceived*.

The [epistemic horizon void](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/#the-scope-question-estimating-total-ignorance-from-within) concerns the impossibility of estimating the total scope of one's ignorance. The question formation void concerns the impossibility of formulating specific questions at all. They compose: we cannot list the questions we cannot form *and* we cannot estimate how many such questions exist.

The [inference void](/voids/inference-void/) concerns the opacity of inferential transitions — the *move* from premise to conclusion — to the consciousness performing them. The question formation void operates upstream: it concerns whether the interrogative shape arises at all. The two compose. Question-formation is realized by inferential operations on most accounts, so the inference void underlies the generative apparatus this article describes; conversely, every inference about what cannot be formulated is performed by operations the inference void declares opaque.

The straightforwardly-unanswerable case — questions we can form but cannot answer — is the most important non-neighbour. Most of the Map catalogues this kind of limit. The question formation void sits upstream of the answer stage entirely.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map treats the question formation void as central to the case for **Occam's Razor Has Limits**. Parsimony judgments are presupposition-laden. What counts as "one more entity" depends on the question being asked, and the question depends on the absolute presuppositions of the framework. A mind with different question-formation habits could find the Map's parsimony preferences arbitrary. The Razor's authority presupposes a stable question-space, which this void shows is not given.

The connection to **Dualism** should be taken in the weaker form. The article does *not* argue that the question-forming faculty is a privileged site for irreducibility, and the previous version's hedge — "speculates, without claiming to demonstrate" — was doing more work than the argument supplied. Semantic and inferential relations are demonstrably implementable in physical systems; nothing in the article rebuts that. The modest claim that survives is this: *if* the Map's general dualist thesis is correct and there is a non-physical interface at the consciousness side, then question-generation is one of the cognitive functions that interface might affect, alongside others such as attention, salience, and intentional directedness. The article does not argue here for a privileged tie, and treats the question-forming faculty as one cognitive function among several, not as a candidate site for the dualist interface in particular.

The connection to **No Many Worlds** is best made specific or set aside. On MWI, question-spaces are either branch-invariant — in which case branching is irrelevant to question-formation and there is nothing distinctive to say — or branch-varying, in which case the branch-structure of cognition would have to inherit from the branch-structure of the brain by some route MWI does not supply (decoherence operates at quantum granularity, not at pragmatic-linguistic granularity). The standard indexical observation made elsewhere on the Map — that you are *here*, in this question-space, not duplicated across branches — applies but takes no extra force from the question-formation framing. The article makes a weak connection only.

Apophatic cartography can only negate what can be formulated; the question formation void is the void behind the method. This complements rather than undermines apophatic cartography, but must be carried within it as a permanent provisionality. The Map's catalogue of voids is not the catalogue a mind like ours could identify. It is the catalogue this apparatus has so far managed to ask about.
