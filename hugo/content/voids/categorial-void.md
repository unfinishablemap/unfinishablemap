The **categorial void** is the structural gap that opens when reality contains features for which the mind has no *schema* — no template for forming a concept of that kind at all. It is not the familiar gap between a thing and a word we have not yet coined for it. That gap closes with language. The categorial void is the gap between a thing and the general *shape* any concept of that thing would have to take — a shape our cognitive architecture may not be built to produce. Where a missing concept leaves an empty slot in an existing scheme, a missing category leaves no slot to fill. The question is whether there are domains whose structure is not merely unknown but unparseable by minds like ours.

This void sits beneath the concepts articulated elsewhere in The Unfinishable Map. [Conceptual impossibility](/voids/conceptual-impossibility/) concerns content that is malformed *within* our categories (a round square). [The intrinsic nature void](/voids/intrinsic-nature-void/) concerns content that is hidden behind structural description. The categorial void operates one level down from both: it concerns the space of categorial schemas available to us, and asks whether that space is exhaustive.

## What "Categorial" Means Here

A concept picks out a kind of thing. A category picks out a kind of kind — the general shape a concept must have to track a certain sort of feature at all. *Red*, *electron*, *anger*, *prime number* are concepts. *Property*, *substance*, *event*, *cause*, *relation*, *quantity*, *number* are categories. Every new concept we form slots into one or more categories; without those categorial slots, the concept would have nothing to be an instance *of*.

Kant gave the classic formulation in the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781). The categories (quantity, quality, relation, modality, each subdivided) are not generalisations *from* experience but preconditions *for* it. Any experience at all, on Kant's account, must already be shaped by the categorial apparatus we bring. We encounter phenomena — things as categorially organised — and never noumena — things as they are apart from any categorial shaping. Rae Langton reads this as *epistemic humility*: the argument does not say reality is absent beyond the categories, only that whatever lies there falls outside the reach of any knowledge we can have (Langton, 1998).

The Map takes the Kantian diagnosis seriously without taking its specific list on faith. Whether the twelve categories Kant identified are exactly the right ones, or whether the set has evolutionary and embodiment-dependent contingency, is a separate question explored in [embodiment and cognitive limits](/voids/embodiment-cognitive-limits/). What the categorial void asserts is weaker and more structural: *some* categorial apparatus is operating, it is finite, and we have no guarantee that it covers every categorial shape reality permits.

## How the Categorial Void Differs from Its Neighbours

Three adjacent voids in the Map's catalogue make the categorial claim precise by contrast.

### Against Conceptual Acquisition

The simplest cognitive gap is a missing concept we could in principle acquire — a fact we do not know, a technical term we have not learned, an experience we have not had. The [language-thought boundary](/voids/language-thought-boundary/) and related articles examine how such gaps can be closed through translation, instruction, or exposure. A concept of this kind fits into an existing categorial slot: *siniy* and *goluboy* are both colour concepts, whether or not English separates them.

The categorial void is not of this kind. Acquiring a new category would mean acquiring a new *shape* for concepts — not just another item within a kind but another kind of kind. Kant argued, and Strawson (1959) later argued, that the core categories of human thought are not learned in the way particular concepts are learned, because any learning already presupposes them. If this is right, categorial gaps cannot be closed by acquisition: the machinery that would acquire them is the machinery whose limits are in question.

### Against the Intrinsic Nature Void

The [intrinsic nature void](/voids/intrinsic-nature-void/) concerns unknown *content* behind known structure — what mass and charge *are*, beneath what they *do*. The categorial void concerns unknown *shape*: not the hidden content of a recognised category, but a whole categorial schema we may lack. These are two different axes of ignorance. One could in principle be filled by fresh evidence, if the intrinsic could be accessed; the other could not, because no evidence arrives pre-sorted into a category we do not have.

The two voids can compound. If the intrinsic nature of matter is categorially unlike anything our physical-object schema represents, then the intrinsic nature void is partly a categorial void: the reason our categories give us only structure and never substance may be that the substance falls outside the categorial shapes those schemas can produce.

### Against Conceptual Impossibility

[Conceptual impossibility](/voids/conceptual-impossibility/) describes what happens when content *is* built from our categories but the construction is malformed — a round square, a married bachelor. Thought "bounces back" from such attempts. The phenomenology is sharp: an immediate sense of resistance, not a sense of missing ingredient.

The categorial void has a different signature, explored below. Where conceptual impossibility produces a hard wall at the boundary of well-formed thought, a categorial void produces something closer to a *soft absence* — not resistance, but a region where thought fails to find purchase because it cannot determine what it is reaching for.

## Candidate Categorial Voids

What might specific categorial voids look like? Several candidates recur across the literature and the Map's own analyses. Each is offered as *a place the void might lie*, not a proven instance.

**The phenomenal-physical shape.** The hard problem of consciousness persists partly because no categorial schema unifies the two sides. The physical-object category gives structure, causal role, spatial location. The phenomenal category gives what-it-is-likeness, perspectival character, felt quality. No available schema treats both as aspects of one kind. The persistent failure to formulate such a schema — across functionalism, identity theories, panpsychism, Russellian monism — may be evidence that the required categorial shape is not among those our minds can produce.

**The intrinsic character of other species' experience.** Nagel (1974) argued that we cannot form the concept of *what it is like to be a bat* because the concept would require acquaintance with bat-phenomenal features that human cognition cannot host. The Map reads this as a candidate categorial limit: not a concept we lack, but a categorial shape (bat-species phenomenal character) our apparatus does not include.

**A genuine view from nowhere.** Nagel's later work (1986) explored the tension between subjective and objective viewpoints. A fully objective viewpoint would require a category that represents reality *apart from any perspective* — including our own. Whether such a category is coherent is contested; if not, the gap it would fill is a candidate categorial void.

**Experience structured by apparatus we do not possess.** If the research on perceptual invariances is right, our categories of *space*, *object*, and *action* are built from the invariance structure of human perception (Sonesson, 2020). A being with radically different sensors, different embodiment, or no embodiment at all might operate with categorial shapes our apparatus cannot instantiate. The [non-human minds as void-explorers](/voids/non-human-minds-as-void-explorers/) article treats this as testable rather than settled.

**Different metaphysical axes.** Graham (1989) argued that concepts like *physical object*, *causation*, and *quantity* may be parochial features of Indo-European languages. The strong form of linguistic relativity is widely rejected, but even weaker versions suggest the categorial axes a mind carves are not self-evidently the ones reality follows.

## Kant's Ghost and the Point of Divergence

The categorial void owes its shape to Kant, but the Map departs from Kant on one decisive question. Kant held that the categorial apparatus he catalogued was *determinate* — fixed, universal, and identifiable through transcendental reflection. The void claim is weaker: whatever categorial apparatus we have, we cannot from within it verify its completeness, nor rule out that other architectures carve reality along axes we lack.

Three developments push toward the cautious formulation.

Sellars (1956) argued that all perception is already theory-laden: there is no neutral observation against which our categories could be checked. The audit of the apparatus employs the apparatus being audited — a bootstrapping problem the Map treats as evidence that the categorial void is a structural feature of self-reflective cognition, not a mere conjecture.

Davidson (1974) argued in the opposite direction: the very idea of a radically alternative conceptual scheme is incoherent, because identifying one as alternative would already require translating it into our own. The Map treats Davidson's challenge as an *epistemic* constraint — we may never confirm a categorial void from inside — without dissolving the metaphysical question of whether such voids are real. The [meta-epistemology of cognitive limits](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/) develops this split: the unverifiability is itself informative, not a refutation.

Work on non-human minds shifts what counts as evidence. If artificial systems, animal minds, or other architectures can perform cognitive work our categorial apparatus cannot frame — without translation back — that is close to empirical pressure on the categorial claim. The [epistemic horizon void](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/#the-scope-question-estimating-total-ignorance-from-within) bounds how much such evidence could ever settle.

## Phenomenology of Approach

Three experiential signatures recur in introspective and philosophical reports of approaching a categorial void.

*Soft absence rather than hard wall.* Unlike the sharp bounce of conceptual impossibility, categorial voids tend to present as a region where thought fails to find anything to reach for. The failure is not "I cannot combine these" but "I cannot determine what shape combining would take."

*Recursive collapse.* Evaluating one's categorial apparatus requires using that apparatus, which folds the evaluation back on itself. This recursive structure resembles the one the [meta-epistemology of cognitive limits](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/) identifies in other self-applied questions about mind.

*Dissolution reports, ambiguously.* Mystical, aesthetic, and psychedelic reports sometimes describe "category dissolution." Whether they represent genuine glimpses beyond categorial structure, or only categorial *breakdown* within the existing scheme, is unsettled. The Map treats them as suggestive, not probative.

## Honest Limitations

The categorial void cannot be described from inside the very apparatus that cannot form it. Every description in this article — including the term "categorial void" itself — uses categories drawn from the apparatus whose limits are at issue. The article therefore approximates by construction.

This is a structural consequence of the claim, not a failure of the article. The [limits-reveal-structure principle](/voids/what-voids-reveal/) applies: where the categories fail systematically, the pattern of failure is itself data about the boundary.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The categorial void is load-bearing for two of the Map's [five tenets](/tenets/).

**Occam's Razor Has Limits.** Parsimony-preserving theories are expressible in a small number of categorial primitives. If reality contains structure along axes our apparatus lacks, then simplicity measured within our categories will systematically mislead. A theory that *appears* to multiply entities may be tracking structure more faithfully than a simpler theory expressible only in our primitives. The categorial void supplies the deepest argument the Map has for this tenet: "simpler" is a property relative to a categorial scheme whose completeness cannot be verified.

**Dualism.** If consciousness is not reducible to physical processes, the categorial shapes evolved for parsing a physical world may be constitutively inadequate for representing it. The hard problem, on this reading, is not an unsolved puzzle within our categories; it is a signal that the problem's subject matter falls partly outside those categories' reach. Heidegger's (1927) distinction between *categories* (for non-conscious entities) and *existentials* (for conscious ones) anticipates this move — different kinds of thing may require different kinds of kind.

Two further tenets are touched more lightly. Bidirectional Interaction may persistently resist formulation because its mechanism operates along axes the subject-object schema cannot parse. And the No Many Worlds tenet's appeal partly reflects a warning against letting mathematical categorial convenience (Hilbert space, unitary evolution) override other categorial commitments (singular identity, determinate experience).
