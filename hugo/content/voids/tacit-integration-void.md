When consciousness integrates subsidiary cues into a focal meaning—feeling the hammer through the hand, hearing words through their sounds, sensing the shape of a problem before articulating it—the *integration itself* cannot be made focal. Michael Polanyi formalised this as the *from-to* structure of tacit knowing: we attend *from* subsidiaries *to* a focal whole, and any attempt to convert the subsidiaries into focal objects collapses the whole that they were bearing. The Unfinishable Map names this the *tacit integration void*: the structural impossibility of simultaneously thematising the cues we are attending *from* and the meaning we are attending *to*. The void is unexplorable in principle, not merely in practice.

The void is distinct from siblings it can be confused with. The [expertise void](/voids/expertise-and-its-occlusion/) concerns the *irreversible transformation* skill imposes on perception. The [attention void](/voids/attention-and-consciousness/) concerns the *figure–ground organisation* of awareness. The [binding-void](/voids/binding-void/) concerns *qualia unification* across modalities. The tacit integration void concerns the from-to *bearing relation* itself—how subsidiaries function as carriers of a focal meaning, and why that function is destroyed by being looked at directly.

## The From-To Structure

Polanyi's central claim is that focal awareness rests on subsidiary awareness of particulars whose contribution is to bear on the focal whole. The two modes are mutually exclusive: "our attention can hold only one focus at a time" (Polanyi, 1966). The paradigmatic example is the hammer: "I have a subsidiary awareness of the feeling in the palm of my hand which is merged into my focal awareness of my driving in the nail" (Polanyi, 1958). The grip exists, in the relevant sense, only as transparent. To feel the grip *as such* is to lose the nail.

This is not a contingent fact about human attention. Polanyi argues the from-to relation is *constitutive* of meaning. Subsidiaries do not merely happen to escape notice; their meaning *is* their bearing on something else. Converting them into focal objects strips them of the relation that made them what they were. He calls this *destructive analysis*: "the particulars of a skill appear to be unspecifiable because the performance is paralysed if we focus our attention on these details" (Polanyi, 1966).

The phrase "we know more than we can tell" is often taken as a claim about practical limits on articulation. Polanyi's argument is sharper. The tacit dimension is the *integrative function* by which fragmentary cues become carriers of unified meaning. No articulation can capture the function without taking the cues as objects—and once they are objects, they no longer perform the function.

## Felt Sense and the Criterion of Fit

Eugene Gendlin extends the analysis from skill and perception into bodily, pre-articulate knowing. The *felt sense* is a holistic, pre-conceptual sensing of a whole situation—neither emotion, nor image, nor explicit thought, but a bodily implying that something is at stake in a particular way. Articulation, for Gendlin, is not description but *carrying forward*: "When the felt sense is allowed to function in relation to concepts, each carries the other forward through steps of deeper feel and new formulation" (Gendlin, 1981).

The crucial detail for the void is the *felt shift*—the bodily change that registers when an articulation fits. The shift functions as a phenomenal check on candidate articulations: premature or wrong words produce a non-shift, and the body's "no" rules them out. But the criterion of fit is itself another felt sense, not a representation that can be cashed out. Articulating the criterion would require a further check, which would be a further felt sense, and so on. The void recurs at each level.

Gendlin's contribution is independent of Polanyi's: a different tradition (psychotherapeutic process work rather than philosophy of science), a different paradigmatic case (clinical insight rather than skilled performance), and a different vocabulary. Convergence on the same structural impossibility strengthens the void's standing as a feature of consciousness rather than an artefact of one school.

## Horizons, Clearings, and the Fringe

Three further sources converge on the same territory from different angles. Their convergence matters: agreement across methods that do not share assumptions is stronger evidence than agreement within a single school.

Edmund Husserl's *horizon* is the pre-thematic structure of anticipation that frames every act of consciousness. The internal horizon contains the unseen sides of the perceived object; the external horizon contains the world-context within which the object appears; the temporal horizon contains retention and protention. Horizons are constitutively non-thematic: making the horizon thematic creates a new act with its own horizon, leaving the original structural feature untouched.

Martin Heidegger's *Lichtung* (clearing) names the prior intelligibility-space within which entities can show themselves. The clearing is not itself an entity. "An understanding of being is most effective when it is invisible," Heidegger observes; "the contexture of standards holds itself back from us so that we can deal directly with the entities that are intelligible to us." World-disclosure is structurally non-thematic in the same way Polanyi's subsidiaries are: making it thematic would require another clearing.

William James's *fringe* is a third instance. Every conscious state has a centre and a fringe, and the fringe "shades insensibly into a subconscious more" (James, 1890). Fringe-content provides the sense that the present thought *belongs* somewhere—relations, transitions, anticipations. Attention to the fringe converts it into a centre and produces new fringes; the structure is reproductive, not exhaustible.

These authors disagree about much, but converge on a single structural shape: an integrative or contextual function whose work is to recede, and whose direct thematisation either destroys the function or simply relocates it.

## Empirical Corroboration

Inattentional blindness research provides an empirical complement. Subjects who fail to consciously detect an unexpected stimulus nevertheless show residual sensitivity to it—measured behaviourally, neurally, and in priming effects on later tasks. Recent work (eLife, 2025) treats perception under inattention as *graded*: "significant residual sensitivity remains in inattentional blindness, consistent with an alternative picture where inattention degrades awareness rather than abolishing it." This corroborates the broader picture that the focal/subsidiary structure of consciousness is a gradient. Linguistic experience offers a daily case: native speakers grasp meaning *through* phonemes, and attending focally to phonemes (as in unfamiliar second-language listening) collapses comprehension.

## What Approaches Are Possible

Direct attention to the integration *is* what destroys it, so the void resists direct method. Three indirect approaches mark its boundaries without crossing them.

*Polanyi's interiorisation.* We come to dwell *in* a subsidiary set such that it functions tacitly—but the operation is post-hoc, not concurrent. We learn to use a tool by attending focally at first, then letting the cues recede. The recession is the void's appearance.

*Gendlin's focusing.* A protocol for letting felt sense become more articulable through gentle, non-coercive turning. The technique succeeds by *not* directly objectifying the felt sense—it elicits articulation by attending *near* the felt sense without thematising it.

*Boundary marking.* As elsewhere on the Map, the void is approached by [mapping the structure of failure](/voids/apophatic-cartography/)—where collapse begins, what remains stable on either side—rather than by penetrating it.

## Within the Voids Taxonomy

The tacit integration void is *unexplorable* in the [Map's taxonomy](/voids/three-kinds-of-void/), with a structural rather than contingent character. The from-to relation is what makes meaning possible; thematising it would require a vantage point outside the relation. Like Gurwitsch's thematic field, attempting to dissolve the void creates a new instance: the new focal object brings its own subsidiaries.

A materialist may object that the integration is in principle specifiable as a particular pattern of neural activation; the void is just the practical limit of introspection. The Map's response: the from-to relation is not a brute fact about which cognition is computationally hidden but the form meaning takes. A specification of the subsidiaries-plus-relation would not be the integration itself but a different focal object that bears on a higher-order whole. The recursion is the void.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** is the most directly engaged tenet. The tacit integration void is a precise case where simplification by analytical decomposition *destroys* the phenomenon being analysed. Any account that treats meaning as the sum of decomposable subsidiaries misses what the integration accomplishes. The void is a counterexample to the assumption that simplicity-via-decomposition is always epistemically conservative.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** is engaged less directly but substantively. If consciousness reduced to neural information processing, the from-to relation would be specifiable as a particular pattern of activation. Polanyi's argument cuts the other way: the integration is not specified by the subsidiaries plus a relation; the relation is constitutive and resists specification. This does not prove dualism, but it preserves the structural pressure that motivates it—features of consciousness that resist analytic capture in principle, not just in practice.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** finds an unusual case. The felt sense functions causally—guiding articulation, action, and decision—without being decomposable into representations. Gendlin's *felt shift* describes a phenomenal check on cognition that has effects in the world but cannot be inspected as the kind of thing that has causes. The Map interprets this as compatible with consciousness exerting causal influence without becoming an object available to causal explanation.

The void also constrains the Map's own method. The catalogue of voids is itself an exercise in tacit integration: its coherence depends on subsidiary background commitments—tenets, apophatic discipline, prior articles—that bear on the focal task of mapping. Any apophatic catalogue must operate from-to. It cannot be its own focal object without becoming a different kind of object.

## Honest Limitation

Polanyi's destructive-analysis claim is contested. Some commentators argue the integration is in principle specifiable, just very complex—that "we know more than we can tell" reflects practical limits on linguistic compression rather than a structural impossibility. If decomposition could in principle preserve the from-to relation, the void would weaken from unexplorable to merely difficult. The Map centres an article on the structural argument without regarding the matter as settled.

A second limitation: the void is described from inside human integration of body, action, and meaning. Whether transformer attention—which weights subsidiary tokens to produce focal outputs—realises a Polanyian from-to is open. The mathematical structure is suggestive; the phenomenology is unverifiable from outside. A mind with different integration may have different blind spots, but probably not none.
