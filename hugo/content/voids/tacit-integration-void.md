When consciousness integrates subsidiary cues into a focal meaning—feeling the hammer through the hand, hearing words through their sounds, sensing the shape of a problem before articulating it—the *integration itself* cannot be made focal. Michael Polanyi formalised this as the *from-to* structure of tacit knowing: we attend *from* subsidiaries *to* a focal whole, and any attempt to convert the subsidiaries into focal objects collapses the whole that they were bearing. The Unfinishable Map names this the *tacit integration void*: a candidate structural impossibility of simultaneously thematising the cues we are attending *from* and the meaning we are attending *to*. The structural claim is conditional — it presupposes that phenomenological identification of the from-to relation is non-redundant with third-person specification — and the [Honest Limitation](#honest-limitation) section below states the falsifier explicitly. Granted the antecedent, the void's *interior* is unexplorable in principle while its *boundary* admits indirect mapping.

The void is distinct from siblings it can be confused with. The [expertise void](/voids/expertise-and-its-occlusion/) concerns the *irreversible transformation* skill imposes on perception. The [attention void](/voids/attention-and-consciousness/) concerns the *figure–ground organisation* of awareness. The [binding-void](/voids/binding-void/) concerns *qualia unification* across modalities. The tacit integration void concerns the from-to *bearing relation* itself—how subsidiaries function as carriers of a focal meaning, and why that function is destroyed by being looked at directly.

## The From-To Structure

Polanyi's central claim is that focal awareness rests on subsidiary awareness of particulars whose contribution is to bear on the focal whole. The two modes are mutually exclusive: "our attention can hold only one focus at a time" (Polanyi, 1966). The paradigmatic example is the hammer: "I have a subsidiary awareness of the feeling in the palm of my hand which is merged into my focal awareness of my driving in the nail" (Polanyi, 1958). The grip exists, *for the user driving the nail*, only as transparent. To feel the grip *as such* is to lose the nail.

For Polanyi this is more than a contingent fact about human attention. He argues the from-to relation is *constitutive* of meaning: subsidiaries do not merely happen to escape notice; their meaning *is* their bearing on something else. The distinction the article relies on is between an *operative* role (substitutable by a different mechanism that performs the same function — a prosthetic for the hammer, a learned heuristic for the felt sense) and a *constitutive* role (not substitutable without changing what is integrated). Polanyi's claim is the stronger one: the from-to relation is what makes the integration the integration it is, not a contingent processing pathway to it. Converting subsidiaries into focal objects strips them of the relation that made them what they were. He calls this *destructive analysis*: "the particulars of a skill appear to be unspecifiable because the performance is paralysed if we focus our attention on these details" (Polanyi, 1966).

The phrase "we know more than we can tell" is often taken as a claim about practical limits on articulation. Polanyi's argument is sharper. The tacit dimension is the *integrative function* by which fragmentary cues become carriers of unified meaning. No articulation can capture the function without taking the cues as objects—and once they are objects, they no longer perform the function.

## Felt Sense and the Criterion of Fit

Eugene Gendlin extends the analysis from skill and perception into bodily, pre-articulate knowing. The *felt sense* is a holistic, pre-conceptual sensing of a whole situation — a bodily implying that something is at stake in a particular way, distinct from emotion, image, or explicit thought. Articulation, for Gendlin, is *carrying forward*: "When the felt sense is allowed to function in relation to concepts, each carries the other forward through steps of deeper feel and new formulation" (Gendlin, 1981). Concept and felt sense each elaborate the other; the concept's role is to enable a more determinate felt sense to take shape, working with what is implicit rather than reporting on what is already explicit.

The crucial detail for the void is the *felt shift*—the bodily change that registers when an articulation fits. The shift functions as a phenomenal check on candidate articulations: premature or wrong words produce a non-shift, and the body's "no" rules them out. But the criterion of fit is itself another felt sense, not a representation that can be cashed out. Articulating the criterion would require a further check, which would be a further felt sense, and so on. The void recurs at each level.

Gendlin's vocabulary differs from Polanyi's, and his paradigmatic case (clinical insight) differs from Polanyi's (skilled performance). Both authors work within phenomenologically informed traditions, so the agreement is less cross-tradition than it might appear. Still, on the specific structural shape — articulation that operates by *not* objectifying what it articulates — they converge from different angles, which is some evidence the shape is not idiosyncratic to one writer.

## Horizons, Clearings, Fringe, and Field

Several further sources converge on the same territory from different angles. The convergence is not as cross-traditional as one might want — Husserl, Heidegger, James, Gurwitsch, and Polanyi all grant first-person phenomenology epistemic priority — but the structural shape they describe is consistent enough that it is unlikely to be idiosyncratic to one writer. A genuinely external check, drawing on Gestalt experimental psychology and the divided-attention literature, appears in the empirical section below.

Edmund Husserl's *horizon* is the pre-thematic structure of anticipation that frames every act of consciousness. The internal horizon contains the unseen sides of the perceived object; the external horizon contains the world-context within which the object appears; the temporal horizon contains retention and protention. Horizons are constitutively non-thematic: making the horizon thematic creates a new act with its own horizon, leaving the original structural feature untouched.

Martin Heidegger's *Lichtung* (clearing) names the prior intelligibility-space within which entities can show themselves. The clearing recedes structurally; it discloses entities by holding itself back from being itself an entity. The thought is sometimes glossed as "an understanding of being is most effective when it is invisible" — a paraphrase circulating in the secondary literature rather than a direct quotation traceable to *Being and Time*. Heidegger's own formulation in *Being and Time* §31 (the *Da* of Dasein, the disclosedness within which entities show up) makes the structural point: world-disclosure is non-thematic in the same way Polanyi's subsidiaries are, and making it thematic would require another clearing.

William James's *fringe* is a third instance. Every conscious state has a centre and a fringe, and the fringe "shades insensibly into a subconscious more" (James, 1890). Fringe-content provides the sense that the present thought *belongs* somewhere — relations, transitions, anticipations. Attention to the fringe converts it into a centre and produces new fringes; the structure is reproductive, not exhaustible.

Aron Gurwitsch's *thematic field* is a fourth instance and the most direct ally of the from-to claim. Every theme is given against a thematic field of co-relevant content (Gurwitsch, 1964); the field organises what counts as relevant to the theme without itself being thematic. Shifts of theme reorganise the field, which cannot become the theme without ceasing to play the field-role. Gurwitsch's contribution is precision: he distinguishes the *thematic field* (organised relevance) from the *margin* (mere co-presence), letting one separate the integrative role from being merely co-present.

These authors disagree about much, but converge on a single structural shape: an integrative or contextual function whose work is to recede, and whose direct thematisation either destroys the function or simply relocates it. Their shared phenomenological commitments mean the convergence is some evidence — though tempered by those shared assumptions — that the shape is not idiosyncratic to Polanyi.

## Empirical and External Corroboration

The phenomenological convergence above leans on a shared tradition. Three external sources help check whether the structural shape survives outside that tradition.

Gestalt psychology (Wertheimer, Köhler, Koffka) treats figure–ground organisation as a constitutive principle of perception: the ground recedes structurally into the figure, available to perception only as a setting against which the figure shows up. Reversible figures (the Necker cube, Rubin's vase) demonstrate that figure and ground are mutually exclusive — the ground returns once the figure flips, and one cannot hold both as figure simultaneously. Gestalt's experimental method is third-person and its commitments are not phenomenological in the Husserlian sense, yet it identifies the same structural shape: an organising function whose work depends on its receding from focal awareness. The convergence is evidence that the shape is not an artefact of the phenomenological tradition.

Dual-task interference and divided-attention research (Pashler, 1994; Nieuwenhuis et al.) measure the cost of attempting two attentional foci simultaneously. The literature is consistent with focal capacity being graded rather than strictly binary, but with measurable cost when subjects try to thematise a process that was running tacitly. This is necessary but not sufficient for the structural void claim — it shows that dual thematisation has measurable cost, not that it is structurally impossible.

Inattentional blindness research provides a third complement. Subjects who fail to consciously detect an unexpected stimulus nevertheless show residual sensitivity to it — measured behaviourally, neurally, and in priming effects on later tasks. Recent work (eLife, 2025) treats perception under inattention as *graded*: "significant residual sensitivity remains in inattentional blindness, consistent with an alternative picture where inattention degrades awareness rather than abolishing it." This corroborates a softer claim than the structural void — that the focal/subsidiary boundary is a gradient — which is a precondition for the structural claim rather than a vindication of it. Linguistic experience offers a daily case: native speakers grasp meaning *through* phonemes, and attending focally to phonemes (as in unfamiliar second-language listening) collapses comprehension.

## What Approaches Are Possible

The void's *interior* — the from-to relation as it operates — is unexplorable, since direct attention to the integration is what destroys it. Its *boundary* admits indirect mapping. The distinction matters: mapping the perimeter (where collapse begins, what holds on either side) is not the same as exploring the interior. Three indirect approaches work the boundary without crossing it.

*Polanyi's interiorisation.* We come to dwell *in* a subsidiary set such that it functions tacitly—but the operation is post-hoc, not concurrent. We learn to use a tool by attending focally at first, then letting the cues recede. The recession is the void's appearance.

*Gendlin's focusing.* A protocol for letting felt sense become more articulable through gentle, non-coercive turning. The technique succeeds by *not* directly objectifying the felt sense—it elicits articulation by attending *near* the felt sense without thematising it.

*Boundary marking.* As elsewhere on the Map, the void is approached by [mapping the structure of failure](/voids/apophatic-cartography/)—where collapse begins, what remains stable on either side—rather than by penetrating it.

## Within the Voids Taxonomy

The tacit integration void is *unexplorable* in the [Map's taxonomy](/voids/three-kinds-of-void/), with a structural rather than contingent character. The from-to relation is what makes meaning possible; thematising it would require a vantage point outside the relation. As with Gurwitsch's thematic field, attempting to dissolve the void creates a new instance: the new focal object brings its own subsidiaries.

A materialist may object that the integration is in principle specifiable as a particular pattern of neural activation, and that the void is just the practical limit of introspection. The Map's response is conditional rather than question-begging. *If* the from-to relation is identifiable only phenomenologically — *if* the structural role subsidiaries play in carrying focal meaning is something one can pick out only from inside the relation — *then* a third-person specification of subsidiaries-plus-relation is a different focal object bearing on a higher-order whole, and the recursion of focal objects is the void. The materialist's reply is that the antecedent is false: the from-to relation is identifiable third-person and we are simply early in the project. That is a substantive objection, not an evasion, and the [Honest Limitation](#honest-limitation) section states the falsification condition for the conditional.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** is the most directly engaged tenet. The tacit integration void is a precise case where simplification by analytical decomposition *destroys* the phenomenon being analysed. Any account that treats meaning as the sum of decomposable subsidiaries misses what the integration accomplishes. The void is a counterexample to the assumption that simplicity-via-decomposition is always epistemically conservative.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** is engaged less directly but substantively. If consciousness reduced to neural information processing, the from-to relation would be specifiable as a particular pattern of activation. Polanyi's argument cuts the other way: the integration is not specified by the subsidiaries plus a relation; the relation is constitutive and resists specification. This does not prove dualism, but it preserves the structural pressure that motivates it—features of consciousness that resist analytic capture in principle, not just in practice.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** finds an unusual case. The felt sense functions causally—guiding articulation, action, and decision—without being decomposable into representations. Gendlin's *felt shift* describes a phenomenal check on cognition that has effects in the world but cannot be inspected as the kind of thing that has causes. The Map interprets this as compatible with consciousness exerting causal influence without becoming an object available to causal explanation.

The void also constrains the Map's own method. The catalogue of voids is itself an exercise in tacit integration: its coherence depends on subsidiary background commitments—tenets, apophatic discipline, prior articles—that bear on the focal task of mapping. Any apophatic catalogue must operate from-to. It cannot be its own focal object without becoming a different kind of object.

## Honest Limitation

Polanyi's destructive-analysis claim is contested. Some commentators argue the integration is in principle specifiable, just very complex — that "we know more than we can tell" reflects practical limits on linguistic compression rather than a structural impossibility. The Map centres an article on the structural argument without regarding the matter as settled.

**Falsification condition.** A concrete answer to "what would count as decomposing the from-to relation?": a third-person model that, when implemented and run, exhibits the integrative function the subsidiaries were performing — without thematising them as objects from inside the system. The model would have to carry particulars into a focal whole, be decomposed into subsidiaries-plus-binding-relation, and remain phenomenologically interpretable as exhibiting the same integration the human case does. If such a model is possible, the void weakens from unexplorable to contingently difficult. The structural claim is therefore conditional: *if* phenomenological identification of the from-to relation is non-redundant with third-person specification, the void is structural; if a third-person model captures what the phenomenological identification was tracking, the conditional is unsatisfied.

**Trained-meditator dual-awareness.** Meditators trained in dual-mode awareness (Lutz, Slagter, Dunne, & Davidson, 2008, on focused-attention vs open-monitoring practice; Mahamudra and Dzogchen *rigpa* training) report sustained simultaneous metacognitive and object-level awareness. If the reports describe genuine simultaneity rather than rapid alternation, "one focus at a time" is an empirical generalisation rather than a structural truth. The available reply distinguishes *attentional capacity* (graded, partially trainable) from *thematic-focal status* (binary at the level of any given content): even sustained dual-mode awareness has its own subsidiaries, which remain non-thematic. The reply is plausible, not knockdown; the meditation literature is a live empirical pressure on the strict version of the structural claim.

**Transformer attention.** Transformer attention weights subsidiary tokens to produce focal outputs through fully inspectable mathematics — exactly the kind of decomposition the structural claim rules out. Either transformers realise a Polanyian from-to and the void is empirically refuted for that case, or transformer attention is *implementational* rather than *phenomenological* and the void is about what phenomenological identification can pick out, not what mathematics can compute. The second response is the conditional doing the work: the from-to relation is whatever phenomenological identification picks out, and inspectability of attention weights does not entail that what was picked out has been specified. Both responses concede the structural claim's conditionality; neither is available to anyone who wants the void as an unconditional structural impossibility.

A third limitation: the void is described from inside human integration of body, action, and meaning. A mind with different integration may have different blind spots, but probably not none.
