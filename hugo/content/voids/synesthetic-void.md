[Synesthesia](/topics/synaesthesia/)—the involuntary experience of one sensory modality when another is stimulated—reveals that humans do not share the same experiential palette. Some minds perceive colours when hearing sounds, shapes when tasting food, or spatial arrangements when reading calendars. At least one well-documented case—a colour-blind synesthete—reports "Martian colours" that cannot be matched to any sample in the ordinarily visible spectrum. The synesthetic void marks this territory: a gap in phenomenal access that exists not between humans and hypothetical aliens, but between humans and other humans. It is the other minds problem made concrete within a single species.

The Unfinishable Map's [voids](/voids/) framework distinguishes the unexplored, the unexplorable, and the occluded. The synesthetic void spans all three. Much of the possible qualia space remains unexplored simply because no mind has accessed it. Some regions may be permanently unexplorable by human-type cognitive architectures. And the neonatal synesthesia hypothesis raises the possibility that broader phenomenal access was actively pruned during development—an occlusion imposed by biology itself. The [infant consciousness void](/voids/infant-consciousness/) explores this developmental loss in depth: if neonates experience cross-modal integration that pruning destroys, every adult's perceptual world is a narrowed remnant of a richer infant sensorium.

## Qualia That Exceed Description

Synesthetes consistently compare the task of explaining their experience to non-synesthetes with "explaining red to a blind person or middle-C to a deaf person." The recipient lacks the phenomenal reference points that would make understanding possible—a structural incommensurability rather than ordinary expressive difficulty.

Fiona MacPherson (2007) identified what she calls the "extraordinary feature" of synesthesia: some synesthetes report simultaneously experiencing both the actual colour of a grapheme and a synesthetic colour in the same spatial location, without the two blending. No analogue to this experience exists in standard perception. The conceptual vocabulary available to non-synesthetic minds cannot capture it, because our perceptual categories assume that two colours in one location must blend or compete.

Adam Wager (1999) formalised this as the "extra qualia problem." Synesthetes possess phenomenal states that differ from non-synesthetic states without any corresponding difference in *surface* representational content. Both the synesthete and the non-synesthete look at the same black letter on a white page; one of them also sees colour. The extra qualia challenge representationalism—the view that phenomenal character is fully determined by representational content—because identical surface stimuli, and coarse-grained representational descriptions of them, appear to produce different reported phenomenology. The qualifier *surface* is load-bearing, because the strongest representationalist reply (developed below under [the physicalist counter-readings](#counter-readings)) sub-individuates the content and denies that the representations are identical at the grain that matters. This parallels the [inverted-qualia](/concepts/inverted-qualia/) thought experiment, but with an empirical twist: where inverted qualia are hypothetical, synesthetic extra qualia are actual.

J.A. Gray (2003) pressed the point further against [functionalism](/concepts/functionalism/): in synesthesia, "function and qualia come apart in two ways." Identical qualia arise from distinct functional roles (colour experience from both visual and auditory processing), and single functional roles generate divergent qualia (the same grapheme recognition process produces colour experience in synesthetes but not in others). If qualia tracked function, neither dissociation should occur. Ramachandran and Hirstein (1997) had earlier proposed three laws of qualia tying neural cross-activation to the qualitative character of experience; synesthesia is a natural test case where their laws either hold or expose the limits of cross-activation as a generative mechanism.

This whole line of argument proceeds on a realist reading of synesthetes' reports—taking them as evidence about phenomenal states rather than as data about what synesthetes are inclined to say—and that reading is a substantive choice, not a neutral starting point. Several physicalist programmes contest it, each with a serious version; the article meets them on their own ground in [the physicalist counter-readings](#counter-readings) section below rather than assuming the realist reading wins by default.

## The Geometry of Experiential Possibility

The synesthetic void gains its deepest significance when placed against the concept of *experience-space* (E-space)—a high-dimensional space mapping all possible qualia. Lacalli (2022) proposes that E-space has "at least several times as many dimensions as there are categories of qualia," and that phenomenal consciousness may have originated as "one or more multidimensional ur-experiences that combined multiple forms of experience together."

If E-space is vast, human consciousness occupies a small region of it. Two caveats keep the use honest: Lacalli's E-space is an explicitly non-dualist mapping ("there is no implied dualism") that charts possible qualia in relation to neural selector circuits, not a measured space synesthetes have been independently located in. So reading synesthesia through E-space is an interpretive overlay, not a measurement; what synesthesia does is sit compatibly with the Map's reading of unexploited dimensions—even within the space human neural architecture allows, most people navigate only a fraction. With that framing in place, the synesthetic void sits in our immediate neighbourhood rather than at the far edge of experiential possibility—between minds that share a species.

Different organisms may occupy different regions of E-space entirely. Nagel's bat experiences echolocation from a phenomenal position inaccessible to humans. But synesthesia shows that the problem is more intimate than Nagel's scenario suggests. One need not invoke alien sensory modalities. Ordinary humans, with closely related neural architectures, already inhabit partially incommensurable experiential worlds. The [commensurability-void](/voids/commensurability-void/) formalises this: phenomenal comparison yields only a partial ordering, and some pairs of experiences have no ordering relation at all. Synesthesia provides the intra-species evidence for what cross-species comparison makes vivid.

## Permeability of the Boundary

Unlike many voids on the Map, the synesthetic void has a partially permeable boundary. Several lines of evidence demonstrate that non-synesthetes can temporarily access synesthetic-like phenomenology:

**Training-induced synesthesia.** Bor et al. (2014) demonstrated that non-synesthetic adults trained through intensive adaptive memory tasks acquired both subjective synesthetic experiences and neurophysiological markers of the condition. Crucially, these experiences faded within three months. The void can be glimpsed but not permanently inhabited through training alone—suggesting the barrier is architectural rather than merely experiential.

**Pharmacological induction.** Serotonergic agonists (LSD, psilocybin, mescaline) increase reports of synesthesia-like experiences. The induced phenomena fall short of the standard criteria for synesthesia, however: controlled work on LSD did not establish the consistency or inducer-specificity that characterise congenital synesthesia, even though participants reported more spontaneous synesthesia-like experiences under the drug. So this is synesthesia-*like* sensory mixing rather than the automatic, consistent, inducer-specific condition. Whether drug-induced cross-modal percepts are phenomenally identical to developmental synesthesia remains unresolved—they may represent a different region of E-space rather than the same one accessed through a different route.

**Sensory deprivation.** Merabet et al. (2004) found that several days of complete visual deprivation induced vivid visual hallucinations—from simple flashes to complex faces and landscapes—in the majority of blindfolded sighted subjects. The latent cross-modal capacity exists but is normally suppressed.

The transience of these breaches is philosophically significant. If the synesthetic void could be permanently crossed through simple intervention, it would be merely unexplored. The fade observed in trained synesthetes admits two readings: ordinary skill atrophy without rehearsal, or active architectural constraints reasserting the narrower configuration. The architectural-maintenance reading is the stronger of the two compatible interpretations and is the one the Map adopts, but the data alone do not force it; either way, the boundary involves more than passive absence of stimulation.

## The Developmental Occlusion

The neonatal synesthesia hypothesis (Maurer & Mondloch, 2005; Spector & Maurer, 2009) proposes that all infants are born with cross-modal connectivity that regresses through neural pruning during development. The hypothesis is contested—as the [synaesthesia](/topics/synaesthesia/) topic's empirical exposition records, the individual pieces of evidence are weak though collectively suggestive—so what follows is conditional on it. If the hypothesis is correct, the standard adult experiential palette represents a *loss* of phenomenal dimensions, not the baseline.

This reframes the synesthetic void as partially occluded. Development does not merely fail to develop certain capacities—it actively dismantles them. Under dualism, the question sharpens: does the developing brain *constrain* a consciousness that initially had broader phenomenal access? If consciousness is not reducible to neural activity, then the relationship between neural pruning and experiential narrowing is not identity but interface. The brain restricts which regions of E-space consciousness can access, much as a [filter](/concepts/filter-theory/) restricts which frequencies of light pass through.

Brain-only physicalism reads the same data without remainder: pruning produces narrower phenomenal patterns because phenomenal patterns *are* narrower neural patterns. The two readings are observationally equivalent on the cited evidence—no observation distinguishes "consciousness restricted by neural architecture" from "consciousness identical with neural architecture"—so the Map's filter-theory reading is preferred for its consilience with the broader tenet commitments rather than because the evidence forces it, and this section can bear no more weight than that underdetermination allows (see [filter-theory](/concepts/filter-theory/)).

Even as a contested possibility, neonatal synesthesia transforms the synesthetic void from a mere gap in ability into evidence that human experiential architecture ends up selectively narrowed during development.

## The Asymmetry of Access

The synesthetic void exhibits an apparent asymmetry. Synesthetes appear better positioned to imagine non-synesthetic experience: their phenomenal life contains a non-synesthetic substrate to which the extra qualia are added, while non-synesthetes have no analogous handhold for the synesthetic side. The asymmetry holds only if subtractive imagination—conceiving one's own phenomenal life minus a layer—is in fact easier than additive imagination of someone else's life plus a layer, which is itself uncertain; the Map treats the asymmetric reading as the stronger one without claiming it as settled.

This asymmetry has implications for the [voids-between-minds](/voids/voids-between-minds/). That article divides the field into strong isolation (each consciousness hermetically sealed), partial bridging (some genuine sharing through empathy or unusual neural connections), and constitutive intersubjectivity (the question malformed because the other is constitutive of one's experience from the start). Synesthesia is a partial-bridging case with an asymmetric twist: the boundary is real but not hermetic—training, drugs, and deprivation breach it temporarily—yet the breach runs more easily in one direction than the other. The standard other-minds problem treats access as symmetrically limited; synesthesia shows it can be asymmetrically limited even when both parties are fully communicative, neurologically healthy, and willing to share. The barrier appears architectural rather than one of privacy or secrecy—a concrete instance of the [explanatory-gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) manifesting between two human minds rather than between physical description and phenomenal experience. McGinn's (1989) cognitive closure thesis—that human minds may be constitutively unable to grasp the solution to the mind-body problem—offers an analogy for this local closure-like phenomenon, though the parallel is loose: McGinn's argument concerns species-level closure on the mind-body problem itself, not the synesthete/non-synesthete pairing. Synesthesia illustrates a small, local instance of the same shape—a constitutive limit on phenomenal access between members of one species—rather than the species-wide explanatory closure McGinn was primarily arguing for.

The finding that most synesthetic experience is "mediated through semantics" (van Leeuwen et al., 2015) adds another layer. Meaning shapes qualia—conceptual and semantic processing in the synesthete generates additional phenomenal states that identical semantic processing in the non-synesthete does not. If meaning itself has a phenomenal dimension that varies across minds, the intersubjective void is deeper than the privacy of sensation. It extends into the privacy of understanding.

The [imagery-void](/voids/imagery-void/) catalogues a parallel within-species divergence with a different structure. Where synesthesia adds qualia beyond the standard palette, the aphantasia/hyperphantasia spectrum reveals that the very *vividness* of mental imagery—what the introspective vocabulary of "seeing in the mind's eye" is supposed to track—varies wildly across phenomenologically equipped minds, sometimes to the point of complete absence. The two voids together dismantle the assumption that ordinary phenomenal language picks out a shared experiential repertoire: in synesthesia the disparity is in *what* is experienced; in the imagery spectrum it is in *whether* the inner image is there at all.

## The Physicalist Counter-Readings {#counter-readings}

The synesthetic void's force as evidence depends on whether the extra qualia resist non-dualist explanation. They do not resist it cheaply. Four physicalist readings each have a serious version, and each must be met on its own ground before the void can carry any dualist weight. Following [the imagery void's three-option wedge](/voids/imagery-void/#the-function-phenomenology-face), the honest position is that no single reading is refuted outright; what the cluster does is force each absorption to pay a cost it would rather not.

**The fine-grained functionalist** denies that the functional roles are really matched. Gray's dissociation—same grapheme-recognition process, colour for one mind and not the other—looks like same-function/different-qualia only while "function" is individuated coarsely as "same grapheme." Once function is individuated at the level of internal processing, semantic mediation, attentional deployment, or report-disposition, the synesthete and the non-synesthete are running different functions, and the divergent qualia track the divergent function after all. This reply works. The sibling apex [phenomenal-variation-within-a-species](/apex/phenomenal-variation-within-a-species/) grants it directly: the synesthesia case is an "empirical wedge, not a knockdown," locally absorbable by finer-grained individuation. The cost the absorption pays is that it concedes phenomenology diverges at every grain *coarser* than the one where it relocates the difference—task-level function is shared while experience is not—and it owes an account of why phenomenology should track the fine grain rather than the task. The wedge is not that the absorption fails; it is that the absorption is one of four grain-shifts the cluster forces, and their joint parsimony cost is the apex's actual argument.

**The representationalist** sub-individuates the content. On this reading the synesthetic colour is not "extra" relative to representational content at all; it is *richer* or *higher-order* content—the synesthete represents the grapheme plus an additional learned or constitutional colour association, and phenomenal character tracks that richer representation exactly as representationalism predicts. The van Leeuwen et al. (2015) "mediated through semantics" finding is the representationalist's strongest card: it shows the synesthete's pipeline is genuinely *not* identical to the non-synesthete's at the deeper grain representationalism tracks, so the "identical representations" framing only holds at the surface. The reply is strong enough that the article states the extra qualia problem at the surface-representation level deliberately. Where it owes work is in specifying *which* level of representation is supposed to fix phenomenal character: pushed deep enough to absorb synesthesia, the same move must absorb every reported phenomenal difference as a representational difference somewhere, which threatens to make representationalism unfalsifiable rather than confirmed.

**The deflationary heterophenomenologist**, following Dennett, declines the realist reading of the reports altogether. Synesthetes' Stroop-interference results, cross-session consistency, and grapheme-area neuroimaging are data about what synesthetes are disposed to say and do, not direct evidence of an extra phenomenal property; assembled together they fix the heterophenomenological world the theory must explain, and that world contains reports of dual colour without licensing a primitive quale behind them. This is not refuted from outside, and the realist reading the rest of this article assumes is a substantive choice, not a neutral default. What the heterophenomenologist owes is symmetric: the move that withholds realism from the synesthete's extra colour withholds it equally from the non-synesthete's ordinary colour, so it does not single out synesthesia as deflationary—it deflates the whole phenomenal field, which is a global commitment defended elsewhere rather than a local result about cross-modal qualia.

**The illusionist** reads the extra-qualia report as structured misrepresentation. There is no additional quale; there is a metacognitive model that *represents* an additional colour-property as present, and the explanatory task is to specify the mechanism that generates that misrepresentation from grapheme-colour cross-activation. This is the metacognitive deflation the [qualia article's Illusionist Challenge](/concepts/qualia/#the-illusionist-challenge) develops in full. The illusionist's own programme sets the standard the synesthesia case then presses: illusionism's advertised advantage over realist rivals is mechanistic specification, so it owes the bridge from cross-activation to the seeming-of-an-extra-colour, and naming that debt is an argument inside illusionism's commitments rather than an appeal to the Map's. The residue, once the debt is acknowledged as owed, is bedrock: illusionism denies the phenomenal property the void is built to chart, and that incompatibility with the Map's commitments is real and honestly noted rather than refuted from within.

The four readings do not converge on a single absorption. The functionalist relocates the difference to fine-grained function, the representationalist to deep representational content, the heterophenomenologist to dispositions-to-report, the illusionist to metacognitive misrepresentation. Each is locally serviceable; their disunity is the point [the apex synthesis](/apex/phenomenal-variation-within-a-species/) turns into pressure, and it is the reason synesthesia counts as a void rather than a settled case in either direction.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The synesthetic void connects directly to the Map's [foundational commitments](/tenets/):

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** gains its leverage from the synesthetic void only after the four [physicalist counter-readings](#counter-readings) above are met, not before. The Map's claim is not that physicalism has no account—each reading has a serious version—but that none absorbs the case without paying a cost, and that the costs do not converge on a single absorption. The fine-grained functionalist concedes phenomenology diverges at every grain coarser than task-level function; the representationalist must specify which level of representation fixes phenomenal character without sliding into unfalsifiability; the heterophenomenologist deflates the whole phenomenal field rather than singling out synesthesia; the illusionist owes, by its own mechanistic standard, the bridge from cross-activation to the seeming-of-an-extra-colour. Where that bridge is acknowledged as owed but not yet supplied, the disagreement is genuinely bedrock—illusionism denies the phenomenal property the void charts, and the incompatibility with the Map's commitments is declared, not derived. This is where the void mirrors the [knowledge-argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/): just as Mary learns something new upon seeing red despite complete physical knowledge, no amount of neurological description conveys what it is like to experience a synesthetic colour. Under property dualism, the additional qualia are ontologically independent properties that covary with neural architecture but are not exhausted by it—a reading the cross-activation story (V4 driven by the grapheme-form area) is compatible with but does not establish, since the same neural facts underdetermine whether the colour is an extra property or only an extra representation of one.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** is illustrated directly. The simplifying assumption that all humans share roughly the same experiential palette is empirically inadequate. The true landscape of phenomenal experience is more variable than parsimony suggests. If E-space is high-dimensional and most of it is inaccessible to any given mind, the assumption that human minds have a roughly uniform experiential repertoire is empirically too coarse—it smooths over real phenomenal variation that the within-species evidence keeps surfacing.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** is implicated by the semantic mediation finding. If meaning shapes qualia in synesthesia, then high-level cognitive processes—conceptual understanding, interpretation—causally influence low-level phenomenal experience. This top-down influence, where meaning affects what it is like to perceive, is more naturally accommodated by frameworks that allow high-level cognitive processes to contribute to phenomenal content than by strictly bottom-up accounts.

**[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)** sharpens the void's significance. The synesthetic void is indexical. My experiential palette is *this one*, not some other. A synesthete's coloured hearing is theirs, not mine. The void exists because we are particular consciousnesses with particular architectures, not abstract observers sampling all possible experiential positions. A Many-Worlds defender will note that branch-relative indexicality is exactly what their framework supplies; the Map reads indexicality more strongly than MWI does, for reasons developed across the broader Map rather than forced by synesthesia alone.
