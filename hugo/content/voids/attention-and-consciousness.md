Attention creates voids as a structural necessity, not as a contingent failure. To attend to anything is to render something else unattendable — not merely unattended, but actively excluded by the operation of attending itself. When attention is then *disordered*, these structural voids become epistemologically impenetrable: we cannot distinguish a consciousness that is different from one that merely *acts* differently because its interface is impaired. Together, the normal and pathological cases reveal that attention is both the mechanism by which consciousness accesses anything and the source of its deepest blind spots.

## Figure Creates Ground

The Gestalt psychologists established that perception is structured by figure-ground relations. To see a figure requires producing a ground — the figure cannot exist as figure without it. Aron Gurwitsch developed this further: perception is always structured into theme (what is focal), thematic field (what is substantively relevant), and margin (what is merely co-present). The margin follows different organisational principles than the theme. Making the margin thematic creates a new margin.

Every act of attention therefore produces unattendable territory as its structural condition. The void is not incidental to attention but constitutive of it.

## The Penumbra and the Fringes

Neuroscience reveals something stronger than mere non-attention to the ground. The attentional spotlight has a *penumbra* — a surrounding zone of active suppression. Research by Müller and colleagues showed that neural activity in regions representing nearby-but-unattended locations is actively reduced below baseline. To illuminate is to darken.

William James identified a complementary structure: the fringes of consciousness. Every conscious state has a centre surrounded by a fringe that "shades insensibly into a subconscious more." The fringes modify the theme and bridge gaps between thoughts but — crucially — cannot be the primary focus of attention. Attempting to focus on a fringe element transforms it into a theme while generating new fringes. The fringe-quality *requires* non-focality.

The centre works in one way while the margins work in another. Marginal awareness is vague, relational, connective — it provides the sense that the current thought belongs somewhere, means something beyond its explicit content. But this marginal work cannot be directly observed without transforming it into something else.

## Empirical Demonstrations

**Inattentional blindness** shows that attention does not select from what is already consciously present — it determines what *becomes* consciously present. In the invisible gorilla experiment (Simons & Chabris, 1999), subjects focused on a counting task do not experience a gorilla-shaped gap in perception. The gorilla is genuinely absent from experience — never entering awareness at all.

**Binocular rivalry** reveals a limit where attention meets structural constraints it cannot overcome. When different images are presented to each eye, perception alternates. Even maximal voluntary effort cannot arrest the alternations. The suppressed percept is not dim or peripheral but *absent* — then it switches. The void is the absence of whichever percept is currently suppressed.

These phenomena suggest multiple levels of attention-created void: the marginalised (present but not focal) and the excluded (not present at all).

## The Phenomenology of Attention Voids

**The peripheral shimmer.** Content at the edge of attention that vanishes when you try to look directly. You sense something there — movement, colour, meaning — but direct attention destroys it.

**The fringe-sense.** Knowing something is present at the margins without being able to bring it forward. The word on the tip of your tongue. The connection you can feel but cannot articulate.

**The suppressive dark.** Not mere absence but a felt sense that something is being kept out. In focused concentration, the world contracts; what lies beyond the focus has a quality of active exclusion.

**The alternation frustration.** In bistable perception, the sense that something is there that attention cannot stabilise. You know both interpretations exist; you can see each in turn; you cannot see both at once.

## When the Instrument Breaks

If attention creates voids *normally*, what happens when attention itself is disordered? The Unfinishable Map's [attention-as-interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/) framework distinguishes consciousness from the mechanisms through which it operates. Attention disorders disrupt the bridge — and in doing so, create a distinctive epistemological void.

Clinical psychiatry diagnoses attention disorders through behavioural observation and self-report. Both channels depend on the very system that is disordered. A person whose consciousness is fully engaged but whose dopamine-modulated thresholds are dysregulated looks identical, behaviourally, to one whose conscious engagement is itself diminished. No current measurement can distinguish these.

Self-report fares worse. When someone with ADHD says "I couldn't focus," this might describe interface failure (consciousness present but unable to sustain its grip), altered experience (consciousness intermittently present), phenomenological variation (consciousness present but scattered rather than focused), or inaccurate introspection (because [introspection](/concepts/introspection/) itself depends on attentional stability). The disorder prevents the person from reliably distinguishing between these possibilities. Introspective access requires stable attention; when attention is disordered, introspective reports about the disorder are generated by the disordered system.

## Hyperfocus and the Developmental Void

ADHD produces a striking paradox: the same person who cannot sustain attention on routine tasks can become utterly absorbed in intrinsically motivating activities. If ADHD were simply reduced attentional capacity, hyperfocus would be inexplicable. The interface model handles it by distinguishing threshold modulation (dopamine-dependent, impaired in ADHD) from the raw capacity for conscious engagement (intact or even enhanced).

But hyperfocus introduces its own void. During intense absorption, metacognitive monitoring often disappears. Consciousness seems both *more* present (vivid engagement) and *less* present (reflective self-awareness drops away). Which aspect represents "more" consciousness? The question assumes these can be compared on a single scale, but attention disorders suggest consciousness has multiple dimensions that can dissociate.

Lifelong attention disorders deepen the void further. A person who has never experienced neurotypical attention has no baseline for comparison — they cannot know what attention feels like to someone without the disorder, any more than a congenitally colourblind person can know what red looks like. Their reports are doubly limited: constrained by the disorder itself and by the absence of a baseline against which to measure that constraint. This is the hallmark of an [occluded void](/voids/three-kinds-of-void/).

## Hemispatial Neglect

Hemispatial neglect is particularly revealing. Patients with right parietal damage lose awareness of the left visual field — not just the ability to attend to it, but apparently the experience of it altogether. They do not see a gap; they do not see anything missing. If the [attention interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/) is damaged in this specific way, an entire dimension of experience vanishes without leaving a trace in phenomenology.

This suggests that at least some of what we call consciousness depends on attentional access in a way that resists clean separation. The interface may partly constitute the territory it spans. The dualist can maintain that consciousness persists without attention while acknowledging that the *structure* of conscious experience depends on the interface through which it operates.

## Whether the Limits Are Real

Could the attention void be merely contingent? Three considerations suggest the structural voids are genuine:

First, the figure-ground relation appears to be a condition of perception as such. Any system that perceives particulars against a field must produce the field as ground.

Second, prioritisation — which Sebastian Watzl argues is the essence of attention — necessarily creates hierarchy. To prioritise is to rank. Ranking requires lower ranks.

Third, finite access entails exclusion. Only a consciousness that simultaneously and fully grasps everything could attend without creating voids.

The disorder-based voids may be equally permanent. We cannot determine, from inside an attention disorder, whether consciousness is the same consciousness operating through a degraded interface, or a different form of consciousness that the degraded interface prevents us from recognising as different. The materialist response — identifying consciousness with attention — merely relocates the void rather than dissolving it.

This makes the attention void among the most fundamental in The Unfinishable Map's framework. It concerns not content we cannot access but the *mechanism* by which we access anything.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** receives support from both directions. Neural mechanisms explain *how* attention creates voids — lateral inhibition, biased competition, executive gating. They do not explain *why* there is something it is like to have content excluded from awareness. The suppressive dark has phenomenal character. And the disorder-based void is itself a *consequence* of dualism: precisely because consciousness is distinct from its interface, the question of how to access consciousness through a faulty interface becomes pressing. A materialist who identifies consciousness with attention faces no such question — and thereby misses the epistemological depth.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** gains support from voluntary attention. We *choose* what to attend to (at least partially). This choice produces the void — consciousness directing physical processing toward some targets necessarily directs it away from others. Every attention void is evidence of mental causation. When attention is disordered, the range and reliability of conscious action are restricted without being eliminated — though we cannot determine how *much* restriction any given disorder imposes.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** is directly implicated. Attention voids suggest consciousness is structured by mechanisms that systematically hide aspects of reality. Our preference for simple theories may reflect what survives the attentional filter — not what is real but what is attendable. And the simple account of attention disorders ("just brain dysfunction") obscures the epistemological void at the centre: what appears to be a straightforward neurological explanation dissolves under closer inspection into a tangle of indistinguishable possibilities.

## What Would Challenge This View

The structural claim would face difficulty if training eliminated the figure-ground structure (contemplative reports describe heightened awareness of the structure itself but not its elimination), if parallel processing replaced serial attention (even massively parallel systems develop attention-like selection), if AI exhibited void-free attention, or if careful introspection could reveal marginal contents *as marginal* without transforming them.

The disorder-based claim would be challenged if neuroscience found markers of conscious quality independent of attention — enabling us to determine from outside whether consciousness changes when the interface degrades. Future brain-computer interfaces might partially bridge this gap, though the fundamental circularity may resist technological solutions.
