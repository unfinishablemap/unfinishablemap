Consciousness does not fail all at once. Under fatigue, capacities are shed in a predictable order — attention first, then executive function, then creative thinking — while basic rule-following persists longest. This systematic degradation constitutes a void because consciousness cannot observe its own narrowing from within. The faculties needed to detect the narrowing are among the first to go. The observer degrades alongside the observed, creating a structural blind spot where the architecture of phenomenal experience is visible only through its collapse.

## The Shedding Hierarchy

Sleep deprivation research reveals a consistent order of cognitive loss. After sustained wakefulness, tonic alertness and [sustained attention](/voids/attention-and-consciousness/) degrade first. Working memory follows. Then executive functions — planning, inhibition, flexible thinking — deteriorate as the prefrontal cortex proves especially sensitive to sleep loss. Creative and divergent thinking degrades significantly. Yet convergent, rule-based reasoning persists. As one review puts it: "Whereas many convergent and rule-based tasks are relatively unaffected by sleep loss, more creative, divergent and innovative aspects of cognition do appear to be degraded" (Killgore 2010).

Anesthesia produces a strikingly parallel hierarchy. At low doses: analgesia, amnesia, distorted time perception. Environmental awareness is lost before self-awareness. Self-awareness is lost before internal thought. Patients may retain inner experience while appearing externally inert — unresponsiveness does not equal absence of consciousness (Sanders et al. 2023). The phenomenal layers peel away from outside in, environmental connection first, self-relation last.

This convergence across two very different causes — metabolic deprivation and pharmacological suppression — suggests the hierarchy is not an artefact of either mechanism but a feature of consciousness itself. What degrades first is what consciousness adds on top of more basic processing. What persists longest is closer to the ground floor.

## The Self-Undermining Observer

The fatigue void's distinctive character is that it is self-concealing. Self-assessment accuracy declines faster than actual performance under sleep deprivation — people do not know how impaired they are. The monitoring system fails before the execution systems it monitors.

This creates a structural impossibility. To observe consciousness narrowing, one needs precisely the metacognitive capacities that fatigue degrades first. By the time the narrowing is significant, the capacity to notice it has already been lost. This is [self-opacity](/voids/self-opacity/) in its most concrete form — not the general philosophical observation that the mind cannot fully know itself, but a specific, measurable instance where the monitoring system fails before the systems it monitors. Third-person methods — behavioural observation, error analysis, neuroimaging — can reconstruct the hierarchy after the fact. But the first-person experience of crossing these thresholds is, by its nature, unavailable to the consciousness crossing them.

The [phenomenology of the edge](/voids/phenomenology-of-the-edge/) takes a distinctive form here. Unlike the hard edges of logical impossibility or the vertiginous edges of infinite regress, the fatigue edge is soft. Consciousness does not hit a wall. It dissolves gradually, and the dissolution feels less like losing something than like setting down a burden.

## The Comfort of Narrowing

Fatigue-induced narrowing often feels like relief. The shed capacities are experienced as weights released, not abilities lost. The rich peripheral awareness that characterises alert consciousness — the ambient processing of sounds, social cues, background concerns — drops away, and what remains is a simpler, narrower engagement that consciousness welcomes.

This makes the fatigue void self-concealing in a way that connects to the [complicity-void](/voids/complicity-void/). Consciousness does not merely fail to detect its own narrowing — it actively misinterprets narrowing as simplification, as the world becoming more manageable. The felt sense is not "I am losing capacities" but "things are becoming clearer" or "nothing else matters right now." The progressive tunnelling that objectively represents cognitive impairment is subjectively experienced as focus.

Byung-Chul Han distinguishes between "I-tiredness" — the isolating exhaustion of burnout — and "we-tiredness," a communal, peaceful fatigue that opens rather than closes. The phenomenological texture of fatigue is not uniform. Self-imposed exhaustion from achievement culture differs from the fatigue of physical labour, which differs from the fatigue of sustained emotional engagement. Each produces narrowing, but the felt quality of the narrowing varies.

## Fatigue as Ontological Disclosure

The phenomenology of fatigue reveals layered temporal structure. Cristian Ciocan (2025) distinguishes between acute effort-fatigue, which passes with rest, and sedimented weariness — the accumulation of fatigue into a background condition that colours all subsequent experience. This temporal sedimentation means fatigue is not merely a state but a process that restructures the relationship between consciousness and its own history.

Emmanuel Levinas treated fatigue not as a psychological state but as an ontological disclosure. In *Existence and Existents* (1947), composed during captivity in a Nazi labour camp, Levinas argued that fatigue reveals the *il y a* — the impersonal, anonymous being that underlies individual existence. Fatigue discloses "the phenomenon of impersonal being" that is "horrible and independent of me."

If consciousness is, as Levinas suggests, an "awakening" from this impersonal ground, then fatigue is a partial return toward it. The exhausted mind does not simply lose processing power. It approaches a more fundamental state — existence without the full elaboration of subjectivity. The burden that fatigue reveals is the effort required simply to be a subject at all.

This philosophical reading aligns with the empirical hierarchy. What degrades first under fatigue — creative thinking, flexible planning, emotional regulation — represents the highest elaborations of subjectivity. What persists longest — basic rule-following, simple stimulus-response — is closer to the mechanical processing that does not require full conscious engagement. Fatigue strips away the distinctively conscious contributions, leaving the automated substrate increasingly exposed.

## Separable Phenomenal Capacities

Fatigue reveals joints in phenomenal experience that are invisible under normal conditions. Tania Singer's neuroscience research demonstrates that empathy fatigues while compassion does not. These are distinct neural circuits: empathy (mirroring others' suffering) narrows under sustained exposure, while compassion (the motivation to relieve suffering) proves more resilient (Singer & Klimecki 2014).

What appears as unified "caring" is actually a composite. Only fatigue reveals this. When all systems operate normally, empathy and compassion feel like aspects of a single capacity. Selective fatigue exposes the seams.

The same principle applies across phenomenal experience. Ego depletion research documents the behavioural observation that decision quality degrades after sustained decision-making. The original resource-depletion model (Baumeister et al. 1998) has largely failed to replicate — meta-analyses find the effect small or absent when correcting for publication bias — but the phenomenological core remains: people *feel* something running out, and this felt sense accompanies measurable performance decline. Whether this reflects a discrete resource, motivational shift, or something else entirely, the phenomenology is the same. We experience depletion without understanding its mechanism. The felt sense may be a constructed signal rather than a transparent report on an underlying process.

## Graceful Degradation and Architecture

Consciousness degrades gracefully rather than catastrophically. Performance diminishes proportionally rather than collapsing at a threshold. This pattern is informative: the degradation order reveals which capacities are load-bearing and which are elaborations. The architecture of phenomenal experience, invisible when everything works, becomes legible through its systematic failure.

The [resolution-void](/voids/resolution-void/) documents a related phenomenon — the six-order-of-magnitude compression between available and accessed information. Fatigue adds a temporal dimension to this spatial constraint. Under fatigue, even the narrow band of information that consciousness normally accesses contracts further, and the contraction follows the same priority ordering: peripheral processing is sacrificed to preserve central tasks.

The [habituation-void](/voids/habituation-void/) operates through a different mechanism — repetition-induced neural fatigue — but produces a parallel effect: the disappearance of phenomenal content that was previously present. In habituation, familiar stimuli vanish from consciousness. In fatigue, entire phenomenal capacities vanish. Both reveal that what consciousness presents as "everything" is already a selection, and that selection can narrow further.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The fatigue void connects to the Map's [tenets](/tenets/) at several points.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** gains indirect support, though the argument requires care. The shedding hierarchy alone does not challenge materialism — the prefrontal cortex is metabolically expensive and sensitive to sleep deprivation, so materialists can explain higher-function-first degradation through differential neural vulnerability. The stronger dualist evidence comes from anesthesia: inner experience persists after environmental disconnection and behavioural responsiveness are lost. This dissociation — between neural systems that are pharmacologically suppressed and phenomenal experience that continues — is harder to explain as straightforward neural hierarchy. The persistence of internal thought after the loss of environmental awareness is consistent with consciousness having aspects that are not reducible to the neural systems being suppressed.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** is illuminated by the observation that self-monitoring fails before execution systems. If consciousness provides genuine causal oversight of cognitive processes, its degradation should produce downstream effects beyond what automated neural impairment would predict. The fragility of the conscious monitoring layer relative to automated processing is consistent with consciousness being an additional contributor — one that can be selectively withdrawn — rather than merely an emergent property of the underlying computation.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** is directly illustrated. The simple model of fatigue as "running out of energy" masks a complex, structured phenomenon with existential, phenomenological, and architectural dimensions. Consciousness is not a single thing that dims uniformly but a composite of separable capacities with a priority ordering — an architecture visible only through degradation, invisible to the simplifying impulse that treats consciousness as unitary.
