The Vagueness Void is the structural impossibility, for any mind operating from inside its own conceptual repertoire, of locating where its concepts stop applying. Almost every concept consciousness deploys—*red*, *tall*, *anger*, *person*, *conscious*—admits borderline cases where neither application nor refusal is determinately correct. The void has three structural faces conjoined: third-personal Sorites indeterminacy (no incremental method can fix the cut-off), first-personal introspective indeterminacy (the same fuzz applies to the concepts consciousness uses on its own states), and meta-level higher-order vagueness (the borderlines themselves have borderlines, recursively, with no fixed point accessible from inside). Each face inherits the indeterminacy of the others, and consciousness has no neutral perch from which to fix any of them.

The void is not exotic. It sits beneath every judgement of typicality, every diagnostic categorisation, every introspective report of the form "this is roughly anger." Subjects describe concepts as sharp from the centre and as dissolving at the edges; incremental refinement *generates* new edges rather than dissolving the old. The Vagueness Void marks the place where that dissolution becomes structural rather than incidental.

The scope is "almost every," not every, and the exception is load-bearing. Formal concepts—mathematical (*two*, *prime*), logical (*and*, *for all*), and self-referential indexical ones (*this very token*)—have determinate extensions and admit no Sorites series; nothing incremental connects *two* to *three* through borderline integers. The void therefore covers the empirically-deployed concepts of mind—perceptual, emotional, social-categorical, and the mental-state concepts consciousness turns on itself—not the formal repertoire a mind can also wield. This is how the "no neutral perch" claim survives: consciousness possesses sharp categories, but none is a vantage point on the vague ones, because fixing the borderline of *anger* requires *anger*-judgements, not arithmetic.

## Three Faces, One Void

The void exhibits the [conjunction-coalesce](/apex/conjunction-coalesce/) structure used elsewhere for [suspension-void](/voids/suspension-void/), [agency-void](/voids/agency-void/), [imagery-void](/voids/imagery-void/), and the [aspect-perception void](/voids/aspect-perception-void/): a single void with multiple faces whose informativeness lies in their seam. The apex's sub-typology classifies this void's seam-relationship as **reflexive**—the seam is constituted by self-application, with the metalanguage in which any face is stated itself subject to the indeterminacy the faces describe. Vagueness-void is currently the typology's only exemplar of the reflexive type; the [suspension-void](/voids/suspension-void/)'s verification face is reflexive at one face only, not across the whole seam. Each face alone is a recognised problem in the literature; the seam between them, demonstrated in §"The Seam" below, is the analytical claim.

What makes the three faces one void rather than three adjacent problems is that the higher-order face is not a separate vagueness sitting above the other two but the *same* vagueness applied reflexively. The third-personal Sorites face concerns *C*-judgements about the world; the first-personal introspective face concerns *C*-judgements consciousness makes about its own states; the higher-order face concerns the predicate "borderline" used to describe either. These are not three vocabularies but one vocabulary deployed at three targets, and the metalanguage in which "borderline" is fixed is drawn from the very repertoire whose object-level applications are vague. So the third face does not add indeterminacy from outside; it shows that the resources a mind would use to bound the first two faces—to say which precisifications are "admissible," which traces are "borderline," which orders are "fixed"—are themselves first-order-vague terms. The unification is structural: removing the higher-order face would leave the other two dissolvable by a sufficiently sharp metalanguage, and that face is precisely the demonstration that no such metalanguage is available from inside.

### The Sorites Face (Unexplorable, with Occluded traces)

For almost any concept *C*, there is an incremental series along which *C* neither sharply applies nor sharply fails. One grain is not a heap; adding one grain to a non-heap does not yield a heap; therefore no number of grains makes a heap. The argument is formally valid in classical logic, so rejecting its conclusion requires rejecting a premise—that is, locating a cut-off—or modifying the logic. Either move is costly.

Williamson's epistemic theory ([Williamson 1994](#references)) preserves classical logic by accepting that vague terms have sharp boundaries fixed by unknowable patterns of usage. The position survives because of his **margin-for-error principle**: if a subject knows *p*, then in all situations similar enough to the subject's actual situation, *p* is still true. Any belief sharp enough to identify the cut-off of "tall" would fail this constraint—the cut-offs of vague predicates are unknowable in principle, not merely in practice. Williamson predicts this unknowability and treats it as a feature of the view, not a cost; the present article therefore engages epistemicism on its commitment to *determinate* cut-offs (the higher-order face below) rather than on its acceptance that the cut-offs are inaccessible.

Supervaluationism, fuzzy logic, contextualism, and intuitionism each reject sharp cut-offs but inherit revenge problems. Supervaluationism preserves classical consequence by treating a sentence as true iff true under all admissible precisifications—but which precisifications count as "admissible" is itself vague. Bobzien's 2024 modal "generic solution" is compatible with several theories simultaneously, showing the Sorites is *technically* dissolvable while leaving the underlying metaphysics underdetermined. No theory dissolves the void without paying somewhere.

The Sorites face is primarily *Unexplorable* in the sense the [three-kinds typology](/voids/three-kinds-of-void/) reserves for limits that resist mapping by their own architecture: any method deployed from inside must itself use *C*-judgements, and is subject to the same indeterminacy it tries to resolve. It carries an Occluded trace as well, because vague predicates leave third-person signatures—patterns of disagreement, hedging, and graded assent across competent users. What keeps the dominant classification Unexplorable is the direction of the residue: the Sorites face's traces point *outward*, at public usage a mind can study in others, and the architecture that defeats mapping is the logical one (no cut-off survives incremental pressure), not a perceptual blind spot. The introspective face below inverts this—its traces exist too, but the system cannot perceive its *own* borderlines from within, so occlusion rather than logical resistance is load-bearing. The two faces share a logical structure and differ only in whether unmappability is owed chiefly to the logic or to the first-person blind spot—exactly the distinction the typology tracks.

### The Introspective Face (Occluded)

Vagueness extends inward to the concepts consciousness uses to categorise its own states. Asking "is this still anger or now resentment?" admits no determinate answer, and the asking does not reliably produce one. Hampton's typicality data show graded membership across cultures and tasks: robins are more typical birds than penguins, and typicality predicts response time, ease of recall, and inference patterns. Multiple-definitions models suggest that graded membership arises because subjects sample multiple internal definitions, each with different boundaries. Cognitive vagueness is robust and not eliminable by stipulation.

The face goes deeper than category extension. Phenomenal concepts themselves may be vague. Papineau argues they are; Antony argues the argument fails; Simon argues "phenomenally conscious" admits no borderline cases; Schwitzgebel argues it does. Schwitzgebel's quadrilemma—nothing is conscious; everything is conscious; sharp boundary; consciousness is vague—defends the fourth horn, holding that a borderline conscious state is one for which it is neither determinately like-something nor determinately not to undergo it. Whether the void here is real or merely conceptual is itself within the void.

Simon's case for sharp boundaries on "phenomenally conscious" rests on the conceivability of zombies, not on any intrinsic-character premise. He defends a Positive Characterization Thesis—every genuine borderline case admits a positive characterization that shows a competent speaker what makes it borderline—and argues that the conceivability of zombies (creatures physically identical to conscious ones but wholly lacking phenomenal consciousness) entails that no such characterization can exist for "phenomenally conscious." If so, the predicate has no borderline cases: for any creature at any time it is determinate whether phenomenal consciousness is present. Schwitzgebel contests exactly this, holding that borderline cases are conceivable once an inappropriately demanding standard of conceivability is relaxed, so a state can be indeterminately conscious without a sharp present-or-absent structure. The substantive question is whether *being like something* can have borderline cases without collapsing into incoherence. The present article cannot resolve that from inside; the disagreement runs at the level of whether zombie-conceivability forecloses positive characterizations of borderline phenomenal states, where the article's introspective-face commitments are incompatible with Simon's framework. Simon's position would, if correct, close this face of the void from the other side. The article proceeds with Schwitzgebel's reading because it coheres with the other two faces and with the empirical psychiatric record below, while granting that Simon's argument is not refuted inside Simon's framework here.

Empirical psychiatry confirms the structural reading. The literature finds "no sharp boundary between the normal and the pathological" and notes that semantic vagueness "by its very nature cannot be eliminated through scientific discoveries" ([Vagueness in Psychiatry](#references)). For exactly the mental kinds the Map cares about, vagueness is structural rather than provisional.

This face also intersects [mutation-void](/voids/mutation-void/): for inward concepts, the asking can shift the state under examination without either earlier or later state being definitely the answer. The introspective face is *Occluded* in the [three-kinds typology](/voids/three-kinds-of-void/): the structure leaves traces and shapes outcomes, but the system whose categories it concerns cannot perceive its own borderlines while operating within them.

### The Higher-Order Face (Unexplorable)

The borderline of borderline cases is itself borderline. The vagueness of "tall" generates a vagueness of "borderline-tall", which generates a vagueness of "borderline-borderline-tall", with no fixed point accessible from inside and no determinate hierarchy of orders that the system whose concepts they govern can survey. Williamson's account preserves classical logic by *predicting* unknowable cut-offs at every order—a consequence the view embraces rather than a price it pays. Supervaluationism makes higher-order vagueness explicit but admits an indefinite hierarchy of "admissibility" predicates each itself vague. The hierarchy has no top from inside.

This face is meta: it concerns the metalanguage in which the other faces are stated. The Schwitzgebel/Simon/Antony/Papineau exchanges illustrate the pattern—the dispute over whether phenomenal concepts admit borderlines is itself indeterminate, because the disputants are using vague concepts in the metalanguage to argue about vagueness in the object language. Like the Sorites face, this is *Unexplorable*—but its unexplorability operates one level up from the level of concept application.

## The Seam

None of the three faces alone establishes the void. The Sorites face alone admits dissolution by epistemicism's unknowable cut-offs, supervaluationism's admissible precisifications, or fuzzy logic's graded truth-values—a logician's bargain. The introspective face alone yields to sharper operationalisation, finer-grained typicality data, or stipulative refinement of psychiatric kinds—a psychologist's bargain. The higher-order face alone yields to fixing a finite hierarchy of orders and disallowing infinite ascent—a metalinguistic bargain. Together they close every escape. The introspective face removes the supervaluationist escape because admissibility-of-precisification is a third-person property—a precisification counts as admissible if competent users would extend the predicate that way—and for inward concepts no third-person admissibility data exist that are not themselves first-person reports drawn from the same machinery whose neutrality the face puts in question; the asking can shift the state under examination and stipulation cannot reliably help. The higher-order face removes the epistemicist escape because meta-vagueness regenerates whatever cut-off the first-order epistemicist names: any precisification of "knows the cut-off" itself admits borderline cases, and Williamson's margin-for-error principle applied at the meta-level predicts that the meta-cut-off too is unknowable, so first-order determinacy yields second-order indeterminacy at every order. The Sorites face removes the finite-hierarchy escape because the metalanguage in which any bounded hierarchy is fixed must itself use vague predicates ("admissible", "competent", "similar enough"), and the Sorites argument iterated on those predicates regenerates the indeterminacy the hierarchy was supposed to bound. The faces are logically severable—an architecture could in principle have any one without the others, and the AI illustration in §"Approaches to the Edge" is a partial case—and that severability is what makes the human case's conjunction informative rather than tautological. The reflexive seam argued in §"Three Faces, One Void" is what closes the escapes jointly: each face's bargain is paid in the same vague metalanguage the next shows to be unfixable.

## Phenomenology of the Edge

Reports from subjects approaching borderline cases share a recognisable pattern, well-documented across the psychological and philosophical literatures:

- **Either-way comfort.** Subjects describe the borderline case as sitting comfortably under either label and equally uncomfortably under either.
- **Stipulative verdicts under pressure.** Pressed to decide, subjects describe the verdict as arbitrary—chosen rather than discovered.
- **Recursive frustration.** Subjects who refine the criterion describe new borderlines emerging; the refinement is reported as displaced rather than converged.
- **Introspective wobble.** For inward concepts, subjects report that the asking can shift the answer without either state being described as definitively the answer.

These report-patterns cannot themselves be sharpened into a determinate mark of borderline-ness; the descriptive vocabulary applied to vagueness is itself vague at its edges—a feature shared with the broader [edge-states-and-void-probes](/voids/edge-states-and-void-probes/). The framing is heterophenomenological: these are reports about borderline-case engagement, not a privileged first-person data-stream the introspective face has just declared unreliable.

## Approaches to the Edge

Direct methods do not work by construction. Stipulating a cut-off does not eliminate vagueness; it fixes one tier of the hierarchy and produces a new vagueness one level up. Indirect methods do better:

- **Apophatic mapping** ([apophatic-cartography](/voids/apophatic-cartography/)): catalogue the cases that are clearly in, clearly out, and the residue that resists classification, treating the residue as structural data rather than as a target to be eliminated.
- **Margin-for-error reasoning**: Williamson's framework yields determinate negative results—we can say what a knowable cut-off would have to look like and demonstrate that no actual cut-off can satisfy those conditions.
- **Multiple precisifications**: characterise the determinate space of admissible boundaries even where each boundary within it is indeterminate.
- **Compound failure signatures** ([compound-failure-signatures](/voids/compound-failure-signatures/)): where vagueness compounds with another void—e.g., recognition of one's own emotional state ([recognition-void](/voids/recognition-void/))—the joint failure pattern can be data about cognitive architecture even where each component is individually unmappable.

AI systems with explicit decision thresholds illustrate one disambiguation of vagueness rather than vagueness as this article uses the term. Such systems have determinate cut-offs at model output whose location shifts with prompt and context—context-relative sharpness rather than the void's structural indeterminacy—and the underlying representations show graded structure analogous to human typicality. One can audit a model's behaviour across a Sorites series in a way one cannot audit a human's, but the audit reveals context-conditional sharpness rather than borderline cases that resist resolution from inside the system's own repertoire. Whether AI systems also instantiate the introspective and higher-order faces—and therefore the void rather than the neighbouring phenomenon—is not settled by the threshold-shift evidence; [non-human-minds-as-void-explorers](/voids/non-human-minds-as-void-explorers/) earn their keep mapping the neighbouring phenomenon while leaving the void-instantiation question open.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The vagueness void connects most directly to the Map's tenet that **Occam's Razor Has Limits**. The semantics that preserves classical logic for vague predicates—sharp boundaries—yields cognitively inaccessible cut-offs (Williamson predicts and welcomes this consequence; he treats unknowability as a feature of the view, not a cost it incurs). Every alternative theory pays a comparable price elsewhere: supervaluationism's admissibility regress, fuzzy logic's higher-order order-relations, contextualism's context-individuation problem. Parsimony, applied here, produces opacity rather than transparency. On the Map's reading, this is a paradigm case of the tenet: simplicity reaches a wall where each "simple" fix generates revenge problems.

Secondary tenet alignment runs through **Dualism**. If consciousness is not reducible to physical processes, the concepts it uses on its own states need not have sharper boundaries than physical kinds—the introspective face aligns with the Map's view that self-categorisation lacks classical introspectionism's assumed sharpness.

A Popperian honesty is owed. The void claim, as the article uses "structural impossibility", is consistent with every observable distribution of borderline-case behaviour—any apparent counter-instance can be absorbed as a use of vague concepts attempting to fix vagueness from inside, and that reabsorption is invited by the architecture rather than by the data. The claim is therefore metaphysical-structural rather than empirically falsifiable. The Map accepts this cost in light of the Occam's-Razor-Has-Limits tenet: in domains of incomplete knowledge, parsimony cannot be the sole criterion, and an unfalsifiable structural claim earns its keep through its cartographic work—naming the joint pattern, organising the literature's revenge-problem regularities, anticipating what alternative remedies would have to look like—rather than through empirical risk. The void claim competes with physicalism not on empirical grounds but on which framework better maps the indeterminacy the evidence leaves open.

A reflexive consequence follows: every void article uses vague concepts (*structural*, *in-principle*, *borderline*), and the void framework operates within vagueness it cannot dissolve. The boundary between [conceivable](/voids/conceptual-impossibility/) and inconceivable is itself vague; the boundary between phenomenally graspable and merely formal magnitudes ([the-quantitative-comprehension-void](/voids/the-quantitative-comprehension-void/)) is borderline; the framework boundaries in [conceptual-scheme-void](/voids/conceptual-scheme-void/) are vague at the seams. The catalogue boundary itself ("is this a void or merely difficult terrain?") is borderline. The Map treats this as the framework operating consistently on itself, in line with [apophatic-cartography](/voids/apophatic-cartography/)'s principled incompleteness.
