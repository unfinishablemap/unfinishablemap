The Unfinishable Map's [interface model](/concepts/mind-matter-interface/) treats the brain as a structured surface through which consciousness engages the physical world. This interface is not delivered whole at birth, nor does it remain static through life. It develops through a sequence of qualitative restructurings — each with characteristic gains and losses — from the infant's wide-aperture, low-control configuration through the focused efficiency of adulthood and into the loosening and eventual dissolution of old age.

A clarification of evidential ambition belongs up front. The developmental record below is *accommodated* by the interface model, not predicted uniquely by it. Every datum cited — synaptic pruning, critical-period closure, maturing executive control, neurodegeneration — is equally accommodated by the leading physicalist account of the same machinery, [predictive processing](/topics/predictive-processing-and-dualism/) and active inference, which read the trajectory as the brain tuning a hierarchical generative model. The article does not claim the data favour the interface reading over that rival. Its claim is narrower: the model offers a coherent way to *organise* the developmental facts, and the felt continuity of the experiencing subject across radically different configurations is what the rival, on the Map's view, leaves unexplained. Where the prose says the trajectory "reveals" or "demands explanation," read it as a claim about organising power, not about the facts discriminating in the model's favour.

## Five Phases of Interface Change

### Infancy and Early Childhood: Wide Aperture, Low Control

The infant brain is hyper-connected — roughly twice the synaptic density of an adult brain — and maximally plastic. On the Map's framework consciousness itself is held constant across the lifespan; what varies is the interface. So the right description is that the infant *interface* offers broad, weakly filtered access: universal phoneme discrimination, cross-race face recognition, possible synesthetic cross-modal integration. Control, by contrast, is minimal — infant attention is salience-driven and reflexive. Crucially, broader discrimination at the interface is not a richer phenomenal field; wider input sensitivity is not the same as more conscious experience.

The Map's framework interprets this as an interface offering many potential [quantum selection sites](/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/) but limited capacity to exploit them deliberately — an immature attentional mechanism that cannot yet choose among them systematically. The result is the paradox of the infant interface: broad access, low agency. The [infant consciousness void](/voids/infant-consciousness/) examines the epistemic consequence: this broad-access, low-control configuration is permanently inaccessible to the adult mind that emerged from it.

Piaget's sensorimotor period captures this configuration: cognition is inseparable from action. The infant does not think about objects and then act — thinking *is* acting, reaching, grasping, mouthing. Object permanence emerges gradually as the interface gains capacity to maintain representations independently of current sensory input.

### Late Childhood and Adolescence: Narrowing and Specialisation

Between roughly ages 5 and 25, the interface undergoes its most dramatic restructuring. Critical periods close. Perineuronal nets solidify around neurons, fixing circuit architecture. Synaptic pruning eliminates up to half of prefrontal cortex connections. Myelination of long-range axons accelerates, increasing communication speed at the cost of flexibility.

This is where [biological-cognitive-closure](/voids/biological-cognitive-closure/) becomes strongly stabilised — region-specific, and ordinarily not recoverable rather than absolutely irreversible. Experimental work in animals shows that some critical-period plasticity can be reopened (degrading perineuronal nets reinstates juvenile ocular-dominance plasticity in adult visual cortex), so the closure is better read as a high barrier than a sealed door. Infants discriminate phonemes from all human languages; by 10-12 months, discrimination narrows to native-language sounds. Infants recognise faces across all races; by 9 months, the "other-race effect" sets in. Neonates may experience cross-modal sensory integration that most adults lose entirely. These are the costs of specialisation — the interface trading breadth for depth.

These narrowings should not be tallied as several independent confirmations of the framing. Pruning, critical-period closure, perineuronal-net formation, and the phoneme- and face-discrimination losses are expressions of one underlying mechanism — experience-dependent refinement — not separate lines of evidence converging from different directions ([common-cause-null](/project/common-cause-null/)). The cluster carries the evidential weight of a single pattern, read through whichever framework the reader brings to it.

Simultaneously, the frontoparietal attention networks the Map identifies as the [control surface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/) for consciousness undergo their most significant maturation: executive function, working memory, and deliberate attentional control all improve. The interface is remodelled toward fewer sites of potential interaction but vastly better capacity to select among those that remain.

The most consequential development is metarepresentation — the capacity to represent one's own representations as representations. This underpins [theory-of-mind](/concepts/theory-of-mind/). Some looking-time studies have been interpreted as showing implicit false-belief sensitivity as early as 15 months (Onishi & Baillargeon 2005), but this interpretation is replication-contested and is better described as implicit sensitivity to belief-like behavioural regularities than as full belief attribution. Explicit false-belief passage typically occurs between ages 3-5, and the full suite of metarepresentational abilities continues developing well into middle childhood. Before explicit metarepresentation, children have consciousness — they experience the world, form preferences, exhibit emotional responses — but lack the recursive self-awareness that lets consciousness take its own operations as explicit objects of attention.

On the Map's reading, metarepresentation depends on neural infrastructure — prefrontal maturation and frontoparietal integration — that develops on a biological timetable. Consciousness gains no new fundamental capacities here; the interface gains structural complexity in stages, each enabling more sophisticated recursive processing. The same staged emergence underwrites the developmental arc of shared intentionality — joint attention at nine months, collective intentionality by age three — that the Map reads as [a window on what consciousness adds to social cognition](/topics/consciousness-and-social-understanding/#the-developmental-arc-of-shared-intentionality).

This creates a developmental asymmetry: the interface is most plastic when its control affordances are least developed, and by the time it supports the sophistication needed to direct its own restructuring, the most plastic periods have closed.

The reshaping raises distinctive ethical questions: if the interface is configured during critical windows, enhancement interventions in childhood would interact with natural pruning to permanently alter which territories consciousness can access. The [ethics of cognitive enhancement](/topics/ethics-of-cognitive-enhancement-under-dualism/) explores why configuring another person's interface without their consent carries special moral weight under dualism.

### Adult Maturity: Peak Efficiency, Narrowest Aperture

The mature interface — loosely, the decades from the mid-twenties onward — represents the configuration most discussions of consciousness implicitly assume. These ranges are population-level and domain-specific, not sharp thresholds: prefrontal myelination tapers rather than stopping at a fixed age, and adult brain organisation keeps changing nonlinearly. A 2025 lifespan analysis of structural connectivity (Nature Communications; N≈4,200, ages 0-90) places the major adult reorganisation around age 32, with further turning points near 66 and 83 — so "maturity" is itself a sequence of restructurings rather than a plateau. Across it, the interface is at peak efficiency: bandwidth for deliberate selection is maximised, while the territory available for selection has narrowed to its adult configuration.

This interface is optimised for exploitation over exploration: automatised skills operate below conscious selection, freeing bandwidth for novel problems, and the [phenomenology of effort](/concepts/mental-effort/) is most calibrated during this period. But it carries accumulated [closure](/concepts/mysterianism/) — not only the developmental closures of childhood but expertise effects that compound through adult life: the curse of knowledge, domain-specific rigidity, the progressive inability to access one's own former ignorance.

### Middle Age and Early Aging: Loosening Control

Beginning variably in the fifties and sixties, the interface enters a new phase: processing speed declines, working memory contracts, reduced [dopaminergic signalling](/topics/dopamine-and-the-unified-interface/) diminishes the precision of attentional selection, and neural noise rises as inhibitory circuits weaken.

The dualist framework offers a reading consistent with this: since consciousness is not the neural machinery, neural decline degrades the *interface* without necessarily diminishing the consciousness operating through it. An aging person may know what they want to attend to yet find the mechanism increasingly sluggish — thoughts that almost form, names on the tip of the tongue, intentions that dissipate before execution. (Identity theory reads the same data as decline in consciousness itself; the symptoms do not discriminate.)

Aging does not uniformly degrade the interface. Crystallised intelligence continues to grow, and Cabeza's (2002) HAROLD model documents that older adults recruit bilateral regions for tasks younger adults process unilaterally — mainstream interpretation reads this as compensatory. The Map's framework raises a further possibility: the loosening of tightly specialised circuits may incidentally reopen territory maturation had foreclosed, even as the capacity to exploit it deliberately declines.

### Late Life and Terminal Phase: The Interface Dissolving

In advanced aging and terminal illness, the interface enters its final transformation. [Neurodegenerative conditions](/topics/consciousness-and-neurodegenerative-disease/) progressively dismantle the structures through which consciousness engages the physical world, following characteristic patterns in which the most recently evolved structures (prefrontal cortex, hippocampal formation) typically degrade before more ancient ones (brainstem, limbic system).

For the Map's dualist framework this trajectory poses a question rather than a refutation. Progressive cognitive loss as neural structures degrade is consistent with the interface model — consciousness need not diminish for its interface to narrow and fail — but equally consistent with identity theories in which the cognitive loss *is* the consciousness loss. Neurodegeneration's monotonic trajectory does not discriminate the two.

The phenomenon that would discriminate is terminal lucidity: the reported occurrence of brief, apparently full cognitive clarity in patients with severe neurodegenerative disease shortly before death. If documented at high fidelity, this would be hard to reconcile with strict identification of cognition with intact circuitry. Earlier discussions noted that the base rested on Nahm et al.'s (2012) retrospective case collection rather than prospective data; that is no longer the situation. Prospective clinical work now exists — Tollock and colleagues report a multi-site prospective study of paradoxical lucidity in moderate-to-severe dementia (2025) — and the prospective record has not vindicated the dualist reading. The episodes it documents are brief and trigger-linked, consistent with the same neurochemical mechanisms identity theorists already offer: transient cholinergic or noradrenergic surges from brainstem nuclei that remain relatively preserved in Alzheimer's; temporary reduction of cerebral oedema or inflammation in the days before death; transient hippocampal disinhibition as inhibitory circuits fail before excitatory ones; and observer-effect bias, in which clinicians and family disproportionately remember episodes that fit a narrative of recovery. None of these has been decisively ruled out.

The honest summary: terminal lucidity is congenial to the interface model and would be awkward under identity theories *only if* it turned out to be robust and irreducible to transient neurochemical events. Neither condition has been established, and the prospective evidence so far points the other way. The Map treats it as a phenomenon worth continued study, not as evidence for its reading.

This invites a frank statement of what would settle the matter. The one developmental phenomenon that could discriminate the interface reading from identity theory — high-fidelity lucidity irreducible to neurochemical surges — is exactly the one the Map declines to count as evidence on the current record. With that candidate set aside, the interface reading is at present a metaphysical interpretation rather than an empirically discriminated thesis. It would be put under genuine pressure by robust irreducible terminal lucidity (for it) or by a complete predictive-processing account closing the felt-continuity gap without remainder (against it); neither has arrived. Saying so is the discipline the Map asks of itself, not a concession that the reading is idle.

## Cognitive Stages as Interface Modes

Jean Piaget's stage theory identified something the interface model can sharpen: cognitive development proceeds through qualitatively distinct modes of engagement, not continuous quantitative improvement. The **preoperational interface** (~2 to ~7 years) brings symbolic representation — language, pretend play, letting one thing stand for another — so consciousness can operate on symbols rather than only on sensory-motor engagement; egocentrism reflects an interface that supports representation but not yet the recursive operation of comparing one's own representations against alternatives. The **concrete operational interface** (~7 to ~11 years) brings systematic logical reasoning — conservation, classification, seriation — but only over concrete situations, as the frontoparietal networks enabling deliberate [attentional control](/concepts/attention-as-interface/) mature without yet integrating enough for fully abstract thought.

The **formal operational interface** (~11 years onward) introduces hypothetical-deductive reasoning: holding multiple hypothetical states at once, thinking about thinking. This requires genuinely recursive operations — [metacognition](/concepts/metacognition/) in its mature form. Not everyone reaches full formal operational capacity, and those who do, do so unevenly across domains (Kuhn, 2006) — consistent with formal operations depending on prefrontal networks that develop on different timelines in different individuals.

## Skill Acquisition Reveals the Interface Learning

The same task — learning a musical instrument, mastering a sport — engages different modes of interface operation depending on when it is undertaken. **Early acquisition** exploits interface plasticity without reflection. A five-year-old learning violin does so through intensely embodied repetition during a period of high [plasticity](/concepts/neuroplasticity/), unable to reflect on her own learning; early-acquired skills are deeply embedded, resistant to extinction, and difficult to articulate. **Late acquisition** compensates for reduced plasticity through metacognitive control: an adult can analyse errors, compare technique to models, and devise strategies, with [mental effort](/concepts/mental-effort/) explicitly felt as the interface reshapes solidified circuits — the [interface-friction](/concepts/interface-friction/) of adult learning is metacognitive sophistication gained at the cost of effortless rewiring.

**The developmental sweet spot** falls between roughly ages 7 and 14, when the interface retains significant plasticity while gaining enough metacognitive capacity for structured learning. A ten-year-old learning chess can both absorb patterns through immersive play *and* reason about strategy — the combination behind late childhood's rapid acquisition, before myelination and perineuronal-net formation tilt the balance toward efficiency over flexibility.

## The Comparative Dimension

### Extended Human Development as Interface Investment

Human development is remarkably prolonged. A chimpanzee reaches sexual maturity at 8-10 years, a human at 12-15, and human prefrontal myelination continues into the mid-twenties — far longer than any other primate. This timeline represents an enormous investment in interface complexity: two decades of dependence on caregivers is the cost of constructing the most sophisticated consciousness-brain interface known.

### Why Ape Development Plateaus

Great apes develop through stages paralleling human sensorimotor and early preoperational development, acquiring tool use, social cognition, and basic problem-solving. But [ape cognitive development](/topics/animal-consciousness/) plateaus where human development accelerates, roughly at the transition to concrete operations. Apes show mirror self-recognition, tactical deception, and some implicit sensitivity to others' knowledge states, but not the explicit, recursive metarepresentation underlying human teaching, [cumulative-culture](/concepts/cumulative-culture/), and systematic self-reflection.

The [baseline cognition](/concepts/baseline-cognition/) framework suggests that without mature metarepresentational capacity, cognition plateaus near the ape level regardless of brain size or enrichment; the additional interface complexity human development builds beyond this baseline is what enables distinctively human modes of consciousness.

### Developmental Trajectories Shape Adult Interfaces

Developmental trajectory determines adult interface configuration. An octopus's distributed neural architecture produces a fundamentally different interface from any vertebrate; a corvid's brain, structured unlike a mammal's, arrives at comparable cognitive sophistication through convergent evolution. What varies across species is not brain size but the developmental programme that builds the interface — raising the question of [interface-heterogeneity](/concepts/interface-heterogeneity/), whether the coupling mechanisms themselves differ across lineages rather than merely the content flowing through them.

## The Trajectory Is Not Linear

The standard narrative — growth, maturity, decline — treats the lifespan as an arc peaking in adulthood. The interface model organises it differently:

| Phase | Aperture | Control | Characteristic |
|-------|----------|---------|----------------|
| Infancy | Very wide | Very low | Access without agency |
| Adolescence | Narrowing | Increasing | Specialisation for efficiency |
| Adulthood | Narrow | High | Peak deliberate selection |
| Middle age | Slightly loosening | Declining | Efficiency loss, possible rebroadening |
| Late life | Variable/failing | Low | Interface dissolution |

No phase represents the "correct" state of the interface; each is a configuration with characteristic capacities and costs. The infant has access the adult lacks, the adult has control the infant lacks, and the elderly person may have a form of integrative breadth that neither possessed.

**Plasticity and closure are in tension throughout life.** At every stage the interface faces a version of the explore-exploit tradeoff: [plasticity](/concepts/neuroplasticity/) allows adaptation but costs stability; closure provides efficiency but forecloses territory. [Contemplative traditions](/concepts/neurophenomenology-and-contemplative-neuroscience/) claim sustained practice can partially counteract this narrowing, though far less than the restructurings of early development.

**Interface change outpaces consciousness.** The deepest restructurings occur when consciousness is least equipped to direct them, suggesting the interface's developmental programme operates largely independently of the consciousness that will use it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The developmental trajectory of the interface connects to all five of the Map's [tenets](/tenets/).

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** is illuminated by the stage transitions. Modern physicalism — functionalist, higher-order, or global-workspace — can accommodate them as changes in cognitive architecture, but at a characteristic cost: it must either identify consciousness with the functional organisation at each stage (making infant and adult consciousness different *kinds* of consciousness) or treat it as an emergent property scaling with complexity (raising the question of what fixes the emergence threshold). The Map holds instead that the same irreducible consciousness encounters different interface configurations at each stage: the infant who loses phoneme discrimination does not become a lesser consciousness — the interface through which that discrimination was available has been restructured.

The empirical authorities this article draws on — Hensch on critical periods, Werker on phoneme narrowing, Gopnik on the explore-exploit account of childhood, Cabeza on aging compensation, and the synaptic-pruning literature — are physicalists and naturalists who offer their findings as complete physical explanations of development, not as evidence for a non-physical interface. Their results are cited as the developmental record any account must fit; the dualist reading of that record is the Map's, and they would not endorse it.

The strongest objection to this framing is constructivist, and it deserves a direct response. [Parfit-style reductionism](/concepts/parfit-reductionism/) holds that trans-stage identity consists in nothing over and above causal continuity of psychological states plus the narrative integration that binds them into a life — no irreducible subject *having* the experiences, only a sequence of experientially connected moments. On this view the article's own data — phoneme-discrimination loss, face-recognition narrowing, terminal-stage dissolution — describe a stream of cognitive configurations that happen to be causally and narratively linked, and the "same consciousness across stages" claim looks question-begging.

The Map's reply grants that causal continuity and narrative integration are real and do much of the work we assign to identity, but holds them insufficient for the explanandum at issue: the *first-person* continuity of being the one now missing capacities one's younger self possessed. Connectedness gives a third-person pattern; it does not specify why *this* strand is the one I am indexed to. On the Map's framework that indexing is what the interface does. This is an open commitment, not a proof; the constructivist account remains a live alternative, and the Map's [Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits) tenet accepts that the developmental data do not force the choice.

**[Bidirectional interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** shifts character across development. In early stages the brain's developmental programme dominates — the interface is built on a largely genetic blueprint, and consciousness has minimal capacity to direct the process. In mature adulthood, consciousness-directed attention increasingly shapes the interface through deliberate practice; teaching, a consciousness-enabled capacity, literally reshapes other brains. The trajectory is a story of bidirectional interaction progressively coming into balance.

**[Minimal quantum interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)** is *consistent with* interface change, not predicted by it. If consciousness influences neural outcomes through quantum selection at specific sites, developmental restructuring would alter which sites are available and critical-period closure would reshape the set of interaction points. But this article does not discharge the quantitative burden. The Tegmark (2000) decoherence calculation — coherence in neural environments collapsing in roughly 10⁻¹³ seconds, twelve orders of magnitude faster than cognition's timescales — is the standing objection to any neural quantum mechanism, and the magnitudes and candidate protection mechanisms are treated in [quantum-neural-timing-constraints](/topics/quantum-neural-timing-constraints/) and [decoherence](/concepts/decoherence/), whose conclusions this article assumes rather than restates. Read this section as describing how the developmental data would fit the tenet *if* a viable quantum-selection mechanism exists, not as evidence that one does.

**[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)** connects to developmental irreversibility. On the Map's collapse-realist framework, when a critical period closes the sensitivities it supported are genuinely lost — one definite developmental outcome is selected. An MWI proponent can grant that closure is experientially real within any branch; the Map's actual objection is that the ethical weight of interface-shaping decisions in childhood — a window either receives enrichment or does not, with permanent consequences — requires that the unchosen outcomes genuinely did not happen, not that they occur in parallel branches.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** is evident in the complexity of developmental transitions. Physicalism can explain stage-like transitions through nonlinear dynamics in network maturation; what it handles less naturally is the felt continuity of being the same subject *across* radically different cognitive modes — the trans-stage identity the constructivist reply above contests and the interface model grounds in the same consciousness, differently interfaced at each stage. Which account is simpler is exactly the judgement this tenet warns is unreliable when the underlying mechanism is unknown.
