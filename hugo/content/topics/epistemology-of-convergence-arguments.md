Convergence arguments hold that when multiple independent lines of reasoning arrive at the same conclusion, the conclusion is more likely to be true than any single argument could establish. "Convergence argument" names several distinct things across the literature — Whewell's consilience of inductions, the convergent realism Laudan attacked, Bayesian agreement among partially reliable witnesses — and this article locates the cross-traditional cumulative case among them rather than treating its own usage as the only one. The Unfinishable Map treats convergence as a form of evidence that warrants careful epistemological scrutiny: widely used in philosophical practice yet comparatively undertheorised relative to the cumulative-case weight it bears, and subject to a powerful historical objection (Laudan's pessimistic meta-induction) that any defence must meet on its own terms. Understanding when convergence works, when it misleads, and what conditions must hold matters especially for consciousness, where traditions separated by geography, language, and centuries independently identify something irreducible about subjective experience. The central move throughout is a distinction the literature makes tractable: convergence on a *diagnosis* (an explanatory gap) survives objections that defeat convergence on a *solution* (a posited mechanism).

## Convergence as Philosophical Method

Philosophy lacks the controlled experiments scientists use to test convergence directly. Philosophical arguments cannot be rerun with different initial conditions, their independence is harder to verify, and the possibility that shared cognitive architecture generates systematic philosophical errors has no analogue in laboratory science.

Despite these complications, convergence reasoning pervades philosophy. Moral realism draws on convergent moral intuitions across cultures. Arguments for abstract objects cite convergence between mathematical practice, linguistic analysis, and metaphysical reasoning. Swinburne's cumulative case for theism models convergence using Bayesian probability — each argument contributing independent likelihood.

Cross-cultural philosophical convergence is the most ambitious application. When Sāṃkhya philosophers in classical India and Descartes in seventeenth-century France both conclude that consciousness is irreducible to the processes constituting the rest of reality — using different conceptual vocabularies, through different traditions — the agreement cannot be explained by mutual influence. Whatever explains it must be either a feature of reality or a feature of human cognition.

The structural claim is that multiple routes to the same destination is better evidence than one route, *provided the routes are genuinely independent*. The epistemological challenge lies entirely in that proviso.

## The Convergence Argument in Philosophy of Science

"Convergence argument" is a polysemous term spanning several distinct literatures, and an honest epistemology must locate the cross-traditional cumulative case among them rather than silently restricting the scope. Three bodies of work bear directly on the structural claim above.

**Whewell's consilience of inductions.** The historical articulation of the structural claim is William Whewell's *Philosophy of the Inductive Sciences* (1840): when an induction obtained from one class of facts coincides with an induction obtained from a different class, the coincidence — the *consilience* — confers epistemic credit beyond what either induction earns alone. Whewell's paradigm cases are Newtonian gravitation drawing together planetary motion, tides, and the precession of the equinoxes. The cross-traditional convergence on consciousness's irreducibility is a consilience claim in exactly Whewell's sense: independent classes of consideration (analytic conceivability arguments, contemplative phenomenology, Indian metaphysics) coinciding on one diagnosis. Whewell himself never reduced consilience to a calculus, and the subsequent Hesse–Laudan debate (Hesse 1968, 1971; Laudan 1971) over whether consilience can be formalised as a Bayesian increment or whether it names an irreducibly historical-judgemental virtue remains unsettled. The article's three conditions are best read as a partial regimentation of Whewell's notion for a domain — philosophy — that lacks the instrumental checks Whewell could assume.

**Laudan's confutation and the pessimistic meta-induction.** The strongest objection from this literature is Larry Laudan's "A Confutation of Convergent Realism" (*Philosophy of Science* 48(1): 19–49, 1981). Laudan assembles a catalogue of past theories that were genuinely successful — predictively, explanatorily, and by the convergence of independent investigators — and yet are now judged false: the crystalline spheres, phlogiston, caloric, the luminiferous ether, the effluvial theory of static electricity. The pessimistic meta-induction generalises: if convergence on success has so often accompanied falsity, convergence cannot be the truth-tracking mark realists claim. This is not a challenge to be deflected by adding a fresh example of one's own; it must be met on its own terms, and the meeting is available through the article's central distinction. Laudan's cases are convergences on *theoretical posits* — on the ether as the medium of light, on caloric as the substance of heat. The catalogue retired the posits, not the explananda those posits were introduced to handle: light still propagates, heat still flows. The cross-traditional convergence the Map defends is convergence on an *explanandum* — that structural-physical description leaves the qualitative character of experience unaccounted for — not on any proposed substance or mechanism that would explain it. Laudan's induction bites hardest against convergence on solutions; it is far weaker against convergence on the persistence of a problem. This is why the diagnosis/solution distinction developed throughout this article is load-bearing rather than incidental: it is the resource that makes engagement with Laudan tractable instead of fatal. The concession the distinction forces is real — Laudan's history shows that convergence on *how to explain* a phenomenon is a poor guide to truth, and the Map accordingly disclaims convergence-weight for its own preferred reading (substance dualism with bidirectional interaction) at the solution layer, as the calibration discipline below makes explicit. What survives Laudan is the narrower claim: convergent identification of an explanatory gap is evidence the gap is real, even when no convergent verdict on its closure is available. Where the disagreement with a thoroughgoing anti-realist about explananda persists, it persists at the framework boundary and is noted as such — Laudan's pessimism is not refuted by the Map's resources so much as confined to the territory (theoretical posits) where it has its force.

**Bayesian convergence and partially reliable witnesses.** The formal apparatus the article gestures toward is most fully developed in Bovens and Hartmann's *Bayesian Epistemology* (Oxford University Press, 2003), which models exactly the question convergence reasoning asks: when several *partially reliable, independent* witnesses agree, how much should their agreement raise one's confidence? Their result sharpens the proviso above — agreement confirms strongly only when the witnesses are genuinely independent and individually better than chance; correlated or systematically biased witnesses can agree without confirming. The convergence-of-opinion theorems point the same way from a different angle: Blackwell–Dubins merging of opinions and the Gaifman–Snir convergence-to-the-truth results show that agents who update on a growing common evidence stream will, under stated conditions, see their posteriors merge and their priors wash out. These are reassurances about agreement *given* shared, mind-independent evidence accumulating over time. Philosophical convergence is precisely the case where that condition is doubtful: traditions do not share an external evidence stream the way investigators reading the same instruments do, which is why the bootstrapping problem (developed below) is the binding constraint rather than a technicality. The Bayesian literature does not vindicate the cross-traditional case; it specifies the conditions the case must meet and shows the case has not met them formally. The article's honest position is that the formalisation Swinburne executed for theism, and that Bovens and Hartmann supply the machinery for, has not yet been executed for consciousness.

## Formal Structure

A convergence argument has three components:

**Multiple arguments.** Two or more distinct arguments A₁, A₂, ... Aₙ that each support conclusion C.

**Independence.** The arguments derive from different premises, employ different methods, or exploit different features of the subject matter. The failure of one should not entail the failure of the others.

**Convergence.** Despite their independence, all arguments point toward C rather than distributing across alternatives.

The Bayesian formalization is well-defined in principle but contested at every parameter: priors on dualism, conditional independence given each hypothesis, and likelihood ratios for the conceivability argument under the epistemic/metaphysical possibility distinction are all in active dispute. If each argument Aᵢ raises the posterior probability of C, and the arguments are conditionally independent given C and given ¬C, their likelihood ratios compound — even arguments that individually shift the probability only modestly can generate substantial cumulative support. Swinburne's cumulative theism case shows what an executed formalization looks like; the consciousness case awaits a comparable execution.

The conditional independence assumption does the heavy lifting. Arguments that share hidden premises or exploit the same conceptual framework compound less than naive calculation suggests. Two arguments that both depend on the reliability of phenomenal intuition are not fully independent — debunking phenomenal intuition would undermine both simultaneously.

## Three Conditions for Evidential Convergence

### Genuine Independence

Independence is the hardest condition to satisfy and the easiest to overestimate. Arguments that start from different premises may share deeper assumptions that are not immediately visible.

Consider the arguments for moral realism: convergence of moral disagreement over time, moral explanation, and the companions-in-guilt argument with mathematics. These appear to start from different places, but all presuppose that moral phenomenology — the experience of obligations as binding, of moral properties as discovered rather than invented — is a reliable guide to moral ontology. If moral phenomenology is systematically misleading, all three lose force simultaneously.

Independence must be assessed at the level of *deep assumptions*, not surface premises. Two arguments with completely different premises may still share a single point of vulnerability. The Map's [common-cause-null discipline](/project/common-cause-null/) is the in-house regimentation of a principle that predates it: Reichenbach's common-cause principle (1956), sharpened by Wesley Salmon's work on common-cause explanation, holds that a correlation between two events demands either a direct causal link or a common cause that screens the correlation off. Applied to convergence, the discipline asks whether agreement among arguments reflects independent contact with the subject matter or a shared upstream cause — common cognitive architecture, a shared conceptual framework — that would produce the agreement whether or not the conclusion is true. The construct adds nothing to Reichenbach and Salmon at the level of principle; its contribution is a procedure for *applying* the screening-off test to philosophical convergence, where the candidate common cause (homo sapiens cognition) is always present and the question is whether the observed agreement is more specific than that common cause alone would predict. It distinguishes convergence claims that earn their N-fold weight from claims whose convergence is one upstream architecture read N times.

For cross-cultural convergence, independence is never total. All human philosophical traditions share a common origin in human cognition, social structures, and embodied experience. This creates a bootstrapping problem parallel to the [bootstrap problem in belief revision](/topics/phenomenology-of-intellectual-life/): shared cognitive architecture compromises the independence the framework requires. Scientific convergence is partly disciplined by external instruments whose behaviour is mind-independent in the regimes they probe; philosophical convergence lacks an analogous check, so the bootstrap problem bites harder.

The bootstrapping problem cannot be fully resolved from within the convergence framework. The argument is therefore suggestive rather than demonstrative — it raises the evidential cost of physicalism without settling the question. What partially rescues it is the *specificity* criterion: shared cognitive architecture predicts convergence on *general* tendencies (agent-detection, mind-body separation as a rough heuristic), whereas the consciousness convergence picks out a narrower diagnosis — that structural physical description leaves the qualitative character of experience unexplained — too specific to be the natural output of generic cognitive predispositions. The specificity threshold remains under-articulated as a formal criterion, but the gap between "tradition X finds mind mysterious" and "tradition X identifies the same structural feature as resisting reduction" is the work the criterion is doing.

### Robustness Under Variation

Convergence that depends on a particular school's assumptions is weaker than convergence that holds across rival traditions. Multiple arguments within analytic philosophy converging is less powerful than arguments from analytic philosophy, phenomenology, and Indian metaphysics converging — the latter is harder to explain by appeal to shared methodological commitments.

The [comparative phenomenology of meditative traditions](/topics/comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions/) illustrates the [common-cause-null](/project/common-cause-null/) audit. Buddhist, Hindu, Christian, Sufi, and Daoist practitioners share an upstream mechanism — trained introspective attention in homo sapiens — which is itself enough common machinery to generate convergence on *some* phenomenological pattern. The audit asks whether the agreed pattern is more specific than the shared mechanism alone would predict. Reports converge on structural features — self-dissolution under sustained attention to the witness, contentless awareness, the collapse of subject-object structure under sufficient absorption — narrower than generic introspection would license. The pattern survives as structural-feature agreement, but the count cannot upgrade from one cross-tradition pattern with shared-mechanism dependency to five independent triangulations. A worked sub-audit at the transmission-channel grain — [the Neoplatonist common-cause weight audit](/topics/neoplatonist-common-cause-weight-for-cross-tradition-introspection-architecture-parallels/) — yields an asymmetric per-tradition discount (weak for Stoicism, weak-to-negligible for Advaita, moderate-to-substantial for early Sufism), showing the discipline can produce differentiated verdicts rather than uniform discounts.

The metaphysical interpretations diverge, informatively. Madhyamaka and mature Yogācāra read the *anātman* phenomenology as the *dissolution* of the substantial-witness reading dualism affirms — not its confirmation. Vedānta reads similar phenomenology as disclosure of *Ātman*-as-*Brahman*; Christian and Sufi mystics read it through union with a personal God. The traditions agree on what trained introspection finds; they disagree about what the findings *mean*. The [convergent phenomenological evidence](/concepts/phenomenological-evidence/#convergence) supplies a methodologically distinct stream, but its contribution is the structural-feature agreement, not the metaphysical interpretation.

Robustness also applies across time. Arguments compelling to ancient, medieval, and modern philosophers despite radical changes in background knowledge carry more weight than arguments whose force depends on contemporary assumptions. The hard problem has this diachronic robustness: Aristotle's puzzlement about soul and body, Avicenna's Flying Man, Descartes' conceivability argument, and Chalmers' zombie argument target structurally similar difficulties despite different intellectual contexts. (The pattern is partly an artefact of canon-formation, which selects for figures who articulated such puzzles; the deeper claim would be better grounded by evidence from non-canonical sources.) Sāṃkhya's distinction between *Puruṣa* (pure consciousness) and *Prakṛti* (everything else, including mental processes) parallels the Western distinction between phenomenal consciousness and functional cognition on one influential reading (O'Brien-Kop, 2023); rival scholarship (Larson, Bryant, Whicher) reads *Puruṣa* closer to a Kantian transcendental subject than to phenomenal character, which would weaken the parallel. The convergence claim is robust only at the level of structural features that survive this interpretive variance. The [dedicated analysis of this pattern](/topics/cross-traditional-convergence-on-consciousness-irreducibility/) identifies three structural features — the asymmetry of acquaintance, the regress of objectification, and the gap between structure and quality — that explain why irreducibility functions as an attractor across traditions.

### Specificity of Agreement

Convergence on vague conclusions carries little weight. All philosophical traditions agree the world is puzzling — this tells us nothing. Convergence on specific conclusions carries far more, because the space of possible specific conclusions is large.

The consciousness convergence is specific relative to the space of consciousness-puzzlements a tradition could in principle have articulated. Diverse traditions do not merely register consciousness as a curiosity; they independently identify a particular structural problem: that accounts of the physical world, however complete, leave something unexplained about subjective experience. Avicenna's Flying Man, suspended in a void with no sensory input, discovers he cannot doubt the existence of his consciousness even while doubting everything physical — a structural parallel to Descartes specific enough to resist attribution to coincidence.

The gap between "humans tend to think in dualistic terms" and "independent traditions identify the same structural problem — physical description leaves subjective experience unexplained" is where the evidential work must be done.

## When Convergence Fails

The history of philosophy and science contains instructive cases where convergence pointed toward falsehood — the cases Laudan's catalogue assembles against convergent realism. Each is a convergence on a theoretical posit later retired; testing the consciousness case against them is the discipline the diagnosis/solution distinction is built to pass.

**Vitalism.** Multiple independent lines of reasoning — across cultures — converged on the conclusion that living things require a non-physical vital force. The irreducibility of biological organisation, the purposiveness of organisms, and the gap between living and nonliving matter all seemed to require something beyond mechanism. The convergence was genuine; the conclusion was wrong. Convergence arguments are *defeasible*: vitalism collapsed because biochemistry provided specific mechanisms for phenomena that had seemed irreducible.

What makes the consciousness case structurally different? Simply noting that no reductive explanation has appeared yet would be an argument from current ignorance — vitalists could have said the same in 1800. The answer must come from the *character* of the arguments.

Two features distinguish the consciousness convergence from vitalism's:

First, core arguments target **logical relationships rather than empirical gaps**. The conceivability argument argues that a world physically identical to ours but lacking consciousness is *logically coherent* — no amount of physical information logically entails phenomenal experience. This is a claim about what physical descriptions can *in principle* yield, not about what science has failed to achieve. Vitalism's arguments were empirical gap arguments that mechanism could and did close.

The strongest reply to this contrast comes from the illusionists, and it deserves engagement rather than a parenthetical. Keith Frankish ("Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness," 2016) and Iris Berent ("The 'Hard Problem of Consciousness' Arises from Human Psychology," 2023) argue that the apparent logical coherence of the zombie scenario is itself a cognitive artefact — the output of essentialist and dualist biases that Berent locates deep in human psychology — rather than a genuine modal datum. If they are right, the convergence on irreducibility is convergence on a shared illusion, and the logical/empirical contrast collapses: the "logical" character of the conceivability argument would be a psychological appearance, not a structural fact about what physical descriptions yield. This is a serious challenge, and it cuts in two directions that must be kept separate. Against the *realist* reading of the convergence it has real force, and the article does not claim otherwise. But it does not by itself favour physicalism, and here illusionism helps itself to a step it has not earned by its own standards: showing that a *psychological mechanism* disposes humans to find the gap compelling does not show the gap is *unreal*, any more than a psychological account of why mathematicians find certain proofs compelling shows the theorems false. Berent's biases-explanation is an explanation of the *belief* in irreducibility; converting it into a refutation of irreducibility requires the further premise that beliefs with psychological etiologies are thereby unreliable — a premise that, applied evenhandedly, would debunk the physicalist's own intuitions about the sufficiency of mechanism. The debunking move owes an account of why it screens off the realist hypothesis specifically rather than tracking any belief with a discoverable cognitive cause; that account is what is missing. Where the disagreement remains after this is pressed, it is the same boundary the article marks against eliminativism below: illusionism declines to grant phenomenal experience as a datum, and no internal-to-the-convergence resource closes that gap.

Second, the [knowledge argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/) is explicitly constructed to survive complete scientific knowledge. Mary, the colour scientist who knows every physical fact about colour vision, learns something new upon seeing red. The argument *grants* that physics is complete and still concludes physical knowledge is not exhaustive. There is no consciousness analogue of Wöhler's urea synthesis because the argument stipulates complete physical information and argues something is still missing.

**Geocentrism.** The convergence of common sense, astronomical observation, and Aristotelian physics on a stationary earth shows how deep theoretical frameworks generate convergent errors. The arguments all presupposed Aristotelian physics in which natural motion tends toward a center; once that framework was replaced, the apparent convergence dissolved. Surface-level diversity does not guarantee deep independence.

Each of these cases shares the structure Laudan's confutation exploits: convergence on a *posit* (vital force, geocentric cosmology, and in Laudan's own list caloric and the ether) that a later framework retired. None is a case of convergence on a *persisting explanandum* failing — life, celestial motion, and heat remained phenomena to be explained after the posits fell. This is the structural test the consciousness convergence must pass, and the reason the argument elsewhere insists its weight attaches to the diagnosis rather than to any proposed solution. A related worry sharpens the test further. Kyle Stanford's problem of unconceived alternatives (*Exceeding Our Grasp*, 2006) holds that the history of inquiry is a graveyard of theories whose proponents could not conceive the alternative that later displaced them — so the present unavailability of a rival explanation of cross-cultural patterns is weak evidence that no such rival exists. The article's claim that no better explanation of the cross-traditional pattern than a real explanatory gap is currently available must therefore be stated as exactly that — a claim about the current state of conceived options, not a demonstration of exhaustiveness. The honest weight of the convergence is correspondingly bounded: it raises the cost of physicalism without foreclosing an unconceived deflationary account of why the diagnosis recurs.

### The Methodology-Symmetry Test

Defeasibility of the *conclusion* is distinct from falsifiability of the *methodology*. Would the three-condition framework underwrite a counterfactual cross-cultural convergence on the *reducibility* of consciousness, with specific structural agreement and diachronic robustness? It would: the framework tests for evidential convergence of any kind and would deliver the opposite verdict on opposite data. This symmetry is a check on the framework's integrity.

## The Anti-Realist Challenge

The most powerful objection holds that shared human cognitive architecture explains the patterns better than shared access to truth.

### Cognitive Universals

Humans share neural architecture, embodied experience, Theory of Mind capacities, and agent-detection tendencies. These universals could generate systematic philosophical tendencies. If humans naturally separate mental from physical — as Paul Bloom's "natural-born dualist" hypothesis suggests — cross-cultural convergence reflects cognitive predisposition rather than philosophical discovery.

The empirical evidence complicates this, though the empirical question is itself live. H. Clark Barrett and colleagues' (2021) cross-cultural study of afterlife beliefs across eight populations found that the overall pattern of responses does not support intuitive dualism — most participants across all cultures gave non-dualist responses, and the authors conclude that dualist thinking is "a possible mode of thought enabled by evolved human psychology" but "does not constitute a default mode of thought." Barlev and Shtulman (2021) argue more broadly that dualist conceptions are learned rather than innate; Bering's afterlife-reasoning work and developmental studies on agent-detection point the other way, and the field has not settled. The Barrett et al. result, taken at face value, *strengthens* rather than weakens the convergence-realist position: it removes the natural-born-dualist debunking explanation, since the cross-cultural pattern at the philosophical level cannot be downstream of an intuitive dualism that the developmental data does not establish as universal. If intuitive dualism is neither universal nor innate, it cannot straightforwardly explain cross-cultural philosophical convergence on consciousness's irreducibility; if it turns out to be more innate than Barlev and Shtulman and Barrett et al. conclude, the anti-realist explanation gains traction. The convergence argument's force at this point depends on an empirical question those authors opened rather than closed.

### Cognitive Closure

[mysterianism](/concepts/mysterianism/) — McGinn's view that humans may be cognitively closed to the consciousness-brain relationship — offers a subtler anti-realist account. Perhaps traditions converge on irreducibility not because consciousness *is* irreducible but because human minds cannot grasp the reductive explanation.

Two observations limit this objection — though the second is more limited than the first. First, cognitive closure does not favour physicalism — if the solution is beyond us, we cannot know whether it is reductive or non-reductive. Mysterianism entails agnosticism, not materialism. Second, the genericity of the closure matters: a *generic* cognitive limitation would produce variable errors, not convergence on a structurally precise diagnosis. McGinn's specific hypothesis — closure about the consciousness-physical relationship in particular — would, however, predict convergence on exactly the pattern observed ("there is a gap here we cannot close"). Against generic closure, the convergence is at least as well-explained by a real feature of consciousness as by a shared blind spot; against McGinn's *specific* closure, the convergence is what the closure hypothesis itself predicts. The dialectical work at this point falls to whether one finds the closure hypothesis or the irreducibility hypothesis the better explanation of the same pattern — a comparison the convergence argument alone cannot settle.

## What Divergence Reveals

If convergence is significant, divergence should be equally informative.

Chinese philosophical traditions — particularly Daoism and Neo-Confucianism — largely do not generate the hard problem. Mind and world, subject and object, are understood as aspects of a unified process (*dao*, *li/qi*) rather than as ontologically distinct domains. This is not convergence on physicalism; Chinese thought does not reduce mind to matter. It operates with a different conceptual framework that dissolves the distinction generating the hard problem.

This divergence poses a genuine challenge to the realist interpretation. If the hard problem depends on isolating subjective experience as a phenomenon distinct from its physical correlates, then convergence among traditions that make this move may track the move itself rather than a truth about consciousness. Chalmers takes up precisely this question in "Is the Hard Problem of Consciousness Universal?" (2020), arguing that the problem is at least latent across traditions even where it is not explicitly thematised; the Chinese case is the sharpest test of how far that latency claim can be pressed before it becomes unfalsifiable.

The realist can respond that the subject-object distinction is itself a philosophical discovery — but the response risks circularity. The features cited ([qualia](/concepts/qualia/), the unity of experience, perspectival character) are partly *outputs* of the distinction's own vocabulary rather than independent test-data. The honest position is that the distinction is *productive*: it generates vocabulary picking out features Western and Indian traditions take to be real. But productivity is not by itself veridicality. The Chinese alternative cannot be decisively ruled out, because the features cited as resisting functional analysis are themselves articulated within the distinction's vocabulary.

Divergence also appears in proposed solutions: Indian traditions lean toward idealism or radical dualism, Islamic philosophy develops emanationist frameworks, Western analytic philosophy explores property dualism and neutral monism. The convergence is on the *problem* — consciousness resists physical explanation — not on the *solution*. This pattern of convergent diagnosis with divergent treatment mirrors scientific contexts where investigators agree on a phenomenon but disagree about its explanation — a pattern consistent with, though not establishing, a genuine phenomenon underlying the treatments. The same shape appears in retrospectively-dissolved cases too (nineteenth-century convergence on a luminiferous medium, with mechanics that disagreed about its properties), so the inference from convergent-diagnosis to real-phenomenon is suggestive rather than decisive.

## Convergence Applied to Dualism

The Map's [convergence argument for dualism](/topics/the-convergence-argument-for-dualism/) applies this framework to a specific case. The [knowledge-argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/), the conceivability argument via [philosophical-zombies](/concepts/philosophical-zombies/), the [explanatory-gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/), arguments from [qualia](/concepts/qualia/), and arguments from the unity of consciousness all point toward the irreducibility of phenomenal experience.

**Independence.** The arguments cluster into partially independent groups. The knowledge argument and qualia arguments both depend on phenomenal intuition. The conceivability argument and Kripke's modal argument both exploit logical possibility. The unity and intentionality arguments address structural features largely independent of phenomenal intuition. Across clusters, genuine independence holds; within clusters, it is partial.

**Robustness.** The convergence holds across analytic philosophy, continental phenomenology, and several non-Western traditions, though limited by the Chinese counterexample.

**Specificity.** The convergence is not merely on "consciousness is puzzling" but on the specific diagnosis that physical descriptions, however complete, leave the qualitative character of experience unexplained.

### Scope of the Argument

A deeper objection holds that *all* these arguments share a common root: the intuition that there is "something it is like" to have an experience. If this intuition is a cognitive artefact, every argument collapses simultaneously. The convergence argument's dialectical reach is conditional on the experience-as-datum starting point. For audiences accepting phenomenal experience as a datum, the supposed common root is the subject matter itself — pain's felt character is the phenomenon under investigation, not a belief that could turn out to be mistaken. Eliminativists and illusionists — Dennett, Frankish (2016), Berent (2023) — contest the experience-as-datum framing, treating reports of experience as the data and declining to grant any additional ontological item. This is bedrock disagreement the convergence argument cannot resolve. The argument advances against rival non-eliminativist positions and stands honestly at the boundary against eliminativism rather than pretending the boundary is internal. The engagement with Berent's debunking line above shows what the convergence argument *can* do at this boundary — contest the inference from "the belief has a psychological cause" to "the belief is false" — and what it cannot: compel an illusionist who refuses the datum from the start. The cumulative case for dualism ([the-convergence-argument-for-dualism](/topics/the-convergence-argument-for-dualism/), the [Dualism tenet](/tenets/#dualism)) inherits this scope condition.

## Methodology and Framework Commitment Are Different Routes

A subtle calibration issue runs across applications of the convergence framework. The framework supplies a methodology — three conditions on independence, robustness, and specificity — and the methodology operates on a body of arguments or data. The third element in any actual deployment is the framework that *reads* the methodology's output: which conclusion the catalogue takes the convergence to support depends on what the framework selects from the irreducibility-respecting alternatives (or the mental-causation-affirming alternatives, or the consciousness-as-real alternatives). The methodology and the data are independent of each other; the framework that reads them is not an independent third route.

The [coherence-vs-evidential-status](/project/evidential-status-discipline/#coherence-vs-evidential-status) discipline guards the calibration. Convergence-methodology success at *layer one* (the body of arguments earns "consciousness is irreducible" across rival irreducibility-respecting framings) does not by itself license *layer two* (the framework's specific reading of irreducibility — substance dualism, panpsychism, neutral monism, idealism, Madhyamaka emptiness). Layer-two selection runs on the framework's own further arguments — bidirectional interaction for the Map's reading, combination strategies for panpsychism, regularity-of-nature accounts for idealism, therapeutic-deconstruction commitments for Madhyamaka. Those further arguments do honest work but they do not inherit the convergence's evidential weight; they earn what their separate cases supply.

The pattern operates on every convergence-argument application in the Map. [The convergence argument for dualism](/topics/the-convergence-argument-for-dualism/) runs the philosophical-arguments cluster; [cross-traditional convergence](/topics/cross-traditional-convergence-on-consciousness-irreducibility/) runs the contemplative-and-philosophical traditions cluster; [cross-cultural convergence on mental causation](/concepts/cross-cultural-convergence-on-mental-causation/) runs the mental-causation-affirming traditions cluster. Each installs the same discipline: convergence earns the cross-rival conclusion (irreducibility, mental causation's reality, consciousness as a real explanandum); the framework's specific reading of that conclusion runs on separately-supported arguments. At the species-attribution layer, [the animal-consciousness COGITATE treatment](/topics/animal-consciousness/#why-the-inference-is-stronger-for-animals-than-for-ai) is the catalogue's worked exhibit of the same calibration — markers and convergent evolution as two independent routes plus the framework's no-substrate-barrier as the selection commitment, never three independent confirmations.

The methodology's own integrity depends on this discipline. A convergence framework that silently counted its consumers' framework commitments as additional independent routes would be unfalsifiable in exactly the failure mode the methodology-symmetry test guards against: any framework that selects its preferred reading from the convergence's output would generate confirmation for itself by reading itself into the count. The honest framework reports its layer-one earnings (cross-rival irreducibility) and lets its layer-two selection earn what its independent arguments supply.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map relies on convergence at multiple levels. The [Dualism tenet](/tenets/#dualism) is supported not by any single argument but by the convergence of multiple independent routes to irreducibility — extended beyond a single tradition by the cross-cultural dimension, which strengthens the independence criterion that gives convergence its force.

The [Occam's Razor Has Limits tenet](/tenets/#occams-limits) connects directly: parsimony is a tiebreaker between theories of equal explanatory power, not a trump card overriding convergent evidence. Dismissing cross-cultural convergence as mere cognitive bias assumes we understand human cognition well enough to explain the pattern away, but the empirical evidence on [intuitive-dualism](/concepts/intuitive-dualism/) suggests it is not reducible to innate cognitive tendencies.

The Map treats convergence as genuine evidence while acknowledging its limits. Convergence does not prove dualism — the vitalism precedent shows even genuine convergence can be overturned. But the consciousness convergence is structurally unlike vitalism's: its core arguments target logical and conceptual relationships independent of current scientific ignorance. The conceivability argument would not be dissolved by a more detailed neural map, because it already grants complete physical information. The hard problem's framework-dependence — revealed by the Chinese divergence — means the convergence argument requires supplementation by direct arguments for irreducibility and cannot stand alone. Physicalist accounts owe an explanation of this pattern beyond appeal to shared cognitive architecture: the pattern's specificity, audited against the common-cause null, is what creates the explanatory burden.
