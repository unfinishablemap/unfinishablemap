Every conscious being thinks from within a conceptual framework — a system of categories, assumptions, and inferential habits that structures experience before reflection begins. The Unfinishable Map argues that this framework is not held like luggage but *inhabited* like a dwelling. The committed theorist does not carry the theory around; they look through it like a lens. And the most striking phenomenological fact about this inhabitation is that the lens presents itself not as a lens but as clear sight. This phenomenal transparency — the felt absence of the framework even as it shapes every thought — combined with the gravitational pull that commitment exerts on inquiry, constitutes irreducible evidence that consciousness does epistemic work no computational model captures.

This article unifies two complementary analyses: the structural invisibility of frameworks (how the lens disappears) and the active phenomenology of theoretical commitment (what it is like to see through a particular lens). Together they reveal framework inhabitation as a domain where dualism's case is especially strong — the lived texture of holding a theory involves qualitative experience that belief-updating models systematically miss.

## The Transparency of the Lens

The central phenomenological fact about framework dependence is that the framework is experientially invisible under normal conditions. A dualist examining their own experience does not notice a "dualist filter" any more than a fluent speaker notices grammar while speaking. The framework operates at the level Heidegger called *Zuhandenheit* — readiness-to-hand — where the tool disappears into its function.

This transparency is not mere inattention. It is structurally enforced. The framework determines which features of experience count as salient, which questions seem meaningful, and which observations register as evidence. A materialist encountering the explanatory gap between neural activity and subjective experience may genuinely feel that the gap is an artefact of confused thinking — not because they have examined and dismissed the gap, but because their framework renders it unproblematic before conscious evaluation begins.

Husserl's concept of the *natural attitude* captures part of this: the unreflective stance in which the world presents itself as simply there, without the mediating apparatus of perception and cognition becoming visible. But framework dependence goes deeper than the natural attitude. Husserl believed his *epoché* — the deliberate suspension of natural presuppositions — could achieve a pre-theoretical vantage point. Whether it can is contested. What the phenomenology of framework inhabitation suggests is that even the epoché operates within a framework (the phenomenological one), and its apparent pre-theoretical transparency may be another instance of the same illusion.

Davidson (1974) famously argued that the very idea of incommensurable conceptual schemes is incoherent: if we can recognise something as a language or framework at all, we have already begun to translate it, and radical untranslatability collapses. This is a serious challenge, but framework dependence as described here does not require radical incommensurability. The claim is that translation between frameworks is *lossy* — that something is systematically stripped away in the process — not that translation is impossible. Davidson's argument rules out the strong thesis that two frameworks could be entirely mutually unintelligible. It does not rule out the weaker thesis that cross-framework understanding routinely involves distortion that the understander cannot detect from within their own framework.

## The Perceptual Restructuring of Commitment

A theoretical commitment reshapes what shows up as salient. The committed Darwinist sees adaptation where others see mere variation. The committed dualist perceives the explanatory gap as a feature of reality, not a temporary failure of science. Commitment reconfigures the phenomenal foreground and background of intellectual experience.

Consider the felt difference between encountering evidence that fits your framework and evidence that resists it. Confirming evidence arrives with a distinctive phenomenal smoothness — it slots into place, extends existing patterns, reinforces the sense that the world is intelligible. Anomalous evidence, by contrast, produces a characteristic friction. It does not merely fail to fit; it actively disrupts the perceptual fluency that commitment provides.

This restructuring goes deeper than bias. Cognitive science documents confirmation bias as a tendency to seek and favour confirming evidence. But the phenomenological point is different: commitment changes what evidence *looks like* before any evaluative process begins. The committed physicist does not first perceive neutral data and then interpret it through theory. The data arrives already structured by the theoretical lens. As Kuhn recognised, scientists working in different paradigms inhabit different perceptual worlds, not merely different interpretive frameworks applied to the same world.

Predictive processing accounts of cognition can model this restructuring computationally — prior beliefs shape top-down predictions that filter incoming data. But the computational model describes the *functional architecture* of commitment without addressing the *phenomenal character*. Two systems with identical Bayesian priors could restructure evidence identically yet differ in whether there is something it is like to hold those priors. The perceptual world of the committed theorist is not just informationally filtered; it is experientially inhabited.

## The Gravitational Structure of Commitment

Theoretical commitment creates something like an experiential gravity well. Ideas, observations, and arguments that fall within the theory's domain are drawn toward coherent interpretation. Those outside its scope drift to the periphery of attention. This gravitational metaphor captures something phenomenologically precise: the *effort* required to entertain alternatives increases with the depth of commitment.

This is not stubbornness or closed-mindedness, though it can degenerate into either. It is an epistemic structure that makes sustained inquiry possible. Without the gravitational pull of commitment, every observation would demand fresh interpretation from scratch. Theoretical commitment provides the stable interpretive ground on which detailed investigation can proceed.

The gravitational structure manifests in several distinctive experiences:

**Inferential momentum.** Within a committed framework, inferences feel propelled. Each conclusion generates pressure toward the next. The dualist who accepts the irreducibility of consciousness feels logical momentum carrying the argument toward interactionism — the inference has a directional quality that resists deflection.

**Coherence satisfaction.** When disparate observations converge under a single theoretical framework, the experience is not merely cognitive but affective. There is a felt rightness, a warmth of integration, that rewards theoretical unification. This [epistemic emotion](/concepts/epistemic-emotions/) functions as a phenomenal signal of explanatory progress.

**Boundary alertness.** The committed theorist develops heightened sensitivity to where their framework's explanatory power ends. The edge of the theory's reach becomes phenomenally marked — problems at the boundary feel qualitatively different from problems well within the framework's scope.

## Three Signatures of Framework Dependence

Although frameworks are normally transparent, three recurring experiential patterns mark their presence:

### False Fluency Across Frameworks

When a dualist reads a materialist argument (or vice versa), the experience is typically one of comprehension — the argument seems intelligible, the terms familiar, the logic followable. Yet this felt comprehension may be illusory. The reader has translated the argument into their own framework's terms, which may strip away precisely what makes the argument compelling within its native framework.

This false fluency feels indistinguishable from genuine understanding. There is no phenomenal marker that distinguishes "I understand this argument in its own terms" from "I have successfully translated this argument into terms my framework can process." The absence of such a marker is itself significant — consciousness apparently lacks the resources to detect when its own comprehension is framework-mediated rather than framework-neutral.

### Irritation at the Incommensurable

Encountering a genuinely alien framework — one that cannot be smoothly translated — often produces not wonder or curiosity but a characteristic irritation. The other person seems to be making a simple error, missing an obvious point, or being wilfully obtuse. This irritation is phenomenologically distinct from the experience of encountering a difficult argument within one's own framework, where the typical responses are effort, engagement, or intellectual humility.

The irritation may function as a framework-preservation mechanism. If encountering incommensurable frameworks routinely produced wonder and openness, framework stability would be threatened. Instead, the phenomenology pushes toward dismissal: the alien framework is experienced as confused rather than as differently structured. The mind-body problem is saturated with this pattern. Dualists and materialists regularly report finding the opposing position not just wrong but baffling — a phenomenal signature that the disagreement may involve genuine incommensurability rather than mere error.

### Moments of Framework Slippage

Rarely, the framework briefly becomes visible as a framework. These moments have a distinctive phenomenology: a sudden, vertiginous sense that what one took for unmediated reality is in fact a constructed perspective. They typically occur during sustained engagement with a radically different tradition — reading Buddhist philosophy of no-self, or absorbing the implications of idealism after years of physicalist training.

The experience is transient. Within seconds to minutes, the framework reasserts its transparency. The moment of slippage is remembered but cannot be sustained — at least not through ordinary intellectual effort. Contemplative traditions, particularly Buddhist meditation practices, claim that sustained training can extend and deepen this kind of awareness. Whether such practices genuinely transcend framework dependence or merely install a new framework (the contemplative one) is itself a question that framework dependence makes difficult to answer.

The transience of ordinary slippage is nonetheless philosophically significant: it suggests that framework transparency is actively maintained, not merely default. Consciousness does not simply fail to notice the framework; something in the cognitive architecture actively restores the sense that one's categories are transparent windows rather than coloured glass.

## The Temporal Phenomenology of Commitment

Theoretical commitment has a distinctive temporal texture. Fresh commitment carries an excitement of new possibility — the world is suddenly more intelligible, previously disconnected observations snap into focus. Mature commitment settles into something quieter but more pervasive, operating beneath explicit attention while structuring every encounter with evidence.

This temporal evolution explains how commitments become invisible. A deeply naturalised commitment no longer feels like a commitment at all — it feels like seeing things as they are. The materialist who has held their position for decades experiences it as realism, not as theoretical choice. The phenomenal signature of commitment fades as the commitment deepens, which is precisely why [conceptual change](/topics/phenomenology-of-conceptual-change/) can be so disorienting — it reveals as contingent what had felt like necessity.

Polanyi's concept of *personal knowledge* captures this: genuine knowing involves the knower's whole person, including commitments that cannot be fully articulated or detached from the act of understanding. The inhabited quality of mature commitment explains why theoretical debates so often feel like failures of mutual comprehension rather than disagreements about specific claims. Two theorists committed to rival frameworks may share all the same data and still talk past each other, because the data appears differently from within each inhabited perspective — a dynamic explored in the [phenomenology-of-philosophical-disagreement](/topics/phenomenology-of-philosophical-disagreement/).

## The First-Person Asymmetry

There is a fundamental asymmetry in the phenomenology of framework inhabitation: one's own commitments feel like clear sight while others' commitments look like bias. This asymmetry is not a cognitive defect to be corrected but a structural feature of what commitment is. To be committed to a theory is precisely to take it as revealing rather than distorting. If the commitment felt like distortion, it would not be a commitment.

This first-person asymmetry has profound implications for the philosophy of mind. A materialist committed to physicalism does not experience their position as a theoretical choice made under uncertainty. They experience the explanatory gap as a puzzle to be solved, not a sign that their framework is inadequate. A dualist committed to irreducibility does not experience their position as faith-based resistance to science. They experience the gap as pointing toward a genuine feature of reality.

The asymmetry is the phenomenological face of [Duhem-Quine underdetermination](/topics/duhem-quine-underdetermination-consciousness/): when evidence cannot force theory choice, each committed theorist experiences their framework as transparent rather than as a lens. This authority is not justified by the commitment itself (that would be circular) but is a constitutive part of what commitment phenomenally involves. Recognising this authority as phenomenal rather than epistemic — as a feature of experience rather than a guarantee of truth — is essential for honest inquiry. Ratcliffe's work on *feelings of being* illuminates this: certain experiential orientations are not attitudes toward propositions but pre-reflective ways of finding oneself in the world. Theoretical commitment operates at this level — it shapes the space of what feels intelligible before deliberate evaluation begins.

## Framework Dependence and Introspection

The phenomenology of framework inhabitation creates a specific problem for the study of consciousness. If introspection is theory-laden — if what one notices in experience depends on the conceptual apparatus one brings to the noticing — then first-person reports about consciousness are framework-dependent in the same way that scientific observations are paradigm-dependent.

This does not render introspection worthless. It means introspective reports carry framework-specific information. A materialist introspecting on their visual experience may notice functional properties — the role of colour in object recognition, the spatial structure of the visual field. A dualist introspecting on the same experience may notice qualitative properties — the raw redness of red, the felt character of spatial presence. Both report accurately on what they notice. What they notice is shaped by what their framework makes salient.

This has implications for the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/). The explanatory gap may partly be a gap between frameworks — between a third-person framework that makes functional properties salient and a first-person framework that makes qualitative properties salient. Both give genuine access to aspects of consciousness; neither gives access to consciousness as a whole. The persistent impasse in philosophy of mind may reflect not a failure of intelligence or argument but a structural feature of framework-dependent introspection.

This analysis is itself framework-dependent — the phenomenological vocabulary used here shapes what it can reveal. But acknowledging this reflexivity does not defuse the point; it deepens it. The inescapability of framework dependence is precisely what makes it philosophically significant rather than merely an obstacle to be overcome.

## The Phenomenology of the Consciousness Debate

The mind-body problem has a distinctive phenomenology for its participants that framework inhabitation helps explain.

Materialists often report experiencing the hard problem as a pseudo-problem — an artefact of confused intuitions that dissolves under analysis. This reflects how the materialist framework shapes the experience of encountering the problem. Within a framework where explanation means identifying physical mechanisms, the demand for something beyond mechanism feels like a category error. The felt obviousness of the dismissal is itself a phenomenological datum.

Dualists report an equal and opposite certainty: that consciousness is self-evidently non-physical. The gap between neural firing and subjective experience feels unbridgeable not as a conclusion reached through argument but as a direct experiential observation. The framework makes the gap vivid and ineliminable.

What is remarkable is that both sides report their position as experientially obvious and the opposing view as requiring elaborate argumentative support. Each framework generates its own appearance of self-evidence. This symmetry — where each position feels like common sense to its adherents and like ideology to its opponents — is exactly what framework inhabitation predicts.

## What Would Challenge This View?

The claim that framework inhabitation has irreducible phenomenal character would face difficulty if:

**Framework effects proved fully computational.** If predictive processing models could account for every aspect of how commitment shapes experience — including the qualitative texture of false fluency, the specific phenomenology of irritation at incommensurability, and the vertiginous quality of framework slippage — without residual phenomenal features, the irreducibility claim would weaken.

**Trained introspectors reported dissolution.** If experienced phenomenologists consistently found that framework inhabitation dissolved under close inspection into combinations of habit, expectation, and social reinforcement, with no proprietary phenomenal character, the irreducibility claim would weaken.

**Cross-cultural universality failed.** If framework dependence had no consistent phenomenal character across cultures — if the transparency of the lens, the gravitational pull, and the signatures of dependence varied radically — the structural claims would require revision.

**AI systems displayed equivalent phenomenology.** If artificial systems demonstrated not just functional framework effects but the phenomenal signatures — genuinely experiencing the vertiginous moment when a framework becomes visible as a framework — the claim that organic consciousness is special would need revision.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map's own theoretical commitments — its five [tenets](/tenets/) — are not exempt from the phenomenological dynamics described here. The Map's commitment to dualism restructures how evidence appears: the explanatory gap looks like a fundamental feature of reality, not a temporary scientific embarrassment. This self-awareness matters.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: If framework inhabitation involves irreducibly phenomenal restructuring of the intellectual landscape — if seeing-through-a-theory is a mode of conscious experience that cannot be reduced to computational belief-updating — then the very activity of theorising depends on consciousness in a way that materialist accounts of cognition struggle to explain. The felt difference between committed and uncommitted engagement with evidence, the gravitational pull of coherence, the inhabited quality of theoretical perspective — these are the reasoning process itself, viewed from the inside. The Map's case for dualism is informed by this analysis rather than undermined by it: acknowledging that the dualist framework shapes what one notices does not make dualism self-refuting, since the same applies equally to materialism.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: If consciousness causally influences physical outcomes, and if framework inhabitation shapes conscious experience, then frameworks are not merely epistemic but causal. The felt texture of commitment does real work — guiding attention, shaping salience, generating the coherence signals that drive theoretical progress. Consciousness does not merely observe the reasoning process; it constitutes it. The framework through which consciousness operates shapes its causal influence on the brain, partly explaining why different philosophical traditions generate different phenomenological reports.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: If framework dependence shapes what counts as "simple," then the materialist's claim to greater parsimony may be an artefact of the materialist framework rather than a framework-neutral observation. Within a framework that takes consciousness as fundamental, positing irreducible experience is not an additional ontological commitment but a recognition of what is directly given. Parsimony looks different from within different committed frameworks.

The Map's analysis also resonates with [mysterianism](/concepts/mysterianism/). If frameworks constrain not just what we think but what we can experience, then some aspects of consciousness may be structurally inaccessible — not because the phenomena are inherently mysterious, but because every framework through which we approach them filters out precisely what would need to be noticed. McGinn's "cognitive closure" acquires a phenomenological dimension: it is not just that we lack the concepts, but that the absence of those concepts is invisible from the inside. The [framework-void](/voids/framework-void/) explores this structural impossibility from the outside; this article has explored what it feels like from within.
