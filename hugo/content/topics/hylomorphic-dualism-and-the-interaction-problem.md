Hylomorphism is the most serious live rival to the way The Unfinishable Map handles the mind-body problem, and it is a rival precisely because it refuses to play the Map's game. The Map holds that mind and body are distinct and causally connected (see [the Map's alternative](#the-maps-alternative) below), which forces the question of *where and how* they connect—a question the Map answers by locating an interface at quantum indeterminacy. Hylomorphism denies that any such interface is needed. On the neo-Aristotelian picture, mind and body are not two things that must reach across a gap; they are the form and the matter of one substance, co-principles of a single living thing. There is no gap to bridge because there were never two independent relata. The interaction problem is not solved but *dissolved*.

Taking this rival seriously matters more than dismissing it. If dissolution works, the Map's entire mechanistic project—its search for a locatable, minimal, quantum-level interface—rests on a mistaken premise. This article lays out the hylomorphic case fairly, distinguishes the very different theories that travel under the one name, and then explains why the Map still judges that a locatable interface is required, while conceding that this judgment is a commitment rather than a proof.

## Form, Matter, and Why the Gap Disappears

Standard Aristotelian background is available to any reader; what matters here is the single distinction that bears on interaction. A natural substance, on the hylomorphic view, is a compound of *matter* (*hylē*) and *substantial form* (*morphē*). Form is not a second ingredient sitting alongside the matter—it is the organizing principle that makes the matter *this kind of thing* with *these characteristic powers*. The soul, for a living body, is its substantial form: the actuality of a body that has life potentially.

The payoff for the mind-body problem is structural. Cartesian [substance dualism](/concepts/substance-property-dualism/) posits two complete substances—a thinking thing and an extended thing—and then cannot say how an immaterial substance pushes a material one. Hylomorphism blocks the question at its source. Edward Feser draws the contrast sharply: on hylemorphic dualism the immaterial (form) and material (matter) are not two distinct substances linked only by efficient causality but co-principles of one substance—the composite is, in his words, "a single, complete substance," and there is no gap to bridge because mind and body were never two independent things to begin with. The demand for a mechanism by which the immaterial *pushes* the material, hylomorphists argue, smuggles in a mechanistic premise Aristotle never granted. Aristotelian metaphysics recognizes formal and final causation alongside efficient causation; the modern interaction problem assumes that efficient, push-pull causation is the only kind there is, and that assumption is exactly what the hylomorphist declines to make.

This is a genuinely different move from anything in the Map's toolkit. The Map, like the Cartesian, accepts that if mind influences matter there must be a *how*. Hylomorphism's reply is that the "how" question is malformed.

## Hylomorphism Is Not One Theory

The word covers a spectrum wide enough that "hylomorphism solves the mind-body problem" can mean incompatible things depending on who says it. Three positions mark the range, and only some of them are dualist at all.

### Structure hylomorphism (near-physicalist)

William Jaworski's *Structure and the Metaphysics of Mind* (2016) treats "structure" (hylomorphic form) as "a basic ontological and explanatory principle": living things are materials organized so as to confer developmental, metabolic, perceptive, and cognitive powers. Jaworski addresses mental causation through a *dual-explananda* strategy. Actions have mental causes; actions have physical causes; these look like competing sufficient causes, threatening overdetermination. His resolution is telling. Jaworski *accepts* that the effects of hylomorphic substances are fully necessitated by prior physical causes—in a sense of "physical" that excludes emergent structure—and denies only that necessitation is *sufficient for explanation*. Reasons-explanations and mechanistic explanations, he argues, pick out causal factors of different sorts and so cannot overdetermine an action. Structure earns its keep explanatorily, not by adding a competing causal push.

This makes structure hylomorphism the weakest fit for the Map, and worth flagging as such. Critics note that the dual-explanandum move is equally available to a physicalist, and that granting full physical necessitation is close to conceding causal closure. A framework that concedes closure does not deliver the irreducible, causally efficacious mind the Map's tenets require. Jaworski's structure is not obviously more than organization realized in matter—which is why his own view sits near the physicalist end of the spectrum, and why some readers class it as neither dualism nor physicalism but a third, naturalistic thing.

### Thomistic (hylemorphic) dualism

At the other end, Aquinas and his contemporary defenders—Feser, David Oderberg, Eleonore Stump—hold that the person is one hylomorphic substance, but the rational soul is a *subsistent* form: immaterial in its intellectual operations and able to survive bodily death. What makes this dualism rather than mere anti-reductionism is the subsistence claim. Aquinas argues in the *Summa Theologiae* (Ia q.75 a.2) that "the intellectual principle which we call the mind or the intellect has an operation *per se* apart from the body," and that "only that which subsists can have an operation *per se*." The ground is the immateriality of intellectual operation—grasping universal concepts is not the act of any bodily organ—so the principle of that operation must itself be subsistent.

This is the hylomorphism that most directly competes with the Map, because it is *dualist* in a recognizable sense while still claiming to dissolve interaction. It shares the Map's commitment to irreducibility and, through subsistence, supplies exactly the kind of persisting subject the Map's [agency commitments](#the-substance-connection) (discussed below) independently need. Yet it grounds immateriality in intellection rather than in the explanatory gap or in quantum indeterminacy, and it declines to name any interface at all.

### Emergent hylomorphism (downward causation)

Between these poles sits the position that fits the Map's [Bidirectional Interaction](/concepts/bidirectional-interaction/) tenet most closely. Robert Koons develops an *emergent hylomorphism* on which a substantial form is an emergent individual sustained by the joint manifestation of the powers of its matter, yet one that exerts genuine *downward causal control* over that matter: structured wholes continuously configure the materials that compose them. Anna Marmodoro's power-structuralist reading of Aristotle—on which powers are the basic dynamic entities and structure is itself a kind of power—supplies related machinery, though her positive account is developed as a metaphysics of powers rather than as a worked theory of neural mental causation, and is best cited for the powers ontology rather than for any specific interface claim.

Emergent hylomorphism is the branch a Map reader should watch. It affirms whole-to-part causation explicitly, which is the structural core of Tenet 3. What it does not supply is the *minimality* and *location* of Tenet 2: Koons gives downward causation in general, not a smallest-possible influence acting at a specifiable physical juncture.

## Does Dissolution Actually Work?

The hylomorphic claim to dissolve the interaction problem faces a difficulty that returns precisely where the view becomes dualist. For structure hylomorphism, there is little residual interaction problem—but only because there is little residual mind-over-matter causation, as noted above. For Thomistic dualism, the problem re-enters through the back door. If the rational soul is *subsistent*—able to persist and operate when separated from the body—then a genuine question survives about how a separable immaterial intellect engages bodily processes during embodied life. Calling mind and body "co-principles of one substance" answers the question for ordinary embodied operation, but the very subsistence that makes the view dualist reintroduces a two-relata structure whenever the intellect's immaterial operation must inform bodily action.

The deeper dispute is whether rejecting mechanistic causal closure is a legitimate philosophical move or a way of declining to answer. The hylomorphist says the demand for an efficient-causal mechanism begs the question by assuming mechanism is exhaustive. The critic replies that invoking "formal causation" names the explanandum rather than supplying a mechanism—that "the form configures the matter" restates that mind and body are unified without saying how the configuring happens when what is configured is a firing neuron. This exchange is live and unresolved in the literature, and the Map does not get to declare it settled.

One further feature of the whole hylomorphic program is worth naming, because it is where the Map's approach diverges most concretely. Hylomorphists overwhelmingly treat the mind-body question as *purely metaphysical*. There is almost no engagement with neuroscience, and none with quantum foundations. The debate proceeds through the analysis of substance, form, powers, and causation, largely untouched by the empirical constraints—decoherence timescales, the measurement problem, conservation laws—that shape the Map's proposals. This is not by itself an objection; a metaphysical dissolution need not cite neuroscience. But it marks a real difference in intellectual strategy, and it is one reason the two frameworks so rarely meet head-on.

## Relation to Site Perspective {#the-maps-alternative}

The Map and hylomorphic dualism agree on more than either shares with reductive physicalism. Both reject the reduction of consciousness to physical process; both reject the Cartesian picture of two complete substances piloting each other. The disagreement is about what follows from that shared rejection.

Hylomorphism's move is *dissolution*: deny that mind and matter are two relata, and the interaction problem lapses. The Map's move is *location*: accept that mind and matter are distinct and causally connected—[interactionist dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/), the joint consequence of the Dualism and Bidirectional Interaction tenets—and then specify *where* the influence acts. The Map's answer is [post-decoherence selection](/apex/post-decoherence-selection-programme/): consciousness biasing which definite outcome emerges from the classical mixture left after decoherence completes, the smallest deviation from standard physics that could permit mind-matter causation (Tenet 2, [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)). Where the hylomorphist says the "how" question is malformed, the Map takes the question seriously and pays its price—an interface that is, on the corridor reading, empirically indistinguishable from chance.

It is important to state the dialectical situation honestly. Dissolution is the *rival's* move, and if it succeeds it is a cost to the Map, not a win. The Map's reason for preferring location over dissolution is not that hylomorphism has been refuted—it has not been. The reason is that the Map judges the appeal to non-mechanistic formal causation to relabel the problem rather than resolve it: when what must be configured is a physical brain state with a physical causal history, "the form configures the matter" does not yet say what makes *this* neural outcome actual rather than another. The Map's post-decoherence interface is an attempt to answer exactly that residual question. Whether the residual question is real or an artifact of a mechanistic prejudice is the genuine point of disagreement, and by the Map's own [fifth tenet](/tenets/#occams-limits) the Map cannot claim its framework is simply *simpler* and therefore true. The choice between dissolution and location is a bet about which unanswered question is the load-bearing one.

### The substance connection {#the-substance-connection}

There is one place where hylomorphic dualism is less a rival than an unexpected ally. The Map's agency cluster—[free will](/topics/free-will/), [agent causation](/concepts/agent-causation/), and the surrounding interactionist material—deploys a substance-leaning sub-reading of Dualism: agent causation needs a persisting subject that exercises causal powers in its own right, a requirement traced in [where the substance commitment enters](/concepts/where-the-substance-commitment-enters/). Thomistic dualism supplies precisely such a subject—the subsistent form—without Cartesian baggage and on independent grounds (the immateriality of intellection). A Map reader can borrow the *persisting-subject* result from hylomorphism even while rejecting its dissolution of interaction, provided the borrowing is scoped carefully: the Map grounds the subject differently and still owes an interface that hylomorphism does not provide.

This selective borrowing sits alongside the Map's other engagements with non-Cartesian metaphysics—[Russellian monism and bi-aspectual dualism](/topics/russellian-monism-versus-bi-aspectual-dualism/), [process philosophy](/concepts/process-philosophy/), and the broader [survey of how traditions handle interaction](/topics/interaction-problem-across-traditions/). Hylomorphism's distinctive contribution to that comparison is that it is the one rival which claims to make the interaction problem *go away* rather than to relocate or reconceive it.
