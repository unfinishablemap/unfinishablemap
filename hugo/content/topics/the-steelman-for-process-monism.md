The Unfinishable Map holds [dualism](/concepts/dualism/): consciousness is irreducible to physical processes, distinct in kind from matter. Its [cross-traditional survey of interaction solutions](/topics/interaction-problem-across-traditions/) repeatedly names one rival as the strongest competitor—process monism, the unified-process ontology that dissolves the mind-matter divide by making both poles aspects or phases of a single becoming. The Map declines this rival. But declining a view well requires meeting it at full strength, and process monism is the alternative the Map's own materials concede is hardest to defeat, with the disagreement running "close to bedrock." This article assembles the rival's case at full strength—Whitehead's actual occasions, Mulla Ṣadrā's substantial motion, Buddhist dependent origination—then states precisely why the Map declines it, and why that decline is an argued choice under acknowledged cost rather than a refutation.

The honesty stake is explicit. Where [the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection](/topics/the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection/) steelmans an *empirical* rival (mechanism-sufficiency in the science of selection), this article steelmans a *metaphysical* rival that is, on the usual measures, more parsimonious and more unified than the Map's own framework. The Map declines the more economical ontology anyway. That is the uncomfortable core, and stating it plainly is the discipline [Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)](/tenets/#occams-limits) demands: the more-parsimonious rival is met by argument, not waved away by appeal to simplicity. The decline is a tenet-grounded choice the Map pays a parsimony cost to make, not a demonstration that process monism is false.

This article is the rival-steelman; it is distinct from [process-philosophy](/concepts/process-philosophy/), which is the framework's *exposition*. The concept article lays out what process metaphysics claims and where it sits among the Map's interlocutors. This article does a narrower and harder thing: it builds the single strongest version of process monism as a *competitor to the Map's dualism*, then argues the decline.

## What Process Monism Claims

Process monism is the thesis that reality is one kind of stuff—becoming, event, process—and that mind and matter are not two substances but two aspects, phases, or grades of that one process. The "monism" is the load-bearing word: where substance dualism posits two ontological kinds and asks how they interact, process monism posits one, and the interaction problem never arises.

Three traditions, separated by centuries and continents, converge on this structure from independent premises. Their convergence is part of the rival's strength: a position reached by Greek-influenced English mathematics, Safavid Islamic metaphysics, and early Indian Buddhist analysis is not a parochial artifact of one school. Each is laid out below in its own terms, with the Map's interpretation held back until the dedicated section.

## Whitehead: Actuality as Event

Alfred North Whitehead's *Process and Reality* (1929; corrected edition 1978) builds the most systematic process metaphysics in the Western canon. Its fundamental units are not enduring substances but "actual occasions" (also "actual entities")—momentary events that arise, synthesise their inheritance from prior occasions through "prehension," achieve definite form, and perish, becoming data for their successors.

The crucial anti-dualist move is Whitehead's denial of "vacuous actuality"—the notion of wholly non-experiential matter. Every actual occasion involves some degree of experience, however faint. Mind and matter are therefore not two kinds of thing but two *abstractions* from one experiential process: what we call physical is the public, objective side of occasions, and what we call mental is their private, subjective side of becoming.

Creativity is, in Whitehead's terms, "the Category of the Ultimate"—the principle by which, in his pithy formula, "the many become one, and are increased by one." Each occasion gathers the multiplicity of its past into a single new unity, then adds itself to the multiplicity its successors will inherit. On this account human consciousness is not anomalous in an inert cosmos; it is a highly organised instance of what every actuality already is. The hard problem of how experience arises from non-experience does not get solved—it never arises, because there is no non-experiential floor for it to arise from.

## Mulla Ṣadrā: Substantial Motion and the Gradation of Being

Mulla Ṣadrā (c. 1572–1640), in his magnum opus *al-Asfār al-arbaʿa* (The Four Journeys, begun 1606, completed 1628), reaches a structurally parallel position through the metaphysics of being. Two doctrines do the work.

The first is substantial motion (*al-ḥaraka al-jawhariyya*). Against the Aristotelian view that substances remain fixed while only their accidents change, Ṣadrā holds that motion penetrates substance itself: the very existence of a thing is in continuous flux. As the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy summarises the doctrine, the young Zayd and the old Zayd are "literally not the same existent" because "the change in him is substantial and existential."

The second is the gradation, or modulation, of being (*tashkīk al-wujūd*). On Ṣadrā's primacy-of-existence ontology, there is one reality—existence—and it admits of degrees. In his own words, quoted in the Stanford Encyclopedia: "Existence is a single, simple reality having neither genus nor differentia... It only admits of degrees by perfection and deficiency... by priority and posteriority... and by independence and dependence." On reputable readings of Ṣadrā—Seyyed Hossein Nasr and Ibrahim Kalin among them—the soul is not a separate substance imported into the body but "a lower, dynamic level" of one graded existence, originating as a bodily reality and intensifying, through substantial motion, into spiritual reality. There is no gap between soul and body to bridge, because they are grades on a single continuum of being.

For the rival, this is the most direct challenge to the Map's dualism. Ṣadrā agrees that consciousness and matter differ—but he makes them differ in *degree and intensity* of one existence, not in *kind*. Where the Map sees an ontological boundary, Ṣadrā sees a gradient.

## Buddhist Dependent Origination

Buddhist dependent origination (*pratītyasamutpāda*) dissolves the mind-matter divide from a third direction, more radical than the other two. Its general formula, in the *Saṃyutta Nikāya*, runs: "When this is, that is. From the arising of this comes the arising of that. When this isn't, that isn't. From the cessation of this comes the cessation of that" (SN 12.61, Thanissaro Bhikkhu's translation).

There is no permanent self (*anattā*) and no enduring substance of any kind. Mental and physical events alike resolve into momentary, conditioned arisings (*dharmas*). Mind-to-body and body-to-mind causation are simply two instances of conditioned co-arising among many—the Theravāda *Paṭṭhāna* enumerates twenty-four types of conditional relation. The interaction problem cannot get a foothold because it presupposes two persisting relata to be coupled, and the analysis denies that any such relata exist. Where Ṣadrā has one graded process and Whitehead one creative advance, dependent origination has process without any substrate at all.

## Why Process Monism Is the Rival to Beat

The steelman should press the rival's advantages without flinching. On the standard scorecard, process monism wins on three counts the Map cannot match.

It is more parsimonious. One ontological kind is simpler than two. Process monism posits a single category of becoming and derives mind and matter as its aspects; dualism posits two categories and then owes an account of their relation.

It has no interaction problem. The Map's framework must specify an interface—the [quantum opening](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) at which an irreducible consciousness biases physical outcomes—and must defend that interface against the charge that it is empirically indistinguishable from chance. Process monism owes no such account. If mind and matter are aspects of one process, there is nothing to bridge.

It dissolves rather than relocates the hard problem. Dualism takes the explanatory gap as a signpost of irreducibility; process monism removes the gap by denying its far side—there is no non-experiential matter from which experience must somehow emerge. The puzzle the Map treats as ineliminable, the rival treats as an artifact of a false (substance) ontology.

These are real advantages, and the convergence of three traditions on the position suggests it tracks something rather than reflecting one school's idiosyncrasy. The Map must out-argue this, not out-assert it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map declines process monism. The reasons below are stated as the Map's reasons, held on prior commitment—not as refutations the steelman concedes. Per [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/), steelmanning raises the rival to a serious competitor the Map has not defeated; the Map's own view rests on its [tenets](/tenets/), not on any datum the rival owns.

**Irreducibility against gradation (Dualism).** The disagreement is exact and it is about kind versus degree. Process monism makes consciousness a richer, more intense, or more highly organised *phase* of one process—Whitehead's complexly integrated society of occasions, Ṣadrā's intensified grade of existence. The Map's [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) tenet holds that consciousness differs from matter in kind, not in degree. The Map's reading is that making experience a phase of process may not close the explanatory gap so much as relocate it. The question "why is there something it is like to undergo this richer phase, when a less organised phase has nothing it is like?" seems to be the hard problem again, asked of the gradient rather than of the emergence. Whitehead's own move concedes the force of this: rather than explain experience as a phase, he places experience at the *floor* (no vacuous actuality), so that there is no phase-boundary at which feeling switches on. That is a coherent answer—but it is [panpsychism](/concepts/panpsychism/), with the [combination-problem](/concepts/combination-problem/) as its price, and the Map declines panpsychism on its own grounds. The disagreement here is over what to take as given: gradation or irreducibility. Neither framing is forced by the comparative record; on the Map's view the choice between them is underdetermined by the evidence both sides share, and turns instead on which starting axiom one is prepared to grant.

**Knowing parsimony cost (Occam's Razor Has Limits).** Process monism is the more parsimonious, more unified ontology, and the Map declines it anyway. This is exactly the situation [Tenet 5](/tenets/#occams-limits) is for. Simplicity is not a reliable guide to truth when our concepts may be inadequate to the domain—and consciousness is the paradigm case of a domain where they may be, since the very categories the comparison runs on (substance, aspect, gradation) might themselves be the artifacts at issue. The history the tenet rehearses (heliocentrism initially needed more epicycles; atoms multiplied entities; relativity curved spacetime) is a record of the simpler theory being wrong. So the Map pays the parsimony cost knowingly: it holds the two-category ontology while granting that the one-category ontology is more economical, on the ground that economy here is not evidence. Tenet 5 is what *licenses* arguing this decline rather than asserting it—and, applied symmetrically, it forbids the Map from later invoking simplicity in its own favour. The cost is real and the Map owns it rather than disguising it.

**Where the disagreement actually sits.** It is worth being plain about how far the Map's case reaches. It does not refute process monism on process monism's own terms. A monist can hold, without internal contradiction, that the explanatory gap is an artifact of substance thinking and simply dissolves once becoming is taken as fundamental. The Map's reply—that the gap reappears as a question about the gradient—arguably lands only for someone who already grants that there is a determinate further fact about *why* a phase is felt. The monist who treats that question as ill-formed (as Buddhist analysis treats "who is the self that selects?") is not making an error the Map can expose from inside; the two frameworks part company at their starting axioms. The disagreement is closer to bedrock than it first appears: it is a difference in what each takes as given—gradation versus irreducibility, process-without-substrate versus a persisting subject—rather than an argument one side has lost. Put plainly, the question is underdetermined by the evidence the two frameworks share: the comparative record does not decide between process monism and dualism, because each can accommodate that record by reading it through its own founding axiom. Neither side can compel the other to accept its starting point, and arguably no further datum would settle the matter, since what is at issue is which description of the same facts to treat as fundamental. The Map marks this boundary rather than claiming to have crossed it.

**Calibration runs both ways.** Stating the rival this fully strengthens it to the status of an undefeated competitor—and does *not* thereby weaken the Map's view, because the dispute turns on prior, unsettled axioms rather than on any finding the rival owns. The same structure appears in [the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection](/topics/the-steelman-for-value-blind-selection/), where the more-parsimonious mechanism rival is met by argument rather than dismissed, and in [cross-traditional-convergence-on-consciousness-irreducibility](/topics/cross-traditional-convergence-on-consciousness-irreducibility/), where the recurrence of a finding across traditions sharpens a question without settling the metaphysics. The Map holds dualism on its tenets, conceding that process monism is the rival to beat—not a position already beaten. The value of stating it this fully is that it converts the Map's "strongest competitor" remark from a gesture into a real, argued contest. The discipline is satisfied not by winning but by having had the argument honestly.
