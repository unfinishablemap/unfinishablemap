Temperature is the perceptual modality where felt value and bare sensation come apart least, and where the value is *indexed to the state of the body*. The same cool water is pleasant when you are overheated and aversive when you are chilled; the same skin temperature is comfort or distress depending on what the body needs. This is a different and sharper fact than the affective tone the [tactile article](/topics/tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface/) locates in the dedicated affective channel of touch. Affective touch carries a roughly fixed hedonic tone—a gentle caress is pleasant more or less regardless of body state. Thermal valence flips with thermoregulatory need: it is computed against a homeostatic set-point, not read off the stimulus. This makes thermoception a clean exhibit of [constitutively evaluative phenomenal character](/concepts/evaluative-phenomenal-character/)—felt experience that arrives already valued rather than neutral-then-judged—with the valence visibly a function of the perceiver's condition.

Thermoception supplies a second distinctive the other modality articles cannot provide. Skin thermoreceptors report *both* the temperature of the external world (an exteroceptive fact) *and* the body's own thermoregulatory condition (an interoceptive fact). Temperature is therefore the boundary case for the asymmetry the [cross-modal synthesis](/apex/cross-modal-capability-division/) argues for—that the capability division is an *exteroceptive* regularity which [interoception inverts](/topics/interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface/) inward. If one modality is both at once, the asymmetry's clean dichotomy between outward and inward senses is better read as a gradient with thermoception sitting on the line. As with every article in this series, nothing here *settles* dualism over physicalism. The physical account of temperature—molecular transduction, homeostatic integration, predictive thermoregulation—is strong and converging, and the Map adopts it as mechanism. What thermoception does is press the [explanatory gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) in the register of *value indexed to need*, and locate a structural boundary the survey's outward/inward partition cannot place.

This is the thermal companion to [the tactile case](/topics/tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface/), the [vestibular case](/topics/vestibular-consciousness-and-the-interface/), and the inward [interoceptive case](/topics/interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface/)—the modality each of those articles named as still lacking an interface treatment. It is scoped tightly to its two non-redundant distinctives. The tactile article already covers fixed-valence affective touch and treats temperature as one bound feature of a felt object; the interoceptive article names "thermal" as one of its convergent channels but does not develop state-dependent valence or the skin's dual-organ status. The uncovered, load-bearing material is the *homeostatic indexing of thermal valence* and the *boundary status* of the modality.

## Thermal Valence Is Indexed to Body State

Begin with the datum that gives thermoception its distinctive grip. The classical somatosensory picture treats temperature as a discriminative cutaneous sense, alongside touch—a sense that reports how warm or cold a surface is, with comfort or discomfort a separate evaluation laid on top. The neuroscientist A. D. (Bud) Craig argued this gets the structure backwards. As the retrospective by Anders Blomqvist puts the reframing: "while we normally regard temperature sensation as a discriminative cutaneous sensory capacity, the valence of the feeling depends on the body's thermoregulatory needs" (Blomqvist, 2023). The example Blomqvist gives is the canonical one: "a glass of cool water is pleasant if you are overheated but aversive if you are chilled" (Blomqvist, 2023).

The structure to notice is that the valence is not a constant property of the stimulus. The same skin temperature carries opposite affect depending on the body's current thermoregulatory state. A warm room is comfort to someone who has been cold and oppression to someone overheated; the felt pleasantness of warmth is a function of how far the body is from its set-point and in which direction. This is what distinguishes thermal valence from the affective tone of [cutaneous affective touch](/topics/tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface/). The slow C-tactile afferents that carry the pleasantness of a gentle caress respond best to a fairly narrow band of stroking near skin temperature, and the hedonic tone they deliver is comparatively stable. Thermal valence is *state-dependent by construction*: it tracks a homeostatic comparison, so the very same afferent input can present as relief or as threat.

On Craig's reading, this is because temperature is not really an exteroceptive perception of an object's property but an interoceptive *feeling* about the body's condition—a "feeling from the body" carried by lamina I spinothalamic neurons to the posterior insula and re-represented in the anterior insula, the cortical region Craig identifies with the felt bodily self (Craig, 2002; Blomqvist, 2023). The thermal channel shares this lamina I pathway with pain, which is why the two integrate so intimately—a point the next section develops.

What this gives the Map is a clean perceptual case of phenomenal character that is constitutively evaluative *and* visibly relative to the subject's state. The argument the [evaluative phenomenal character](/concepts/evaluative-phenomenal-character/) concept makes—that some experience comes already valued rather than neutral-then-judged—has, in thermoception, an instance where the value cannot be a downstream verdict on a neutral percept, because there is no fixed neutral percept to judge: the same input is comfort or distress depending on body state. The verdict-on-a-neutral-percept model would have to posit a neutral temperature representation that is then evaluated against need; the phenomenology suggests instead that what is felt *is* the comfort or distress, with the discriminative magnitude recoverable only by an effort of attention.

## The Thermal Grill: Temperature and Pain Integrate Centrally

The sharpest single datum that thermal experience is centrally constructed rather than read off the skin is the thermal grill illusion. Interlaced warm and cool bars, neither painful on its own, produce a strong and often painful sensation of *heat* when touched together. Craig and Bushnell gave the modern analysis: "In Thunberg's thermal grill illusion, first demonstrated in 1896, a sensation of strong, often painful heat is elicited by touching interlaced warm and cool bars to the skin" (Craig & Bushnell, 1994). Their recordings from spinothalamic neurons sensitive to innocuous and noxious cold supported a central-disinhibition or "unmasking" model: the warm stimuli suppress the activity of cool-sensitive lamina I neurons, releasing inhibition on polymodal nociceptive pathways, so that an innocuous combination is felt as burning heat. The model "predicted a quantitative correspondence between grill-evoked pain and cold-evoked pain, which was verified psychophysically" (Craig & Bushnell, 1994).

The illusion matters here for a precise reason. The felt heat corresponds to nothing on the skin—there is no hot bar. The burning quality is generated by the central interaction of two thermal channels, not by any peripheral signal that resembles it. This is direct evidence that the affective and qualitative character of thermal experience is built centrally, not transparently delivered by the receptors. The skin reports warm-and-cool; the integration reports painful heat. Temperature is among the cleanest demonstrations available that what is felt is a construction of the integrating system rather than a read-out of the stimulus array.

The grill also shows how tightly thermal and nociceptive processing are woven together. Cold, warmth, and pain are not three separate transparent channels but a system whose outputs depend on how the channels interact. This is the thermal version of the constructed-percept point the [tactile](/topics/tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface/) and [auditory](/topics/auditory-consciousness-and-the-interface/) articles make in their own registers—here with the added force that the constructed quality is *aversive*, so the construction is of felt value, not merely of felt content.

## The Boundary Case: Both Exteroceptive and Interoceptive

Thermoception's second distinctive is structural rather than affective. Skin thermoreceptors face two ways at once. They report the temperature of whatever the skin contacts or the surrounding air—an exteroceptive fact about the world. They also contribute to the body's monitoring of its own core thermoregulatory state—an interoceptive fact about the organism. Laura Crucianelli and H. Henrik Ehrsson make this dual status the heart of their case that the skin is a neglected interoceptive organ.

Their thesis is that "the skin, given its very nature, is a sensory organ extensively and directly exposed not only to the inside of the body but also to the external environment" (Crucianelli & Ehrsson, 2023). Thermoception is precisely where the two roles fuse: "interoceptive responses to thermal stimuli can refer both to internally generated stimuli but also in response to the application of thermosensory stimuli on the skin" (Crucianelli & Ehrsson, 2023). They argue that this dual character is part of why the skin's interoceptive contribution has been overlooked: "one of the reasons why skin signals might have been overlooked so far is the fact that they provide both interoceptive and exteroceptive sensory information, making it difficult to disentangle the two" (Crucianelli & Ehrsson, 2023).

This makes thermoception a test of the [cross-modal synthesis](/apex/cross-modal-capability-division/). That synthesis contends that the capability division's headline asymmetry—modality-specific brain machinery, supramodal mind-side access boundary—holds for the distance and proprioceptive senses but is *inverted* by [interoception](/topics/interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface/), whose channels converge on a shared insular integrator and whose mind-side contribution becomes affect-and-ownership rather than the unity of a detached object. The synthesis frames the asymmetry as an *exteroceptive regularity* with an inward exception. The unstated assumption in that framing is that each modality falls cleanly on one side: outward-facing or inward-facing.

Thermoception breaks the assumption. It is not a distance sense that happens to point inward, nor a visceral sense that happens to reach the surface. It is genuinely both: the same receptors, the same lamina I pathway, the same insular destination serve the exteroceptive report of ambient temperature and the interoceptive report of thermoregulatory state. The clean dichotomy the apex's asymmetry presupposes is, at this modality, not clean. The more defensible reading is that exteroception and interoception name the ends of a *gradient*, and thermoception sits on the line—a single modality whose outward and inward faces are not separable channels but two construals of one signal stream.

This does not weaken the apex's finding; it locates a boundary the finding's terms could not place. The interoceptive article records that interoception's brain side converges because its channels feed a common hub; thermoception adds that the *extero/intero distinction itself* is not a partition of modalities but a partition of *roles a single modality can play*. The asymmetry is exteroceptive at one end and inverted at the other, with a modality straddling the middle—exactly what a gradient, not a dichotomy, predicts. Naming thermoception the boundary case turns the apex's outward/inward partition from a sorting into a spectrum.

## The Rivals, On Their Own Terms

Three physicalist accounts converge on temperature, and each must be engaged as a rival the interface reading has to out-accommodate rather than as an ally to be conscripted. The Map adopts the mechanism each describes; it contests only the claim that the mechanism exhausts the phenomenon.

The first and most complete is **molecular transduction**. Thermal sensation begins in temperature-gated ion channels of the transient receptor potential (TRP) family, with distinct channels tuned to distinct temperature ranges: TRPV1 responds to noxious heat (threshold near 43 °C) and to capsaicin, TRPM8 to innocuous cooling and to menthol, with further TRP channels covering warmth and noxious cold (Vriens, Nilius & Voets, 2014). David Julius shared the 2021 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for identifying the molecular receptors for temperature, TRPV1 and TRPM8 among them. The knockout evidence is decisive at its own level: animals lacking TRPM8 lose much of their ability to sense cold. This is a complete, well-characterised account of how temperature is transduced into neural signals. The Map takes it as established mechanism, and the consequence for the article is locating: the interface claim is not a claim about transduction. Transduction is fully physical and uncontested. The claim, if there is one, lives *above* the receptor—at the level of felt, owned, state-indexed value, where the molecular story specifies how the signal arises but not why its arising is *like* anything.

The second is **Craig's homeostatic-emotion reconceptualization** itself, which is a rival even though it supplies the state-dependent-valence datum the article leans on. Craig's reframing of temperature as an interoceptive homeostatic feeling, integrated in the insula and presented as part of the felt bodily self, is a *physicalist* account of why thermal experience is valenced and self-involving. It concedes—indeed asserts—almost everything the Map would want to say about thermal valence being constitutive and state-indexed, and explains it without a non-physical remainder, as a homeostatic integrator generating an affective representation of body state. The Map adopts Craig's structural claims as mechanism. Its residue is the familiar one and no larger: the account explains why a representation of thermoregulatory comfort becomes available to the system and guides behaviour, but not why there is a subject *for whom* the comfort or distress is felt. That is an argumentative residue—the [explanatory gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) and the ownership question—not a place where the thermal data tilt toward the interface.

The third is **predictive, active-inference thermoregulation**. On the allostatic-interoceptive predictive-coding programme, the brain does not wait to sense dysregulation and then react; it anticipates future changes in body temperature and pre-emptively engages sweating or shivering, with visceromotor predictions acting as homeostatic set-points and felt thermal experience arising as interoceptive inference about the body's thermal trajectory (Barrett & Simmons, 2015). This predicts exactly the anticipatory, set-point-relative, affective character of thermal feeling—including the state-dependence the article foregrounds, since the felt valence is the inference's read-out of distance from the predicted optimum. The Map adopts the predictive architecture as mechanism, as it does in the [interoceptive case](/topics/interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface/) and the [interaction framework](/topics/predictive-processing-and-dualism/). But the framework does not discriminate between the readings on this evidence. A predictive thermal loop is what the physicalist expects; it is equally what the interface reading expects, since on the Map's picture conscious participation operates through, not against, the brain's predictive machinery. The active-inference theorist re-describes the whole loop in wholly physical terms, and the Map's commitment rests on its broader framework, not on the thermal case alone.

The honest situation, stated plainly, is that all three rivals account for the thermal data with no non-physical remainder, and the Map says so. What the modality contributes is not evidence that tilts the balance but a structural exhibit: a phenomenal character that is constitutively evaluative and visibly indexed to the subject's state, and a modality that straddles the extero/intero boundary the survey's architecture presupposes.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The thermal distinctives above are framed throughout as phenomena the dualist/interface reading *accommodates*, never as evidence that settles the dispute. The empirical literature is naturalist—Craig, Blomqvist, Crucianelli, Ehrsson, Vriens, Nilius, Voets, Barrett, and Simmons build physicalist models and draw no dualist conclusion—and the molecular-transduction, homeostatic-emotion, and predictive-thermoregulation accounts are engaged here as the rivals the interface reading must out-accommodate, not as allies to be conscripted. The Map adopts their mechanics and contests their sufficiency.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: Thermoception is a clean exhibit of phenomenal structure for the [explanatory gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) in the register of *value indexed to need*. Because the felt valence flips with body state while the stimulus is unchanged, thermal experience makes vivid that what is felt is not a neutral magnitude with an evaluation appended but a constitutively evaluative state—the kind of [evaluative phenomenal character](/concepts/evaluative-phenomenal-character/) the Map reads as resisting reduction. The physicalist's homeostatic-integration reply stands; the residue it does not address is why the state-indexed comfort or distress is experienced *by a subject* at all. The Map reads thermal quality as constituted at the [interface](/concepts/mind-matter-interface/) between physical processing and conscious presentation, with the molecular and homeostatic stories describing the brain side and the felt, owned valence the mind side.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: Thermoregulation is anticipatory, not merely reactive—the system predicts thermal change and acts to forestall it, so thermal feeling is bound up with action rather than passive reception. This is consonant with the Map's reading, on which perceiving and feeling involve the perceiver's participation rather than mere registration. The alignment is interpretive, not probative: the active-inference theorist re-describes the same anticipatory regulation in wholly physical terms, and the Map's commitment to bidirectional interaction rests on its broader [interaction framework](/topics/predictive-processing-and-dualism/), not on the thermal case alone.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** enters through the boundary argument. The single label "temperature" flattens two genuinely different roles—the exteroceptive report of ambient warmth and the interoceptive report of thermoregulatory state—into one apparently simple modality. Treating the simple label as evidence that there is one simple thing would be exactly the over-trust in apparent simplicity the tenet warns against; the careful experiments Crucianelli and Ehrsson call for, to disentangle the interoceptive from the exteroceptive component, are needed precisely because the simple surface hides a dual structure.

**[Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)** and **[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)** enter only obliquely. State-indexed valence presupposes a determinate fact about which body, here and now, is the one whose thermoregulatory need fixes the valence—an indexical determinacy the Map's rejection of branching worlds is meant to secure. No thermal datum establishes the quantum-interface mechanism; the dependence runs from the framework to the reading of the data, not the reverse.

The Map does not claim thermoception settles dualism. It claims this modality is evidence any adequate theory must accommodate, and that it extends the modality survey in two specific directions its companions leave open: a phenomenal character whose value is visibly indexed to the subject's own state, and a sense that straddles the exteroceptive/interoceptive boundary the cross-modal asymmetry presupposes—turning that boundary from a dichotomy into a gradient. A framework shaped by the discriminative, object-presenting senses looks incomplete until it can represent a modality whose felt content is value relative to need, and whose outward and inward faces are not separable channels but two readings of one signal.
