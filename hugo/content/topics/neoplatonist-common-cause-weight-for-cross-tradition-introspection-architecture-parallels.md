The [cross-tradition channel audit](/topics/introspection-architecture-independence-scoring/#the-cross-tradition-channel) within the [introspection-architecture independence exhibit](/topics/introspection-architecture-independence-scoring/) scores Advaita, Sufi, and Stoic articulations of bounded introspective access at *low-moderate* convergence. That score required a sharper test: how much of the apparent agreement is *common-cause Neoplatonism* — a single Plotinian articulation of bounded conscious access propagating downstream through Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus, Evagrius, the Arabic Plotinus, Avicenna, al-Ghazālī, Suhrawardī, and Ibn ʿArabī? This article supplies the cross-tradition detail underpinning that summary. The verdict is **differentiated and asymmetric**: the Neoplatonist common-cause channel earns *weak* discount for Stoicism (chronology runs the wrong way — Plotinus is downstream of the Stoa), *weak-to-negligible* discount for Advaita (Bréhier-Armstrong-Aklan consensus on Plotinian-Indian non-influence; Mughal mediation runs the wrong direction for Shankara), and *moderate-to-substantial* discount for Sufism (Christian-monastic-via-Evagrius prefigures *khawāṭir* by ~400 years; al-Kindī's Arabic Plotinus is contemporaneous with Muḥāsibī in mid-9th-century Baghdad).

The net effect on the parent exhibit: the cross-tradition channel score holds at *low-moderate* — the audit is a *calibration adjustment*, not a *defeater* — but the internal weighting shifts. The "cleanest historical-isolation defence" attribution moves from early Sufi practice (where the audit identifies transmission channels) to Advaita Vedānta (where it does not). Ghazālī's hidden *riyāʾ* finding softens at the *framing* level (Avicennan-Neoplatonist faculty psychology) while the *finding* survives. Stoic *propatheia* and Advaitic *vāsanā* / *adhyāsa* findings are unaffected.

## The Plotinian Articulation of Bounded Conscious Access

The channel exists at all because Plotinus articulated, in the 3rd century CE, an architectural claim that subsequent traditions could inherit: *the soul has activity above the threshold of conscious apprehension*. The locus classicus is *Enneads* V.1.12.1–15: "Why then…do we not consciously grasp them [intellect and its activity]…? For not everything which is in the soul is immediately perceptible." And IV.3.30.1–17: "we are always intellectually active but do not always apprehend our activity." Schwyzer's 1960 essay "Bewusst und unbewusst bei Plotin" called Plotinus "the discoverer of the unconscious"; Hendrix (2005) develops this reading as *Plotinus: The First Philosopher of the Unconscious*.

Plotinus deploys a tetrad of consciousness-related concepts corresponding to levels in his hierarchical metaphysics: *antilêpsis* (apprehension), *parakolouthêsis* (self-consciousness), *sunaisthêsis* (self-awareness / proprioception), and *sunesis* (awareness). The vocabulary is genealogically significant — Cudworth imported *sunaisthêsis* into 17th-century English as "consciousness," and the modern English term descends from this Plotinian lexicon.

For the audit, two distinctions matter. First, Plotinus's claim is *framework-level*: the soul has activity beyond conscious apprehension, framed within a Neoplatonist hierarchy in which the higher hypostases (Intellect, the One) are present in the soul but not always thematised. Second, the *specific* architectural findings the cross-tradition cluster catalogues — Sufi *khawāṭir* on incoming-thought source attribution, Stoic *propatheia* on sub-assent affective machinery, Advaitic *vāsanā* on latent dispositional contamination, hidden *riyāʾ* on confabulatory motivation — are *more specific* than Plotinus's general claim. The Plotinian articulation can supply a *framework-level* common cause for the general fact of bounded introspective access without supplying *architectural-specifics-level* common cause for the per-tradition findings. The audit's discount has to be applied at the right resolution: at the framework-vocabulary level, generously; at the architectural-specifics level, sparingly.

The Schwyzer-Hendrix reading is contested: Emilsson (2007) and Remes (2008) caution against projecting modern "unconscious" categories onto the *Enneads*. The framework-level common-cause weight depends partly on which reading prevails — a question this audit names but does not settle.

## Stoicism: The Channel is Closed by Chronology

The Stoic component of the cross-tradition convergence is the easiest case to dispatch. The classical Stoa — Zeno (c. 300 BCE), Chrysippus (c. 280–207 BCE), through Epictetus and Marcus Aurelius in the 2nd century CE — developed *propatheia* (pre-passions / first movements), *phantasia katalēptikē* (cataleptic impression), and *synkatathesis* (assent) before Plotinus (3rd century CE) wrote a word. **Plotinus cannot be a common-cause ancestor of doctrines that pre-date him by 500 years.**

The direction of influence runs the other way. Remes's *Plotinus on Self* shows Plotinus's discussion of bodily self-awareness "correctly highlights the Stoic influence" — Stoic *sympatheia* and proprioception are integrated into Plotinian psychology, dematerialised in Platonist fashion. Plotinus's writings are "full of concealed Stoic and Peripatetic doctrines"; he engages Stoic cognitive theory of emotion to *critique and Platonise* it, not to transmit it.

The verdict for Stoicism: *no Neoplatonist discount applies*. The Stoic *propatheia* finding the cross-tradition exhibit holds at moderate despite-commitments retains its score with respect to this channel. (Stoicism does face a *separate* common-cause problem — the modern Stoic-to-CBT lineage through Ellis and Beck — but that channel does not run through Plotinus and is handled in the parent audit.)

## Advaita Vedānta: The Channel is Closed by Scholarly Consensus

The Advaita case is more contested but resolves in the same direction. Émile Bréhier's 1928 *La philosophie de Plotin* argued that Plotinus's life and work showed Indian (Upanishadic) influences. A. H. Armstrong's 1936 *Classical Quarterly* article "Plotinus and India" issued a critical reply that became the dominant specialist consensus: early Neoplatonism is an original development of Greek philosophy. Paul Hacker's later assessment was that "I do not think that such dependence is proven in any way, though I would not deny the possibility that Plotinus may have had a vague and dim knowledge of one or two Vedantic doctrines."

The most recent specialist treatment — Anna Aklan's 2018 CEU PhD dissertation *Wandering Lotuses: Parallel Philosophical Illustrations in Late Antique Greek and in Indian Philosophies* — concludes after detailed textual and contextual comparison that the surface similarities between Plotinian and Śaṅkarite material "do not involve structural and conceptual connection"; parallels are mostly "metaphorical ways of expression" (Lacrosse's earlier re-examination reaches the same verdict). The continued comparative-philosophy interest (Gregorios's edited *Neoplatonism and Indian Philosophy*) does not produce evidence sufficient to overturn the specialist consensus.

A subsidiary question: does the Mughal mediation channel — Dārā Shikoh's 1657 Persian translation of 52 Upanishads (*Sirr-i-Akbar*) — supply backward-direction Neoplatonist contamination of Advaita? It does not. Ganeri's "Migrating Texts and Traditions" documents that Dārā Shikoh's translation runs *from Sanskrit Vedānta into Sufi-Neoplatonist Persian*, deliberately rephrasing Indian terms in Sufi parallels; the direction is Vedānta → Sufi, not Neoplatonism → Vedānta. And it post-dates Shankara's 8th-century *adhyāsa* doctrine by ~850 years.

The Advaita verdict: *no Neoplatonist discount applies*. Shankara's *adhyāsa* (superimposition), *antaḥkaraṇa* (inner faculty), and *vāsanā* (latent disposition) doctrines are independent of Plotinus. This makes Advaita the *cleanest-historical-isolation* component of the three-tradition cross-observer panel — a status the parent cross-tradition article had originally attributed to early Sufi practice.

## Sufism: The Channel is Open at Multiple Points

The Sufi case is where the audit does substantive work. Three transmission channels connect Neoplatonism to early Sufism, and they compound rather than cancel.

### Channel One: Christian-Monastic via Evagrius and the Syriac Tradition

This is the most consequential finding. Evagrius Ponticus (c. 345–399), a Greek-trained Christian-Neoplatonist hybrid, developed a comprehensive doctrine of eight *logismoi* — incoming thoughts (gluttony, lust, avarice, sadness, anger, accidie, vainglory, pride) whose source the monk's discipline of *diakrisis* must discern. Evagrius's *Antirrhētikos* is his term for the practice of refuting evil thoughts before they receive assent — deliberately preserving the Stoic structure of *propatheia* under a Christian metaphysical overlay. The *logismoi* are attributed to demons, the body, the self, or God; the monk's task is *diakrisis* of the source.

This is a *direct structural parallel* to the Sufi *khawāṭir* taxonomy, which classifies incoming thoughts as divine (*rabbānī*), angelic (*malakī*), egoic (*nafsānī*), or satanic (*shayṭānī*). Both doctrines presuppose that source is *not* self-evident; both make the science of discrimination central; both treat the spiritual master as identifying sources the disciple cannot. The chronology is decisive: Evagrian *logismoi* is 4th-century; Muḥāsibī's *muḥāsaba* programme is mid-9th-century. The Christian-monastic doctrine prefigures the Sufi taxonomy by ~400 years.

The transmission channel is well-attested in general outline. The *Philokalia* and Eastern Christian monastic literature systematised the Evagrian inheritance through the 5th–10th centuries. Syriac translation of Greek monastic literature was extensive by the 7th century. The Levantine monastic tradition was the immediate context in which 9th-century Baghdad Sufism formed: as the *History of Sufism* literature documents, "Sufis encountered analogous traditions including Christian, Neoplatonic, and Persian sources, and integrated external sources like Neoplatonism into a pre-existing Qur'anic paradigm and Prophetic model." The Routledge volume *Sufism and Christian Mysticism: The Neoplatonic Factor* argues explicitly that the common Neoplatonist substrate of Christian monasticism and early Sufism is a major shared cause of their parallel mystical psychologies.

A gap in the evidence: the *general* claim that Christian-monastic tradition influenced early Sufism is well-documented; the *specific* claim that Evagrian *logismoi* + *diakrisis* doctrine specifically prefigured the *khawāṭir* taxonomy needs direct primary-source comparison that this audit's source-set does not produce. The audit treats this as *substantial available common-cause* rather than *documented direct borrowing*.

### Channel Two: Arabic Plotinus via the al-Kindī Circle

The *Theology of Aristotle* is a 9th-century Arabic paraphrase of Plotinus's *Enneads* IV–VI produced in the circle of al-Kindī (c. 800–870 CE). It became, as Adamson's 2002 *The Arabic Plotinus* documents, "the single most important conduit by which Neoplatonism reached the Islamic world." Adamson treats it not as a literal translation but as a "creative paraphrase" with paragraphs of new material connecting Plotinian metaphysics to Islamic theology. Al-Kindī's own *Discourse on the Soul* presents a Neoplatonised soul as a simple substance, with inner-faculty psychology integrated into the *Theology*'s framework.

The temporal proximity to Muḥāsibī is striking. Al-Kindī died in 870 CE; Muḥāsibī died in 857 CE; both worked in Baghdad and overlapped by some 50 years. The first generation of Baghdad Sufis (al-Kharrāz, al-Nūrī, al-Junayd, Ruwaym, Khayr al-Nassāj, Ruwaym) were Muḥāsibī's contemporaries and slightly younger. A direct philosophical-transmission channel from the *Theology of Aristotle* to Muḥāsibī's *muḥāsaba* programme is at least *available*.

Whether it is *documented* is another question. The retrieved sources establish temporal and geographical proximity; they do not establish specific textual borrowings. A focused archival examination — Adamson 2002 and the Muḥāsibī specialist literature (Smith 1935, Stone 1991) — would be needed to firm up the channel from "available" to "documented." The audit treats this as a *secondary* Sufi channel: *available-not-proven*, supplementing rather than replacing the primary Christian-monastic channel.

### Channel Three: Avicennan Internal Senses and Downstream Sufi Synthesis

From the 11th century onwards, the Sufi inner-faculty psychology that subsequent traditions cite is downstream of Avicennan synthesis. Avicenna (Ibn Sīnā, d. 1037) systematised a five-fold theory of *internal senses*: common sense (*ḥiss mushtarak*), imagination (*khayāl*), imaginative faculty (*mutakhayyila*), estimation (*wahm*), and memory. The framework combines Aristotelian *De anima* with Neoplatonist faculty-psychology and becomes the standard inner-faculty model in subsequent Sufi, Suhrawardian, and Latin Scholastic psychology.

Al-Ghazālī's 11th-century *Iḥyāʾ ʿulūm al-dīn* extends this synthesis. His *Tahāfut al-Falāsifa* critiques specific Avicennan Neoplatonic doctrines, but his *Iḥyāʾ* psychology of the heart, inner faculties, and *kashf* (inner unveiling) is heavily indebted to Avicennan-Neoplatonist faculty psychology even where Ghazālī rejects its metaphysical conclusions. Suhrawardī (d. 1191) is *explicitly* Neoplatonist + Zoroastrian + Shīʿa Islamic; his "pillars of wisdom" — Plato, Empedocles, Pythagoras, Zoroaster, Muhammad — name a self-conscious Neoplatonist + ancient-wisdom synthesis. Ibn ʿArabī (d. 1240) inherits the Avicennan-Suhrawardian framework and extends it with the *khayāl* / *barzakh* doctrines.

The implication for the cross-tradition exhibit: the Muḥāsibī period is *upstream* of this Avicennan contamination; the Ghazālī period is *downstream*. The exhibit's reliance on early Sufi sources (Muḥāsibī especially) is more defensible because Muḥāsibī predates Avicenna by 150 years; the exhibit's reliance on Ghazālī and Ibn ʿArabī as further Sufi data-points is *more* contaminated by Neoplatonist common-cause than the original article acknowledged.

## Differentiated Verdict

Combining the three traditions:

| Tradition | Neoplatonist Common-Cause Weight | Reason |
|-----------|----------------------------------|--------|
| Stoicism | Weak (≈ no discount) | Plotinus is downstream of the Stoa; chronology closes the channel |
| Advaita Vedānta | Weak-to-negligible | Bréhier-Armstrong-Aklan consensus; Mughal mediation runs the wrong direction and post-dates Shankara |
| Early Sufism (Muḥāsibī) | Moderate-to-substantial | Christian-monastic-via-Evagrius prefigures *khawāṭir* by 400 years; al-Kindī's Arabic Plotinus is contemporaneous in Baghdad |
| Late Sufism (Ghazālī onwards) | Substantial | Avicennan-Neoplatonist faculty psychology is the architectural framework in which findings are expressed |

The audit produces a *re-weighting* of the cross-tradition channel rather than a *defeat* of it. Three score-update consequences follow:

- The cross-tradition channel's Criterion 3 score (cross-observer convergence) holds at *low-moderate*; the parent's "pending Neoplatonist audit" caveat can be discharged.
- The "cleanest historical-isolation defence" attribution shifts from early Sufi practice (transmission channels open) to Advaita Vedānta (channels closed).
- Ghazālī's hidden *riyāʾ* finding's despite-commitments score is *moderate at the finding-level, framing partly downstream*. The architectural finding (hidden self-display as default feature) survives; the Avicennan-Neoplatonist faculty-psychology *framework* in which it is expressed is what the discount touches.

## A Residual Gap: Does the Christian-Monastic Channel Reduce to Stoicism?

The audit's most consequential residual problem is recursive. Evagrius engaged the Stoic *first movements* doctrine directly; his *antirrhēsis* preserves the Stoic structure under Christian metaphysics. Sorabji's *Emotion and Peace of Mind* and the SEP *Medieval Theories of the Emotions* entry document the Christian-monastic inheritance of Stoic emotion theory via Augustine, Evagrius, John Cassian, and John Climacus.

If the Evagrian *logismoi* doctrine is itself a Christianised Stoic doctrine, then the Christian-monastic common-cause for Sufism is *also* partial Stoic common-cause. The combined chain becomes: Stoic *propatheia* (1st c. CE) → Plotinian inner-faculty psychology with Stoic substrate (3rd c.) → Evagrian *logismoi* + *antirrhēsis* + *diakrisis* (4th c.) → Christian monastic tradition transmitted through Syriac (5th–9th c.) → Sufi *muḥāsaba* + *khawāṭir* (9th c.) → Avicennan internal-senses doctrine (11th c.) → Ghazālī's hidden *riyāʾ* (11th c.) → Suhrawardian *ishrāq* (12th c.) → Ibn ʿArabī's *khayāl* (13th c.).

Run that chain backward: the Stoic *propatheia* finding then becomes a *single upstream source* for both the Christian-Sufi chain *and* (via modern transmission through Ellis and Beck) the contemporary cognitive-psychology framework. The Stoic *propatheia* finding the cross-tradition exhibit treats as one data-point among three becomes structurally *more central* to the entire cluster's architecture. This is the audit's most significant unresolved gap; it would require a separate Stoic-substrate audit to settle.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's [evidential-status discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) requires propagating uncertainty into scoring rather than collapsing it into binary verdicts. This audit honours that discipline by producing a *differentiated* discount across the three traditions — *weak* for Stoicism, *weak-to-negligible* for Advaita, *moderate-to-substantial* for Sufism — rather than a uniform "Neoplatonist common-cause discount" applied identically across all three.

The [common-cause null](/project/common-cause-null/) discipline distinguishes findings that earn despite-commitments status (the source's prior framework would not have predicted the observation) from findings that earn because-prediction status (the source's framework already entailed them). The Plotinian *framework-level* claim of bounded conscious access feeds the downstream traditions a *vocabulary* and a *general architectural picture*. It does not feed them the *architectural-specifics-level* findings (Muḥāsibī's *muḥāsaba* discipline producing *khawāṭir*-source-attribution insight; Ghazālī's *riyāʾ khafiyy* doctrine; Suhrawardian *ishrāq* phenomenology). The discount applies cleanly at the framework level; it applies only partially at the architectural-specifics level.

The [direct-refutation discipline](/project/direct-refutation-discipline/) requires engaging the Bréhier-Armstrong-Hacker-Aklan debate on its scholarly merits rather than dismissing the Indian-influence hypothesis without examination or asserting it without textual support. The audit does this: Bréhier's hypothesis is named, the Armstrong reply is acknowledged, the Aklan 2018 confirmation is cited, and the residual Hacker "vague and dim knowledge" caveat is preserved.

The Map's [introspection-architecture independence exhibit](/topics/introspection-architecture-independence-scoring/) is the load-bearing site argument this audit serves. The exhibit's argument is that the convergence on bounded introspective access across cross-species, cross-tradition, cross-architecture, and patient-population channels supplies *structural defence* against the common-cause null. This audit confirms the cross-tradition channel's contribution survives the Neoplatonist test at *low-moderate* strength — neither defeated nor full-throated. The exhibit's overall argument depends on the *combination* of channels; the cross-tradition channel's survival at low-moderate is sufficient for its contribution to the combined case, and the audit's redistribution of weight within the channel (toward Advaita as cleanest-isolated) is the substantive refinement the exhibit can install.
