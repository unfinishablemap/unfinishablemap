The correlation problem asks the epiphenomenalist a hard question: if conscious experience causes nothing, why does it track biological significance so reliably? Pleasures accompany fitness-promoting behaviours; pains accompany fitness-threatening ones, across species and sensory modalities. The interactionist explains the correlation causally—pleasure promotes the behaviour it accompanies *because* it drives approach. The sophisticated epiphenomenalist's best rejoinder is **co-optimization**: neural systems selected to produce adaptive behaviour also produce corresponding phenomenal states, not by coincidence but because the same optimization process shapes both at once. The Unfinishable Map holds that this reply is the strongest live physicalist answer to the correlation problem—and that the available clinical evidence *constrains* it without *discriminating* between it and interactionism. The dissociation cases narrow the space of viable epiphenomenalist stories; they do not, on their own, settle that consciousness does causal work. The choice between co-optimization and interactionism, on the present evidence, appears underdetermined by the data: the cases rule some stories out without selecting between the two that survive.

This article lays out the co-optimization model on its own terms (source exposition), states the "why this specific pairing?" challenge it must answer, asks whether the model and interactionism actually diverge empirically, and only then turns to the Map's reading. The parent article, [the evolutionary case for mental causation](/topics/evolutionary-case-for-mental-causation/#the-correlation-problem), compresses this entire exchange into one paragraph; the aim here is to give the strongest physicalist move room to breathe before assessing it.

## The Co-Optimization Model

Co-optimization is a response to the [evolutionary argument](/topics/evolutionary-case-for-mental-causation/) that grants the argument's central premise. Yes, consciousness evolved. Yes, selection preserves traits by their effects. The epiphenomenalist's move is to relocate what selection acts on: selection shapes the neural machinery, and the phenomenal states are a structurally determined consequence of that machinery, not an independent target of selection.

The model has two components. The first is a claim about what evolution optimizes. Selection builds nervous systems that classify situations as to-be-pursued or to-be-avoided and that generate the corresponding behaviour. A system that reliably approaches food and flees predators is fitter than one that does not. This much every party accepts.

The second component is the substantive epiphenomenalist claim: the *phenomenal character* of the states realizing these classifications is fixed by the same neural organization that fixes the behaviour. On this view there is a systematic mapping—call it a psychophysical regularity—from neural-functional organization to phenomenal quality. Whatever neural configuration implements "approach this," that configuration also happens to feel good; whatever implements "avoid this" happens to feel bad. Because selection shapes the functional organization, and the functional organization fixes the phenomenal character, the two are optimized together. The correlation between valence and fitness is then no more coincidental than the correlation between a key's shape and the lock it was filed to fit.

The strength of the reply is that it dissolves the appearance of cosmic coincidence without granting valence any causal role. The epiphenomenalist does not have to say that pleasant-feeling and avoidance-promoting just *happened* to line up across billions of years. They line up because they are two outputs of one optimized system. This is a genuine advance over the cruder epiphenomenalism that treats the correlation as brute luck—the version Robinson (2023) surveys as the standard target of the evolutionary objection—and it is why the Map treats co-optimization as the position to beat rather than a straw target.

## The "Why This Specific Pairing?" Challenge

Co-optimization buys its explanatory power with a commitment it must then defend. It requires a *determinate* psychophysical regularity linking functional organization to phenomenal character—and it must explain why *this* regularity holds rather than another, while insisting that the phenomenal character it fixes does no causal work.

Make the challenge precise. The co-optimization story needs the neural system to pair beneficial behaviours with pleasant experiences and harmful ones with unpleasant experiences. But on the epiphenomenalist's own terms, pleasantness and unpleasantness are causally inert—they make no difference to what the organism does. So consider the inverted-valence scenario: an organism in which injury feels *pleasant* and feeding feels *painful*, yet which still avoids injury and seeks food, because the avoidance-and-approach machinery is wired exactly as in us and the phenomenal valence rides on top without touching behaviour. If valence is genuinely inert, this organism is behaviourally indistinguishable from a normal one and equally fit. Selection, which sees only behaviour, cannot prefer the normally-valenced organism to the inverted one. The co-optimization model therefore cannot explain *why* actual organisms exhibit the aligned pairing rather than an inverted or scrambled one—unless it smuggles in a constraint that makes the alignment non-arbitrary.

There are two ways the epiphenomenalist can answer, and each carries a cost:

**Necessity.** The pairing is metaphysically or nomologically necessary: the very neural organization that implements avoidance *cannot but* feel bad, as a matter of psychophysical law. This blocks the inverted-valence scenario by declaring it impossible. The cost is that the necessity is asserted, not explained—it reintroduces a brute psychophysical regularity at the level the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) already flagged as resistant to physical explanation, and it makes the phenomenal character supervene so tightly on the functional that the position edges toward a functionalism for which there is no separable quale to be epiphenomenal about.

**Indifference.** The pairing is not necessary, and inverted-valence organisms are genuinely possible—we simply happen not to be them, and there is no fact about why. This preserves the inertness of valence honestly but abandons the explanatory ambition that made co-optimization attractive: it concedes that the alignment between valence and fitness is, after all, not explained by the optimization, only permitted by it.

The challenge is not that co-optimization is incoherent. It is that the reply must choose between explaining the alignment (and thereby positing a brute, behaviour-irrelevant psychophysical law it cannot ground) or preserving valence's inertness (and thereby giving up the explanation it was introduced to provide). This is a real dilemma for the position. It is not, by itself, evidence for interactionism—a point the next section makes good on.

## Where the Accounts Actually Diverge—If They Do

The honest question is whether co-optimization and interactionism make any *different empirical predictions at all*. If they do not, then the dilemma above is a theoretical-economy complaint, not an empirical defeat, and the choice between them turns on prior commitments rather than data.

The natural place to look for divergence is the dissociation cases, where phenomenal valence and the behaviour it normally accompanies come apart.

[Pain asymbolia](/concepts/pain-asymbolia/) dissociates nociceptive sensation from affective suffering. Following the sensory-limbic disconnection characterized by Berthier, Starkstein, and Leiguarda (1988), patients detect and localize noxious stimuli but lack the felt awfulness—and their protective behaviour is impaired; some smile when pricked. Congenital insensitivity to pain produces a different dissociation: loss-of-function mutations in *SCN9A*, which encodes the Nav1.7 sodium channel of nociceptive neurons, abolish nociceptive signalling at the periphery, and affected individuals suffer unnoticed injuries, fractures, and burns, with elevated morbidity and mortality (Cox et al., 2006). One case removes the felt badness while sensory detection survives; the other removes the signal before it can be felt at all.

Both cases show the same pattern at the level of behaviour: where the felt-bad layer is absent, protective behaviour degrades. The interactionist reads this as expected—valence was doing the work, so removing it removes the behaviour. But here is the decisive point for calibration: **the co-optimization epiphenomenalist predicts the identical behavioural outcome.** On the co-optimization story, the lesion or mutation disrupts the *neural-functional* organization, and because behaviour is fixed by that organization, behaviour degrades. The phenomenal change is a co-effect of the same disruption, not its cause. Both accounts predict: damage the affective-evaluation system, and avoidance fails. Neither the asymbolia data nor the congenital-insensitivity data, taken at face value, separates them.

A [predictive-processing](/concepts/predictive-processing/) framing sharpens why. On that account, affective valence enters as a hierarchical prior over the nociceptive signal, and insular/anterior-cingulate damage disrupts valence-prior *assignment*; the behavioural collapse follows because the prior drives avoidance-policy selection. This is a fully physicalist mechanism that produces the dissociation and the behavioural deficit without granting felt awfulness any causal role beyond its functional realization. It is precisely a co-optimization-compatible story.

So where, if anywhere, could the accounts diverge? Three candidate fault lines exist, and each is currently *open* rather than settled:

1. **Selective behavioural degradation tracking the phenomenal change.** If the loss of avoidance behaviour mapped onto the loss of felt badness *more tightly* than it mapped onto any independently specifiable functional disruption, that would favour the view that the felt layer is load-bearing. But disentangling "loss of the feeling" from "loss of the function that realizes the feeling" requires a way to vary one while holding the other fixed—exactly what the dissociation cases do not cleanly provide, since the lesion disrupts both together.

2. **Felt-quality / functional-role mismatches.** Cases where felt valence and computational reward signals come apart—Berridge's dissociation of "wanting" (dopaminergic) from "liking" (hedonic), for instance—are the genuinely discriminating tests, because they ask which of two physically separable variables selection-relevant behaviour follows. The [valence-and-selection article](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/#discriminating-evidence) develops these as the live route by which the internal value-blind/value-sensitive fork might be resolved. They bear on co-optimization too: if behaviour tracks felt liking rather than computational wanting when the two conflict, the inert-valence story owes an account of why behaviour follows the supposedly causally idle variable.

3. **The constitution-versus-doing-work bridge.** The Map's substantive disagreement with the predictive-processing account does not locate at the dissociation. It locates at whether felt awfulness is *constituted by* the sub-personal valence-prior's computational role (predictive processing) or *adds causal work* the prior alone does not perform (the Map). No current behavioural measure distinguishes these, because they predict the same behaviour by construction.

The honest verdict is therefore the one the parent article already reaches: the dissociation cases **constrain but do not discriminate**. They rule out the crudest epiphenomenalism (valence-fitness alignment as brute luck) by showing the affective layer is systematically tied to protective behaviour. They do not rule out co-optimization, which predicts the same data. And they do not, by their failure to rule out co-optimization, count as positive evidence for interactionism. The accounts appear to diverge in *theoretical structure*—what they say is doing the work—more clearly than they diverge in *predicted observation*.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's interest in this exchange is governed by [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction): the tenet that consciousness causally influences the physical world. Co-optimization is the most disciplined way to hold that consciousness influences nothing while still explaining the correlation Bidirectional Interaction is often invoked to explain. Taking it seriously is therefore a test of the Map's own evidential honesty.

The Map interprets the dilemma in the "why this specific pairing?" section as a real cost to co-optimization—the position must either posit an unexplained necessity that brings it close to functionalism or concede that it permits but does not explain the valence-fitness alignment. The Map reads this as what may be a point in interactionism's favour *on grounds of theoretical economy*, since interactionism explains the alignment directly: valence tracks fitness because valence drives the behaviour, and selection shapes valence through that causal channel ([valence as selection currency](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/#evolutionary-considerations)). But the Map does not claim the clinical evidence *settles* this. The asymbolia and congenital-insensitivity cases appear consistent with the Map's reading and equally consistent with the predictive-processing co-optimization reading. To present their compatibility with interactionism as confirmation would commit exactly the calibration error the [constrain-vs-establish discipline](/concepts/pain-asymbolia/#causal-efficacy-of-phenomenal-quality) warns against.

This restraint matters for [Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits). Co-optimization is the parsimonious-looking position—one fewer kind of causation. The Map's claim is not that it is refuted but that its parsimony is purchased by an unexplained psychophysical necessity, so the apparent simplicity may hide an explanatory debt. Whether that debt is worse than interactionism's own commitment to [a quantum interaction channel](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) is exactly the kind of question this tenet says simplicity cannot adjudicate on its own.

The Map's settled position is therefore deliberately modest: co-optimization is the strongest epiphenomenalist reply to the correlation problem, it survives the dissociation evidence, and the case against it is a theoretical-economy case rather than an empirical knockdown. The empirical discriminators that would do more—felt-quality/functional-role mismatches like wanting-without-liking—remain open research questions rather than results.
