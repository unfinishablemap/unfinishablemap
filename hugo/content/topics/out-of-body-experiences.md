Out-of-body experiences (OBEs) are conscious episodes in which the experiencer seems to perceive their environment from a location outside the physical body. Surveys put their lifetime prevalence in the general population at roughly 5–10%, with higher rates in clinical samples and among those reporting other altered states (Blackmore, 1982; Blanke and Mohr, 2005). They occur spontaneously near sleep, during illness or stress, under drugs such as ketamine, in lucid dreams, near death, and — most decisively for the science — under direct cortical stimulation of the right [temporo-parietal junction](#tpj-stimulation) (TPJ), explained below.

The Unfinishable Map's reading: most lab-induced and many spontaneous OBEs are best understood as dislocations of the [phenomenal self-model](/concepts/self-and-self-consciousness/) — the brain's representation of where "I" am located is decoupled from the body's actual location, while consciousness itself remains tied to the brain. This is an *illusion of disembodiment*, not literal disembodiment. Critically, that this mechanism exists does not entail that every OBE is reducible to it. The strongest dualist claim — that consciousness can in some cases occupy a perspective the body cannot occupy — depends on rare and contested veridical-perception cases, and the controlled evidence from the AWARE studies has so far returned zero hits on hidden targets. The Map therefore treats OBEs as phenomenologically real and evidentially mixed: they refute strong claims that the bodily self-model is necessary for consciousness, but they do not, on current evidence, demonstrate that consciousness leaves the body.

## The Phenomenology of Disembodiment

OBEs share recurring structural features. Experiencers typically report (i) a vantage point outside the body, often from above and behind; (ii) a vivid visual scene that may include their own body seen externally (autoscopy); (iii) intact thinking, memory, and agency; and (iv) emotional tone ranging from peaceful to terrifying. Some report a "second body" or shadowy double; others report being a disembodied point of view with no felt body at all. Vestibular sensations — floating, tilting, spinning — are common during onset and offset.

Phenomenology is not the same as ontology. That an experience presents itself as occurring outside the body does not establish that it does so. Visual hallucinations present themselves as veridical perception; that presentation does not make them so. The OBE's first-person structure is part of what needs explaining, not part of the answer.

What the phenomenology *does* establish is that conscious experience can persist coherently while the felt locus of the self is dissociated from the body's actual location. This is itself philosophically significant. Strong embodiment theses — that conscious experience is constitutively tied to bodily self-modelling — must accommodate cases in which the bodily self-model is reconfigured rather than abolished. The body does not disappear from experience during an OBE; it appears at a distance, as an object rather than a subject.

## TPJ Stimulation and the Illusion-of-Disembodiment Programme {#tpj-stimulation}

The most decisive empirical finding in OBE research came from Olaf Blanke's group at the University Hospitals of Geneva. In a 2002 *Nature* report, electrical stimulation of the right angular gyrus during pre-surgical evaluation of an epileptic patient reliably induced OBE-like sensations: at low currents, the patient reported "sinking into the bed" and "falling from a height"; at higher currents, "I see myself lying in bed, from above, but I only see my legs and lower trunk" (Blanke et al., 2002, p. 269). The induction was repeatable, current-graded, and confined to a small cortical region.

Subsequent work mapped the relevant region to the right TPJ and connected OBE phenomenology to multisensory integration of vestibular, proprioceptive, and visual signals (Blanke et al., 2004; Blanke and Mohr, 2005). Two converging strands of evidence followed.

First, lesion and clinical-population studies linked spontaneous OBEs to right TPJ damage and to vestibular dysfunction. OBEs cluster with autoscopic hallucinations, heautoscopy, and feeling-of-presence experiences in patients with focal lesions in this region.

Second, two laboratory groups produced controlled OBE-like illusions in healthy participants without any pathology. Lenggenhager and colleagues (2007) used virtual reality to project a video image of the participant's own back, viewed from behind, while stroking the real back synchronously with the visual stroking; participants reported localising themselves toward the virtual body — *Video ergo sum*, in the paper's title. Ehrsson (2007), working independently, used head-mounted displays and visuotactile stimulation to induce a "perceptual illusion of being located outside one's physical body" (Ehrsson, 2007, p. 1048).

The conclusion these researchers draw, in their own terms, is that the [bodily self](/concepts/somatic-interface/) is constructed from continuously integrated multisensory signals, and that the construction can be experimentally pulled apart. They explicitly distinguish "self-location" (where I feel I am located in space) from "self-identification" (which body I identify with). Both can be manipulated; neither is rigidly tied to the actual body. Blanke's programme treats OBEs as "illusory perceptions" arising from disrupted multisensory integration, not as evidence of consciousness leaving the body.

The Map accepts this characterisation for the cases the programme studies. TPJ-induced and VR-induced OBEs are self-model dislocations. Nothing in the experimental data suggests consciousness occupies the apparent vantage point in any sense beyond representation; the participants do not report verifiable information available only from that vantage point, and the experiments are not designed to test whether they could.

## Spontaneous OBEs and Their Epidemiology

Spontaneous OBEs are more heterogeneous and harder to study. They occur in roughly 5–10% of the general population at least once, with higher rates among those reporting other altered states such as lucid dreaming, sleep paralysis, or psychedelic experience (Blackmore, 1982). They cluster around hypnagogic and hypnopompic states, illness, sleep deprivation, extreme stress, and meditative absorption. Susan Blackmore's psychological model treats them as state-dependent shifts in the cognitive model of self, triggered when sensory input to the self-model is reduced or destabilised.

Spontaneous OBEs in healthy populations rarely include claims of veridical perception. Experiencers typically report an apparent external perspective on their own bedroom or the local environment, but the perceived contents tend to match what they already know about the space. This is consistent with the self-model account: the brain constructs the external perspective from existing spatial knowledge, and the contents reflect that knowledge rather than independent perception.

The interesting subset is those who report perceptions from the OBE perspective that they could not have known by ordinary means. Such claims appear regularly in the older parapsychological literature, but are vulnerable to retrospective confabulation, leakage of information by ordinary channels, and selection effects. The controlled-condition replication of such claims is the empirical question that matters; that work has happened mainly in the near-death context.

## Near-Death OBEs and the Veridical Perception Question

Near-death contexts are where the dualist case for OBEs becomes empirically loaded. [Near-death experiences](/concepts/near-death-experiences/) frequently include an OBE component: experiencers report viewing their own resuscitation from a vantage point outside the body, sometimes describing equipment, personnel, or events they claim to have witnessed during cardiac arrest.

Pam Reynolds' 1991 "standstill" surgery case is the most-cited individual example: she reported observing surgical details during a procedure in which she was deeply hypothermic, her EEG was flat, and her brain was drained of blood (Sabom, 1998). Critics argue that the perceptions could have occurred during anaesthesia induction or emergence rather than during the standstill itself; the timing cannot be independently verified.

The systematic test is the AWARE programme. Sam Parnia's group placed hidden visual targets — images visible only from above, near the ceiling of resuscitation rooms — and recorded auditory stimuli, then interviewed cardiac arrest survivors for accurate reports. AWARE I (Parnia et al., 2014) found one suggestive case in which a patient reported timing-consistent details of the resuscitation, but no patient identified a hidden visual target. AWARE II (Parnia et al., 2023) interviewed 28 survivors of 567 cardiac arrests; 21.4% reported transcendent experiences, but only one identified the auditory stimulus and none identified the hidden visual image.

The Map reads this evidence as it stands. The strongest dualist claim — that during NDE-OBEs consciousness occupies a perspective from which it can perceive information unavailable to the body — is testable, has been tested, and has so far not been confirmed. That non-result is real evidence against the literal-exit interpretation, even when the sample is small and the situations are difficult to control. The phenomenology of NDE-OBEs remains striking and the broader [paradox of heightened experience during cerebral compromise](/concepts/near-death-experiences/) remains intact, but the specific veridical claim is unsupported by current controlled data.

## The Argument from Mechanism

A common move in OBE discussion is: TPJ stimulation produces OBEs; therefore OBEs are TPJ artefacts; therefore OBE reports do not bear on questions of consciousness and the body.

The first inference is a non-sequitur. That a phenomenon can be produced by mechanism M does not show that all instances of the phenomenon are produced by M. Visual experience can be induced by direct cortical stimulation; that does not make ordinary vision hallucinatory. Pain can be induced by pinching a nerve; that does not make all pain a nerve-pinch artefact. The correct inference from Blanke's stimulation work is narrower: there exists a neural mechanism *sufficient* to produce OBE-like phenomenology. Whether it is *necessary* — whether every OBE goes through this pathway — is a separate question that stimulation studies cannot answer. The discipline that names this constrain-vs-establish move and treats this section as the catalogue's canonical materialist-side instance is [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/): the stimulation evidence *constrains* the strongest "consciousness leaves the body" reading without *establishing* the further "all OBEs reduce to TPJ artefacts" claim.

The Map's position is calibrated to this distinction. For TPJ-stimulation OBEs and for VR-induced full-body illusions, the mechanism is sufficient and the participants are not claiming veridical perception from the apparent vantage point. These are dislocations of the self-model. For NDE-OBEs that include verifiable claims, the question is whether the same mechanism explains the perceived content; here the empirical evidence (no hits on hidden targets) currently favours the self-model account, though the strongest cases remain anecdotal and the controlled studies are small.

What stimulation work *does* refute is the strong version of embodied consciousness which holds that the bodily self-model is constitutive of conscious experience. If the self-model can be moved while consciousness persists coherently, then consciousness is at least separable from the *located-in-this-body* representation, even if not from neural activity overall. That separability is philosophically significant in its own right, and it is where the OBE literature most clearly contributes to the consciousness debate.

## Self-Model Dislocation and Disembodied Consciousness

A useful distinction emerges from the OBE literature. The brain's representation of "where I am" can come apart from the body's actual location — call this **self-model dislocation**. Whether consciousness itself can come apart from the brain — call this **disembodied consciousness** — is a different question. Self-model dislocation is well-established by Blanke's and Ehrsson's work. Disembodied consciousness is the contested claim that NDE-OBE veridical perception would establish if it were demonstrated, and which remains undemonstrated under controlled conditions.

This distinction prevents two errors. It blocks the dualist over-reach of citing TPJ-stimulation cases as if they showed consciousness leaving the body — they do not, and they are not designed to. It also blocks the materialist under-reach of treating self-model dislocation as if it dissolved every philosophical question OBEs raise — it dissolves some, including the strong embodiment thesis, but not the question of veridical perception, which remains open empirically though not currently supported.

The [filter model](/concepts/filter-theory/) sits naturally on the self-model-dislocation side of this distinction. If consciousness is filtered through neural systems, then dislocations of the bodily self-model are dislocations of the *filter's representation* of the body, not of consciousness's location. Filter theory does not require literal disembodiment to explain ordinary OBEs; it predicts that the bodily self-model is part of what gets filtered, and that the filter can produce coherent perceptual scenes from various perspectives because consciousness is not bound to a single bodily vantage in the way production models would require.

## Relation to Site Perspective

OBEs connect to the Map's [tenets](/tenets/) in three ways.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: Self-model dislocation refutes the strong embodiment thesis that conscious experience is constitutively bound to the bodily self-model. This does not establish dualism, but it removes one materialist-friendly constraint and makes room for filter-theoretic readings on which consciousness is not produced by the bodily self-model but is filtered through it.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: The intactness of agency and reasoning during OBEs — experiencers think, decide, and remember coherently from the dislocated perspective — is consistent with consciousness retaining its causal role even when its bodily anchor is reconfigured. Strong [epiphenomenalist](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) readings have to explain why the dislocated self-model continues to support the same kinds of integrated cognition.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: The temptation to settle the OBE question by citing Blanke and declaring the matter closed is precisely the kind of premature parsimony the tenet warns against. The TPJ work is genuine science and the Map accepts it; what the Map resists is the further inferential step that converts "sufficient mechanism" into "all OBEs explained." That step requires evidence the stimulation studies do not provide.

What OBEs do *not* clearly support, on current evidence, is literal exit of consciousness from the body. The Map therefore separates the philosophically robust claim — that the bodily self-model is dislocatable while consciousness persists — from the empirically weaker claim that consciousness can perceive from a location the body does not occupy. The first is well supported. The second awaits controlled evidence the AWARE studies have so far not produced.

## What Would Challenge This View?

The Map's reading would face difficulty if:

1. **Replicated veridical perception under controlled conditions**: If future AWARE-style studies produced reliable hits on hidden targets at rates exceeding chance, the disembodied-consciousness reading would gain serious empirical support, and the self-model-only account would have to accommodate genuinely external perceptual access.

2. **Complete OBE phenomenology induced experimentally**: If TPJ stimulation or VR techniques produced not just the perspectival shift but the full coherent NDE-OBE narrative — including the specific affective tone, the sense of "more real than real," and the lasting transformation effects — the case for treating NDE-OBEs as a distinct phenomenon from lab OBEs would weaken substantially.

3. **Predictable contents from self-model dislocation alone**: If the contents of spontaneous OBEs were shown to be entirely predictable from the experiencer's prior spatial knowledge — with no residual cases of accurate perception of unknown details — the self-model account would be vindicated for spontaneous cases as well as lab cases.

4. **Resolution of the heightened-experience asymmetry**: If the broader NDE puzzle of enhanced cognition during cerebral compromise received a compelling materialist explanation (see [near-death-experiences](/concepts/near-death-experiences/)), the OBE component of NDEs would lose its association with the larger filter-theoretic argument.

None of these would be definitive on their own, but each would shift the evidential balance the Map currently treats as open.
