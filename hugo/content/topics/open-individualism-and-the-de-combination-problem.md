Two ideas in the philosophy of consciousness are routinely run together because both involve "one subject and many"—but they are different claims answering different questions. **Open individualism** is a thesis about *identity*: there is numerically only one subject of experience, who is everyone, and the apparent separation between persons is an illusion of perspective. **The de-combination problem** is a *grounding* problem facing **cosmopsychism**: if one cosmos-sized conscious subject is fundamental, in virtue of what does it ground or fragment into the many "little" subjects we observe? The first is a positive metaphysical position; the second is a difficulty a position must answer. They are cousins, not twins—they meet because any one-subject metaphysics owes an account of the appearance of many, but conflating them obscures both.

This article disambiguates the two, develops each with its primary literature, and shows where they connect. The Unfinishable Map declines both—but for distinct reasons, and the distinction matters. Open individualism is a *rival the Map rejects* on indexical and haecceity grounds. The de-combination problem is a *cost the Map's rivals pay and the Map structurally avoids*, because interactionist dualism posits neither micro-experience nor a single cosmic field, so there is nothing to combine or de-combine. The Map pays the [interaction problem](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) instead.

## Open Individualism: One Subject, Appearing as Many

Open individualism is the thesis that there exists numerically only *one* subject of experience, who is identical with every conscious being. The term is Daniel Kolak's, from his book-length defence *I Am You* (2004). On this view, the boundary that seems to separate you from me carries none of the metaphysical weight common sense assigns it; the felt distinctness of being *this* person rather than that one is a perspectival illusion, not a fundamental fact.

Kolak situates open individualism within an exhaustive three-position taxonomy of personal identity—the part of his work the Map most often draws on (it already underwrites [consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation](/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation/)):

- **Closed individualism** is the commonsense view: personal identities are particular to individuals and persist over time. You are you; I am me; the boundary is real and determinate. Almost all philosophy and every legal system assumes it.
- **Empty individualism** holds that there is no persisting self; identity corresponds to a momentary pattern that vanishes with time's passage. Kolak files Derek Parfit's reductionism here. Subject-boundaries are conventional, not metaphysical.
- **Open individualism** holds that there is one subject, appearing as many.

The taxonomy is the verified, widely-cited core of Kolak's contribution. His detailed argument for why closed individualism fails—the claim that bodily continuity, psychological continuity, and Lockean memory all fail to ground determinate closed-individualist boundaries, so that open individualism becomes the best competing explanation—is laid out at length in *I Am You*; the present article reports the position's shape rather than reconstructing each step, since the fine-grained steps are not the load-bearing piece here. The book's subtitle, *The Metaphysical Foundations for Global Ethics*, signals the motivation: if I am literally you, harming another is harming oneself, and a global ethics follows.

Arnold Zuboff reached a structurally similar conclusion independently, under the label *universalism*, in "One Self: The Logic of Experience" (1990). Zuboff's route runs through the *logic of first-person immediacy*: the "I am the one having this experience" character is the same wherever consciousness occurs, and he argues there is no principled fact dividing that immediacy into separate owners. Where Kolak presses on the failure of identity criteria, Zuboff presses on the structure of the first-person standpoint itself.

The lineage is long and includes figures who are cited as precursors rather than systematic defenders: the Upanishadic *tat tvam asi* ("you are that"), Schopenhauer, Spinoza, and—among physicists—Erwin Schrödinger, whose line that "consciousness is a singular of which the plural is unknown" is the most-quoted open-individualist sentiment from the sciences. These are the position's roots; the contemporary analytic defence belongs to Kolak and Zuboff.

Open individualism is genuinely distinct from cosmopsychism, with which it is often confused. Open individualism is a claim about *persons and identity*—about how many subjects there are and where their boundaries lie. It need not posit that the one subject is the *cosmos*, or that consciousness is fundamental to physics, or that minds are *grounded in* a larger mind. It is the de-combination problem that lives in cosmopsychism, and it is to that problem the discussion turns next.

## The De-Combination Problem: How One Subject Grounds Many

The de-combination problem belongs to a different metaphysics: **cosmopsychism**, the view that the cosmos as a whole is the one fundamental conscious subject, and that individual minds are not built *up* from micro-subjects but are *grounded in*, *derived from*, or *dissociated from* the cosmic subject. Cosmopsychism was proposed in large part *because* it appeared to dodge panpsychism's [combination problem](/concepts/combination-problem/)—the puzzle of how micro-subjects (electrons, particles) could combine into a unified macro-subject. With no micro-subjects to sum, the thought went, the combination problem cannot arise.

The de-combination problem is the charge that cosmopsychism has not escaped the difficulty but merely inverted it. Gregory Miller gave the canonical statement in "Can Subjects Be Proper Parts of Subjects? The De-Combination Problem" (2018). Miller argues that the de-combination problem facing the cosmopsychist "is equivalent to the combination problem"—both are concerned with whether subjects can stand in proper-parthood relations to other subjects. The cosmopsychist must explain how the one cosmic subject can have the many "little" subjects as proper parts; the panpsychist must explain how the many micro-subjects can be proper parts of one macro-subject. These are the same puzzle approached from opposite ends. Miller suggests that two responses are open to *both* theorists symmetrically: a distinction between absolute and relative phenomenal unity, and a modification of the essential nature of subjects. The symmetry is the point—whatever helps the cosmopsychist helps the panpsychist equally, so the cosmopsychist's supposed advantage evaporates.

David Chalmers had already registered the parallel. In "The Combination Problem for Panpsychism" (2017) he names the cosmopsychist's analogue the *decomposition problem* and judges it "just as hard as the combination problem." The *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* frames it as the *derivation problem* and states the conceivability mirror crisply: just as we can conceive of micro-level subjects existing in the absence of macro-level subjects, so it seems we can conceive of a conscious universe existing without having conscious parts—without anything within the universe being conscious. That conceivability gap is the de-combination analogue of Goff's micro-experiential zombie: if a conscious cosmos without conscious parts is conceivable, the cosmic facts do not necessitate the existence of the little subjects, and the grounding the cosmopsychist needs has not been secured.

### The Cosmopsychist Replies

Cosmopsychists do not concede the equivalence. The SEP records the consensus position among them: cosmopsychism *eases* the problem in various respects even if it does not entirely avoid it. Several distinct strategies have been developed, and a bridge article's job is to lay them out where the scattered Map mentions never have:

- **Grounding by subsumption (Philip Goff, 2017).** Goff argues that the cosmic subject grounds the little subjects by *subsuming* them—the cosmic perspective contains the local perspectives as aspects rather than building them from independent parts. The relation is asymmetric grounding, not mereological summing.
- **Priority cosmopsychism (Yujin Nagasawa & Khai Wager, 2016).** On this framing, the cosmos is *prior* to its parts in the order of being—the whole is fundamental and the parts derivative—so the relation need not be literal parthood at all. (The position is set out in "Panpsychism and Priority Cosmopsychism," not the working title sometimes attached to it.)
- **Partial grounding via cosmic capacities (Itay Shani, 2015).** Shani's holistic cosmopsychism cashes out the cosmos's grounding of ordinary experience through the cosmos's own perspectival and experiential capacities, aiming for a relation weaker than constitution.
- **Filtering / resonance (Itay Shani & Joachim Keppler, 2018).** Their model treats ordinary experience as *grounded by* cosmic consciousness through a filtering or resonance mechanism, explicitly denying that little subjects are literally "fractured from" the cosmic subject—sidestepping the parthood that Miller's argument turns on.
- **Aperspectival dissolution (Miri Albahari, 2019).** Albahari's *perennial idealism* makes the most radical move: she argues the de-combination problem *exposes* a perspectival assumption baked into modern cosmopsychism, and posits an *aperspectival*, non-dual cosmic consciousness. If the cosmic subject is not itself a perspective, there is no perspectival subject to de-combine in the first place. This dissolves rather than solves the problem.

The critical verdict in the literature (Miller, Chalmers) is that none of these has clearly succeeded: cosmopsychism has *relocated* the combination problem rather than eliminating it. Whether grounding little subjects in a big one requires intelligible subject-subject parthood (Miller's hard nut) or can be cashed out by a non-constitutive relation (filtering, dissociation, perspectival partition) remains an open and active dispute. This is precisely the Map's reading: the debate is unsettled, and the cosmopsychist's escape from combination is contested rather than secured.

## The Bridge: What One-Subject Metaphysics Owes

Open individualism and the de-combination problem meet at a single structural demand. Any metaphysics that posits *one* subject where experience appears as *many*—open individualism, cosmopsychism, and monistic [idealism](/concepts/idealism/) alike—incurs a debt: it must explain the *appearance of many*. For open individualism the debt is phenomenological and indexical (why does the one subject seem split into separate first-person standpoints, each ignorant of the others?). For cosmopsychism the debt is metaphysical and mereological (in virtue of what does the one subject ground or fragment into the many?). The de-combination problem is the sharpest name for the cosmopsychist form of this debt; the "appearance of separation" puzzle is its open-individualist form.

The connection runs in the other direction too. If open individualism is true and there is one subject appearing as many, then the individuation question—what draws boundaries around discrete subjects—does not vanish but *inverts*: the puzzle becomes not what separates subjects but what manufactures the *appearance* of separation. That inverted puzzle is the de-combination problem wearing identity-thesis clothing. The two ideas are cousins because they are two faces of the same underlying question—*how does one become many?*—posed once for identity and once for grounding.

But they remain distinct, and three differences keep them apart:

1. **Subject matter.** Open individualism is about persons and the boundaries of identity. The de-combination problem is about the grounding relation between a cosmic mind and ordinary minds. One can hold open individualism without any cosmopsychist grounding story (Zuboff's argument from immediacy makes no claim about the cosmos grounding anything), and one can face the de-combination problem without endorsing open individualism (a cosmopsychist may hold that the little subjects are genuinely distinct, not illusory).
2. **Status.** Open individualism is a *position*—something asserted. The de-combination problem is a *problem*—something a position must survive.
3. **Direction of the appearance.** Open individualism treats the many as *illusory* (there is really one). Cosmopsychism's de-combination problem typically treats the many as *real but derivative* (there genuinely are many little subjects, grounded in the one). This is why a cosmopsychist owes a *grounding* story while the open individualist owes a *debunking* story.

The Map's apex synthesis [The Mereology of Mind](/apex/mereology-of-mind/) uses de-combination as its organising hinge, treating subject-summing and subject-dividing as one impossibility approached from two ends. This bridge article supplies the disambiguation that hinge presupposes: combination and de-combination are mereological cousins, and open individualism is the identity-thesis cousin of both—related, but not to be collapsed into either.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map declines open individualism and avoids the de-combination problem, and the two stances are not the same stance.

**Open individualism conflicts with the Map's [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) tenet and its haecceity commitment.** The load-bearing argument against many-worlds is the *indexical* objection: a framework that makes every branch equally real cannot answer "why am *I* this branch rather than another?"—and that question is meaningful only on a non-deflationary conception of the subject, an "I" whose [thisness](/concepts/haecceity/) picks out a determinate this-one. Open individualism collapses exactly that fact. If there is numerically one subject who is everyone, then "why am I *this* person and not another?"—the [vertiginous question](/topics/vertiginous-question/)—has no foothold, because there is no plurality of persons for the indexical to discriminate among. The Map is a *closed-individualist* framework: it holds that there are many numerically distinct subjects and that the boundary between them is real, even though its *ground* is a void the Map does not claim to have filled (see [consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation](/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation/)). Open individualism is therefore a rival the Map rejects, not a problem it inherits—and the rejection is honest engagement at a framework boundary, not a refutation from inside open individualism's own resources. The disagreement turns on whether the felt this-ness of being *this* subject is a genuine metaphysical fact (the Map: yes, possibly primitive) or a perspectival illusion (open individualism: no). That is bedrock disagreement, and the Map registers it as such. The felt this-ness is compatible with both readings—the Map's primitive-thisness and open individualism's perspectival illusion—so neither reading is forced by the phenomenology alone, and the Map's closed-individualist commitment is perhaps best understood as tenet-driven rather than empirically compelled; an open individualist could read the same first-person data the other way without contradiction.

**The de-combination problem is a cost the Map's rivals pay and the Map's [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) tenet structurally avoids.** The Map denies both directions of phenomenal composition: matter has no micro-experience, so there is no combination to perform; and there is no single cosmic conscious field, so there is no de-combination or derivation to explain. Consciousness is a distinct ontological category that *interacts* with matter rather than being built up from it or fragmented down from a cosmic whole (see [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) and [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)). With no micro-subjects and no cosmic subject, there is nothing to combine, share, or de-combine. This is the same structural avoidance the Map claims against the combination problem, now extended to its mirror: where [manyism](/concepts/manyism/) takes the *more-subjects* bet and cosmopsychism takes the *one-subject* bet, the Map declines the compositional question on both sides.

The avoidance is not a free lunch, and the Map names the price plainly. In place of the combination and de-combination problems, the Map pays the *interaction problem*: how does a non-physical subject causally influence physical outcomes? That difficulty is real and unresolved at the level of detailed mechanism. The honest comparison is that the interaction problem is at least *empirically approachable*—it concerns a locus (quantum indeterminacy) where physics leaves outcomes undetermined—whereas combination and de-combination are *conceptual* problems about whether subject-subject parthood is even intelligible. Whether trading a conceptual mystery for an empirical one counts as genuine progress is a question the Map acknowledges rather than presumes to have settled.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** governs the comparison. Cosmopsychism and open individualism both promise a kind of simplicity—one subject rather than billions. The Map does not reject them on parsimony grounds, because Tenet 5 binds the Map's own use of parsimony as tightly as its critics'. The de-combination problem shows that the cosmopsychist's apparent simplicity conceals a deep difficulty, exactly as the combination problem shows panpsychism's apparent simplicity does; monism about the number of subjects does not buy simplicity about explanation. The Map's case against both rests on what the frameworks *fail to explain*—the indexical fact, the grounding relation—not on counting subjects.
