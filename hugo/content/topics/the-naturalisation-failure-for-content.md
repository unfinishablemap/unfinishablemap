Whether mental content can be naturalised is one of the long-running stress tests for physicalism, and Daniel Hutto and Erik Myin's Hard Problem of Content (HPC) is the strongest contemporary argument that the answer is no. Their claim, developed across *Radicalizing Enactivism* (2013) and *Evolving Enactivism* (2017), is that the resources naturalism makes available — causal covariation between internal and worldly states, biological proper function, prediction-error minimisation — cannot deliver the truth-conditional, intensional, referential properties that contentful states are supposed to have. The Unfinishable Map borrows the diagnosis without endorsing the cure: it accepts that content cannot be reduced to natural relations among physical states, but rejects Hutto and Myin's conclusion that basic minds are *contentless*. The Map's alternative is that content is constitutively conscious-mediated — a sister position to the [normativity-of-reason](/topics/consciousness-and-the-normativity-of-reason/) argument that the *ought* of inference also resists physical description.

The topic deserves treatment in its own right, separate from the [concept page](/concepts/hard-problem-of-content/) that explains the HPC argument and from the [intentionality](/concepts/intentionality/) page that surveys aboutness more broadly. The HPC isolates the *content question* as a hard problem that runs alongside, not derivative of, the phenomenal hard problem of consciousness. A reader who is unmoved by qualia arguments may still be moved by the content argument. A reader who accepts both arguments faces the harder question of whether they are independent failures of physicalism or two faces of one underlying gap. This article works through both possibilities and lands on the second.

## The Naturalising Project

To naturalise content is to give an account of how a physical state — a neural pattern, a functional configuration, a dynamical trajectory — comes to *say* something about the world. The state must be capable of being true or false, accurate or inaccurate. It must be *about* particular objects or properties under particular guises. And the account must use only resources a working scientist would recognise: causal relations, statistical correlations, evolutionary history, dynamical coupling, computational role.

Three families of resources have been pressed into this service. Fred Dretske's *informational semantics* derives content from reliable causal correspondence: a state means *that p* when, under specified conditions, it would not be tokened unless *p* obtained. Ruth Millikan's *teleosemantics* adds biological function: a state means what consumer mechanisms downstream of it were selected by evolution to use it as a sign of. Andy Clark's *predictive processing* casts the brain as a hierarchical generative model whose internal states represent the world by encoding parameters that minimise prediction error. Each story leans on a different feature of the natural order, and each is sophisticated enough that it cannot be dismissed by gestures.

What unites the three is a commitment that content is grounded in something the natural sciences already track. If naturalism in cognitive science is to be a substantive thesis rather than a label, at least one of these stories — or some descendant — must work. Hutto and Myin's argument is that none of them does, and the failures are not detail-level but structural.

## The Argument Structure

Hutto and Myin's clearest formulation is that "covariance does not constitute content." In their words, "if covariance is the only scientifically respectable notion of information that can do the work required by explanatory naturalists, it follows that informational content does not exist in nature." The argument can be presented as a dilemma that catches each naturalising resource at the same chokepoint.

The first horn targets pure covariation. Reliable correspondence between an internal state and a worldly state explains *when* the state will be tokened. It does not explain what the state *says*. A thermometer reading correlates with ambient temperature without representing it; a neural state characterised purely by its covariation profile is in the same predicament. The relation captured by "S correlates with P" is too weak to ground "S means *that P*", because the meaning relation is normatively loaded — it admits of correctness and error, and naturalised correlation does not.

The second horn targets teleosemantics. Adding evolutionary proper function gives the theorist a way to specify *which* covariations the consumer system was selected to track, and so introduces a minimal normative dimension: the state can misfire by being tokened in the absence of the condition for which the consumer was selected. Hutto and Myin grant this much. Their objection is that proper function does not deliver intensionality — the difference between representing something *as* food and representing it *as* the nutritionally optimal nearby item. As one critic puts the worry, "biology lacks the resources for specifying under which guise such states might represent" their targets (Roberts, NDPR review of *Radicalizing Enactivism*). Selection history can fix what consumer systems track; it cannot fix the conceptual mode under which they track it.

The third horn targets predictive processing and other recent representationalist programmes. These models often appear to escape the dilemma by recasting representation as the encoding of generative-model parameters tuned by prediction-error minimisation. Hutto and Myin's response, central to their *Evolving Enactivism* programme, is that these accounts inherit the same problem. Calling a parameter a "representation" because it figures in a model that minimises error does not change the fact that what the parameter does is covary with something. The predictive-processing literature, on Hutto and Myin's reading, is doing distinguished engineering work without showing how prediction-error dynamics deliver content rather than reliable behaviour.

The dilemma's joint pressure is what gives the HPC its bite. Whichever family of natural relations the naturalist leans on, the chokepoint is the same: causal and historical facts cannot, on their own, deliver the normative-cum-intensional facts that content requires.

## A Distinct Hard Problem

The HPC is structurally similar to the [hard problem of consciousness](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) but logically independent of it. Both arguments identify a feature of mind — phenomenality in one case, aboutness in the other — that resists derivation from physical resources. Both stay inside the naturalist's preferred framework and use the framework's own resources to expose its limits. Both have respectable defences from the other side and have not been refuted.

The independence matters because it changes the dialectical landscape. A physicalist who reads Chalmers and remains unmoved — judging that the felt unbridgeability of the explanatory gap is a cognitive illusion or a temporary explanatory shortfall — still faces the HPC as a separate front. The two arguments do not stand or fall together. Conversely, a dualist persuaded by Chalmers acquires the HPC as an independent piece of evidence: physicalism is failing in two places, on two different topics, with two different argument structures. That is a stronger position than failing in one place.

But the independence is dialectical, not metaphysical. The Map's [phenomenal intentionality](/concepts/intentionality/) tradition argues that content is grounded in consciousness — that genuine aboutness requires "what it is like" to be in a mental state. If that view is right, then the two hard problems are deeply connected at the metaphysical level even as they are independent at the argumentative level. A Map article on this topic should keep both possibilities alive. The HPC tells us where physicalism fails on content; whether the failure is a separate metaphysical fact or a downstream consequence of the consciousness gap is a further question.

## Parallel to the Normativity of Reason

The HPC sits naturally alongside the Map's treatment of [the normativity of reason](/topics/consciousness-and-the-normativity-of-reason/). Both arguments share a diagnostic structure: a feature of mind that is normatively loaded — content has correctness conditions, inference has prescriptive force — meets a description language that is purely descriptive — covariation, function, causation, dynamical coupling — and falls through.

The normativity-of-reason argument identifies the *ought* of inference: the fact that, given certain premises, you ought to draw a particular conclusion, and that this *ought* is not captured by any account of what the brain *will* do. The HPC identifies the *aboutness* of content: the fact that an internal state is *about* something specific, under a specific guise, and that this aboutness is not captured by any account of what the state *correlates with*. Different topics, same structural diagnosis. In each case, naturalisation gets the descriptive shape right and misses the normative-intensional dimension that makes the phenomenon what it is.

The parallel suggests a unified line of defence for non-physicalist views of mind. Where physicalism keeps hitting boundaries — phenomenal experience, rational normativity, mental content — the boundaries form a consistent pattern. The Map's claim that consciousness is the common ground for all three (developed in the [intentionality](/concepts/intentionality/) and [normative-force](/topics/consciousness-and-normative-force/) articles) is one available unification. It is not the only one, but it is the unification that fits the Map's [tenets](/tenets/).

## The Map's Position: Content as Conscious-Mediated

The Map endorses the negative half of Hutto and Myin's argument and declines the positive half.

The negative half — that the standard naturalising resources cannot deliver content — the Map takes to be substantially correct. The HPC succeeds against the strong reading of what naturalised content is supposed to be: truth-evaluable, intensional, referential. Where Hutto and Myin's argument has been resisted (most notably by Mann and Pain in 2021), the resistance has come at the cost of conceding that intensionality is not deliverable and reframing what naturalists should hope for. The Map reads this as a victory for the diagnosis: if the surviving naturalist position is "content without intensionality," then the kind of content that folk psychology and most philosophical traditions take intentional states to bear is not on the table for physicalism.

The positive half — that basic minds are contentless and that content is a sociocultural achievement scaffolded by claim-making practices — the Map declines. Hutto and Myin's *Radical Enactive Cognition* (REC) preserves naturalism by eliminating content from basic biological cognition. The Map takes this to be the wrong direction. The Map's [phenomenal intentionality](/concepts/intentionality/) commitment locates content in conscious creatures from the bottom up: a creature with experience has aboutness intrinsic to that experience, not deferred to a sociocultural overlay. A pre-linguistic infant attending to her mother is *experiencing the mother as her mother* — not engaging in skilled coupling that becomes contentful only when it later participates in claim-making practices. The empirical pressure points on REC's positive thesis (subitization, numerical cognition in pre-linguistic infants and non-human animals; see Ash and Welshon 2020, Pantsar) align with this reading.

The Map's alternative is that content is constitutively conscious-mediated. The reason naturalising content keeps failing is that the resources naturalism allows are restricted to physical-causal facts, and content is a feature of the conscious subject's relation to the world that is not exhausted by such facts. This is the same move the Map makes for the normativity of reason: the *ought* is real, and it is grounded in what conscious grasping is, not in what brains do. Substituting "content" for "ought" yields the parallel diagnosis. Both moves accept the [limits of parsimony](/concepts/parsimony-epistemology/): keeping naturalism tidy by eliminating the explanandum is a price the Map judges too high.

## Honest Limitations

Three limitations should be flagged.

First, REC remains a live alternative the Map cannot dismiss by stipulation. Hutto and Myin are sophisticated naturalists who have anticipated most of the moves the Map makes, and their preferred response is precisely to deny that "phenomenal aboutness from the bottom up" is the right starting point. The argument that conscious experience comes already content-laden is contested both by REC's anti-representationalism and by autopoietic enactivism (Thompson, Di Paolo) which locates intentionality in embodied biological organisation rather than in phenomenal consciousness. The Map's position is principled but not undisputed.

Second, the Mann-Pain (2021) reply shows the HPC is not a knock-down argument. It is possible to keep teleosemantics by narrowing what content is supposed to deliver. The Map's response is that this narrowing concedes the substantive point — naturalism cannot deliver folk-psychological, intensional, fully conceptual content — and rebrands the loss as acceptable. That response is dialectically reasonable, but readers who accept the narrowing will find the Map's argument less compelling.

Third, Hutto and Myin themselves would reject the Map's conclusion. They are naturalists who take their negative argument to *dissolve* rather than vindicate non-physicalism. Borrowing their argument as a structural ally of dualism is a use they did not intend and would not endorse. The alliance is at the level of shared diagnosis, not at the level of metaphysical commitment.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The naturalisation failure for content connects to three of the Map's [tenets](/tenets/).

**Dualism.** If naturalised content theories cannot deliver truth-conditional, intensional content, and content is a real feature of conscious mental life, then the grounds for content lie outside the natural-physical order as currently described. This does not, by itself, establish dualism — it could be that further inquiry will produce a naturalising story that works — but it provides a piece of evidence congruent with the Map's commitment that mind is irreducible. The convergence with the [phenomenal hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) and the [normativity of reason](/topics/consciousness-and-the-normativity-of-reason/) strengthens the case: physicalism is not failing once but repeatedly, and at the same kind of joint each time.

**Bidirectional Interaction.** Content shapes behaviour. What an organism takes the world to be, under what guise, drives what the organism does. If content is genuinely conscious-mediated, then the causal pathway from content to behaviour is a pathway from consciousness to physical action — instantiating the bidirectional structure the Map's tenet requires. A purely epiphenomenal consciousness could not anchor content because it could not figure in the causal explanation of why an organism reaches for one thing rather than another under one description rather than another.

**Occam's Razor Has Limits.** REC is itself a parsimony move whose cost can be named precisely: to save naturalism about cognition, REC eliminates content from basic minds. The Map's [treatment of parsimony](/concepts/parsimony-epistemology/) argues that simplicity claims should be weighed against what they leave unexplained. REC trades a difficulty (where does content come from?) for a deeper one (how can a creature with no contentful states still engage the world *as* a world of meaningful objects?). The trade is not obviously favourable, and it is the kind of trade Tenet 5 cautions against making too quickly.

The HPC is therefore one of the Map's standing arguments — borrowed from a position whose conclusions the Map rejects, deployed in service of a position those originators would reject in turn. The borrowing is honest because the diagnosis is portable: the failure to naturalise content is a feature of the explanatory landscape that survives whatever the diagnostician's preferred remedy.
