Phantom limb phenomena are conscious experiences of body parts that are absent — most often after amputation, but also in aplasia and spinal cord transection. Roughly 60–80% of amputees experience phantom sensations, and a similar proportion phantom limb pain (Ramachandran and Hirstein, 1998; Flor et al., 2006). The phantom can feel its position, attempt to move, ache, itch, cramp, and adopt impossible postures.

The Map's reading: phantoms support the claim that the felt body is constructed by the brain rather than transmitted from the periphery. This appears to be the convergent view of modern pain science — neuromatrix, predictive processing, and the biopsychosocial tradition all share it within physicalism, so the base observation is not Map-distinctive and does not on its own embarrass twenty-first-century materialism. Three features extend the base observation: (i) phantom *qualia* seem as vivid as ordinary bodily qualia in a setting where central production conditions are unusually specifiable, without obviously narrowing the [explanatory gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) between specification and felt content; (ii) phantom pain can be modulated by purely visual interventions in some patients, suggesting evidence relevant to [top-down causation](/concepts/mental-causation-and-downward-causation/) — though Guémann et al. (2023) find no durable clinical effect in placebo-controlled RCTs, so the strength of the case is uncertain; (iii) congenital phantoms in a minority of aplasics suggest the bodily self-model may not be built up entirely from sensorimotor experience with one's own specific limb. These are differently-shaped extensions of one observation, not independent confirmations (see [audit](#common-cause-null-audit) below). None proves dualism; all sharpen what a theory of embodied consciousness must explain, and the evidence remains compatible with both interpretations.

## What Phantoms Are Like

Phantom sensations have the same first-person directness as sensations in intact limbs. Amputees feel the position of the missing arm, sense rings worn at the time of amputation, attempt to gesture, and are startled when the phantom passes through a wall. Many report *telescoping* — the phantom shortens over months, sometimes ending with the phantom hand attached at the shoulder; others report a *frozen* phantom locked in the posture the limb occupied at amputation.

Phantom limb pain typically presents as burning, crushing, stabbing, or electrical sensations referred to specific regions of the absent limb, distinct from stump pain and resistant to standard analgesics. A striking variant is *pain memory*: phantom pain reproducing the qualitative character and location of pre-amputation pain years later. Patients commonly report the phantom hand in an impossible posture — fingernails digging into the palm of a hand that does not exist — with the pain tracking the impossible posture rather than any tissue damage. The phenomenology violates intuitive constraints: felt position without proprioception, felt movement without efferent-afferent loops, felt pain without nociceptive transduction at the felt site, and in congenital cases felt limb-shape with no history of having had the limb (Brugger et al., 2000).

## Cortical Body Maps: From Remapping to Persistent Representation {#cortical-reorganisation}

The dominant 1990s account located phantoms in reorganisation of somatosensory and motor cortex: face and stump representations invading the deafferented region. Stimulating the cheek produced felt touch on specific phantom fingers (Ramachandran et al., 1992); Flor et al. (1995) reported that remapping correlated with phantom-pain intensity. The *maladaptive cortical plasticity* model became the textbook story.

That picture has been substantially contested by higher-resolution longitudinal work from the 2010s onward, and three claims should now be kept apart. *Persistent sensorimotor representation* is well supported: the Makin programme has shown across more than a decade of fMRI studies that the cortical signature of the missing hand is *preserved* rather than erased — amputees with stronger phantom pain show *stronger* residual representation in the former hand area, and face-into-hand invasion is largely absent (Makin et al., 2013; Wesselink et al., 2019) — and Schone et al. (2025), imaging three adults before and after planned arm amputation, find hand and lip representations stable across the surgery. *Some plasticity* is also supported, but Makin & Krakauer (2023) reread the classic adaptive-remapping findings as potentiation of pre-existing architecture rather than a distinct reorganising process. What is now *disputed* is the 1990s large-scale-remapping or cortical-*takeover* account — neighbouring face representation extensively invading the deafferented hand territory and driving phantom pain (Ramachandran et al., 1992; Flor et al., 1995) — which the 2013–2025 literature treats as contested rather than established. With Root et al. (2022) on differential remapping after *congenital but not acquired* hand loss, the leading reading is that adult cortical body maps are largely stable through amputation, though the mechanism and its interpretation remain unsettled.

For the Map this revision appears welcome. The Flor-era story explained phantoms deflationarily as cortical territory finding new uses; the newer picture says something stronger — the felt body persists in the cortex because the cortical body model does, with or without the limb. That arguably sharpens the [explanatory gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) by setting *stable* neural activity as the explanandum, and matches the structural commitment the [somatic-interface](/concepts/somatic-interface/) and [filter-theoretic](/concepts/filter-theory/) readings expect. The discipline the currency of this literature requires: none of these neural findings — stable representation, the residual plasticity, or the now-contested large-scale remapping — by itself distinguishes a nonphysical lived limb from an embodied [predictive](/concepts/predictive-processing/) or sensorimotor model, since central construction of a persistent body map is equally available to a materialist account. The cortical revision constrains what any theory of the felt body must explain; it does not adjudicate between them. Peripheral mechanisms (stump neuromas, ectopic firing, spinal sensitisation) remain essential for early phantom pain (Flor et al., 2006).

## Mirror Therapy and Top-Down Modulation {#mirror-therapy}

Ramachandran and Rogers-Ramachandran (1996) reported that a vertical mirror in the patient's midline, with the reflection of the intact limb occupying the phantom's position, could produce vivid felt movement and immediate pain relief — seeing the phantom *un-clench* releases the impossible posture and reduces the associated pain.

Three claims must be separated. *Phenomenological modulation* — vivid felt movement, sometimes immediate change in pain quality — is well-attested in single-subject and small-n case series back to 1996. *Short-term subjective pain reduction* is moderately but heterogeneously supported by early randomised trials (Chan et al., 2007; Foell et al., 2014). *Durable placebo-controlled clinical efficacy* is weakly supported: Guémann et al. (2023), restricting to placebo-controlled RCTs, found no evidence of clinical effectiveness. The article's load-bearing pathway-shape claim rests on the phenomenological-modulation effect; Rajendram et al. (2022) report mirror therapy, virtual reality, and graded motor imagery producing equivalent outcomes where any works, weakening the specifically-visual-feedback claim.

The philosophically important feature appears to be the causal structure: a purely visual stimulus may modulate pain with no peripheral generator at the felt site, by targeting the patient's *visual experience* of their own body. On the persistent-body-model reading, the mirror alters the visual evidence the stable cortical body model receives — possibly connecting to [other evidence](/topics/clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation/) of intentional mental activity changing the brain without peripheral or pharmacological intervention. The pathway shape is compatible with materialism and equally compatible with the Map's [interactionist reading](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/); predictive processing [below](#predictive-processing) absorbs the same shape as visual prediction errors revising a generative body model — compatibility on both sides rather than differential support, so neither interpretation is forced by the pathway data alone.

## Congenital Phantoms and the Innate Body Schema {#congenital-phantoms-and-the-innate-body-schema}

The most surprising phantoms come from people born without the limb. Brugger et al. (2000) documented a woman with congenital bilateral upper-limb aplasia who reported vivid phantom arms and hands; imaging during phantom movement showed sensorimotor, premotor, and parietal activation. Base rates are contested: Melzack et al. (1997) reported sensations in roughly 20% of children with congenital limb deficiency; reviews place estimates at 10–40%. Most aplasics do not report phantoms.

The strongest published critic is Price (2006), who proposes a developmental / mirror-neuron alternative: the body schema is built during a sensitive period from external visual experience of other bodies, with the *prosthesis-fitting correlation* (functional prosthesis before age seven predicts later phantoms) as the load-bearing observation. The article's claim is the weaker one: under some developmental conditions, the brain may have the capacity to generate a body schema with default human form even when the limb is absent, perhaps locating the body model deeper than peripheral history with that specific limb. Whether the capacity is innate-then-calibrated-by-external-bodies (Price) or innate-then-modulated-by-own-sensorimotor-experience (Brugger) is what Crawford (2014) calls "one of the longest running and most acrimonious debates" in the literature. Root et al. (2022) is the partial defence of a structural-default reading: facial remapping following *congenital but not acquired* hand loss appears to yield a cortical signature pure visual-exposure accounts do not predict — though a predictive-processing developmental theorist can absorb it as differential precision-weighting on body-model priors. The Map does not commit between these accounts; congenital phantoms appear to shift the locus of the felt body from peripheral history to a more general neural architecture, though the evidence is consistent with both developmental and structural-default readings.

## What Phantom Pain Tells Us About Pain

Phantom limb pain provides one of the cleanest dissociations in the philosophy of pain: no tissue damage at the felt site, no peripheral nociceptive transduction, yet a fully phenomenal pain experience — the inverse of [pain asymbolia](/concepts/pain-asymbolia/), where nociception is preserved but the affective dimension is missing. Together the dissociations chart a logical landscape no peripheralist account can occupy. The Map's [treatment of pain](/topics/pain-consciousness-and-causal-power/) takes this seriously: pain is real, phenomenal, and causally efficacious without requiring the periphery. Phantom pain is one of the strongest cases for central construction because the periphery's absence is undeniable, and one in which the gap between central construction and felt content is unusually stark.

## The Neuromatrix Picture {#neuromatrix}

The contemporary physicalist account most directly tailored to phantoms is Melzack's body-self neuromatrix theory (Melzack, 1990; 2001): the felt body is generated by a distributed neural network — somatosensory, thalamocortical, limbic — whose characteristic activity Melzack called a *neurosignature*. Peripheral input modulates but is not required; cognitive and affective neuromodules shape output; a hard-wired component makes sense of congenital phantoms.

Neuromatrix theory already commits to most of the article's empirical picture: brain-constructed felt body, persistent central representation, innate default-human body schema, top-down modulation. The three "philosophically loaded" features are findings it was constructed to explain, not findings it must accommodate against the grain. What phantoms supply may be a clinically vivid instance of the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) where central production conditions are unusually specifiable and the failure to bridge to felt content is correspondingly stark.

The Map's reading goes beyond neuromatrix theory by declining the framework-level commitment that the bridge from neurosignature activity to felt burning, itching, or postural content is redeemable within physicalist resources. Neuromatrix theory treats the bridge as promissory; the Map suggests reading the mechanism story as the empirical anatomy of a [somatic interface](/concepts/somatic-interface/) that [filter theory](/concepts/filter-theory/) expects — a framing choice the data alone do not adjudicate.

## The Predictive-Processing Account {#predictive-processing}

The working successor to neuromatrix theory is the Bayesian-brain / [predictive processing](/concepts/predictive-processing/) account: the brain is a hierarchical generative model, perception is active inference. De Ridder, Vanneste & Freeman (2014) treat phantom percepts as resolutions of sensory uncertainty — with no contradicting input from a missing limb, the high-precision prior on body configuration continues to dominate. Kiverstein et al. (2022) develop pain as interoceptive inference under precision-weighted prediction error; Lersch et al. (2023) and Sanders et al. (2021) extend the treatment to analgesia and sensory disconnection. Makin's own lab now frames its programme as a predictive-coding test. Predictive processing is the strongest contemporary materialist competitor.

The account appears to handle each "philosophically loaded" feature: felt position without proprioceptive input (the high-precision prior may dominate when contradicting error is absent), felt pain without peripheral nociception (the model infers damage absent any peripheral signal), mirror therapy (visual prediction errors revise posture priors), congenital phantoms (innate priors over body configuration dominate in aplasia). What predictive processing does not specify is why hierarchical Bayesian inference is accompanied by felt burning, itching, or impossible-posture cramp; the bridge to phenomenal character appears promissory for the same reasons as the neuromatrix bridge [above](#neuromatrix), and the Map's framework-boundary disagreement is the same.

Predictive-processing vocabulary seems content-laden — priors are *about* body configurations, prediction errors signal *mismatch*, the model *infers* damage. Felt impossible-posture pain has the phenomenal character of *being-about-an-unrealised-bodily-state*, and that phenomenal-aboutness may be what the inferential vocabulary borrows without paying for. The framework's reply — that phenomenal-aboutness is itself derived from hierarchical inference — is internally available; the disagreement is whether the promissory note can be paid.

## Common-Cause-Null Audit of the Three Pillars {#common-cause-null-audit}

The three "philosophically loaded" features can read as independent lines of evidence converging on the Map's reading. The [common-cause-null](/project/common-cause-null/) discipline asks whether N observations are independent triangulations or one observation read N ways. The shared upstream here appears to be the *brain-constructed felt body*: vivid qualia, top-down modulation, and congenital phantoms each presuppose central construction. The base is single; the three pillars are not three independent confirmations. What partial independence each pillar adds: the explanatory-gap pillar adds *clinical specifiability*; the top-down pillar adds *causal-pathway-shape* (modulation runs through what the patient consciously sees); the innate-schema pillar adds *deeper-than-personal-history* on the qualified reading earned against Price. The consequence is calibrative rather than retractive: predictive processing arguably absorbs pathway-shape as visual prediction error and deeper-than-personal-history as innate-prior architecture; only the residual specification-to-felt-content gap appears to sit at the framework boundary, honouring [evidential-status discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/). The dialectical situation, on this audit, is that neither interpretation is forced by the phantom evidence alone — the data are compatible with either reading and the choice between them is made on framework-level grounds.

The framework-boundary commitment — that the bridge from neural mechanism to felt content "will not be redeemed within physicalist resources" — needs an operational anchor. By *redemption within physicalist resources* the Map means a third-person account, framed entirely in non-content vocabulary, from which the phenomenal-aboutness of impossible-posture pain might be derived without additional bridge premises; a successful derivation of this form would warrant retracting the commitment. Relatedly, the article's reading of central construction as the empirical anatomy of a [somatic interface](/concepts/somatic-interface/) is a framework-level commitment imported from that concept article, not earned by the phantom case itself: the findings appear equally available to neuromatrix-as-empirical-anatomy and PP-generative-body-model ontologies. Phantoms are consistent with the interface reading; they do not independently motivate it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Phantom limb phenomena bear on three of the Map's [tenets](/tenets/).

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: Phantoms may make the [explanatory gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) clinically vivid by displaying felt content whose central production conditions are unusually specifiable. The shift from remapping to persistent representation appears to tighten this: a stable, partly innate central body model is the explanandum, and the gap is between that activity and the felt burning, itching, or postural content. Neuromatrix theory and predictive processing were constructed to specify exactly this production-conditions story; phantoms perhaps exhibit the standard hard problem in a setting where the failure to bridge is particularly stark, without differentially refuting materialism. The Map's framework-boundary commitment is that the bridge will not be redeemed within physicalist resources; phantoms appear consistent with that reading rather than independent evidence for it.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: Top-down modulation may provide evidence that what a patient *sees and attends to* causally influences pain intensity. The Map's [interactionist reading](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) suggests that conscious selection among candidate body-models does real causal work, with the mirror as environmental scaffold. The pathway-shape (conscious visual content → nociceptive modulation, without peripheral intervention) is equally absorbed by predictive processing's body-model update story — compatibility on both sides rather than differential prediction success.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: The inference from "we know the production conditions" to "we have explained the phenomenon" elides the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/). The Map accepts the neuroscience and resists the extension that converts a sufficient-mechanism story into a complete account.

Phantoms do *not* support consciousness existing independently of the brain. The phantom is brain-mediated; its conditions of existence are neural, even when the limb is not. The Map's dualism claims consciousness is not reducible to the brain, not that it operates without one.

## What Would Challenge This View?

1. **Complete pharmacological control of phantom pain**: If a peripheral or pharmacological intervention reliably abolished phantom pain across populations, the case for top-down causation would weaken.

2. **Further failure of mirror therapy under controlled conditions**: Partially met. Guémann et al. (2023) found no evidence of durable clinical effectiveness in placebo-controlled RCTs, and the article has downgraded its claim to phenomenological modulation. Further well-powered placebo-controlled work returning null results for *phenomenological modulation* specifically would shift the balance against the top-down causation pillar; Rajendram et al. (2022) already weakens the specifically-visual-feedback claim.

3. **Predictive, fine-grained neural correlates of phantom content**: A bar set at *bridging* reductive accounts would be structurally unfalsifiable; [evidential calibration](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) requires specifying what *evidence* would shift the balance. If neural-correlate work matured to *predictive content-specific correlations* — readouts predicting felt quality, location, and modal character across subjects and conditions, discriminating among reports not yet generated — the explanatory-gap argument from phantoms would lose force. Partial results in vision and motor-imagery decoding suggest the terrain is not closed.

4. **Vindication of the developmental account**: Price (2006) has published the critique the structural-default reading must answer; the residual reading leans on Root et al. (2022) for the congenital/acquired differential cortical signature. If the differential reduces to precision-weighting on body-model priors, or the prosthesis-fitting correlation eliminates the unaccounted residue, the stronger architectural claim would be retracted while the weaker "deeper than peripheral history" reading would survive.

None would be definitive on its own; each would shift the evidential balance.
