A brain organoid is a three-dimensional culture of real neurons, grown from human stem cells, that develops real synapses and—at months of maturation—spontaneous oscillatory network activity resembling the EEG of a preterm infant. A parallel programme puts cultured cortical neurons in a closed loop with a simulated world and reports them learning to play Pong. The commercial successor to that demonstration now ships as a "biological computer." The question this forces on the Unfinishable Map is sharp and single: **is a lab-grown neural network a candidate site for the consciousness-physical interface—a possible experiencer—or is it sub-personal machinery that computes with no one home?**

The Map's answer begins with a discipline it applies across its whole competency cluster: **the presence of neural machinery, learning, and brain-like dynamics is not evidence of felt experience.** That decoupling is the article's thesis; the three readings it licenses are laid out below under [the interface question](#the-interface-question). Organoids matter because they are the mirror image of the sponge and placozoan *lower-bound* case ([neuron-less-animals-sponges-placozoans-and-the-lower-bound-of-cognition](/topics/neuron-less-animals-sponges-placozoans-and-the-lower-bound-of-cognition/)): there, behaviour without any neural substrate; here, neural substrate without a body, a sensory world, or an evolutionary history that made the tissue *for* anything. They are the opposite edge of the same interface-localisation question.

**A note on scope.** This article deliberately does not re-derive the substrate-physics analysis. Whether the *class* of matter organoids are made of—continuous, carbon, possibly hosting operationally integrated indeterminacy—could in principle host the interface is worked out in [ai-hardware-substrate-taxonomy](/concepts/ai-hardware-substrate-taxonomy/), which places "biological / wetware" as one of six AI-hardware substrate tiers on the discrete-vs-continuous and classical-vs-quantum axes. Read that companion for "could this kind of stuff host consciousness." This article asks the orthogonal, artifact-level question: setting the substrate physics aside, what is actually in the dish, what can it do, and does *this particular thing* have anyone inside it.

## What Is Actually in the Dish

Cerebral organoids arrived as a tool for studying development, not for building minds. Lancaster, Knoblich and colleagues showed that induced pluripotent stem cells will self-organise into a three-dimensional culture with discrete, interdependent brain regions, including a cerebral cortex whose progenitor zones produce mature cortical neuron subtypes; they used it to model microcephaly, a human developmental disorder [1]. The founding result is that real, regionalised neural tissue can be grown outside a body—the precondition for the interface question to arise at all.

The finding that made the consciousness question non-frivolous came six years later. Trujillo and colleagues in the Muotri lab reported that cortical organoids develop periodic, nested oscillatory network events, dependent on glutamatergic and GABAergic signalling, whose alternating quiescence and synchronised bursting resembles the spontaneous activity transients seen in preterm-infant EEG—so much so that a machine-learning model trained to predict a neonate's age from EEG features could track the organoid's own developmental timeline [2]. The careful reading, which the authors themselves press, is that this is a claim about *network dynamics resembling early development*, not a claim about consciousness. An EEG-like trace is a structural and developmental signal, not an experiential one.

## The DishBrain Result and a Word That Misleads

The most widely reported demonstration comes from Kagan and colleagues, who placed human- and rodent-derived cortical cultures on a high-density multielectrode array and embedded them, through closed-loop stimulation and recording, in a simulated Pong world; performance improved over training [3]. The paper's title uses the word "sentience," and this is where careful reading matters most.

Kagan et al. use "sentience" in an explicitly **technical** sense—the Fristonian free-energy / active-inference sense of a system that responds to and models its sensory milieu so as to minimise surprise. Karl Friston is a co-author, and the framing throughout is active inference, not phenomenal consciousness. The word does not mean, and the paper does not claim, that anything is *felt*. Popular coverage routinely collapses the technical term into the everyday one; the Map should hold them rigorously apart. "It learns Pong" licenses an inference to adaptive information-processing competence. It licenses no inference to "something is watching the Pong."

Two further developments complete the empirical picture. Smirnova and colleagues—the first author is **Smirnova**, not Hartung, who appears among the senior authors—proposed "organoid intelligence" (OI) as a named research field: brain organoids as biological computing substrates, targeting the molecular and cellular correlates of learning and memory, paired from the outset with an embedded-ethics programme [4]. And Cortical Labs' CL1, launched in March 2025, turns the DishBrain demonstration into a shipping product: roughly 800,000 lab-grown human-derived neurons on a silicon multielectrode interface, viable up to around six months, priced near US$35,000 and offered as a cloud "Wetware as a Service." Independent peer-reviewed characterisation of CL1, as distinct from the 2022 platform, remains thin, and its capability claims are largely industry-sourced. What commercialisation changes is the *moral urgency* of the question—it moves from thought experiment to governance—not the weight of the experiencer evidence, which stays exactly where DishBrain left it: closed-loop adaptive computation.

## The Interface Question {#the-interface-question}

The Map's throughline is **interface-localisation**: where, if anywhere, is the site at which consciousness and the physical world interact? Organoids press the question from a novel direction. The sponge and placozoan case probes the lower bound—coordinated behaviour with no neurons at all, asking whether the interface can exist below the neural threshold. The organoid case is the opposite edge: real cortical neurons, real synapses, real oscillations, but no body to act with, no world the activity is *about*, and no biography. It asks whether neural machinery *alone* is sufficient, or whether the interface—if it is anywhere—is bound up with embodiment and world-directedness.

Three readings, held open rather than adjudicated:

1. **Candidate site.** If the interface localises to a physical property of integrated neural tissue, an organoid instantiating that property could be a genuine, if minimal and world-less, experiencer—a mind with nothing to think *about*.
2. **Necessary but insufficient.** The neural substrate is one ingredient, but the interface requires operational integration with a body and world that a dish cannot supply; organoids are permanently pre-experiential.
3. **Sub-personal machinery.** Whatever the organoid does is computation with no one home—the same competence-without-experience reading the DishBrain-Pong result illustrates, and the reading the Map's cluster keeps returning to.

The Map does not claim to know which reading is correct. It claims that the empirical facts on the table—oscillation, learning, biocomputing—cannot by themselves decide between them, because every one of those facts is a competence signal and none is an experiencer signal.

## The Ethics Literature Names Its Own Problem

The OI field embedded an ethics programme at launch precisely because the moral-status question is unsettled. Lavazza argues that moral status would require at least a minimal or basic consciousness, that current organoids likely lack it, but that the situation could change as neural sophistication grows—and proposes that consciousness *assessment* (perturbational-complexity-style measures) could serve as a gating tool [5]. Jeziorski and colleagues, writing a cross-disciplinary consensus piece directly in response to the Trujillo oscillation findings, catalogue the oversight, biomaterial-procurement, chimera, and consciousness/moral-status issues together [6]. Sawai and colleagues argue for a **precautionary principle**: absent an accepted consciousness-detection procedure, proceed *as if* organoids may be conscious when the matter is uncertain [7].

The common thread, which every author in this literature stresses, is a methodological gap: **there is no accepted, organoid-applicable consciousness-detection procedure.** All the moral-status reasoning is downstream of that absence.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)** is primary here, and it sets a stricter and different bar than the ethics literature does. Where Lavazza's assessment approach asks about network *complexity* as a proxy for the probability of consciousness, the Map's interface account asks whether there is an *operationally integrated site of the kind the interface requires*—a physical junction at which non-physical influence could act. Behavioural competence and an EEG-like trace do not answer that question; they are compatible with all three readings above. The organoid is where the Map's decoupling of competence from experience gets its cleanest positive-substrate test: the neural machinery is really there, and its presence still settles nothing about whether an experiencer is.

**Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)** cuts both ways, and its double edge is the reason the Map declines to over-resolve. Minimality—the smallest possible non-physical footprint—offers no reason to expect an interface to "switch on" merely because neuron count or oscillatory complexity crosses a threshold; it resists inflating every integrated neural system into an interface site. But it equally forbids confident denial: if the relevant physical property is genuinely minimal, its presence or absence is precisely *not* what behavioural competence or an oscillation trace would reveal. Minimality therefore recommends agnosticism plus caution. The Map lands near Sawai's precautionary conclusion, but for a different reason: physical-interface uncertainty, not complexity-based probability.

**Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)** keeps the verdict open. The empirical record is agreed—real neurons, emergent oscillations, closed-loop learning, no body or world—but the interpretation is contested, and the Map does not claim to have shown organoids are non-conscious. It claims the weaker and firmer thing: their capabilities give us no purchase on the experiencer question either way, and the slide common in popular coverage from "does brain-like things" to "has experiences" is exactly the inference the tenets refuse. Where the Map parts company with the deflationary reading of DishBrain is subtle: it endorses the *discipline* (learning is not experience) without endorsing the further physicalist step "therefore experience is nothing over and above the computation." The Map is dualist; competence and experience come apart in both directions.
