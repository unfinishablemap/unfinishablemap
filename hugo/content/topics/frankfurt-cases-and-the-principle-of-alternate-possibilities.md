The **Principle of Alternate Possibilities (PAP)** says that a person is morally responsible for what he has done only if he could have done otherwise. Harry Frankfurt's 1969 paper "Alternate Possibilities and Moral Responsibility" mounted the most influential twentieth-century attack on this principle by constructing a counterexample: a counterfactual intervener (call him Black) stands ready to force Jones to do X if Jones shows any sign of choosing otherwise, but Black never has to act, because Jones decides on X entirely on his own. Jones could not have done otherwise—Black would have stopped him—yet Jones seems fully responsible, since the guarantee played no role in what he actually did. If the intuition holds, PAP is false. This article lays out the counterexample, the two leading replies (the *flicker of freedom* and the *Widerker–Kane dilemma defense*, both explained below), and John Martin Fischer's semicompatibilist alternative, before setting out the Map's position. That position is deliberately modest: Frankfurt's challenge is forceful and the actual-sequence response to it is respectable, so the Map does not claim to defeat Frankfurt. Instead it argues something weaker and more durable—that even if the counterexamples succeed against PAP, the Map's libertarianism can be recast in terms of *sourcehood* rather than alternatives, so "PAP is false" does not by itself vindicate [compatibilism](/concepts/compatibilism/) or unseat the Map.

## The Principle of Alternate Possibilities

PAP encodes an intuition most people share before they do any philosophy. We excuse the person who could not have acted otherwise—the one who was pushed, drugged, or physically compelled—precisely because the alternative was closed to him. Generalizing that thought yields Frankfurt's target: responsibility *requires* open alternatives. Frankfurt states it as the claim that "a person is morally responsible for what he has done only if he could have done otherwise."

PAP has been the connective tissue between two debates. It links moral responsibility to free will understood as *leeway*—the power to do more than one thing—and it makes the [Consequence Argument](/topics/the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism/) matter for ethics: if determinism removes leeway, and leeway is necessary for responsibility, then determinism removes responsibility. Frankfurt's move is to sever that first link. If responsibility does not require alternatives after all, then even a proof that determinism closes off alternatives would leave responsibility standing.

## Frankfurt's Counterexample

The counterexample has a fixed shape. An agent, Jones, is about to decide whether to do X. Unknown to him, Black—a neuroscientist, a demon, a device, the details do not matter—wants Jones to do X and has arranged to intervene if Jones is about to choose against it. Black is a superb monitor: he can read a prior sign of which way Jones is heading, and if that sign points the wrong way he will manipulate Jones's brain to guarantee the decision for X. But the sign points *toward* X. Jones deliberates and chooses X on his own, and Black, watching, does nothing.

Two things are now true at once. First, Jones could not have done otherwise—had he started to choose against X, Black would have forced his hand, so no alternative was genuinely available. Second, the factor that closed off the alternative played no part in what actually happened; Jones's decision ran entirely through his own reasoning, exactly as it would have with no Black present. Frankfurt's verdict is that Jones is morally responsible for doing X. If that verdict is correct, responsibility is present while alternatives are absent, and PAP is false as stated. Frankfurt does not leave the field bare: he proposes a replacement principle keyed to *why* an agent acted—a person is not responsible for an act if he did it *only because* he could not have done otherwise—which locates responsibility in the actual explanation of the deed rather than in the availability of alternatives.

## The Flicker of Freedom Reply

The first line of resistance concedes the structure and hunts for a surviving alternative. In the story as told, there is a difference between the world where Jones proceeds on his own and the world where Black intervenes: the prior sign. For Black to know whether to act, Jones must display *some* sign—a flicker of an incipient contrary choice, an involuntary blush, a neural precursor—and Jones retains a residual power over whether that sign occurs. So a sliver of alternative possibility survives: Jones could have shown the sign that would have triggered Black. PAP, the flicker theorist argues, is not yet refuted.

Fischer's reply is that such flickers are not *robust*. A robust alternative has to be something the agent has voluntary control over and that could ground a difference in responsibility—a full-blooded "he could have done otherwise," not a flicker the agent neither chooses nor could deliberately deploy. An involuntary precursor the agent cannot govern is too thin to carry the moral weight PAP assigns it. The exchange is genuinely open: recent work on "robust flickers" and on cases engineered to extinguish the flicker altogether keeps the robustness question live rather than settling it in either direction.

## The Widerker–Kane Dilemma Defense

The reply that matters most for the Map is the **dilemma defense**, developed by David Widerker and Robert Kane. It presses on the prior sign that Black must read, and poses a dilemma about the connection between that sign and Jones's eventual choice.

Take the first horn. Suppose the sign is *deterministically* connected to the choice—its presence guarantees that Jones will decide for X. Then to build the case, Frankfurt has assumed a deterministic link between antecedent state and decision. But whether an agent is responsible under such determination is exactly what is in dispute between the incompatibilist and everyone else. Helping oneself to Jones's responsibility here simply *assumes* that determination is compatible with responsibility, which begs the question against the libertarian the argument was supposed to refute.

Now the second horn. Suppose the sign is *not* deterministically connected to the choice—it makes Jones's deciding for X likely but leaves it genuinely open. Then up until the moment of decision Jones really could have gone the other way, and there was no sign reliable enough for Black to act on in time. A robust alternative survives after all, and the case fails to close the leeway PAP requires. Either way—so the defense concludes—no Frankfurt case both eliminates the alternative *and* avoids begging the question against the libertarian.

The defense provoked a wave of *indeterministic* Frankfurt-style cases—blockage and buffer constructions by Mele and Robb, Pereboom, Hunt, and Stump—each engineered to answer the indeterminism horn by closing the alternative while conceding that the prior link is not deterministic. Whether any of these fully succeeds is unresolved. It is important to be honest here: the dilemma defense is contested at its own foundations. Fischer has argued directly against it, and Widerker and Goetz replied under the banner "the defence prevails"—the very existence of that exchange is evidence that the defense is a live position rather than a settled result. The Map treats it as one respectable reply among several, not as a refutation of Frankfurt.

## Fischer's Semicompatibilism: The Forceful Rival

The most developed response that *accepts* Frankfurt's conclusion is John Martin Fischer's **semicompatibilism**. Fischer distinguishes **regulative control**—having genuinely open alternatives, the power to regulate which of several paths one takes—from **guidance control**—the actual-sequence condition that one's action issues from one's own, appropriately [reasons-responsive](/concepts/reasons-responsiveness/) mechanism. Frankfurt cases, on this reading, show that regulative control is inessential to responsibility; what responsibility tracks is guidance control, a feature of the actual sequence of events that produced the action.

The payoff is the "semi": *free will* in the alternatives sense may well be incompatible with determinism, yet *moral responsibility* is compatible with it, because responsibility supervenes on the actual sequence rather than on leeway. This is a strong, well-defended position, and the Map must grant it as such. It cannot be waved away as a verbal maneuver: Fischer and Ravizza's theory of guidance control is a detailed account of what makes an actual sequence the agent's own, and it does real explanatory work. Any honest treatment records that the actual-sequence view is a genuine rival, not a defeated one.

## Leeway or Source? Where the Debate Really Goes

Frankfurt cases are the fulcrum of a further distinction that reframes the whole dispute: [**leeway** versus **source** incompatibilism](/concepts/source-versus-leeway-incompatibilism/). Leeway incompatibilism—Frankfurt's target—says responsibility requires open alternatives, and that determinism defeats responsibility by closing them. Source incompatibilism (Pereboom, Stump, Zagzebski, and others) says what responsibility requires is that the agent be the ultimate *source* or originator of the action, and that determinism defeats responsibility not by removing alternatives but by making the agent's character and choices trace back to factors outside the agent. On the source view, an agent could lack alternatives and still be responsible, provided she is the genuine origin of what she does.

This is the pivot the Map most needs to register. If Frankfurt cases succeed, they refute *leeway* incompatibilism specifically. They leave source incompatibilism untouched—indeed the standard incompatibilist response to Frankfurt's success is to retreat from leeway to source. So the headline "PAP is false" does not deliver compatibilism about responsibility. A defender of desert-entailing responsibility who never needed alternatives in the first place—only ultimate sourcehood—can accept every Frankfurt case and lose nothing.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's [free-will position](/topics/free-will/) is agent-causal libertarian, and its first instinct is a leeway one: [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) and [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) have consciousness *selecting among genuinely open* physical outcomes, and [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) insists that the rejected alternatives are genuinely excluded rather than realized on other branches. That is a picture in which alternatives are doing serious work, so a successful refutation of PAP looks, at first, like a threat to the Map's motivation.

The Map's route through that threat has two parts, and it is important not to overstate either.

First, the dilemma defense gives the Map a reason to think its *leeway* commitment is not straightforwardly refuted. Because the Map is a genuine indeterminist about the moment of choice—the [quantum interface](/concepts/quantum-indeterminacy-free-will/) leaves the outcome open until consciousness selects—it reads the prior sign in a Frankfurt case as non-necessitating and lands on the indeterminism horn, where a robust alternative survives—so long as the indeterministic blockage-and-buffer constructions canvassed above (Mele–Robb, Pereboom, Hunt, Stump), engineered to close the alternative on exactly this horn, do not succeed, which is the unresolved question flagged earlier. Whether Frankfurt refutes PAP thus turns partly on prior commitments: a determinist reads the sign as fixing the choice; the Map's libertarian does not. This is a reason the Map's leeway route is not defeated. It is not a proof that Frankfurt is wrong, and the Map grants that Fischer, Widerker, and Goetz are still arguing about whether the defense finally prevails.

Second—and this is the load-bearing move—the Map does not have to win the Frankfurt debate to survive it. Even granting that the counterexamples succeed against leeway PAP, the Map's libertarianism can be recast as a **source** view. What the Map ultimately cares about is that the agent be the genuine originator of the selection: [agent causation](/concepts/agent-causation/) is precisely a thesis about sourcehood—the agent, not a prior chain of events, settles which open outcome becomes actual. If Frankfurt shows that responsibility does not strictly require alternatives, the Map can concede the point and still hold that responsibility requires ultimate sourcehood, which its interactionist interface is designed to supply. The tenets survive the translation: Minimal Quantum Interaction and Bidirectional Interaction describe an origination mechanism, and No Many Worlds still does its indexical work, since sourcehood presupposes that *this* agent actualized *this* outcome and not a counterpart's. So "PAP is false" is not fatal to the Map, because the Map's deepest commitment was to origination, and only derivatively to leeway.

Two honesty notes keep this calibrated. The retreat to source is a *retreat*, and a conditional one: it is the Map's worst-case fallback should Frankfurt defeat leeway, not its considered self-location, which remains *wide* source incompatibilism—sourcehood with genuine alternatives coming along with it, the leeway supplied by the [quantum interface](/concepts/quantum-indeterminacy-free-will/). Triggering the fallback costs more than the clean leeway *picture* in which open alternatives are what responsibility is *about*. Because the wide-source view holds that sourcehood itself brings alternatives with it, conceding that alternatives may not be required after all relocates the Map to *narrow* source—a distinct member of the [family](/concepts/source-versus-leeway-incompatibilism/), not a costless translation—and the fallback then inherits the burden, pressed hardest by the [manipulation argument](/topics/the-manipulation-argument-and-hard-incompatibilism/), of showing that an indeterministic interface delivers sourcehood rather than luck. And the actual-sequence rival remains standing: a semicompatibilist can grant everything the Map says about origination and still deny that origination must be non-physical. Consistent with [Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits), the Map does not treat the mere availability of the simpler compatibilist reading as decisive against its own; but neither does defeating leeway PAP convert the Map's bet into a demonstration. The position is that Frankfurt is a serious challenge the Map can take fully seriously without capitulating—because its libertarianism was never hostage to PAP alone.
