Libertarianism about free will is a genus with three species. All libertarians share two commitments—determinism is false, and some agents sometimes act freely and are morally responsible—and divide over a third question: what role does indeterminism play, and where does the freedom-grounding source of the action sit? The standard taxonomy, canonised in the Stanford Encyclopedia's "Incompatibilist (Nondeterministic) Theories of Free Will," sorts libertarians into **event-causal** (free actions are nondeterministically caused by agent-involving mental *events*, with no agent-as-substance cause added), **agent-causal** (the agent, an irreducible substance, is a cause over and above any event), and **non-causal** (free actions are basic and uncaused). This survey gives the event-causal wing—Robert Kane's self-forming actions above all—the detailed treatment it deserves, then follows the **luck** and **disappearing-agent** objection that bears hardest on it to locate the Map among the options.

The taxonomy is shared-ground philosophy of action. It carries no commitment to dualism, and nothing in the three-way carving favours the Map's view. The Map occupies one position within the taxonomy (broadly agent-causal, developed at [agent-causation](/concepts/agent-causation/)), and its claim that event-causal libertarianism succumbs to luck is a *framework-relative* argument, not a proof. Readers should meet all three species on their own terms before that location is drawn.

## The Shared Ground

Every libertarian holds that determinism is false and that free will is real. Where they part company is the answer to: *what is the ultimate source of a free action, and what work does indeterminism do?* One useful frame runs across the three answers. Event-causal libertarianism tries to secure free will from indeterminism *without* invoking agent-causation. Agent-causal libertarianism holds that indeterminism among events is not enough, so an irreducible agent-cause is required. Non-causal libertarianism holds that a free action needs no positive causal story at all.

The division cross-cuts the more familiar [source-versus-leeway distinction](/concepts/source-versus-leeway-incompatibilism/). Source and leeway concern *what freedom requires* (ultimate origination, or open alternatives); the event/agent/non-causal split concerns *what does the causing* at the moment freedom is exercised. A theorist fixes a position on both maps.

## Event-Causal Libertarianism

On the event-causal view, a free action is nondeterministically caused by agent-involving mental *events*—the agent's having certain desires, beliefs, reasons, and intentions. These accounts differ from event-causal *compatibilist* accounts only by adding that the relevant causation is indeterministic. Crucially, the agent is not an additional cause over and above these events; the agent's bringing about a choice reduces to states and events involving the agent. This is the reductive, broadly naturalist wing, and its proponents include Robert Kane, Laura Ekstrom, Alfred Mele (as a defended-but-not-endorsed model), Mark Balaguer, and Christopher Franklin.

### Kane's Self-Forming Actions

Kane's model, developed in *The Significance of Free Will* (Oxford University Press, 1996), locates the indeterminism at **self-forming actions** (SFAs)—the difficult, character-shaping moments when an agent is *torn* between competing visions of what to do or become: the moral course against ambition, present desire against a long-term goal. In these **torn decisions** the agent makes competing *efforts of will simultaneously*, and it is undetermined which effort succeeds. Because the indeterminism sits inside the agent's own effort, whichever way it resolves the outcome is something the agent willed and rationally endorsed.

Kane calls this **plural voluntary control**: the agent is such that, whichever option is chosen, the choice will be rational, voluntary, and the agent's own doing—endorsable either way. SFAs need not be frequent. Most actions flow deterministically from a character that earlier SFAs forged, so responsibility for ordinary actions traces back through the character to the undetermined self-forming ones. This is how Kane secures *sourcehood* on a purely event-causal budget: the agent is the ultimate source because the character doing the later determining was itself formed in undetermined efforts that were fully the agent's.

### Ekstrom's Coherentist Account

Laura Ekstrom's *Free Will: A Philosophical Study* (Westview Press, 2000) defends an indeterminist event-causal view built on a *coherence* theory of self-determined action, modelled on Keith Lehrer's coherence theory of knowledge. Preferences count as the agent's own when they cohere appropriately with the agent's evaluative system; free action requires indeterministic causation among events and appeals to agent causation only in a reducible sense. Ekstrom later refined the account to sharpen where the indeterminism must be located to preserve control without ceding it to chance.

### Balaguer's Naturalist Version

Mark Balaguer argues that whether we have libertarian free will reduces to an *open scientific question* about the causal histories of certain neural events. His "torn decisions" do not require Kane-style competing efforts of will; what matters is whether the neural process realising the decision is genuinely undetermined in the right way. On this reading libertarianism becomes an empirical hypothesis about the brain rather than a metaphysical posit, which distinguishes Balaguer's minimalism from Kane's richer psychology of effort.

### A Hybrid at the Seam

Kane's mature view is sometimes described as a hybrid: agent-involving events (reasons, efforts) are necessary but insufficient, leaving a role for the agent. Yet Kane resists positing an agent-*substance* cause; he wants the extra factor *without* agent-causation. Critics reply that this is exactly what cannot be had—the luck objection reappears precisely at the seam where the events run out and no substance-cause is admitted to take over.

## Agent-Causal Libertarianism

Agent-causal libertarians (Roderick Chisholm, Timothy O'Connor, E.J. Lowe, with Randolph Clarke as a guarded expositor) hold that a free action must be caused by the **agent as a substance**, not reducible to causation by prior events or mental states. Chisholm's founding distinction contrasts **transeunt** (event) causation with **immanent** (agent) causation; O'Connor's *Persons and Causes: The Metaphysics of Free Will* (Oxford University Press, 2000; ISBN 9780195133080) gives the leading modern defence, grounding agent-causal power in an emergent capacity of persons. Lowe's non-Cartesian substance dualism offers a substance-dualist version that avoids classical Cartesian liabilities. The Map develops this wing—and its own place in it—at [agent-causation](/concepts/agent-causation/) and does not re-derive that content here; what matters for the taxonomy is that this species answers luck by making the agent, rather than a prior event, the settler of the outcome.

## Non-Causal Libertarianism

Non-causal libertarians (Carl Ginet, Hugh McCann, Stewart Goetz) hold that free actions need not be caused by anything—neither by events nor by an agent-substance. Freedom is a *basic*, uncaused feature of action. Ginet grounds it in an "actish phenomenal quality," the intrinsic way it seems to the agent that she is making the action happen; McCann stresses irreducible intrinsic intentionality. This is the least populated wing, largely because it struggles with two demands: how the agent exercises *active control* (an event with the right "actish" feel could be induced by direct brain stimulation, with no genuine agency), and how an action is done *for a reason* when reason-explanation looks causal.

## The Luck and Disappearing-Agent Objection

The **luck objection** bears hardest on the event-causal wing, and understanding why is the pivot of the whole taxonomy. If, at the moment of choice, it was genuinely undetermined which way the agent would choose, then imagine rolling the universe back to that instant and replaying it. In some replays she chooses A, in others B, with the *same* prior events. What accounts for the difference between the replays? Nothing about the agent—the prior events were identical. So the difference looks like a matter of chance, and chance does not ground responsibility.

Derk Pereboom's **disappearing-agent** formulation sharpens this into a general objection to *all* event-causal libertarianism. With the causal role of the antecedent events already given and yet the outcome undetermined, *nothing settles* whether the decision occurs—so the agent does not settle it. The agent "disappears" from the moment of settling. The objection bites the event-causal picture hardest because that picture, by design, admits no cause beyond the events; when the events leave the outcome open, the theory's own reductive commitment guarantees there is nothing left to do the settling.

Kane replies on two fronts. First, in a genuinely indeterminate effort of will, "exact sameness" across rollback worlds may not be well-defined, so the thought experiment can be accused of smuggling in a determinacy the model denies. Second, by plural rationality: whichever way the torn decision resolves, it issues from the agent's own effort and is rationally endorsable, so it is a willed self-formation rather than mere chance. Critics (Clarke, Mele) grant that this shows the outcome is *voluntary* either way, but press that it does not show the agent *controls which* way it goes—the "which" is still unsettled, and that residue is the luck. The Map's own reply to luck—consciousness *selecting* the outcome rather than receiving it—is developed in full at [luck-objection](/concepts/luck-objection/) and is not repeated here; what the taxonomy adds is the map of *which* libertarian species the objection targets and why.

## The Intelligibility Objection

Agent-causal libertarianism answers luck by making the agent the settler, but it pays for the answer with the **intelligibility objection**: can causation *by a substance*, rather than by an event, be made coherent at all? The orthodox view is that causation relates events; an enduring substance seems the wrong kind of relatum, and critics ask how an unchanging agent produces a change *at a particular time* without a triggering event—and if there is a triggering event, event-causation is back. Defenders reply with neo-Aristotelian substance causation as ontologically fundamental (Lowe, O'Connor, Jacobs and O'Connor) and with causal pluralism, on which events cause things only as changes *in* substances. Each wing, then, buys an answer to one objection at the cost of exposure to another: event-causal views are naturalistically respectable but vulnerable to luck; agent-causal views defeat luck but owe an account of substance causation.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map locates itself in the **agent-causal** wing, and reads event-causal libertarianism as its principal *rival within libertarianism*. The two agree that determinism is false and free will real; they diverge over whether any irreducible agent-cause is needed. The Map's considered view is that the event-causal attempt to get free will out of indeterminism *without* agent-causation succumbs to the luck objection—the undetermined event is not settled by anyone—and that agent-causation is required to close the gap. This is the Map's distinctive move, and it should be read as a framework-relative judgement rather than a demonstration: the taxonomy is neutral, and the strongest event-causal replies (Kane's indeterminate efforts, plural rationality) remain live for those who weight the naturalist commitments more heavily than the Map does.

**Tenet 1 (Dualism)** enters at the agent-causal level, not at the taxonomy. As [agent-causation](/concepts/agent-causation/) records, the [Dualism tenet](/tenets/#dualism) is itself neutral between substance and property dualism; the substance-leaning that agent-causation needs is a commitment of the Map's framework, and a reader who accepts the tenet but rejects substance-bearing agents parts company at the agent-causal step. The event-causal wing is, from the Map's side, what dualism-neutral libertarianism looks like when it declines that step.

**Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)** is why the Map cannot settle for the event-causal or non-causal wings. If consciousness makes a real causal difference to the physical world, the agent must be the *origin* of that difference. Non-causal libertarianism, by denying that the agent causes the action, gives the Map no causal purchase at all; event-causal libertarianism supplies causation but, on the Map's reading, leaves the crucial outcome unsettled by the agent. The Map's [quantum interface](/concepts/quantum-indeterminacy-free-will/) is the concrete proposal for *where and how* an agent-cause makes contact with the physical: a conscious selection at the interface is the agent's own settling of an otherwise-undetermined outcome, which is precisely the settling the disappearing-agent objection finds missing in the event-causal picture. This gives the Map a candidate answer to the intelligibility objection too—it says where substance causation touches physics—though that answer is a hypothesis, not a proof.

**Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)** governs how the Map holds the position against Pereboom. The claim that indeterministic sourcehood is "mere luck" is a plausibility-and-parsimony judgement rather than a theorem; Tenet 5 lets the Map decline to treat it as decisive—while conceding, symmetrically, that declining buys the Map no positive support. The Map adopts the agent-causal reading on independent grounds (its coherence with the other tenets), and the taxonomy's role is to name its neighbours and rivals, not to establish that its condition is met.
