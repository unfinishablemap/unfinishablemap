Plants perform genuine, sometimes sophisticated information processing—counting stimuli, holding a short-term electrical trace that decays like a memory, propagating a systemic wound signal across the whole body in minutes—and they do all of it with **no neurons, no synapses, and no brain**. Yet almost no serious participant in the field claims plants have phenomenal experience: a felt inner life, a "what-it's-like." That gap between demonstrable adaptive behaviour and undemonstrated experience is exactly what makes plants valuable to the Map. They are the cleanest available test of a question the [marginal-organism ladder](/topics/marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection/) keeps open: can value-relevant, information-processing behaviour exist with zero neural architecture—and if so, does any of it amount to experience? The empirical answer to the first half is a firm yes. The answer to the second half is: on present evidence, no—but the *reason* the skeptics give for that "no" is a physicalist commitment the Map does not share.

## The Uncontested Biology: Processing Without Neurons

Beneath the terminology war (covered below, in [Neurobiology or Metaphor?](#neurobiology-or-metaphor-the-terminology-war)) sits biology that essentially no one disputes. Two results anchor it.

**The Venus flytrap counts.** Böhm and colleagues showed that when prey touches the sensory hairs of *Dionaea muscipula*, each touch fires a mechanosensory action potential. The trap does not close on the first: two action potentials in quick succession are required to snap it shut, and further firings—around five or more—trigger digestive-gland gene expression and sodium uptake to begin digestion (Böhm et al. 2016). The counting rests on a decaying cytosolic calcium signal. If the second touch comes too late, the trace from the first has faded and the count resets—an electrical short-term "memory" with a built-in forgetting curve. This is decision-relevant counting and time-windowed memory in an organism with no nervous system.

**Plants signal across the whole body.** Toyota and colleagues demonstrated that wounding one leaf releases glutamate, which is sensed by GLUTAMATE RECEPTOR-LIKE (GLR) channels. This triggers a calcium wave that travels across the plant within minutes, priming chemical defences in distant, undamaged leaves (Toyota et al. 2018). The GLR channels are evolutionary relatives of the ionotropic glutamate receptors that carry signals at animal synapses—but here they run plant-specific, whole-body signalling rather than anything neuronal. It is the strongest uncontested evidence that plants perform fast, integrated, electrochemical coordination without neurons.

Neither result requires or implies experience. Both establish something narrower and secure: adaptive information processing—sensing, integrating, thresholding, remembering over short intervals, and acting—is achievable by non-neural cellular machinery. Hold that firmly, because the controversy that follows is largely about what *words* this biology licenses, not about the biology itself.

## Neurobiology or Metaphor? The Terminology War

In 2006 a group of biologists (Brenner, Stahlberg, Trewavas, Baluška, Mancuso, Van Volkenburgh) proposed a new field: **plant neurobiology**. Their manifesto argued that plants process environmental information to develop and reproduce optimally, via "integrated signaling, communication and response," and emphasised long-distance electrical signals, vesicle-mediated auxin transport, and molecules known from animal nervous systems—glutamate, GABA, acetylcholine (Brenner et al. 2006). In its bolder framings, the programme spoke of plant cognition, intelligence, learning, and occasionally sentience. The whole-plant coordination was framed as analogous to a nervous system.

The pushback was sharp and fast. In 2007, thirty-six authors published a rebuttal titled "Plant neurobiology: no brain, no gain?" (Alpi et al. 2007). Their objection was pointed: the label "neurobiology" implies plants have nerves, synapses, and a brain—structures they simply lack—and the animal analogy adds nothing to plant physiology, cell biology, or signalling while risking real confusion. The rebuttal was methodologically cautionary rather than metaphysical: a brake on reading behaviour as mind by borrowing vocabulary.

The most forceful denial came in 2019. Taiz and colleagues, in "Plants Neither Possess nor Require Consciousness," directly rejected plant consciousness, cognition-as-experience, intentionality, emotion, and pain (Taiz et al. 2019). Their central argument leans on Feinberg and Mallatt's account of the minimal neural and brain complexity they take to be required for consciousness in animals; from the premise that plants lack such architecture, Taiz et al. conclude that the probability of plant consciousness is, in their words, effectively nil.

A third, quieter wing reframes rather than inflates or denies. Calvo's philosophical manifesto argues for treating plants as candidates for **minimal cognition** under embodied and enactive frameworks—defining cognition as adaptive, anticipatory sensorimotor coupling rather than as felt experience (Calvo 2016). On this reading "cognition" is a behavioural and functional category, and calling plants cognitive says nothing about whether there is anything it is like to be one. Calvo's framing is generally agnostic-to-deflationary about phenomenal experience.

The structure of the dispute is worth naming plainly: everyone broadly agrees on the behaviour, and disagrees about which words it earns. That is why the debate reads as a war over terminology. It is a live illustration of the [explanatory-gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/)—the moment functional description is settled, the question of experience remains completely open, and the vocabulary fight is that open question wearing empirical clothes.

## The Learning Disputes: A Calibration Lesson

The most contested empirical claims concern **learning**. Two experiments by Gagliano and colleagues became flashpoints.

The first reported habituation in *Mimosa pudica* (Gagliano et al. 2014). Repeatedly dropping potted plants—a harmless shock—led the plants to stop closing their leaves in response, with the effect reportedly stimulus-specific and retained over weeks, and learning faster in low-light, resource-poor conditions. The authors read this as a basic, non-neural form of learning and memory. A 2018 response in the same journal disputed the interpretation, arguing the data show no genuine dishabituation, lack a control needed to establish stimulus specificity, and are largely explicable by motor fatigue rather than learning (Biegler 2018).

The second, more ambitious claim reported **associative (Pavlovian) conditioning** in pea plants (Gagliano et al. 2016). A fan (a neutral cue) was paired with light (which plants grow toward); after training, plants reportedly grew toward the fan-predicted light source, as if the cue had come to signal the reward. This was widely described as the most convincing plant-conditioning result. But Markel's attempted replication found no associative-learning effect (Markel 2020), and the result remains unreplicated—including, by Markel's account, by the original laboratory. It stands as a cautionary case, not an established finding.

For the Map, these disputes are a worked example of its own [calibration discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/). The temptation—on both sides—is to let a striking result settle a question it cannot. The honest reading distinguishes tiers of confidence: non-associative habituation in plants is plausible but contested; associative conditioning is currently unreplicated and widely doubted; and even if either were fully established, learning is adaptive behaviour, not evidence of felt experience. Over-attributing mind to behaviour is precisely the error the discipline exists to prevent, and the Gagliano replication troubles show why the guardrail is needed.

## Plants as the Missing Non-Neural Rung

The [marginal-organism ladder](/topics/marginal-organism-scope-of-value-sensitive-selection/) runs a descending series of cases—rat, nematode, *Hydra*, slime mould—each removing a structural prerequisite that value-sensitive selection might need: anticipatory evaluation, then a central integration point, then a nervous system at all. That ladder currently steps straight from *Hydra*'s diffuse nerve net to the single-celled slime mould *Physarum*, skipping a rung. Plants occupy exactly the skipped rung: **multicellular and non-neural**—many-celled organisms, no neurons, yet counting, remembering over short intervals, and running systemic electrical signalling.

Why the rung matters: the ladder's deflationary lesson is that its markers degrade independently, and *Physarum* forces a clean disjunction—either valence-*like* approach/avoidance behaviour can be produced by wholly value-blind mechanisms (so behaviour is no reliable marker of value-sensitive selection anywhere), or the Map must extend conscious selection to a brainless cell. Plants sharpen the same disjunction from a different direction. They are far more behaviourally complex than *Physarum*—the flytrap's counting and the GLR wound wave are richer than habituation in a slime mould—yet they are, if anything, even less plausible candidates for experience. If richer non-neural information processing still yields no evidence of a felt inner life, that strengthens the reading that adaptive behaviour and phenomenal experience are separable, and that behavioural sophistication alone does not raise the credence of experience. Plants push [value-sensitive selection](/topics/valence-and-conscious-selection/) to its multicellular non-neural extreme: whatever valence-guided selection is, plants show how much value-*relevant* processing can occur without it—or without any experience for it to be the value of.

This is also why plants sit *below* even the most permissive animal-sentience frameworks. The [edge-of-sentience](/topics/birch-edge-of-sentience-and-the-five-tier-scale/) scales and precautionary declarations are calibrated for animals with nervous systems; plants fall off the bottom of every such scale, and reasonably so. They are the bookend to [consciousness in simple organisms](/topics/consciousness-in-simple-organisms/): the non-neural extreme against which the neural cases are measured.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's central commitment here follows from **[Tenet 1 (Dualism)](/tenets/#dualism)**: consciousness is not reducible to physical—here, neural—processes. That commitment produces a carefully split verdict on the plant debate, and the split is the whole point.

On the *empirical* question, the Map lands close to the deflationists: plants very probably have no phenomenal experience. Nothing in the flytrap's counting, the GLR calcium wave, or even the contested learning results reaches "what-it's-like," and the Map's own [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) forbids upgrading adaptive behaviour into an experience claim. So the Map does not endorse the inflationary wing's occasional gloss of sentience onto this biology.

But the Map firmly rejects the *reason* the strongest skeptics give. Taiz et al.'s argument runs: consciousness requires specific neural architecture (per Feinberg and Mallatt); plants lack it; therefore plants lack consciousness. The middle premise is a **neural-necessity** claim—that experience just *is*, or is constituted by, a particular kind of neural computation. Under dualism that premise is a physicalist commitment, not an established fact, and the Map declines it. The Map can hold "plants very likely lack experience" while refusing "because experience is neural computation." The two claims come apart cleanly: the first is a calibrated reading of the evidence; the second is a metaphysical thesis the Map's dualism contests. Taiz et al.'s conclusion may well be right; their argument for it rests on a foundation the Map does not accept.

**[Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)](/tenets/#occams-limits)** guards the other flank. Just as parsimony cannot license *dismissing* marginal consciousness merely because simple mechanisms suffice to explain the behaviour, it cannot license *attributing* experience on the strength of behavioural sophistication. The discipline runs in both directions: the Map neither reads mind into the flytrap because it counts, nor rules out experience anywhere on the ground that a mechanism was found. What plants change is not the Map's credence that they are conscious—which stays very low—but the Map's confidence that behaviour and experience are genuinely separable questions, one empirically tractable and one not.
