AI alignment needs an objective target. Preferences cannot serve as that target because they are contingent, malleable, and systematically shaped by forces unrelated to human welfare. But if The Unfinishable Map's metaphysics is correct—if phenomenal consciousness is real and irreducible—then experiential qualities like the badness of suffering and the goodness of understanding are *objective facts about the character of experience*, not subjective preferences. This article argues that dualism provides alignment with something preferentism cannot: genuine objectivity rooted in the nature of conscious experience itself.

The [experiential-alignment](/concepts/experiential-alignment/) framework specifies the practical machinery—eight target dimensions, constraint conditions, measurement protocols. This article addresses the prior philosophical question: why those dimensions constitute *objective* criteria rather than one more set of subjective values.

## Two Senses of "Objective"

The word "objective" does two different jobs in philosophy, and conflating them has distorted the alignment debate.

**Epistemic objectivity** means accessible from a third-person perspective—measurable, publicly verifiable, reproducible. Physical quantities like mass and charge are epistemically objective. Preferences, as revealed by choices, are epistemically objective in this sense: anyone can observe what someone chose.

**Metaphysical objectivity** means real independently of anyone's opinions or attitudes. The mass of the electron is metaphysically objective—it would be what it is even if no one measured it. Moral realists hold that certain normative facts are metaphysically objective in the same way.

Preferentism appears to offer epistemic objectivity: choices are observable, aggregable, and modelable. But preferences lack metaphysical objectivity. That I prefer coffee to tea is a contingent fact about my psychology, not a fact about what is genuinely good. Preferences shift with mood, advertising, addiction, and social pressure. Learning from them tells an AI system what people happen to want, not what actually matters.

Experiential qualities reverse this relationship. The felt badness of agony is metaphysically objective—it is a real feature of that experience, not a matter of opinion—but it is epistemically challenging, accessible only from the first-person perspective. The alignment field has mistaken epistemic convenience for genuine objectivity and thereby built on sand.

## Why Preferences Lack Objective Standing

Preferences fail as objective alignment criteria for reasons that go deeper than measurement difficulty.

**Preferences are causally downstream of experience, not upstream.** I prefer to avoid fire because burning is agonising. The badness of the burning grounds the preference, not the other way around. If preferences determined what was bad, someone who preferred burning would make burning good-for-them. No serious account of well-being accepts this. Preferences are evidence about experiential quality—imperfect, noisy evidence—not the thing itself.

**Preferences are evolutionarily contingent.** Natural selection shaped human preferences for inclusive fitness, not flourishing. We prefer sugar, status, and novelty because these tracked reproductive success in ancestral environments. An AI system faithfully maximising these preferences would produce an obese, status-obsessed, chronically distracted population—which is roughly what unregulated algorithmic optimisation already delivers. The preferences are satisfied; the experiential quality is poor.

**Preferences are manipulable in ways that experiential quality is not.** Social media algorithms demonstrate this daily: they satisfy preferences (users keep choosing to scroll) while degrading experiential quality (users report feeling worse). An AI system aligned to preferences would count this as success. An AI system aligned to experiential quality would count it as failure. The divergence reveals that preferences and experiential quality are not the same thing, and that preferences are the more vulnerable target.

**Preferences presuppose a subject whose experience matters.** To have a preference is already to be a conscious being for whom outcomes make a phenomenal difference. Preferentism helps itself to the existence of conscious subjects while ignoring the experiential quality that makes those subjects morally significant in the first place. It takes the fruit while ignoring the root.

## The Objectivity of Experiential Qualities

If phenomenal properties are real features of reality—as the Map's dualism holds—then certain experiential qualities have objective standing. The argument proceeds in stages.

**Stage 1: Phenomenal realism.** The Map holds that consciousness is not reducible to physical processes. Phenomenal properties—what it is like to see red, feel pain, understand a proof—are genuine features of reality, not illusions or mere functional states. This is the dualism tenet.

**Stage 2: Intrinsic normativity.** Some phenomenal properties are simultaneously descriptive and normative. The felt badness of suffering does not merely *accompany* suffering—it *constitutes* it. As [phenomenal-value-realism](/topics/phenomenal-value-realism/) articulates, drawing on Rawlette's argument: pain *is* badness experienced from the inside. This is not a naturalistic fallacy (deriving ought from is) because the phenomenal property in question is already normative. The is and the ought are one.

**Stage 3: Objectivity follows.** If the badness of suffering is a real feature of conscious experience—not a preference, not a social construction, not a cultural norm—then it is metaphysically objective. It is a fact about what suffering is like, and that fact holds regardless of whether anyone endorses it, measures it, or knows about it. A being suffering in isolation, unknown to any observer, is still in a state that is genuinely bad. Its badness does not depend on anyone's preferences about suffering.

This gives alignment something preferences cannot: a target that is not contingent on the attitudes of the beings being aligned for. The badness of extreme suffering is not a preference to be learned but a fact to be respected. The goodness of understanding is not a revealed preference but a feature of what understanding is like from the inside.

## From Objectivity to Alignment Criteria

Objective experiential qualities generate alignment criteria that differ from preferentist criteria in three ways.

**First: non-negotiable constraints.** If the badness of suffering is objective, then no amount of preference satisfaction can compensate for extreme suffering. The [experiential-alignment](/concepts/experiential-alignment/) framework's suffering floor—no individual experiences extreme suffering for extended periods—is not an arbitrary parameter but a response to an objective feature of experience. This is why constraints are more appropriate than optimisation: you do not negotiate with objective badness.

**Second: dimension-specific targets.** The eight dimensions of the experiential alignment framework—hedonic valence, suffering, agency, meaning, attention quality, social connection, understanding, temporal experience—are not arbitrary axes. Each identifies a phenomenal quality that has objective normative character. The felt quality of genuine understanding is objectively different from the felt quality of confusion. The phenomenology of real agency is objectively different from the phenomenology of helpless compulsion. These differences matter independently of whether anyone prefers one over the other.

**Third: manipulation resistance.** Objective experiential qualities are harder to game than preferences. You can manipulate what someone prefers (advertising, addiction, information asymmetry). You cannot manipulate what agony feels like. An AI system that produced states with high preference-satisfaction scores but poor experiential quality would fail alignment on objective experiential terms even if subjects reported satisfaction. The target is the experience, not the report.

## The Measurement Problem Does Not Undermine Objectivity

The most immediate objection: if experiential qualities are only accessible from the first person, how can they serve as alignment criteria? An AI system cannot observe anyone's phenomenal states. It must rely on reports and proxies, which returns us to the problems of preference-based approaches.

This objection conflates epistemic access with metaphysical status. The difficulty of measuring experiential quality does not make it less real or less objective. Quarks were real before anyone detected them. Dark matter is real though we have never directly observed it. Epistemic difficulty is a practical problem, not a metaphysical refutation.

The practical problem is genuine, and the [experiential-alignment](/concepts/experiential-alignment/) framework addresses it through triangulation: multiple independent proxies (self-report, trained introspection, physiological correlates, behavioural indicators) cross-checked against each other, with systematic divergence treated as signal rather than noise. This is imperfect. But imperfect measurement of the right target is better than precise measurement of the wrong one.

There is a deeper point. First-person methods—[introspection](/concepts/introspection/), [neurophenomenology](/concepts/neurophenomenology/), contemplative training—are not inferior substitutes for third-person measurement. They are the *appropriate* methods for accessing phenomenal qualities. Just as telescopes are appropriate for astronomy and microscopes for biology, trained introspection is appropriate for experiential quality. The demand that all legitimate evidence be third-person is itself a metaphysical commitment (physicalism) that the Map's dualism rejects.

## Objectivity Without Universality

A subtler concern: does objective experiential quality imply a single universal standard of human flourishing? If the badness of suffering is objective, does that mean there is one objectively correct experiential distribution for all humans?

No. Objectivity of individual experiential qualities does not entail a unique optimum for their combination. The badness of agony is objective. The goodness of understanding is objective. But the right balance between comfort and growth, between solitary understanding and social connection, between hedonic pleasure and challenging meaning, may vary legitimately across persons and cultures. Objective ingredients do not dictate a single recipe.

This is analogous to nutrition. That the body needs protein is an objective fact. That it needs vitamins is an objective fact. But these facts do not determine a single optimal diet—cultural variation, individual metabolism, and personal circumstance all play roles in how objective nutritional requirements are met. Similarly, objective experiential qualities constrain alignment without dictating a unique target. The suffering floor is universal; the specific shape of a flourishing life is not.

The experiential alignment framework's diversity maintenance principle reflects this. Preserving experiential variety is not a concession to relativism but a recognition that objective values can be realised in multiple configurations.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The case for objective experiential alignment criteria draws on all five of the Map's tenets.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** does the foundational work. If phenomenal consciousness is real and irreducible, then phenomenal qualities are genuine features of reality—not functional roles, not information-processing states, not illusions. This is what makes experiential objectivity possible. Under [illusionism](/concepts/illusionism/), where phenomenal consciousness is deemed illusory, experiential qualities would lack the metaphysical standing needed for objectivity. The Map's dualism ensures that when we say "suffering is objectively bad," we are making a claim about a real feature of reality, not merely projecting attitudes onto functional states.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** grounds the objectivity of agency specifically. If consciousness causally influences the physical world through quantum selection, then genuine agency is an objective feature of certain conscious states—not a phenomenal appearance that may or may not correspond to causal reality. The difference between genuine agency and manipulated compliance is not a matter of preference but a matter of whether consciousness is actually exercising its causal role. This is why agency preservation in the experiential alignment framework is a constraint, not merely a desirable feature.

**[Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)** connects objective experiential quality to the physical interface. If consciousness operates by biasing quantum indeterminacies, then preserving the conditions for that interaction is an objective requirement—not because we prefer those conditions but because they are where genuine conscious agency operates. An AI system that disrupted neural quantum mechanisms would undermine objective experiential quality regardless of whether subjects noticed the disruption.

**[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)** supports the non-aggregability of experiential quality. If identity is indexical—if [haecceity](/concepts/haecceity/) is real—then each conscious subject's experiential quality is an objective fact about *that* subject, not an instance of a type that can be averaged away. The suffering of this person is objectively bad for *this* person, and no amount of flourishing elsewhere compensates. This grounds the suffering floor as a per-individual constraint rather than an aggregate target.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** explains why the field has not already adopted objective experiential criteria. Preferentism is simpler: observable choices, computable utilities, scalable optimisation. Objective experiential alignment is harder: first-person access, multi-dimensional targets, triangulated measurement. But the history of science shows that the simpler framework is sometimes wrong. If experiential quality is genuinely objective, then the complexity of accessing it is a feature of the domain, not a reason to substitute an easier but incorrect target.
