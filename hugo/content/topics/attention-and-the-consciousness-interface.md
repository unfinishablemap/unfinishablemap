Attention and motor planning share neural substrates. The premotor theory of attention demonstrates they use the same fronto-parietal networks—directing attention *is* preparing to act. The Unfinishable Map argues this convergence transforms the mind-body problem: if consciousness influences matter through attention (the [attention-as-interface hypothesis](/concepts/attention-as-interface/)), and attention is motor planning, then a single interface connects mind to world. Consciousness selects what to perceive and what to do through the same mechanism, at the same neural sites, on the same timescale. Empirical dissociations show attention and consciousness come apart—related but distinct. Clinical cases from ADHD to OCD reveal what happens when specific interface components are compromised. The leading materialist alternative, Attention Schema Theory, explains reports about consciousness without explaining consciousness itself.

## The Convergence of Attention and Motor Planning

Giacomo Rizzolatti's premotor theory of attention (PMTA) holds that selective attention and movement planning use the same neural substrates. Preparing an eye movement to a location improves visual processing there; microstimulation of motor regions enhances processing at the target. Wise and Murray (2022) extended PMTA, arguing "the entirety of the prefrontal cortex can be seen as fundamentally premotor in nature"—executive function is motor preparation at varying levels of abstraction.

Three signatures distinguish willed from automatic processing in both domains: frontal theta oscillations (present for willed attention, absent for instructed; Rajan et al. 2019), bidirectional theta-band coherence between frontal and parietal regions, and matching commitment timing (~300ms for willed attention, ~280ms before movement; Thura & Cisek 2014). If attention and motor selection were independent systems, this convergence would be coincidental.

Traditional philosophy of mind treats perception and action as separate puzzles. Because attention is motor planning, selecting what to perceive is already selecting what to do about it—one interface, not two.

## The Competition-Selection Architecture

Paul Cisek's research demonstrates that the motor system represents multiple options simultaneously—in premotor cortex, 43% of task-related neurons fired for either of two potential reach targets before selection occurred. Competing representations accumulate support until one reaches threshold; the basal ganglia tonically inhibit motor programs, and selection means releasing the brake on one option.

The [trilemma of selection](/topics/trilemma-of-selection/) is exhaustive: determinism, randomness, or mental causation—only the third preserves authorship. The Map proposes consciousness biases which option wins at the threshold crossing moment, satisfying [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction).

A 2024 *Nature* study (Cai et al.) reveals a striking dissociation: dopamine-deficient mice retain motor capacity but lose goal-directed initiative—they "can move" but "won't move." Cai et al. establish two layers: cortical option generation, preserved without dopamine, and [dopamine](/topics/dopamine-and-the-unified-interface/)-modulated salience and selection threshold. Berridge's (2007) "wanting vs. liking" distinction reinforces the second layer—dopamine mediates incentive salience, not hedonic pleasure. The Map adds a third layer the paper does not test: that consciousness biases which salient option crosses threshold once dopamine has weighted the alternatives. The first two layers are licensed by the empirical work; the third is the Map's interpretation, beyond what Cai et al. set out to show. Parkinson's patients move when externally prompted but struggle with self-initiated movement—interface hardware degraded, not the consciousness that uses it.

## The Attention-Consciousness Dissociation

Attention and consciousness can come apart. [blindsight](/concepts/blindsight/) patients orient toward stimuli they deny seeing—attention without awareness. Peripheral vision delivers conscious experience without focal attention—awareness without attending. Koch and Tsuchiya's (2007) framework organises these into four cases:

| Attention | Consciousness | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Present | Present | Normal focused awareness |
| Absent | Absent | Dreamless sleep |
| Present | Absent | Blindsight, masked priming |
| Absent | Present | Peripheral awareness, gist perception |

If consciousness were identical to attention, these cases would be incoherent. Nartker et al. (2025, *eLife*) found that inattentionally blind participants retain visual sensitivity to stimuli they deny noticing. A complementary dissociation runs the other way: consciousness reports *being focused* and *being distracted*, but the transit between them—the instant attention is captured—is unavailable at the moment-of-capture level, even where retrospective reconstruction is sometimes available (the [transit-void](/voids/transit-void/)).

Ned Block's overflow argument sharpens the picture. In iconic memory experiments, subjects report seeing all 12 letters in a brief flash but can name only 3-4. If consciousness overflows what attention captures, its scope exceeds any functional mechanism—creating problems for [functionalist](/concepts/functionalism/) theories. Kim and Chong (2024) propose visual experience is "not rich, but not sparse"; the question remains open.

The dissociation constrains what consciousness can be. Not identical to attention—blindsight shows attention operating unconsciously. Not identical to access—peripheral awareness suggests conscious content without global broadcast. And the explanatory gap remains: the 2025 COGITATE adversarial collaboration (Melloni et al., *Nature*) found neither Global Workspace Theory nor Integrated Information Theory was vindicated, with conscious content tracking posterior cortex rather than prefrontal regions.

The Map's [attention-as-interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/) hypothesis proposes attention is how consciousness *engages* with matter, not what consciousness *is*. The interface model treats dissociation as expected—a driver and their steering wheel are separable.

## Why Attention Schema Theory Fails

Michael Graziano's Attention Schema Theory (AST) proposes consciousness is the brain's simplified model of its own attention. The framework captures a genuine insight—the brain does construct internal models—but stipulates rather than explains the move that does the heavy lifting: that the schema's representation of attention as phenomenal *just is* phenomenality. By the materialist standard the eliminativist programme prides itself on—specify the mechanism, do not stipulate the upshot—this is a foundational move AST has not earned. A self-representing system can describe its own state without that description being felt: a thermostat tracks temperature without feeling hot. AST's reply that the relevant difference is *recursive* self-modelling redescribes the explanandum rather than discharging it; the bridge from recursive representation to felt quality is exactly what wants explaining. Raymond Tallis's slogan "misrepresentation presupposes presentation" presses this point without requiring an experiencer to be smuggled in: the question is why a representational system would generate content that even *seems* phenomenal, given that self-modelling in non-conscious systems generates no such seeming.

Where AST and the Map disagree at bedrock is on whether the question "what experiences this?" is coherent at all. AST treats it as a confused reflex generated by the illusion the theory explains; the Map takes phenomenal experience as ontologically primary. The catalogue notes this disagreement as running at the framework's edge rather than dressing it as in-AST refutation. The bridge problem above does not run at the edge: even granting no experiencer separate from the schematising process, AST owes a mechanistic specification of why recursive schematising produces apparent phenomenality when ordinary self-representation does not.

[Mental effort](/concepts/mental-effort/) sharpens this. If effort is the schema representing attention as costly, AST owes the bridge from an energy-expenditure signal to a felt cost rather than a merely tracked metric. The distinctive neural signatures of willed versus instructed attention are similarly unexplained, since the schema tracks attention regardless of initiation source. AST explains access consciousness well; what remains is the access-to-phenomenal gap, marked here as a gap rather than treated as if tenet-incompatibility had closed it.

## Clinical Evidence: What Disorders Reveal

Attention disorders are natural experiments. Each disrupts a specific component of the attentional system while leaving others intact. If attention is the interface through which consciousness engages with the brain, the disorders should produce characteristic signatures: impaired *engagement* with specific content while consciousness persists behind the degraded channel.

### ADHD: Threshold Without Capacity Loss

ADHD disrupts voluntary sustained attention while leaving—and sometimes enhancing—captured attention. People with ADHD can become absorbed in intrinsically motivating tasks with intensity exceeding neurotypical engagement, even as they struggle with tasks lacking immediate reward. Volkow et al. (2009) found reduced dopamine receptor availability in the reward pathway; stimulants restore function by modulating decision thresholds rather than generating attentional capacity. Stimulant-treated patients report not altered consciousness but that their intentions translate more reliably into action (Barkley 2015). The integration/access materialist can accommodate this: what is impaired is threshold-modulation, not the integrative machinery that makes content conscious. The interface model agrees on the locus and reads the patient's report differently—friction at the interface (intentions failing to translate into action), not thinned-out experience. AST has a parallel resource available (the schema's threshold-modulation function is impaired, not the schema itself); the catalogue does not claim the disorder refutes either materialist position, only that it maps cleanly onto disruption of the middle (dopamine-modulated) layer of the three-layer architecture.

### Hemispatial Neglect: Erasure Without Remainder

Hemispatial neglect, typically following right parietal damage, eliminates awareness of the left side of space. Patients do not perceive a gap—the left side simply ceases to exist for them. Marshall and Halligan's (1988) landmark case showed neglected information can still influence behaviour: a patient who denied seeing any difference between two houses (one on fire on the left) consistently chose the non-burning house as preferable. Dualism must explain how consciousness persists *as consciousness* when an entire spatial dimension is erased. The integration/access materialist owes a different account: their position is that only integrated or accessed processing constitutes awareness, not processing as such. The challenge is then to specify why integration cleaves to the parietal mechanism so cleanly that processing which still discriminates the fire fails to enter the integrated channel at all—a descriptive vocabulary the materialist position has, but a principled account of why integration follows lesion topography this precisely is not yet in.

### Balint Syndrome: Sequential Awareness Only

Balint syndrome, caused by bilateral parietal damage, restricts attention to a single object at a time—*simultanagnosia*. A patient can describe a fork or a knife in detail but cannot perceive a place setting. Coslett and Saffran (1991) showed Balint patients correctly classify scenes they cannot consciously perceive as multi-object displays. Robertson (2004) demonstrated that binding features requires spatial attention: without it, colour and shape "float free," producing illusory conjunctions. The unity of normal visual experience depends on the parietal mechanisms Balint destroys.

### Extinction: Competition Reveals Architecture

A patient with extinction can detect a single stimulus on either side, but when two stimuli appear simultaneously, the contralesional stimulus is extinguished from awareness. Driver and Vuilleumier (2001) demonstrated that extinguished stimuli still activate early visual cortex and prime subsequent responses—information reaches the brain but loses the competition for conscious access. Consciousness does not passively receive all available information; it operates through a [bandwidth-limited](/concepts/interface-friction/) selection mechanism whose capacity tracks the lesion.

### The Phenomenological Kaleidoscope

| Disorder | Component Disrupted | Consciousness | Implicit Processing |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| ADHD | Voluntary threshold modulation | Preserved, vivid | Intact |
| Neglect | Spatial representation access | Half-field erased | Preserved (Marshall & Halligan) |
| Balint | Simultaneous spatial binding | Sequential only | Scene classification persists |
| Extinction | Competitive selection | Competition-dependent | Priming intact |

Each disorder removes a different piece of the architecture. Consciousness continues, but its scope, structure, or reliability tracks the damaged component—and in every case implicit processing of the "lost" content persists. This gap is the interface. If consciousness were neural processing, no such systematic divergence should appear; if consciousness were independent of the brain, disorders should not alter phenomenology at all.

### Fatigue, OCD, Meditation

Sustained attention depletes. The [phenomenology of effort](/concepts/mental-effort/) intensifies with fatigue; motivation can temporarily override it—the resource is rationed, not exhausted. Jeffrey Schwartz's OCD treatment is sometimes adduced for top-down causation: patients learn to recognise obsessive thoughts as symptoms and redirect attention, with PET imaging showing reduced caudate nucleus activity after the cognitive protocol (Schwartz et al., 1996). The study is small (18 participants, only 9 new), unreplicated outside Schwartz's group, and the cognitive-versus-pharmacological pathway divergence is consistent with ordinary cognitive-neuroscience accounts of intervention-route specificity. The catalogue does not treat the Schwartz result as load-bearing evidence; it survives in the article as an illustrative case that motivated the interface framing rather than as empirical support for it.

Meditation trains the interface. Earlier claims of structural brain changes have been challenged by Kral et al. (2022), whose well-powered study (218 participants, active controls) found no structural changes from mindfulness-based stress reduction. Certain Advaita Vedanta strands report [witness-consciousness](/concepts/witness-consciousness/) as awareness prior to mental contents; Madhyamaka and Dzogchen draw the opposite inference from similar phenomenology—practice deconstructs any felt-sense of a fixed witness rather than reifying it. The interface model can accommodate the Advaita reading without claiming the Madhyamaka reading is mistaken (developed in [non-temporal-consciousness](/topics/non-temporal-consciousness/) and [phenomenology-of-returning-attention](/topics/phenomenology-of-returning-attention/)). AST has a standard rejoinder: practice may deepen the schema's apparent fundamentality rather than reveal anything beyond it. The catalogue notes the rejoinder rather than treating contemplative reports as decisive against AST.

### The Epistemological Challenge

Reports about a disordered attention system are produced by that same disordered system. A neglect patient's introspective report about the absent left side is produced by a system missing half its spatial representation; a person with lifelong ADHD has no neurotypical baseline. This creates an [occluded void](/voids/three-kinds-of-void/)—a knowledge limit hidden by the mechanism that produces it (see [attention-and-consciousness](/voids/attention-and-consciousness/)). The absence of a reference point does not invalidate reports but limits what they can reveal about the underlying consciousness.

## Effort, Libet, and Neural Sites

Sustaining voluntary attention and sustaining motor intention feel effortful in the same way. If effort is epiphenomenal, its systematic correlation with task difficulty is unexplained; if consciousness contributes to selection, harder selections naturally require greater engagement. William James observed "volition is nothing but attention"—the convergence vindicates him.

Benjamin Libet's 1983 experiments appeared to show the brain "decides" before consciousness knows. Schurger's 2012 model reinterprets the readiness potential as averaged neural noise; Sjöberg (2024) shows patients with the supplementary motor area resected retain the sense of willing while losing movement execution—one data point against the strong Libet reading that the readiness potential just is the conscious decision, consistent with the weaker interpretation that the potential reflects motor preparation rather than choice. On the convergence model, consciousness contributes selection, not initiation, biasing which option crosses threshold ~280ms before movement.

The Map doesn't commit to a specific neural mechanism. Candidates include ion channels (Stapp's quantum Zeno mechanism), microtubules (Penrose-Hameroff Orch OR), and the posterior-frontal junction where COGITATE's conscious content correlates meet attention control networks. Tegmark (2000) calculated decoherence times of approximately 10⁻¹³ seconds for ion-channel coherence and 10⁻²⁰ seconds for microtubule superposition in warm brain tissue—twelve to twenty orders of magnitude below the ~280–300ms attention timescale. Stapp's reply is that the relevant quantum effect operates at ion-channel transitions themselves, so the required coherence time is the transition's own brief duration, not the macroscopic attention timescale; bias integrates across many short-lived transitions. Penrose-Hameroff cites microtubule shielding and biological-quantum-coherence work at warm temperatures; direct extension to neural microtubules at body temperature remains contested, and Reimers et al. (2009) and McKemmish et al. (2009) argue Orch OR is physically incoherent on independent grounds. Tegmark's calculation thus remains the deepest unanswered quantum-skeptic objection, partially answered by Stapp's transition-scale rejoinder, unanswered for any mechanism requiring macroscopic neural coherence.

Conscious throughput, measured behaviourally, is roughly 10 bits per second (Zheng & Meister, 2024)—orders of magnitude below the rate at which sensory transduction delivers information to the brain. The two figures measure different stages of processing (behavioural output versus sensory input) rather than a single neural bandwidth, but the asymmetry between them motivates [the interface-friction model](/concepts/consciousness-bandwidth-architecture/): 10 bits/sec suffices for 3-4 selections per second among small alternative sets—the competition-selection architecture.

## Predictions and Falsification

The interface hypothesis generates one prediction specific to its mechanism. Because consciousness is hypothesised to bias which option crosses threshold within the salience-weighted competition, phenomenal effort should scale with the *entropy of the option set* at the threshold-crossing moment—the closer the competing alternatives are in pre-selection weight, the more effort the choice should feel. This is operationally separable from discrimination difficulty: vary the prior weighting of two response options in a forced-choice task while holding stimulus discriminability constant; under the interface model, subjective effort tracks competition entropy independently of discriminability. Standard cognitive neuroscience predicts the contrary—effort should track discrimination difficulty and be insensitive to prior weighting once discrimination is controlled. If competition entropy adds no variance to effort reports once discriminability is controlled, the interface mechanism is constrained at exactly the point where it claims to operate.

Three weaker predictions follow from the broader framing: regions where willed attention shows distinctive signatures should exhibit quantum effects absent in automatic processing; higher attentional effort should correlate with longer neural pattern maintenance; and disorders affecting different interface components should produce distinct impairment profiles. The neural-pattern-maintenance and neuroplasticity claims are also predicted by standard cognitive neuroscience and so do not by themselves discriminate the interface model from rivals.

The framework would also face serious challenge from rival-framework vindication: (1) precision weighting and competitive selection explain all attention phenomena without remainder, including effort phenomenology; (2) no quantum effects survive at candidate sites on attention-relevant timescales; (3) [many-worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) is confirmed, making "selection" mere self-location; (4) illusionism successfully explains "seeming" without a phenomenal subject; or (5) contemplative training reveals consciousness as schematic rather than prior to its contents. These five conditions are background-theory-failure conditions rather than forward predictions of the interface model—an asymmetry the catalogue notes honestly. Most of the model's falsification risk currently rides on rival-framework vindication, not on its own quantitative claims; the effort-entropy prediction is the first attempt to stake a forward claim of the model's own.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** — Both attention and motor selection feel like *you* doing something; parietal intention dissociates from premotor execution. People with attention disorders don't report diminished consciousness—they report difficulty directing it. If consciousness *were* any functional mechanism, it couldn't dissociate from that mechanism's operation.

**[Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)** — Motor force comes from metabolism, options arise from neural dynamics, and consciousness contributes only bias at the moment of resolution—tilting indeterminate outcomes where physics leaves room. Conservation laws hold because the energy for any selected action is ordinary biological energy.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** — The brain presents options (brain → mind); consciousness selects among them (mind → brain). The epiphenomenalist must explain why evolution built a unified attention-motor system with distinctive "willed" signatures that does nothing.

**[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)** — Within MWI, the standard indexical reply is that phenomenology in *this* branch correlates with Born-rule amplitude weight rather than with branches being on equal footing—so effort can in principle have a within-branch causal story even if all options execute somewhere. The Map's reply runs at bedrock rather than as in-MWI refutation: the convergence model requires a genuine selection, and the disagreement with MWI is a tenet commitment honestly noted, not a victory over MWI on its own terms. The case for the interface mechanism does not rest on having refuted MWI from inside MWI's framework.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** — AST explains access consciousness while leaving the phenomenal/access distinction at the level of stipulation rather than derivation. Calling that explanation is a parsimony move; whether it is the right parsimony move depends on whether the stipulated identity does work that earns its keep, which is the open question the previous section pressed.

## Honest Limitations

The convergence argument identifies *where* consciousness might influence brain states and *when* selection occurs; it does not explain *how* a non-physical consciousness biases physical outcomes. The quantum Zeno mechanism remains contested—decoherence objections have not been definitively answered. Attention disorders are consistent with the interface model but also with computational accounts; we cannot determine from inside any disorder whether consciousness is unchanged behind a degraded interface or is itself altered. The Map builds on findings from researchers—Rizzolatti, Cisek, Schurger, Schwartz, Graziano—who would not all endorse the dualist framework applied to their work.
