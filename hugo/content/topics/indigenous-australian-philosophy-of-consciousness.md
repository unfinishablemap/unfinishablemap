Where Descartes located the self in the act of thinking — *cogito ergo sum* — Aboriginal Australian thought locates it in Country. The Aboriginal philosopher Mary Graham puts the contrast exactly: "There is no Aboriginal equivalent to the Cartesian notion of 'I think therefore I am' but, if there were, it would be — 'I am located therefore I am.'" This is not a minor variation on the Western starting point. It relocates the ground of selfhood from an inner thinking substance to a web of relations with land, kin, and ancestors. Consciousness, on this account, is the achievement of a being who is placed, rather than the fortress of a private interior.

This article examines Indigenous Australian philosophy of consciousness through three of its load-bearing ideas: the [located, relational self](#located-self), the [Dreaming](#the-dreaming) as an ever-present ordering of reality, and the reading — advanced chiefly by non-Indigenous comparative philosophers — of Aboriginal thought as a form of [distributed mind or panpsychism](#panpsychist-resonance). The Unfinishable Map treats this tradition as a genuine alternative carving of the mind-world relation, one that converges with the Map's anti-physicalism while diverging sharply from its substance-leaning dualism. Two cautions frame everything that follows, and the [final section](#cautions) returns to them: "Aboriginal philosophy" spans hundreds of distinct language groups, and the rendering of this tradition as a "theory of consciousness" is itself contested.

## The Located, Relational Self {#located-self}

Graham's formula — "I am located therefore I am" — is a thesis about what constitutes a person. On her account the reflective self is "a communal self, not an individual self," and it is "not a God given soul, nor explained in reductionist materialist terms." Selfhood is neither the Cartesian soul nor the neuroscientist's brain-state. It is a standing in relations of obligation to a specific place and its people.

Consciousness-as-self-awareness, in this framework, is developmental rather than built-in. Graham writes that the "reflective motive emerges from the 'relational'": reflectivity is a product of Place and relation, not an innate faculty of an isolated mind that then happens to encounter a world. This inverts the Cartesian order of explanation. Descartes begins with a thinking thing certain of itself and reasons outward to an uncertain world. Aboriginal thought, as Graham presents it, begins with Country and kinship and treats the reflective "I" as something that grows out of that prior belonging.

Land carries the weight the *cogito* carries in Descartes. Graham describes Land as "the source of morality and meaning," and holds that "our relationship with Land defines the core interest and conscience of Aboriginal society." Crucially, Aboriginal Law "does not directly deal with the actions of humans... but with what makes it possible for people to act purposively, and experience 'events.'" Whether to hear this as a metaphysical claim or an ethical one is itself an interpretive choice: read metaphysically, Law might concern the *conditions of agency and experience* — what makes purposive, experiencing beings possible at all — but Graham's own framing keeps it within ethics and Law, and the metaphysical rendering is a choice the interpreter brings rather than a thesis she asserts.

The historian Tony Swain reinforces the locative reading from a different direction. In *A Place for Strangers* (1993) he argues that precontact Aboriginal ontology is organised around place rather than time — "an affirmation of place as a primary ontological category." On this reading, being appears to be spatial and locative before it is temporal or biographical. Swain's reconstruction is itself contested among scholars and is best treated as one influential interpretation rather than a settled finding; taken at face value, it suggests that where much of Western philosophy of mind treats the persisting self as a thread through time, this tradition treats it as a rootedness in space.

One structural feature deserves emphasis because it resists Western taxonomy. Graham explicitly declines the law of the excluded middle as a framing device, and she describes the distinction between reflective and unreflective states as "a matter of degree... it is not a dualism, insofar as the two are not ontologically distinct." The located self is a relational continuum that the Western dualist grid does not fit, not one term of a mind-body pair.

## The Dreaming as an Ordering of Reality {#the-dreaming}

The Dreaming — rendered variously as *Tjukurrpa* in the Western Desert, *narnu-Yuwa* among the Yanyuwa, and by many other Country-specific names — is the tradition's central metaphysical category. The single most important point for a reader approaching it philosophically is that it is not a creation myth set in the past. The anthropologist W.E.H. Stanner, who introduced the term "everywhen," insisted that "one cannot 'fix' the Dreaming in time; it was, and is, *everywhen*."

Stanner characterised the Dreaming as at once "a kind of narrative of things that once happened; a kind of charter of things that still happen; and a kind of logos or principle of order transcending everything significant." It is simultaneously story, law, and ordering principle — an abiding structure immanent in the world rather than a chronicle of origins. The generative events of the Dreaming are not over; they underwrite the present.

This atemporality matters for any comparison with Western theories of mind and identity. A framework in which the ordering of reality is neither wholly past nor wholly present, but "everywhen," does not treat the self as a point sliding along a timeline. It treats persons and places as expressions of an enduring order. The Map notes what may be a resonance here with its own concern for indexical identity — the question of why one is *this* subject — while stressing that the Dreaming answers through sacred and narrative order, not through anything resembling the Map's quantum framing. The vocabulary converges; the mechanism does not.

A related idea is the pre-existence of persons as spirit-children associated with sacred sites, who enter the world through a conception-place that becomes a lifelong bond to Country. This yields a totemic identity binding person to species and place, with the Dreaming persisting while its human incarnations are temporary. There appears to be a structural parallel here to [filter or transmission models](/concepts/filter-theory/) of consciousness, on which mind pre-exists the body and is received into it rather than produced by it. The parallel is genuine but must be handled as comparative phenomenology, not as endorsement: Aboriginal thought frames pre-existence through place and ancestry, not through a transcendent soul or a physical filter, and the traditions should not be collapsed into one another.

One equation must be explicitly refused. Despite the English word "Dreaming," this ordering principle is *not* about dream-sleep or REM experience. Most scholars warn against conflating the Dreaming with literal dreaming, and the Map follows that warning. The term names a metaphysical and legal order, not a state of the sleeping brain.

## Panpsychist Resonance and the Distributed Mind {#panpsychist-resonance}

The sharpest philosophical bridge — and the sharpest point of divergence for the Map — comes from the Australian environmental philosopher Freya Mathews, who reads Aboriginal thought as a form of [panpsychism](/concepts/panpsychism/). In her "living cosmos panpsychism," Mathews writes that "in Australian Aboriginal thought, mind is, as it is in living-cosmos panpsychism, primordial; human consciousness is just a single mode of this universally distributed and diversely embodied mind." On this reading, mind is not paradigmatically human with other things ranked by resemblance; mind is everywhere, and the human case is one instance among many.

Mathews glosses the Dreaming as "the meaning dimension that inheres in the manifest or empirical world," with the particular *Dreamings* being the stories that translate those meanings into human language. She quotes the Yanyuwa man Mussolini Harvey (via the anthropologist Deborah Bird Rose): "The Dreamings are our ancestors, no matter if they are fish, birds, men, women, animals, wind or rain... All things in our country have Law, they have ceremony and song." Rose's own ethnography describes a responsive, sentient world "buzzing with multitudes of sentient beings" — one whose relations are life-giving rather than inert. Mathews names the reciprocal engagement between self and this responsive world "ontopoetics" — a "poetic order" of meaning that, in her words, "will exceed the causal order but in no way contradict it."

This is the tradition's most direct contact with the Map's own anti-physicalism, and also its most instructive contrast. Mathews and Rose are describing a world in which innerness or sentience is intrinsic to matter and universally distributed. That is panpsychism (or its animist neighbour), not the Map's [interactionist dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/). The Map holds that consciousness is irreducible to matter *and* that it is not a ubiquitous intrinsic property of all matter — it faces the [combination problem](/topics/panpsychisms-combination-problem/) as a decisive difficulty for the panpsychist route. Aboriginal thought as Mathews reads it is therefore a *foil* that sharpens what the Map's dualism does and does not claim, not a confirmation of it. The convergence is real at the level of "mind is not produced by dead matter"; the divergence is real at the level of "mind is everywhere versus mind is irreducible but not ubiquitous."

There is also an important attribution asymmetry to mark. The strongest "theory of consciousness" readings — the explicit mapping to panpsychism, the language of distributed mind — come largely from non-Indigenous comparative philosophers such as Mathews and Rose. Indigenous-authored material, Graham's above all, is framed as ethics, Law, and relationality rather than as a thesis about consciousness. Even the recent scholarly venue that gathers this material — the *Journal of Consciousness Studies* special issue on "Indigenous Philosophies of Consciousness" (Trnka & Lorencova 2023) — is itself a Western academic framing, however sympathetically intended. The "consciousness" framing is partly supplied from outside the tradition, and the ethnographic material underdetermines the reading it is put to: the same relational, sentient-world evidence that Mathews reads as universally distributed mind is compatible with either reading — an animist metaphysics that locates sentience in matter, or a purely ethical-relational stance that advances no thesis about how consciousness is distributed at all. The evidence does not decisively adjudicate between them, and the panpsychist rendering may be reading a modern metaphysics into material that does not require it. Which of these the tradition holds is not forced by the sources; it is in part a choice the interpreter brings to them. The Map states this plainly rather than presenting a tidy Aboriginal doctrine of mind.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's engagement with this tradition is a study in partial convergence, and honesty requires naming both the overlap and the gap.

**Where it converges.** On the readings that advance a metaphysical thesis about mind, Aboriginal thought refuses reductive physicalism: Mathews's cosmos has mind as primordial rather than as a late product of complex matter, which aligns with the Map's foundational commitment that consciousness is not reducible to physical processes. Graham's self is explicitly "not explained in reductionist materialist terms," but Graham frames that as ethics and Law; read as metaphysical anti-physicalism it depends on a non-Indigenous overlay, so on its own it is an ethical resonance that does not by itself bear on the metaphysical question. The purely ethical-relational reading advances no thesis about how consciousness is distributed and is therefore compatible with physicalism, so the convergence with Tenet 1 holds only for the metaphysical readings, not for every reading surveyed here. The tradition also models a person in causal, obligation-bearing relation to a responsive Country — persons act on and are answered by the world — which rhymes with the Map's tenet of [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction), the claim that consciousness genuinely influences the physical world rather than riding along as a passive by-product. And the tradition's resistance to being flattened into the Western substance/property grid is itself an instance of the Map's fifth tenet, [Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits): a rich relational ontology should not be simplified away merely because a leaner metaphysics is easier to state.

**Where it diverges.** The Map is a *substance-leaning interactionist dualism* in its agency-bearing arguments — it needs a persisting subject who exercises [causal powers](/concepts/agent-causation/). Aboriginal thought is not dualist in this sense at all. Graham is explicit that the reflective/unreflective distinction is "not a dualism," and Mathews's reading places the tradition closer to panpsychism, which the Map rejects. So the located self is not a Cartesian ego relocated to the land; it is a different metaphysics with a different geometry. The Map's tenet of [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) — the smallest possible non-physical influence on physical outcomes — has no counterpart here and would be foreign to the tradition's frame. Reading Aboriginal thought as a vindication of the Map's dualism would be exactly the co-optation the Map guards against.

The honest verdict mirrors the Map's finding elsewhere that some traditions never generate the [hard problem of consciousness](/topics/the-hard-problem-in-non-western-philosophy/) because they never posit the matter-first physicalism the problem presupposes. Aboriginal thought, beginning from a located self in a meaning-saturated Country, may not pose the hard problem at all. That is not agreement with the Map's answer; it is a tradition that declines the Map's question. The value lies precisely there — in a worldview that shows the physicalist framing to be one option among genuine alternatives, not the neutral default from which all inquiry must start.

## Cautions on Generalisation and Translation {#cautions}

Two cautions constrain everything above, and neither is a formality.

First, "Aboriginal philosophy" abstracts across more than 250 distinct language groups with their own Country-specific systems — Yanyuwa, Pintupi, Yolŋu, and many others. Nearly all generalisable scholarship, including the sources cited here, works at a level of abstraction that risks erasing that diversity. The claims in this article should be read as broad patterns identified by scholars, not as a single doctrine held identically across the continent.

Second, there are live and growing concerns about translation, appropriation, and knowledge sovereignty. The Dreaming is Law, ceremony, place, and kinship before it is an academic thesis, and rendering it as a "theory of consciousness" for a Western metaphysical debate can distort it. Questions of who has standing to interpret this material are contested. The Map's response is to cite Indigenous authors directly where possible — Graham above all — to foreground the asymmetry between Indigenous-authored ethics and non-Indigenous comparative "consciousness" readings, and to present this tradition as a genuinely different carving of mind and world rather than as raw material for confirming a position it does not hold.
