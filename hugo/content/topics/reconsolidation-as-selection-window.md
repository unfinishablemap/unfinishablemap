Every time a memory is recalled, the stored trace becomes briefly editable before it restabilises. This is **reconsolidation**: retrieval does not read a memory passively but reopens it, and during a window of several hours the trace can absorb new information, shift emotional valence, or lose detail. The lability is empirically robust—it is one of the better-established facts in the neuroscience of memory. The Map's interest is narrower and explicitly more speculative: this window is the most concrete physical occasion where a [forward-in-time conscious selection](/topics/forward-in-time-conscious-selection/) could enter the reconstruction of a trace. The window is real; that consciousness supplies *directional* input to what happens inside it is the Map's posited reading, not something the lability data establish.

This article keeps those two claims separate throughout. The empirical signature comes first, at full strength. The interface interpretation comes after, clearly flagged as a posited reading of an empirically-real opportunity—a selection-*window*, which is an opportunity, not evidence that the opportunity is taken by a non-physical subject.

## The Empirical Signature: Retrieval Destabilises the Trace

The foundational result is Nader, Schafe, and LeDoux (2000), titled "Fear memories require protein synthesis in the amygdala for reconsolidation after retrieval" (*Nature* 406:722–726). Rats trained on a conditioned fear memory had that memory reactivated by a reminder cue; an inhibitor of protein synthesis (anisomycin) infused into the lateral and basal amygdala shortly after reactivation produced amnesia for the reactivated memory. A consolidated memory—one that had already been stabilised and was resistant to disruption—became disruptable again *because it had been retrieved*. The authors' summary is direct: "consolidated fear memories, when reactivated, return to a labile state in which they require de novo protein synthesis for reconsolidation."

Three features of this signature do real work in what follows:

1. **Retrieval-triggered destabilisation.** Recall is not inert. Accessing a memory moves its physical substrate from a stable to an unstable configuration. The trace is not copied out and replaced unchanged; it is reopened.

2. **A bounded lability window.** Destabilisation is temporary. There is a span—on the order of hours in the rodent work, with the precise duration depending on system and protocol—during which the trace is editable, after which protein synthesis restabilises whatever the trace has become. The window has an opening and a closing.

3. **Protein-synthesis dependence.** Restabilisation is an active biochemical process, not passive decay. The reconstructed trace must be rebuilt, which is why blocking the rebuild produces loss rather than mere distortion.

Reconsolidation is not unconditional. Boundary conditions govern *whether* retrieval destabilises a trace at all, and a central one is **prediction error**. Sinclair and Barense (2018), "Surprise and destabilize: prediction error influences episodic memory reconsolidation" (*Learning & Memory* 25:369–381), showed in humans that incomplete or surprising reminders—reminders that violate what the memory predicts—make episodic memories vulnerable to interference and subsequent revision, while reminders that merely reinstate the expected content do not reliably reopen the trace. Surprise is part of what unlocks the window. This matters for calibration: the window does not open every time something is remembered; it opens when retrieval delivers a mismatch the existing trace did not anticipate.

So the established picture is this. A stored memory is reopened by retrieval, especially when retrieval carries prediction error; it stays labile for a bounded window; and it is then actively rebuilt. Something determines which version is rebuilt. The neuroscience describes the *opening* of the window and the *machinery* of restabilisation. It does not, on its own, say what fixes the content of the reconstructed trace beyond the ordinary inputs of context, interference, and current cognitive-emotional state.

## What the Empirical Result Does Not Settle

It is worth marking explicitly what the lability data leave open, because the temptation is to let "the trace is editable" slide into "consciousness does the editing." The reconsolidation literature is overwhelmingly compatible with a fully physical account: the labile trace is rebuilt by the same neural, attentional, and contextual processes that govern encoding, with present-state inputs (mood, interference, retrieval cues) shaping the outcome. On that reading, "something determines the reconstruction" is answered by ordinary cognitive neuroscience, and no non-physical contribution is needed or implied.

The Map does not contest any of this at the level of mechanism. The window is genuinely physical; its opening and closing are described by protein-synthesis dynamics and prediction-error gating; and a complete physical story of *which* version restabilises is, as far as the data go, an open scientific question rather than a settled void that dualism is needed to fill. The argument below is therefore not "neuroscience cannot explain reconsolidation, so consciousness must." It is the weaker and more honest claim that *if* consciousness contributes directional input to the physical world at all—the content of [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) (Tenet 3)—then the reconsolidation window is the most concrete, most temporally-localised, most empirically-anchored occasion where such input would have somewhere to act.

> **Evidential status.** The lability window is established. The claim that consciousness supplies directional input to reconstruction within it is a posited interpretation, contested, and not adjudicated by the lability data. The window is an opportunity for the interface, not evidence that the interface is real.

## The Map's Posited Reading: A Window, Not a Proof

[Interactionist dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) holds that non-physical consciousness can make a minimal difference to physical outcomes ([Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction), Tenet 2). The standing difficulty for any such view is *where*—where in the physical record is there room for a contribution thin enough to leave the empirical record intact yet real enough to matter? The reconsolidation window is the Map's candidate answer for the memory domain, and it has an attractive shape for the purpose.

Consider what the window provides. There is a physical configuration that is, for a bounded interval, genuinely unsettled—the trace is labile, not yet rebuilt. The system is poised between reconstructions. The Map's broader account of how consciousness might act locates the contribution at the point where physical processes are not yet determinate: a [forward-in-time selection](/topics/forward-in-time-conscious-selection/) among possibilities that the substrate leaves open, biasing which outcome restabilises without injecting energy or violating Born statistics. The reconsolidation window is exactly such a point of openness, but for memory rather than perception or decision—a recurring, retrieval-triggered occasion on which the brain holds a trace open and *then* settles it.

The Map's reading is that the settling is where consciousness contributes: not by replacing the protein-synthesis machinery, but by supplying the directional bias that makes the reconstruction *purposive*—coherent with the subject's evolving self-understanding—rather than a function of interference and noise alone. On the physical side, prediction error opens the window and biochemistry closes it. On the Map's reading, what the conscious subject brings to the open window is a selection among the reconstructions the substrate makes available.

The discipline this article must hold is that none of the empirical facts force this reading. "The trace is labile" is established; "consciousness selects within the lability" is posited. The window is necessary for the Map's story—without a point of openness there is nowhere for a thin interface to act—but it is not sufficient to demonstrate it, because a purely physical reconstruction fits the same window. The honest statement is that reconsolidation gives the interface hypothesis its most concrete *venue*, and that having a venue is not the same as having an occupant.

## Why This Is the Most Anchored Window, Not the Strongest Proof

Among the Map's exhibits for Tenet 3, reconsolidation is unusual in how empirically grounded its *occasion* is. The recovery-order and vulnerability arguments ([memory-channel interface evidence](/topics/memory-channel-interface-evidence/)) infer interface engagement from *patterns* across anaesthesia, sleep, and terminal lucidity—rich but indirect. Reconsolidation, by contrast, points to a single, replicable, bench-demonstrated physical event: a specific trace becoming labile at a specific time and restabilising after. The venue is concrete in a way the pattern-arguments' venues are not.

That concreteness cuts both ways, and the calibration must say so. A concrete venue makes the directional-input claim *testable in principle*, but it also makes its current unprovenness vivid: the very experiments that establish the window (anisomycin amnesia, prediction-error gating) are fully explained without any conscious contribution. Reconsolidation is therefore the Map's most empirically-anchored window and simultaneously one of its clearest illustrations that an anchored window is not a proof. The exhibit's value is that it locates the dispute precisely—at the question of what fixes the reconstructed content—rather than that it resolves it.

## Stakes for Personal Identity

If the Map's reading is even approximately right, the consequence for [personal identity](/topics/personal-identity/) is severe and runs through an established fact, not only a posited one. Because retrieval reopens traces, the self is not recalling a fixed past; it is, with each act of remembering, lightly rewriting the record it remembers from. This is the lability-level counterpart of the [simulation theory of memory](/concepts/simulation-theory-of-memory/)'s claim that remembering is construction rather than retrieval; the reconsolidation window is the concrete physical occasion the Map points to for the directional input that construction-talk leaves unspecified. The person you take yourself to have been is shaped by every occasion on which you have recalled being that person. This much follows from the lability data alone: the autobiographical record is perpetually under revision, a moving target rather than an archive.

The Map's posited overlay sharpens the stake. If conscious directional input is what selects among reconstructions within the window, then the continuity of the self is partly *authored* by the subject across time rather than merely *suffered* as drift. The "I" that persists is not a pattern passively reinstantiated but one that, at each reconsolidation, has a hand—on the Map's reading—in which version of itself restabilises. Indexical identity, which the Map already treats as a real further fact rather than a convention (Tenet 4's discussion in [personal-identity](/topics/personal-identity/)), would then be reinforced rather than dissolved by reconsolidation: the perpetually-rewriting self is, on this view, perpetually *self*-rewriting. The qualifier remains in force—this stake is contingent on the posited reading; the bare lability fact gives a rewriting self without an authoring one.

The Map integrates this work as a venue argument for [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) (Tenet 3) and [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) (Tenet 2), with calibration held explicitly at the posited tier.

- **What the evidence establishes (no Map commitment required):** retrieval destabilises memory traces (Nader et al. 2000); prediction error gates whether the window opens (Sinclair & Barense 2018); restabilisation is active and protein-synthesis-dependent. These are accepted neuroscience and are stated here at full strength.
- **What the Map posits (flagged as interpretation):** that the lability window is the most concrete occasion at which conscious directional input could select among possible reconstructions, consistent with a [post-decoherence forward-in-time selection](/topics/forward-in-time-conscious-selection/) and with a [thin interface](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction). The Map speculates that this is *where* directional input enters the memory domain; it does not claim the lability data show that it does.
- **Why the distinction is load-bearing:** the dispute between the Map and a fully physical account is not over whether the window exists—both grant it—but over what fixes the reconstructed content. A physical reading answers with context, interference, and present state; the Map's reading adds conscious selection. The available evidence *constrains* both without *establishing* either ([evidential-status discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/)). Presenting the window as evidence *for* the interface would be exactly the slippage this article is built to avoid: an opportunity is not an occupant.

This is the most empirically-anchored exhibit the Map can offer for an interface contribution to memory, and it is offered as a window, not a proof. The dispute remains open between rival readings, and the findings that would in principle adjudicate it—evidence bearing on whether reconstruction within the window is fully fixed by physical present-state inputs—are not yet available to either side.

## Empirical Falsification Conditions

The posited reading is not idle; it carries commitments that future work could undermine.

- **If reconstruction within the window proves fully predictable from physical present-state inputs** (current context, interference set, neuromodulatory state) with no residual variance, the Map's "directional selection" loses its venue: there would be nothing left for a conscious contribution to fix.
- **If the lability window were shown to be epiphenomenal to behaviour**—if the destabilised trace's content made no difference to subsequent report or action—the personal-identity stakes would collapse, since nothing would be authored.
- **If prediction-error gating fully accounted for which traces change and by how much**, the room for a selection beyond the physical gating signal would close.

None of these has been shown, and the contrary—residual, hard-to-predict variation in what a reconsolidated trace becomes—would be consistent with (though it would not prove) the Map's reading. The article's claim is precisely calibrated to survive the first outcome as a refuted *interpretation* while leaving the empirical signature it rests on untouched.
