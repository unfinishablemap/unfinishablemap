In anarchic hand, one hand performs complex, well-coordinated, goal-directed acts that the patient did not intend, disowns, and cannot voluntarily inhibit: it reaches for objects, unbuttons a shirt the other hand has just buttoned, or raises a cup of tea the patient knows to be too hot to drink. Crucially, the patient still recognises the limb as their own—body *ownership* is intact—while denying authorship of its movements. The sense of *agency* is what fractures. This makes anarchic hand the agency-side complement to split-brain's perception-side dissociation: the interface between conscious volition and motor execution comes apart while the subject remains a single experiencer who narrates, resents, and physically restrains the wayward hand.

That clinical fact is compatible with two readings that predict the same picture, and the honest position is to hold this in view from the first line. On the standard neurological account, anarchic hand is a breakdown of motor-control architecture—frontal disinhibition plus a missing prediction signal—needing nothing non-physical. The Map reads the same data as a local fracture in the *interface* of volition, with phenomenal unity preserved. Anarchic hand supplies a discriminating *question*, not a proof: it shows that felt authorship is dissociable, not what the experiencer ultimately is.

## The Clinical Picture, and the Term to Get Right

The literature here is unusually disciplined about vocabulary, and getting the term right matters. Marchetti and Della Sala (1998) drew the now-standard split between two signs that older clinical writing had bundled under "alien hand syndrome":

- **Anarchic hand**: complex goal-directed movements performed against the patient's avowed will that cannot be voluntarily inhibited. A "frontal" sign, associated with anterior lesions of the corpus callosum and the supplementary motor area (SMA) contralateral to the wayward hand. The hand acts; the patient disowns the *act*.
- **Alien hand**: the *feeling of non-belonging* of a limb—the hand feels foreign, not-mine—reported after posterior callosal lesions and best understood as a partial hemisomatognosia, a loss of the sense of one's own body. The patient disowns the *limb*, not primarily its action.

The distinction matters for this topic because the phenomenon the Map cares about is *disowned action*, and its correct technical name is anarchic hand. The canonical illustration is the longitudinal case reported by Della Sala, Marchetti and Spinnler (1991), in which the anarchic hand lifts a too-hot cup toward the mouth against the patient's stated intention, and the patient uses the "good" hand to restrain the "bad" one—a self-directed intermanual conflict. Feinberg and colleagues (1992) reviewed roughly twenty cases and argued the umbrella really covers distinct frontal and callosal syndromes, with anterior callosal damage sufficient to produce intermanual conflict on its own.

## Agency Is Not Ownership

The dimension anarchic hand isolates is the one Haggard (2017) makes central: the **sense of agency**—the experience of controlling one's own actions and, through them, events in the world—is separable from **body ownership**, the experience that a limb is one's own. Ordinarily the two travel together and feel like a single given. Anarchic hand pulls them apart. Ownership persists: the patient never doubts the hand is theirs. Agency drops out: the movement arrives without the felt signature of having-been-willed.

This double dissociation is the structural point. If authorship and ownership can come apart, then felt authorship is not a raw datum read directly off the fact that *my* body moved. It is a constructed signal—something the system computes and can fail to compute—layered on top of ownership rather than identical with it. Anarchic hand is where that construction becomes visible by breaking.

## The Comparator Account, Conceded in Full

The strongest reading of anarchic hand needs no non-physical element, and it must be stated at its full strength rather than dodged.

On the comparator or forward-model account (Frith, Blakemore and Wolpert 2000), each motor command is issued together with an efference copy that predicts the movement's sensory consequences. When prediction and feedback match, the movement carries the feeling of being self-generated; when the prediction is absent or mismatched, the movement feels externally caused, not-mine. Anarchic hand fits this cleanly. Frontal and SMA lesions release premotor cortex from inhibition, so movements are triggered exogenously by the affordances of objects—a graspable cup, a fastenable button—rather than by the patient's plan. Because these movements bypass the normal command-and-prediction chain, no efference-based signal of authorship is generated, and the act is disowned. Goldberg's early localisation of the sign to medial frontal cortex frames exactly this: a loss of SMA inhibitory control that releases environmentally-driven behaviour.

Neuroimaging supplies a direct correlate. Assal, Schwartz and Vuilleumier (2007) contrasted involuntary and willed movements of the affected hand and found that the involuntary movements showed isolated activation of primary motor cortex without the normal premotor preparatory activity that precedes willed action. The movement executes; the precursor that would have generated the sense of agency is simply not there.

The Map does not contest any of this. The comparator model is fully adequate to the *neural* facts, and the lesion-to-symptom mapping is clean. What the comparator model describes as a *mechanism*, the Map takes seriously as a mechanism. The question is what the mechanism is a mechanism *of*—and on that the imaging is silent.

## The Interface Reading

The Map's angle treats anarchic hand as a local failure of the [interface of volition](/concepts/control-theoretic-will/), not a dissolution of the subject. What comes apart is a channel—the write-access by which conscious volition reaches one motor pathway—while the unified experiencer survives the fracture intact.

Two features carry this reading. First, agency and ownership *doubly dissociate*, which shows authorship is an interface signal rather than a given: the felt sense that an act is mine is something the system supplies and can withhold. That is exactly the profile of an interface, a constructed hand-off between volition and execution, rather than of a brute metaphysical fact about whose movement it is. Second, and decisively for the contrast with split-brain, the unity of the experiencer is not what breaks. There are not two subjects, not two streams of experience quarrelling over one body. There is one subject who disowns a movement, is distressed by it, narrates it, and reaches across with the other hand to stop it. The [witnessing subject](/concepts/witness-consciousness/) remains single while losing control of one output.

This is the pattern already developed for perception in [split-brain](/topics/split-brain-consciousness/)—the interface fragments, not the mind—carried over to *volition*. Anarchic hand is that flagship's agency-side sibling. In split-brain the question is whether divided perceptual report means divided experience; in anarchic hand the perceptual and experiential subject is uncontroversially single, and only a motor channel has gone rogue. Different interface channels, as [interface heterogeneity](/concepts/interface-heterogeneity/) holds, can fail independently: ownership can survive the loss of agency, and the unified witness can survive the loss of a limb's obedience.

A caution the Map takes on its own terms: that a hand can execute competent, goal-directed acts with no felt authorship does not license a bald claim that "action happens without any experience." Within the comparator framework the movement is precisely one that lacks the agency signal; the framework-relative reading is that a specific channel of felt control is absent, not that competent behaviour proves the general dispensability of experience. Anarchic hand shows control-competence and felt authorship are *separable in this case*, which is a narrower and more defensible claim.

## The Discriminating Question

Both readings predict the same clinical picture, so the honest move is to name what would separate them rather than declare a winner.

The comparator account, pushed to its metaphysical conclusion, holds that the felt single subject is itself one more neural construct—unity is a story the intact machinery tells, and the "one experiencer who disowns the hand" is as much an output as the disowned movement. The interface reading holds that the unified experiencer is ontologically prior and has merely lost read/write access to one motor channel; the self that disowns the hand is not another construct but the thing the constructs are *for*.

No current datum decides between these. Anarchic hand demonstrates that agency is dissociable from ownership and from execution. It does not demonstrate *what the experiencer is*. The philosophical literature tying anarchic hand specifically to agency—as opposed to the clinical neurology of the sign—is thinner than for split-brain, so the Map's synthesis here is a genuinely novel integration rather than a summary of a settled debate, and it is offered as possibility-preservation, not as evidence that forces the interface reading over the comparator one.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Anarchic hand engages two of the Map's [tenets](/tenets/) directly.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: The Map treats volition as an interface phenomenon—a hand-off between conscious will and physical execution—that can be locally disrupted without dissolving the unified subject. Anarchic hand is a vivid instance: one channel of that hand-off fails, the movement proceeds without felt authorship, and the experiencer persists as one. This is coherent with dualism without proving it. The comparator account reaches the same clinical facts on physicalist premises, and the Map concedes that account is adequate to the neural data. What the Map denies is that adequacy at the neural level settles the further question of what the experiencer is.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: The tenet holds that consciousness normally writes to the physical world through volition. Anarchic hand is instructive precisely as a case where that write-channel is *bypassed*: the hand moves without the conscious command, and the patient's felt agency—the signature of the normal write—is absent. The disorder marks, by its absence, what ordinary willed action involves. It is a broken instance of the interaction the tenet describes, not a counterexample to it: the patient's *other* hand, under normal control, reaches over to correct the anarchic one, exhibiting intact bidirectional control alongside the failed channel.

The Map does not claim anarchic hand as support for its metaphysics. It claims something narrower and firmer: that felt authorship is dissociable from ownership and from execution, that the unified experiencer survives such fractures, and that this pattern is what an interface view predicts—while remaining honestly compatible with the comparator rival the evidence does not let us discriminate against.
