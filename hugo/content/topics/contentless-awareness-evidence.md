Two traditions report a mode of consciousness with no object: a vivid wakefulness that is *of* nothing in particular. Vedantic contemplatives call the limiting case *turiya*, the "fourth" beneath waking, dream, and dreamless sleep; the philosopher Thomas Metzinger calls a kindred phenomenon **minimal phenomenal experience** (MPE), studied through the introspective reports of meditators. Both raise the same tantalising possibility: that consciousness does not switch off in dreamless sleep but merely empties of content. This article asks a narrower question than *whether* that is true. It asks **what could count as evidence either way** — and argues that the strongest honest answer separates two claims usually run together, and locates a structural reason the question resists empirical settlement.

The short version: the *phenomenological datum* — that careful subjects in two traditions report a contentless-but-vivid mode of awareness — is reasonably well-supported, though not immune to introspection-skepticism (a qualification the next section registers). The *metaphysical claim* — that a witness persisted through a stretch of genuine unconsciousness — is not, and cannot easily be, because the apparatus that would report such persistence is exactly what is offline in the target state. This article belongs to the [sleep-consciousness void](/voids/sleep-consciousness-void/) and refines the evidence side of the question the [essential-versus-contingent-consciousness](/topics/essential-vs-contingent-consciousness/) debate leaves open.

## The Datum: Two Traditions, One Report

What both traditions actually deliver, before any metaphysics, is a *report about phenomenal character*. Practitioners describe episodes of awareness without an intentional object — no perceptual content, no thought, no sense of a bounded self facing a world — yet the episode is reported as conscious rather than as a blank. That the report is convergent across trained subjects is its main credential — but it is not beyond the reach of introspection-skepticism: a contemplative schooled for years in what *turiya* should feel like is not a neutral instrument, and reports of a contentless-*but-vivid* state are exactly where introspection is most theory-laden. "Reasonably well-supported" here means *convergent among trained reporters*, not *immune to the confabulation worry the rest of this article presses* — the same worry, applied to the datum rather than only to the persistence-claim it is later marshalled to support.

Metzinger's program treats this report as data to be modelled. His 2020 paper introduces MPE as "a candidate for the simplest form of consciousness," entering the problem through "the phenomenology of 'pure' consciousness" as described by meditators (Metzinger, 2020). On Metzinger's predictive-processing reconstruction, pure awareness is "the content of a predictive model, namely, a Bayesian representation of tonic alertness" — a model of "an unpartitioned epistemic space" rather than a window onto a contentless reality (Metzinger, 2020). Crucially, this is a treatment of the *phenomenology*: Metzinger explores what it would take to specify the minimal phenomenal content of such episodes. He does not, in this instalment, certify that the state is metaphysically contentless or that it occurs during objectively verified unconsciousness; the project is explicitly the first of a series building toward a minimal-model explanation, not a finished verdict.

The Vedantic report is older and more metaphysically committed. The *Mandukya Upanishad* analyses consciousness into four: waking, dream, dreamless sleep (*sushupti*), and *turiya*, the witnessing awareness said to underlie all three. Gaudapada's commentary (the *Mandukya Karika*, c. 7th century CE) treats *turiya* as self-luminous consciousness in which the subject–object division has ceased. The tradition's central move concerns dreamless sleep: on waking, one reports "I slept well; I knew nothing," and Advaita reads this as a *known* absence — and an absence, the argument runs, can be known only by a witness present throughout. (See [witness-consciousness](/concepts/witness-consciousness/) for the *sakshi*, the witness-self this reading turns on.)

So the two traditions converge on a phenomenological report and diverge on what it shows. The convergence is real and worth taking seriously — but one caution: Metzinger's MPE program draws its data from the introspective reports of meditators, so it formalises the contemplative datum rather than corroborating it from an independent source. The apparent agreement of two traditions is closer to one report-stream examined twice than to two witnesses testifying separately, and the Map's own discipline against double-counting convergent evidence applies here to itself. The convergence is also, by itself, only a report about how certain episodes seem — not yet a finding about what was the case during unobserved unconsciousness.

## The Claim: A Witness Through the Gap

The interesting metaphysical claim is not that contentless episodes occur during meditation, where the subject is awake and can later report. It is the stronger thesis that contentless awareness *persists through dreamless sleep* — that something is experienced even in the state we ordinarily call unconscious. This is the claim that would, if established, settle the [essential-versus-contingent](/topics/essential-vs-contingent-consciousness/) question in favour of essential consciousness: consciousness as the nature of the non-physical aspect of mind, never wholly absent.

It is important to be exact about the status of this claim. **Contentless awareness in dreamless sleep is best treated as a commitment the evidence underdetermines** — a reading careful investigators hold and argue for — rather than as a hypothesis awaiting the observation that would confirm it, because (as the next section argues) no such observation is available even in principle. It has not earned an upgrade to the status of a realistic possibility established by evidence, still less a probable truth. The reasons follow.

## Why the Claim Resists Settlement

The dispute *looks* empirical. Surely we could find out whether anything is experienced in dreamless sleep — measure it, wake the subject, ask. But it resists settlement for a structural reason, and naming that reason is the central contribution this article can make.

**Every candidate datum is a report, and the report is produced after the fact.** Episodic memory encoding is suppressed in deep sleep — the hippocampal encoding of new declarative memories is diminished in slow-wave sleep, which is turned toward the consolidation of already-acquired traces rather than the laying-down of fresh ones (Diekelmann & Born, 2010); the cognitive machinery that turns an experience into a recallable, reportable judgement is reduced or offline in precisely the state under investigation. So the felt sense, on waking, of *having witnessed* one's own dreamless sleep is not, by itself, evidence of veridical witnessing. It is a present judgement *about* a past stretch — and a present judgement can be a post-hoc reconstruction assembled as the apparatus comes back online, rather than a faithful trace of a witnessing that was actually going on.

This cuts both ways, which is what makes it a genuine underdetermination rather than a merely practical caution. Consider the two readings of the same waking report:

- **Essential reading:** awareness continued throughout, contentless and unremembered; no episodic trace was laid down, so nothing is recalled.
- **Contingent reading:** there was no awareness to recall; only a persisting bearer remained, and "I knew nothing" reports a genuine, retrospectively inferred absence.

Both readings predict the *identical* observable: a waking subject who reports a blank. No introspective report distinguishes "I was aware but laid down no trace" from "I was not aware," because both yield the same blank. The trained contemplative's richer report — "I witnessed the sleep" — raises the stakes but does not break the symmetry: a vivid present conviction of having witnessed is exactly what a sophisticated post-hoc reconstruction would also produce. The reporting apparatus is the thing whose absence is in question; it cannot serve as an impartial witness to its own absence.

This is why the [evidential-status discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) matters here more than anywhere. The discipline forbids collapsing two distinct questions: *how strongly does the framework favour a reading?* and *how strongly does the evidence support it?* The phenomenological datum is one kind of thing; the metaphysical persistence-claim is another; and the inference from the first to the second runs through exactly the apparatus that the target state shuts down.

## What Would Not Be Evidence

Three moves tempt the unwary, and each fails.

**First, the felt vividness of the report is not evidence of its veridicality.** That an experience of "pure awareness" feels deeply real, even revelatory, is a fact about the phenomenology, not a warrant for its metaphysical interpretation — the same discipline the Map applies to every other revelatory-feeling report. The intensity of a conviction does not transmit to the truth of what it is a conviction about.

**Second, physiological correlates would underdetermine the verdict.** Suppose researchers find that meditators entering reported pure-awareness states show a distinctive signature — say, sustained tonic alertness without the usual content-bearing activity, the kind of profile Metzinger's predictive-processing model anticipates. This would strengthen the case that *something distinctive is happening* and that the reports are not confabulated noise. It would not, on its own, show that the state is *contentless awareness* rather than a low-content state that merely seems contentless, nor that the same thing recurs in objectively unwitnessed deep sleep. A neural signature is a third-person measure; whether it is accompanied by experience is the very point at issue, and no measure of the apparatus settles whether the lights are on behind it.

**Third — and this is a discipline the Map imposes on itself — a tenet is not evidence.** One might be tempted to argue: the Map's commitment to [dualism](/tenets/#dualism), or its picture of indexical identity, makes essential consciousness more coherent, so contentless awareness in dreamless sleep is more likely. But a framework commitment can at most *remove a defeater* — show that the hypothesis is not ruled out — without supplying any positive evidence for it. Coherence with one's prior commitments raises the framework-internal weight of a reading; it does not raise its evidential status. Treating the tenet as though it argued *for* the persistence-claim would be exactly the collapse the evidential-status discipline forbids. The honest standard here is the hardline-empiricist one the parent article also adopts: evidence is evidence, coherence is coherence, and the two must not be laundered into each other.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map commits to [dualism](/tenets/#dualism): consciousness is irreducible to physical process. That commitment makes the contentless-awareness question *natural* to ask — if mind has a non-physical aspect, whether that aspect can persist unconscious is a real and open question — but it does not answer it. Dualism is compatible with both essential consciousness (the non-physical aspect is conscious by nature) and contingent consciousness (it bears consciousness sometimes and lacks it at others). Nothing in the tenets adjudicates between them, and this article's whole point is that the available evidence does not either.

One boundary should be marked honestly. The essential/contingent fork presupposes a persisting bearer of mind — something that could either carry or lack consciousness across the gap — and a major tradition, Madhyamaka Buddhism, denies exactly that continuant, holding the witnessing self to be empty rather than a substance that endures unwitnessed. That is a foundational disagreement the Map's commitments about indexical identity and individuation answer differently, not a thesis this article refutes within Madhyamaka's own terms. The fork is well-formed for a view that grants a persisting subject; it is one the no-self traditions would decline to enter, and saying so is part of keeping the dialectic honest.

The deepest connection is to **[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**. Two traditions of careful investigators — Vedantic contemplatives refining the *turiya* analysis over centuries, and analytic philosophers and cognitive scientists pursuing the MPE program — examine the same territory and reach different verdicts about what the contentless-awareness reports establish. When sophisticated inquiry persists in disagreement, that persistence is itself a reason not to expect parsimony to settle the matter. The simpler-looking view — that there is straightforwardly nothing experienced in dreamless sleep — is not thereby the better-supported one. Parsimony is a heuristic about theory choice under good information; here the information is structurally unavailable, and the heuristic loses its grip.

The Map's resulting stance is therefore deliberately modest. It records the phenomenological datum as a datum, treats contentless awareness in dreamless sleep as a reading the evidence underdetermines rather than one it endorses as established, and declines every move that would smuggle the hypothesis up the scale of evidential warrant — whether by the vividness of the report, by physiological correlate alone, or by tenet-as-evidence. The Map can hold a metaphysical reading as a commitment the evidence underdetermines; it must not present that commitment as a finding. This is the same restraint exercised in the [minimal-consciousness void](/voids/minimal-consciousness-void/), where the question of how little experience can be present shades into the question of whether any is present at all.
