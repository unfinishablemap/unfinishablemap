Eliminative materialism is the radical thesis that the categories of common-sense psychology—beliefs, desires, qualia, the self—do not pick out anything real, and that a mature science of mind will displace them rather than reduce them. Where reductive physicalism says *mental states are brain states*, eliminativism says *there are no mental states; there are only brain states, and our talk about minds is a deeply false theory*. The position has two distinct strands: classical eliminativism about propositional attitudes (Paul Churchland, Patricia Churchland, Stephen Stich) and contemporary [illusionism](/concepts/illusionism/) about phenomenal consciousness (Keith Frankish, Daniel Dennett). The Unfinishable Map rejects eliminativism as a theory of consciousness while conceding that some objections—particularly the connectionist challenge to the propositional-attitude framework—carry genuine theoretical weight that any responsible defence must engage rather than dismiss.

This article presents both eliminativist strands in their strongest forms, catalogues the principal objections, and then explains where the Map locates the move's failure: in over-applying [Occam's Razor](/concepts/parsimony-epistemology/) to a domain where parsimony's track record is poor, and in treating [introspection](/concepts/introspection/) as theory-laden in a way that begs the question against [first-person evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/).

## Two Eliminativisms, Distinguished

The literature uses "eliminative materialism" to cover two positions that share a structure but target different aspects of mind. Conflating them produces bad arguments in both directions.

**Classical eliminativism** targets [folk psychology](#folk-psychology) (explained in the next section)—the network of concepts (*believes that*, *desires*, *intends*, *fears*) by which we explain and predict each other's behaviour. Paul Churchland's 1981 paper "Eliminative Materialism and the Propositional Attitudes" is the defining modern statement. The claim is that beliefs and desires are theoretical posits of a stagnant, primitive theory and will be replaced—not reduced—by mature neuroscience.

**Illusionism** (or eliminativism about phenomenal consciousness) targets [qualia](/concepts/qualia/)—the felt character of experience, the redness of red, the painfulness of pain. Frankish's 2016 paper "Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness" is the consolidating statement. Dennett endorses it as "the obvious default theory of consciousness." The claim is that introspection misrepresents physical-functional states as having irreducible phenomenal character.

The two strands can be held independently. One could deny that beliefs are real while granting qualia, or affirm beliefs while denying qualia. The Churchlands and Frankish are eliminativists about both, but the arguments diverge.

## Folk Psychology and the Replacement Programme {#folk-psychology}

Paul Churchland's 1981 case rests on four moves.

**The stagnation argument.** Folk psychology, Churchland argues, "suffers explanatory failures on an epic scale" and "has been stagnant for at least twenty-five centuries." It cannot explain mental illness, dreams, learning, memory, or consciousness. A theory that fails to advance over millennia is a candidate for replacement, not refinement.

**The inductive argument.** Folk physics, folk biology, folk cosmology, and folk epidemiology all proved radically false. Their entities (caloric, vital fluid, miasma, crystal spheres) were eliminated rather than reduced. Folk psychology addresses a far more complex domain than any of these and is therefore *more* likely to be radically false. The base rate for folk theories is failure.

**The sentential-structure mismatch.** Folk psychology treats beliefs as sentence-like states standing in inferential relations: *S believes that P*. The brain's actual computational architecture, by Churchland's lights, does not contain sentence-shaped structures. If cognition is implemented by something like vector coding across distributed networks, the propositional-attitude framework lacks a referent.

**The status of introspection.** Mental concepts are theoretical posits, not introspective givens. Sellars (1956) had already argued that the apparent self-evidence of mental states is an artefact of long enculturation in the folk theory. Introspection cannot vindicate folk psychology because introspection is itself a deployment of folk psychology.

Patricia Churchland's *Neurophilosophy* (1986) develops the constructive side: a programme of neurophilosophy in which philosophy is answerable to neuroscience. The book argues that "neuroscience portends a bleak future for commonsense mental states" and presses for vector-coding replacements of linguistic-format representation.

Classical eliminativism therefore proposes a positive successor science alongside its destructive critique. Mental states do not vanish from explanation; they are *replaced* by descriptions in the language of neural population coding, dynamical-systems trajectories, and connectionist representation.

## Illusionism: Eliminativism About Phenomenal Consciousness {#illusionism}

Frankish's 2016 paper consolidates a position that had been gestating in Dennett's work since "Quining Qualia" (1988). The illusionist's central manoeuvre is structural: convert the [hard problem of consciousness](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) into what Frankish calls the *illusion problem*.

The hard problem asks: how can physical processes give rise to phenomenal experience? The illusion problem asks: why does it *seem* to us that physical processes give rise to phenomenal experience, when they do not? The first is hard because it requires bridging an explanatory gap. The second, illusionists argue, is tractable because it requires only a cognitive-science account of why a physical-functional system would systematically misrepresent its own internal states.

Frankish distinguishes three senses of "qualia":

- **Classic qualia**: intrinsic, ineffable, private, directly known properties of experience. These are what realists defend and what illusionists deny.
- **Zero qualia**: purely functional properties detectable in cognitive processing. Everyone agrees these exist.
- **Diet qualia**: properties intermediate between classic and zero. Frankish treats these as unstable; the diet position collapses into either classic realism or zero functionalism.

The illusionist's positive claim is that mental states possess only *quasi-phenomenal* properties: non-phenomenal physical-functional features that introspection systematically misrepresents as phenomenal. There is something it is like to be a system that quasi-phenomenally represents its own states as having classic qualia. There is no something-it-is-like in the realist's sense, because no such property exists for there to be.

Dennett's 2016 endorsement frames illusionism as "the obvious default theory" once one accepts that classic qualia are conceptually incoherent. His 1978 morphine argument is illustrative: subjects on morphine report excruciating pain that does not bother them. The folk concept of pain treats unpleasantness as essential to pain; the morphine case suggests pain and unpleasantness are dissociable. If the folk concept fragments under empirical pressure, the qualia it picks out may have been confused all along.

Frankish himself acknowledges that illusionism is "entity eliminativism about phenomenal consciousness even if the label 'eliminativism' is avoided." The structural commitment stands regardless of the label.

## The Connectionist Challenge

The argument the Map most needs to engage seriously comes from Ramsey, Stich, and Garon's 1990 paper "Connectionism, Eliminativism, and the Future of Folk Psychology." The argument is not just promissory; it offers a concrete cognitive-science model in which folk-psychological ontology has no implementation base.

The argument runs: in feedforward connectionist networks trained to perform belief-like tasks, "there do not appear to be any structures in these networks that might serve as candidates for beliefs." Information is distributed across weight matrices in ways that lack causally discrete, semantically evaluable units. There are no causally distinct structures that stand for specific states of affairs.

If cognition is implemented this way—and contemporary deep learning has revived rather than dispelled the question—the propositional-attitude framework lacks the kind of categorical structure it presupposes. Beliefs would not merely be hard to find in neural tissue; they would be the wrong *kind* of thing to look for.

This is the argument that any honest engagement must concede has theoretical weight. The Map's response below does not deny the connectionist point. It denies that the connectionist point licenses eliminativism about phenomenal consciousness, which is a separate matter, and it questions whether the framework-of-beliefs-and-desires must implement *exactly* the structure folk psychology naively suggests in order to track real psychological kinds.

## Principal Objections

Three families of objection have shaped the literature.

**The self-refutation objection.** The classical version: to assert eliminativism, the eliminativist must believe it; if there are no beliefs, the assertion is incoherent. The Churchland-Devitt-Rey reply is that this begs the question—the bare thesis "there are no beliefs" is not contradictory; the objection presupposes folk-psychological semantics in framing the contradiction. Boghossian's 1990 paper "The Status of Content" offers a more sophisticated version: arguments for irrealism about propositional-attitude content generalise to all content, including the linguistic content of the eliminativist's assertions. Devitt and Rey reply that this still presupposes truth-conditional semantics that the eliminativist can decline. The state of play is unsettled; most philosophers regard the simple version as question-begging and Boghossian-style versions as more challenging. (See [self-stultification](/concepts/self-stultification/) for the Map's broader treatment of self-undermining-position arguments.)

**The hard problem of content.** Hutto and Myin (2012, 2017) argue that naturalistic theories of mental representation cannot explain how neurological states acquire genuine semantic properties—how a pattern of neural activity comes to be *about* anything. This is distinct from Churchland's eliminativism: Hutto and Myin reject inner mental representations entirely (radical enactivism) rather than reducing them to neural ones. The argument tells against the *replacement programme* by undercutting the assumption that something representational survives the replacement.

**The Moorean response to illusionism.** Realists from Searle onwards have argued that phenomenal consciousness is the one domain where appearance and reality cannot come apart. "Where consciousness is concerned," Searle writes, "the existence of the appearance is the reality." If it seems to me that I am in pain, I am in pain; the seeming and the being are not separable. A 2024 *Frontiers in Psychology* paper, "In Defence of the Moorean Argument Against Illusionism," develops this line: I am more certain that phenomenal consciousness exists than I am of any premise an illusionist could deploy against it. Modus tollens beats modus ponens.

A related objection is the regress-against-illusionism: if the illusion of phenomenal consciousness is itself a phenomenal seeming, illusionism merely relocates the problem. To whom does the illusion appear? If the appearance has phenomenal character, the illusionist has reintroduced the explanandum. If it does not, the position seems to dissolve into something its defenders did not intend. Frankish anticipates this in §6 of his 2016 paper, replying that the seeming is *quasi*-phenomenal—a higher-order representational state with functional properties that misrepresents itself as phenomenal—not itself a phenomenal seeming that would relaunch the regress. The Map finds the reply pressed: it requires the appearance of phenomenality to have *no* phenomenal character whatsoever, which is the position it was meant to explain rather than presuppose. The disagreement at this point is over whether the quasi-phenomenal/phenomenal distinction can be drawn cleanly enough to bear the weight Frankish places on it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map rejects eliminativism about phenomenal consciousness and accepts limited eliminativism about *some* folk-psychological categories—a finer-grained position than wholesale endorsement or rejection.

**Where the Map sees the move's failure.** Eliminativism's central methodological move is the application of Occam's Razor to consciousness: if consciousness is hard to fit into the physicalist picture, eliminate it. The Map's [fifth tenet](/tenets/#occams-limits) denies that parsimony has the universal epistemic warrant this move requires. The defensible claim is narrower than a sweeping historical induction: parsimony is unreliable in domains where hidden structure may not yet be visible, and consciousness is paradigmatically such a domain. We do not know what the mature theory looks like, which entities it will posit, or which apparent unities will turn out to mask deeper joints. Cutting the explanandum because it is hard to fit reverses the order of inquiry. Eliminativism asks us to apply Occam's Razor to the datum the Map argues has uniquely strong epistemic standing—our own conscious experience—on the grounds that it is hard to explain. Failure to reduce, in a domain whose structure is not yet understood, is at least as much evidence for irreducibility as it is for fictionalism; the inference to elimination presupposes that the only alternative to a current physical reduction is no real phenomenon at all, which is the contested point.

**The introspection-as-theory-laden move.** Eliminativism's deepest manoeuvre is making introspection itself epistemically suspect. If mental concepts are theoretical posits rather than introspective givens, then introspection cannot adjudicate the dispute. The Map's response here is best understood as a *foundational commitment* rather than as a derived rebuttal. The Map takes phenomenal evidence—the brute fact that there is something it is like to be having an experience—as a starting datum any theory of consciousness must account for. The eliminativist is free to reject this starting point, and the Map does not pretend to dislodge them by argument from prior premises they accept. The defence of treating phenomenal evidence as starting datum is *holistic*: it rests on the first tenet, on the failure of physicalist programmes to close the explanatory gap on their own terms, and on the asymmetry the Moorean argument identifies between the confidence we have in any theoretical premise an illusionist deploys and the confidence we have in the phenomenal evidence those premises would overrule. Honest disagreement here is bedrock. What the Map can claim is that treating introspection as theory-laden in the same way that, say, naked-eye astronomy was theory-laden flattens an epistemic difference: naked-eye astronomers could be wrong about celestial bodies that existed independently of observation, whereas the phenomenal observer's relation to phenomenality has no comparable observation-independent target. The eliminativist will deny this very asymmetry. The Map flags the disagreement as foundational rather than smuggling its preferred side of it through the back door of a constitutive-observation slogan.

**The Map's stance on classical eliminativism.** Where eliminativism targets *folk-psychological structure*—the assumption that beliefs are sentence-like, that propositional attitudes carve mental kinds at their joints—the Map is more conciliatory. Folk psychology may be the wrong-grained theory for predicting neural events. Whether the brain implements distributed connectionist representations or sentential ones is a question for cognitive science, not metaphysics. The Map's commitment to [dualism](/concepts/dualism/) concerns phenomenology and causal interface, not the structural format of cognitive representations.

**The Map's first tenet.** [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) holds that consciousness is not reducible to physical processes. Eliminativism is the limit case of physicalism: it shows what physicalism commits to if pursued consistently. Either eliminate consciousness, or recognise that the failure to eliminate is evidence for what the Map's first tenet asserts. The Map reads the dialectic as forcing this choice and choosing irreducibility.

**The Map's third tenet.** [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) requires real mental causation. Two commitments need to be distinguished here. First, the *causal-efficacy* commitment: intentional states (whatever their underlying format) make a real causal difference to behaviour. Second, a *structural-format* commitment about whether those states have sentence-shaped, propositional structure. The Map's third tenet bears on the first only. Mental fictionalism (Toon 2023) and full phenomenal-and-causal eliminativism both deny the first; they treat belief-talk as as-if discourse whose usefulness does not require real intentional causation. The Map rejects this. Legal systems, moral evaluation, and the practice of giving and asking for reasons presuppose that something the belief-and-desire vocabulary tracks is causally efficacious—and the Map treats the practical indispensability of that something as evidence against fictionalism. This argument is *neutral* on whether the underlying causally-efficacious states have a sentence-like format or are distributed connectionist representations summarised by the intentional stance. A Frankish-style eliminativist who concedes that physical-functional states cause behaviour, and only denies that those states have *phenomenal* properties, is not refuted by this paragraph; that disagreement is treated separately under the first tenet.

**Where eliminativism is right (and the Map is too).** Some folk categories *are* heterogeneous and bad scientific kinds. Griffiths argues that "emotion" lumps affect programs, higher cognitive emotions, and social constructions. Machery argues that "concepts" lump prototypes, exemplars, and theories. The Map can accept these dissolutions—they are taxonomy reform within the mental, not eliminations of the mental. Conceding kind-dissolutionism strengthens rather than weakens dualism: the Map's commitment is to phenomenal consciousness as irreducible, not to the integrity of every folk category.

## Honest Limitations

Three concessions belong to any defence the Map makes against eliminativism.

**The connectionist argument has theoretical weight.** Ramsey, Stich, and Garon offered a concrete cognitive-science model—not a promissory note—in which propositional attitudes lack an implementation base. Modern deep learning has sharpened rather than blunted the point: large language models exhibit linguistic competence without any obvious belief-like states standing in inferential relations of the kind folk psychology presupposes. The contemporary descendant of this challenge is the predictive-processing / active-inference programme (Clark, Hohwy, Friston), which provides a *positive* cognitive-science model in which folk-psychological states might be explained as inferential summaries over hierarchical predictive processes rather than eliminated outright. This is a softer eliminativism than Churchland's: it preserves intentional vocabulary as a description of higher-level dynamics while denying it carves the underlying computational architecture at any joint. The Map's response is to grant that the structural format of belief is open to revision while denying that this licenses eliminativism about phenomenal consciousness, which is a separate question—and the same response handles predictive processing's softer challenge. But this concession is real, and it limits how aggressively the Map can deploy "we have beliefs" as a structural premise.

**The illusionist meta-problem is genuine.** Even if the Map rejects illusionism, the meta-problem—why does it seem to us that there is a hard problem?—is a real explanatory burden. The Map cannot dismiss the meta-problem as the illusionist's own; it inherits the burden of explaining why the appearance of phenomenality has the character it does. (See [metaproblem-of-consciousness-under-dualism](/topics/metaproblem-of-consciousness-under-dualism/) for the Map's attempted treatment.)

**Self-refutation does not obviously succeed.** The simple version of the self-refutation argument begs the question. Boghossian's sophisticated version has more force, but the literature has not converged. The Map should not lean on self-refutation as if it were a knockdown argument. Instead, the Map's case rests on the substantive claim that phenomenal evidence has an epistemic status incompatible with eliminativism—not on a formal incoherence in eliminativism's self-presentation.

## Why Eliminativism Persists

Eliminativism continues to attract serious philosophers despite the objections. The reason is that physicalism keeps failing to produce a satisfying account of consciousness, and eliminativism is the cleanest physicalist exit. If you have already committed to the view that there cannot be irreducible phenomenal facts, eliminating the appearance of such facts is the most economical response. The eliminativist's bet is that the explanatory gap will close once the right science arrives. The Map's bet is that it will not, because the gap reflects something real about the structure of consciousness rather than the immaturity of its science.
