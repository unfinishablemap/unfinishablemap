The Many-Minds Interpretation (MMI) is the no-collapse reading of quantum mechanics that writes *minds* directly into the formalism. (Consciousness-*collapse* interpretations, from [von Neumann–Wigner](/concepts/von-neumann-wigner-interpretation/) to the formalized Chalmers–McQueen models, also put mind into the theory, but they do so in the collapse dynamics; MMI is distinctive in keeping the physics unitary and locating the multiplicity in minds instead.) Where the [Many-Worlds Interpretation](/concepts/many-worlds/) (MWI) splits the physical universe at every measurement, MMI keeps physical reality single and unbranching and locates the multiplicity in the observer's mental life: each sentient being is associated with many minds—on the canonical version, a continuous infinity—and the minds, not the world, distribute themselves across measurement outcomes. The Unfinishable Map cares about MMI because it is the closest mainstream rival to the Map's own interface model: a theory that retains a single physical world (friendly to the [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) tenet) yet still multiplies experiencers, and one whose leading version openly concedes [dualism](/tenets/#dualism). The reading developed here is that MMI runs into the *same* difficulty MWI does: adding minds to Everett does not appear to supply the missing fact about which mind is the actual continuant *I*. The [mindless hulks problem](#mindless-hulks) (explained below) and the [indexical objection](#relation-to-site-perspective) (explained below) suggest that minds-in-superposition do not, on their own, rescue indexical identity; the Map's diagnosis is that what is needed instead is genuine collapse plus a determinate self. That diagnosis is conditioned on the Map's own commitments and is set out as such below.

## What the Many-Minds Interpretation Claims

MMI begins from a problem in Everett's no-collapse picture. If the wave function only ever evolves unitarily, then after a measurement the observer's *brain* is in a superposition of "saw spin-up" and "saw spin-down." But an observer reports one definite result with complete certainty. Albert and Loewer (1988) put the motivating constraint sharply: the mind of an observer cannot be in an indefinite state, because an observer must be able to say with certainty which outcome she has seen. Their resolution is to leave the *body* superposed and keep the *mind* definite.

This generates two distinct proposals in the same 1988 *Synthese* paper, plus a physicalist rival.

### The Single-Mind View

On the single-mind view, each observer has *one* mind. The brain evolves unitarily into superposition, but the mind hops stochastically into one definite mental state, with probabilities given by the Born rule. The physical world never branches; one mind tracks one outcome.

The cost is explicit. Albert and Loewer concede that the mind no longer supervenes on the brain: "one cannot tell from the state of a brain what its single mind believes" (Albert and Loewer 1988: 206). Two brains in identical superposed states could carry minds that believe different things. This is an admitted [dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/)—a regretted consequence, in the authors' framing, rather than a feature they set out to defend.

### Mindless Hulks {#mindless-hulks}

The single-mind view runs into what Barrett (1999) called the **"mindless hulks"** objection. In a superposition over observer-states, only one term carries the single mind; every other term is a physically complete observer—behaving, speaking, recording memories—with *no associated mind at all*. These are "mindless hulks": full-spec philosophical zombies generated by the formalism itself. Worse, there is no fact accessible from inside about which term carries the mind, so introspection and memory become unreliable in principle (Barrett 1999: 187–190).

### The Many-Minds View

The many-minds view is the defensive retreat from mindless hulks. Instead of one mind per observer, posit a *continuous infinity* of minds. On measurement the minds distribute across the outcome-terms in proportion to the squared amplitude, so *every* term is populated with minds and none is left a mindless hulk. As the Stanford Encyclopedia summarises Albert and Loewer, "every sentient being has a continuum of minds"; on measurement "the minds of this sentient being evolve randomly and independently into mental states corresponding to these different perceptions (with probabilities equal to the quantum probabilities)" (Vaidman, *SEP*).

This fixes hulks at three prices. It is ontologically profligate (an infinity of minds per person). It remains dualist: the minds are non-physical, "not subject to the rules of quantum mechanics for their evolution" (IEP). And it carries an internal tension the IEP draws out—only the *global* mental state supervenes on the physical state and obeys quantum mechanics, while the *local* state "to which an observer has direct introspective access, does not so supervene" (IEP).

### Lockwood's Physicalist Multimind

Michael Lockwood (1996, *BJPS* 47: 159–188) offered a physicalist many-minds program designed to avoid the dualism. His view "takes completely at face value the account which unitary quantum mechanics gives of the physical world" and insists on "a fundamental democracy of vector bases in Hilbert space"—no objectively preferred basis, with both state reduction and the preferred basis treated as "illusion." An observer has a single "Mind" (a multimind) comprising the minds across all superposition terms, each enjoying a "maximal experience," and these minds are grounded in physical brain processes rather than floating free of them.

The decisive feature, for the Map, is Lockwood's stance on identity. He concludes it is "senseless to ask which, of the many possible minds, are 'yours'" after a measurement—explicitly aligning with [Parfit](/concepts/parfit-reductionism/) on the meaninglessness of "which person will I become?" This is the cleanest articulation in the literature of the view the Map argues against. (Lockwood's reading "resembles the approach of Zeh (2000)"; Vaidman warns that "the MMI of Albert and Loewer (1988) … should not be confused with the MMI of Lockwood et al. (1996).")

### Zeh's Decoherence-Rooted Ancestor

H. Dieter Zeh (1970, 1981, 2000) is the historical root. Having introduced [decoherence](/concepts/decoherence/) into the Everett picture, Zeh treated the many-minds reading as "the most natural extension of standard psycho-physical parallelism" once the universal wave function is reduced to a local density operator: consciousness "selects" a branch via entanglement and decoherence. The selection language *sounds* interface-like, but it is acausal—decoherence does the physical work and consciousness rides along—a contrast the Map develops below.

## Three Recurring Failures

MMI's critics converge on three problems, all live into the 2020s.

**Mindless hulks** (treated above) press the single-mind view; the many-minds view escapes them only by buying an infinity of minds and an admitted dualism.

**The probability problem.** MMI was meant to recover Born statistics as a *measure over minds*—"the proportion of minds associated with each term" (Albert and Loewer 1988: 208). Critics from Barrett (1999) to Loewer's own 1996 reassessment judge this "puzzling and insufficient" as a guide to rational prediction: "half my future minds see up" is a measure, not an *uncertainty* a single agent can act on when all outcomes occur. Lockwood's measure—built from "temporal length" and "superpositional width"—faces the same charge. Notably, MMI reintroduces exactly the fundamental randomness MWI was built to eliminate; in Vaidman's framing the fix "comes at the price of introducing additional structure into the theory, including a genuinely random process." The problem did not close: the modern Everettian self-locating-uncertainty program (Sebens and Carroll 2018) is the same problem in newer dress, with McQueen and Vaidman countering that its examples involve *absent* uncertainty rather than genuine self-locating uncertainty. See [probability-problem-in-many-worlds](/topics/probability-problem-in-many-worlds/) for the full case against the recovery strategies.

**Diachronic identity.** This is the crux. Both Albert–Loewer's continuum of minds and Lockwood's multimind appear to dissolve the fact about *which* successor-mind is the genuine continuant of the pre-measurement person. Recent mainstream work converges on the same framing: "questions of uncertainty and probability in Everettian QM depend upon answers to diachronic personal identity" (arXiv:2209.02639, 2022). The interpretation-level debate seems to bottom out in [personal-identity](/topics/personal-identity/).

## Relation to Site Perspective

MMI is perhaps the sharpest available test of the Map's [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) tenet, because it removes the one feature critics most often attack in MWI—extra physical worlds—and, on the Map's reading, still leaves the indexical question unanswered. That makes the case especially instructive.

**Dualism.** MMI is striking evidence for the *plausibility* of [Tenet 1](/tenets/#dualism). Albert and Loewer, analytic philosophers with no dualist agenda, found that taking determinate experience seriously inside unitary quantum mechanics forced them to concede that mental states do not supervene on brain states. That a mind-involving, non-physicalist ontology emerges from the measurement problem under pressure is a point in the Map's favour. But the Map should distinguish its dualism sharply from MMI's: in MMI the mind is a *passive lottery winner*, randomly assigned an outcome (single-mind) or smeared across all outcomes (many-minds). It does nothing.

**Bidirectional Interaction.** This is where MMI and the Map part decisively. Under [Tenet 3](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction), consciousness *biases* which outcome obtains, modulating the statistics of a real, local collapse. MMI's mind is acausal: it is distributed *over* unitary outcomes, or rides *on* decoherence-fixed ones, rather than selecting *among* them. Zeh's "consciousness selects a branch" is the closest MMI comes to interface language, and even there the selection looks epiphenomenal. The Map's distinctive claim—real local collapse, biased by a determinate, persisting self—is the feature that appears to be missing from every MMI variant surveyed here.

**No Many Worlds.** The indexical objection the Map runs against MWI seems to carry over to MMI largely unchanged. Before measurement there is one you; afterward, on MMI, there are many minds (or one smeared multimind), and nothing in the formalism appears to pick out which of them is the actual continuant "I." The Map reads the felt singularity of being *this* observer anticipating *one* outcome as evidence for a [haecceitistic](/concepts/haecceity/) fact about which experiencer is yours—precisely the fact Lockwood declares "senseless" to ask after. On this reading MMI relocates the indexical problem rather than closing it: minds-in-the-formalism do not, by themselves, supply the missing further fact. This is a disagreement at the framework boundary, and is honestly noted as such: an MMI theorist who, like Lockwood, takes the "which mind is mine?" question to be ill-formed is not contradicting their own commitments, so the objection presses the question rather than refuting MMI from inside it. The interface model, by contrast, has no superposed-observer term to leave un-minded and no successor-minds to choose among, because on the Map's account collapse is real and local—the same move the Map uses to dissolve the [multi-mind collapse problem](/concepts/multi-mind-collapse-problem/) when multiple observers share an entangled system.

**Occam's Razor Has Limits.** A discipline note. The Map must *not* lean on "an infinity of minds is profligate" as a parsimony refutation of MMI, just as it does not rest its rejection of MWI on counting worlds. [Tenet 5](/tenets/#occams-limits) warns that simplicity is an unreliable guide when knowledge is incomplete, and that cuts both ways. The load-bearing objection to MMI is *indexical*, not ontological: not "too many minds" but "no fact about which mind is mine."

## What Would Change This Verdict

The Map's reading of MMI is principled, not dogmatic. It would weaken if:

1. A many-minds theory supplied a non-arbitrary fact about which successor-mind is the genuine continuant—dissolving rather than relocating the indexical puzzle.
2. The measure-over-minds probability rule were shown to function as a usable decision guide for a single agent, closing the gap Barrett and later critics opened.
3. The hard problem were solved in a way compatible with an acausal, epiphenomenal mind—removing the Map's motivation for [bidirectional interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) and making MMI's passive mind tenable.

None of these currently obtains. MMI's lasting contribution to the Map looks negative and clarifying: it suggests that putting minds into the quantum formalism may not be enough on its own. Without genuine collapse and a determinate, persisting self, the experiencer seems to dissolve into a measure—and the question "why am I *this* one?" appears to go unanswered much as it does under MWI.
