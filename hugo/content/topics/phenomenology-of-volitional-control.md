When you decide to raise your hand, something happens that neuroscience has spent four decades trying to explain away. A readiness potential appears in your motor cortex hundreds of milliseconds before you report the conscious intention to move. Since Benjamin Libet's landmark 1983 experiments, this temporal gap has been treated as evidence that consciousness arrives too late to cause anything — that the feeling of willing is an afterthought to neural processes already underway. The Unfinishable Map argues this conclusion was premature. The phenomenology of volitional control — the first-person structure of willing, vetoing, and selecting among actions — reveals features that the timing data cannot dissolve and that point toward genuine [conscious causation](/concepts/mental-causation/).

## The Libet Experiments and Their Reinterpretation

Libet et al. (1983) asked subjects to flex their wrist whenever they felt like it while watching a clock. The readiness potential (RP) — a slow negative EEG shift in premotor cortex — began approximately 550 milliseconds before movement, while subjects reported conscious awareness of their intention only around 200 milliseconds before movement. The gap of roughly 350 milliseconds was widely interpreted as proof that the brain decides before consciousness knows about it.

This interpretation rested on treating the RP as an unconscious decision signal. Schurger, Sitt, and Dehaene (2012) dismantled that assumption. Their accumulator model showed that the RP's shape and timing are exactly what would be expected from ongoing stochastic fluctuations in neural activity — random noise drifting toward a motor threshold — rather than a goal-directed preparatory process. In Libet's paradigm, where subjects must eventually move but choose the moment, premotor activity hovers near threshold. Random fluctuations determine when the threshold is crossed. As Schurger et al. put it, "This interpretation of the readiness potential thus takes the sting out of the Libet argument against free will."

Sjöberg's 2024 analysis of patients who have had the supplementary motor area (SMA) resected — the very region generating the readiness potential — provides independent confirmation. These patients retain their subjective sense of willing while losing voluntary movement execution. If the RP were the neural signature of genuine volition, removing its source should impair the experience of willing. It does not.

Alfred Mele's philosophical critique reinforced this reinterpretation from a different angle. What subjects become aware of at the 200-millisecond mark may be "an urge to flex that has already arisen," not a decision already made. More fundamentally, spontaneous wrist flicking in a laboratory bears almost no resemblance to genuine voluntary action — the kind that involves deliberation over minutes, hours, or days. The ecological validity of the paradigm is low. Libet's experiments test impulse timing, not [free will](/concepts/concept-of-free-will/).

## The Structure of Voluntary Action: Agency and Ownership

The phenomenological tradition, particularly the work of Shaun Gallagher and Dan Zahavi, reveals that the experience of voluntary action has an internal structure that simple timing studies cannot capture. (For extended treatment of the agency-passivity axis, see [Phenomenology of Agency vs Passivity](/topics/phenomenology-of-agency-vs-passivity/).) Two components are distinguishable in first-person experience:

**Sense of ownership** — the feeling that *my body* is moving. This is present in both voluntary and involuntary movement. When a doctor taps your knee and your leg kicks, you experience ownership ("that's my leg") without agency.

**Sense of agency** — the feeling that *I am initiating* the action. This is present only in voluntary movement. When you deliberately raise your hand, ownership and agency coincide; when your hand jerks in a startle reflex, only ownership remains.

This distinction matters because the Libet experiments concern only the timing of one narrow aspect of agency — the moment of conscious awareness of an urge — while ignoring the broader phenomenological structure. Gallagher further distinguishes motor-level agency (SAm), the feeling of controlling specific bodily movements, from intentional-level agency (SAi), the feeling of pursuing goals through action. The readiness potential relates to motor preparation. It says nothing about the higher-order, intentional level where agents form plans, weigh reasons, and select among possible courses of action.

Clinical evidence strengthens this analysis. In schizophrenia, patients may experience ownership without agency — their body moves, but they do not feel they are causing the movement, attributing it instead to external forces (Gallagher, 2000). In anarchic hand syndrome, a limb performs purposeful actions the patient disowns. These dissociations demonstrate that the sense of agency tracks real causal structures in the nervous system rather than being a simple post-hoc confabulation. If agency were merely a story the brain tells after the fact, it would be difficult to explain why it can be selectively disrupted by specific neural pathologies while ownership remains intact.

## Conscious Veto: The "Whether" Component

Libet himself recognized that his experiments did not eliminate conscious causal power. He proposed "free won't" — the idea that while the brain may initiate action unconsciously, consciousness retains the power to veto prepared movements in the approximately 150 milliseconds before execution. Subjects in his experiments could and did cancel planned movements after becoming aware of the urge.

Marcel Brass and Patrick Haggard (2007, 2008) provided neural evidence for this proposal through their "what, when, whether" model of intentional action. Each component has distinct neural substrates:

- **What** (which action to perform) — lateral prefrontal and premotor areas
- **When** (the timing of action) — supplementary motor area
- **Whether** (to act or refrain) — dorsal fronto-median cortex (dFMC)

The "whether" component is the most philosophically significant. When subjects intentionally inhibited a prepared action, Brass and Haggard observed specific activation in dFMC, which exerts top-down inhibition of premotor areas. This is not merely the absence of a go signal — it is an active neural process of cancellation, with its own distinct circuitry.

Critics have objected that the veto itself must be unconsciously initiated, generating an infinite regress. But this objection assumes what it needs to prove — that every neural event must have a prior neural cause sufficient to explain it. The Map's framework offers an alternative: the "whether" decision is precisely the kind of process where [consciousness selects among neural patterns](/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/), biasing quantum-indeterminate outcomes in the brain toward one resolution rather than another. The veto does not require a prior unconscious veto because it operates at the [interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/) where conscious selection meets neural preparation. For how this mechanism might work through the quantum Zeno effect applied specifically to motor selection, see [Motor Control and the Quantum Zeno Framework](/topics/motor-control-quantum-zeno/). The [timing constraints](/topics/quantum-neural-timing-constraints/) for such quantum effects in neural tissue remain an active area of investigation.

## Against the Illusion Theory

Daniel Wegner's *The Illusion of Conscious Will* (2002) represents the strongest case for treating volitional experience as entirely epiphenomenal. Wegner argued that the experience of willing is produced by a brain system separate from the system that produces action — a kind of "modular [epiphenomenalism](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/)" where the feeling of authorship is always a post-hoc construction.

Wegner drew on cases where willing and action dissociate: automatisms, hypnotic behaviour, the ideomotor effect, alien hand syndrome. In each case, either action occurs without a feeling of willing, or a feeling of willing occurs without the corresponding action. Wegner concluded that the link between the two is always inferential, never causal.

Eddy Nahmias (2002) identified the central weakness in Wegner's argument: showing that the feeling of willing *can* be miscalibrated does not show it is *always* an illusion. Perception can produce illusions — optical illusions are well documented — yet no one concludes that vision is therefore always illusory. The cases Wegner cites (automatisms, hypnosis, alien hand) are precisely the *pathological* exceptions against which the normal operation of conscious will is defined. They demonstrate that the sense of agency can malfunction, which is evidence that it normally tracks something real rather than evidence that it never does.

The Map's [reportability argument](/topics/the-epiphenomenalist-threat/) applies with particular force here. If conscious will were genuinely illusory — if the experience of willing never causally influenced action — then our reports about the experience of willing would be causally disconnected from the experience itself. Wegner, writing a book about conscious will, would have to be producing those words through neural processes that happen to correlate with his experience of willing, without the experience playing any causal role. The position is self-undermining: it cannot be rationally held by someone who arrived at it through reflection on their own volitional experience.

## The Temporal Puzzle Reconsidered

Braun, Wessler, and Friese's 2021 meta-analysis of Libet-style experiments found that the evidence for RP-preceding-intention is "uncertain and based on only a small number of studies." The field has moved substantially from the early claim that neuroscience disproved free will. What remains is a genuine puzzle about temporal ordering — neural preparation does precede reported awareness of intention — but this puzzle admits multiple interpretations:

1. **Consciousness selects among ongoing activations.** The brain continuously generates candidate motor patterns. Consciousness does not initiate activity from scratch but selects which of many competing preparations to permit or suppress — consistent with the "whether" component and [neural pattern selection](/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/).

2. **Reported timing may not reflect actual timing.** Introspective reports about the moment of awareness are themselves complex judgements. The clock-watching method Libet used introduces its own latencies and biases. The reported time of awareness may lag behind the actual moment consciousness begins to influence the process.

3. **Neural preparation is not decision.** Schurger's stochastic model shows that neural activity ramping toward threshold is not the same as an unconscious decision. The brain prepares many possible actions simultaneously. That preparation precedes awareness is no more surprising than a pianist's fingers hovering near keys before a deliberate chord.

None of these interpretations require that consciousness is epiphenomenal. All are compatible with the Map's framework of [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) in which consciousness causally participates in action through selecting among quantum-indeterminate neural outcomes — a specific instance of the broader [causal powers](/topics/consciousness-and-causal-powers/) the Map attributes to consciousness. For the broader convergence of evolutionary, cognitive, neurological, and quantum-biological evidence supporting this selection hypothesis, see [Empirical Evidence for Consciousness-Selecting](/topics/empirical-evidence-for-consciousness-selecting/).

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map's [tenets](/tenets/) predict exactly the pattern the evidence reveals. The **Dualism** tenet holds that consciousness is irreducible to physical processes — and the phenomenological structure of volitional experience (the agency-ownership distinction, the multi-level nature of intentional action) resists reduction to neural timing data. These are first-person structural features that no third-person measurement captures.

The **Bidirectional Interaction** tenet holds that consciousness causally influences the physical world. Brass and Haggard's "whether" component provides a plausible neural locus for this influence — a decision point where conscious veto operates through distinct neural circuitry. The Map interprets this as a site where consciousness modulates quantum-indeterminate processes, consistent with the **Minimal Quantum Interaction** tenet.

The **Occam's Razor Has Limits** tenet is directly relevant to the Libet debate's history. The "simpler" interpretation — that the RP proves consciousness is epiphenomenal — was accepted for decades before Schurger's reinterpretation revealed it as premature. The rush to eliminate conscious causation in favour of a parsimonious neural account illustrates how simplicity can mislead when understanding is incomplete.

The Map speculates that the phenomenology of volitional control points toward a richer picture than either the illusionist or the simple libertarian account provides. Consciousness may not initiate neural activity from nothing, but it is not merely along for the ride. It selects, vetoes, and modulates — operating at the interface between quantum indeterminacy and neural pattern formation. The felt experience of willing, far from being an illusion, may be our most direct evidence of the mind-body interface in action.
