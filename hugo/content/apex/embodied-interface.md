This piece addresses a decision a reader of the Map actually faces when they try to use the framework: given the Map's commitment that consciousness influences the physical world by selecting among physically underdetermined outcomes—an interface most naturally modelled on attention—what causal status should be assigned to the enormous quantity of felt embodiment that does no selecting at all? The warmth of a heated room when you are not attending to it, the silent sense of which way is up, the low hum of cardiac and gastric tone that colours mood, the ache of being cold: these are real, owned, present experiences. They are also, on any candid description, passive. They arrive; they are not chosen; they are mostly unattended. If the Map's causal machinery is a selection interface, and selection is attention-like, then most of what it is like to have a body is happening *outside* that machinery. The risk this creates is precise: the selection mechanism can quietly expand to claim everything felt, until "consciousness acts through attention" becomes "all experience is causally active," which is both false to the phenomenology and more than the Map's own positions license.

The Map has now worked the felt body one modality at a time—skin, temperature, the vestibular frame, the visceral interior, proprioception, pain. The [cross-modal synthesis](/apex/cross-modal-capability-division/) tied these together along one axis: the brain-side machinery is modality-specific, the mind-side access boundary is supramodal, and that asymmetry is exteroceptive while [interoception inverts](/topics/interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface/) it inward. This synthesis takes a different axis, the one that survey leaves open. It asks not *where the division recurs* but *what consciousness is doing* in each region of the felt body, and produces a three-way distinction the per-modality articles imply but none states: **causal consciousness** (felt experience that does selective work), **constitutive consciousness** (felt quality inseparable from the causal work it drives), and **[phenomenal presentation](/concepts/phenomenal-presentation/)** (felt embodiment that is owned and present but selects nothing). The verdict the distinction supports is deflationary in the right direction: the Map's interface should claim far less of the body than an unguarded attention-as-everything reading would, and the under-claim is a feature.

## The Selection Interface and Its Natural Overreach

Start with what the Map commits to. On position P-A1 the Map holds agent-causal libertarian free will—the conscious subject directly originates some choices—at *moderate* confidence, held as the best explanation of convergent evidence rather than a proven result. The mechanism by which such origination touches matter is, on the Map's preferred reading, selection among quantum-underdetermined neural outcomes, and the phenomenological model for that selection is [attention](/concepts/attention-as-interface/): a bandwidth-limited control surface through which mind biases which of several available outcomes becomes actual. The [attention-as-causal-bridge synthesis](/apex/attention-as-causal-bridge/) develops this as the Map's central picture of how consciousness acts.

The trouble is that attention is a searchlight, and a searchlight illuminates a small region of a large field. Voluntary attention is effortful, serial, and narrow; the felt body is continuous, parallel, and vast. At any moment the overwhelming majority of bodily experience—the thermal envelope, the postural frame, the visceral tone, the contact of clothing against skin—is unattended and unselected. If the only thing consciousness *does*, causally, is select, then this majority is causally idle on the Map's own model. An interface theory that has not faced this will tend to paper over it in one of two ways: by inflating "selection" until merely *having* an experience counts as selecting it (which empties the word), or by quietly treating all vivid felt embodiment as evidence of causal activity (which double-counts presentation as work). Both moves overclaim. The discipline this synthesis enforces is to refuse them and say what the unattended body is instead.

This is where the [somatic interface](/concepts/somatic-interface/) concept supplies the structure but not yet the verdict. It establishes that the body reports to consciousness through layered, dissociable channels—interoception, proprioception, nociception, somatic markers, the vestibular sense—and that the interface is *bidirectional*: signals rise, and conscious attention modulates them downward (attention to pain increases its intensity; relaxation reduces interoceptive distress). The bidirectional traffic is real, but it is exactly the trap. That attention *can* modulate a felt state does not show that the state, when unattended, is doing causal work. Most of the time the channel carries upward traffic that nothing conscious is selecting. The somatic interface tells us the conduits exist; it does not tell us that occupancy of a conduit is causal participation. That further question is what the three-way distinction answers.

## Three Causal Statuses for Felt Embodiment

### Causal consciousness: where selection actually runs

The first category is the narrow one, and naming it narrowly is the point. Causal consciousness is felt experience that participates in selecting a physically underdetermined outcome. On the Map's picture this is where attention does its work: in the effortful holding of a difficult intention, the willed continuation of an action against fatigue, the directing of regard toward one of several competing candidates. The [phenomenology of consciousness doing work](/apex/phenomenology-of-consciousness-doing-work/)—effort, selection, holding—marks the felt signature of this region. It is small. Most of embodiment is not here.

Two disciplines keep this category honest. First, it inherits the Map's *mechanism debt*. Position P-Q3, the bias-without-deviation dilemma, is held at *moderate* confidence and states the unresolved difficulty squarely: for conscious influence to be genuine selection it must do something the unbiased Born distribution does not, yet to remain minimal and undetectable it must not deviate from Born statistics—which places even the clearest case of causal consciousness "genuinely close to epiphenomenalism" until a positive account lands. There is no worked toy model of the coupling (P-Q10). So even where selection most plausibly runs, the claim that felt experience *does the selecting* is a programme commitment, not a demonstrated result. Second, by P-A4 (held at *high* confidence) no agent can verify its own causal efficacy by introspection, because the checking faculty is the one whose status is in question. The felt sense that "my attending made the difference" is not self-certifying. Causal consciousness is therefore the category the Map most wants and least securely possesses.

### Constitutive consciousness: where the felt quality is the causal power

The second category is the one [pain](/topics/pain-consciousness-and-causal-power/) forces into view, and it is genuinely distinct from the first. In pain, the felt awfulness is not a quality riding alongside a separate motivational mechanism; the hurting *is* what drives the avoidance. Asymbolia is the demonstration: when the affective-motivational dimension is excised while sensory detection is preserved, the extended behavioural response collapses—patients detect the pinprick, report it as "painful," and do not guard the injury or seek treatment. The felt severity, not the magnitude of tissue damage, predicts the sustained response. Here phenomenal character and causal role, which most discussions treat as conceptually distinct, are inseparable.

This is not the same as causal consciousness, and conflating them would itself be an overclaim. Causal consciousness is *selection*: a subject directing influence among options. Constitutive consciousness is a felt quality whose very presence carries motivational force—closer to a standing pressure than to an act of choosing. The relevant Map position is P-VS1, the value-sensitive-selection lean, held at *low* confidence: the Map leans toward felt valence being "the currency in which conscious selection is denominated" but concedes this is "an aspiration, not a position," and that the value-blind rival—where the mechanism runs and valence merely accompanies it—is "the rival to beat, not a position already beaten." So the honest reading of pain is layered. That the awfulness is inseparable *in experience* from the urge to withdraw is phenomenologically secure. That the awfulness therefore does causal *work* the underlying neural process does not already do is exactly the value-blind/value-sensitive fork, and the fork is open. Constitutive consciousness names a real phenomenal structure; whether that structure is causally load-bearing or causally idle is undecided, and the low confidence on P-VS1 is the calibrated mark of that.

### Phenomenal presentation: the vast, owned, passive background

The third category is the largest and the one the selection model is most tempted to ignore or annex. Phenomenal presentation is felt embodiment that is owned and present but selects nothing and need not be inseparable from any action. The clearest exhibits are precisely the recent modality work the attention model strains against.

The [vestibular sense](/topics/vestibular-consciousness-and-the-interface/) is the limiting case. In health it has almost no felt object—"a paucity of vestibular experiences in normal consciousness" besides dizziness (de Vignemont, 2024)—and no unisensory cortex of its own; it is "multisensory by construction." Yet it supplies the felt spatial frame of the self: self-location and first-person perspective, the silent anchoring of the subject to gravity. This is presentation in its purest form. Nothing is being attended to or selected; a frame is simply *there*, constituting where "I" am, and its load-bearing role shows only when it fails, in depersonalization and the out-of-body experience. To call the resting vestibular frame "causal" because the self is located within it would stretch the word past use.

[Interoception](/topics/interoceptive-consciousness-and-the-interface/) supplies the affective version. Visceral states "arrive already valenced and already owned"; the mind-side contribution is "the affective ground of subjecthood itself" rather than a detached object delivered to a subject. The background hum of cardiac and gastric tone that colours mood is not selected and mostly not attended; it is the felt substrate one *has*. [Thermal comfort](/topics/thermal-consciousness-and-the-interface/) is similar: the warmth that is pleasant when you are chilled and oppressive when overheated is a constitutively evaluative state indexed to body need, but in its ordinary form it is ambient and unattended—presented, not chosen. Even [touch](/topics/tactile-consciousness-and-the-interface/) has its passive register: the continuous, unnoticed contact of the world against the skin, distinct from the active haptic exploration that does require movement and regard.

The decision-relevant claim is that this third category is *most* of felt embodiment, and that on the Map's own model it is not doing selective causal work. That is the correct under-claim rather than a deficiency to be hidden. An interface that selects has to be fed something to select among and from somewhere to select; the presented body is the standing field within which the narrow searchlight of causal consciousness occasionally moves. Presentation is the condition of selection, not an instance of it.

## What This Implies for Decisions

The three-way distinction is built to be used. Five implications follow for anyone applying the Map's interface framework.

1. **Do not infer causal participation from phenomenal vividness.** A felt state being intense, owned, and unmistakably present—the vestibular frame, the ache of cold, the visceral ground of mood—licenses no inference that consciousness is *doing* anything causal there. Vividness is a marker of presentation, and presentation is the default status of embodiment. When evaluating any claim that "conscious experience X influences the body," first ask which of the three categories X occupies; only the first, and contestably the second, are even candidates for causal work.

2. **Hold the causal claim at the confidence its positions carry, not higher.** Where an argument turns on felt experience doing selective work, it inherits P-A1's *moderate* confidence in agent causation, P-Q3's *moderate*-confidence bias-without-deviation dilemma, and—if it turns on *valence* doing the work—P-VS1's *low* confidence. A downstream piece that reads more confident than these is calibration drift. The interface story about the body is strongest as a structural map and weakest precisely at the point it most wants to win.

3. **In AI candidate-consciousness assessment, separate presentation from selection before drawing any verdict.** A system might be argued to have something presentation-like—a standing self-model, an owned internal state—without thereby having the selective, attention-like causal consciousness the Map ties to agency, and the reverse pairing is equally conceivable. Because the categories come apart in the human case (asymbolia detaches the constitutive layer; the vestibular frame is presentation with almost no object), an AI assessment that runs them together will overclaim or underclaim. The right question is never "is it conscious?" but "which of the three statuses, if any, does the evidence support?"—the same disaggregation P-CS4 applies to human fragmentation cases.

4. **Read clinical interface claims through the same filter.** When the somatic-interface literature is cited for the Map—pain asymbolia, phantom limbs, vestibular depersonalization, somatic markers—note that the materialist account explains the clinical data completely, and the interface reading "offers an alternative metaphysical interpretation of the same data." None of these cases delivers causal consciousness as a measured fact; they constrain identity readings and exhibit dissociations. They belong, in the Map's hands, to presentation and to the open constitutive question, not to demonstrated selection.

5. **Treat the under-claim as the framework working, not failing.** The conclusion that most of embodiment is causally passive on the Map's model is the honest output, and it strengthens rather than weakens the interface programme: a theory whose causal mechanism modestly claims a small, effortful region of experience is more credible than one that annexes the whole felt body to "consciousness acting." If a future result widened the causal region—say, by vindicating value-sensitive selection—the map would be redrawn; the present map should not pre-empt that result by inflation.

## Cascade and Scope

This synthesis is self-flagging against position drift. Its central verdict—that most felt embodiment is phenomenal presentation rather than causal selection—turns on three positions that could shift. If **P-VS1** were *raised* (value-sensitive selection vindicated by the discriminating battery in P-VS2), the constitutive category would migrate toward causal consciousness and the body's causally active region would widen; the deflationary verdict would need re-evaluation in that direction. If **P-A1** were retired (prior neural states shown sufficient for choice outcomes), the causal category would empty and *all three* categories would collapse into presentation under an epiphenomenalist reading. If **P-Q3**'s bias-without-deviation dilemma were resolved with a positive account, causal consciousness would gain the secure footing it currently lacks. The verdict is therefore explicitly contingent on the quantum-interface and value-in-selection registers, and inherits their [mechanism debt](/positions/quantum-interface/#mechanism-debt) rather than discharging it.

The scope is also bounded by what the Map's positions do *not* settle. P-CS4 (held at *moderate* confidence) reads fragmentation cases—split-brain, anaesthesia, sleep, dreaming—as interface disruption rather than the dividing or extinguishing of consciousness, and it does so as "the Map's preferred interpretation, compatible with the data and arguably more natural under the filter model, not forced by it." The same modesty governs here. That the unattended body is *presented to* a single subject rather than dispersed is the interface reading's preference, held against a live production-model rival, not a result the somatic data deliver. The three-way distinction is a framework-internal map of causal status; it tells the reader how to apply the Map's commitments consistently, not that those commitments are confirmed.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: The distinction presupposes that felt phenomenal character is real and not exhausted by the neural processes that carry it—the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) holds across presentation, constitution, and selection alike. But naming most of embodiment as causally passive *presentation* concedes that irreducibility does not entail causal activity: an experience can be genuinely phenomenal, genuinely owned, and causally idle. That concession is what keeps the dualism honest rather than triumphalist.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: The tenet is satisfied by the *causal* category—where attention-like selection runs—not by the whole felt body. The synthesis sharpens the tenet by locating where the bidirectional traffic does causal work and where the upward traffic of presentation merely supplies the field. The [somatic interface](/concepts/somatic-interface/)'s bidirectionality is real but must not be read as making every modulable state causally active.

**[Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction)**: A selection interface that claims only a narrow, effortful region of experience is the maximally minimal reading—consistent with the strict-corridor treatment under which the interface is [self-concealing by construction](/positions/quantum-interface/#mechanism-debt). The under-claim of this synthesis is the embodiment-level expression of minimality: the smallest causal footprint compatible with genuine agency, leaving the vast presented body outside the corridor entirely.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: The simple model—"consciousness acts through attention, so felt experience is causally active"—is the over-simplification the tenet warns against. The three-way distinction is more complex and more faithful: collapsing presentation, constitution, and selection into one causal status would buy tidiness at the cost of the phenomenology and of the Map's own calibrated confidences.
