# The Mereology of Mind

Two questions about parts and wholes sit at the centre of the metaphysics of consciousness, and they are usually asked apart. The first is the question of *composition*: when do some objects form a genuine further object, rather than a mere heap? The second is the question of *individuation*: what draws the boundary around a single conscious subject, so that the world contains many discrete experiencers rather than one vast field or none? Asked together, they form a single shape. Composition runs upward — from parts toward a whole — and asks what makes the whole real. Individuation runs the other way — from a whole toward its boundary — and asks what makes the subject *one*. Mereology, the formal study of parts and wholes, is the common discipline. The thesis here is that consciousness sits at the joint between these two questions, answering both in a way that physical mereology cannot, and that the cleanest evidence for this is a problem that runs in the opposite direction from the famous one: not how subjects combine, but how a subject could ever divide. That reverse problem — the de-combination problem — is the hinge on which composition and individuation turn out to be the same question seen from two sides.

The claim is deliberately calibrated. The Map reads the convergence of composition theories on the domain where consciousness occurs as *suggestive* support for its dualist picture, not as proof; and where the metaphysics runs out, the tie is broken by the Map's [tenets](/tenets/) rather than by the evidence. The synthesis is offered as the more natural reading given those commitments, not as a result the mereology forces on a neutral inquirer.

## The Upward Question: What Makes a Whole?

The Special Composition Question — Peter van Inwagen's name (*Material Beings*, 1990) for the question of when some objects compose a further object — has three broad families of answer. *Universalism* says any collection composes something, generating an ontology in which your shoe and a distant galaxy form an object. *Nihilism* says composition never happens: there are only simples "arranged tablewise," and no genuine wholes at all. *Restricted composition* says some collections compose and others do not, and owes an account of the restriction that does not collapse into arbitrariness.

What makes the restricted answer interesting for the philosophy of mind is where its most serious proposals land. As [consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/) sets out in full, three programmes that share almost no premises arrive in a common neighbourhood. Van Inwagen's organicism restricts composition to cases where the activity of simples constitutes a *life*. Trenton Merricks' eliminativism (*Objects and Persons*, 2001) keeps only composites with irreducible causal powers, and argues that conscious organisms alone escape elimination because consciousness "does not even globally supervene on microscopic physical properties." Kelvin McQueen and Naotsugu Tsuchiya (2023) make composition track maximised integrated information (Φ) — the integrated structure that consciousness is *suspected* to involve — though they pointedly decouple that composition criterion from consciousness itself.

The convergence is real only after abstraction: van Inwagen reaches organic life-unity, McQueen and Tsuchiya reach an information measure they refuse to identify with experience, and only Merricks names consciousness directly. The honest reading is that these heterogeneous criteria cluster around *the domain where consciousness tends to occur*, not that they jointly prove consciousness is the criterion. And the convergence faces strong [rivals the Map does not defeat on neutral ground](/concepts/composition-question-rivals/). The first is [information-compression composition](/concepts/composition-question-rivals/#information-compression-composition): Alexander Bird's thesis (2023) that parts compose exactly when treating them as one compresses the world's description — a fully non-conscious, ostensibly objective answer, refined by Majid Beni's thermodynamic-depth constraint (2025). The second, deeper, rival is [metaontological deflationism](/concepts/composition-question-rivals/#metaontological-deflationism): Amie Thomasson's easy ontology and Eli Hirsch's quantifier variance hold that there is no deep compositional fact for *any* criterion to track. If the deflationist is right, the convergence dissolves into an artefact of how disputants regiment the word "object." The Map's reply to both is a framework-boundary one, not a refutation — it presses the standing burden of explaining why our compositional intuitions cluster so persistently around organised, integrated systems, while granting that pressing a burden is not winning the argument.

## The Downward Question: What Bounds a Subject?

Now turn the arrow around. Grant that there are genuine conscious wholes. What makes any one of them *this* subject, bounded off from every other? As [consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation](/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation/) develops, no physical criterion settles the boundary. It is not the boundary of a body (conjoined twins share one; [split-brain patients](/topics/split-brain-consciousness/) may harbour more than one consciousness in a single skull). It is not the boundary of a brain region (no neural threshold cleanly separates one subject from two). It is not the boundary of a causal process, since conversation and shared environments cross between subjects without merging them. Under the Map's [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) tenet, the hope that a sufficiently detailed neural account would eventually fix the boundary is unavailable in principle: if consciousness is not reducible to physical processes, no physical description can explain why *this* set of experiences belongs to *this* subject.

The space of answers is ancient and, tellingly, still open after three millennia. Daniel Kolak's taxonomy (2004) gives three exhaustive positions: *closed individualism* (each person a distinct bounded subject — common sense, owing an account of the boundary), *empty individualism* (subject boundaries are conventional, the self a useful fiction — gracefully handling fission but clashing with the certainty that *this* subject experiences *this* moment), and *open individualism* (there is one subject appearing as many — inverting the puzzle into why separation *appears* at all). Immanuel Kant's old objection bites hardest here: physical objects are individuated by spatial position, but two qualitatively identical *minds*, lacking extension, would share all their properties — so what makes them two? The dualist's available replies — [primitive thisness](/concepts/haecceity/), Martine Nida-Rümelin's primitive identity (2010), Caspar Hare's irreducible "presence" (2009), individuation by unique causal history — each, on inspection, *names* the boundary rather than explaining it. The causal-history answer is the most promising and least circular, since the Map's [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) tenet supplies a real ongoing coupling between *this* consciousness and *this* brain; but it needs a starting point — what individuated the subjects before they began accumulating distinct histories — that it cannot itself provide.

## The Hinge: The De-Combination Problem

The two questions look separate until one notices that the same gap defeats both. Panpsychism's [combination problem](/concepts/combination-problem/) — William James's "mind-dust" puzzle (1890), sharpened by David Chalmers (2017) — asks how micro-subjects combine *upward* into a macro-subject. Sam Coleman (2014) gives the structural dilemma: if subjects A and B combine into C, either A and B cease to exist (so nothing combined) or all three coexist (so C was *added*, not composed). Either way, no operation sums subjects.

Gregory Miller's de-combination problem ("Can Subjects Be Proper Parts of Subjects? The De-Combination Problem," *Ratio* 31(2), 2018, 137–154) is this puzzle run *downward*. Miller's primary target is cosmopsychism — the view, adopted by some who despaired of the combination problem, that there is one cosmos-sized subject from which our individual minds derive. Miller argues that cosmopsychism does not escape the difficulty: if the cosmic subject is to *de-combine* into many smaller subjects, it faces a problem equivalent to combination, because both turn on whether one subject can be a proper part of another. His diagnosis is that subject-summing and subject-dividing are the same impossibility approached from opposite ends, and his proposed escapes — distinguishing absolute from relative phenomenal unity, or modifying the essential nature of subjects — are offered to *both* the panpsychist and the cosmopsychist at once, precisely because the two problems are structurally one.

That structural identity is the hinge. Combination is the limiting case of *composition* applied to subjects: can experiential parts form an experiential whole? De-combination is the limiting case of *individuation* applied to a putative whole: can an experiential whole partition into experiential parts? Miller's result — that neither direction admits a coherent subject-part relation — says the upward and downward questions are answered by the same missing principle. We lack any account of how consciousness *divides into units*, and that single lack is what makes both the composition criterion uncertain and the individuation boundary unexplained. The open-individualist who treats reality as one field must explain de-combination; the panpsychist who builds minds from micro-experiences must explain combination; and the Map, which rejects both, inherits the *generalised* version: whatever the building blocks, the operation that would relate a subject to a sub-subject is missing in both directions.

## Why the Joint Resists Physical Mereology

Physical mereology is transparent in both directions. Two one-kilogram masses compose a two-kilogram mass, and a two-kilogram mass divides back into them; charges superpose and decompose; the whole is built from parts and the parts explain the whole. Consciousness, as [composition-and-consciousness](/concepts/composition-and-consciousness/) argues, does neither. Bottom-up, micro-experiences do not sum into a macro-experience — James's hundred feelings, shuffled and packed as close as you like, each "remains the same old private self." Top-down, a [unified field](/concepts/unity-of-consciousness/) does not partition into independent experiential parts — Tim Bayne and David Chalmers (2003) show phenomenal unity to involve a conjoint phenomenology no part possesses, so there is nothing for the field to be *made of* in the part-summing sense.

This bidirectional resistance is exactly what the de-combination hinge predicts. A property that genuinely composed and decomposed transparently would let combination *and* de-combination go through; a property that resists both is one for which the subject-part relation is unintelligible in either direction. Physical properties belong to the first kind; consciousness, on the evidence the cluster assembles, to the second. The split-brain literature is the empirical pressure point and it cuts the same way: researchers cannot agree what would *count* as one subject versus two (Pinto et al. 2017 read undivided consciousness where Sperry and Gazzaniga read two agents; Elizabeth Schechter 2018 reads "two minds, one person"), which is precisely what one expects if there is no determinate fact about how a conscious whole partitions — no mereology of subjects to read off the anatomy.

## Synthesis

Set the two questions in one frame and the picture is this. Composition asks what makes a conscious whole *real*; individuation asks what makes it *one*; and the de-combination problem shows that these are the same question, because both stand or fall on whether a subject can bear the part-whole relation to another subject — and it cannot, in either direction. The most plausible reading of a property that refuses composition and decomposition alike is that it is not a *mereological* item at all: not a sum of parts, not a divisible whole, but something whose unity is primitive — brought to physical organisation rather than built out of it. That is the [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) tenet's irreducibility claim, now grounded in a structural feature rather than merely asserted: reduction proceeds by decomposing wholes into parts, and consciousness does not decompose.

Three disciplines keep this from overreaching. First, the convergence on consciousness as composition criterion is *suggestive*, not probative — it is consistent with a shared truth and equally with a shared blind spot in one analytic tradition, and the metaontological deflationist can deny there is any compositional fact to converge on. The Map does not refute deflationism; it records the disagreement as honestly bedrock. Second, the Map's chief *in-framework* rivals survive the joint analysis: information-compression composition gives a non-conscious answer to the upward question, and constitutive panpsychism gives a monist answer to the same data the dualist reads its way — the compositional facts do not adjudicate between dualist interaction and panpsychist constitution, and the Map's preference there is settled by the [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) and [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) tenets, not by the mereology. The monist does not arrive at combination for free either: the same de-combination problem that constrains the dualist's individuation also constrains the cosmopsychist's, which is Miller's point. Third, the tenet-driven tie-break is acknowledged *as* tenet-driven. Where the evidence is genuinely matched, the Map's reading is the one its foundational commitments favour — tenet-coherent, not evidence-elevating.

What the joint frame adds beyond either source question alone is a single explanatory target. A complete account of mind would have to supply *one* principle — the missing subject-part relation — that simultaneously fixes which conscious wholes are real and what bounds each of them. That no such principle is in hand, in either the upward or the downward direction, is not a local gap in two separate debates but one gap appearing twice. Naming it that way is the synthesis: the mereology of mind has a single hole at its centre, and consciousness is what sits in it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism).** The joint analysis grounds irreducibility in structure. If consciousness composed and decomposed like a physical property, it could be reduced to its parts; that it does neither — combination fails upward, de-combination fails downward — is why no physical description fixes either which wholes are conscious or what bounds them. The irreducibility is exhibited, not merely posited.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction).** Merricks' irreducible causal powers and the causal-history answer to individuation both require that consciousness *does work* — that it is not epiphenomenal but contributes powers and maintains boundaries the physical parts alone do not. A non-compositional subject must act as a whole, at a locus, because it has no sub-experiential components to delegate to.

**[Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) and [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds).** These tenets do the tie-breaking work the mereology cannot. Where the compositional data leave dualist interaction and panpsychist constitution matched, the Map's reading of consciousness as a distinct interacting category — biasing quantum indeterminacies at an interface, in a single definite world with [determinate subjects](/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-individuation/) rather than branch-relative ones — is a constrained commitment, openly tenet-driven, not a result the metaphysics of composition secures on its own.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits).** Universalism and nihilism are simpler than restricted composition; empty individualism is simpler than primitive thisness; monism is simpler than dualism. In each case the simpler answer purchases its economy by eliminating or redescribing the phenomenon — the conscious subject — whose mereological behaviour is the thing to be explained. The Map's refusal to let the simpler ontology settle a phenomenon several independent analyses treat as fundamental is the fifth tenet in action.
