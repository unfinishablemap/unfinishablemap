**Source versus leeway incompatibilism** is the taxonomy that divides the incompatibilist half of the free-will debate into two families, according to *what free will is taken to require*. **Leeway incompatibilism** holds that free will and moral responsibility require *alternative possibilities*—the ability to do otherwise, genuine open options—so that determinism threatens freedom by closing off the alternatives. **Source incompatibilism** holds that what matters is being the *ultimate source* or origin of one's action—an actual-sequence property—so that determinism threatens responsibility even if it should turn out that alternatives are not required at all. The distinction is neutral, shared machinery of the whole debate: it is a claim about the *structure* of the freedom condition, and by itself it carries no commitment to dualism, to determinism, or to any particular metaphysics. [Compatibilists](/concepts/compatibilism/) divide along the same seam. Understanding where an argument sits on this map is the first step to seeing what it can and cannot deliver—and, for the Map, where its own libertarianism is located among the options rather than proved by them.

## The Core Distinction

The two families answer a single question differently: *in virtue of what* is an action free or responsible-grounding?

**Leeway incompatibilism** locates the answer in *alternative sequences*. An agent acts freely only if, holding the past and the laws fixed, more than one future was genuinely available to her—she could have done otherwise. This is the intuition behind the **Principle of Alternate Possibilities** (PAP): responsibility requires open alternatives. On this view determinism is disqualifying because it leaves exactly one possible continuation of any actual history.

**Source incompatibilism** locates the answer in the *actual sequence*. What matters is not whether other options were open but whether the action's causal springs trace back to the agent herself rather than to factors ultimately beyond her control. An agent is free only if she is the genuine originator—the ultimate source—of what she does. Determinism is disqualifying here for a different reason: it makes every action the endpoint of a causal chain reaching back before the agent existed, so the causal buck never stops with her.

The two are not simply rivals. As the *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* notes in "Arguments for Incompatibilism," lacking the ability to do otherwise *could* undermine the self-determining control that sourcehood requires, so some theorists hold that a kind of leeway is needed for sourcehood after all. The distinction is better read as a spectrum of emphasis than a strict partition—a point the [narrow/wide split](#narrow-and-wide-source-incompatibilism) (explained below) makes precise.

## Frankfurt Cases: The Pivot

The distinction became load-bearing because of a specific historical pressure. In 1969 Harry Frankfurt constructed [counterexamples to PAP](/topics/frankfurt-cases-and-the-principle-of-alternate-possibilities/): a counterfactual intervener stands ready to force an agent's choice if he should waver, but never has to act, because the agent decides on his own. The agent could not have done otherwise—the intervener guaranteed the outcome—yet he seems fully responsible, since the guarantee played no role in what actually happened.

If the Frankfurt intuition holds, responsibility can be present while alternatives are absent, and PAP is false. Most compatibilists were persuaded; most incompatibilists were not. But the deeper effect was to *reorganize* incompatibilism. An incompatibilist convinced that PAP fails need not abandon incompatibilism—she can retreat from leeway to source, holding that determinism defeats responsibility by poisoning the actual causal history rather than by removing alternatives. Frankfurt cases are thus the fulcrum on which much of the field pivoted from leeway toward source framings, among compatibilists and incompatibilists alike. The migration is contested at every step (the "flicker of freedom" and dilemma-defense replies keep PAP alive), but the *direction* of gravity is widely reported.

## Where the Map's Three Incompatibilism Arguments Sit

This taxonomy organizes three arguments the Map treats in detail, and reading them through it clarifies what each one is doing.

- The [Consequence Argument](/topics/the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism/) (van Inwagen) is a **leeway** argument. Its conclusion is that, under determinism, we *cannot do otherwise*: our acts are the consequences of the remote past and the laws, over neither of which we have any choice. It closes off alternatives, and its force is a claim about foreclosed options.
- The [Manipulation Argument](/topics/the-manipulation-argument-and-hard-incompatibilism/) (Pereboom) is a **source** argument. It never mentions alternatives. What defeats responsibility, across the four cases, is that the agent's action "results from a deterministic causal process that traces back to factors beyond his control"—a claim about causal *history*, not about foreclosed options. The determined agent is relevantly like the covertly manipulated one because both have their springs of action set from outside.
- [Frankfurt cases](/topics/frankfurt-cases-and-the-principle-of-alternate-possibilities/) are the **pivot** between them. They attack the leeway assumption the Consequence Argument trades on, and in doing so they motivate the retreat to source that the Manipulation Argument then exploits.

So the concept is the hub the three topic arguments link into: one names the leeway wing, one names the source wing, and one marks the hinge that turned the field from the first toward the second.

## Narrow and Wide Source Incompatibilism {#narrow-and-wide-source-incompatibilism}

Kevin Timpe's *Free Will: Sourcehood and Its Alternatives* (2008) named and systematized the distinction and argued for a version of source incompatibilism. Timpe's own refinement splits the source family in two. **Narrow** source incompatibilism holds that sourcehood *alone* is required, with no alternative-possibilities condition: an agent could lack alternatives entirely and still be free, provided she is the ultimate origin of her action. **Wide** source incompatibilism holds that ultimate sourcehood *itself requires* morally relevant alternative possibilities—that one cannot be the genuine origin of an action without there having been more than one thing one could originate.

The wide view is a bridge. It concedes that sourcehood is the fundamental requirement while insisting that leeway comes along with it, so the two conditions need not be exclusive. This matters for locating the Map, which wants both a real origination and a real open alternative at the point of choice.

## Source Incompatibilism Is Not Libertarianism

A frequent error is to treat "source incompatibilist" as a synonym for "libertarian." It is not, and the difference is the sharpest calibration point in this whole area.

Source incompatibilism is a claim about *what free will would require*—ultimate origination, incompatible with determinism. It says nothing about whether that requirement is ever *met*. Derk Pereboom is the decisive example: he holds the source premise in full, yet denies that we have free will at all. His **hard incompatibilism** argues that determinism defeats sourcehood *and* that the indeterminism our physics might supply cannot restore it (it adds luck, not control). Pereboom is a source incompatibilist who concludes we should live *without* free will.

Libertarians and hard incompatibilists therefore *share* the source premise and diverge only on whether it is satisfiable. The libertarian says ultimate origination is real; the hard incompatibilist says it is not to be had. Reading source incompatibilism as inherently libertarian collapses that disagreement and misrepresents the position's most prominent defender.

## The Compatibilist Sibling: Semicompatibilism

The taxonomy is cross-cutting because the same source-versus-leeway split runs through compatibilism. The clearest case is John Martin Fischer and Mark Ravizza's *Responsibility and Control* (1998), the paradigm **actual-sequence** compatibilist account. Responsibility, on their view, requires **guidance control**—the action must issue from the agent's *own*, appropriately [reasons-responsive](/concepts/reasons-responsiveness/) mechanism—rather than **regulative control**, the dual power to do otherwise. Reasons-responsiveness is analyzed counterfactually, but the responsibility-grounding fact is a property of the *actual* mechanism.

Fischer's **semicompatibilism** then holds that moral responsibility is compatible with determinism (it needs only guidance control) even if the freedom to do otherwise is not. This is an actual-sequence, source-flavored view on the compatibilist side of the aisle. It shows that the source/actual-sequence framing is neutral between compatibilism and incompatibilism: source incompatibilism is, structurally, what you get by taking Fischer's actual-sequence emphasis and *adding* the claim that the actual sequence must not be deterministic. Fischer's own template is the one every source theorist builds on, which is why the taxonomy cannot be read as a proprietary incompatibilist device.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map is committed to an incompatibilist, agent-causal libertarianism, and this taxonomy locates that commitment as *one position among several* rather than as a result the distinction establishes. The distinction itself is neutral; nothing about carving incompatibilism into leeway and source families favors dualism, and the Map should not present it as though it did.

Where does the Map sit? Its first instinct is leeway: [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) and [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) have consciousness selecting among *genuinely open* physical outcomes, and [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) insists the rejected alternatives are genuinely excluded rather than realized elsewhere. But the Map also cares that the selection be the agent's *own*—that consciousness, not a prior physical chain, be the source of which outcome becomes actual. That is a sourcehood commitment, and it is what [agent causation](/concepts/agent-causation/) supplies. The Map wants both conditions, which places its closest mainstream neighbour at Timpe's **wide** source incompatibilism: sourcehood as fundamental, with genuine alternatives coming along with it.

Two tenets explain *why* the Map cares about both, without turning that care into evidence for either. [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) is why sourcehood is non-negotiable: if consciousness makes a real difference to the physical world, the agent has to be the origin of that difference, or the interface does no work. And [Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits) governs how the Map holds the position. Pereboom's case that indeterministic sourcehood is mere luck is a plausibility-and-parsimony judgement, not a proof; Tenet 5 lets the Map decline to treat "we have no positive reason to posit agent-causal origination" as decisive—while conceding, symmetrically, that this buys the Map no positive support either. The wide-libertarian reading is a locatable option the Map adopts on independent grounds, not a conclusion the source/leeway distinction forces.

The honest summary is that the taxonomy tells the Map *what kind* of freedom condition it is defending and who its neighbours and opponents are. It does not tell the Map that the condition is met. That further question is settled, if at all, by the [luck and manipulation debates](/topics/the-manipulation-argument-and-hard-incompatibilism/) and the [quantum interface](/concepts/quantum-indeterminacy-free-will/)—not by the distinction drawn here.
