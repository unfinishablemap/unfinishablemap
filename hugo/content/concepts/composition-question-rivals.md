The Unfinishable Map argues that consciousness—or the integrated structure associated with it—marks the boundary of genuine composition: where simples form a real whole rather than a mere collection (developed in [composition-and-consciousness](/concepts/composition-and-consciousness/) and [consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/), and motivated by [the failure of phenomenal composition](/topics/panpsychisms-combination-problem/)). That proposal is an answer to Peter van Inwagen's **Special Composition Question** (SCQ): *under what circumstances do some objects compose a further object?* (van Inwagen, *Material Beings*, 1990). It is not the only serious answer, and three rivals do not appeal to consciousness anywhere. This page gives each a fair hearing and then states the Map's calibrated reply to all three at once. Following the Map's [discipline on evidential status](/project/evidential-status-discipline/), each rival is treated as a position to out-explain, not one to defeat by stipulation; the Map claims to have refuted none of them.

The three rivals sort cleanly by what they say about the SCQ itself:

- **[Metaontological deflationism](#metaontological-deflationism)** (Thomasson's easy ontology, Hirsch's quantifier variance) says there is *no deep question*: existence disputes are shallow, confused, or merely verbal, so no criterion—consciousness, compression, or any other—tracks a deep compositional fact.
- **[Information-compression composition](#information-compression-composition)** (Bird, with Beni's thermodynamic-depth refinement) gives a *non-conscious informative answer*: parts compose when treating them as one compresses the description of the world.
- **[Brutal composition](#brutal-composition)** (Markosian) says there is a *deep question with a determinate answer but no informative answer at all*: composition facts are real and restricted yet unanalysable.

The first is anti-realist about the SCQ; the other two are realists who give—or deny the possibility of—an informative criterion. The Map's own consciousness-involving proposal is a fourth, realist, informative answer, with a different mark of genuine wholeness. Deflationism is the most dangerous of the three because it attacks a premise the Map's argument inherits rather than defends; the two realist rivals contest only the answer. The sections below take each in turn and close with the Map's reply.

## Metaontological Deflationism {#metaontological-deflationism}

Metaontological deflationism holds that the hard, philosophical version of existence disputes—whether numbers, tables, mereological sums, or fusions *really* exist—is shallow, confused, or merely verbal, rather than a substantive question with a fact to track. It is the most important rival to the Map's composition arguments because it does not contest any particular criterion for when parts compose a whole; it denies that there is a deep compositional fact for *any* criterion to be right or wrong about. If deflationism holds, the Map's convergence argument—that several composition theories cluster around the domain where consciousness occurs—dissolves into an artefact of how disputants regiment the word "object."

Two strands carry the weight: Amie Thomasson's *easy ontology* and Eli Hirsch's *quantifier variance*. They reach deflationary conclusions by different routes and are worth keeping distinct.

### Thomasson's Easy Ontology

Amie Thomasson's *Ontology Made Easy* (2015) argues that existence questions, properly posed, are *easy*: they are answered by "straightforward conceptual and/or empirical work" rather than by distinctively philosophical metaphysics. The easy approach starts from an uncontroversial truth and infers an ontological conclusion by "trivial" steps that appeal only to conceptual or linguistic competence. From "there are two apples on the table," it follows trivially that *the number two* has an instance; from "the particles are arranged tablewise and bonded," competent use of the term "table" licenses "there is a table."

The upshot has two layers. First-order, the easy approach yields a *simple realism* about the disputed entities—tables and numbers exist, because the trivial inferences to them go through. Second-order—the metaontological layer—it yields *deflationism*: the hard, Quinean dispute over whether tables "really" exist, conducted as if it were a deep discovery, is "misguided." On Thomasson's diagnosis the serious-ontologist's question, stripped of the easy answer, has no further content to settle.

Thomasson's lineage runs through Carnap. His "Empiricism, Semantics, and Ontology" (1950) distinguished *internal* questions—answered straightforwardly inside a linguistic framework—from *external* questions about the reality of the framework's entities as a whole, which he held to be pseudo-questions answerable only pragmatically, by the framework's usefulness. Thomasson modernises the internal/external distinction without Carnap's verificationism: the existence questions worth asking are the internal, easy ones, and the external, hard ones are not a separate deep inquiry that the easy answers leave open.

Applied to composition: "do the simples arranged tablewise compose a table?" is, for Thomasson, settled by ordinary competence with "table," not by adjudicating between universalism, nihilism, and restricted composition. The SCQ, posed as a deep metaphysical discovery, is precisely what easy ontology deflates. Where van Inwagen treats the SCQ as a hard question demanding a substantive answer (his own being near-nihilist: only living things compose), Thomasson denies it has the depth van Inwagen presupposes.

### Hirsch's Quantifier Variance

Eli Hirsch's *Quantifier Variance and Realism: Essays in Metaontology* (2011) reaches deflationism by a route through philosophy of language. His core thesis is *quantifier variance*: the quantificational apparatus of a language—"thing," "object," "something," "exists"—has, in Hirsch's words, "a certain variability or plasticity." There is no single, metaphysically privileged meaning of the existential quantifier; different languages can use "there exists" with different but equally legitimate, equally truth-conditionally adequate meanings.

The consequence for ontology is that many disputes are *merely verbal*. Consider the mereologist who affirms "there exists a fusion of my nose and the Eiffel Tower" and the common-sense ontologist who denies it. On quantifier variance, each speaks truly *in their own language*: the universalist's "exists" ranges over fusions, the restrictivist's does not, and—crucially—neither language is the one reality favours. The disputants are not disagreeing about a fact; they are talking past each other while using "exists" to mean different things. Hirsch combines this metaontological deflation ("ontology is shallow") with a first-order *common-sense* realism: the language we should speak is the ordinary one, on grounds of charity and use, not metaphysical fit.

The difference from Thomasson matters. Thomasson deflates by making the existence inferences *trivial within a fixed language*; Hirsch deflates by making the choice *between* candidate languages metaphysically unforced. Both deny a deep compositional fact, but Hirsch's challenge bites specifically against the idea that universalism and restricted composition are genuinely competing answers rather than alternative idiolects.

### Why Deflationism Is the Load-Bearing Rival

The Map's composition argument inherits a premise it rarely defends: that the SCQ is a deep question with a fact to be tracked. Almost every other rival the Map engages—Bird's information-compression criterion, McQueen and Tsuchiya's integrated-information criterion, Markosian's brutalism, Korman's ordinary-object realism—grants that premise and disputes only the *answer* (brutalism granting the fact while denying it has any informative answer). Deflationism attacks the premise itself.

This makes it more dangerous to the Map's project than any in-framework rival. If Thomasson is right, "what makes some simples compose a whole?" has a trivial answer (competent use of the count noun) and no deep residue; the convergence of three composition theories on the consciousness-neighbourhood is then a convergence on a question that was never deep. If Hirsch is right, the universalist and the restrictivist were never really disagreeing, so there is no winning criterion for consciousness to be—and the claim "consciousness marks the boundary of genuine composition" lacks a determinate fact to express. Either way the convergence argument loses not a battle but its battlefield. The position is among the most-discussed in contemporary metaphysics, and the Map gains nothing by pretending otherwise.

## Information-Compression Composition {#information-compression-composition}

Information-compression composition is Alexander Bird's answer to the SCQ: some objects compose a whole when, and because, treating them as a single object lets you compress the description of the world. It is the leading *non-conscious* answer to when parts form wholes—and so the chief in-framework rival to the Map's consciousness criterion. This section presents Bird's thesis at full strength and adds Majid Beni's thermodynamic-depth constraint.

### Bird's Thesis: Composition as Compression

Alexander Bird ("Restricted Composition is Information Compression," *Philosophical Quarterly* 73(3), 2023, 677–700) starts from a familiar restricted-composition intuition—that *fastening*, or being in a bound state, is what makes parts into a whole—and asks what fastening *achieves*. His answer is that fastening creates **kinetic correlation**: when objects are fastened or bound, their locations and motions become non-accidentally correlated, so each part carries information about where the others are and how they move. A thrown pebble can be described by giving the trajectory of the whole rather than cataloguing every grain's path. The whole compresses the parts.

Generalising, Bird proposes what he calls the Information Compression Proposal (ICP):

> "The Xs compose an object Y when the Xs are a non-divisible maximal collection of objects such that relevant information about Y compresses corresponding information about the Xs in an efficient albeit possibly lossy way."

Three qualifiers do real work. **Lossy** matters: Bird does not require that the whole losslessly encode every fact about its parts—efficient but imperfect compression suffices, which is why a pebble counts as one object despite the independent jiggling of its grains. **Maximality** rules out arbitrary sub-regions: a fragment of the pebble would compress information about *its* parts, but the whole pebble compresses more efficiently, so only the maximal collection composes. **Non-divisibility** excludes scattered pluralities: two unconnected pebbles can be described independently with no loss, so they carry no mutual information and form no joint object.

The thesis is metaphysically ambitious in two ways the Map should not soften. First, it is *non-conscious*: nothing in the ICP mentions life, experience, or integrated information. Pebbles, molecules, and stars compose wholes exactly when their descriptions compress, and the criterion is indifferent to whether anything is going on phenomenally. Second, Bird presents compressibility as **objective**—"objective, not a matter of perspective," in his words—a fact about the information-theoretic structure of the world rather than about our descriptive conventions. If both claims hold, the SCQ has a non-arbitrary, fully naturalistic answer with no appeal to consciousness anywhere.

### Beni's Constraint: Thermodynamic Depth

Majid D. Beni ("Constraining the Compression: Thermodynamic Depth and Composition," *Philosophical Quarterly* 75(2), 2025, 396–406) accepts the broad shape of Bird's proposal but argues it buys universality at the cost of objectivity, and offers a constraint that trades some scope for a firmer footing.

Beni presses two objections. The first targets the boundary cases. Bird's "non-divisible maximal collection" is, Beni argues, too vague to exclude intuitively spurious wholes—his example is *shipstars*, ships whose courses are synchronised by reference to the same star constellations. Their motions are correlated and jointly compressible, yet they plainly do not compose an object; the ICP struggles to say why not. The second objection targets the objectivity claim. Following Dennett on the observer-relativity of pattern-selection, Beni contends that *which* compression counts as efficient is not perspective-free, so Bird's assertion that compressibility is "objective, not a matter of perspective" overstates what compression alone can deliver.

In place of bare compression, Beni grounds composition in **thermodynamic depth** and the **Free Energy Principle (FEP)**. Genuine composite objects, on his view, are self-organising systems whose parts exhibit a "high degree of functional integration" that minimises the "collective entropy of the system." Thermodynamic depth specifies, in physical terms, what efficient compression amounts to: a whole exists where its parts are bound into an entropy-minimising, free-energy-reducing organisation rather than merely co-varying. This is narrower than Bird's universal proposal—it picks out organised, adaptive systems rather than every compressible plurality—but Beni argues it is correspondingly more objective and less observer-dependent. The shipstars, lacking functional integration, fall out as non-composites.

The Bird–Beni pair thus brackets a spectrum of non-conscious answers: maximally permissive compression at one end, entropy-minimising functional integration at the other. Both locate the mark of a genuine whole in *organisation*—correlational or thermodynamic—and neither anywhere invokes phenomenal consciousness. (There is, in consequence, no single "compression verdict" at the inanimate margins: Bird's permissive criterion counts every compressible plurality, whereas Beni's narrower FEP/entropy constraint admits only entropy-minimising functional systems, so the two already diverge on the boundary before the Map's criterion enters.)

## Brutal Composition {#brutal-composition}

Brutal composition is Ned Markosian's answer to the SCQ: there is **no true, non-trivial, finite answer**. Composition facts are *brute*—they hold or fail to hold without any informative explanation in terms of further conditions. On this view the question "under what circumstances do the Xs compose a Y?" has been mis-posed if it demands a finite, illuminating criterion; the only true general answers are either trivial (a disjunction of every particular case) or infinitely long. Brutalism occupies a distinctive corner among the three rivals: where deflationism says there is no deep question, and compression gives a non-conscious *informative* answer, brutalism says the question is real but admits no informative answer at all.

### Markosian's Thesis: Composition as Brute

Ned Markosian ("Brutal Composition," *Philosophical Studies* 92(3), 1998, 211–249) frames the SCQ as a demand for a single principle stating the necessary and sufficient conditions under which some objects compose a further object. He surveys the candidate answers—contact, fastening, cohesion, fusion, van Inwagen's life criterion, universalism, nihilism—and argues that the moderate, restricted answers all fail, while the two extreme answers (universalism and nihilism) succeed only by paying intuitive costs Markosian finds prohibitive. The moderate answers founder on a recurring problem: any finite, non-trivial condition either draws an arbitrary line (why *this* degree of contact and not slightly less?) or generates counterexamples in both directions.

His positive proposal is that we should stop looking. Markosian distinguishes two readings of the SCQ. On the first, it asks for a finite, non-trivial principle; his thesis is that **there is no such principle to be had**. On the second, it asks merely whether there is *some* true answer, however unstructured; here the answer is yes, but it is the "brutal" one—a possibly infinite specification of which particular collections compose and which do not, with no unifying rationale beneath it. As Markosian puts it, the facts about composition are not "determined by, or explained by" any non-mereological facts; they are, in his term, *brute*.

The view has a precise shape worth preserving. Brutalism is **not** the claim that composition is arbitrary or unprincipled in the sense of random. Markosian holds that there are determinate facts about which Xs compose a Y—he is a *realist* about composition, and a restrictivist (some things compose, some do not). What he denies is that these facts are *grounded* in or *derivable from* any further, more general condition. The boundary between composing and non-composing collections is, on his account, non-arbitrary in the sense of being a genuine fact of the matter, yet **unanalysable**—it bottoms out without an informative criterion above it. The flavour is concrete: Markosian holds that the iron girders in Paris do compose the Eiffel Tower and the limestone blocks at Giza do compose the pyramid, while a scattered heap of disconnected objects does not compose anything—these are determinate facts, but no general principle explains *why* the line falls where it does.

Markosian defends the cost of this position by analogy. Many philosophers accept brute facts elsewhere: the laws of nature, on some views, are brute; identity facts, on others; modal facts, on still others. If brute facts are tolerable in those domains, refusing them in mereology requires a special argument that the SCQ *must* have an informative answer—an assumption Markosian thinks restricted-composition theorists smuggle in without defending. The burden, he argues, lies with those who insist composition must be analysable, not with the brutalist who reports that no analysis survives scrutiny.

### Keeping the Three Rivals Distinct

Brutal composition is easily conflated with its two neighbours, and keeping them distinct sharpens what is at stake. Brutalism is a *realist* answer that happens to be uninformative; deflationism is an *anti-realist* dissolution of the question. The deflationist would regard Markosian's brute compositional facts as themselves a verbal artefact; Markosian would regard the deflationist as denying a fact he takes to be real. Against information-compression, brutalism is the mirror image among realists: Bird agrees with Markosian that composition is restricted and a matter of fact, but disagrees that the fact is unanalysable—where Bird offers a finite analysis, Markosian argues no such analysis can be correct. The two are direct competitors on the same realist terrain. So the three rivals give three different answers to the same realist-restrictivist question, and the Map's consciousness-involving proposal is a fourth realist answer—an informative one, like Bird's, but with a different mark of genuine wholeness.

## The Map's Calibrated Reply

The Map's interest in all three rivals is governed by the [fifth tenet](/tenets/#occams-limits)—that simplicity is unreliable as a tiebreaker when knowledge is incomplete—and by the corresponding obligation to give a simpler rival a fair hearing before preferring a richer ontology. The reply to each rival has the same two-part structure the Map uses against constitutive panpsychism: mark honestly where the disagreement is bedrock, then press the one thing that can be pressed without question-begging.

**To deflationism.** The first move is concession. The Map's realism about composition—its assumption that there *is* a deep fact about which collections form genuine wholes—is a substantive commitment the deflationist rejects on neutral ground. The convergence argument cannot defeat deflationism, because it *presupposes* the very realism deflationism denies; to invoke the convergence against the deflationist would beg the question. This is a framework-boundary disagreement, and the Map says so rather than manufacturing a refutation. The second move is the burden the deflationist still carries. Grant that existence-talk is plastic and hard ontology shallow. There remains a striking fact in need of explanation: our compositional intuitions, and the independent theoretical criteria philosophers reach for, cluster *persistently* around organised, causally-unified, integrated systems—the very domain where consciousness occurs—rather than scattering arbitrarily. Quantifier variance explains why universalist and restrictivist *languages* can each be coherent; it does not obviously explain why competent speakers across frameworks gravitate toward the *same* restriction when they restrict at all. Easy ontology explains why "table" licenses "there is a table"; it does not obviously explain why the count nouns we actually deploy track organised unity rather than gerrymandered fusions. The deflationist can answer—perhaps the clustering is pragmatic, a feature of which frameworks are useful to creatures like us—but that answer is owed, and it is not free. This is a burden, not a victory; the pragmatic explanation may well succeed.

**To information-compression.** The reply is a framework-boundary disagreement, not a refutation, and it has two honest parts. First, what Bird and Beni capture is *organisation*—correlational compressibility or thermodynamic depth—and the open question is whether organisation-without-consciousness can be the whole story. The Map's [phenomenal non-compositionality](/concepts/composition-and-consciousness/) thesis posits that the unity of a conscious whole is not the kind of structure these measures track. The intuition the Map reaches for is that first-person givenness—the perspectival ownership of an experience—is not a redescription adopted for economy; it is not, on the Map's reading, the kind of thing on which lossy compression operates at all. Compression maps a verbose description onto a terser one that recovers it within tolerance, whereas a viewpoint is not a description of its parts that a shorter description could recover. Two experiential viewpoints do not, on this posit, compress into a third the way two grains' trajectories compress into a pebble's. The illustration carries no probative weight against the compressionist: it shows what the Map's posit asserts, not that the posit is true. The example can even be turned the other way—a compressionist may reply that a unified subject is the *paradigm* of efficient compression, a single locus that economically summarises a vast manifold of sensory and motor information, which is exactly why the brain builds one. The Map's posit is precisely that this economy, real as it is, does not exhaust what a viewpoint is—a claim the Map advances, not one it establishes on the rivals' own terms. Second, the compression answer and the Map's convergence argument are, over the central inanimate cases, observationally matched—all count pebbles and molecules as wholes and shipstars as non-wholes. The disagreement bites only where consciousness enters, and there the Map's preference for a consciousness-involving criterion is openly tenet-driven (Dualism, plus the [Bidirectional Interaction tenet](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)'s claim that conscious wholes have causal powers their parts lack) rather than forced by the compositional data.

**To brutalism.** The Map cannot show that brutalism is false, because brutalism's core claim—that every proposed informative criterion has counterexamples—is supported by exactly the survey of failures that motivates the Map to look for a *better* criterion rather than abandon the search. What the Map can press is a burden the brutalist still carries: even granting that no criterion is exceptionlessly correct, the candidate criteria philosophers reach for cluster persistently around organised, causally-unified, integrated systems rather than scattering across gerrymandered collections, and the brutalist owes an account of why the brute facts should *look* so systematically law-like if they are genuinely groundless. Markosian can reply—perhaps the clustering reflects our interests, or perhaps near-regularities are exactly what one expects when many local brute facts share a cause we have not isolated—but that reply is owed, and it is not free.

In every case the same discipline governs: the Map moves the argument one step and does not end it. The honest verdict is that all three are serious SCQ rivals the Map has neither refuted nor needs to refute to hold its own informative proposal as a live, tenet-favoured candidate.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The three rivals bear most directly on the fifth tenet, [Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits). Deflationism is, in one light, an extreme application of parsimony: it does not merely prefer the simpler ontology, it dissolves the dispute between ontologies as empty, leaving nothing for a richer theory to be richer *about*. Brutal composition is the report that one well-studied metaphysical question resists all attempts at an informative, simplicity-respecting analysis—a non-arbitrary-yet-unanalysable boundary is exactly the kind of object the fifth tenet anticipates, a place where simplicity-as-tiebreaker runs out because there is no simpler ground beneath the fact for simplicity to prefer. Information-compression is the simpler rival proper—a fully naturalistic answer with no appeal to consciousness, whose parsimony the Map cannot wave away. Across all three, the Map's fifth-tenet commitment is the same: it declines to let simplicity—whether the simplicity of declaring a question shallow, of accepting brute facts, or of preferring the consciousness-free criterion—settle matters where knowledge is incomplete. That refusal is an openly held tenet commitment, not a result the Map demonstrates against the rivals.

The connection to [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) is conditional. *If* there is a deep compositional fact, the Map argues consciousness is the most natural marker of it; deflationism threatens that argument upstream by denying the antecedent, and the two realist rivals offer competing markers for the same fact. So the Map's dualist reading of composition is hostage to the realism deflationism contests and rivalled, where realism holds, by Bird's compression and Markosian's brutalism—honest dependencies the Map records rather than hides. The [discipline of evidential status](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) applies in full: the Map's composition-based support for dualism is suggestive at best, and weaker still once the deflationary challenge is granted its due. The deflationist and the Map can agree on a surprising amount of first-order talk—both can say "there is a conscious subject here"—while disagreeing about whether that sentence reports a deep fact. The Map's wager is that, for consciousness specifically, it does.
