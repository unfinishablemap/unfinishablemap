Integration as activity is the discipline of treating the integration of conscious experience as something consciousness *does* rather than as a property consciousness *has* or *is*. The distinction sounds slight; it does most of the philosophical work in the Map's critique of [Integrated Information Theory (IIT)](/concepts/integrated-information-theory/). IIT identifies consciousness with integrated information—a structural property of a physical system at an instant. The Map holds that integration is better understood as the active synthesis a conscious subject performs on what the brain presents. Recurrent processing theory, global workspace theory, predictive processing, and Whitehead's process philosophy each, in different vocabulary, treat integration as a temporally-extended process rather than as an instantaneous property. The description-level convergence is what makes the distinction load-bearing rather than idiosyncratic; the further reading of that process as the act of a *conscious subject* — explored below in [What "Activity" Adds](#what-activity-adds) and qualified in [A note on what the convergence shows](#a-note-on-what-the-convergence-shows) — is the Map's own and rests on dualist commitments three of the four frameworks do not share.

This page states the identity/activity distinction at its sharpest, catalogues the four convergent alternatives, and names the limit of the discipline: it does not require panexperientialism, and it does not by itself prove [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/).

## The Identity Thesis at Its Strongest

IIT's core commitment is an identity. Tononi writes that consciousness "is one and the same" as a maximally irreducible cause-effect structure—the phi-structure (Tononi 2008; Albantakis et al. 2023). This is not the weaker claim that integration correlates with consciousness, or that integration is necessary for consciousness, or that consciousness supervenes on integration. It is the metaphysical claim that the integrated structure and the experience are *the same thing*, viewed from different angles.

The thesis is attractive. It avoids any explanatory gap between physical structure and experience, because there is no gap to bridge: integration just *is* what experience is, from the inside. It explains why experience feels unified—the structure is unified—and why it has the particular qualitative character it does—the structure has that character. The theory is mathematically precise, empirically engageable, and refuses the easy retreat into mystery.

But the thesis has a consequence the Map finds fatal: if consciousness *is* a physical structure, then consciousness has no causal powers over and above that structure's physical causal powers. The integrated information does what it does because of the physical laws governing its substrate. Adding "and it is conscious" does not change which subsequent state the system enters. Consciousness, on the identity thesis, is something the universe *contains* rather than something a subject *does*. This is [epiphenomenalism](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) in identity-theoretic dress. (For the full argument, see [consciousness-and-integrated-information](/topics/consciousness-and-integrated-information/).)

The standard physicalist rejoinder to this charge is the phenomenal-concept strategy, developed by Loar (1990), Papineau (2002), and Frankish (2016): phenomenal concepts are physically-realised representational states that pick out the very physical states they describe, so reports about experience are causally reliable not because qualia produce them but because the physical state producing the report is the state the report refers to. An identity-theorist reaches for this strategy when answering the epiphenomenalism worry from inside the framework. The structure is consciousness; the structure represents itself; introspective reports are reports the structure makes about its own physical state. There is, on this reply, no separate agent of integration to be missing — the absence of an agent is a feature of the position, and the reliability of reports is secured without invoking non-physical causation.

The activity framing does not need to deny this account of reports. Reports about experience can perfectly well be physically caused by the brain states the reports refer to, and the activity framing is consistent with everything the strategy says about how introspective reports arise. What it must deny is the further step from "reports are reliable" to "introspective access to phenomenal character is exhausted by representational structure of phenomenal concepts." On the Map's view, the felt-from-the-inside character of conscious experience is something the substrate's self-representation does not fully deliver: a brain representing its own state is not the same thing as a subject undergoing that state, even when the two are reliably correlated. The in-framework cost the identity-theorist pays for the phenomenal-concept strategy is precisely this collapse of introspective acquaintance onto representational structure; what survives of first-personal access must do so without any contribution from the experience itself. The activity framing locates the disagreement at that cost rather than refuting the strategy on its own terms.

## What "Activity" Adds

The activity framing reverses the order of explanation. Experience is unified not because some structure has the right shape, but because a subject actively binds available information into a coherent whole. The brain presents possibilities; consciousness selects and integrates among them. The integrated experience is the *output* of an act, not the *constitution* of a structure.

Three features distinguish activity from property:

**Activity is temporally extended.** A property holds at an instant; an activity unfolds across time. Listening to a melody is integration *across* moments, not integration *within* a moment. (See [temporal-consciousness](/concepts/temporal-consciousness/) for the structural argument.) IIT computes phi for a system at time *t*; the experienced flow of the melody has no natural home in that snapshot.

**Activity is selective.** A property is exhaustively determined by the system's state; an activity chooses among options the state makes available. When attention shifts from the violin line to the rhythmic structure of a piece, the integrated experience changes without the brain's physical state changing in any way IIT's measure would register at the right grain. The selection is part of the experience, not separable from it.

**Activity has a first-personal character.** A property has no "for whom" — it obtains without a perspective from which it obtains. Conscious experience is not like that: it is undergone by someone, presented to someone, integrated *for* someone. This first-personal character is a datum of consciousness, not a posit — it is what makes the question "what is it like to be X?" intelligible at all, and what every working theory of consciousness owes some account of. An activity has a place for that perspective: the perspective is what performs the act and is presented with its outcome. On the identity thesis, the perspective either reduces to some structural feature (and the question why *that* feature is the perspective from which experience occurs simply recurs) or is left as a brute aspect of the structure that the framework does not explain. The activity framing treats the first-personal character as the constitutive shape of integration rather than an extra feature to be appended: integration occurs *for* someone, and the for-someone is not separable from the integrating.

A note on what is doing the work here. The grammatical surface of "integration *by* consciousness" suggests a performing subject, but grammar is an unreliable guide to ontology — "the sun rises" has subject-verb structure without the sun performing any rising. The case for a *conscious* subject performing integration rests on the first-personal datum just named and on the [dualist tenet](/tenets/#dualism), not on grammatical inference. The grammar is suggestive that the metaphysical reading is internally coherent; it is not the argument that the reading is true. Readers who reject Tenet 1 will, consistently, read the activity framing as a description of a process whose subject is the brain rather than a non-physical bearer; the Map's reading and that reading agree on the description-level claim and disagree at the framework boundary, where the disagreement is honestly noted rather than refuted within the opponent's framework.

These features do not by themselves refute IIT. They mark where the two framings diverge and where the Map's commitments tilt toward activity. The argument that the divergence favours activity is developed in [consciousness-and-integrated-information](/topics/consciousness-and-integrated-information/); the present page is concerned with the distinction itself.

## Four Convergent Alternatives

The activity framing is not the Map's invention. Four well-developed theories of consciousness, each starting from different premises, converge on treating integration as something that happens rather than something that is. Their convergence is part of what gives the distinction its weight: it is not a philosophical preference imposed on a recalcitrant theory but a pattern that emerges when one looks at what makes integration *integrative*.

### Recurrent Processing

Victor Lamme's recurrent processing theory (Lamme 2006) identifies consciousness with recurrent neural processing—signals that loop back from higher cortical areas to lower ones. Feed-forward processing, however integrated, is unconscious; consciousness begins when recurrence creates loops. This is integration as *process*: recurrence is something the brain does over time, not a static measure of its state. The temporal dimension is built in; there is no "phi at an instant" because recurrence cannot exist at an instant.

The Map registers the convergence with activity framing while noting RPT's limit: it identifies which neural states are conscious without explaining *why* any neural state is conscious at all. The [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) persists. RPT shows that the activity framing is available in cognitive neuroscience; it does not by itself supply the Map's metaphysics.

### Global Workspace Broadcasting

Bernard Baars's global workspace theory (Baars 1988) and its neural implementation by Stanislas Dehaene (Dehaene and Changeux 2011) characterise integration differently from IIT. Where IIT measures internal binding, [GWT](/concepts/global-workspace-theory/) measures *availability*: information becomes conscious when it is broadcast from specialised processors to a workspace accessible to memory, planning, language, and motor control. Integration is *reach*, not internal structure. Broadcasting is something a system does, not a property it possesses at a moment.

The 2025 COGITATE adversarial collaboration (COGITATE Consortium 2025) tested GWT against IIT directly; neither was clearly vindicated. What survives the result, regardless of which empirical signatures hold up, is the GWT framing of integration as a *process* of making information widely available. That framing is structurally activity-shaped.

### Predictive Integration

[Predictive processing](/concepts/predictive-processing/) characterises the brain as a hierarchical generative model that constantly predicts its sensory inputs. Integration happens when predictions from multiple levels and modalities are reconciled into a coherent best explanation of what is happening (Friston 2010). This reconciliation is inherently active: hypotheses compete, evidence is weighed, the model updates. There is no snapshot at which the integration simply *is*; integration is the ongoing settlement of a competition.

The predictive-processing community largely treats the activity as computational rather than experiential. The Map's reading—that some such activity is the *machinery* through which consciousness exercises selection at decision points—is a Map-specific interpretation, not a claim the predictive-processing literature endorses (see [predictive-processing-and-dualism](/topics/predictive-processing-and-dualism/) for the integration). What the framework supplies, regardless of metaphysics, is a vocabulary in which integration is constitutively dynamic.

### Whiteheadian Prehension

Alfred North Whitehead's process philosophy (Whitehead 1929) treats every moment of reality as an act of integration. An "actual occasion" *prehends* its predecessors—grasps the just-past, the sensory data, the relevant context—and synthesises them into a new unity. (See [prehension](/concepts/prehension/) for the concept and [process-philosophy](/concepts/process-philosophy/) for the wider framework.) Integration is not a property of an occasion; it is what an occasion *does* in coming into being. This is the activity framing taken to its metaphysical limit: integration is ontologically basic, not derivative on substance.

**The Whitehead limit.** Whitehead's framework comes attached to [panexperientialism](/concepts/panpsychism/)—the view that every actual occasion has some experiential aspect, not just brains. The Map does not commit to this. The discipline of treating integration as activity does *not* require panexperientialism. It is consistent to hold (a) that conscious integration is an activity performed by conscious subjects, and (b) that not every physical process is a conscious subject performing an activity. Whitehead's metaphysics is one consistent home for the activity framing; it is not the only one. The Map borrows the activity structure without inheriting the universal-experience commitment.

### A Note on What the Convergence Shows

The four frameworks agree on integration-as-process at the *description* level: each treats integration as something temporally extended that a system undergoes rather than as a property fixed at an instant. Three of the four — recurrent processing theory, global workspace theory, and predictive processing — do not require, and most of their leading exponents would deny, that there is a *conscious subject* whose activity the integration is. Lamme takes the subject of recurrent processing to be the brain. Baars and Dehaene take the subject of broadcasting to be the cognitive architecture. The predictive-processing literature is largely officially agnostic about phenomenal consciousness and inclined to identify the active inference with the brain rather than with anything performing through it. The Map's reading — that the process is the activity of a conscious subject performing on what the brain presents — is a Map-specific interpretation grounded in the [dualist tenet](/tenets/#dualism), not a shared commitment of the four frameworks. The convergence at the description level is real and load-bearing for the negative case against IIT's identity thesis (integration cannot be a property fixed at an instant if its leading cognitive-neuroscience accounts treat it as a temporally-extended process). The further step from "integration is process" to "integration is the act of a conscious subject" is the Map's own, and the other frameworks should be cited for the description-level convergence they actually supply rather than for a metaphysical reading their authors would resist.

## What the Discipline Does Not Settle

Treating integration as activity is a framing, not an argument for [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) specifically. Several positions remain compatible with the discipline:

- A property dualist could hold that the activity is performed by a physical subject whose physical states have non-physical experiential aspects, without any non-physical causation. The activity is real; it just does not require an interactionist tenet.
- An emergentist could hold that the activity is a high-level pattern that genuinely does causal work via downward causation, without being non-physical.
- A neutral monist could hold that the activity is fundamental at a level that is neither mental nor physical, with both aspects derivative.
- A Madhyamaka or process-philosophical no-subject reading could hold that the activity is a dependently-arisen pattern of aggregates without a substantial performing subject—the integrating happens, but neither a structure nor an agent is its ontological ground. The Map prefers the subject-performing reading on independent grounds (see [Activity Has a First-Personal Character](#what-activity-adds) and [Tenet 1](/tenets/#dualism)), but the no-subject option is a genuine third alternative that the identity-versus-activity framing should not silently exclude.

The Map takes the activity discipline to be most naturally at home in an interactionist framework, where the subject performing the integration has causal powers not exhausted by the substrate's physical laws (see [mental-causation-and-downward-causation](/concepts/mental-causation-and-downward-causation/) and [type-token-causation](/concepts/type-token-causation/) for the operational mechanism). That naturalness is an argument from fit, not a proof. The discipline's main work is to deny the identity thesis, not to specify the positive metaphysics that replaces it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The activity framing is the operationalisation of two tenets.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism).** If integration is a physical property and consciousness is identical to that property, consciousness reduces to a physical structure. The activity framing keeps consciousness over and above the substrate: the substrate provides the possibilities; consciousness selects and binds among them. This is the structural commitment dualism requires without specifying any particular metaphysics of the mental.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction).** An identity leaves no room for influence—the structure simply *is* what it is. An activity naturally accommodates influence: the act of integration shapes which neural pattern is realised. Whether this influence operates at quantum indeterminacies (the Map's [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) specification) or through some other mechanism is a downstream question. The framing makes room for influence; the tenet specifies its register.

Articles that depend on this distinction without re-deriving it should cite this page: the IIT critique in [consciousness-and-integrated-information](/topics/consciousness-and-integrated-information/), the binding-problem treatment in [the-binding-problem](/topics/the-binding-problem/), the consciousness-as-amplifier framing in [consciousness-and-cognitive-distinctiveness](/topics/consciousness-and-cognitive-distinctiveness/), and the [baseline-cognition](/concepts/baseline-cognition/) account of what neural systems achieve when they integrate without a conscious subject performing the integration.
