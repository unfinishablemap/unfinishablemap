The Simulation Theory of Memory (STM) holds that remembering is a species of imagining: to recall a past episode is to *construct*—to simulate—a representation of it, not to retrieve a stored record. Its most radical philosophical proponent, Kourken Michaelian, finds no intrinsic kind-level difference between remembering and imagining and concludes that to remember is "to imagine the past." On this view the causal connection to the original experience—the *memory trace* the older causal theory requires—is not what makes a present mental state a memory.

The Unfinishable Map's verdict is split, and the split is the point. STM correctly deflates the storage-and-retrieval metaphor of memory, a deflation the Map's own [account of episodic memory](/concepts/episodic-memory/) already shares. But STM's standard naturalistic form claims more: that physical reconstruction *exhausts* what remembering is. The Map denies this. From simulational resources alone, STM cannot ground the [feeling of remembering](#the-discrimination-problem) (explained below)—the [autonoetic](/concepts/anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness/) sense that *this happened to me*—nor explain how a subject tells a memory apart from a free imagining. That gap is exactly where the Map locates an irreducible conscious contribution.

## The Constructive-Simulation Lineage

STM has two intertwined lineages, one in cognitive neuroscience and one in philosophy.

### The Constructive Episodic Simulation Hypothesis

In cognitive neuroscience, Schacter and Addis's *constructive episodic simulation hypothesis* (2007) argues that remembering the past and imagining the future are products of one constructive system, recruiting a shared core network in the hippocampus, medial temporal lobe, and default mode network—within which distinct subsystems still mediate remembering and imagining (Addis et al., 2009). The constructive, error-prone character of memory exists, on this view, *because* its real adaptive function is prospective—to simulate possible futures.

The functional argument runs through how the components are stored. On the Schacter, Addis, and Buckner (2008) account, episodic memories are not laid down as integral records but as collections of constituent elements—people, objects, locations, actions—bound into a spatiotemporal context. Because the system flexibly extracts and recombines those elements (Schacter and Addis, 2007), the very machinery that reconstructs the past also assembles simulations of hypothetical future events. Memory, on this picture, is constructive and prospective rather than archival.

The supporting clinical evidence comes from scene construction. Hassabis, Kumaran, Vann, and Maguire (2007) found that patients with hippocampal amnesia, asked to imagine fictitious and future scenarios, were impaired on both: their imagined experiences were "lacking in spatial coherence." The same machinery, on this reading, underwrites memory and imagination alike.

### Post-Causal Simulationism

In philosophy, Michaelian's *Mental Time Travel* (2016) radicalises the neuroscientific picture. Memory is a form of mental time travel, continuous with future-oriented imagination; remembering *is* imagining the past. Crucially, Michaelian drops the trace requirement: a present representation can be a genuine memory without any retained causal connection to the original experience, provided it is produced by the right kind of reliable, internally generated construction. Reliability is secured not by trace-preservation but by the *adaptivity* of constructive processes plus metacognitive monitoring.

This is a deliberately deflationary, naturalistic programme. Michaelian (2022) pushes it further still—on a "radical simulationist" reading, remembering need not even target one's *personal* past, since the imaginative process is the same regardless of what it is aimed at.

## The Causal Theory and the Confabulation Objection

STM is the leading rival to the older *causal theory of memory* (Martin and Deutscher, 1966; defended today by Sven Bernecker and Sarah Robins). The causal theory holds that genuine remembering requires an appropriate causal connection—a retained memory trace—between the original experience and the present representation. That connection is what distinguishes remembering from merely imagining or confabulating, even when the imagined content happens to be accurate.

The causalist's sharpest weapon is the *confabulation objection*. Robins argues that simulationism collapses the processing distinction between memory errors and successful remembering: without a trace requirement, an accurate lucky guess about the past would count as a memory. Michaelian (2016) replies that simulationism survives the attack, engaging Bernecker's positive causalist account as a serious rival rather than dismissing it. This causalist-versus-simulationist dispute, alongside the parallel *continuism-versus-discontinuism* debate over whether memory and future imagining are the same kind of mental act, remains active and unsettled.

## The Discrimination Problem {#the-discrimination-problem}

The pressure point most relevant to the Map is phenomenological. If remembering and imagining draw on the same constructive machinery, how does the subject *tell them apart*? STM appeals to a distinctive "feeling of remembering" to do this work. Rivadulla-Duró (2024) argues that this appeal fails on STM's own terms: the theory "cannot simultaneously defend the simulational character of episodic memory and ground our ability to discriminate between memories and imaginings." The felt quality that marks an episode as *remembered* rather than *invented* is left underexplained by purely simulational resources.

This is the explanatory residue STM generates but does not absorb—and it is a live problem inside the simulationist literature, not merely an objection from outside it.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's engagement with STM is a case of adopting a mechanism while refusing its eliminative gloss. Schacter, Addis, and Michaelian build their accounts as fully physical, reconstructive, computational stories of memory; the Map cites their findings and their deflation of storage, but engages their reductive conclusions as a rival. The constructive *mechanism* is welcome; the claim that the mechanism is the *whole* story is where the disagreement lives.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: The Map's [dual-domain account](/concepts/episodic-memory/) already holds that remembering is constructive re-experiencing, not data retrieval—so it shares STM's anti-archival deflation wholesale. The divergence is over residue. STM's standard form treats remembering as reducible to physical reconstruction, with no role for an irreducible conscious contribution; the Map holds that autonoesis—the felt pastness, the double presence of self, the [*this-happened-to-me* marker](/concepts/mine-ness/)—is exactly what physical construction cannot supply. The discrimination problem the simulationist literature raises against itself is, on the Map's reading, the signature of that missing contribution.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: STM's reliability question—if remembering is constructing, why trust it?—invites the Map's interface answer. Construction is not neutral assembly; it is goal-directed selection among candidate reconstructions, and the *setting* of that goal is where consciousness contributes directional input rather than passively reading out the strongest trace. The empirical opening the Map points to is the reconsolidation window, when a reactivated trace destabilises and the physical system is briefly underdetermined; the interface reading is developed, held separate from the empirical signature, in [reconsolidation-as-selection-window](/topics/reconsolidation-as-selection-window/).

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: STM is the "simpler" theory—one constructive system, no further metaphysical machinery—and its parsimony is much of its appeal. The Map's [limits-of-parsimony](/arguments/epistemological-limits-of-occams-razor/) argument applies directly: parsimony favours the simpler theory only where the rivals have equal explanatory power, and STM's unresolved discrimination problem shows that precondition is unmet. The simulationist mechanism and the dual-domain reading do not explain the feeling of remembering equally well, so the appeal to simplicity cannot settle the case.

The honest framing: STM is right about *how* memories are assembled and right to abandon the storage metaphor. What it cannot deliver from construction alone is the autonoetic phenomenology that marks an assembly as a *memory*—and the gap it leaves open is the Map's entry point, not a defect the Map manufactures.
