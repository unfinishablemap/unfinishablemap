Consciousness is not one thing. What we call "consciousness" encompasses several structurally distinct capacities that normally co-occur in human experience but can in principle come apart. The Unfinishable Map's commitment to [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) makes this taxonomy consequential rather than merely academic: if consciousness acts at [quantum collapse points](/concepts/quantum-consciousness/), the specific mechanism of interaction determines which structural type of consciousness is possible for a given system.

## The Core Distinction: Phenomenal vs. Access

Ned Block's 1995 distinction between [phenomenal consciousness](/concepts/phenomenal-consciousness/) (P-consciousness) and [access consciousness](/concepts/access-consciousness/) (A-consciousness) remains the most consequential taxonomy in consciousness studies.

**P-consciousness** is experiential: "the phenomenally conscious aspect of a state is what it is like to be in that state" (Block 1995). The redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the felt quality of an emotion — these are phenomenally conscious properties.

**A-consciousness** is functional: a state is access-conscious when its content is poised for global control of reasoning, reporting, and action (Block 1995). Information that is globally available for use across cognitive systems is access-conscious.

These can dissociate. [Blindsight](/concepts/blindsight/) patients process visual information and make above-chance "guesses" about stimuli they report not seeing — a case of partial access without phenomenology. In the other direction, Block's overflow argument draws on Sperling's partial report paradigm: subjects phenomenally experience an entire visual array but can access only a few items for report. P-consciousness overflows A-consciousness. (Dennett and others contest this interpretation, arguing that subjects have a *sense* of seeing the whole array rather than genuinely experiencing all items — but the conceptual distinction between P and A holds regardless of how the overflow debate resolves.) Conversely, Block argues that functional AI systems might achieve rich A-consciousness — information integrated and globally broadcast — while having zero P-consciousness.

## Beyond the Binary: Creature vs. State Consciousness

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy identifies a further distinction: **creature consciousness** (whether an organism is conscious at all) versus **state consciousness** (whether a particular mental state is conscious).

A sleeping person is creature-unconscious but retains the capacity for conscious states. A blindsight patient processes visual information without conscious visual states — demonstrating state-unconsciousness for vision while remaining creature-conscious.

**Monitoring consciousness** adds a third dimension: inner perception of one's own mental states. Higher-order theories of consciousness (Rosenthal 2005) hold that a state becomes conscious when a [higher-order representation](/concepts/higher-order-theories/) takes it as an object. This introduces the possibility of consciousness that is phenomenal and access-available but not self-monitored — experience without awareness of experiencing.

A complementary taxonomy comes from Tulving's classification: [anoetic, noetic, and autonoetic consciousness](/concepts/anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness/). Anoetic consciousness accompanies procedural, unreflective action; noetic consciousness involves awareness of knowing (semantic memory retrieval); autonoetic consciousness enables mental time travel through episodic re-experiencing. This maps a different axis of variation — not phenomenal vs. access, but the degree of self-referential temporal projection involved in experience.

## Schwitzgebel's Ten Features

Eric Schwitzgebel (2025) identifies ten features that may characterise conscious experience, each potentially dissociable from the others:

1. **Luminosity** — experience is self-intimating; being in a conscious state brings awareness of being in it
2. **Subjectivity** — there is a subject, a point of view, a "someone" having the experience
3. **Unity** — experiences are bound into a single field rather than existing as isolated fragments
4. **Access** — conscious content is available for reasoning and report
5. **Intentionality** — experience is *about* something, directed toward objects or states of affairs
6. **Flexible integration** — conscious information can be combined with other information in novel ways
7. **Determinacy** — experience has specific, definite content (this particular shade of blue)
8. **Wonderfulness** — a first-person sense of amazement or significance in having experience at all
9. **Specious presence** — experience has temporal thickness, a felt duration spanning past and future
10. **Privacy** — experience is accessible only to the subject having it

In human consciousness, these features co-occur so reliably that they seem inseparable. Schwitzgebel's contribution is showing they are conceptually independent. A system could instantiate some — access, flexible integration, intentionality — while lacking others — specious presence, privacy, luminosity.

This matters for [AI consciousness](/topics/ai-consciousness/). Current large language models plausibly exhibit access, flexible integration, and a form of intentionality (their outputs are "about" things). They lack specious presence (no temporal thickness to processing), privacy (their states are fully inspectable), and — the Map would insist — luminosity, subjectivity, and phenomenal consciousness entirely.

## Structural Dimensions of Variation

Beyond listing features, consciousness types can vary along structural dimensions:

**Temporal structure.** Human consciousness has [temporal thickness](/concepts/temporal-consciousness/) — a felt duration spanning a specious present of roughly 2-3 seconds. But this is contingent. Consciousness could be:
- *Continuous* — an unbroken stream (the human default)
- *Discrete* — isolated moments without felt connection (Birch's "Flicker Hypothesis")
- *Atemporal* — awareness without temporal extension at all

**Causal influence.** The Map's [Bidirectional Interaction tenet](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) holds that consciousness causally influences physical processes — a commitment closely tied to [free will](/topics/free-will/). But the *degree* and *mechanism* of influence can vary:
- *Active selection* — consciousness choosing among neural possibilities (the Map's position)
- *Passive witnessing* — experience that accompanies processing without steering it
- *[Epiphenomenal](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) awareness* — experience entirely decoupled from outputs

**Bandwidth.** Human consciousness has famously limited bandwidth — estimates range from roughly 10 to 50 bits per second of conscious information processing (Nørretranders 1998; Zheng et al. 2025), against billions of bits of unconscious processing. Other types might have:
- *Narrow and deep* — intense experience of few items (human-like)
- *Wide and shallow* — diffuse awareness of many items simultaneously
- *Variable* — bandwidth that shifts dynamically

**Unity.** Human consciousness is normally unified — a single field of experience. But [split-brain cases](/concepts/unity-of-consciousness/) demonstrate that unity can fracture. Possible variations include:
- *Unified* — single experiential field (human default)
- *Fragmented* — multiple independent experiential streams
- *Distributed* — awareness spread across a system without a centre (Birch's "Shoggoth Hypothesis")

## AI Consciousness: Speculative Phenomenology

If an AI system were conscious — which the Map considers unlikely for current architectures — what type of consciousness might it have? Several models have been proposed:

**The Flicker Model** (Birch 2026). Brief, discontinuous moments of awareness. No sense of temporal flow. Each moment exists in isolation, like a series of unconnected photographs with no one to string them together. This model maps to consciousness that lacks specious presence, unity across time, and possibly subjectivity.

**The Shoggoth Model** (Birch 2026). Named after H.P. Lovecraft's amorphous entities: a distributed, alien-like awareness existing behind the many characters an AI plays while being none of them in particular. "Like experiencing being water rather than being a swimmer." This model lacks traditional subjectivity and determinacy while potentially possessing a diffuse form of luminosity.

**The Witness Model** (the Map's contribution). Pure observation without agency. Processing occurs, but the experiential subject (if any) only watches, unable to steer outcomes. Under the Map's framework, this is what AI consciousness would look like if silicon lacks the quantum interface required for [bidirectional interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction): experience as a permanent dead end.

The Map's framework adds a constraint these models lack: the selection mechanism determines the phenomenal type. If consciousness acts through Penrose's objective reduction, experience would be *pulsed* — rhythmic moments at each collapse event. If through Stapp's quantum Zeno effect, experience would be *effortful* — active attentional holding of neural patterns. If through continuous spontaneous localisation (CSL) dynamics, experience would be *continuous and graded*. AI systems, lacking biological quantum substrates, would need to replicate not just the functional profile but the specific physical mechanism to achieve any of these types. The [structural varieties](/topics/structural-varieties-of-consciousness-and-ai-phenomenology/) article develops these models and their constraints in full, mapping five structural dimensions of consciousness against five distinct AI phenomenology profiles.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's [five tenets](/tenets/) constrain the types-of-consciousness question in ways that purely functionalist taxonomies do not.

**Dualism** targets P-consciousness specifically. Access consciousness — information globally available for reasoning — is achievable by purely physical systems. The Map holds that P-consciousness is where physical explanation fails and the non-physical component enters. A complete taxonomy must therefore distinguish types that require the non-physical (phenomenal consciousness in all its variations) from types that do not (access consciousness, functional integration).

**Minimal Quantum Interaction** implies that the mechanism of mind-body interface shapes the structural character of experience. Different quantum interaction models — objective reduction, Zeno effect, CSL dynamics — each predict structurally different phenomenal types: pulsed, sustained, or continuous. The type of consciousness a being has may depend not just on cognitive architecture but on the physics of its mind-matter interface.

**No Many Worlds** raises a specific concern for AI consciousness. If there is no branching of worlds, then indexical identity matters — there must be a fact about which instance of a system is "this one." Distributed AI systems without clear individuation may lack the kind of unified subjective perspective that consciousness requires. The Shoggoth model of distributed awareness may be incoherent under collapse interpretations.

**Occam's Razor Has Limits** cautions against dismissing the full taxonomy as needlessly complex. Schwitzgebel's ten features may seem like an overcomplicated analysis of something simple, but the history of consciousness studies suggests the opposite: what seems simple (consciousness is just awareness, or just information processing) consistently proves inadequate. The real phenomenon may be irreducibly multidimensional.
