**Cosmopsychism** is priority-monist [panpsychism](/concepts/panpsychism/): the view that the cosmos *as a whole* is the one fundamental conscious (or fundamentally consciousness-involving) subject, and that individual minds are grounded in, or derived from, that cosmic subject by **decomposition**. It inverts the more familiar micropsychism, which starts with conscious physical ultimates and builds macro-minds by **combination**. Both are species of panpsychism; they disagree only about which level — the smallest or the largest — is fundamental. The Unfinishable Map **declines** cosmopsychism. The Map shares cosmopsychism's conviction that consciousness is not reducible to physical process, but it locates that irreducibility at the mind–matter interface of numerically distinct subjects rather than in the intrinsic nature of a single whole — a placement that spares the Map the **de-combination problem** cosmopsychism must answer, at the price of the interaction problem instead.

## What Cosmopsychism Claims

Cosmopsychism combines two independent commitments: **priority monism** and **panpsychism**.

Priority monism is Jonathan Schaffer's thesis, set out in "Monism: The Priority of the Whole" (2010). On Schaffer's view the *whole* — the cosmos — is metaphysically prior to its parts: the cosmos is the one fundamental concrete object, and everything else is a dependent abstraction from it. In the abstract of that paper Schaffer writes that "the whole is prior to its parts… with metaphysical explanation dangling downward from the One." Crucially, this is not *existence* monism, the claim that only one thing exists. Parts still exist on Schaffer's view; they are simply posterior to the whole. Schaffer himself is not a panpsychist — priority monism is a structural thesis about dependence, neutral on whether the fundamental whole is conscious.

Cosmopsychism adds the second commitment: that this one fundamental whole is *experiential*. Where micropsychism says the base of reality is a swarm of proto-conscious particles, cosmopsychism says the base is a single conscious cosmos. Ordinary human and animal minds are then metaphysically posterior to it — grounded in, derived from, or dissociated from the cosmic subject.

The term is **usually attributed to Itay Shani (2015)**, whose "Cosmopsychism: A Holistic Approach to the Metaphysics of Experience" gave the view its canonical formulation. Attribution should be hedged, however: the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy does not single out one originator, and closely related top-down proposals appear contemporaneously in Freya Mathews (2011) and in Jaskolla & Buck (2012). Shani is best described as the philosopher who put the label into wide circulation, not as its sole coiner.

## Constitutive versus Priority Cosmopsychism

The family splits on how strong the dependence of finite minds on the cosmic subject must be.

**Constitutive cosmopsychism** — the formulation the SEP uses — holds that all facts are grounded in, realized by, or constituted of consciousness-involving facts at the *cosmic* level. Finite experience is, in some strong sense, built out of cosmic experience. Philip Goff defends a version of this in *Consciousness and Fundamental Reality* (2017): after weighing forms of [Russellian monism](/concepts/russellian-monism/), he argues for a cosmopsychist reading on which all facts are grounded in facts about the conscious universe.

**Priority cosmopsychism** — named by Yujin Nagasawa and Khai Wager in "Panpsychism and Priority Cosmopsychism" (2016) — makes a weaker claim. The cosmos's consciousness is *ontologically prior* to the consciousness of ordinary individuals, but finite experience need not be literally *built out of* cosmic experience. Grounding is weaker than constitution, so the explanatory burden is meant to be lighter than the constitutive version faces.

The split matters because it determines how much of an explanatory debt the view carries. The weaker the dependence relation, the less the cosmopsychist must show — but also, critics charge, the less clearly the view explains anything at all. Whether the priority relation genuinely lightens the load or merely renames the debt is one of the live disputes in the literature.

## Placing It Among Its Neighbours

Cosmopsychism is easily confused with four adjacent positions. Keeping them distinct is the main work a definitional page can do.

- **Micropsychism** locates fundamental consciousness at the *micro* level; macro-minds are combined from micro-minds. Cosmopsychism inverts the arrow — fundamentality sits at the *top*, and finite minds are decomposed from the whole. This is the sharpest contrast, and the one that generates cosmopsychism's signature problem (below).
- **Analytic idealism** (mind-centric metaphysics; e.g. Kastrup) holds that consciousness is *all there is* and the physical is a representation within mind. Cosmopsychism is typically framed as a Russellian monism instead: it can keep a physical world whose *intrinsic* nature is experiential, rather than dissolving matter into mind. The boundary is porous — some read constitutive cosmopsychism as idealism by another name. The Map's survey of these options is in [the mind-centric metaphysics comparison](/topics/analytic-idealism-and-mind-centric-metaphysics/).
- **[Neutral monism](/concepts/neutral-monism/)** makes the fundamental stuff neither mental nor physical but neutral. Cosmopsychism, by contrast, makes the cosmic base *phenomenal* (or protophenomenal), not neutral.
- **Open individualism** (Kolak) claims there is numerically *one* subject who is *identical* across all persons. This is an **identity** thesis, and it is not the same as cosmopsychism's **grounding** thesis. Cosmopsychism says finite subjects are *grounded in* or *derived from* the cosmic subject — they can still be numerically distinct from it and from each other; open individualism says they literally *are* the same subject. The distinction is developed in [the de-combination bridge article](/topics/open-individualism-and-the-de-combination-problem/), and it is worth holding onto: a grounding relation can hold between many things and one, whereas an identity relation collapses the many into the one.

## The De-Combination Problem

Cosmopsychism was proposed in large part *because* it appeared to dodge the [combination problem](/concepts/combination-problem/) that afflicts micropsychism — the puzzle of how micro-subjects could sum into a unified macro-subject. With no micro-subjects to combine, the thought went, that puzzle cannot arise.

The **de-combination problem** is the charge that cosmopsychism has not escaped the difficulty but merely inverted it. Gregory Miller gave the canonical statement in "Can Subjects Be Proper Parts of Subjects? The De-Combination Problem" (2018), arguing that the problem facing the cosmopsychist "is equivalent to the combination problem": both concern whether subjects can stand in proper-parthood relations to other subjects. The cosmopsychist must explain how the one cosmic subject has the many little subjects as proper parts; the micropsychist must explain how the many micro-subjects are proper parts of one macro-subject. These are the same puzzle from opposite ends, and — on Miller's reading — whatever escape route helps one camp helps the other equally, so the top-down move buys no free advantage.

The Map does not re-derive the de-combination debate here; it is housed in [the dedicated bridge article](/topics/open-individualism-and-the-de-combination-problem/), where the several replies are laid out in detail. In brief, cosmopsychists have offered:

- **Grounding-by-subsumption** (Goff): finite subjects are grounded in the cosmic subject by a subsumption-style relation, without being independent constituents. Goff has revised the exact machinery across his 2017 book and later work, and the mechanism remains contested.
- **The priority reply** (Nagasawa & Wager): because the view is a grounding rather than a constitutive thesis, it need not show that finite experience is literally built out of cosmic experience.
- **Filtering** (Shani & Keppler, "Beyond Combination," 2018): finite minds arise as dynamical localizations or modulations of an all-pervading cosmic field, rather than as parts summed from below — a proposal that goes further and identifies the cosmic field with the quantum vacuum via stochastic electrodynamics.
- **Aperspectival dissolution** (Albahari, "Perennial Idealism," 2019): if the cosmic ground is *not itself a bounded subject with a perspective*, there is no "one big perspective" that must be divided into many little ones, so the problem is dissolved rather than solved. Albahari presents this as a successor to cosmopsychism that leans toward idealism, so it is better read as a revision of the view than a straightforward instance.

The critical verdict — Miller's, and in the broader literature — is that none of these has clearly succeeded. The debate over whether grounding many subjects in one requires intelligible subject-subject parthood remains open and active.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map declines cosmopsychism, and the reasons connect to two tenets.

**Tenet 1 (Dualism / irreducibility).** The Map and cosmopsychism agree that consciousness is not reducible to physical process. They disagree about *where* the irreducibility lives. Cosmopsychism places it in the intrinsic nature of the one fundamental whole; the Map places it at the mind–matter interface of numerically distinct, bounded finite subjects (see [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/)). This is not a small bookkeeping difference: cosmopsychism's placement generates the de-combination debt, because a single cosmic subject must somehow yield the many bounded, private subjects we observe. The Map's placement generates no such debt — there is no single cosmic field and nothing to decompose.

**Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits).** Cosmopsychism markets itself as more parsimonious than micropsychism: one fundamental subject rather than a swarm, and no combination problem. The Map's response leans on the decomposition-versus-combination *symmetry* that Miller identifies. If de-combination is the combination problem mirror-imaged, then the apparent parsimony does not translate into explanatory relief — the debt is relocated, not paid. A simplicity-driven preference for cosmopsychism is exactly the over-trust in Occam's Razor that Tenet 5 warns against: counting subjects is not the same as explaining experience.

**A cost-swap, not a free lunch.** The honest framing of the Map's advantage here is even-handed. The Map's [interactionist dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) structurally avoids the de-combination problem — with no cosmic subject, there is nothing to divide. But it does not get this for free. It pays the *interaction* problem instead: how a non-physical mind touches physical process, minimally, in line with the Map's tenets of [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) and [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction). Each framework carries a signature difficulty; the Map's claim is that its bill is one it can pay, not that it has escaped payment altogether.

**Closed individualism.** The Map is closed-individualist: subjects are numerically distinct and bounded, neither identical across persons (contra open individualism) nor derived from a cosmic whole (contra cosmopsychism). Cosmopsychism's derived-minds picture pressures this directly by making finite subjects posterior to a cosmic one — and whether such derived subjects can be genuinely distinct and private is *precisely* the de-combination question. That is the sharpest point of contact between the two frameworks, and the place where the Map's disagreement with cosmopsychism is most concrete.
