Compatibilism is the position that free will and moral responsibility are compatible with the truth of causal determinism. Its defining move is to reinterpret "could have done otherwise" so that it no longer demands the libertarian's categorical alternative — the same agent, with the same past and the same laws, genuinely able to do either of two things. Instead, the compatibilist reads that phrase as a conditional ("would have, had one chosen otherwise"), a dispositional capacity, or an actual-sequence property of the will, and locates freedom there. In the 2020 PhilPapers survey it is the majority view among professional philosophers by a wide margin.

The Map is a libertarian, interactionist project, so compatibilism is its most sophisticated rival — and a rival the Map takes seriously precisely because it shares most of the Map's *moral* conclusions (responsibility is real, blame and gratitude are warranted) while denying the *metaphysics* the Map builds on. Under Tenet 3 (bidirectional interaction), consciousness genuinely selects among quantum-open alternatives, which requires the categorical ability-to-do-otherwise that compatibilism reinterprets away. This page defines the position and its variants and situates them against the Map's existing free-will lattice; it does not re-derive the incompatibilist arguments those pages already cover.

## Locating the Position

Three broad answers dominate the question of whether we have free will:

- **Hard determinism / "no free will":** determinism is true and it *rules out* free will and responsibility.
- **Libertarianism:** we have genuine free will, and this *requires* the falsity of determinism — real, categorical alternatives must be open. (The Map is broadly in this camp; see [event-causal-libertarianism](/topics/event-causal-libertarianism/) for the taxonomy of libertarian views.)
- **Compatibilism:** we have free will and responsibility *whether or not* determinism is true, because the freedom worth having was never the kind determinism would threaten.

Compatibilism thus occupies the reconciling middle. It is not a claim that determinism is true — many compatibilists are agnostic about physics — but a claim about what free will *is*: whatever it is, it is not the categorical exemption from causation that libertarians and hard determinists both assume it must be. The disagreement between libertarian and hard determinist is, from the compatibilist's chair, a shared mistake about the concept.

## The Organizing Axis: Reinterpreting "Could Have Done Otherwise"

Compatibilism is a family, not a single doctrine, and its variants are most cleanly distinguished by *how each one handles the phrase "could have done otherwise."* This is the axis on which the Map's own distinguishing work is done, because Tenet 3 makes that ability real and physical rather than conditional or emergent. Three strategies recur:

1. **Reinterpret it** as a conditional or dispositional ability (classical analysis; new dispositionalism).
2. **Bypass it** — argue that freedom and responsibility never required alternatives at all (hierarchical/mesh theories; semicompatibilism).
3. **Relocate it** — grant that real alternatives matter, but site them at a higher level of description that is autonomous from deterministic physics (emergentist compatibilism).

The variants below are grouped by which strategy they take.

## Variants

### Classical / conditional-analysis compatibilism (reinterpret)

The oldest strand, running from Hobbes through Hume to A. J. Ayer, opposes freedom to *constraint* rather than to causation. An act is free if it is not compelled — not the product of external force, coercion, or inner compulsion such as the kleptomaniac's — even if it is fully caused. Ayer gives the crisp twentieth-century statement of the *conditional analysis*: to say one could have done otherwise is to say one *would* have done otherwise had one chosen to. Hume had earlier framed liberty as the power to act according to the determinations of the will, absent external impediment (a paraphrase of the *Enquiry*, Section VIII, not a verbatim quotation).

The conditional analysis is widely held to fail, because the antecedent choice is itself determined: if I could not have chosen otherwise, the conditional secures no genuine openness. This is the pressure point of the [Consequence Argument](/topics/the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism/), which the Map's lattice already treats in detail. The classical view survives today mainly as refined into dispositionalism.

### New dispositionalism (reinterpret)

Kadri Vihvelin's *Causes, Laws, and Free Will* (2013), whose subtitle stakes the claim outright — "Why Determinism Doesn't Matter" — revives the conditional analysis by grounding the ability to do otherwise in *dispositions*. Free will, on this view, is a bundle of dispositions (abilities) to respond to reasons, together with an environment containing no obstacle to their manifestation. Crucially, Vihvelin engineers the dispositions analysis to dodge the "mask" and "fink" counterexamples that sank the old conditional analysis — cases where a disposition is present but blocked from manifesting, or removed at the moment of testing. Michael Fara's "Masked Abilities and Compatibilism" (*Mind*, 2008) is the co-canonical source for this fink/mask-proofing move.

The dispositionalist's payoff is direct: the relevant ability *survives* determinism, because a fragile glass remains disposed to break in a deterministic world even on an occasion when it does not break. If ability-to-do-otherwise is a disposition, no indeterministic gap — and no quantum interface — is needed to secure it. This is among the strongest live challenges to the Map's premise that leeway must be metaphysically categorical, and it has no dedicated page elsewhere in the lattice.

### Hierarchical / mesh theories (bypass)

Harry Frankfurt's "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person" (*The Journal of Philosophy* 68, no. 1, 1971, pp. 5–20) reorients the debate away from alternatives entirely. Freedom, for Frankfurt, is a matter of the *structure* of the will: a person has first-order desires (to do this or that) and *second-order volitions* (to want a particular first-order desire to be one's effective will). One is free when there is a mesh — a harmony — between the will that moves one and the will one wants to have; one *identifies* with the desire that is effective. A creature with first-order desires but no second-order volitions is, in Frankfurt's terminology, a "wanton" rather than a person.

The mesh theory deliberately sidesteps the ability to do otherwise: it analyses the structure of the *actual* will, not access to alternatives. The Map's [dedicated page](/concepts/frankfurt-hierarchical-mesh-theory-of-the-will/) develops the identification machinery, and [Frankfurt cases](/topics/frankfurt-cases-and-the-principle-of-alternate-possibilities/) handle the companion argument that responsibility does not require alternate possibilities. Both are cross-references here, not re-derivations.

### Reasons-responsiveness / semicompatibilism (bypass)

John Martin Fischer and Mark Ravizza's *Responsibility and Control* (Cambridge University Press, 1998) grounds moral responsibility in *guidance control* rather than *regulative control* — the latter of which would require access to alternatives. An agent has guidance control when the action issues from the agent's *own* mechanism (secured by a history of "taking responsibility" for it) and that mechanism is *moderately reasons-responsive*. Their position is *semicompatibilism*: moral responsibility is compatible with determinism *even if* the freedom-to-do-otherwise is not. Responsibility is thereby decoupled from leeway altogether — see the Map's [reasons-responsiveness](/concepts/reasons-responsiveness/) page for the mechanism-ownership details.

This is arguably the sharpest rival framing the Map must answer. Semicompatibilism concedes, for the sake of argument, that the Map may be right that determinism removes categorical leeway — and then denies that this touches responsibility at all. The Map cannot rest with showing that leeway is real; it must show that leeway is *what responsibility needs*, against a theory built to sever exactly that link.

### Neo-compatibilist emergentism (relocate)

Daniel Dennett and Christian List represent a naturalist strand that treats free will as a real, higher-level capacity rather than a fundamental-physics property. In *Elbow Room: The Varieties of Free Will Worth Wanting* (MIT Press / Bradford Books, 1984) and *Freedom Evolves* (Viking, 2003), Dennett argues that the free will "worth wanting" is self-control and meaningful deliberation — an evolved, gradually acquired capacity — and that determinism does not entail inevitability for agents who are good anticipatory systems: avoiders can still avoid in a deterministic world.

Christian List's *Why Free Will Is Real* (Harvard University Press, 2019) is the most interesting near-neighbour to the Map. List treats free will as a genuine higher-level psychological phenomenon, emergent from but autonomous relative to physics, in the way an ecosystem or an economy is. Distinctively, he *retains* alternative possibilities and intentional agency as requirements, arguing they hold at the agential level even if the underlying physics is deterministic — agential-level indeterminism coexisting with physical-level determinism. List therefore agrees with the Map that leeway must be *real*, not merely conditional; the two part company only on where leeway lives. Neither Dennett nor List has a dedicated page in the lattice, so this variant carries new material.

### Strawsonian (reactive-attitudes) compatibilism

P. F. Strawson's "Freedom and Resentment" (*Proceedings of the British Academy*, vol. 48, 1962; the essay is paginated pp. 1–25 in the widely reproduced offprint and pp. 187–211 within the bound volume) shifts the ground from metaphysics to the *reactive attitudes* — resentment, gratitude, indignation, guilt — that are constitutive of interpersonal life. We excuse or exempt in particular cases, Strawson argues, but a *global* suspension of the reactive attitudes on the abstract ground that determinism is true is not something we could do or have reason to do. Responsibility is grounded in the practice, not read off a prior metaphysics. The Map's [reactive-attitudes-and-strawsonian-responsibility](/topics/reactive-attitudes-and-strawsonian-responsibility/) page develops this; it is cross-referenced, not duplicated.

## The Majority Position

Compatibilism is not a fringe reconciliation but the mainstream view of the discipline. In the 2020 PhilPapers survey (Bourget & Chalmers), roughly 59% of respondents accept or lean toward compatibilism on the free-will question, with libertarian and hard-determinist ("no free will") views each drawing considerably smaller minority support. (Some secondary summaries report a higher figure by conflating respondent subsets; the main-results figure is about 59%.) Any project that rejects compatibilism, as the Map does, is therefore committed to explaining why the discipline's plurality is mistaken — a burden the Map accepts rather than dodges.

## Situating the Objections

The Map's lattice already hosts the principal arguments compatibilism must answer, and the point here is to show which strategy each argument targets, not to restate the arguments:

- The [Consequence Argument](/topics/the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism/) (van Inwagen) presses the *reinterpret* strategies hardest: if our acts are consequences of the laws plus the remote past, over neither of which we have control, then the conditional and dispositional readings of "could have done otherwise" seem not to deliver control at all.
- The [Manipulation Argument](/topics/the-manipulation-argument-and-hard-incompatibilism/) (Pereboom's four-case argument) presses the *bypass* strategies: a covertly manipulated but reasons-responsive agent looks structurally like an ordinary determined one, threatening the claim that guidance control or mesh suffices for responsibility.
- The Map's in-house [compatibilist-symmetry-challenge](/concepts/compatibilist-symmetry-challenge/) asks each variant to explain why the compatibilist treats determined-and-manipulated and determined-and-normal cases asymmetrically. A mature compatibilism page situates itself against this challenge rather than repeating it.

The deeper structural fault line is captured by [the source/leeway distinction](/concepts/source-versus-leeway-incompatibilism/): leeway-incompatibilists demand open alternatives, while source-incompatibilists demand that the agent be the ultimate origin of the action. Compatibilist variants can be sorted by which of these demands they reject — the bypass theories reject the leeway requirement, while all of them reject the source requirement's ultimacy condition.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's disagreement with compatibilism is narrow but fundamental, and it is worth stating precisely because the two positions agree on so much. Compatibilism and the Map converge on the *moral* conclusions: moral responsibility is real, the reactive attitudes are warranted, and the fatalist's counsel of despair is a mistake. The Map does not treat compatibilists as covert deniers of responsibility; on the contrary, it regards the compatibilist account of *why* responsibility survives — reasons-responsiveness, identification, the embeddedness of the reactive attitudes — as largely correct description of the phenomena.

The divergence is metaphysical. Under **Tenet 3 (bidirectional interaction)**, consciousness exerts a genuine causal influence on the physical world by selecting among quantum-open alternatives — the [quantum interface](/concepts/quantum-indeterminacy-free-will/) is what makes the ability-to-do-otherwise *categorical* rather than conditional. Where the compatibilist reinterprets "could have done otherwise" until it no longer requires an open future, the Map holds that the phrase means what it appears to mean, and that the physical world is indeterministic in exactly the way that lets it be true. The Map's distinguishing labour, then, is not to argue that responsibility can survive determinism — the compatibilist has already shown that it can — but to argue that the leeway is *real*, and that a world in which it is real is a better fit for both the phenomenology of choice and the demands of ultimate authorship.

Two variants deserve special note from the Map's chair. **List's emergentism** is the closest neighbour: he too insists that alternative possibilities are real rather than conditional, and the disagreement reduces to a question of locus — emergent agential-level autonomy (List) versus physical influence on quantum outcomes (the Map). The Map takes the emergentist route to face a hard question about whether higher-level indeterminism can be genuine if the underlying physics is closed, a question the quantum interface is designed to answer at the physical level. **Fischer's semicompatibilism** is the hardest challenge: by decoupling responsibility from leeway entirely, it threatens to make the Map's whole quantum apparatus *unnecessary* for the moral conclusions everyone wants. The Map's reply is that leeway does real work beyond grounding responsibility — it is what makes deliberation about a genuinely open future non-illusory — but the burden of that reply is one the Map carries rather than waves away.

Consistent with **Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor has limits)**, the Map declines to treat compatibilism's parsimony — its avoidance of any non-physical or indeterministic machinery — as decisive. Simplicity is an unreliable guide where our knowledge of the mind's relation to the physical is incomplete, and the compatibilist's economy is purchased by reinterpreting the very datum (categorical openness) that the Map takes the phenomenology of agency to report.
