Manyism is Luke Roelofs' name for the thesis that **many overlapping, slightly different conscious subjects exist in one place, wherever we assumed there was just one**. It is the load-bearing move in his book-length defence of *constitutive panpsychism* against the [combination problem](/concepts/combination-problem/): instead of explaining how micro-subjects fuse into a single new macro-subject, Roelofs allows a single token experience to belong to more than one subject at once—"experience-sharing"—so a composite subject and its micro-subject parts coexist rather than one replacing the other. For The Unfinishable Map, manyism is the strongest contemporary attempt to make constitutive panpsychism work, and therefore the most credible monist rival to the Map's [interactionist dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) on the unity-and-combination terrain. It is engaged here as a rival to be taken seriously, not as evidence for dualism.

## The Core Claim: Subjects Overlap and Share

The combination problem asks how billions of micro-subjects (electrons, particles) could constitute one unified human subject when each subject seems "shut in its own skin." The standard panpsychist hope is that micro-subjects somehow merge into a new macro-subject. Sam Coleman's much-cited dilemma presses on exactly this: if subjects A and B combine into subject C, either A and B cease (so nothing combined) or A, B, and C all persist (so C is an addition, not a combination).

Roelofs' manyism takes the second horn and embraces it. In his words, "there's nothing wrong with 'manyism'—the idea that many overlapping, slightly different, conscious subjects exist in one place." The micro-subjects do *not* vanish into the composite; the composite does not replace them. Both exist, overlapping, with the composite subject *sharing* the very experiences its parts have.

The engine that makes this intelligible is **experience-sharing**: the claim that a single token experience can be had by more than one subject. Roelofs develops this through the idea that the unity of consciousness can hold *between* distinct subjects, not only within one (Roelofs 2016). His recurring analogy is that consciousness combines roughly the way mass combines—a composite's mass is not a new thing replacing its parts' masses but is constituted by them. If experiences can be shared rather than fused, the subject-summing problem dissolves: there is no need for micro-experiences to disappear into a macro-experience, because the same experience can be had by part and whole at once.

Within one human head, this implies not one mind but anywhere from two (the person plus the brain) to trillions (via the Problem of the Many—the indefinitely many slightly-different aggregates that have equal claim to constitute "you").

## Manyism Among Its Panpsychist Siblings

Manyism is one branch of the constitutive-panpsychist response to subject-summing. Its distinctive signature is that **the parts persist as subjects**. The named-anchor summaries below distinguish it from the rival solutions it competes with inside the panpsychist family.

- **Phenomenal bonding (Goff) and co-consciousness (Dainton, Miller)** posit a primitive relation that *forges one new subject* out of the micro-subjects. The contrast with manyism is sharp: bonding tries to manufacture a single owner; manyism keeps the parts as owners and lets them share. Critics of bonding (developed in [panpsychisms-combination-problem](/topics/panpsychisms-combination-problem/)) charge that it names the problem rather than solving it.

- **Fusionism (Mørch) and layered emergentism (Lewtas)** hold that micro-subjects *fuse into, or are superseded by,* a new macro-subject; the old subjects cease. This is the direct foil to manyism—where manyism's parts persist, fusionism's do not. The Map reads fusionism as relabelling emergence, the very thing constitutive panpsychism aimed to avoid.

- **Subject-eliminativism (Harris)** drops subjects entirely, retaining only consciousness and its contents, so there is no subject-summing problem because there is nothing to sum.

- **Cosmopsychism plus de-combination (Albahari; Goff's later work)** inverts the direction: start from one cosmic subject and explain how it fragments into many. Gregory Miller calls this the *[de-combination problem](/topics/open-individualism-and-the-de-combination-problem/)*—how a subject can have other subjects as proper parts—and argues it is no easier than combination. The two are the same impossibility approached from opposite ends, which is why the Map's [mereology-of-mind](/apex/mereology-of-mind/) apex makes de-combination its organising hinge. Manyism is, in effect, the opposite bet from cosmopsychism: more subjects, not one.

## The "Too Many Minds" Objection and Roelofs' Reply

Manyism's most obvious cost is the multiplication of minds: if a single head contains trillions of overlapping subjects, has the view reduced itself to absurdity? Roelofs addresses this directly in "No Such Thing as Too Many Minds" (2024), surveying metaphysical, phenomenological, and moral arguments that such multiplication is *uniquely* intolerable for minds. His reply is that each argument fails, and that "many overlapping conscious minds is no more problematic than many overlapping physical objects"—the Problem of the Many already forces us to tolerate indefinitely many overlapping *physical* objects wherever we name one, so minds inherit a difficulty they do not uniquely create.

The position is not idle metaphysics. Roelofs and Sebo (2024) argue that near-future neurotechnology could let two subjects share a single token mental state—one feeling of pleasure—and show this generates a live problem for utilitarian aggregation: if subjects share few states, the value should be counted twice; if they share many, once. Whichever way one resolves it, experience-sharing has testable-ish ethical stakes, which is part of why the position is taken seriously even by readers who reject panpsychism—Eric Schwitzgebel's review of *Combining Minds* called it "a trove of intricate, careful, intellectually honest metaphysics" while declining to accept the framework.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's central commitment is [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism): consciousness is not reducible to physical processes. Constitutive panpsychism is a *monist* programme designed to make consciousness compositional—built up out of basic consciousness the way bodies are built up out of matter—which is precisely the move the Map denies. Manyism is the most developed version of that programme on the unity question, so the Map's relation to it is the relation to its strongest rival here.

The cleanest point of contact is the **unity of consciousness**. Manyism treats phenomenal unity as an *achievement*—something constituted between subjects through experience-sharing. The Map treats [unity as primitive](/concepts/unity-of-consciousness/)—intrinsic to consciousness, not built from or shared across parts. This is where the rival genuinely bites, and where the disagreement is sharpest.

Two disciplines govern how the Map engages here. First, manyism's chief difficulty—that experience-sharing's *intelligibility* remains contested rather than secured—must not be misread as positive evidence for dualism. The monist helps itself to combinability without having earned it by the field's own standards, just as the Map holds phenomenal non-compositionality against the physicalist; that the burden is unmet is a cost the monist owes, not a point scored *for* the Map's ontology. Second, the incompatibility between manyism and the Dualism tenet is a framework boundary, not a refutation. The honest in-framework pressure the Map can apply is Coleman's dilemma and the unmet intelligibility burden on experience-sharing; beyond that, the disagreement stands at the boundary between a monist who finds shared token experiences intelligible and a dualist who does not. The Map does not refute manyism, and manyism does not refute the Map.

Manyism also touches [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds), but it is important not to conflate the two multiplications. Many-worlds multiplies *worlds* and dissolves the indexical question of why I am *this* branch; manyism multiplies *subjects* within a single world without branching reality. The Map rejects the former on indexical grounds it does not extend to the latter—manyism's proliferation of overlapping subjects is a distinct metaphysical bet that the No-Many-Worlds objection does not directly touch. A third multiplication is worth distinguishing from both: the [Many-Minds Interpretation](/topics/many-minds-interpretation/) of quantum mechanics multiplies *minds* per observer (a continuum of them) across measurement outcomes within a single unbranching physical world. Where manyism's overlapping subjects share token experiences in ordinary life, MMI's minds are quantum-measurement artefacts distributed by squared amplitude; the Map's indexical objection *does* bite against MMI (nothing picks out which mind is the continuant "I") in a way it does not against manyism's experience-sharing.

The Map's own avoidance of the combination problem is structural: it denies that matter has micro-experiences at all, so there is nothing to combine, share, or de-combine. Whether trading combination for an unspecified quantum *interaction* is genuine progress is a question the Map acknowledges rather than presumes to have settled (see [combination-problem](/concepts/combination-problem/)). What manyism shows is that the leading monist alternative pays its own steep price on exactly the terrain—unity—where the Map locates its strongest case.
