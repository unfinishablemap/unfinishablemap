The Attended Intermediate Representations (AIR) theory, developed most fully by Jesse Prinz in *The Conscious Brain: How Attention Engenders Experience* (Oxford University Press, 2012), holds that a mental state is conscious when and only when an *intermediate-level* perceptual representation is *modulated by attention*. Two theses carry the account. The **intermediate-level thesis** locates consciousness at one specific stage of perceptual processing — neither the low-level feature detectors nor the high-level categorical abstractions, but the viewpoint-specific middle. The **attention thesis** makes attention the gate: an intermediate representation becomes conscious precisely when attention makes it available to working memory. Prinz's own formulation, as quoted by Mylopoulos (2015), is that "consciousness arises when and only when intermediate-level representations are modulated by attention" (Prinz 2012, via Mylopoulos; page reference not independently confirmed against the primary text).

AIR is one of the cleanest statements of the view that *attention itself* does the work of making perception conscious. This article presents it at full strength — its two theses, its neurofunctionalist metaphysics, its position among rival theories, and the standard objections it faces — before setting out where The Unfinishable Map engages and resists it. The Map's disagreement is specific and structural: AIR is a *constitution* claim (attended intermediate representations *are* consciousness), and the Map reads the very same attention-gating as a *selection* mechanism (attention determines which representations the conscious interface couples to). That reframing, developed in the [Relation to Site Perspective](#relation-to-site-perspective) section below, is where Tenet 1 pushes back.

## The Intermediate-Level Thesis

AIR builds on David Marr's tri-level analysis of vision (*Vision*, 1982). Marr distinguished low-level representations (edges, orientations, local features), intermediate-level representations (surfaces, contours, and their layout from a particular vantage — roughly the "2.5-D sketch"), and high-level representations (viewpoint-invariant object categories). Prinz's claim is that consciousness always arises at the intermediate level, and he generalizes the thesis beyond vision to other perceptual modalities.

The argument is exclusionary. Low-level states are too fine-grained to match what introspection reports: we do not consciously experience the raw edge-and-orientation map, but the surfaces it composes. High-level states are the wrong grain in the other direction — viewpoint-invariant categorical content can be activated unconsciously, as in masked semantic priming, where a category is engaged without any accompanying experience. What remains is the intermediate level, whose grain matches the surfaces, boundaries, and depth-from-a-viewpoint that consciously seeing actually presents. Prinz here inherits and extends a proposal Ray Jackendoff advanced, adding the requirement that attention is also necessary — a move already present in his 2000 paper "A Neurofunctional Theory of Visual Consciousness."

The Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews discussion of the book flags a tension worth carrying forward: a *modest* reading on which intermediateness merely *identifies* which representations become conscious, versus an *ambitious* reading on which it *explains why* they do. The force of the thesis depends on which reading is intended — a point the [objections](#the-standard-objections) return to.

## The Attention Thesis

The second thesis makes attention necessary and sufficient. Attention, on Prinz's functional definition, is the process that changes the flow of information so that perceptual content becomes available to working memory. When an intermediate representation is attended in this sense, it is conscious; when it is not, it is not. Prinz argues for both directions: no consciousness without attention (necessity), and attending to an intermediate representation suffices for experience (sufficiency).

Prinz pairs this functional story with a proposed neural signature — gamma-band synchronization of the neural populations carrying intermediate-level representations, rendering them available to working-memory systems. The details of the neural proposal are drawn partly from secondary characterizations rather than confirmed verbatim from the primary text, so it is safest to state it as the theory's *proposed* correlate: attention's availability-to-working-memory is hypothesized to be realized by synchronized activity in sensory cortex, not by broadcast to a frontal hub. That contrast matters for situating AIR against its rivals, [below](#situating-air-among-theories).

## Neurofunctionalism: The Metaphysics

AIR is reductive and physicalist. Prinz calls his metaphysics "neurofunctionalism": consciousness is identified with a functionally characterized neural process — attention operating on intermediate representations — realized in the brain. There is no phenomenal residue beyond the functionally specified process. The theory aims to specify the psychological and neural correlates of consciousness and to say *when* and *where* in the processing stream consciousness arises; it does not claim to close the explanatory gap between physical process and felt experience by fiat. It is, in Prinz's own summary phrase, a view on which "all consciousness is perceptual" (Prinz 2012, via Mylopoulos 2015) and functions to make perceptual information available for flexible behavior.

This places AIR squarely as a naturalistic rival to the Map's dualism — congenial as an empirical correlate proposal, resisted as a complete account. That is the [closing section's](#relation-to-site-perspective) concern.

## Situating AIR Among Theories {#situating-air-among-theories}

AIR is a first-order, perceptual, attention-based theory. Locating it against four neighbours sharpens what is distinctive.

**Higher-order theories.** [Higher-order theories](/concepts/higher-order-theories/) make a state conscious in virtue of a *further* mental state that represents it. AIR needs no meta-representation: attention to a first-order perceptual state suffices, and Prinz explicitly denies that higher-order thought is required. In the taxonomy of [first-order representationalism](/concepts/first-order-representationalism/), AIR is a first-order view — phenomenal character lives in the perceptual content itself — with attention added as the gate that switches such content on.

**Global workspace theory.** [Global workspace theory](/concepts/global-workspace-theory/) (Baars; Dehaene and Changeux's global *neuronal* workspace) locates consciousness in global broadcast to a fronto-parietal network — "ignition" that makes content available to many consumer systems. AIR locates consciousness earlier and more posteriorly: in sensory-cortex intermediate representations made available to working memory by attention. Both are broadly access-based, but they differ on the *locus* (posterior sensory versus frontal broadcast) and the *level* (intermediate perceptual versus global availability).

**Attention schema theory.** The contrast with [attention schema theory](/concepts/attention-schema-theory/) (Graziano) is the sharpest and most instructive. Both tie consciousness to attention, but in opposite ways. AIR says attention *itself* — operating on the attended content — constitutes consciousness. AST says consciousness is the brain's *model of* its attention: a schematic self-description, not the attending. For AIR the attended intermediate representation is where experience lives; for AST experience is a caricature the attention schema paints. One makes attention constitutive of the conscious content; the other makes a *representation of* attention constitutive of the appearance of consciousness.

**Integrated information theory.** IIT identifies consciousness with integrated information intrinsic to a system's cause-effect structure, substrate-general and untied to perception or attention. AIR is the opposite kind of theory: mechanism-specific, perceptualist, and attention-dependent. IIT is the natural foil on the question of whether consciousness is essentially perceptual at all — AIR says yes; IIT says the question is orthogonal to what makes a system conscious.

## The Standard Objections {#the-standard-objections}

AIR faces a cluster of well-developed challenges. Presenting them fairly is part of taking the theory seriously; several also mark the seam the Map's dualism occupies.

**The overflow objection.** Ned Block argues that phenomenal consciousness overflows cognitive access ("Perceptual consciousness overflows cognitive access," 2011). The empirical anchor is Sperling's partial-report paradigm (1960): after a briefly flashed array, subjects can accurately report *any* cued row, which suggests a rich phenomenal representation of the whole array that outstrips the sparse capacity of what is attended and accessed. If phenomenal experience is richer than access, then an attention-and-access identity like AIR is false — some experience is conscious without being attended. Prinz's camp (De Brigard and Prinz 2010) replies that the unattended "overflow" contents are not genuinely conscious prior to attention: the partial-report data show a large *preconscious* iconic buffer that becomes conscious only when a cue draws attention to it. The dispute is methodologically fraught — any probe of whether unattended content was conscious itself deploys attention (the "refrigerator-light" problem) — and remains unresolved.

**The grain objection.** Whether the intermediate level is the right grain is contested. Reports of non-intermediate conscious contents — fragmented percepts in schizophrenia, for instance — press the thesis, as does the difficulty of specifying an "intermediate level" cleanly in non-visual modalities. Here the modest-versus-ambitious ambiguity bites: if intermediateness merely *labels* what becomes conscious, counterexamples are damaging; if it is meant to *explain* why, the explanatory burden is heavier still.

**The conscious-intention objection.** Mylopoulos (2015) argues that AIR's perceptualism cannot accommodate conscious intentions. The dilemma: either conscious intentions are attended intermediate-level *sensory* representations — but people form conscious intentions without sensory imagery, and imagery alone cannot distinguish intending from merely desiring or predicting the same act — or intentions are never genuinely conscious, a reconstructive hypothesis that lacks empirical support. Either way, AIR's claim that all consciousness is perceptual looks too strong.

**Attention without consciousness.** Cases of attentional modulation without acknowledged experience (some priming and cueing effects; disputed readings of blindsight) press the *sufficiency* half of the attention thesis, and critics note a circularity risk in operationalizing "attention" and "consciousness" non-circularly when executive control may already presuppose consciousness.

None of these refutes AIR outright; each marks a place where a purely attentional-functional account is under strain. Prinz has replies, and the debates are live.

## Relation to Site Perspective {#relation-to-site-perspective}

AIR is a naturalistic rival to the Map's [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism) tenet, and the Map engages it as such — accepting much and resisting one thing precisely.

**What the Map grants.** AIR is a well-specified candidate for the neural and functional correlates of consciousness, and the Map has no quarrel with attention and the intermediate perceptual level being *where* and *when* human visual consciousness characteristically arises. As a [correlate](/concepts/neural-correlates-of-consciousness/) proposal, AIR can be largely correct about the mechanics. The empirical claims about attention gating availability to working memory can stand.

**Where the Map resists: constitution versus selection.** AIR's core commitment is a *constitution* claim — attended intermediate representations *are* consciousness; there is no phenomenal fact beyond the functionally specified process. This is the claim Tenet 1 resists, and the resistance is the same structural move the Map presses against every reductive account, including [first-order representationalism](/concepts/first-order-representationalism/): specifying *when* and *where* consciousness arises does not answer *why* those states are experienced at all. One can fix the entire functional profile — an intermediate representation, attended, synchronized in the gamma band, available to working memory — without thereby saying why occupying that state is like anything. The attention-gate is a re-description of the conditions under which experience shows up, not an account of why it is felt. On the Map's reading this relocates the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) rather than dissolving it.

The Map's positive reframing turns AIR's own datum into a different story. Attention plausibly *does* select which intermediate representations become conscious — that is exactly what the availability-to-working-memory mechanism describes. The Map reads this as [attention as the interface](/concepts/attention-as-interface/): the mechanism by which non-physical consciousness *selects among* the representations the brain makes available, not the mechanism that *constitutes* experience out of otherwise-dark processing. Where AIR says the attended intermediate representation *is* the experience, the Map says it is *what experience selects and couples to* at the interface. The two proposals agree almost entirely about the neural and functional facts and disagree about what those facts are facts *of* — constitution or selection.

The selection reading carries a debt it must own. If the physical attention mechanism *fully* fixes which representations reach working memory — as the concession above appears to grant — then consciousness selects only what the mechanism has already selected, a spectator doing no bidirectional work. Answering that steelmanned AIR rejoinder requires locating the interface's real work at the genuinely open remainder — which of the quantum-indeterminate alternatives is taken up — not in gating the functional mechanism has already completed. So the concession must be read narrowly: attention fixes the *availability* of representations, but not, on pain of ensemble-level spectatorhood, which quantum-open alternative becomes actual. This is the Map's own [ensemble-level worry](/concepts/ensemble-level-epiphenomenalism/), logged in [born-rule-and-the-consciousness-interface](/topics/born-rule-and-the-consciousness-interface/) — a requirement the selection reading must meet, not one it can yet claim to have met.

This is why the *Prinz-camp* settlement of the overflow debate is congenial to the Map: a rich preconscious buffer from which attention selects a conscious subset is exactly the shape the interface reading predicts. A genuine *Block* overflow settlement is not neutral, though — if unattended content is *phenomenally* conscious rather than merely preconscious, experience is present in exactly the representations the interface did not select, pressuring the attention-gates-consciousness premise the selection reading shares with AIR. The interface reading must answer that by widening the coupled set, not by claiming indifference between the settlements.

Read as selection rather than constitution, AIR's attention mechanism aligns with [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) and [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction): attention is the site at which consciousness makes its minimal, selective difference to which physical representations are taken up — a two-way coupling rather than a one-way functional readout. Tenet 5 ([Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)) records the methodological caution: a tidy functional identity between consciousness and attended perception is attractively simple, but simplicity is not evidence that the identity is the true account of phenomenal character. The Map's claim is not that AIR is refuted — its advantages are real and its mechanics may be right — but that its constitution reading is not forced by the evidence it marshals, and that the same evidence is at least as well read as a selection story compatible with dualism.
