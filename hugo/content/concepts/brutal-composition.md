Brutal composition is Ned Markosian's answer to the Special Composition Question (SCQ)—Peter van Inwagen's question (*Material Beings*, 1990) of when some objects compose a further object: there is **no true, non-trivial, finite answer**. Composition facts are *brute*—they hold or fail to hold without any informative explanation in terms of further conditions. On this view the question "under what circumstances do the xs compose a y?" has been mis-posed if it demands a finite, illuminating criterion; the only true general answers are either trivial (a disjunction of every particular case) or infinitely long. This is the third of the restricted-composition-question rivals the Map engages, alongside [metaontological deflationism](/concepts/metaontological-deflationism/) and [information-compression composition](/concepts/information-compression-composition/)—and it occupies a distinctive corner: where deflationism says there is no deep question, and compression gives a non-conscious *informative* answer, brutalism says the question is real but admits no informative answer at all. This page presents Markosian's thesis at full strength, then gives the Map's calibrated reply. Following the Map's [discipline on evidential status](/project/evidential-status-discipline/), brutal composition is treated as a serious SCQ rival, and the Map declines to claim it has been refuted.

## Markosian's Thesis: Composition as Brute

Ned Markosian ("Brutal Composition," *Philosophical Studies* 92(3), 1998, 211–249) frames the SCQ as a demand for a single principle stating the necessary and sufficient conditions under which some objects compose a further object. He surveys the candidate answers—contact, fastening, cohesion, fusion, van Inwagen's life criterion, universalism, nihilism—and argues that the moderate, restricted answers all fail, while the two extreme answers (universalism and nihilism) succeed only by paying intuitive costs Markosian finds prohibitive. The moderate answers founder on a recurring problem: any finite, non-trivial condition either draws an arbitrary line (why *this* degree of contact and not slightly less?) or generates counterexamples in both directions.

His positive proposal is that we should stop looking. Markosian distinguishes two readings of the SCQ. On the first, it asks for a finite, non-trivial principle; his thesis is that **there is no such principle to be had**. On the second, it asks merely whether there is *some* true answer, however unstructured; here the answer is yes, but it is the "brutal" one—a possibly infinite specification of which particular collections compose and which do not, with no unifying rationale beneath it. As Markosian puts it, the facts about composition are not "determined by, or explained by" any non-mereological facts; they are, in his term, *brute*.

The view has a precise shape worth preserving. Brutalism is **not** the claim that composition is arbitrary or unprincipled in the sense of random. Markosian holds that there are determinate facts about which xs compose a y—he is a *realist* about composition, and a restrictivist (some things compose, some do not). What he denies is that these facts are *grounded* in or *derivable from* any further, more general condition. The boundary between composing and non-composing collections is, on his account, non-arbitrary in the sense of being a genuine fact of the matter, yet **unanalysable**—it bottoms out without an informative criterion above it. The flavour is concrete: Markosian holds that the iron girders in Paris do compose the Eiffel Tower and the limestone blocks at Giza do compose the pyramid, while a scattered heap of disconnected objects does not compose anything—these are determinate facts, but no general principle explains *why* the line falls where it does.

Markosian defends the cost of this position by analogy. Many philosophers accept brute facts elsewhere: the laws of nature, on some views, are brute; identity facts, on others; modal facts, on still others. If brute facts are tolerable in those domains, refusing them in mereology requires a special argument that the SCQ *must* have an informative answer—an assumption Markosian thinks restricted-composition theorists smuggle in without defending. The burden, he argues, lies with those who insist composition must be analysable, not with the brutalist who reports that no analysis survives scrutiny.

## How Brutalism Differs From Its Two Siblings

Brutal composition is easily conflated with its neighbours, and keeping them distinct sharpens what is at stake.

[Metaontological deflationism](/concepts/metaontological-deflationism/) (Thomasson's easy ontology, Hirsch's quantifier variance) denies that the hard SCQ has *any* deep fact to track: existence-talk is either trivially answered by conceptual competence or plastic across equally legitimate quantifier meanings. Markosian rejects this. For the brutalist there *is* a deep, determinate fact about which collections compose—it simply has no informative explanation. Brutalism is a *realist* answer that happens to be uninformative; deflationism is an *anti-realist* dissolution of the question. The deflationist would regard Markosian's brute compositional facts as themselves a verbal artefact; Markosian would regard the deflationist as denying a fact he takes to be real.

[Information-compression composition](/concepts/information-compression-composition/) (Bird, with Beni's thermodynamic-depth refinement) is brutalism's mirror image among the realists. Bird agrees with Markosian that composition is restricted and a matter of fact, but disagrees that the fact is unanalysable: parts compose, on Bird's view, exactly when treating them as one *compresses* the description of the world, an informative and non-conscious criterion. Where Bird offers a finite analysis, Markosian argues no such analysis can be correct. The two are direct competitors on the same realist terrain.

So the three rivals give three different answers to the same realist-restrictivist question: deflationism says there is no deep question; information-compression gives a non-conscious *informative* answer; brutal composition says there is a deep question with a determinate answer but **no informative answer at all**. The Map's own consciousness-involving proposal (developed in [consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition](/topics/consciousness-and-the-metaphysics-of-composition/)) is a fourth realist answer—an informative one, like Bird's, but with a different mark of genuine wholeness.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map's interest in brutal composition is governed by the [fifth tenet](/tenets/#occams-limits)—that simplicity is unreliable as a tiebreaker when knowledge is incomplete—and here the connection is unusually direct.

Brutalism is, in effect, the report that one well-studied metaphysical question resists all attempts at an informative, simplicity-respecting analysis. The standard methodological move when candidate criteria proliferate and each draws an arbitrary line is to reach for the simplest unifying principle, or failing that, to suspect the question is confused. Markosian's contribution is to take seriously a third possibility the simplicity reflex tends to suppress: that the boundary is real, determinate, and *non-arbitrary in fact* while remaining unanalysable in principle. A non-arbitrary-yet-unanalysable boundary is exactly the kind of object the fifth tenet anticipates—a place where simplicity-as-tiebreaker runs out, because there is no simpler ground beneath the fact for simplicity to prefer. The Map reads brutal composition, on this point, not as an embarrassment to be explained away but as a *datum* consonant with its fifth tenet: brute facts are precisely where the demand for a simpler explanation has to stop, and pretending otherwise manufactures false economy. The alignment is one of methodological consistency rather than positive evidence—the credibility of a brutalist position *illustrates* what the fifth tenet anticipates, but a methodological illustration is not an empirical confirmation, and the Map claims only the former.

This reading is a point of partial alignment, and the Map should not overclaim it. Markosian draws no dualist or anti-physicalist conclusion; his brutalism is metaphysically austere and consciousness plays no role in it whatever. The Map's own answer to the SCQ is *informative*—it proposes that consciousness, or the integrated structure associated with it, marks genuine wholes (see [composition-and-consciousness](/concepts/composition-and-consciousness/)). That proposal is in straightforward tension with brutalism's central claim that *no* informative criterion is correct. So the relationship is two-sided. On methodology, the Map and Markosian agree that the simplicity reflex can mislead, and the existence of a credible brutalist position is itself a fifth-tenet illustration. On the first-order question, brutalism is a *rival* to the Map's consciousness-criterion, not an ally: if Markosian is right that no finite criterion succeeds, the Map's consciousness criterion fails along with Bird's compression criterion and van Inwagen's life criterion.

The Map's calibrated reply to that rivalry is modest. It cannot show that brutalism is false, because brutalism's core claim—that every proposed informative criterion has counterexamples—is supported by exactly the survey of failures that motivates the Map to look for a *better* criterion rather than abandon the search. What the Map can press is a burden the brutalist still carries: even granting that no criterion is exceptionlessly correct, the candidate criteria philosophers reach for cluster persistently around organised, causally-unified, integrated systems rather than scattering across gerrymandered collections, and the brutalist owes an account of why the brute facts should *look* so systematically law-like if they are genuinely groundless. Markosian can reply—perhaps the clustering reflects our interests, or perhaps near-regularities are exactly what one expects when many local brute facts share a cause we have not isolated—but that reply is owed, and it is not free. As with deflationism and compression, this moves the argument one step; it does not end it. The honest verdict is that brutal composition is a serious SCQ rival the Map has neither refuted nor needs to refute to hold its own informative proposal as a live, tenet-favoured candidate.
