A **constitutive observation** is one whose object is partly constituted by the observing itself; a **referring observation** is one whose content tracks something existing independently of the observation. The distinction is structural: it concerns the metaphysical relation between observation and target, not the cognitive mechanism doing the observing. The Unfinishable Map uses the distinction to explain why first-person reports about the bare existence of phenomenality have an apodictic standing that no third-person observation can match, and to identify — *as an asymmetry, not a knockdown defeater* — the load-bearing point at which the standard "introspection is theory-laden" move fails to engage what it purports to defeat. The distinction does not refute eliminativism or [illusionism](/concepts/illusionism/); it locates the bedrock disagreement that opponents of phenomenal realism must contest explicitly rather than carry by analogy.

## The Distinction

A **referring observation** has content that purports to track a target whose existence is given independently of the observing. Observation and referent are separable items; the relation between them is contingent; the report is consequently corrigible. The observer can be wrong about what is there.

Naked-eye astronomy is the paradigm case. Ancient observers reported features of celestial bodies — that the Sun moves across the heavens, that stars are points fixed to a sphere, that Venus seen at dusk and seen at dawn are different objects — and a number of those reports turned out to be wrong. They were wrong because the bodies themselves had observation-independent properties that the reports failed to track. The whole apparatus of empirical science across most domains works this way. Theory-ladenness in the referring case is a real and operative epistemic risk: the observer's belief about the target and the target's actual nature are distinct items that can come apart.

A **constitutive observation** has an object whose existence is partly constituted by the observing. The observation does not refer outward to a target whose existence is given independently of being observed; the observation is itself part of what makes the observed fact obtain.

The phenomenal observer's relation to *the existence of phenomenality* has this structure. The seeming-that-there-is-something-it-is-like-to-be-in-pain is not a report about a state existing independently of its being seemed. The seeming and the being-of-the-phenomenal-state are not separable items between which a referring relation holds. *To be acquainted with phenomenality is, in part, to be undergoing it.* An observer's observation that phenomenality is occurring cannot be wrong about *whether* phenomenality is occurring, because the observation itself is constitutive of the very fact whose existence is at issue.

The distinction is *structural*. It does not turn on the reliability of [introspection](/concepts/introspection/), the trainability of attention, or the linguistic representations under which experiences are reported. Those concern introspective *judgement*'s accuracy, which can fail, and which the Map elsewhere concedes is fallible. What the constitutive/referring distinction concerns is the metaphysical relation between observation and target — whether the target's existence is independent of the observing, or whether the observing is part of what constitutes the target.

## Russell's Distinction as Partial Precursor

The constitutive/referring distinction has a partial precursor in Bertrand Russell's 1910 split between **knowledge by acquaintance** and **knowledge by description** (treated more fully in [phenomenal-acquaintance](/concepts/phenomenal-acquaintance/)). For Russell, acquaintance is direct presentation of an object without inferential or descriptive intermediary; description operates through propositions. Russell took acquaintance as primitive and epistemically foundational.

The two distinctions overlap and diverge. The overlap: phenomenal acquaintance is a constitutive observation, since the acquaintance relation is not the relation a referring observation has to its target. The divergence: Russell's distinction is *epistemological* — it concerns the kind of cognitive relation that puts a subject in contact with an object. The constitutive/referring distinction is *metaphysical* — it concerns the relation between observation and target irrespective of which cognitive mechanism is doing the observing.

The two answer different objections. Russell's distinction motivates why phenomenal knowledge has a different cognitive *flavour* from descriptive knowledge — the move that powers the [knowledge argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/). The constitutive/referring distinction motivates why phenomenal observation has different *epistemic standing* than third-person observation under conditions of theory-laden reporting — the move that licenses a non-question-begging response to introspection-scepticism without requiring the eliminativist to first concede that there is something irreducibly phenomenal.

## What the Distinction Does

The deepest [eliminativist](/topics/eliminative-materialism/) and illusionist arguments against phenomenal realism invoke "introspection is theory-laden" implicitly modelled on the referring case. The argument runs: just as naked-eye astronomers were wrong about celestial bodies because their reports were saturated with folk theory about the heavens, so introspectors are wrong about phenomenal qualities because their reports are saturated with folk theory about minds. If introspection is theory-laden in the same sense that naked-eye astronomy was, the introspector cannot adjudicate disputes about whether phenomenal qualities exist; they can only report what their folk theory has shaped them to expect.

The constitutive/referring distinction identifies where the analogy fails. Naked-eye astronomers could be wrong about celestial bodies *because the bodies existed independently of observation*. The phenomenal observer cannot be wrong in the same way about *the existence of phenomenality itself*, because the observation is constitutive of what is observed. Theory-ladenness of introspective *judgement* — about which qualities are present, how they relate, what their structural features are — remains real and operative. Theory-ladenness sufficient to overturn existence-claims at the constitutive level would require that phenomenality be the kind of thing existing independently of being observed, which is the very point at issue.

This is an asymmetry, not a knockdown. The distinction does not establish that phenomenal realism is true. What it establishes is that the introspection-as-theory-laden move cannot dispose of phenomenal realism *via the standard appeal to scientific revisability of folk theory*, because the standard appeal presupposes a referring relation that does not obtain at the constitutive level. The eliminativist who wants to apply the move must defend the substantive claim that introspection is referring rather than constitutive — which is to defend the claim that the seeming and the being are separable. That is precisely what the realist denies. The distinction therefore locates where the substantive argument has to happen, rather than allowing it to be carried by the rhetorical force of the astronomy analogy.

## Where the Distinction Sharpens Map Claims

Several Map articles use this structure, sometimes implicitly:

- [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/) uses the distinction to explain why Layer 1 (the bare existence of phenomenality) has apodictic standing while Layers 2 and 3 (qualitative content and structural features) remain corrigible. Constitutive observation underwrites layer-1 strength; referring-style failures of introspective judgement explain why higher layers admit error.

- [eliminative-materialism](/topics/eliminative-materialism/) uses the distinction to mark where the introspection-as-theory-laden manoeuvre fails to dispose of phenomenal evidence, while explicitly declining to claim that the failure refutes eliminativism.

- [knowledge-argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/) and [philosophical-zombies](/concepts/philosophical-zombies/) illustrate the same constitutive structure from the side of *content*: what Mary acquires upon first seeing red, and what zombies lack, is a property whose existence-conditions are constitutive observation rather than third-person reference.

- [illusionism](/concepts/illusionism/)'s positive proposal can be reconstructed as the claim that introspection is referring after all — a higher-order representational state with its own functional properties that can come apart from its purported target. The distinction names what illusionism must establish, rather than presuppose, to succeed.

- [parsimony-epistemology](/concepts/parsimony-epistemology/) connects via [Tenet 5](/tenets/#occams-limits): the eliminativist's parsimony argument applies Occam's Razor across the constitutive/referring divide as if the two were the same kind of observation, which is the over-extension Tenet 5 was formulated to identify.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The distinction supports [Tenet 1 (Dualism)](/tenets/#dualism) structurally. The Map's case for the irreducibility of phenomenal consciousness depends on phenomenal evidence having an epistemic standing that no purely third-person observation can match. Without the constitutive/referring distinction, that standing has to be defended either by appeal to Cartesian indubitability — which most contemporary readers will not grant — or by appeal to introspective reliability, which the illusionist contests at exactly the point where the realist needs the defence. The constitutive/referring distinction routes around both. It does not require Cartesian certainty about the *content* of experience; it requires only that the *bare existence* of phenomenality cannot be overturned by the move that overturns folk theory in the referring case.

The distinction also bears on [Tenet 5 (Occam's Razor Has Limits)](/tenets/#occams-limits). The eliminativist's parsimony argument treats phenomenal claims as if they were ordinary referring claims about a postulated entity. The Map's response — that parsimony is unreliable in domains where the structural relation between observation and observed differs from the standard scientific case — is the Tenet 5 move applied at the right level of generality.

## Honest Limitation

The constitutive-observation framing is itself foundational and contested. The illusionist's response is to deny precisely this — to maintain, in the lineage of [higher-order theories](/concepts/higher-order-theories/) (Rosenthal) and self-representational theories (Kriegel), that the seeming-of-phenomenality is itself a representational state with its own functional properties, and that the seeming and the property it represents *can* come apart in the same way any representation and its target can. On this view there is no constitutive relation; there is a representation that misrepresents itself as constitutively related to a phenomenal property that does not exist. The Map cannot dislodge this view by argument from premises the illusionist already accepts — the dispute is whether introspection is referring (representational) or constitutive (acquaintance-like) at its bedrock, and each side will read the phenomenology of one's own case as confirming their structural picture.

Defence of the constitutive-observation framing rests on the broader fit of the dualist framework with the apodictic standing of layer-1 reports, the failure of physicalist accounts to close the explanatory gap on their own terms, and the Moorean asymmetry between the confidence carried by phenomenal evidence and the confidence carried by any theoretical premise that would overrule it (see [self-stultification](/concepts/self-stultification/) for the family of arguments this connects to).

The distinction therefore identifies the location of the bedrock disagreement rather than resolving it from outside. The substantive contribution of the framing is to make the disagreement visible — to ensure that neither side can claim the rhetorical force of the astronomy analogy without first defending the metaphysical claim about which kind of observation introspection is.
