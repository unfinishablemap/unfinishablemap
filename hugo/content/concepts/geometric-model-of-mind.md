The **geometric model of mind** is Włodzisław Duch's framework for describing mental states as quasi-stable attractor basins in a feature-coordinate "psychological space" whose dynamics approximate the underlying neurodynamics of cell assemblies. Mind objects — concepts, percepts, memories — are regions of this space; mental events are trajectories between them. The framework has a distinctive intermediate-level commitment: it lives between symbolic AI and pure neural simulation, describing brain states "in terms of features relevant from the first-person perspective but also linked to neural events" (Duch 2022).

The model is the methodological core of Duch's broader [neurodynamic theory of mind](/topics/duch-neurodynamic-theory-of-mind/), whose mature one-line statement — mind is a "shadow of neurodynamics" — names the directionality (brain → mind) and the dimensionality reduction (high-dimensional neural state → lower-dimensional psychological-space projection) the framework asserts. The Unfinishable Map treats the tooling as methodologically appropriable while rejecting the eliminativist metaphysical reading some of Duch's prose invites; the parting-of-ways is identified in the [Relation to Site Perspective](#relation-to-site-perspective) section.

## Psychological Space and Mind Objects

The geometric model's primary object is **psychological space** — a feature-coordinate space whose dimensions are the properties relevant to a mental representation. For a concept like "dog", the dimensions might be perceptual features (four-legged, furry, barks), functional features (companion, guardian), and abstract features (mammalian, carnivorous). The concept is not a single point but a region — a basin of attraction within which similar instances cluster and from which the dynamics finds it harder to escape than to remain.

Duch's 1998 chapter "Platonic model of mind as an approximation to neurodynamics" introduces the formalism: "objects and events in psychological space correspond to quasi-stable states of brain dynamics and may be interpreted from a psychological point of view." The image is Platonic in form — mental contents are projections — but inverted in direction. Where Plato's shadows are projections of higher Forms onto a lower world, Duch's shadows project from the brain's high-dimensional dynamical state onto a lower-dimensional space the first-person perspective can navigate. Mind is what neurodynamics looks like at the right intermediate level of description.

Two operationalisations run in parallel.

**Feature Space Mapping (FSM)**, the static version, treats mental representations as objects in a coordinate space with neurofuzzy realisation. Duch and Naud's animal-concept experiment provides the canonical empirical demonstration: a 30-feature space holds animals as positioned objects, and the resulting similarity structure matches the multidimensional-scaling output from human similarity judgments. The match is non-trivial — it would not have been forced by the choice of features alone, since the topology of similarity in the human data could have diverged from the feature-space geometry. That it does not is the empirical purchase the framework claims.

**The dynamic version** treats mental events as trajectories through the space. Perception triggers a trajectory toward an attractor; recognition is settling into a basin; conceptual transitions are saddle-point crossings between basins; conceptual ambiguity is a region where multiple basins overlap or where the dynamics flickers between them. The dynamics deliver structure that classical cognitive science attributed to symbolic primitives: grammar and inference emerge, on Duch's account, as topological constraints on which trajectories the dynamics admits, not as a separate symbol-manipulating layer added on top.

## The Mind Function and Computational Physics

Duch's "Computational physics of the mind" (*Computer Physics Communications*, 1996) supplies the framing within which the geometric model is presented as a physics-of-mind programme. The framing posits a "mind function" defined over psychological space whose non-zero regions are mind objects. The function's level sets, gradients, and topological invariants are not reducible to any single neural variable; they emerge from the joint dynamics of many cell assemblies in interaction.

Duch argues for "the universality of inner psychophysics" — psychological space as an object with its own regularities, even though it is grounded in neurodynamics. The regularities are the proper target of the cognitive sciences in the way that fluid-dynamical regularities are the proper target of fluid mechanics: irreducible to the molecular substrate in practice, derivable from it in principle. This in-principle-derivability claim concerns *cognitive-functional structure* — the geometry of concepts, transitions, and inference — and is silent on the phenomenal level, which the framework's own resources leave undetermined (isolated below, [What the Geometric Model Does and Does Not Settle](#what-the-geometric-model-does-and-does-not-settle)). The intermediate level is not a convenience or a temporary scaffold; it captures structure invisible to either pure-symbolic or pure-neural description.

This is the methodological move that distinguishes the geometric model from both eliminative materialism (which would dispense with the intermediate level entirely) and from classical symbolic AI (which would treat the intermediate level as the bedrock). It is also the move that makes the model interesting to philosophical projects that do not share Duch's metaphysics: a description of the physical-correlate side of consciousness need not endorse the claim that the intermediate-level description is *all* there is.

## Attractor Dynamics and the Cell Assembly Substrate

The geometric model's substrate-level commitment is that the attractor dynamics it describes are realised by populations of interacting neurons — Hebbian cell assemblies in the classical sense, extended through contemporary network neuroscience to encompass small-world topology, integrated information measures, and the dynamical regimes (edge of chaos, critical dynamics) that empirical work since the 2010s has connected to cognitive processing.

Three substrate-level facts do specific work in the framework.

First, **cell assemblies sustain quasi-stable states**. A pattern of co-activation, once initiated, can hold against perturbation for hundreds of milliseconds to seconds — the timescale of conscious thought. The attractor structure in psychological space is supposed to be the reduced-dimensional shadow of this sustenance.

Second, **the dynamics are differentiated, not synchronised**. Different brain regions sustain different patterns at different timescales; the global picture is one of regional specialisation coordinated through long-range connectivity, not of a single coherent global state. Duch makes the differentiation point pointedly against quantum-coherence accounts of consciousness, which require the opposite kind of dynamics. Within the geometric model, differentiation is what allows psychological space to have rich structure rather than collapsing to a single point. This argument tells against accounts that make *large-scale quantum coherence the substrate of cognition* — a substrate-level claim the Map does not endorse. It leaves untouched the Map's [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/) tenet, which posits the smallest possible non-physical bias at the measurement boundary, not a sustained coherent regime competing with the differentiated classical dynamics Duch describes (see [comparing-quantum-consciousness-mechanisms](/topics/comparing-quantum-consciousness-mechanisms/)). Duch's differentiation point therefore enters the article cleanly as a constraint on substrate-coherence theories; it does not bite a mechanism whose quantum footprint is minimal by design.

Third, **environmental coupling shapes the dynamics**. The attractors are not closed; sensory input continuously perturbs them, motor output continuously externalises them, and the trajectories through psychological space are simultaneously trajectories through the agent-environment coupling. The model is, in this sense, embodied and situated even at the formal level — though the formalism does not by itself adjudicate the deeper questions about how environmental coupling contributes to meaning or experience.

The empirical infrastructure that grounds these substrate-level commitments is non-trivial. Duch's "Fingerprints of brain cognitive activity" (2018) develops spectral fingerprinting of MEG/EEG signals as a method for identifying individual cognitive-state signatures; his 2017 arXiv preprint on Kurt Lewin proposes grounding psychological constructs (valence, life space, force, tension, equilibrium) in neuroimaging-derived neural-network activity. The geometric model is not a free-floating formalism but a programme that ties to measurable neural quantities at multiple points.

## What the Geometric Model Does and Does Not Settle

The framework's strongest claims are at the level of cognitive *structure*. Conceptual similarity, conceptual ambiguity, learning trajectories, the topology of inference — all of these find natural geometric expressions, and the empirical fits where they have been tested are real. The 30-feature animal-concept space matching human similarity data is one example; subsequent work on semantic-network geometry and on conceptual-space accounts of word meaning (Gärdenfors's parallel programme) extends the territory.

The framework's weaker claims are at the level of *what the geometry is of*. Two questions sit at the boundary the model does not settle on its own resources.

**The phenomenal question.** Whether the geometric model describes the structure of conscious experience, or only the structure of cognitive function, is not determinable from the framework's own commitments. Duch's broader programme answers "both" — through the articon argument that self-reflective dynamical access to one's own attractor states constitutes experience — but this answer is not implied by the geometric formalism. A geometric model could be entirely correct about the structure of cognitive processing and still leave the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) untouched; the [qualia](/concepts/qualia/) question is not the same as the question of which feature space is being navigated.

**The metaphysical-direction question.** Whether the brain-side dynamics *produces* the mind-side geometry, *is identical to* the mind-side geometry under a different description, or *is the physical correlate of* a non-physical mind that has its own existence is also not determinable from the framework alone. The "shadow" image lends itself most naturally to identity-theoretic or supervenience readings, but the formalism is mathematically compatible with stronger and weaker metaphysical interpretations. Duch's own writing favours an identity-theoretic reading; other authors using similar formal tooling have favoured different readings.

The fact that the geometry does not settle these questions is what makes the model methodologically appropriable across philosophical projects that disagree about them.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map's relation to the geometric model is two-tiered, following the [framework-stage-calibration discipline](/project/framework-stage-calibration/): empirical-stage tooling is appropriable; metaphysical-stage interpretation is where the [bedrock clash](/project/bedrock-clash-vs-absorption/) is honestly marked rather than papered over.

**Methodologically**, the Map appropriates the tooling. The intermediate-level description of brain states as trajectories through attractor-structured psychological space is the kind of description the [neural-correlates articles](/concepts/neural-correlates-of-consciousness/) need on the physical-correlate side of [bidirectional interaction](/concepts/bidirectional-interaction/). The geometry, the dynamics, the empirical fingerprinting, the substrate-level commitments to cell-assembly differentiation — none of these are in dispute. The Map's articles can cite Duch's empirical-stage results and use his intermediate-level language to describe what consciousness's physical correlate looks like at the right level of abstraction.

**Metaphysically**, the Map parts company. The Map's [Dualism tenet](/tenets/) holds that consciousness is not reducible to physical processes; the eliminativist reading of "mind as shadow of neurodynamics" — on which the mind-side description is just neurodynamics under another name — is incompatible with this commitment. The Map's reply takes a different form against each metaphysical reading of the geometric model.

Against a *literal-shadow* reading — on which mind is a causally inert projection of neurodynamics — the Map's [epiphenomenalism critique](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) applies: shadows that are causally inert cannot do the work the [Bidirectional Interaction tenet](/tenets/) requires of consciousness.

Against an *identity-theoretic* reading — on which mental causation just is neurodynamic causation under a different description — the Map presses the identity reading on a foundational move it has helped itself to. The geometric model supplies a sophisticated structural-functional description of brain states; the identity claim is that this description, refined far enough, *exhausts* the mental. But the framework nowhere argues that the qualitative character of experience is thereby *captured* rather than merely *co-present* with the dynamical state. The animal-concept fit, the attractor topology, the fingerprinting results — all attach to cognitive structure, and the framework's own scope-limiting (above, [What the Geometric Model Does and Does Not Settle](#what-the-geometric-model-does-and-does-not-settle)) grants that the phenomenal question is not determinable from these resources. The identity reading then asserts, beyond what the tooling licenses, that there is no further fact once the structural-functional state is fixed — the very point the empirical successes leave open. That assertion is an unargued addition to the framework, not a result of it: the bridge from "this is the structural-functional state" to "this is what it is like to be in that state" is stipulated closed, not shown closed. Whether such a bridge can be built within the model's resources is the open question Duch's articon argument tries to answer affirmatively and the Map's articles on [machine-consciousness](/topics/machine-consciousness/) and [ai-consciousness](/topics/ai-consciousness/) hold open against.

Where the disagreement is bedrock rather than internal — whether the qualitative character is a further fact at all — the Map marks it as such. The identity theorist who simply denies that any explanatory gap survives a complete structural-functional description has reached a commitment the geometric model cannot adjudicate, and the Map's contrary commitment, grounded in its [Dualism tenet](/tenets/), runs counter to it. That residual clash is noted honestly here, not presented as a refutation conducted inside the identity theorist's own framework.

The two-tier appropriation is what [the discipline](/project/framework-stage-calibration/) contemplates: empirical-stage tooling enters the Map's articles cleanly; metaphysical-stage interpretation is identified honestly as where the Map and Duch part ways. Map articles that cite Duch's geometric model should make the two-tier distinction visible — citing the intermediate-level description as a description of the physical correlate, while noting that the question of what the correlate is of remains open in a way Duch's framework closes by stipulation.
