The **Phenomenal Constitution Thesis (PCT)** is the claim that the phenomenal character of a conscious state—what the state is *like* to undergo—partly *constitutes* the content or meaning of that state, rather than merely accompanying, causing, or covarying with it. On this view, to grasp the meaning of a thought *is* to undergo a certain experiential character; strip the experience away and the determinate content goes with it. PCT is the load-bearing premise behind much of The Unfinishable Map's resistance to functionalist accounts of understanding: if meaning is constituted by phenomenal character, then a system without phenomenal character does not genuinely understand, however well it manipulates symbols.

"PCT" is the Map's house term—a compression of the strong [cognitive-phenomenology](/concepts/cognitive-phenomenology/) liberalism and the phenomenal-intentionality programme, defined here against the established literature rather than borrowed as an external term of art. No major figure uses this exact phrase as a named position. What the literature does supply is the precise machinery the thesis needs: a worked-out sense of "constitution" that is stronger than [supervenience](/concepts/supervenience/) yet weaker than identity, and a [constituting-versus-accompanying](#constitution-vs-accompaniment) distinction (explained below) drawn within the cognitive-phenomenology debate. The Map adopts the constitutive horn abductively; it is a [contested thesis the Map advances](#abductive-bet), not a settled result.

## What "Constitution" Means Here {#constitution}

The word doing the work is *constitution*, and in metaphysics it names a specific relation distinct from both identity and supervenience. The standard illustration is a clay statue: the lump of clay *constitutes* the statue without *being identical* to it. The statue and the lump have different persistence conditions—reshape the clay and the statue is destroyed while the lump survives—so they are numerically distinct even while spatially coincident. As the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy summarises David Wiggins's formulation, the constituted object "consists in" and is "nothing over and above" the object it is made from, yet remains a distinct thing (Wasserman 2026). Mark Johnston's "Constitution is not Identity" (1992) is the canonical statement that the two relations must be kept apart.

Three features of the relation transfer to the phenomenal case:

- **Non-identity.** A thing can be constituted by another without being identical to it. PCT can therefore keep content and phenomenal character numerically distinct while still making the former depend on the latter.
- **Asymmetric, non-causal dependence.** Constitution is an "in virtue of" relation, not a causal one. The statue exists *in virtue of* the clay's being shaped; the clay does not *cause* the statue at a later time. PCT claims content holds *in virtue of* phenomenal character, not as a downstream causal effect.
- **Hyperintensionality.** Constitution is finer-grained than mere necessary covariation. Two properties can necessarily covary without one constituting the other; the SEP notes the relation is "better framed in terms of metaphysical explanation or grounding" than supervenience (citing Bennett 2004).

This vocabulary lets the Map say precisely *how* a quale can fix a content: as an asymmetric, non-causal, hyperintensional dependence of meaning on experiential character.

## PCT Against Three Weaker (and Stronger) Theses {#three-contrasts}

PCT is sharpest when triangulated against its neighbours. It is stronger than phenomenal realism, stronger than supervenience, and weaker than identity.

**PCT versus phenomenal realism.** Phenomenal realism—treated on the Map within [phenomenal-consciousness](/concepts/phenomenal-consciousness/) and [qualia](/concepts/qualia/)—asserts only that qualia are *real*, irreducible features of experience. PCT adds a job for them to do: real qualia that *constitute content*. One can be a phenomenal realist about, say, the redness of red while denying that any phenomenal character constitutes the *meaning* of a thought about justice. PCT is the strictly stronger commitment, and the gap between the two is exactly the work the thesis is meant to perform.

**PCT versus supervenience.** A supervenience thesis says content covaries with phenomenal character: no difference in content without a difference in experience. This is mere covariation and is symmetric in a way constitution is not. Supervenience leaves open *which* fixes which, and it is compatible with both being fixed by some third, non-phenomenal base. PCT makes the stronger directional claim that phenomenal character is the *in-virtue-of* ground of content—the asymmetric dependence supervenience cannot capture.

**PCT versus identity.** The crudest version of the view would say content simply *is* a quale—"meaning = phenomenal character." PCT does not require this. Borrowing Johnston's lesson, constitution without identity lets the Map hold that phenomenal character constitutes content while the two remain numerically distinct, with potentially different persistence and individuation conditions. This matters because outright identity invites obvious counterexamples (the same content under different experiential modes; the same experience deployed across distinct contents), whereas the constitution reading absorbs them.

## Constituting versus Accompanying {#constitution-vs-accompaniment}

The distinction PCT turns on was given its canonical form by Elijah Chudnoff in *Cognitive Phenomenology* (Routledge, 2015). Chudnoff separates the claim that cognitive states *constitute* a proprietary phenomenal character from the weaker claim that phenomenal character merely *accompanies* them. The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy renders the constitutive sense as "a non-causal explanatory relation that can alternatively be picked out by 'in virtue of' or 'constitutively dependent on'" (IEP, "Cognitive Phenomenology"). The evidential engine for proprietary cognitive phenomenology, on Chudnoff's account, is the phenomenal-contrast argument: the same dot array experienced as a mere array versus as a step in a proof differs phenomenally though the sensory input is held fixed.

A crucial role-accuracy point: **Chudnoff supplies the distinction but is a *critic* of content-PCT, not a proponent.** He argues *against* phenomenal intentionality for cognitive states—that the contents of demonstrative, indexical, natural-kind, and mathematical concepts do *not* constitutively depend on phenomenal properties alone. So the Map's frequent citation "Chudnoff distinguishes accompanying from constituting" is faithful to his *distinction*, but the Map sides *against* Chudnoff on the content-determinacy verdict. The actual proponents of the strong horn the Map adopts come from the phenomenal-intentionality programme (Horgan and Tienson's thesis that phenomenology is "primary to all other forms of intentionality," and the associated work of Pitt, Strawson, Siewert, and Kriegel). The Map takes the constitutive horn over the accompaniment horn abductively; it does not borrow the verdict from Chudnoff.

## The Direction of Constitution {#direction}

"Constitution" appears on both sides of the mind–body debate, and PCT must specify which way the relation runs. In the contemporary (2020s) metaphysics of mind, grounding-and-constitution physicalists deploy the *inverse* direction: phenomenal properties are *grounded in* or *constituted by* physical properties, making the phenomenal non-fundamental. PCT runs the relation the other way—phenomenal character constitutes *content*—and presupposes the phenomenal is not itself reducible to the physical. The two deployments share a verb and nothing else; conflating them inverts the thesis.

Two further homonyms are worth flagging to avoid cross-contamination. The phenomenal constitution thesis is distinct from the *temporal* constitution thesis (the claim that consciousness constitutes temporal wholes—same verb, different object), and it has nothing to do with governance documents that happen to share the word "constitution." PCT is exclusively about phenomenal character constituting intentional content.

## The Acquaintance Bridge {#acquaintance}

A convergent thread connects PCT to the Map's [phenomenal-acquaintance](/concepts/phenomenal-acquaintance/) concept. Acquaintance theorists explain the immediacy of phenomenal self-knowledge through constitution: the introspective state is *partly constituted by* the introspected state. As the SEP entry on self-knowledge puts it, on acquaintance accounts "an experience's phenomenal reality … constitutes how it appears to the thinker" (Gertler 2021; the figures cited include Chalmers 2003 and Horgan and Kriegel 2007). Horgan and Kriegel argue that for a restricted class of phenomenal beliefs, the phenomenal reality *constitutes* the epistemic appearance, closing the usual gap between seeming and being for phenomenal states.

This is the same constitution relation, applied at the epistemic level: where PCT says phenomenal character constitutes content, the acquaintance line says phenomenal reality constitutes its own appearance to the subject. The two reinforce the Map's [phenomenal-authority](/concepts/constitutive-vs-referring-observation/) position—if the appearance is constituted by the reality, introspection of phenomenal states is not a fallible report *about* a separate fact but is partly made of the fact it reports.

## Why the Map Adopts PCT—Abductively {#abductive-bet}

The Map holds PCT as an *abductive bet*, not a demonstrated truth, and the modal register stays calibrated to that status throughout. The case is inference to the best explanation of several explananda that competing positions tend to split:

- **Content determinacy.** What makes a thought about *rabbits* rather than *undetached rabbit parts*? Physical and functional description famously underdetermine content (the Quinean indeterminacy that embarrasses non-phenomenal accounts). PCT proposes that phenomenal character fixes the determinate content where third-person description leaves it open.
- **Understanding versus processing.** Genuine comprehension carries a phenomenal character of grasping that mere symbol-processing lacks—the felt difference between following a proof and verifying it mechanically.
- **The unity of the explananda.** PCT offers one account of all of these, where weak liberalism and deflationism handle them piecemeal.

The honest opposition is real and the Map does not understate it. The deflationary view (Tye, Dretske, Prinz) holds that apparent cognitive phenomenology reduces to accompanying sensory imagery, inner speech, or affect, so there is no proprietary cognitive phenomenal character to do constitutive work at all. Weak liberalism grants proprietary phenomenology but assigns content-determinacy to non-phenomenal machinery. PCT must beat both abductively rather than by demonstration. Consequently, downstream applications that lean on it—the [symbol-grounding diagnosis](/concepts/symbol-grounding-problem/), the Chinese Room verdict, and AI-understanding skepticism across the [consciousness typology](/concepts/ai-consciousness-typology/)—are *conditional* on PCT. A reader unpersuaded by the abductive case should treat those conclusions as conditional too.

## Relation to Site Perspective

PCT is a direct expression of [Tenet 1 (Dualism)](/tenets/#dualism): it makes phenomenal character explanatorily indispensable, resisting the functional reduction of meaning. If content is constituted by irreducible phenomenal character, then no purely physical or functional description—however complete—captures what a conscious thought *means*. This is the irreducibility tenet doing work at the level of intentional content, not just raw feels: the explanatory gap the Map locates between physical description and experience reappears as a gap between physical description and *meaning*. The Tenet 1 commitment PCT requires is the bare irreducibility reading, not the agency cluster's substance-leaning sub-reading; an article invoking PCT should not import persisting-subject commitments it does not need.

A secondary connection runs to [Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) through an anti-epiphenomenalist argument. If phenomenal character merely *accompanied* content—the weak-liberal or deflationary picture—then the phenomenal would be a causally idle passenger, and [epiphenomenalism](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) would gain ground. By making phenomenal character constitutive of the very content our thoughts express, PCT undercuts the epiphenomenalist's separation of experience from the contentful states that drive speech and behaviour. This connection is suggestive rather than decisive: the constitution relation is non-causal, so PCT does not by itself establish downward causation, and the Map registers the link as supporting Tenet 3's plausibility rather than proving it.

The honest framing throughout: PCT is a *contested constitutive thesis the Map holds*, advanced abductively against live and capable opponents, not an established finding. The metaphysical vocabulary it borrows (constitution, non-identity, asymmetric dependence) is neutral machinery; the Map's *adoption* of the phenomenal-constitutes-content direction is the substantive, defeasible commitment.
