The Map's agency cluster keeps reaching for one phrase—"the agent, *as a persisting substance*, exercises causal powers." That phrase presupposes a whole metaphysics it rarely names: E.J. Lowe's **four-category ontology** and the **substance causation** it grounds. This article supplies that foundation. It does two things: it lays out what the four-category ontology is (only as much as the dualist framing needs), and it shows how, on Lowe's account, *substances rather than events* do the causal work that lets a conscious subject count as a genuine cause. It closes by staging a deliberate fork—Lowe's model secures mental causation with **no gap in physics at all**, whereas the Map's [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) tenet locates a *minimal physical difference* at quantum-indeterministic junctures (developed in [Relation to Site Perspective](#relation-to-site-perspective) below). The Map uses Lowe to sharpen its own commitment, not to adopt his wholesale.

## The Ontological Square

Lowe's *The Four-Category Ontology* (2006) builds a systematic metaphysics from two cross-cutting distinctions. The first is **particular versus universal**: this individual match here (a particular) against matchhood or the kind *match* (a universal). The second is **substantial versus non-substantial**: things that exist in their own right against ways things are (properties, characteristics, qualities). Crossing the two distinctions yields four fundamental categories—the "ontological square," with Aristotle's *Categories* as its ancestor:

| | Substantial | Non-substantial |
|---|---|---|
| **Particular** | **Objects** (this match) | **Modes** (this match's specific shape) |
| **Universal** | **Kinds** (the kind *match*) | **Attributes** (flammability in general) |

- **Objects** are substantial particulars: individual persisting things—a match, an electron, a person.
- **Kinds** are substantial universals: the sortal categories objects instantiate—*match*, *electron*, *person*.
- **Attributes** are non-substantial universals: properties and relations construed as universals—flammability, negative charge.
- **Modes** (tropes) are non-substantial particulars: the property-instances that characterise a given object—*this* match's *particular* flammability.

Two formal relations tie the square together. **Instantiation** runs from universal to particular (this match instantiates the kind *match*; its flammability-mode instantiates the attribute flammability). **Characterisation** runs across the substantial/non-substantial divide (the flammability-mode characterises this match; the attribute flammability characterises the kind *match*). Lowe argues laws of nature are relations between **kinds and attributes**, and that **dispositions and powers** fall out of the grid rather than being bolted on—the metaphysical structure is, in his subtitle's phrase, "a foundation for natural science."

What matters for the Map is narrow. The four-category grid makes room for **objects with irreducible causal powers**—substances that are the source of change, not mere sites where events happen. That is the payload; the generic trope-theory machinery is left to the metaphysics literature.

## Substance Causation: Substances, Not Events, Do the Work

On the dominant contemporary view, the causal relata are **events**: one event causes another according to laws. Lowe inverts this. In *Personal Agency* (2008) he defends **substance causation** as the primary form—"strictly speaking there is no such thing as event causation," only substances (animate and inanimate alike) exercising their powers. A match does not undergo an igniting-event that causes a burning-event; *the match*, exercising its power to ignite, meets *the wood*, exercising its liability to burn. Causation is the manifestation of a **power** meeting a reciprocal **liability**.

Lowe argues the reduction runs one way only. "A caused B" can be analysed as "some substance exercised powers that brought about B," but the reverse analysis fails: unpacking "substance S exercised power P" already presupposes S itself as an ungrounded relatum. If he is right, substance causation is the primitive and event-causation is at best a *façon de parler*.

This reframes agency. **Agent causation is not a special, sui generis kind of causation**—it is simply substance causation with a rational agent as the substance. What makes human agency distinctive is not a new causal relation but the *rational* character of the powers exercised. Chief among these is **volition**: on Lowe's account a volition is a basic mental action, a "spontaneous" exercise of the will, and crucially a **two-way power**—one an agent can exercise or refrain from exercising in light of reason. Free agents are, in his phrase, "unmoved movers, or initiators of new causal chains." "Uncaused" here means uncaused *by prior events*; a volition still has an *agent*-cause, namely the substance whose power it is.

The powers-and-liabilities restatement in Lowe's 2013 chapter "Substance Causation, Powers, and Human Agency" makes the mechanism-language explicit and compact: the will is "a 'spontaneous' power" exercisable rationally, of a piece with the powers of any other substance. The Map's separate [causal powers](/concepts/causal-powers/) article covers powers-realism generically (Bird, Mumford, dispositions); Lowe's distinctive contribution is making substances—not properties or events—the *bearers and exercisers* of those powers, which is what an agent-causal position needs.

## The Fact-Causation Model: Mental Causation Without a Physics Gap

Lowe's most consequential move for interactionists is his reply to the **exclusion problem**. Kim's argument runs: physical causal closure says every physical event has a sufficient physical cause; if a distinct mental cause also produced that same physical effect, we would have either systematic overdetermination or an excluded mental cause. The dualist appears trapped.

Lowe denies the argument's shared premise—that the mental and physical causes target *the same effect under the same causal relation*. His model, set out in "Non-Cartesian Substance Dualism and the Problem of Mental Causation" (*Erkenntnis*, 2006) and *Personal Agency*, distinguishes **fact-causation** from **event-causation**:

- A decision or volition is causally responsible for **the fact that a movement of a certain *kind* occurs**.
- The **specific** neural-muscular event—the particular trajectory of this arm—is caused by neural events.

The two do not compete for one effect. The mental cause **structures** what kind of movement happens; the physical chain supplies the specific movement. Because the relata differ (facts versus events) and the roles differ (kind-determination versus specific-production), the exclusion premise—"one effect, two sufficient causes"—is never triggered. As critics such as Max Kistler reconstruct it, the mental on this model **adds no energy to the brain and redistributes none**; it makes no difference to the physical event-chain's energetics. That is precisely how Lowe purchases compatibility with "what is known about the central nervous system": his dualism requires **no rewiring of neuroscience and no gap in the physical chain**.

Lowe pairs this with **Non-Cartesian Substance Dualism (NCSD)**: the self is a distinct substance from the organic body—not identical to it or to any part—yet is *necessarily embodied*, bearing both mental and physical properties. He supports the non-identity with an **entanglement argument**: the neural causes of several parallel bodily movements can be causally entangled with one another, while their mental causes are not, so the mental causes cannot be identical to the neural ones.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The four-category ontology is a **metaphysical framework the Map leans on**, not empirical evidence for dualism. It earns its place because it grounds a phrase the agency cluster already relies on—the agent *qua* substance exercising causal powers—and because it makes agent causation continuous with ordinary causation rather than metaphysically exceptional. This directly serves **[Tenet 3 (Bidirectional Interaction)](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: if all causation is substances exercising powers, a conscious subject causing physical events is the same *type* of causation as anything else, so [interactionism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) loses the charge that it *couldn't* be causation at all. It also gives a substance reading of **[Tenet 1 (Dualism)](/tenets/#dualism)**.

Two framework-boundary qualifications keep this honest. First, NCSD is **a congenial option, not the Map's mandatory commitment**. The Dualism tenet is officially neutral between substance and property dualism (see [substance vs property dualism](/concepts/substance-property-dualism/) and [where the substance commitment enters](/concepts/where-the-substance-commitment-enters/)); the Map's substance-leaning is downstream of the agent-causal commitment, not of the tenet itself. Lowe supplies the strongest *substance* version of that lean, without making it obligatory. Second, the framework is scaffolding for locating consciousness, not an argument that consciousness exists—it says *how a subject could be a genuine cause*, given that there is one.

**The deliberate fork.** Here the Map parts company with Lowe, and the departure is instructive. Lowe buys causal-closure compatibility by making the mental cause **structuring rather than energetic**: on fact-causation, consciousness determines *which kind* of movement occurs while making **no difference to the physical event-chain**—explicitly no gap, no energy, no deviation. The Map's [Tenet 2 (Minimal Quantum Interaction)](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) takes the other branch. It accepts a **minimal physical difference**, located precisely where physics is *already indeterministic*: at quantum junctures, physics fixes probabilities but not outcomes, so there is no sufficient physical cause of the specific outcome, and consciousness biases *which* outcome becomes actual within the Born distribution. The two routes share a goal—avoiding a crude interventionist "gap" that would violate the empirical record—but they answer the crux question oppositely: does the mental make *any* difference to the physical event-chain? Lowe says no (the difference is at the level of facts about kinds); the Map says yes, but only a minimal, statistically undetectable one at indeterministic points.

Neither route is obviously forced. Lowe's has the advantage of touching no physics at all; its cost is that critics (Kistler, and Gibb in her refinements) press whether "causing the fact that a kind of movement occurs" is doing genuine causal work or relabeling. The Map's route makes a concrete physical claim about outcome-selection and thereby a genuine testability commitment—one it pays knowingly (see the [bias-without-deviation](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) discussion). The Map takes its branch because it wants consciousness to be a token-level *difference-maker* to the physical world, not only a determiner of facts about kinds—and because a purely structuring cause can look uncomfortably close to the epiphenomenalism the Map rejects. Staging the choice this way is the point: Lowe's fact-causation is the sharpest available foil for saying exactly *what* the Map's minimal-interaction commitment adds, and *why* it pays the empirical cost that Lowe's route avoids.

A residual worry the Map inherits rather than settles: Lowe's "spontaneous" volition faces Libet-style timing objections (neural readiness potentials appearing to precede conscious will). The Map's treatment of that data lives in its [agency and will positions](/positions/agency-and-will/) and [luck objection](/concepts/luck-objection/) material; the four-category framework tells us *what a volition is metaphysically*, not whether the timing evidence undercuts its spontaneity.
