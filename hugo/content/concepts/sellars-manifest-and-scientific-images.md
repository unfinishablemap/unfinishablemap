Wilfrid Sellars (1912–1989) divided our total conception of "man-in-the-world" into two rival frameworks and made their reconciliation the central task of philosophy. The **manifest image** is the refined framework in which man first came to be aware of himself *as* man — the framework of persons, perceptible objects, and irreducible normativity. The **scientific image** is the framework of postulated theoretical entities — the austere, particulate ontology of physics. Sellars called the successful fusion of the two a *stereoscopic* vision. This article supplies the definition of that distinction, since the Map's corpus leans on it across many articles (notably [one-structure-three-vocabularies](/topics/one-structure-three-vocabularies/)) without stating it in one place. It is expository infrastructure, not advocacy: Sellars is a load-bearing meta-framework the Map assumes, and — as the [Relation to Site Perspective](#relation-to-site-perspective) below sets out — he was a scientific realist whose endpoint the Map's tenets resist even where his diagnoses are congenial.

## The Manifest Image

The manifest image is easy to caricature and Sellars is emphatic against the caricature. It is *not* naive folk belief and *not* pre-scientific superstition. It is, on his account, a critical and methodical framework in its own right — a correlational and categorial refinement of a prehistoric "original image," disciplined by the same correlational-inductive methods that make any framework empirical. What distinguishes it from the scientific image is not rigour but *ontology*: the manifest image restricts itself to observable objects and their perceptible properties, and it does not postulate imperceptible theoretical entities to explain them.

Its central and irreducible category is the **person**. In the manifest image, persons are not complex objects among others but the locus of experience, of reasons, and of the normative relations — commitments, entitlements, community intentions — that constitute a shared conceptual life. Sellars treats the person, and the normativity that comes with it, as the feature of the manifest image most resistant to absorption by anything else.

## The Scientific Image

The scientific image is the framework of *postulated* entities: particles, fields, quantum transitions — imperceptible posits introduced to explain what the manifest image can only correlate. Sellars insists it is not a mere abstraction from or shorthand for the manifest image. It is a methodologically autonomous construction that presents itself as a *rival* — a complete account claiming to describe what there really is, sometimes in ways that contradict the manifest image's own picture (the smoothly coloured object versus the mostly-empty lattice of colourless particles).

The two frameworks therefore clash, and the clash is not superficial. Each purports to be a complete image of man-in-the-world. Sellars' problem is that both appear "equally public, equally non-arbitrary," so the philosopher cannot simply discard one.

### Scientia Mensura

Sellars' own resolution of the clash tilts toward the scientific image on the question of *being*. His scientific-realist thesis — traditionally labelled *scientia mensura* after the phrase in which he glosses it — holds that in the dimension of *describing and explaining* the world, science is the measure of what exists and what does not. (The Map paraphrases the thesis rather than quoting it: the famous formulation is widely attributed to §41 of *Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind* but was not verbatim-confirmed against the primary text for this article.)

The qualifier is essential and Sellars insists on it: the thesis is restricted to description and explanation. It does *not* license eliminating the normative or prescriptive dimension. Science measures being; it does not replace the space of reasons in which we justify, commit, and hold one another to account. This is why Sellars is not an eliminativist about persons even though he is a scientific realist about what there is. The manifest person is retained — but retained as something to be *naturalized* and located, not as a second kind of thing.

## Stereoscopic Fusion

The synoptic goal is to see the two images "fall together in one stereoscopic view" — as the two slightly different images from two eyes fuse into a single scene with depth. In Sellars' intended fusion, the scientific image supplies the descriptive-explanatory account of what is real, while the language of intentions and norms remains indispensable for characterizing human agency. Whether the person survives this fusion as genuinely *irreducible* or as *naturalized-but-retained* is exactly the fault line dividing later "right-wing" and "left-wing" Sellarsians — a division that turns on how seriously the space of reasons resists relocation into the scientific image. The Map's stake in that fault line is developed [below](#relation-to-site-perspective).

## The Grain Problem and Sensa

Sellars' most Map-relevant contribution is an obstacle he found in his *own* project — a proto-hard-problem now known as the **grain problem**. Consider the pink of a pink ice cube. As presented in the manifest image, that pink is *ultimately homogeneous*: a continuous expanse every region of which, however small, is pink through and through. But the scientific image describes the cube as a discontinuous, particulate structure — a lattice of colourless particles with gaps between them. A smooth, gap-free quality cannot, Sellars argued, simply be *identified* with a grainy, gappy microphysical structure. The character of sensory consciousness resists the grain of the scientific image. This is the phenomenal-quality obstacle the Map develops under [grain-mismatch](/concepts/grain-mismatch/).

Sellars did not treat this as a refutation of naturalism but as a demand on it. His late metaphysics turned to *sensa* — sensory items he classed as "physical-1" (real, space-time bound, causally efficacious) but not "physical-2" (not reducible to the mechanistic laws adequate to non-living matter). The endpoint is a pure-process ontology in which sensory qualities are primitive constituents of an *expanded* physics. The scientific image is stretched to accommodate homogeneous quality rather than a second substance being admitted alongside it.

## The Myth of the Given

The epistemological backdrop, from the earlier *Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind* (1956), is Sellars' attack on the **Myth of the Given**: the foundationalist assumption that some cognitive states carry positive epistemic status independently of their inferential relations to other states. Sellars argued no item can be both epistemically independent and epistemically efficacious. To know is to place an episode in the *space of reasons* — the normative space of justifying and being able to justify what one says. Knowledge is thus irreducibly a matter of standing in normative relations, which is precisely why the person and the space of reasons press back against any framework, like the scientific image, that trades only in causes. The Map treats this normative dimension under [rational-normativity](/concepts/rational-normativity/).

## Relation to Site Perspective

Sellars is best presented to the Map's readers as a **meta-framework the corpus assumes, not as an ally it has recruited.** Two of his moves are congenial to the Map's dualism (Tenet 1) and its wariness of premature simplicity (Tenet 5); one is in direct tension with both.

**Congenial.** The manifest image's *irreducible conscious person* is close kin to what the Map's dualism treats as ontologically basic: the subject of experience and reasons that does not dissolve into a description of parts. And the **grain problem** is a pre-Chalmers line of pressure on physicalism the Map can enlist. That homogeneous sensory quality resists particulate structure is an independent diagnosis, reached from within a scientific-realist project rather than from a dualist one — which makes it stronger evidence, not weaker, for the mismatch the Map already documents at [grain-mismatch](/concepts/grain-mismatch/).

**Resisted.** Sellars' *endpoint* is the opposite of the Map's. Under *scientia mensura* he grants the manifest person its due only in order to *naturalize* it: science remains the measure of being, and the grain problem is answered by *sensa* inside an expanded physics rather than by admitting anything non-physical. The Map takes the irreducibility Sellars locates to be *ontological*, not a bookkeeping problem for a wider naturalism. So the Map enlists the *diagnosis* (the manifest person, the grain problem) while declining the *cure* (naturalization under science-as-measure). Presenting Sellars as a dualist ally would misattribute a commitment he explicitly refused; presenting him as merely a physicalist would miss why his framework is worth assuming at all. The honest reading is framework-relative: a scientific realist whose best problems outrun his own solution.

This also clarifies the Map's relation to eliminativism. Where [eliminative-materialism](/topics/eliminative-materialism/) would let the scientific image simply supersede the manifest, Sellars — and the Map more firmly — hold that the person and the space of reasons cannot be dropped. The Map differs from Sellars only on whether keeping them requires expanding physics or exceeding it. The parallel worry that rational causation cannot be vindicated inside a purely physical order is pursued under [argument-from-reason](/topics/argument-from-reason/).
