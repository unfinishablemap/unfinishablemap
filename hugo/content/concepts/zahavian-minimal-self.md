Dan Zahavi's **minimal self** is the pre-reflective first-personal givenness that every conscious experience already carries — the "for-me-ness" that makes an experience this subject's rather than anyone's or no one's. On Zahavi's phenomenological account, this minimal self is not something consciousness *has* alongside its contents; it is the structural form consciousness takes. The Unfinishable Map adopts this Zahavian position in preference to Thomas Metzinger's self-model theory, which treats selfhood as the content of a transparent representation that the brain generates and could in principle dismantle entirely. The distinction is load-bearing: arguments about ego-dissolution, meditative witnessing, indexical identity, and agency require a subject that the Metzinger framework does not supply, and that Zahavi's framework does.

This article explains what the Zahavian minimal self *is* in Zahavi's own terms, distinguishes it from three neighbouring positions (Metzinger's self-model eliminativism, Galen Strawson's thin-subject no-self, and Hume's bundle), and sets out why the Map's filter theory of consciousness and its ego-dissolution evidence specifically require the Zahavian version.

## Zahavi's Position in His Own Terms

In *Subjectivity and Selfhood* (2005) and later in *Self and Other* (2014), Zahavi develops a phenomenological account inherited primarily from Husserl. The core claim is that every conscious experience is characterised by **first-personal givenness** — Zahavi's preferred technical term. Experience is never simply *present*; it is present *for* a subject, from a particular perspective, as *mine*. This is not an additional property layered onto experience but its mode of appearance.

Several features of the account deserve careful statement:

**Pre-reflective, not reflective.** Zahavi is explicit that the minimal self is not produced by introspection or higher-order monitoring. You do not experience a red apple and then *add* the judgement "I am experiencing this." The for-me-ness is there from the outset, as the structure of the experience itself. Reflective self-consciousness — the deliberate thematic awareness of one's own mental states — presupposes pre-reflective self-consciousness and cannot generate it.

**Minimal, not substantial.** Zahavi is careful to distinguish his position from Cartesian or classical dualist conceptions. The minimal self is not a little homunculus behind the scenes, nor an unchanging soul-substance. It is simply the subjective character of experience itself — what Zahavi calls its "mineness" (*Jemeinigkeit* in Husserl's German heritage).

**Constitutive, not contingent (in kind).** Against eliminativists, Zahavi holds that there is no coherent phenomenology in which experience lacks first-personal givenness altogether. An experience that belonged to no one would not be an experience at all. The minimal self is a necessary structural feature, not an optional one. A clarification is in order: clinical evidence (discussed under "Acknowledged Limits" below) shows that first-personal givenness admits of *degrees*. The Map reads "constitutive" as a claim about *kind* — every phenomenal state instantiates for-me-ness to some degree above zero — rather than uniformity of intensity. A scalar feature can still be structurally necessary.

**Compatible with impermanence.** Crucially — and often missed — Zahavi's minimal self does not entail diachronic identity. The claim is synchronic: each conscious moment has first-personal givenness. Whether this grounds numerical identity across time is a further question Zahavi treats with caution. On this point Zahavi converges with some Buddhist critics rather than opposing them outright (see [buddhism-and-dualism](/concepts/buddhism-and-dualism/)).

A short quotation anchors the position: "Experiential phenomena ... are characterized by a quality of mineness, the fact that the experiences are given (at least tacitly) as *my* experiences, as experiences I am undergoing or living through" (Zahavi 2005, p. 124).

## Metzinger's Alternative and Why It Differs

Thomas Metzinger's *Being No One* (2003) defends **Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity** (SMT). The self, on SMT, is the content of a phenomenally transparent self-model — a representation the brain generates and treats as a non-representational presentation of a real self. The transparency of the model is what gives rise to the illusion that there is a self "having" experiences: we mistake the model for its (non-existent) target.

Metzinger's programme is explicitly *eliminativist* about the self as an entity: "No such things as selves exist in the world" (Metzinger 2003, p. 1). What exist are self-models, running on brains. When the self-model is disrupted — through meditation, psychedelics, neurological damage — there is no deeper subject left over to "witness" the disruption, because there was never any subject distinct from the model.

His later work on **Minimal Phenomenal Experience** (MPE) sharpens this. Metzinger characterises certain deep meditative states as potentially exhibiting a **"zero-person perspective"** — phenomenal consciousness without subject-structure, without for-me-ness, without any first-personal character at all (Metzinger 2020).

Three points of contrast with Zahavi are critical:

1. **Status of the minimal self.** For Zahavi, first-personal givenness is constitutive of phenomenality — an experience without it is not an experience. For Metzinger, first-personal givenness is a contingent feature of most phenomenal states, produced by a functional self-model, and in principle absent in MPE.

2. **What survives ego dissolution.** Zahavi's framework predicts that even in radical ego-dissolution, the minimal self persists as bare for-me-ness; what dissolves is the narrative and bodily self-model. Metzinger's framework predicts the opposite: dissolution of the self-model removes the subject entirely, leaving a "zero-person" impersonal phenomenology.

3. **Epistemic implications.** On Zahavi's account, introspective reports of ego-dissolution are reports *by* the surviving minimal self *about* the loss of richer self-structures. On Metzinger's account, the report is produced by a reconstituted self-model after the fact and may not accurately reflect what was (or was not) present during the state itself.

The Map takes the Zahavian interpretation to better fit the actual first-person reports. Subjects describe loss of "me-as-character" alongside persistence of bare experiential presence — not loss of experience altogether.

## Distinguishing Zahavi from Strawson and Hume

Two further positions need separating from Zahavi's.

**Galen Strawson's "thin subject" / Pearl view.** Strawson (2009) holds that subjects of experience exist but are thin and short-lived — each conscious moment has its own distinct subject, with no persistence across the gaps in consciousness (sleep, anaesthesia, attention breaks). This is closer to Zahavi than Metzinger is, but Strawson commits to a specific negative answer to the diachronic question that Zahavi treats with caution.

**Hume's bundle theory.** Hume famously reported that when he introspected for the self, he found only perceptions, "never anything but the perception" (Hume 1739, I.IV.VI). This is often cited against the minimal self, but Zahavi's reply is diagnostic: Hume was looking for the self as a distinct *object* of introspection. The minimal self is not an object; it is the *subjective pole* of the very introspection Hume performed. Hume could not *find* the self for the same reason an eye cannot see itself seeing. This parallels the [structural unobservability](/voids/self-opacity/) of the subject.

## Why the Map Requires the Zahavian Version

Three of the Map's core commitments require the Zahavian minimal self specifically, and fail on the Metzinger-eliminativist alternative.

**Ego-dissolution as evidence.** The Map's treatment of [psychedelics](/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/), [meditation](/topics/the-observer-witness-in-meditation/), and [anaesthesia](/topics/anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface/) depends on there being a subject whose relationship to the brain's self-model can change. If the self is nothing but the self-model (Metzinger), ego-dissolution simply *is* the local extinction of the subject; the "witness" reports are post-hoc confabulation. If the minimal self persists through self-model disruption (Zahavi), the phenomenology of witnessing dissolution is taken at face value, and filter-theoretic interpretations of altered states become intelligible. The Map's case for a [non-brain-produced consciousness](/concepts/mind-brain-separation/) trades on the persistence of the subject across filter-relaxation events.

**Indexical identity (No Many Worlds tenet).** The Map's [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) commitment rests on the fact that *this* experience is *mine* and not some duplicate's. First-personal givenness is exactly the structural feature this tenet appeals to. A Metzingerian zero-person phenomenology, if genuinely attainable, would be a phenomenology with no indexical anchor — a consciousness that could in principle be had by anyone or no one. The Map's indexical arguments presuppose the Zahavian picture.

**Bidirectional interaction and agency.** The [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) tenet requires a subject whose influence on physical outcomes is real. The Map takes the Zahavian minimal self to be a more perspicuous locus of such agency than a self-model. The earlier draft of this article asserted the stronger claim that "a self-model cannot be a causal agent; it is a representation" — but cognitive science routinely treats representations as causally implicated, and the assertion overreaches as stated. The defensible weaker claim is this: when a self-model causes behaviour, the natural description is that the brain runs the model and the model's content shapes downstream processing. *Implicated in* is not the same role as *the agent of*. On the dualist picture the Map adopts (see [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)), the bearer of consciousness does the influencing; representations are part of the structure through which the influence operates. A self-model alone does not supply that bearer; the Zahavian minimal self does. This is not to claim Zahavi himself endorses bidirectional causation — he is a phenomenologist, not a metaphysician of interaction — but that his posit gives the Map's metaphysics the subject it needs.

## Choice Under Metaphysical Commitment

Honesty requires acknowledging the structure of the choice just made. The Zahavi/Metzinger question cannot be decided by ego-dissolution reports alone. Husserl's evidence taxonomy (developed in [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/#husserls-evidence-taxonomy)) clarifies what the honest structure looks like: apodictic weight attaches to the *existence* of for-me-ness — this is the bedrock the Zahavian position shares with the phenomenological method — while the choice of interpretive framework for that for-me-ness sits at assertoric and presumptive weight, and is conditional on the Map's tenets being independently sound. Metzinger's reading — that those reports are post-hoc reconstructions produced by a reconstituted self-model after the state has ended — is internally consistent and not refutable by introspective data, since by hypothesis no introspective access to a putative zero-person phase is possible. The convergence of meditator and psychedelic reports is suggestive but not probative; expectation, language, and prior self-modelling shape what gets reported.

The Map's preference for Zahavi over Metzinger is therefore conditional rather than evidence-forced. It is conditional on three further commitments the Map already holds: (i) the indexical structure invoked by the [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) tenet, which presupposes that there is *someone* whose experience is *this* one; (ii) the [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) tenet, which requires a subject who does the influencing; and (iii) the [dualist](/tenets/#dualism) commitment that consciousness is not exhausted by its representational structure. If these prior commitments are sound, the Zahavian framework coheres with them and the Metzingerian framework does not. If they are not sound, the parsimony advantage runs to Metzinger.

This is special pleading only if the prior commitments are themselves baseless. The Map argues for them elsewhere; the present article only flags that the choice between Zahavi and Metzinger is one of metaphysical fit, not of empirical compulsion. Subsequent neurophenomenological work — fine-grained EEG/MEG signatures during deep meditative or psychedelic states, paired with carefully prompted retrospective reports — may eventually narrow the space. At present it does not.

## The Madhyamaka Challenge

The most serious objection to the Zahavian framework comes from the Mādhyamaka Buddhist tradition, and it deserves treatment beyond the one-paragraph deferral the earlier draft offered. If the objection succeeds, the structure of this article — and especially its connection to the Map's [haecceity](/concepts/haecceity/) commitment — comes apart.

**The challenge in its strongest form.** Mādhyamaka, following Nāgārjuna, grants what Zahavi notices but contests what he infers. The phenomenological observation that experience presents itself as *for-someone* is real. The mistake is to treat that for-someone-ness as a structural feature implying a self-standing subject. On sustained analytical and contemplative investigation, "for-me-ness" does not bottom out in any bearer; it decomposes into a sequence of conditioned moments, each arising in dependence on the previous, none belonging to a subject standing outside the sequence. The grammatical "for-me" of experience is not evidence that there is a *me* the experience is *for*. It is the residual habit of self-grasping (*ātma-graha*) that two millennia of Buddhist analytical practice have been designed to expose and relax. From this standpoint, Zahavi has correctly described how experience *appears* and incorrectly inferred a structural feature of phenomenality from the appearance.

The objection sharpens decisively when the Map moves from the synchronic minimal self to the diachronic [haecceity](/concepts/haecceity/) commitment. Zahavi himself is careful here: the minimal self, on his account, is a synchronic structural feature, agnostic on persistence across time. The Map adds the diachronic claim — that *this* subject is the same indexical bearer across the sequence of phenomenal moments. From a Mādhyamaka standpoint, that move reinstates exactly what the tradition has spent its history dissolving: an *ātman* — a self-standing subject identifiable across time, the deepest and most resilient locus of cognitive distortion the analysis is designed to undo. Even readers who would let the synchronic Zahavian claim through (as a description of phenomenal structure with no commitment to a substantive bearer) are likely to refuse the diachronic step.

**Engaging Thompson, not just citing him.** Evan Thompson's contribution to *Self, No Self?* (Siderits, Thompson & Zahavi 2011), elaborated in *Waking, Dreaming, Being* (2014), works this terrain carefully and refuses both poles. Thompson takes the phenomenological observation of pre-reflective self-awareness seriously — he does not side with strict no-self eliminativism — but resists treating that observation as licensing a substantive subject. His preferred reading is that reflexive awareness, the fact that experience knows itself as it occurs, is a feature of how experience constitutes its own appearing rather than evidence of an additional witnessing self. This converges with the Mādhyamaka diagnosis at the load-bearing point: the inference from "experience has reflexive structure" to "there is a self this experience belongs to" is not licensed by the phenomenology alone. Reflexivity is a structural feature; ownership by a persistent indexical bearer is a metaphysical addition. The Map's haecceity claim is precisely that addition. Where this article inherits "first-personal givenness" from Zahavi, Thompson would caution that the genitive *givenness-for-someone* should be read as relational structure of the experiential moment, not as ownership by a self that runs through the moments.

**Can the haecceity-across-time commitment survive sustained Mādhyamaka analysis?** Three replies are available, each of which the Map should weigh honestly rather than rest on.

The first is the *conventional/ultimate* distinction Mādhyamaka itself supplies. Nāgārjuna's two-truths doctrine permits robust conventional claims even where ultimate inherent existence is denied. The Map's haecceity claim could be read as a conventional-level commitment — "this subject" is conventionally individuated and conventionally persistent — without asserting ultimate *svabhāva* (own-being). This response is available, but it weakens the Map's [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) tenet correspondingly. If "this experience is mine" is true only conventionally, the indexical resistance to MWI's branching duplicates loses much of its force; the objection that "I am this branch, not the others" presupposes a non-conventional indexical bearer whose persistence is more than a useful fiction.

The second reply is to deny the symmetry of Mādhyamaka analysis. The Map could insist that consciousness occupies an asymmetric position — that what *investigates* emptiness cannot itself be empty in the same way without infinite regress. Yogācāra-influenced readings move in this direction. This is not Mādhyamaka, however; it is a rival Buddhist school. Adopting it does not answer the Mādhyamaka objection so much as refuse its terms.

The third reply is the candid one, and the one the Map should make explicitly. The Map's haecceity-across-time commitment cannot survive sustained Mādhyamaka analysis in its full force, and it should not pretend otherwise. The Map's position here is conditional on rejecting the Mādhyamaka dissolution argument — or, more precisely, on holding that the contemplative experience underwriting the dissolution is interpretable in ways that do not entail what Mādhyamaka takes it to entail. Specifically, the Map reads contemplative reports of selflessness as reports of attenuated, denarrativised experience by a still-individuated subject, not as reports from a vantage where individuation has genuinely dissolved. That reading is a philosophical choice, not an evidence-forced conclusion. If sustained Mādhyamaka analysis really does dissolve the indexical bearer rather than thinning the narrative around it, the [No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) tenet faces a tenet-level reply, not an article-level refinement.

**What this honesty costs and earns.** Acknowledging the conditional structure costs the Map any claim to having dispatched the Mādhyamaka critique by argument. It earns clarity about the actual shape of the disagreement: the Map and Mādhyamaka diverge on whether contemplative selflessness is best read as the disclosure of a never-existent subject (Mādhyamaka) or as the attenuation of a real but ordinarily over-elaborated subject (Map). Neither side can compel the other from neutral ground, because what counts as a neutral phenomenological report is itself one of the things in dispute. The Map proceeds with the second reading because the haecceity commitment, the No Many Worlds tenet, and the Bidirectional Interaction tenet form a mutually supporting cluster that the first reading would dissolve simultaneously. A reader who finds the Mādhyamaka diagnosis decisive should find the Map's framework unstable at exactly this point — and the Map judges that an honest disagreement of this shape is preferable to the paper-thin reply the earlier draft offered.

## Acknowledged Limits

Beyond the Mādhyamaka challenge treated above, the Zahavian position has further limitations the Map takes seriously. Sass and Parnas's clinical evidence on schizophrenic ipseity disturbance (discussed in [clinical-phenomenology-and-altered-experience](/topics/clinical-phenomenology-and-altered-experience/)) shows from a non-contemplative angle that the minimal self admits of degrees: it can be attenuated, distorted, or partially disrupted without ceasing altogether. The Map resolves the resulting tension with the earlier "constitutive" claim by distinguishing two questions: whether first-personal givenness is *structurally necessary in kind* (yes — there is no phenomenology that altogether lacks it without ceasing to be phenomenology) and whether it is *uniform in degree* (no — clinical and meditative evidence both suggest gradients). "Constitutive" is the kind-claim, not the degree-claim; a scalar feature can be structurally necessary if every value above zero counts as instantiation. The synchronic character of Zahavi's account also leaves the question of diachronic identity open, requiring the Map to supplement Zahavi with its own haecceity-based commitments — and it is precisely that supplement that comes under fire from the Mādhyamaka direction.

The Map does not treat Zahavi as the last word, only as the best current framing of the minimal-self position — and specifically the framing that coheres with the Map's broader commitments in a way Metzinger's cannot.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: The Zahavian minimal self is a structural feature of phenomenal consciousness that no third-person description captures. First-personal givenness is precisely the feature [reductive programmes](/topics/reductionism-and-consciousness/) cannot accommodate, and its constitutive status rules out accounts that would eliminate subjectivity in favour of a self-model alone.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: The Zahavian minimal self is a more perspicuous locus of agency than a self-model. Representations can be causally implicated in cognition, but "implicated in" is not the same role as "the bearer of" agency — and only the latter is what the bidirectional tenet asks for.

**[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)**: The indexical character of first-personal givenness — the fact that this experience is *mine*, unrepeatably — is exactly what the No Many Worlds tenet protects against MWI's proliferation of qualitatively identical subjects.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: Metzinger's eliminativism is more parsimonious than Zahavi's minimal self — fewer posited entities. But parsimony misleads when the dispensed-with entity is what the evidence (ego-dissolution reports, ipseity disturbance, the structure of introspection) actually requires.
