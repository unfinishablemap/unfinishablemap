Ask whether "conscious being" is a *natural kind* and you inherit a well-worn philosophical machine. Natural kinds are the categories that carve nature at its joints — the ones that support prediction and explanation because the world, not our interests, groups their members together. Water, electron, and tiger are candidates; *bachelor* and *things heavier than my desk* are not. The question of whether consciousness belongs on the first list has been asked before, but almost always in a specific key: *can* consciousness be studied scientifically as a kind (Shea 2012; Bayne & Shea 2020)? This article asks a different, sharper question. It tests "conscious being" against **Richard Boyd's homeostatic property cluster (HPC)** theory — the dominant essence-free account of special-science kinds — and finds that the fit forces a decision the Map cares about: whether the conscious/non-conscious boundary is *constitutively vague*, as every HPC kind's boundary is, or *sharp* in a way no property cluster can be. That axis (the **sharpness-versus-vagueness debate**, explained below) turns out to be the Map's dualism question wearing different clothes.

The components here are established; the wiring is new. Nobody in the retrieved literature has run "is consciousness a Boyd HPC kind?" as a dualism-versus-materialism diagnostic. So this is a synthesis, not a discovery — and it carries a real intellectual burden, named explicitly in the final section: the sharpness that dualism seems to predict is a double-edged sword that threatens some versions of dualism too.

## Boyd's homeostatic property clusters

Richard Boyd introduced HPC theory in "Realism, Anti-Foundationalism and the Enthusiasm for Natural Kinds" (1991) to explain how the special sciences — biology, psychology, medicine — can have genuine natural kinds without the sharp essences that the Kripke–Putnam picture demands. On the essentialist account, kind membership is fixed by a shared microstructural essence (water *is* H₂O), and the boundary is crisp: a sample either has the essence or it does not.

Boyd's kinds work differently. An HPC kind is a **family of properties that reliably co-occur** because some underlying "homeostatic mechanism" makes their clustering stable — deviations from the cluster tend not to persist. Three features follow, and all three matter here:

1. **No shared essence.** No single property in the cluster need be individually necessary for membership. Members resemble one another the way a family does, not the way samples of a compound do.
2. **Vague boundaries.** Because membership is a matter of *how much* of the cluster is present and how tightly the mechanism holds it, kinds have fuzzy edges and admit genuine borderline cases.
3. **Kind-hood earned by inductive yield.** What makes an HPC grouping a *natural* kind is not mind-independence or a demonstrable essence but that it "supports inductive/explanatory generalization" — the cluster lets us project from some members to others.

Boyd's paradigm case is biological species, developed in "Homeostasis, Species, and Higher Taxa" (1999): a species is a homeostatically sustained clustering of morphological, genetic, and ecological properties, vague at the edges, with no essence that every member shares. This is the anti-essentialist orthodoxy for biological taxa. It sits between two poles it explicitly rejects: Kripke–Putnam essentialism (sharp, essence-based) on one side, and conventionalism — kinds carved purely to human interests — on the other.

## Cluster kinds go mental

HPC did not stay in biology. Kendler, Zachar, and Craver, in "What Kinds of Things Are Psychiatric Disorders?" (2011), applied the framework to mental disorders — and in doing so introduced a terminological nuance worth flagging. They **rename HPC the "mechanistic property cluster" (MPC)**, dropping "homeostatic" because it wrongly implied physiological equilibrium; psychiatric kinds, they argue, are held together by heterogeneous and variable causal mechanisms rather than by anything equilibrium-like. MPC, they conclude, "offers the most promising answer to the question of what kinds of things psychiatric disorders are," while cautioning that it may be too early to force psychiatric categories into the mold. Their move is the direct precedent for asking the same of "conscious being": here is the cluster-kind template applied to a mental domain, treating its members as vague, marginal-case-admitting, and mechanism-sustained.

Two refinements extend the program. Slater's "Natural Kindness" (2015) argues that cluster theory should drop its commitment to *causal homeostatic mechanisms* and keep only the **cohesiveness and stability** those mechanisms happen to provide — the "Stable Property Cluster" (SPC) account, on which properties may be held together by multiple mechanisms or merely reliably correlated. Khalidi's *Natural Categories and Human Kinds* (2013) offers a liberal-realist alternative in which kinds are nodes in causal networks and categories may crosscut, so a single thing can belong to several overlapping natural kinds at once. The takeaway: the cluster-kind research program is live and evolving, not a fixed target — which makes "conscious being" a fair candidate to test against it.

**A terminological trap to avoid.** Antonio Damasio's talk of "homeostatic feelings" — consciousness as a solution to the body's homeostatic regulation problem — uses "homeostatic" in a *biological* sense that has nothing to do with Boyd's *kind-theoretic* homeostasis. Damasio is theorizing the biological function of feeling; Boyd is theorizing what holds a natural kind together. Conflating the two would be a pun mistaken for an argument.

## Can consciousness be a natural kind at all?

The closest existing work to this article's question is Nicholas Shea's "Methodological Encounters with the Phenomenal Kind" (2012), which argues that phenomenal consciousness **can and should be studied as a natural kind**. Shea frames it through "nomological clusters" — properties clustering under natural laws — a natural-kind *methodology* closely related to HPC but not labelled as such, and one that needs no intrinsic essence, only reliable inductive support. Bayne and Shea's "Consciousness, Concepts, and Natural Kinds" (2020) develops the practical payoff: treating consciousness as a natural kind is among the most promising routes to *validating measures of consciousness* in hard cases — infants, brain-damaged patients, non-human animals, and AI — and they carefully distinguish the **phenomenal concept** of consciousness from the **natural-kind concept** that the science needs.

This is where the novelty of the present article has to be scoped honestly. Shea and Bayne–Shea have already argued that consciousness can be a natural kind. What they have *not* done is run that thesis through Boyd's specific HPC apparatus and read the result as a fork between materialism and dualism. Their verdict is materialist-friendly and essence-free, and it does not engage the dualist sharp-boundary alternative at all. The claim to novelty here is narrow and specific: the wiring of **HPC's constitutive boundary-vagueness** to the **sharpness intuition** to a **dualist ground for sharpness**. Every component is borrowed; only the circuit is new.

## The axis: is "phenomenally conscious" vague or sharp?

The HPC question collapses onto a debate that philosophy of mind has been running independently. Because every HPC kind has vague boundaries by construction, classifying "conscious being" as an HPC kind *presupposes* that consciousness admits borderline cases. Whether it does is exactly what the sharpness-versus-vagueness debate contests.

On the **sharpness** side, Michael Antony's "Vagueness and the Metaphysics of Consciousness" (2006) names the "intuition of sharpness": for many people, CONSCIOUS STATE and CONSCIOUS CREATURE seem *determinately* satisfied or not — there is no such thing as a borderline case of there being something it is like to be a creature. The light is on or off. Jonathan Simon's "Vagueness and Zombies" (2017) supplies the strongest recent argument for that verdict: every genuine borderline case admits a *positive characterization*, but the conceivability of zombies shows there can be no positive characterization of a borderline case of "phenomenally conscious," so the phenomenal sorites fails and there are no borderline cases. (The Map's treatment of this argument is developed in [phenomenal-sorites-problem](/concepts/phenomenal-sorites-problem/).)

On the **vagueness** side, David Papineau holds that phenomenal concepts are vague — insufficiently specified to pick out exactly one property — and Eric Schwitzgebel's "Borderline Consciousness" (2023) defends genuine gray-zone cases in which it is neither determinately true nor determinately false that experience is present, on the model of baldness or borderline colors. This is the natural home of an HPC or gradualist reading.

One distinction has to be preserved or both camps get misrepresented. Birch, Schnell, and Clayton's "Dimensions of Animal Consciousness" (2020) replaces a single more-or-less-conscious scale with a **multidimensional profile** — perceptual richness, evaluative richness, integration at a time and across time, self-consciousness — each varying continuously. But gradation in the *richness or contents* of experience is a separate matter from vagueness about *whether there is any experience at all*. One can hold, without contradiction, that the contents of consciousness come in continuous degrees (as [degrees-of-consciousness](/concepts/degrees-of-consciousness/) explores) while the on/off fact of any experience whatsoever is perfectly sharp. Sharpness is defended about the on/off fact; gradualism is usually about the contents. Conflating them makes the debate look already settled when it is not.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Map holds, as its first tenet, a **dualism** on which consciousness is not reducible to physical processes. Run that through the analysis above and the prediction is clean: if consciousness is a genuine non-physical fact rather than a summary of clustered physical capacities, then "conscious being" is not an HPC kind. It has an essence-like, sharp joint — for any creature at any moment there is a determinate fact about whether the non-physical difference is being made. This coheres with the sharpness intuition that Antony and Simon defend, and it sits against the pragmatic, inductive-yield parsimony of the HPC reading, which is exactly the kind of simplicity the Map's fifth tenet (**Occam's Razor has limits**) warns against over-trusting: a cluster grouping can be maximally useful for prediction and still fail to track a real joint in nature.

Two guards keep this from overreaching.

**The claim is coherentist, not a proof.** HPC's poor fit with consciousness would be *consistent with* dualism; it would not *establish* it. A materialist can consistently maintain that "conscious being" is an HPC kind with vague edges and marginal cases — that is a respectable live position, not a straw man. The Map's line is conditional: *if* the Map's dualism, *then* a sharp joint follows. The sharp boundary is entailed by the tenets, not offered as independent evidence for them.

**The double edge must be answered.** Antony's sharpness argument is not a gift to dualism — it cuts dualism too. His conclusion is that *if* the concept of consciousness is sharp and correct, then identity theory, functionalism, *and* dualism are all false, because each makes consciousness supervene on a *vague* physical or functional base, and nothing sharp can supervene on something vague. Any theory that grounds the conscious/non-conscious fact in a smeared, gradually-varying substrate inherits that substrate's vagueness and cannot deliver a sharp joint. This is the article's real burden, and it has a specific answer. Antony's argument targets theories on which consciousness supervenes on *complex physical or functional properties* — property dualism and functionalism included. The Map's dualism is not of that shape. On the Map's picture, consciousness makes a **discrete non-physical difference** at a specific locus — the minimal quantum interaction of the second tenet, developed in [interface-threshold](/concepts/interface-threshold/). If the fact of consciousness is fixed at that discrete interaction interface rather than by a graded supervenience base, there is a determinate fact about whether the interaction is occurring, and the sharp joint is grounded in something that is not itself vague. Interactionist dualism with a discrete interface escapes the supervenience-on-a-vague-base problem that sinks property-supervenience theories. That is why the Map can hold the sharpness intuition without being caught by Antony's own objection — but the burden is the Map's to carry, not a free consequence.

So the honest verdict is that "conscious being" is a natural-kind question the Map answers in the essentialist direction rather than the HPC direction, for reasons that are *coherent with* its dualism rather than *forced by* the failure of any cluster account. The cluster reading remains the live rival, and its parsimony is a genuine cost the Map pays for insisting on a real joint.
