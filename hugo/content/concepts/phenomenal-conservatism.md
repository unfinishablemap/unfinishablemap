On Michael Huemer's principle of **phenomenal conservatism** (PC), if it seems to a subject that P, then—absent defeaters—the subject thereby has at least some prima facie, defeasible justification for believing P. The principle is general: it treats appearances of *every* kind—perceptual, introspective, intuitive, memorial, moral—as a single uniform source of justification, rather than building a separate epistemology for each. This page defines PC as a thesis in general epistemology. Its application to consciousness—licensing first-person phenomenal reports as evidence—is developed separately in [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/), which invokes PC as its epistemological grounding.

The relationship mirrors the division of labour between [functional-seeming](/concepts/functional-seeming/) (a crisp concept) and the broader [illusionism](/concepts/illusionism/) topic that applies it: here PC is stated as a principle; the consciousness payoff lives elsewhere.

## The Principle and Its Evolution

Huemer first named and defended PC in *Skepticism and the Veil of Perception* (Rowman & Littlefield, 2001), where it anchors an internalist, foundationalist direct realism offered as the principled answer to perceptual skepticism. The original formulation grants prima facie justification directly from the way things seem, with no further inference required.

The canonical general statement came in "Compassionate Phenomenal Conservatism" (*Philosophy and Phenomenological Research*, 74(1), 2007, pp. 30–55). Huemer's 2007 wording: "If it seems to S that P, then, in the absence of defeaters, S thereby has at least some degree of justification for believing that P." Two features of that sentence carry the theory's weight. The qualifier **at least some degree of** keeps the justification modest and defeasible—a seeming confers a defeasible presumption in favour of its content, one that background knowledge can override, not a guarantee. The clause **in the absence of defeaters** locates PC within a defeasible-reasoning framework rather than a foundational infallibilism.

The principle concerns *propositional* justification in the first instance—justification a subject *has* for believing P—and this should be kept apart from two neighbouring notions the Map's application otherwise risks conflating with it. *Doxastic* justification additionally requires that the subject's belief be *based* on the seeming rather than held for an unrelated reason; a seeming can make P propositionally justified while a believer arrives at P by wishful thinking. *Credal* justification, a specific degree of confidence fixed by a probability, is more specific still: "some degree of justification for believing P" is not by itself a Bayesian credence or a stated probability update. The three come apart precisely where the Map's use of PC turns—showing that a subject is rationally permitted some confidence in P does not settle how much a third party should update on the subject's report.

The paper's title is a topical pun rather than a doctrine. Huemer has since explained that "compassionate" alluded to the phrase "compassionate conservatism" then current in US politics, that "compassion has nothing to do with the paper," and that "phenomenal" derives from the Greek *phainomenon*, "appearance." The substantive move the paper makes—the *no-privileging* thesis developed below—is that no class of appearance is entitled to a standing the others lack. That claim stands on its own; it is not what the word "compassionate" labels.

### Weak and Strong Versions

The literature distinguishes a **weak** and a **strong** reading of PC, and the distinction governs what the Map may borrow. *Weak PC* is a sufficiency claim: an undefeated seeming that P confers *some* prima facie justification for P. *Strong PC* adds a necessity claim—that seemings are the *only* source of immediate justification—and later proposals extend the seeming's role into inferential justification as well (McAllister 2023; Moretti 2020). Huemer's 2007 wording states the weak sufficiency principle; his wider foundationalism is strong. Geertjan Holtrop (2026) argues that Strong PC faces a mutual-defeat problem the weak version escapes. The Map needs only the weak sufficiency claim—that a robust introspective seeming carries defeasible evidential weight—and should not be read as endorsing the stronger necessity thesis or its inferential extensions.

A note on the literature: there is no dedicated Stanford Encyclopedia entry for PC. It is discussed within the SEP's treatment of foundationalist theories of justification, where it is paired with James Pryor's dogmatism and both face the shared worry that they make justification "too easy." The dedicated encyclopedia entry is at the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy—authored by Huemer himself, so authoritative on the view's content but not a neutral third-party survey.

## What Is a Seeming?

PC's plausibility turns on what a seeming *is*, and the question is not a preliminary terminological detail. The main candidates in the current literature are a *sui generis* experiential state with propositional content (Huemer's own view), a belief or disposition to believe, a felt inclination short of belief, and a broadly representational state. Huemer marks genuine seemings by a distinctive phenomenal "forcefulness" or *pushiness* toward their content, but that diagnostic is itself contested rather than agreed—one of the questions taken up across the 2023 collection *Seemings: New Arguments, New Angles* (McCain, Stapleford & Steup, eds.).

The answer bears directly on the Map's use of PC. If an epistemically justificatory seeming must itself be phenomenally conscious, PC and illusionism collide at once; if a seeming could be a doxastic, dispositional, or *functional* state, the collision is deferred. This is exactly the disagreement [functional-seeming](/concepts/functional-seeming/) joins from the other side: Keith Frankish holds that the seeming *of* phenomenality need not itself be phenomenal, whereas Huemer-style PC treats seemings as experiences with felt content. The two concepts are more than an organisational analogy—they take opposite views on whether the states doing the epistemic work are conscious at all.

## The Self-Defeat Argument

PC's signature argument is the **self-defeat argument**: denying PC is self-undermining. The reasoning runs roughly as follows. Whatever alternative epistemology a critic adopts, they hold it because, on reflection, it *seems* to them correct. Their belief in that alternative is based on how things seem. So if seemings conferred no justification, the critic's own belief in the denial of PC would itself be unjustified. The denial saws off the branch it sits on.

Huemer treats this as the strongest consideration in PC's favour, because it does not depend on antecedently accepting PC—it turns any rival's own doxastic practice against the rival's conclusion. The argument is narrower than its rhetoric suggests, though. At most it pressures the claim that *no* appearance justifies *anything*; it does not by itself secure Huemer's unrestricted sufficiency condition, since a critic may grant that appearances are *a* source of justification while denying that every undefeated seeming suffices absent only defeaters. Restricted principles and additional background conditions remain available. Aaran Burns ("Safely Denying Phenomenal Conservatism," *Erkenntnis*, 2022) presses exactly this line: there are non-self-defeating grounds for rejecting PC as a *general* principle, and the self-defeat argument's conclusion is compatible with properly denying it. Whether an epistemology can consistently reject PC while explaining its own warranted acceptance therefore remains contested. The move is worth isolating because it is structurally the same one the Map deploys elsewhere as [self-stultification](/concepts/self-stultification/): a position that must rely on the very resource it denies.

## Generality: The No-Privileging Thesis

The distinctive claim of "Compassionate" PC is that there is no principled reason to privilege one class of appearance over another. Perceptual experience, rational intuition, introspective awareness, apparent memory, and moral seemings all fall, on this view, under a common default schema: each is a defeasible source of prima facie justification absent defeaters. Sharing the schema does not entail that they justify to the same degree, with the same phenomenology, or under the same defeat conditions—recent explanationist work distinguishes several grades of appearance and assigns them different justificatory strengths—but an epistemology that trusts perception while distrusting intuition owes an account of the asymmetry that, Huemer argues, cannot be supplied without covertly relying on seemings of the disfavoured type.

This generality is what separates PC from the adjacent view it is most often compared with. **Pryor's dogmatism** holds that perceptual experience with content P immediately (non-inferentially) justifies believing P—though it is standardly read as restricted to *perceptual* experience. PC is the wider thesis, covering introspective and intuitive seemings as well. Chris Tucker's edited collection *Seemings and Justification: New Essays on Dogmatism and Phenomenal Conservatism* (Oxford University Press, 2013) shaped the terrain for a decade, situating PC alongside dogmatism and working through the nature of seemings, cognitive penetration, Bayesian modelling, and the epistemology of moral disagreement. It has since been joined by book-length treatments—Luca Moretti's critical reconstruction (2020) and Blake McAllister's systematic defence (2023)—and by the 2023 collection *Seemings: New Arguments, New Angles*. The organising question across this literature is narrow and sharp: under what conditions, if any, can a seeming justify its own content?

## Objections at the General Level

Several families of objection recur, each aimed at PC as a general principle rather than at any specific application. The debate is genuinely two-sided: alongside the critics below stand recent book-length defences (McAllister 2023; and, more critically, Moretti 2020), and Huemer's 2024 reply "No Need for Explanation" contests the leading current objections directly. What follows is a map of live disputes, not a settled scorecard with one objection crowned decisive.

**Crazy or rogue seemings (over-liberality).** Peter Markie's canonical objection is that PC is too permissive. His 2005 example is a subject to whom it simply *seems*, with nothing in the experience to support it, that a particular walnut tree was planted on 24 April 1914; PC appears committed to granting that bizarre seeming *some* justification (Markie 2005). Critics read this as a reductio. The important feature of Huemer's reply is often mislaid: he does *not* mainly insist that such a state fails to be a genuine seeming. Where it really is an undefeated seeming, the phenomenal conservative typically accepts that it confers *some* prima facie justification, while stressing that background knowledge readily defeats it and that only a modest, defeasible boost is ever at stake. Three cases should be kept apart: a mere wish or inclination, which may be no seeming at all; a genuine but bizarre seeming, which PC is committed to treating as minimally justificatory; and an etiologically contaminated seeming, where the dispute (below) is whether provenance undercuts justification. Answering the second and third by reclassifying them as the first understates the theory's real commitment.

**Cognitive penetration (the bad basis).** Susanna Siegel argues that a seeming's causal history can downgrade its justificatory power: if an irrational background belief *causes* an experience, the resulting seeming should not inherit justification it does not deserve (Siegel 2012; and her *The Rationality of Perception*, Oxford University Press, 2017). PC, holding that justification depends on a seeming's present content rather than its provenance, seems committed to trusting etiologically tainted seemings. This remains among the most discussed objections, though Moretti (2020) argues that cognitive penetration does not in fact defeat PC, so its standing is contested rather than decisive.

**The metajustification demand.** Laurence BonJour presses that trusting seemings itself requires independent evidence that seemings are reliable; without such a metajustification, PC simply helps itself to a foundational assumption (BonJour, "In Search of Direct Realism," *Philosophy and Phenomenological Research* 69, 2004, pp. 349–367, esp. 357–360). Huemer answers that the demand either generates a vicious regress—every source would need a further meta-source, and that one another—or begs the question, since PC's rivals covertly rely on seemings to motivate their own metajustifications.

**The isolation objection.** Kevin McCain ("An Isolation Objection to Phenomenal Conservatism," *Erkenntnis* 82(6), 2017, pp. 1381–1390) argues that a theory on which seemings alone justify faces a problem analogous to the isolation objection to coherentism—a seeming evidentially cut off from the rest of a subject's beliefs looks unable to transmit genuine justification—and that PC can escape only by giving up its internalist character. The objection is sometimes mislabelled in secondary discussion: Chris Tucker, editor of the 2013 volume, is a *defender* of PC and dogmatism rather than the author of this objection, and Luca Moretti's signature objection is a different one (reflective awareness, below).

**Reflective awareness.** Moretti ("Phenomenal Conservatism and the Problem of Reflective Awareness," *American Philosophical Quarterly* 55(3), 2018, pp. 267–280) argues that once a subject becomes reflectively aware of an appearance, the appearance can lose the inherent justifying force PC ascribes to it. Huemer's 2024 reply resists this, but the objection is part of the present debate rather than a closed matter.

**Bayesian compatibility.** A recurring worry asks whether PC's non-inferential boost can be reconciled with Bayesian updating, where evidence works through conditional probabilities rather than by fiat. The current verdict runs against the worry: Moretti (2020) and, jointly, McCain and Moretti (*Appearance and Explanation*, Oxford University Press, 2021) argue that PC is compatible with Bayesian reasoning and is not defeated by the incompatibility charge—the two authors sometimes cited *for* a standing Bayesian objection are, in fact, the source of the leading reconciliation. The live question is no longer whether PC is formally Bayesian-incompatible but whether appearance-based justification, so reconciled, retains the anti-sceptical force Huemer advertises.

**Easy knowledge and bootstrapping.** The Stanford Encyclopedia places easy knowledge among the principal difficulties for foundationalist views of this shape: if a bare seeming justifies P, a subject seems able to "bootstrap" to the further conclusion that the source delivering the seeming is reliable, acquiring justification too cheaply (Vogel 2000; Cohen 2002). The dispute continues—Moretti (2020) argues that standard bootstrapping objections do not defeat PC, while Guido Tana (2025) argues that PC cannot dispel the resulting charge of arbitrariness. This family is especially pointed for a page that leans on the encyclopedia's "too easy" framing.

**Truth-conduciveness.** Because PC is an *internalist* account, it can explain why relying on a seeming is rational from the subject's point of view even when the process producing the seeming is unreliable. Critics therefore ask whether PC connects justification to *truth* at all. Declan Smithies ("On the Global Ambitions of Phenomenal Conservatism," *Analytic Philosophy* 60(3), 2019, pp. 206–244), the leading recent systematic critique, presses a version of this: the theory's very simplicity, he argues, distorts the epistemic phenomena—introspection, inference, a priori justification—it is meant to explain. The worry bears directly on the Map, which needs phenomenal seemings to be evidence about the *constitution of reality*, not merely internally blameless bases for belief.

## Relation to Site Perspective

PC matters to the Map primarily through **[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** (Tenet 5). It answers a prior question the Map's whole methodology depends on: why do first-person phenomenal seemings count as evidence *at all*? One familiar reason for setting them aside—that a simpler, purely physical theory need not posit them—is a parsimony argument, and Tenet 5 holds that parsimony is an unreliable guide to truth where knowledge is incomplete. PC supplies the positive epistemology to match: seemings carry defeasible justificatory weight, so dislodging a robust seeming requires a *defeater*, not merely a theory that would be tidier without it.

The honest opponent, though, is not simply someone who "discounts" the evidence, because PC is metaphysically neutral. A physicalist can accept PC in full: on a phenomenal-concept account of the kind Brian Loar defended, introspection yields genuine prima facie justification about one's experience while the concepts it deploys pick out physical states under a special mode of presentation. Such a physicalist grants the seeming its evidential weight and disputes instead what the seeming *represents*—its content, its mode of presentation, and whether it entails a non-physical property. The real disagreement is therefore a downstream one about what an admitted seeming shows, not a choice between trusting the seeming and protecting physicalism. That is a framework-boundary disagreement the Map states honestly rather than one PC settles in the Map's favour.

This is why PC's bearing on **[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** (Tenet 1) is deliberately limited. PC secures the *evidential base*—that phenomenal seemings are admissible evidence. It does not, by itself, deliver the *metaphysical conclusion* that phenomenal properties are irreducible; that further step still requires the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/), the [knowledge argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/), and conceivability considerations. Conflating the two would overstate PC's reach: the principle earns phenomenal reports a seat at the evidential table without settling what they ultimately show. Honouring that boundary is exactly the discipline that [evidential-status discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) requires.

The same limit governs agency. What a seeming can underwrite is the *phenomenology* of acting—that action feels active, effortful, self-initiated. It does not underwrite the further claim that this phenomenology accurately represents non-physical downward causation. The Map's **[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** (Tenet 3) is explicit that consciousness cannot first-person-verify its own causal power: bidirectional interaction is a framework-level commitment, not a directly introspectible datum. PC should not be read as supplying by seeming what the tenet page correctly locates as an indirect, theory-level posit.

A related caution governs the Map's reliance on the "pain hurts" paradigm. The Map does not need PC in its full generality; its case rests on robust, persistent, cross-traditional *introspective* seemings, and the seeming that pain hurts is not plausibly a product of theory. But that security comes at a price worth stating. On the thin reading—*there is an unpleasant experience occurring now*—the seeming is close to constitutive self-knowledge and exceptionally secure, yet on its own it contributes little toward dualism. On the thicker reading the metaphysical payoff needs—that the experience has *a qualitative character exceeding what functional description captures*—the content is useful but no longer theory-neutral, and a physicalist can grant the unpleasant experience while denying that introspection presents it as non-functional. The invulnerability of the thin seeming does not transfer to the thick one, and the argument must not let it silently do so. The application of this narrower, disciplined reliance is worked out in [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/), which decomposes phenomenal reports into layers of warranted trust and identifies precisely where the objections above bite hardest.
