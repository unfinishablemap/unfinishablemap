On Michael Huemer's principle of **phenomenal conservatism** (PC), if it seems to a subject that P, then—absent defeaters—the subject thereby has at least some prima facie, defeasible justification for believing P. The principle is general: it treats appearances of *every* kind—perceptual, introspective, intuitive, memorial, moral—as a single uniform source of justification, rather than building a separate epistemology for each. This page defines PC as a thesis in general epistemology. Its application to consciousness—licensing first-person phenomenal reports as evidence—is developed separately in [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/), which invokes PC as its epistemological grounding.

The relationship mirrors the division of labour between [functional-seeming](/concepts/functional-seeming/) (a crisp concept) and the broader [illusionism](/concepts/illusionism/) topic that applies it: here PC is stated as a principle; the consciousness payoff lives elsewhere.

## The Principle and Its Evolution

Huemer first named and defended PC in *Skepticism and the Veil of Perception* (Rowman & Littlefield, 2001), where it anchors an internalist, foundationalist direct realism offered as the principled answer to perceptual skepticism. The original formulation grants prima facie justification directly from the way things seem, with no further inference required.

The canonical general statement came in "Compassionate Phenomenal Conservatism" (*Philosophy and Phenomenological Research*, 74(1), 2007, pp. 30–55). Huemer's 2007 wording: "If it seems to S that P, then, in the absence of defeaters, S thereby has at least some degree of justification for believing that P." Two features of that sentence carry the theory's weight. The qualifier **at least some degree of** keeps the justification modest and defeasible—a seeming confers a starting credence that background knowledge can override, not a guarantee. The clause **in the absence of defeaters** locates PC within a defeasible-reasoning framework rather than a foundational infallibilism.

The label "compassionate" marks the paper's central move, explained in the next section: PC extends the same epistemic charity to *all* appearances, refusing to privilege any one source.

A note on the literature: there is no dedicated Stanford Encyclopedia entry for PC. It is discussed within the SEP's treatment of foundationalist theories of justification, where it is paired with James Pryor's dogmatism and both face the shared worry that they make justification "too easy." The dedicated encyclopedia entry is at the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy—authored by Huemer himself, so authoritative on the view's content but not a neutral third-party survey.

## The Self-Defeat Argument

PC's signature argument is the **self-defeat argument**: denying PC is self-undermining. The reasoning runs roughly as follows. Whatever alternative epistemology a critic adopts, they hold it because, on reflection, it *seems* to them correct. Their belief in that alternative is based on how things seem. So if seemings conferred no justification, the critic's own belief in the denial of PC would itself be unjustified. The denial saws off the branch it sits on.

Huemer treats this as the strongest consideration in PC's favour, because it does not depend on antecedently accepting PC—it turns any rival's own doxastic practice against the rival's conclusion. Critics dispute whether the argument really succeeds: a published rebuttal has argued that it overreaches, contending that one can reject PC as a *general* principle while still accounting for the justified acceptance of one's own epistemology by narrower means. Whether an epistemology can consistently reject PC while explaining its own warranted acceptance remains contested. The argument is worth isolating because it is structurally the same move the Map deploys elsewhere as [self-stultification](/concepts/self-stultification/): a position that must rely on the very resource it denies.

## Generality: The No-Privileging Thesis

The distinctive claim of "Compassionate" PC is that there is no principled reason to privilege one class of appearance over another. Perceptual experience, rational intuition, introspective awareness, apparent memory, and moral seemings are all, on this view, defeasible sources of justification in exactly the same way. An epistemology that trusts perception but distrusts intuition (or vice versa) owes an account of the asymmetry that, Huemer argues, cannot be supplied without covertly relying on seemings of the disfavoured type.

This generality is what separates PC from the adjacent view it is most often compared with. **Pryor's dogmatism** holds that perceptual experience with content P immediately (non-inferentially) justifies believing P—but it is restricted to *perceptual* experience. PC is the wider thesis, covering introspective and intuitive seemings as well. The 2013 collection *Seemings and Justification: New Essays on Dogmatism and Phenomenal Conservatism*, edited by Chris Tucker (Oxford University Press), is the field-defining volume mapping this terrain: it situates PC alongside dogmatism and works through the nature of seemings, cognitive penetration, Bayesian modelling, and the epistemology of moral disagreement. The organising question across the volume is narrow and sharp: under what conditions, if any, can a seeming justify its own content?

## Objections at the General Level

Four families of objection recur, each aimed at PC as a general principle rather than at any specific application.

**Crazy or rogue seemings (over-liberality).** Peter Markie's canonical objection is that PC is too permissive: a seeming produced by wishful thinking, prejudice, or a crystal-ball gazer's conviction would, on PC, still confer *some* justification (Markie 2005). Critics read this as a reductio. Huemer's reply distinguishes genuine seemings—which have a distinctive phenomenal forcefulness or "pushiness" toward their content—from mere inclinations, wishes, or guesses, and notes that marginal seemings are readily defeated by background knowledge, so only *some* defeasible justification is ever at stake.

**Cognitive penetration (the bad basis).** Susanna Siegel argues that a seeming's causal history can downgrade its justificatory power: if an irrational background belief *causes* an experience, the resulting seeming should not inherit justification it does not deserve (Siegel 2012; and her later *The Rationality of Perception*, Oxford University Press, 2017). PC, holding that justification depends on a seeming's present content rather than its provenance, seems committed to trusting etiologically tainted seemings. This is widely regarded as the most philosophically live objection.

**The metajustification demand.** Laurence BonJour presses that trusting seemings itself requires independent evidence that seemings are reliable. Without such a metajustification, PC simply helps itself to a foundational assumption. Huemer answers that the demand either generates a vicious regress—every source would need a further meta-source, and that one another—or begs the question, since PC's rivals covertly rely on seemings to motivate their own metajustifications. (BonJour's critique appears in his mid-2000s work on direct realism; precise pagination is left unstated here pending verification.)

**Isolation and Bayesian incompatibility.** A more recent line, associated with Moretti and Tucker, presses an *isolation objection*: a seeming that is evidentially isolated from the rest of a subject's beliefs looks incapable of transmitting genuine justification. A parallel worry asks whether PC's non-inferential boost can be reconciled with Bayesian updating, where evidence works through conditional probabilities rather than by fiat. These objections probe whether the "prima facie justification from bare seeming" mechanism is formally coherent, not merely whether it is too generous.

## Relation to Site Perspective

PC matters to the Map primarily through **[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)** (Tenet 5). PC answers a prior question that the Map's whole methodology depends on: why do first-person phenomenal seemings count as evidence *at all*? The dominant reason for discounting them—that a simpler, purely physical theory need not posit them—is a parsimony argument. Tenet 5 holds that parsimony is an unreliable guide to truth where knowledge is incomplete. PC supplies the positive epistemology to match: seemings carry defeasible justificatory weight, and dislodging a robust seeming requires a *defeater*, not merely a theory that would be tidier without it. The felt hurt of pain and the felt reality of agency thereby enter as prima facie evidence rather than as data to be explained away.

Secondarily, PC bears on **[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** (Tenet 1), but with a deliberate limit. PC secures the *evidential base*—that phenomenal seemings are admissible evidence. It does not, by itself, deliver the *metaphysical conclusion* that phenomenal properties are irreducible. That further step still requires the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/), the [knowledge argument](/concepts/knowledge-argument/), and conceivability considerations. Conflating the two would overstate PC's reach: the principle earns phenomenal reports a seat at the evidential table without settling what they ultimately show. Honouring that boundary is exactly the discipline that [evidential-status discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) requires.

The Map also does not need PC in its full generality. Its case rests on robust, persistent, cross-traditional *introspective* seemings—the paradigm case least vulnerable to the crazy-seemings and cognitive-penetration objections, since the seeming that pain hurts is not plausibly a product of theory. The application of this narrower reliance is worked out in [phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence](/topics/phenomenal-authority-and-first-person-evidence/), which decomposes phenomenal reports into layers of warranted trust and identifies precisely where the objections above bite hardest.
