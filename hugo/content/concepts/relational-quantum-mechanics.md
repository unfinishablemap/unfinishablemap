Relational quantum mechanics (RQM) is Carlo Rovelli's interpretation of quantum mechanics in which physical properties exist only as relations between systems, not as intrinsic features of things. There is no "view from nowhere"—all quantum facts are relative to specific observers, where "observer" means any physical system, not necessarily a conscious one. RQM explicitly removes any special role for consciousness in quantum measurement.

This interpretation directly conflicts with The Unfinishable Map's [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) tenet. Understanding why RQM works—and what it sacrifices to work—clarifies what's at stake in adopting a consciousness-based alternative.

## The Core Idea

Rovelli's insight begins with an analogy to special relativity. Einstein showed that velocity is always relative to a reference frame—there is no absolute velocity. RQM extends this relationality to quantum states: there is no absolute quantum state, only states relative to particular observers.

When system S interacts with system O, the interaction produces definite values—but only relative to O. Another system O′ that hasn't interacted with S may still describe S as in superposition. Both descriptions are correct within their respective perspectives. Apparent contradiction dissolves because neither perspective claims absolute truth.

Consider Wigner's friend: inside a lab, a physicist measures a spin and sees "up." Outside, Wigner hasn't yet interacted with the lab. According to RQM, the spin has a definite value relative to the physicist, but remains in superposition relative to Wigner. When Wigner opens the lab and interacts with the physicist, he will find results consistent with the physicist's earlier measurement. No paradox arises because "the spin is up" was never an absolute claim—it was always implicitly "the spin is up relative to the physicist."

## How RQM Dissolves the Measurement Problem

The [measurement problem](/concepts/measurement-problem/) asks: what causes quantum superpositions to collapse into definite outcomes? Standard quantum mechanics leaves "measurement" undefined, creating a conceptual gap.

RQM dissolves the problem by denying its premise. Collapse isn't a special process requiring explanation—it's what interaction looks like from the interacting system's perspective. Every physical interaction is a "measurement" that produces definite values for the interacting systems. No special observer status is needed. No boundary between quantum and classical is required. The wave function isn't a real entity that collapses—it's a calculational tool for predicting interaction outcomes.

The ontology consists of discrete events (sometimes called "flashes") where systems interact and properties actualize—always relative to the interacting partner. Reality is a web of relative facts, not an absolute state evolving in time.

## Challenges to RQM

RQM faces several objections, of unequal weight. Two—the intersubjectivity problem and the solipsism dilemma it generates—are now the centre of gravity in the 2020s literature; the others are older.

### The intersubjectivity problem and cross-perspective links

If facts are relative to observers, how does science work? How can physicists compare results if each has only their own relative facts? The standard formulation of RQM appears to leave each observer trapped in their own perspective—what critics have called the "island universes" problem. If your laboratory measurement of an electron's spin is a fact relative to you, and my later measurement of your apparatus is a separate fact relative to me, what guarantees that the two facts cohere? Nothing in the original RQM postulates required them to.

Adlam and Rovelli (2023) address this directly with a new postulate they call Cross-Perspective Links (CPL), proposed explicitly as a replacement for RQM's original fourth postulate ("Relativity of comparisons"). The CPL postulate states that when observer Alice measures variable V on system S, any subsequent observer Bob who measures Alice's physical record of V will obtain a matching result—provided the record has not been destroyed through measurements in a non-commuting basis. The information-destruction condition is specified using the Heisenberg uncertainty relation; the postulate is precise rather than hand-waving. The aim is to recover a "shared observable reality composed of a large number of variables whose values all the observers agree on" while leaving the original observer-relativity intact at the level of single quantum events.

CPL is the most serious technical move RQM has made in twenty years. It is also the move on which the interpretation now turns. If CPL succeeds on its own terms, RQM has answered its sharpest contemporary objection; if it fails or comes at unacceptable cost, RQM faces a deeper structural problem than the original-postulate formulation acknowledged.

### The solipsism dilemma

Peter Lewis (2024) argues that CPL does not in fact save RQM, and that the situation generates a dilemma the interpretation cannot escape. Without CPL, RQM collapses toward what Lewis calls epistemic solipsism: an observer cannot in principle know anything beyond their own immediate present experience, because every claim about another observer or about the past is itself just another relative fact and there is no postulate connecting these facts across perspectives. With CPL, the solipsism is dissolved—but Lewis argues the cure removes what was distinctively relational about the interpretation. CPL effectively reinstates frame-independent facts about observers' records: Alice's reading of the electron's spin becomes something Bob can measure and find. At that point the relational ontology has been quietly augmented with absolute facts about measurement outcomes, and the interpretation's selling point—that there is no view from nowhere—has been softened to the point where RQM and a standard observer-independent reading look structurally closer than the relational rhetoric suggests.

Muciño, Okon and Sudarsky (2022) press a related but distinct objection. They argue, on grounds the interpretation should accept by its own standards of clarity and logical soundness, that Rovelli's claim that RQM provides a satisfactory realist non-solipsistic description of the world is untenable—either the relational ontology collapses toward solipsism, or what is added to prevent the collapse is itself ad hoc relative to RQM's foundations. Rovelli's published reply (2021) contends that the assessment presupposes precisely the framework RQM rejects. The exchange is now a stable impasse, with the disagreement located at the foundations rather than at empirical predictions.

The Map's reading of this exchange: the cross-perspective-links proposal is RQM's most credible attempt to recover frame-independent facts, and it deserves the engagement Adlam and Rovelli give it. But CPL is doing work that RQM's original postulates did not earn—it imports frame-independent agreement as a postulate, where the original framework treated such agreement as derivative. Whether this counts as a successful repair or as a quiet abandonment of what made the interpretation distinctive is precisely what Lewis's dilemma and Muciño-Okon-Sudarsky's assessment leave open.

### The locality question

RQM claims to preserve locality despite Bell violations, arguing that EPR correlations reflect "common cause in indeterministic context" rather than faster-than-light influence. Critics like Pienaar argue this empties locality of meaningful content. If "locality" is compatible with perfect correlations between spacelike-separated measurements, the term no longer constrains anything.

### What counts as an observer

RQM says any physical system can be an observer—but this leaves unclear what determines the boundaries of a "system." When does a collection of particles become an observer with its own perspective? The ontology of relative facts requires something to be relative to, but RQM provides no principled way to individuate observers. The Map's distinct objection to this move appears in the next section: the universalisation of "observer" severs the relativity from the kind of observer-relativity the Map's framework needs.

## What RQM Removes and the Map's Specific Disagreement

Rovelli is explicit: "There is nothing subjective, idealistic, or mentalistic, in RQM." This is a feature, not a bug—RQM succeeds by eliminating the special status of consciousness that other interpretations invoke.

The consequence: RQM provides no mechanism for [mental causation](/concepts/mental-causation-and-downward-causation/). If any physical interaction is a "measurement," then a rock measuring a photon is equivalent to a physicist measuring one. Consciousness adds nothing. The [Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction) tenet—the claim that consciousness causally influences physical outcomes—has no place in RQM's framework.

The disagreement here is precise enough to be worth stating cleanly. The Map does not object to observer-relativity as such. The post-decoherence selection programme is itself a framework in which the actualised outcome is relative to the conscious system whose neural states are being differentiated, and the [brain-interface-boundary](/concepts/brain-interface-boundary/) picture is one in which each subject's collapses are local to their own brain. What the Map rejects is RQM's specific move of universalising "observer" to mean any physical system whatever, which severs the observer-relativity from the consciousness-related observer-relativity the Map's framework needs. The relativity-to-physical-systems reading buys RQM its broad applicability—any interaction is a measurement, the early universe collapses itself, the [multi-mind-collapse-problem](/concepts/multi-mind-collapse-problem/) and the [prebiotic collapse problem](/concepts/prebiotic-collapse/) are both dissolved automatically—but it does so by detaching the relativity from the only kind of observer the Map's tenets identify as load-bearing. A rock measuring a photon is not the kind of measurement the Map's framework is about; the Map's framework is about what happens at the [brain-mind interface](/concepts/brain-interface-boundary/) in conscious systems, and RQM's universalisation makes that distinction unavailable from inside the interpretation.

This is the substance of the disagreement: RQM and the Map both treat measurement outcomes as relative to *something*, and they differ on what that something is. RQM says: any physical system. The Map says: the conscious system at whose interface the actualisation occurs. The dispute is honest framework-boundary disagreement—neither framework refutes the other from inside; each makes a foundational commitment the other declines. The Map registers this honestly rather than dressing the disagreement as in-framework refutation. What can be argued at framework boundaries is whether each side has earned its foundational move by its own standards, which is the burden the cross-perspective-links discussion above attempts to assess for RQM and which articles such as [brain-interface-boundary](/concepts/brain-interface-boundary/) and the [post-decoherence-selection-programme](/apex/post-decoherence-selection-programme/) attempt to discharge for the Map.

## The Illusionist Reading and the Phenomenology of Observation

[Illusionists](/concepts/illusionism/) such as Dennett and Frankish are RQM's most natural allies. If phenomenal consciousness is an introspective illusion, then RQM's dismissal of consciousness's special role costs nothing—there was never anything special about consciousness to dismiss. On this reading, RQM is the interpretation the hard problem would have us choose if the hard problem itself were dissolved.

The Map's standing objection to illusionism applies here. To say consciousness plays no role in collapse, the illusionist must explain why we *believe* it does. Dennett's heterophenomenology and Frankish's "quasi-phenomenal" properties are the standard moves. Raymond Tallis's objection—"Misrepresentation presupposes presentation"—is the standing Map reply: for consciousness to *seem* special, something must be doing the seeming, and that something resists purely dispositional analysis. Frankish has contested the move directly; the dispute is live rather than settled, and the Map's commitment to dualism is what tips the assessment, not a stand-alone refutation.

Independently of the illusionism debate, RQM treats observation as a purely physical relation between the observing system and the observed one. The phenomenology of observation—accessible through [introspection](/concepts/introspection/) and developed in the [contemplative literature](/concepts/witness-consciousness/) on the subject-object structure—suggests observation involves something the physical-relation account cannot capture: the qualitative character of being an observer, not merely functioning as one. The weight one gives this first-person evidence depends on prior commitments about the epistemology of first-person methods.

## Why Understanding RQM Matters

Engaging with RQM clarifies what is at stake in choosing a consciousness-based framework. RQM shows there is a coherent interpretation where consciousness plays no role; the Map's choice is therefore a philosophical commitment, not a deduction from physics. RQM's treatment of multiple observers also illuminates a real problem for consciousness-based readings—if Alice's consciousness and Bob's both participate in collapse, how are their selections reconciled? RQM relativises collapse to each observer; the Map's [brain-interface-boundary](/concepts/brain-interface-boundary/) picture answers by locality, each consciousness modulating only its own neural substrate. RQM's broader insight that intermediate states need not be coordinated until observers interact carries over to the Map's framework: before Alice and Bob communicate, their brain states (modulated by their respective consciousnesses) need not be compared, and agreement is verified only through physical interaction.

## Process Philosophy Perspective

Whitehead's process philosophy illuminates what RQM sacrifices. For Whitehead, reality consists of "actual occasions"—momentary events that arise, achieve definiteness through "concrescence," and perish, with both physical and experiential aspects. The key contrast: for Whitehead, definiteness is *achieved* through an internal process of becoming, not merely *relative* to an external observer. RQM and Whitehead both deny a view from nowhere, but the structure of the denial differs—RQM relativises facts to observers, Whitehead grounds them in each occasion's own concrescence. On RQM, facts remain relative to observers at all times; there is no moment of genuine becoming where relativity yields to definiteness.

This bears directly on the measurement problem. RQM claims to dissolve it by making collapse relative; Whitehead would say the move redescribes the problem rather than solving it, because something must still account for why each actual occasion achieves the definiteness it does. Relativising the achieving does not explain it.

The Map does not endorse Whitehead's panexperientialism—the view that experience runs all the way down to elementary occasions, raising the [combination problem](/concepts/combination-problem/) of how micro-experiences integrate into unified consciousness. But Whitehead's insistence that definiteness is *achieved* rather than merely *relative* aligns with the Map's view that collapse involves something beyond physical interaction.

## What Would Challenge This View?

The Map's position on RQM would need revision if:

1. **RQM solves the hard problem.** If a satisfying account emerged of why relative facts between physical systems are accompanied by phenomenal experience—if the intersubjectivity solution (cross-perspective links) could explain not just physical agreement but qualitative experience—RQM would become more attractive. Currently, RQM ignores the hard problem rather than solving it.

2. **[decoherence](/concepts/decoherence/) proves sufficient.** If decoherence could explain not just the suppression of interference effects but the emergence of definite outcomes, the role for consciousness at collapse would diminish. RQM's appeal lies partly in not requiring any special collapse mechanism. But [decoherence doesn't solve the measurement problem](/concepts/decoherence/)—it selects preferred bases without explaining why we see specific outcomes.

3. **Contemplative reports prove unreliable.** The phenomenology of observation—the sense that being an observer involves something beyond physical interaction—supports consciousness-causes-collapse over RQM. If this phenomenology were shown to be systematically misleading (as illusionists claim), RQM's dismissal of observer specialness would lose its cost.

4. **[Indexical identity](/concepts/haecceity/) proves illusory.** RQM handles multiple observers by relativizing facts to each. This works if there's no deep fact about *which* observer you are. If indexical identity—the haecceitistic fact of being *this* subject—proves reducible to physical relations, RQM's treatment of observers as mere physical systems becomes more defensible.

5. **The cross-perspective-links proposal is shown to be internally earned.** Adlam and Rovelli's CPL postulate aims to recover frame-independent agreement about measurement records. Lewis (2024) argues the recovery comes at the cost of what made RQM distinctively relational; Muciño, Okon and Sudarsky (2022) argue, on standards RQM should accept, that the realist non-solipsistic reading the postulate is supposed to secure is untenable. If a future technical development could show CPL doing the work Adlam and Rovelli claim without quietly importing frame-independent facts, the case for RQM strengthens considerably. The Map's current reading is that this has not been shown, and that the live exchange is closer to a stable impasse than to a resolved dialectic.

## Relation to Site Perspective

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism):** RQM is compatible with physicalism—it requires no non-physical elements. The Map's dualism finds no support here. However, RQM's emphasis on observer-relativity shares something with the Map's rejection of "view from nowhere" physicalism, even if RQM doesn't draw dualist conclusions.

The deeper issue: RQM's relativization applies to *physical* properties. The phenomenal character of being an observer—what it's like to see the measurement result—is neither relative nor absolute on RQM because RQM doesn't address it at all. Dualism asserts that this phenomenal dimension exists and matters. RQM's silence on phenomenology is not neutrality; it's exclusion. By treating observers as purely physical systems, RQM assumes away the dualist's central datum.

**[Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction):** RQM eliminates the need for any special interaction—consciousness plays no role in collapse. This directly conflicts with the tenet's proposal that consciousness biases quantum outcomes.

Note, however, that RQM faces its own version of the "how does collapse happen?" question. Saying collapse is relative to observers doesn't explain *why* each observer-relative fact takes the value it does. The tenet proposes that consciousness biases outcomes; RQM says facts simply are relative to physical systems. Neither framework provides a complete dynamical mechanism—the Map's proposal lacks specified coupling constants or measurable predictions, just as RQM treats relative facts as brute. The difference is not mechanistic completeness but the kind of variable each framework identifies as relevant: the Map holds that an experiential variable (consciousness) belongs in the account, while RQM excludes it by construction.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction):** **Direct conflict.** RQM explicitly removes consciousness from physics. If RQM is correct, consciousness cannot influence physical outcomes through quantum mechanisms. The Map's central claim—that consciousness participates in determining quantum outcomes—fails.

The [epiphenomenalism concern](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/) cuts both ways, however. RQM makes consciousness epiphenomenal by default—experience accompanies physical interactions (if it exists at all) but plays no causal role. The same considerations that make epiphenomenalism problematic for dualists apply here: why do we talk about consciousness if it does nothing? The bidirectional interaction tenet addresses this by giving consciousness genuine causal work. RQM leaves the explanatory gap unexplained.

**[No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds):** RQM also rejects many-worlds, treating collapse (relative to each observer) as real. Here the Map and RQM agree: we don't branch into multiple copies.

But the agreement is superficial. RQM's rejection of MWI stems from denying that the wave function represents reality—it's a calculational tool. The Map's rejection stems from the [indexical identity problem](/concepts/haecceity/): the meaningfulness of asking "which branch am I in?" presupposes something RQM cannot accommodate—that being *this* observer is a genuine fact, not merely a relative one. RQM and the Map reject MWI for different reasons, and those reasons reveal incompatible commitments about the nature of observers.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits):** RQM appears simpler—no need for consciousness in physics. But simplicity isn't truth. RQM's framework doesn't explain why consciousness exists or why physical processes are accompanied by experience. It solves the measurement problem while ignoring the hard problem.

The deeper point: RQM's parsimony is achieved by restriction of scope, not by genuine explanation. Refusing to address the hard problem is not the same as showing it doesn't exist. A theory of physics that ignores consciousness appears simpler only because it excludes the most complex phenomenon. If consciousness exists and is irreducible (as the dualism tenet holds), RQM's simplicity is illusory—it covers only part of reality.

## Summary

Relational quantum mechanics offers a coherent interpretation where quantum facts are relative to observers, and "observer" means any physical system. It dissolves the measurement problem and avoids multi-observer paradoxes—but only by eliminating consciousness's special role and by universalising "observer" in a way that severs the relativity from the kind of observer-relativity the Map's framework requires.

The interpretation's most credible recent move is Adlam and Rovelli's cross-perspective-links postulate, which attempts to recover frame-independent agreement about measurement records without abandoning observer-relativity at the level of individual quantum events. Whether the postulate earns its work by RQM's own resources—or quietly imports the frame-independent facts the relational framework was supposed to do without—is precisely what Lewis's 2024 dilemma and the Muciño-Okon-Sudarsky exchange leave contested.

The Map's disagreement with RQM is at the framework boundary, honestly registered as such rather than presented as in-framework refutation. RQM and the Map both treat measurement outcomes as relative to something; they differ on what that something is. Engaging with RQM clarifies what the Map's framework adds beyond bare quantum mechanics: a commitment to consciousness as real, irreducible, and causally effective—not merely another physical system among many.
