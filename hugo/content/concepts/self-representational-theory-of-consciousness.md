The **self-representational theory of consciousness** holds that a mental state is phenomenally conscious just when it represents *itself* in the right way. One state does both jobs at once: it is directed at the world (the coffee, the blue sky) and, in the same act, directed at itself. Uriah Kriegel is its leading proponent. The theory is "same-order" rather than "higher-order": where [higher-order theories](/concepts/higher-order-theories/) make a state conscious by having a *separate* thought or perception target it, self-representationalism folds the awareness-conferring work back into the one state, so no second state is needed.

The Unfinishable Map treats self-representationalism as a serious reductive rival to assess, not a view it shares. On the Map's reading, the theory gives a genuine structural description of *for-me-ness* — the sense in which every experience is present *to a subject* — but a structural description of self-intimation is not a reductive explanation of it. The account re-sites the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) inside the self-directed content rather than dissolving it: nothing in "a state represents itself" says why that reflexive structure is *felt* rather than processed in the dark. Tellingly, the theory's own best defenders have drifted from *representation* toward *acquaintance*, a move examined below that converges on the Map's own [phenomenal presentation](/concepts/phenomenal-presentation/) concept.

## The Core Thesis

Kriegel's monograph *Subjective Consciousness: A Self-Representational Theory* (Oxford University Press, 2009) develops the view at length. Its organising distinction is between two components of any conscious experience:

- **Qualitative character** — the specific *what-it-is-like*, the bluish or painful quality.
- **Subjective character** — the *for-me-ness*, the fact that the experience is like anything *for a subject* at all.

Kriegel argues that subjective character is the real heart of the mystery, and that it is constituted by the state's representing itself. On the publisher's summary, the thesis is that conscious mental states "always represent themselves, whereas unconscious ones do not" — or at least not in the right way. The qualifier matters: the self-representation must be **non-derivative, specific, and essential** to confer subjective character, not any incidental way a state might carry information about itself.

Two further commitments shape the view. First, the inner awareness is **peripheral, not attentive**: a ubiquitous, inattentive self-awareness accompanies every focal outer-directed experience. This answers the natural objection "I am aware of the coffee, not of my perceiving" — the self-awareness is real but marginal, not the object of focus. Second, Kriegel offers a naturalisation strategy on which distinct neural vehicles carry the world-directed and self-directed components, integrated into a single state by synchronous firing, so that standard causal-informational theories of representation can apply.

## The Brentano Lineage

Self-representationalism is standardly presented as reviving a long tradition rather than inventing a doctrine. The lineage runs from Aristotle through Franz Brentano to the phenomenologists. Aristotle's *De Anima* observes that "we perceive that we see and hear," and argues that this perceiving-of-perceiving cannot require a further faculty on pain of regress — so the seeing must already include awareness of itself. Brentano's *Psychology from an Empirical Standpoint* (1874) systematised the idea: every mental act takes a **primary object** (the tone heard) and, incidentally (*nebenbei*), a **secondary object** — the act itself, given in inner perception.

This ancestry should be claimed carefully. Whether Brentano is genuinely a self-representationalist forerunner is contested: his medieval-inflected notion of an "object" and the elusive, incidental character of the secondary object do not map cleanly onto contemporary representation-talk. The honest formulation is that the tradition is *standardly claimed* as the lineage, not that Brentano held the modern theory.

## How It Differs from Higher-Order Theories

The contrast with [higher-order theories](/concepts/higher-order-theories/) (HOT/HOP) is the theory's main selling point. On a higher-order view, a first-order state becomes conscious when a numerically distinct higher-order state represents it. That two-state structure invites two objections that self-representationalism is built to dodge:

- **The targetless / misrepresentation problem.** A higher-order thought can target a first-order state that does not exist, or misdescribe one that does. Rosenthal accepts that such "targetless" higher-order thoughts still produce experience — which severs consciousness from its supposed first-order grounding. A self-representing state cannot target a non-existent state, because the target *is* the state itself; it cannot fail to exist when it represents itself.
- **The intimacy worry.** Higher-order theories divide the conscious state and the awareness-of-it between two states that could in principle come apart. Collapsing them into one restores the intimacy between an experience and its being-for-a-subject.

Both objections are answered by the same structural move: dropping the second state. This is why the view is genuinely *same-order* — the awareness is not a level up but internal to the state that is aware.

## Taxonomy: Third Camp or Higher-Order Variant?

Whether self-representationalism is a *rival* to higher-order theory or a *variant* of it is itself contested, and the answer depends on the theorist. This distinction is easy to flatten and should not be.

- **Kriegel and Williford** market self-representationalism as a distinct *third* camp, standing against both first-order representationalism and higher-order monitoring — the framing of their edited volume *Self-Representational Approaches to Consciousness* (MIT Press, 2006).
- **Rocco Gennaro** classifies his own **Wide Intrinsicality View** (developed at book length in *The Consciousness Paradox*, MIT Press, 2012) as "a version of HOT theory": the conscious state is a complex whose intrinsic — but *unconscious* — higher-order component targets a world-directed part.
- The **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy** files self-representational accounts under higher-order theories.

The tracked difference underneath the terminology is substantive: **is the awareness-conferring component itself conscious?** For Kriegel, yes — the inner awareness is peripherally conscious, part of the phenomenal field. For Gennaro, no — the intrinsic higher-order thought is unconscious. That is a real disagreement about the architecture of experience, not merely a labelling dispute, and it is why the two theorists sort the same structure into different camps.

## The Standard Objections

Four lines of objection recur, and the Map regards them as jointly decisive against the reduction.

**The sufficiency gap (Levine).** In his review of *Subjective Consciousness*, Joseph Levine — the "explanatory gap" theorist — endorses Kriegel's *framing* (subjective character is the crux) while rejecting the reduction. Why should a state's representing itself *feel* like anything? A state that represented itself non-derivatively, specifically, and essentially yet had no felt character seems conceivable; if so, self-representation is not sufficient for phenomenality. Levine's own preferred primitive is **acquaintance**, not representation.

**The "same old" critique (Weisberg).** Jonathan Weisberg argues that the same-order theory does not secure the very intimacy it was built to deliver: the self-representing component and the represented component still stand in a representational relation that could in principle misfire, so the structure is "same old" functional machinery. The dilemma he presses cuts both ways — if the intimacy objection fails against higher-order theory, self-representationalism loses its main motivation; if it succeeds, it wounds self-representationalism too.

**Internal misrepresentation.** Dropping the second state may not dissolve misrepresentation so much as relocate it *inside* the complex state. On part-whole versions, the self-directed part can still misdescribe the world-directed part — the mismatch moves in rather than away. Strict-identity versions escape this only by struggling to say how a state represents itself *as* anything without parts.

**Regress (Williford, Zahavi).** Kenneth Williford — himself a defender — catalogues three regress arguments the self-awareness thesis faces (an extensive regress of states, an intensive regress of contents, and the Fichte-Henrich-Shoemaker regress of first-person reference). The phenomenologist Dan Zahavi presses harder: pre-reflective self-awareness is real and ubiquitous but *not representational at all* — treating a state as even a secondary object of itself already objectifies consciousness and reopens the regress. The Map sides with Zahavi here while crediting Kriegel's structural analysis of the datum (see [self-and-self-consciousness](/concepts/self-and-self-consciousness/)).

## The Drift Toward Acquaintance

The strongest dialectical point against the theory comes from inside it. Its most sophisticated defenders have been migrating from *representation* to *acquaintance* — a primitive, non-representational self-presence that Williford's regress analysis treats as an "irreducibly circular" relation-instance. Williford's later work makes acquaintance, not representation, the bearer of subjective character. Anna Giustina's "An Acquaintance Alternative to Self-Representationalism" (*Philosophical Studies*, 2022) argues that phenomenal consciousness is fundamentally explained by acquaintance and only partly, if at all, by representation — marking the live successor debate as self-representation *versus* inner acquaintance, no longer self-representation versus higher-order theory. Kriegel's own recent work engages increasingly with acquaintance.

The Map reads this drift as a concession: the theory's own resources proved insufficient for for-me-ness, and its defenders reached for a primitive presence that representation could not supply. That is precisely the terrain of [phenomenal presentation](/concepts/phenomenal-presentation/) — the felt showing-up of experience that outruns any structural or representational reconstruction.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Self-representationalism engages the Map's **[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)** tenet most directly. The theory is an explicitly reductive programme: it aims to naturalise subjective character as a species of representation, applying causal-informational semantics to a synchrony-integrated state. The Map grants the structural achievement — reflexive self-directedness is a real and underdescribed feature of the *for-me* character of experience — but declines the reduction. A structural description of self-intimation tells us how experience is *organised*, not why the organisation is *lived*. The theory re-sites the hard problem inside the self-directed content: the question "why is this representing felt rather than dark?" survives the move from two states to one. This is the same fork the Map's higher-order-theories treatment runs against HOROR and reality-monitoring accounts — either the self-directed representation is itself felt (presupposing phenomenality) or it is not (leaving the gap intact).

The empirical scaffolding adds a second reservation. Kriegel's naturalisation leans on binding-by-synchrony, whose standing in neuroscience has weakened; a theory whose reduction is hostage to a contested integration mechanism inherits that fragility.

The connection to **[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)** is more oblique. Self-representationalism, like other functionalist reductions, tends to leave conscious states doing no distinctive causal work beyond their neural vehicles — the felt for-me-ness rides along on processes that would run identically without it. The Map holds that consciousness is causally efficacious, so it reads the acquaintance drift as pointing the right way: if presence is primitive rather than a representational achievement, it is the kind of feature that could be a genuine relatum in the mind-world relation rather than a redundant gloss on information processing.

The Map's verdict: self-representationalism is the best structural map yet drawn of *that* experience is for-a-subject, and it fails, informatively, to explain *why*.
