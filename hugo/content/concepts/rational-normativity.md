Rational normativity is the prescriptive dimension of reasoning — the fact that logical and evidential relations dictate what one *ought* to believe. When premises entail a conclusion, the conclusion is not merely what a thinker *will* believe but what they *should* believe. Physical processes lack this prescriptive character entirely. Neurons fire or do not fire; they are never correct or incorrect. The Unfinishable Map holds that rational normativity cannot be reduced to physical description and that its existence provides an independent line of support for [dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/).

## Definition and Scope

Rational normativity refers specifically to the norms governing belief formation and inference — what epistemologists call *epistemic normativity*. It is the "ought" that binds a thinker who grasps that a conclusion follows from true premises. Three features distinguish it from other kinds of normativity:

**Prescriptive force.** Logical relations do not describe what happens when someone reasons; they prescribe what should happen. A rock that falls at an unexpected rate reveals an error in the observer's model, not a failure of the rock. A reasoner who affirms the premises of a valid argument but denies the conclusion violates a genuine norm. The norm exists independently of whether anyone follows it.

**Universality.** The validity of *modus ponens* does not vary with neural architecture, historical period, or species. A brain organised differently might fail to track the inference, but the inference itself remains valid. Physical processes are particular — they occur at specific locations and times. Rational norms are universal.

**Content-sensitivity.** Valid inference depends on what propositions *mean*, not on their physical encoding. "All humans are mortal" bears the same logical relations whether encoded in English neurons, Mandarin text, or silicon registers. Physical causation is sensitive to physical structure; logical validity is sensitive to semantic content.

These features collectively constitute what Wilfrid Sellars captured with his distinction between the "logical space of reasons" and the realm of natural law. To characterise a mental state as knowledge is not to give an empirical description but to locate it within a network of justificatory relations — relations that are normative rather than causal.

## Distinction from Other Normativities

Rational normativity is one species of a broader genus. Other normative domains include:

- **Moral normativity**: what one ought to *do* (ethical obligations, rights, duties)
- **Prudential normativity**: what one ought to do *for one's own good*
- **Aesthetic normativity**: standards of beauty, elegance, proportion

The Map's treatment of [normative force](/topics/consciousness-and-normative-force/) addresses the genus; rational normativity is the species concerned specifically with belief and inference. What makes it philosophically distinctive is its self-referential character: any attempt to explain away rational normativity must itself employ rational normativity. An argument that rational norms are illusory must claim to follow from premises to conclusion — invoking the very norms it seeks to eliminate. This is the [self-stultification](/topics/self-stultification-as-master-argument/) structure.

The concept also connects to [evaluative-phenomenal-character](/concepts/evaluative-phenomenal-character/). If [cognitive-phenomenology](/concepts/cognitive-phenomenology/) is real — if thinking has its own irreducible phenomenal character — then the "ought" of logical inference may be grounded in the phenomenal experience of logical insight, paralleling how moral obligation is grounded in evaluative qualia. The felt difference between a valid inference and a non sequitur, or between a well-supported belief and a wishful one, would be a species of phenomenal normativity. Rational normativity and moral normativity would then be two expressions of the same underlying phenomenon: consciousness doing normative work that no physical process can replicate.

## The Two Senses of "Because"

C.S. Lewis's revised [argument-from-reason](/topics/argument-from-reason/) sharpened the concept by distinguishing two senses of "because":

**Causal because**: "She believes it because neurons fired in pattern X." This explains the belief's *origin*.

**Evidential because**: "She believes it because the evidence supports it." This explains the belief's *justification*.

These are categorically different relations. A belief caused by neurons firing is not thereby justified. A belief justified by evidence is not thereby caused in the same sense. Rational normativity lives entirely in the evidential "because" — the relation that connects propositions as reasons for one another. The question is whether any physical description can capture this relation, or whether it requires something more.

## Why Physical Reduction Fails

Three influential strategies for reducing rational normativity to physical processes each fall short at the critical point:

**Reliabilism** replaces the normative question — "what *should* I believe?" — with a descriptive one — "what process reliably produces true beliefs?" But reliability is itself a normative notion. Which processes count as the same type? Reliable relative to which environments? What counts as truth? Answering these questions requires normative judgement. Reliabilism presupposes rational normativity rather than explaining it.

**[Functionalism](/concepts/functionalism/)** proposes that the right causal-functional organisation constitutes rule-following. The functionalist claims that justificatory relations *just are* certain complex causal patterns viewed at the right level of abstraction. But this move collapses the distinction it needs to preserve: if "A justifies B" reduces to "A causes B through the right functional pathway," the normative force disappears. A system that happens to behave as if it follows logical rules may be doing nothing more than running along causal tracks. The prescriptive dimension — the fact that the conclusion *ought* to follow — is not captured by any functional architecture, however sophisticated.

**Evolutionary teleology** appeals to natural selection: our cognitive faculties were shaped to track truth because accurate beliefs aid survival. But as Plantinga's evolutionary argument against naturalism observes, natural selection rewards adaptive behaviour, not true belief. An organism with systematically false beliefs that generate survival-enhancing behaviour would be equally fit. Evolution explains why we *tend to behave* as if we reason well; it does not explain why we *ought* to follow the evidence. Nagel presses the same point in *The Last Word*: our capacity for logical and mathematical reasoning outstrips anything natural selection could have targeted, suggesting that reason resists naturalistic reduction.

Each strategy attempts to account for rational normativity by redescribing it in non-normative terms. Each succeeds only by quietly smuggling normativity back in or by changing the subject from what one *should* believe to what one *happens* to believe.

## Parallel with the Explanatory Gap

The irreducibility of rational normativity parallels the [explanatory-gap](/concepts/explanatory-gap/) for [qualia](/concepts/qualia/). Just as no amount of physical description explains *why* there is something it is like to see red, no amount of physical description explains *why* evidence creates obligations on belief. Both gaps point to consciousness as introducing dimensions — phenomenality, normativity — that physical description cannot capture.

This parallel is not coincidental. If rational normativity depends on [conscious grasp](/concepts/cognitive-phenomenology/) of logical relations — on there being something it is like to *understand* that a conclusion follows — then the normative gap and the explanatory gap share a common source. Physical processes can correlate with rational inference, just as they can correlate with colour experience. But correlation is not constitution. The prescriptive force of logical norms, like the phenomenal character of experience, exceeds anything available in the physical vocabulary.

## Relation to Site Perspective

Rational normativity connects to the Map's [tenets](/tenets/) at multiple points.

**Dualism.** If rational normativity requires consciousness, and consciousness is irreducible to physical processes, then the normative dimension of reasoning provides an argument for dualism distinct from arguments based on qualia. The normative gap — between "neurons fired" and "one ought to believe" — is as resistant to physical closure as the explanatory gap between neural correlates and subjective experience.

**Bidirectional interaction.** Reasoning causally influences behaviour: we act on conclusions, revise plans in light of evidence, and speak our beliefs. If rational normativity requires consciousness, and reasoning shapes the physical world through action, then consciousness has genuine causal efficacy — a conclusion that also bears on [free will](/topics/free-will/). The self-stultification argument makes this vivid: if consciousness were [epiphenomenal](/concepts/epiphenomenalism/), our reports about reasoning would be causally disconnected from our actual rational processes, rendering them accidentally correct at best.

**Occam's razor has limits.** Physicalism appears simpler — one ontological category rather than two. But a worldview that cannot ground its own rational credentials is not genuinely simpler; it is incomplete. The [historical record](/concepts/parsimony-epistemology/) of parsimony shows that apparent simplicity often masks deeper inadequacy, and the failure to account for rational normativity is a case in point.
