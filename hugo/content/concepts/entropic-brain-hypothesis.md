The entropic brain hypothesis (EBH), introduced by Robin Carhart-Harris and colleagues in 2014, claims that the *entropy* of spontaneous brain activity indexes the informational richness of a conscious state. Ordinary waking experience sits in a narrow, constrained, reality-tested band; psychedelics raise neural entropy toward a [critical point](#brain-criticality) (explained below) and produce richer, less constrained experience. Loss of consciousness moves the brain the other way, into ordered, low-entropy dynamics. The hypothesis supplies something genuinely new to the neuroscience of consciousness: a *quantitative dynamical signature*—a number—that tracks how rich a conscious state is.

The Unfinishable Map's interest is precise. EBH delivers a correlation between a physical quantity and the *richness* of experience while saying nothing about why that activity is *felt* at all. That structure—a measure that tracks consciousness without explaining it—is metaphysically modular: the entropy–richness link survives intact whether one reads it as production or as a widening of the [filter's bandwidth](/concepts/filter-theory/). The Map adopts the empirical finding and resists the physicalist gloss its originators attach, engaging EBH as a rival framework rather than an ally.

## The Core Claim

Carhart-Harris et al. (2014) proposed that "the entropy of spontaneous brain activity indexes the informational richness of conscious states," bounded above and below by thresholds beyond which consciousness is lost. The framework revived a Freudian distinction in dynamical dress: **primary states** (high-entropy, less constrained—psychedelic experience, early psychosis, REM dreaming, infant cognition) versus **secondary states** (normal adult waking, constrained, reality-testing, metacognitive).

The striking move is the placement of ordinary consciousness. On EBH, normal waking is *slightly sub-critical*: its narrowness, its reality-testing, its metacognitive grip are products of *entropy suppression*. What feels like the baseline of consciousness is, on this account, a constrained special case. The markers offered for the approach to criticality include an expanded repertoire of brain states, reduced within-network integrity (notably in the [default mode network](/concepts/default-mode-network/)), increased between-network connectivity, and broader metastability.

This already rhymes with [filter theory](/concepts/filter-theory/). Where the production model expects richer experience to require more neural activity, EBH and the psychedelic data jointly report the reverse: desynchronized, less integrated, higher-entropy activity accompanies *more* experience, not less. The Map develops that reversal at length in [psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/); the contribution here is the entropy/criticality dimension that the filter-model article references but does not unpack.

## Brain Criticality {#brain-criticality}

The 2018 update, *The entropic brain – revisited*, recentred the theory on **brain criticality**: the idea that conscious dynamics operate near a critical point—an "edge of chaos" poised between ordered, periodic activity and disordered, chaotic activity. Critical systems exhibit maximal metastability, heightened sensitivity to perturbation, and avalanche dynamics whose sizes follow power laws. Self-organized criticality, formalised in physics by Bak, Tang and Wiesenfeld (1987), supplies the dynamical vocabulary.

Independent work has since linked near-criticality to conscious level. Toker et al. (2022) report that conscious states are supported by near-critical cortical electrodynamics; cortico-thalamic information transfer peaks near criticality. Anaesthesia drives the system away from the critical point—but asymmetrically. Propofol sharply restricts avalanche size and collapses dynamical complexity, while ketamine preserves more awake-like, near-critical dynamics. That dissociation matters for the Map: it mirrors the agent-versus-substrate pattern explored in [anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface](/topics/anaesthesia-and-the-consciousness-interface/), where different anaesthetics suppress experience by different routes. In 2025, work on DMT reported that shifts in criticality correlate with the *degree of self-dissolution*, tying a dynamical measure to a specific phenomenological dimension rather than to "consciousness" wholesale.

Criticality refines EBH's central quantity but inherits its limit. It specifies, with real precision, *the dynamical regime in which rich consciousness occurs*. It does not specify why occupying that regime is *like anything*. A near-critical system is a system at a particular point on the order–chaos axis; nothing in the power-law statistics of neural avalanches entails experience. The signature is sharp; the explanatory gap is untouched.

## Where the Measure Is Contested

A concept page on EBH that presented entropy as a settled quantity would misrepresent the field. Even the *physical* measure at the theory's core is disputed.

Papo (2016) argued that entropy—a measure of randomness—may be ill-qualified in principle to characterise brain activity, which is neither fully regular nor fully random. The favoured operationalisations can collapse into one another: "metastability," defined as variance in network synchrony, may merely *count* metastable states rather than measure criticality, and entropy, metastability and variance can carry the same information under different names. A genuine configurational entropy would require extensive exploration of the system's phase space; the measures actually used, Papo warned, may capture only "the random side of complexity." Lempel–Ziv complexity, a flagship EBH measure, has in some later studies yielded among the *weakest* associations with conscious level, raising replication and robustness questions.

This is not a peripheral quibble. It bears directly on the Map's [meta-epistemology of limits](/voids/meta-epistemology-of-limits/): having a number is not having an explanation, and when the number's own construction is contested, even the descriptive achievement is provisional. The debate over whether entropy *indexes*, *constitutes*, or merely *correlates with* conscious richness remains live, and the field's drift toward multidimensional taxonomies of consciousness reflects dissatisfaction with any single-scalar account.

## The Predictive-Processing Extension

In 2019, Carhart-Harris and Friston folded EBH into the **REBUS** model ("Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics," the "anarchic brain"). On this account, psychedelics relax the precision of high-level priors, liberating bottom-up information flow; the narrative self is itself a high-level prior whose loosening underlies ego dissolution. REBUS recasts the entropy rise as a relaxation of hierarchical Bayesian control.

The Map treats REBUS as supplying *mechanism*, not *metaphysics*. Its formalism is metaphysically neutral and compatible with—but not discriminating in favour of—the filter reading, exactly as [predictive-processing-and-dualism](/topics/predictive-processing-and-dualism/) argues for predictive processing generally. Critics including Letheby press a fully deflationary reading on which prior-relaxation and entropy *exhaust* the phenomenology within physicalism. That engagement is developed in [psychedelics-and-the-filter-model](/topics/psychedelics-and-the-filter-model/) and is not re-run here; this page cross-links rather than duplicates it. Later refinements—Safron's ALBUS (2025), which adds bidirectional, dose-dependent precision changes—respond partly to the standing charge that the predictive-processing framework has enough free parameters to fit almost any result, an open falsifiability concern the Map registers rather than resolves.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The entropic brain hypothesis is a *physicalist* theory, and the Map engages it as one. Carhart-Harris and Friston build EBH and REBUS to explain psychedelic action *without* any non-physical interface; the empirical neuroimaging may be cited, but the originators do not share—and REBUS was constructed to avoid—the Map's dualist conclusion. The findings are adopted; the framework is engaged as a rival the altered-state evidence does not discriminate against.

What the Map draws from EBH bears on its first tenet, [Dualism](/tenets/#dualism). The theory's own structure separates two things materialism usually runs together: a *physical correlate of richness* (entropy, criticality) and *phenomenality* (that the activity is felt). EBH delivers the first with growing precision and is silent on the second. The entropy–richness correlation can be re-described, with no loss of empirical fit on the altered-state data, as a description of the [filter's bandwidth](/concepts/filter-theory/): raising entropy toward criticality widens the aperture and admits richer content; lowering it narrows the aperture toward the constrained, reality-tested band of ordinary waking. Read this way, criticality is the *dynamical condition under which the consciousness interface operates at maximal bandwidth*, not the *source* of consciousness. This re-description is an interpretive overlay, empirically equivalent to the production reading on this evidence alone—not a discriminating prediction, and the Map marks it as such per the [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/). The direction of the evidence coheres with the filter reading; it does not by itself evidence filter theory over its physicalist rivals.

The theory also illustrates [the limits of parsimony](/tenets/#occams-limits). EBH is attractively economical: one quantity, monotonically tracking conscious richness, spanning psychedelics, dreaming, psychosis, and anaesthesia. But its economy is descriptive, not explanatory—and when even the favoured measure is contested (above), the apparent simplicity may reflect the coarseness of a single scalar rather than a real unification. The Map declines to let "we have a number that tracks richness" be heard as "we have explained richness."
