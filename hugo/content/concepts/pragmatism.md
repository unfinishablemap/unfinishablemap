Pragmatism is the philosophical tradition that evaluates ideas by their practical consequences — what difference does believing this make to how we act, inquire, and live? Founded in 19th-century America by Charles Sanders Peirce, William James, and John Dewey, pragmatism rejects the idea that truth consists in mirroring an independent reality. Instead, truth is what works: beliefs earn their status by guiding action successfully, resolving genuine problems, and opening productive lines of inquiry. The Unfinishable Map finds in pragmatism a method that, when applied consistently to consciousness, supports [interactionist-dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) rather than the [materialism](/concepts/materialism/) most contemporary pragmatists assume.

## Origins and Core Method

### Peirce's Pragmatic Maxim

Charles Sanders Peirce formulated the pragmatic maxim in 1878: to clarify an idea's meaning, consider what practical effects the idea's object might have. The concept of a thing is the sum of its conceivable practical consequences. Peirce intended this as a method for making philosophical disputes precise, not as a theory of truth. If two positions have identical practical consequences, the difference between them is merely verbal.

Applied to the mind-body problem, Peirce's maxim asks: what observable difference would it make if consciousness were reducible to physical processes versus irreducible? If [materialism](/concepts/materialism/) and [dualism](/concepts/interactionist-dualism/) produce different expectations about what we can explain, predict, or do, the dispute is genuine. If they don't, it's idle metaphysics. The Map holds the dispute is genuine — the positions diverge on what science can achieve, on moral responsibility, on personal identity, and on the meaning of death.

### James's Expansion

[William James](/topics/william-james-consciousness/) transformed pragmatism from a method of clarification into a theory of truth. An idea is true insofar as it is useful in navigating experience. This does not mean truth is whatever we want it to be — beliefs must satisfy experiential constraints and cohere with other beliefs. But among beliefs that meet these constraints, practical fruitfulness is the mark of truth, not correspondence to an unobservable reality.

James applied this directly to consciousness. In "Human Immortality" (1898), he argued that the transmissive view of consciousness — the brain as filter rather than generator — has different practical consequences from the production view. If consciousness is transmitted rather than produced, death may not be annihilation, human dignity acquires a non-biological basis, and consciousness has a status in the universe that materialism denies. These consequences matter. The pragmatist cannot dismiss dualism as a default.

In "The Will to Believe" (1896), James added that when a choice is forced, living, and momentous — when we must act on some assumption, when both options have genuine appeal, and when the stakes are high — we may choose based on our nature and needs rather than waiting for conclusive evidence. The consciousness question meets all three conditions.

### Dewey and Inquiry

John Dewey shifted pragmatism's focus from individual belief to the process of inquiry. Knowledge is not a passive reflection of reality but an active reconstruction undertaken by organisms interacting with their environments. A "situation" becomes problematic, inquiry transforms it, and the result is a "warranted assertion" — not a timeless truth but a claim that has survived the test of intelligent practice.

Dewey's emphasis on inquiry as an activity conducted by conscious agents carries implications he did not fully pursue. Dewey himself was a committed naturalist who would have resisted the dualist conclusion — but the logic of his position outruns his commitments. If inquiry is not passive reception but active engagement, then the inquirer's conscious experience — their felt sense of a problem, their recognition that a proposed solution works — is constitutive of the process. Reduce inquiry to physical mechanism and the normative dimension disappears: the system merely processes, with no standpoint from which to assess whether processing has resolved anything.

## Pragmatism After the Founders

### Neopragmatism

Richard Rorty's neopragmatism abandoned the founders' interest in experience. For Rorty, pragmatism means giving up the very idea of "getting reality right." There are no privileged descriptions of the world — only vocabularies that serve different purposes. This linguistic turn pushed consciousness to the margins: if philosophy should stop worrying about the relation between mind and world, the mind-body problem dissolves rather than demands solution.

The Map disagrees. Rorty's move works only if consciousness *can* be dissolved — if phenomenal experience is just one more vocabulary rather than a datum that any adequate vocabulary must accommodate. But the [hard problem](/topics/hard-problem-of-consciousness/) persists precisely because consciousness is not a description of the world but the condition under which description occurs. Dissolving the vocabulary does not dissolve the experience. The [reflexive gap](/topics/consciousness-defeats-explanation/) between explanation and understanding marks the same boundary from a different direction.

### Putnam's Pragmatic Realism

Hilary Putnam's pragmatic realism, developed in *Reason, Truth and History* (1981), steers between metaphysical realism and Rorty's relativism. Truth is what would be justified under epistemically ideal conditions — conditions involving conscious rational agents who can distinguish intended from unintended interpretations. Putnam's model-theoretic argument shows that reference cannot be fixed by physical-causal relations alone; a conscious interpreter is needed to pin down what our words mean. This makes consciousness ineliminable from the framework within which science operates, even if Putnam himself did not draw the dualist conclusion.

## Pragmatism and the Map's Framework

The Map's article on [pragmatism's path to dualism](/topics/pragmatisms-path-to-dualism/) develops the full argument. The core insight is reflexive: pragmatism evaluates ideas by their practical consequences, but evaluation is a conscious activity. The felt sense that one framework illuminates while another obscures — [cognitive-phenomenology](/concepts/cognitive-phenomenology/) — is what gives pragmatic assessment its normative force. If consciousness reduces to physical processes, evaluation reduces to mechanism, and "what works" collapses into "what happens to occur." The pragmatic criterion cannot validate itself from within a framework that reduces it to physics.

This parallels the [argument-from-reason](/topics/argument-from-reason/): if beliefs are fully explained by physical causes, no belief is rationally justified, including materialism. The pragmatic version makes the same structural move in terms of evaluation rather than reasoning.

## Relation to Site Perspective

The Unfinishable Map treats pragmatism as a method that confirms its commitments when applied consistently.

**[Dualism](/tenets/#dualism)**: Pragmatism's reflexive application supports dualism because materialism about consciousness fails pragmatism's own test. A theory that eliminates the evaluative standpoint on which theory-choice depends is self-undermining — not abstractly incoherent, but practically self-defeating by the pragmatist's own criterion.

**[Bidirectional Interaction](/tenets/#bidirectional-interaction)**: If explanation is agent-relative — as van Fraassen's pragmatic theory holds — then conscious evaluation has causal consequences. Scientists choose theories, design experiments, and communicate results based on their conscious assessment. If that assessment were epiphenomenal, the success of science would be inexplicable.

**[Occam's Razor Has Limits](/tenets/#occams-limits)**: Pragmatism provides the natural articulation of this tenet. Parsimony is one theoretical virtue among many. When the "simpler" theory eliminates the standpoint from which simplicity is assessed, pragmatism sides with the richer ontology.
