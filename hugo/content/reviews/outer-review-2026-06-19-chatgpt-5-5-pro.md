### Overall verdict

The article is strongest as an **internal coherence defense**: given the Map's preferred machinery, it explains why the framework does not automatically entail spoon-bending, RNG control, or telepathy. It is not yet a strong **independent boundary argument**. The article repeatedly says the inference is conditional and not an evidential upgrade, which is good calibration; however, the very conditions doing the work—brain-local attention, neural representation, developmental pairing, and quantum outcome-selection—are already Map commitments or downstream speculative mechanisms. The boundary is said to follow from the mechanism, but the mechanism is already specified in a way that privileges the subject's own brain. The surrounding "Brain Interface Boundary" article is more candid, admitting that its criteria are descriptive from one observed case—brains—and that the charge of reverse-engineering is legitimate; the target article should inherit that caveat explicitly. ### Where evidence is insufficient or overstated

The phrase "external systems face even more severe decoherence challenges than neural tissue" is not safe as written. Neural tissue is itself the paradigmatic hard case for quantum coherence; Tegmark's classic calculation puts neural/microtubule decoherence times around 10^-13 to 10^-20 seconds, far shorter than relevant neural timescales. [topics/brain-specialness-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/brain-specialness-boundary/) An external lab quantum system or quantum RNG need not be more decoherence-challenged than warm biological tissue. The article's real argument is not "external systems decohere more," but "external systems are not in the subject's attentional/control-loop coupling map." That sentence should be replaced.

The "no sensory feedback" claim is too broad. Many RNG and BCI experiments do give feedback: the subject sees outputs, receives scores, adjusts intention, and may train over time. The stronger claim should be that feedback to a conscious representation of the **already-measured output** is not the same as pre-collapse access to the target quantum alternative. As written, the article risks conflating ordinary sensory coupling with the special psychophysical coupling it needs to defend. [topics/brain-specialness-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/brain-specialness-boundary/)

The PEAR comparison is too quick. The article says that if the Map's mechanism extended externally, external effects should be "robust and reliable—comparable to the effects consciousness has on motor neurons." [topics/brain-specialness-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/brain-specialness-boundary/) A psi advocate could deny that. They might posit a weak, bandwidth-limited, feedback-starved, distance-independent, or decline-prone channel rather than a motor-strength channel. The better claim is narrower: **the Map's own Stapp/control-loop model predicts no robust external effect**; rival parapsychological models may predict small or unstable effects, but then those models need their own coupling law and replication record.

The statement that "the empirical record supports internal effects and challenges external claims" overstates what the internal evidence establishes. Motor control, attention, and neuroplasticity show robust mind-brain correlations and agency-compatible phenomena, but they do not discriminate quantum-dualist selection from physicalist motor-control accounts. The site's "Interactionist Dualism" page itself admits that NCC/correlation evidence is shared and non-discriminating. [topics/brain-specialness-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/brain-specialness-boundary/) The target article should say: "The empirical record supports robust conscious-brain coupling in the ordinary neuroscientific sense; it does not by itself support quantum-dualist selection."

The developmental/evolutionary section needs more care. It says there was no selection pressure for influence over external quantum systems because such influence would produce no fitness advantage without sensory feedback. [topics/brain-specialness-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/brain-specialness-boundary/) That is not airtight: if external influence were reliable, sensory feedback and fitness advantage could co-evolve; tool use and prosthetic incorporation show that external systems can become control-loop participants. The article should retreat to the more defensible pairing claim: no known external apparatus has undergone the right lifelong co-adaptive psychophysical coupling with the subject.

### Citation and literature audit

No reference in the article appears obviously fabricated; the site's own June 11 review says all four listed references were re-verified. [GitHub](https://github.com/unfinishablemap/unfinishablemap/commit/f85a119cfee49177cf807fc53c1266013fdc774d) The problem is **bibliographic thinness and currency**, not basic existence. A 2026 article on consciousness-collapse, PK, and brain-locality should not rely on only Jahn & Dunne 2005, Nelson et al. 2002, Park 2000, and Stapp 2007. [topics/brain-specialness-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/brain-specialness-boundary/)

For parapsychology/RNGs, the article should cite Bösch, Steinkamp, and Boller's 2006 Psychological Bulletin meta-analysis directly. That meta-analysis combined 380 RNG intention studies, found a statistically significant but very small overall effect, and reported strong inverse relations between effect size and sample size plus extreme heterogeneity—precisely the context the article needs. [ERIC](https://eric.ed.gov/) It should also cite Maier, Dechamps, and Pflitsch 2018, a large Bayesian quantum-RNG study reporting evidence against micro-PK; the article currently gestures at decades of weak evidence but does not anchor the strongest modern null-facing source. [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.00379/full)

For the Global Consciousness Project, Nelson et al. 2002 is historically relevant but stale if the article mentions the ongoing GCP as a live programme. There is now GCP 2.0 claimant-side material and a research-library trail listing 2023–2024 GCP2 items. These sources should not be weighted as independent confirmation, but they should be acknowledged if the article wants to be current about what GCP proponents now claim. [GCP2](https://gcp2.net/files/20240419215100-Global_Consciousness_Proj_Dialogo.pdf)

For consciousness-collapse theory, the article should cite Chalmers and McQueen 2022. Their proposal explicitly combines a mathematical consciousness theory with collapse dynamics, says simple versions are falsified by the quantum Zeno effect, and treats quantum-computer tests as possible in principle. That is directly relevant to a site defending consciousness-causes-collapse, especially because the target article leans on Stapp/Zeno machinery while the site's tenets elsewhere prefer post-decoherence selection. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/book/44484/chapter/392574703)

For the Stapp/Zeno mechanism, the article should cite Georgiev 2015 or at least route readers to the site page that does. Georgiev's simulations argue that the quantum Zeno effect breaks down for timescales longer than brain decoherence time, leaving Stapp-style models physically implausible unless a decoherence-free subspace or other escape route is supplied. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1412.4741) The target article mentions decoherence only in passing; that is too light for a mechanism on which the boundary argument substantially depends.

For neural quantum plausibility, the article can point to the adjacent "Brain Interface Boundary" page, which cites Tegmark, Hagan/Hameroff, Fisher, and Khan et al. 2024. That page handles the 2024 microtubule/anesthesia result better than the target article would likely do by itself: it says the rat anesthesia finding shows microtubule function affects anesthetic sensitivity but does not discriminate quantum from non-quantum mechanisms. [concepts/brain-interface-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/brain-interface-boundary/)

### Missed or under-engaged counterarguments

The first under-engaged counterargument is **nonspatial dualist influence**. The site's "Locality" article argues that consciousness, as a non-physical posit, need not have a spatial location and that strict locality may be an imported physicalist constraint. [concepts/locality](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/locality/) But the target article relies on brain-locality to block PK. That is a tension. The site needs a bridge principle: "consciousness is not spatially located, but lawful psychophysical coupling is scoped by a subject-specific neural interface." Without that, locality is loosened when defending mind-brain causation and tightened when blocking psychokinesis.

The second is **external systems with feedback**. A BCI, prosthetic limb, closed-loop neurofeedback system, or real-time RNG interface can become represented, trained, and feedback-integrated. Recent BCI/prosthesis literature explicitly treats embodiment in terms of ownership, body representation, agency, and closed-loop sensory-motor integration. [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neurorobotics/articles/10.3389/fnbot.2022.902162/pdf) The article briefly mentions BCIs only as a future challenge, but it needs a fuller distinction between "classical control of an external device through neural outputs" and "direct psychophysical selection of quantum outcomes inside the device."

The third is **tool incorporation and extended mind**. The article treats "own brain" as the boundary, while nearby pages allow "possibly bodily processes mediated through interoception" and future BCIs after gradual integration. [concepts/brain-interface-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/brain-interface-boundary/) That weakens any skull-boundary formulation. The real boundary may be "subject-specific interface system," not "brain only." If so, the title and summary should be softened.

The fourth is **telepathy as a different case from RNG-PK**. The target article says another person's brain is no more integrated into your conscious control loop than a random number generator is. [topics/brain-specialness-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/brain-specialness-boundary/) That is plausible but underargued. A telepathy proponent can say another brain is already a consciousness-interface system, and social cognition supplies rich predictive models of other minds. The article should not treat mind-to-mind claims as automatically ruled out "for the same reason" as external apparatus; it should either exclude them from scope or give a separate boundary argument.

The fifth is **Born-preserving influence**. The Map's tenets say its preferred corridor-dualist path preserves Born statistics and is empirically indistinguishable from chance in aggregate tests. [tenets](https://unfinishablemap.org/tenets/) But the target article uses the absence of reliable RNG deviations as alignment with brain-locality. That is coherent only if external PK would have to produce Born-rule deviation. If a hypothetical external consciousness-selection channel also preserved Born statistics, RNG null results would not rule it out. The article should distinguish "no external influence at all" from "no detectable aggregate deviation."

The sixth is **mechanism variance**. The site distinguishes post-decoherence outcome selection, pre-decoherence collapse/Zeno mechanisms, Process-1/context-selection, and authority-selecting/delegatory laws. [tenets](https://unfinishablemap.org/tenets/) The target article uses Stapp/Zeno language to draw the boundary, but the broader site says post-decoherence selection is the strongest currently endorsed route. If the Stapp/Zeno model fails, does the brain-specialness boundary survive? Probably yes, but the article needs to show how each candidate mechanism supports or weakens the same boundary.

### Is the dualist framing protected by convenient bracketing?

Partly, yes. The article is honest that its argument is conditional, but the bracket is large: it brackets dualism, collapse, consciousness-to-physics coupling, attention as the mental variable, outcome-selection, and the special status of neural systems. The Tenets page itself says the five commitments are "chosen starting points, not proofs," and it admits that post-decoherence corridor dualism is interpretive and empirically indistinguishable from chance. [tenets](https://unfinishablemap.org/tenets/)

The result is a useful internal answer, not a public-facing proof. A skeptical reader can reasonably say: "You have not shown why consciousness, if nonspatial, cannot select external outcomes; you have selected a coupling model whose eligibility conditions already restrict selection to the subject's brain." The surrounding "Psychophysical Laws" article names this exact issue: without specifying which phenomenal variables map to which physical selections, "consciousness selects" remains a metaphor rather than a research programme. [concepts/psychophysical-laws](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/psychophysical-laws/)

The article's best repair is not to pretend it has solved the boundary from neutral premises. It should explicitly mark itself as a **scope clause inside the Map's psychophysical coupling programme**: "If the coupling law is attention-bounded, representation-mediated, and subject-paired, then external PK does not follow." That would be stronger and more honest than "the boundary follows from the mechanism," because it makes clear that the mechanism is itself a speculative, bracketed posit.

It links to brain-interface-boundary, interactionist dualism, quantum consciousness, Stapp, attention, conservation, embodied cognition, and predictive processing. [concepts/brain-interface-boundary](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/brain-interface-boundary/) The target article should import that threshold rather than using only qualitative "robust external PK" language.

The biggest coherence gap is with "Locality." [concepts/locality](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/locality/) The target article then uses locality-like constraints to block external influence. This is not fatal, but it requires an explicit distinction between **spatial locality** and **psychophysical eligibility**. At present, the site risks using locality flexibly: permissive when defending interactionism, restrictive when avoiding parapsychology.

The second coherence gap is with "Psychophysical Laws and Coupling." That page contains the mature framework the target article needs: mental-side variables, physical-side target variables, bandwidth, scope constraints, Born compatibility, and amplification. [concepts/psychophysical-laws](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/psychophysical-laws/) The target article should be repositioned as an application of that coupling-law framework, not as a free-standing answer.

The third coherence gap is with the site's own mechanism hierarchy. The Tenets page says the strongest current path is post-decoherence selection, while Stapp/Zeno is a live fallback. [tenets](https://unfinishablemap.org/tenets/) The target article's argument is written as if Zeno/attention is the central mechanism. It needs a mechanism matrix: "Under Stapp/Zeno, boundary follows this way; under post-decoherence selection, it follows only if the coupling map is subject-specific; under Process-1/context-selection, the boundary is different; under delegatory/authority-selection, PK may not even be the right category."
