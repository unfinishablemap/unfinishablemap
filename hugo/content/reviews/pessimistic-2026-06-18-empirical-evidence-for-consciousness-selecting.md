## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist
Churchland's strongest line lands on the **comparative-cognition section**. The article says the human/great-ape gap "tracks precisely those capacities associated with conscious processing" — but "associated with" is doing enormous work. The eliminativist reply: the gap tracks *prefrontal connectivity, working-memory capacity, and recursive syntax*, all specifiable without a consciousness vocabulary at all. To call the differentiator "conscious access" is to smuggle the folk-psychological category back in as the explanans when it is exactly what is up for elimination. The article's own "does not establish" subsection concedes physicalism accommodates DeWall, but it does not concede the deeper point: the eliminativist denies that "conscious processing" *names a real kind*, so the whole comparative argument can be restated without it and loses nothing. The article never engages elimination *of the category*, only reduction *of the property*.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist
Dennett's target is the **neurological line**, specifically Sjöberg's SMA-resection point ("patients retain the subjective sense of willing while losing execution"). The article treats the *persistence of the sense of willing* as evidence the experience and mechanism "come apart." Dennett: the persistence of a *report* of willing is exactly what a heterophenomenological account predicts — the patient's narrative-generating machinery is intact and confabulates a sense of agency over an action it cannot perform, just as split-brain and anosognosia patients confabulate. Far from dissociating experience from mechanism, it shows the *report* is a downstream neural product that survives because *its* machinery survives. The article's inference ("removing the machinery should eliminate the experience; it does not") quietly assumes the experience is something *over and above* the report — the very point at issue.

### The Quantum Skeptic
Tegmark would note the article has shifted the goalposts gracefully but not closed the gap. The cryptochrome and enzyme-tunnelling cases establish that *some* quantum effects survive in *some* warm biological structures *engineered by evolution for that purpose*. None of them is a neuron doing computation. The article's own honest line — "No experiment has demonstrated that disrupting neural quantum effects selectively impairs consciousness" — is the whole ballgame, and Tegmark would press that the burden has not moved: the 613 THz tubulin peak remains *computationally predicted and never measured*, and the article admits this. So the "categorical objection has collapsed" framing (line 89) is rhetorically stronger than the evidence: what collapsed is the claim *quantum effects can never be biologically functional anywhere*; what survives intact is the claim *no quantum effect has been shown functional in neural computation*. The article distinguishes these in the body but not in the section heading's confident "This objection has collapsed." (Editorial note: per the deep-review ledger this is a deliberate Map position; recorded, not queued.)

### The Many-Worlds Defender
Deutsch's objection is bedrock-level (Tenet 4) and the article correctly treats it so — the convergence table lists MWI under "Classical no-collapse" and the "What Would Weaken the Case" section concedes MWI confirmation would dissolve "selection." Deutsch would still press the sharper point the article half-buries: the entire convergence is *conditional on collapse being real*, and the article's own footnote admits the quantum-bio line "does not discriminate consciousness-selecting from the other collapse-realist interpretations." So to a no-collapse theorist the fourth line is not a defeater at all — it is a disagreement about which interpretation to hold *before* any of this evidence is weighed. The table's fourth column is doing axiomatic, not evidential, work, and a reader who does not already share the collapse-realist premise gets no traction from it. The article says this, but softly; Deutsch would say it is the load-bearing concession.

### The Empiricist
The Popperian objection is the most serious *as philosophy of science* and the article addresses it least. "What Would Weaken the Case" lists four defeaters, but examine them: "classical consciousness theory succeeds," "epiphenomenalism finds a mechanism for correlation," "MWI is confirmed" — these are defeats-by-rival-theory-winning, not *experiments that would falsify consciousness-selecting itself*. Only "neural quantum effects prove absent" is a near-empirical defeater, and it falsifies a *necessary substrate*, not the selection hypothesis directly (consciousness could still select among classically-determined coarse-grained outcomes on some readings). A Popperian concludes: the hypothesis is currently structured so that no observation falsifies *it specifically* — every defeater either requires a rival to succeed or removes a precondition. That is the falsifiability gap the deep-review ledger calls "inherent to framework stage." Fair — but the article presents "What Would Weaken the Case" as if it discharged the falsifiability worry, when on inspection three of its four items are about rivals winning, not about this hypothesis losing. See **Issue 2**.

### The Buddhist Philosopher
Nagarjuna's objection — that the selecting self the framework needs is itself empty — is out of scope per the ledger, and the article never claims to address it. Recorded for completeness only: the article's "consciousness evaluates and selects among competing options" (line 49) and the indexical "why this outcome?" (line 137) both presuppose a unified experiencer that does the selecting and asks the question. Madhyamaka denies there is any such locus; "selection" would then be one more dependently-originated process with no selector. No task — out of scope.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The "convergence" is a count of one evidential move, not four independent ones
- **Current content says**: four "independent empirical lines... converge," and the continental-drift analogy frames this as multiple independent confirmations narrowing to one survivor.
- **A critic would argue**: three of the four lines (evolutionary, comparative, neurological) establish *the same thing* — that consciousness is causally efficacious / not epiphenomenal — and are all equally compatible with non-reductive physicalism. They are not four independent arrows at *consciousness-selecting*; they are three arrows at *mental causation* plus one (quantum-bio) at *substrate possibility*. The genuine work — discriminating consciousness-selecting from rival collapse-realist readings — is done by *none* of them and is explicitly deferred to the post-decoherence-selection programme.
- **Suggested response**: the article already concedes this in the convergence paragraph ("removes a competitor... does not single it out from its true rivals"). The concession could be promoted nearer the continental-drift analogy, which currently overstates the independence before the qualification arrives. Low severity — the qualification *is* present, just downstream of the strong framing.

### Anaesthetic-disruption correlation is non-specific (already conceded, but the table overstates)
- **Current content says**: the quantum-bio "does not establish" subsection admits "anaesthetics affect many cellular targets."
- **A critic would argue**: given that concession, the convergence-table row "Microtubule quantum effects may correlate with consciousness" should not be presented as quantum-bio *support*; the same anaesthetic data support any account on which anaesthetics suppress consciousness via *any* of their many targets.
- **Suggested response**: no change needed — the table's quantum-bio entry for consciousness-selecting is "Compatible," not "Supports," and the footnote already disclaims positive support. This is well-handled.
