# Referee Report: Empirical and Logical Vulnerabilities in the Non-Reductive Epistemology of the "Conjunction-Coalesce" Framework

## Introduction to the Conjunction-Coalesce Epistemology and Its Theoretical Ambitions

The manuscript under review advances a highly ambitious, systematically structured epistemology of consciousness aimed at defending a non-reductive, dualistic model of the mind. The core theoretical apparatus deployed by the author is a conceptual and methodological framework termed the "conjunction-coalesce". This framework operates by identifying distinct cognitive limits, opacities, or phenomenological absences—which the author terms "voids"—and structurally merging them. The central philosophical assertion of the manuscript is that when these structurally conjoined philosophical voids are coalesced, the resulting boundary or "seam" between them remains visible, load-bearing, and non-collapsible. By treating this persisting seam as an irreducible architectural feature rather than a mere artifact of incomplete scientific modeling, the author attempts to infer the existence of hard, non-physical constraints on human cognition.

The manuscript operationalizes this conjunction-coalesce framework across an impressive array of phenomenological domains. It attempts to unify disparate features of consciousness into a specialized seam-relationship sub-typology comprising five distinct architectural cells: causal-substrate, architectural, modal-categorical, evidential-triangulation, and reflexive. Within this typology, the manuscript interrogates specific cognitive phenomena, including the temporal asymmetry of episodic memory (the "Temporal Void"), the introspective opacity of mental effort (the "Effort Void"), the structural indeterminacy of concept application (the "Vagueness Void"), and the discontinuity of formal verification (the "Common Knowledge Void"). The explicit goal of this synthesis is to demonstrate that these voids cluster systematically around the interface between consciousness and the physical substrate, thereby providing abductive, if not deductive, evidence for a dualist architecture where phenomenal consciousness cannot be fully reduced to neurocomputational mechanisms.

However, a rigorous, line-by-line audit of the manuscript's empirical claims, neuroscientific citations, counterargument coverage, and logical inferences reveals profound structural vulnerabilities that fundamentally compromise its philosophical viability. The manuscript attempts to bridge the gap between abstract phenomenology and empirical cognitive science, but it does so by relying almost exclusively on obsolete empirical paradigms, severely outdated neuroimaging interpretations, and a systematic evasion of the contemporary 2020-2025 literature that effectively dissolves the very "seams" the author claims are irreducible.

Specifically, the manuscript misinterprets the nature of epistemic feelings by ignoring predictive processing and active inference models; it anchors its claims regarding mental time travel on heavily refuted, two-decade-old fMRI subtraction paradigms while ignoring modern multivariate pattern analysis; it leverages the literature on contemplative practice and introspective accuracy while completely bypassing the replication crisis in metacognition and the nuances of signal detection theory; it revives the defunct transient hypofrontality hypothesis to explain states of flow and mental effort, ignoring modern synchronization theories; and it fundamentally commits a category error by utilizing the Sorites paradox to translate representational noise and epistemic vagueness into ontological dualism.

Furthermore, the manuscript frequently insulates its core claims from falsification by artificially bracketing contested premises, shadowboxing against weak versions of illusionism, and failing to engage with the robust representationalist and functionalist counterarguments that currently dominate top-tier journals in the philosophy of mind and cognitive science. The methodology of the conjunction-coalesce, rather than revealing the architecture of consciousness, ultimately functions as an exercise in map-territory conflation—elevating linguistic and explanatory deficits to the status of positive metaphysical evidence. This report details these fatal vulnerabilities across the manuscript's primary theoretical domains, demonstrating why the proposed framework fails to meet the rigorous academic standards required for publication.

## The Phenomenology of Temporal Asymmetry and Autonoetic Consciousness

A foundational pillar of the manuscript's defense of non-reductive consciousness rests on its analysis of temporal orientation, formalized as the "Temporal Void." The author investigates the phenomenological asymmetry between remembering the past and anticipating the future, arguing that this asymmetry reveals a structural boundary in the architecture of the mind. To establish this, the manuscript focuses heavily on the concept of autonoetic consciousness—the distinctly human capacity to mentally travel through time and re-experience past events. The author asserts that episodic memory possesses an irreducible, non-functional "feeling of pastness," an epistemic feeling that serves as a phenomenal marker of having genuinely lived through an event.

### The Misappropriation of Epistemic Feelings

To substantiate the claim that the feeling of pastness is an irreducible quale that defies functionalist reduction, the manuscript heavily cites the work of De Brigard (2020), who famously characterized the phenomenology of remembering as an "epistemic feeling". The manuscript leverages this classification to argue that the dualist interpretation uniquely preserves this irreducible marker, presenting it as a structural seam that physicalist accounts can only artificially smooth over.

This application of De Brigard's framework betrays a superficial and highly selective engagement with the contemporary philosophy of memory. While De Brigard (2020) does indeed conceptualize remembering as an epistemic feeling, the manuscript egregiously omits the subsequent evolution of this framework and the explicit intent behind it. In the 2020-2025 literature, the classification of remembering as an epistemic feeling was specifically developed to *avoid* the very state-referent ontological commitments the manuscript seeks to establish. By framing pastness as an epistemic feeling, contemporary theorists argue that the cognitive system does not require direct ontological contact with the past (a dualistic or preservationist view of memory traces) but rather generates a metacognitive tag that accompanies constructive simulation.

Furthermore, the manuscript completely ignores the overwhelming shift toward predictive processing and active inference models in the explanation of epistemic feelings. In modern computational phenomenology, the "feeling of pastness" is not a fundamental, irreducible ontological primitive. It is the phenomenological output of a highly sophisticated, sub-personal source-monitoring mechanism operating via precision-weighting. The cognitive system continuously generates simulations of both past and future events using the same underlying constructive machinery. To distinguish between a simulation anchored in prior sensory prediction errors (a memory) and a novel simulation (imagination), the system applies a metacognitive heuristic. The feeling of pastness is simply the phenomenological realization of this precision-weighted tag.

### Continuism and the Dissolution of the Temporal Seam

By citing De Brigard while simultaneously bracketing the computational source-monitoring mechanisms that actually generate these epistemic feelings, the manuscript engages in severe theoretical cherry-picking. The author acknowledges that the epistemic feeling framework is technically "compatible with functionalism" but insists on maintaining a dualist reading without providing any independent empirical or logical justification for why the functionalist reduction fails.

This failure is compounded by the manuscript's total evasion of the "continuist" debate that defines the 2020s literature on episodic memory. Continuism posits that episodic memory and episodic future thinking rely on identical constructive mechanisms, differing only in their temporal orientation and the accompanying metacognitive tagging. If the feeling of pastness is merely a heuristic tag applied by a functional monitoring system—a position heavily supported by behavioral, computational, and neuroimaging evidence—the manuscript's claim that this feeling represents a hard structural "void" or an irreducible dualistic seam collapses entirely. The temporal asymmetry is an epistemic achievement of a representational system, not an ontological boundary mapping the limits of a non-physical consciousness.

## Neurobiological Foundations of Episodic Simulation and the Obsolescence of Subtraction Paradigms

The manuscript's reliance on outdated paradigms extends beyond abstract phenomenology into its interpretation of the underlying neurobiology of mental time travel. To ground the "Temporal Void" in empirical science, the author asserts that there is a fundamental neurobiological asymmetry between remembering the past and anticipating the future. The specific claim advanced is that "future simulation produces greater hippocampal activation than memory retrieval," which the author leverages as empirical proof of an architectural cognitive boundary.

### The Reliance on Outdated 2007 Neuroimaging

To support this massive ontological claim, the manuscript relies entirely on a single neuroimaging study: Addis, Wong, and Schacter (2007). From this 2007 finding, the manuscript extrapolates a deep, irreducible architectural limit regarding how consciousness must exert greater structural effort to construct the future compared to contacting the past.

Using a two-decade-old fMRI subtraction study to anchor a novel epistemology of consciousness is methodologically unacceptable for top-tier philosophical discourse in 2026. Over the past twenty years, the Constructive Episodic Simulation Hypothesis has undergone massive empirical refinement, driven by the advent of multivariate pattern analysis (MVPA), representational similarity analysis (RSA), and high-density neuroimaging techniques. The neuroscientific literature from 2020 to 2025 demonstrates conclusively that hippocampal activation is not strictly directional (past vs. future) as the manuscript claims.

### Constructive Demand versus Temporal Directionality

Contemporary consensus dictates that hippocampal engagement during episodic simulation is tied to the *vividness, novelty, spatial coherence, and constructive demand* of the simulated event, entirely independent of its temporal orientation. When subjects in modern experimental paradigms are asked to recall highly complex, fragmented past events that require significant recombination of disparate mnemonic details, hippocampal activation frequently mirrors or even exceeds the activation seen in standard future simulation tasks. Conversely, when subjects are asked to imagine highly routine, highly probable, or schema-congruent future events, the task requires minimal hippocampal engagement.

The manuscript fails to engage with any literature from the current decade that complicates the clean past/future asymmetry it desperately needs to maintain its "temporal void" construct. The early findings of Addis et al. (2007) were an artifact of the specific experimental paradigms of the time, which inherently compared the recall of highly consolidated, schema-supported past memories with the construction of highly novel, unstructured future scenarios. The differential BOLD signal was tracking cognitive effort and recombination demand, not the ontological directionality of time.

This strips the "Temporal Void" of its neurobiological foundation and exposes the conjunction-coalesce framework as reliant on highly selective, anachronistic science.

## Introspective Opacity, Contemplative Practice, and the Signal Detection Theory of Metacognition

To bridge the gap between empirical psychology and its non-reductive epistemology, the manuscript extensively relies on the literature surrounding contemplative practice, meditation, and introspective accuracy. The author utilizes these practices to probe what is termed the "Introspection-Architecture Void Cluster". The manuscript argues that while phenomenological reports do not natively license metaphysical upgrades on their own, the convergence of meditative reports, Husserl's regress, and cognitive phenomenology is highly suggestive of a non-reductive architectural ceiling. Specifically, the manuscript asserts that contemplative practice reliably enhances introspective accuracy, allowing highly trained subjects to access non-temporal forms of consciousness and directly verify the boundaries of the mind's architecture. To support this empirical claim, the manuscript heavily cites older literature, notably Fox et al. (2012) and Baird et al. (2014).

### The Conflation of Metacognitive Bias and Metacognitive Sensitivity

This section of the manuscript is fatally compromised by its reliance on an outdated and highly contested literature base, coupled with a fundamental misunderstanding of the psychophysics of introspection. The claim that mindfulness or meditation inherently improves objective metacognitive or introspective accuracy has been subjected to intense, rigorous scrutiny in the 2020-2025 period. This contemporary scrutiny has revealed highly inconsistent results, massive methodological flaws in early cross-sectional studies, and a widespread failure to replicate core findings regarding domain-general metacognitive enhancement.

Recent systematic reviews and highly controlled longitudinal studies (e.g., Treves et al., 2024; Zorn et al., 2020) demonstrate that the relationship between meditation and introspective accuracy is, at best, strictly domain-specific (e.g., localized solely to tactile sensitivity or breath monitoring), and at worst, a statistical artifact driven by cross-sectional self-selection biases and demand characteristics. While the manuscript attempts a weak epistemological hedge by noting that Fox et al. (2012) is cross-sectional and that "self-selection cannot be ruled out" , it nonetheless treats the observed correlation as load-bearing evidence for the proposition that phenomenal content is "genuinely accessible through trained attention".

This constitutes an egregious logical and methodological leap. The manuscript completely conflates two distinct psychological constructs that modern signal detection theory rigorously separates: *metacognitive bias* and *metacognitive sensitivity*. Metacognitive bias refers to a subject's overall confidence threshold—how willing they are to make a definitive introspective judgment. Metacognitive sensitivity (usually measured via formal metrics like d′ or meta-d′) refers to the actual statistical correlation between a subject's subjective confidence and their objective performance or physical reality.

Contemporary research into metacognitive training and meditation (e.g., Sella et al., 2023; Rouy et al., 2022) strongly indicates that these interventions frequently alter a practitioner's confidence threshold (bias) without actually improving their introspective access to underlying cognitive architecture (sensitivity). If prolonged meditation merely shifts a practitioner's decision criterion—making them more confident in reporting the absence of temporal structure or the existence of a "void"—this provides absolutely zero evidential support for the metaphysical claim that consciousness can actually "shed temporal structure".

### The Sim-to-Real Gap in Phenomenological Reporting

The manuscript entirely ignores the formal application of signal detection theory to contemplative science and the contemporary 2020-2025 literature that has systematically dismantled the simplistic equation of contemplative phenomenological reporting with objective architectural insight. By bracketing the intense ongoing debate over whether advanced meditative states are constructed (conceptually mediated) rather than discovered (raw access to structural seams), the manuscript assumes the very introspective transparency it elsewhere claims is bounded by "voids."

If the practitioner is merely adopting a different reporting threshold due to the demand characteristics of the contemplative tradition, the "phenomenological evidence" of an architectural seam is nullified. The manuscript conflates subjective certitude—a known artifact of intensive training—with objective architectural transparency, rendering its claims regarding the Introspection-Architecture Void epistemically inert.

## The Effort Void, Causal Machinery, and the Evasion of Adversarial Neuroscience

In its pursuit of mapping cognitive boundaries, the manuscript proposes another conjunction-coalesce cognate termed the "Effort Void." This void is predicated on the proposition that mental effort is fundamentally opaque to itself. The author posits a "phenomenal output and causal machinery dissociation," arguing that complex cognitive operations leave their fully-formed outputs in consciousness but systematically and structurally hide their generative causal operations. To empirically anchor this profound architectural claim, the manuscript relies on the citation of Naccache (2005), invoking a specific single-patient lesion case study (a patient with a left mesio-frontal lesion performing the Stroop task) to argue that phenomenal awareness of cognitive conflict can dissociate entirely from the causal machinery of executive control.

### The Obsolescence of the Naccache (2005) Double-Dissociation Paradigm

Using a 2005 focal lesion case study to make sweeping, non-reductive claims about the structural architecture of consciousness is scientifically anachronistic. The field of consciousness science and cognitive neurology has moved far beyond the simplistic double-dissociation paradigms of the early 2000s, which often falsely equated localized lesion deficits with discrete architectural boundaries. The manuscript's reliance on Naccache (2005) to establish an irreducible "seam" between phenomenal output and causal machinery completely ignores the most significant methodological and empirical developments in the study of consciousness in the current decade.

Specifically, the manuscript discusses Global Workspace Theory (GWT) and its variants as potential functionalist explanations for cognitive limits , but it conspicuously fails to cite or engage with the findings of the massive, multinational adversarial collaborations of the 2020s. The COGITATE Consortium (2025), for instance, directly tested the predictions of the Global Neuronal Workspace against Integrated Information Theory using massive, multimodal neuroimaging (fMRI, MEG, ECoG) in large, highly powered cohorts. These adversarial testing protocols explicitly evaluated the neural correlates of phenomenal output versus task-relevant operational machinery.

The findings from these rigorous adversarial protocols fundamentally challenge the manuscript's premise of a clean, structurally dualistic "dissociation" or an ontological "void." Instead of a metaphysical gap where operations disappear into a non-physical substrate, modern high-density temporal imaging reveals complex, recurrent cortico-cortical dynamics that continuously broadcast intermediate computations. The "opacity" of mental effort is not a structural metaphysical void; it is simply a metabolic bandwidth limitation of the fronto-parietal attention network.

By framing computational bandwidth limits and local representational constraints as dualist "seams," the manuscript engages in an unwarranted mystification of basic cognitive neuroscience. The author addresses the functionalist option by claiming that "cumulative convergence raises the explanatory cost of doing so without dissolving the functionalist option". This is rhetorical sleight-of-hand, not rigorous philosophical argument. The explanatory cost of functionalism has actually decreased dramatically with the advent of predictive processing, active inference, and sophisticated network dynamic models—a reality the manuscript avoids by citing twenty-year-old single-patient lesion data.

### The Collapse of the Transient Hypofrontality Hypothesis in Flow States

The manuscript's evasion of modern network dynamics is further evidenced in its treatment of deep absorption and mental effort, specifically regarding the phenomenology of "flow" states. To explain how consciousness interacts with extreme cognitive effort, the manuscript invokes the "transient hypofrontality hypothesis" (citing Dietrich, 2004). The author suggests that the suspension of prefrontal executive function during flow reveals an underlying, non-reductive phenomenological base—a state where the "void" is laid bare.

The transient hypofrontality hypothesis was a prominent, though speculative, theory in the early 2000s, suggesting that flow states are achieved through the massive downregulation of the prefrontal cortex, leading to a loss of self-reflective consciousness. However, the manuscript fails entirely to address the comprehensive paradigm shift that has occurred in the neuroscientific study of flow states in the 2020s.

Contemporary research has largely abandoned the simplistic hypofrontality model in favor of the *synchronization theory of flow* and advanced network-level integration models. Modern fMRI and EEG coherence studies demonstrate conclusively that flow does not involve a structural "void" or a simple shutdown of the prefrontal cortex. Rather, flow involves highly complex, optimized neural synchronization between the prefrontal cortex, the basal ganglia, and task-specific sensory-motor cortices. The prefrontal cortex is not "hypoactive" in a manner that implies an architectural cognitive boundary or a phenomenological seam; it is merely operating at a highly efficient, automated frequency that minimizes subjective friction and subjective effort.

By anchoring its philosophical claims regarding the structural limits of consciousness on Dietrich's 2004 hypofrontality hypothesis , the manuscript bases its "seam" on a neurobiological premise that is heavily refuted. If the "void" of self-consciousness during flow is actually the result of efficient global neural synchronization rather than a structural disconnection of causal machinery, the manuscript's attempt to use these states as evidence for non-reductive cognitive architecture fails entirely.

## Phenomenal Vagueness, the Sorites Paradox, and Probabilistic Population Coding

Perhaps the most theoretically ambitious, yet fundamentally flawed, section of the manuscript involves its formulation of the "Vagueness Void." The author constructs a conjunction-coalesce cognate featuring three structurally distinct faces (the Sorites face, the introspective face, and the higher-order face) to argue that almost every concept consciousness deploys admits to borderline cases that cannot be resolved from within. The manuscript uses this phenomenal vagueness to mount a direct argument against physicalism, aligning with contemporary philosophical arguments that suggest biological, physical systems cannot ground the sharp, irreducible boundaries required by non-vague phenomenal states.

### The Misapplication of the Sorites Paradox to Phenomenology

The manuscript deploys the classic Sorites paradox (the paradox of the heap) to argue that structural indeterminacy operates at the absolute boundary of every concept application. The underlying logical inference runs as follows: If physicalism is true, consciousness must arise gradually through evolution and biological development, resulting in objective borderline cases of consciousness (organisms or states that are only "partially" conscious). However, because the manuscript posits that true borderline cases of *phenomenal feel* are conceptually impossible (one either has a phenomenal experience or one does not; there is no such thing as being "slightly" phenomenally conscious in kind), physicalism must be inherently incapable of explaining phenomenal consciousness.

This argument is logically fragile and historically myopic, relying on an outdated understanding of how physical systems represent information. It entirely ignores the robust 2020-2025 literature on representational noise, semantic contextualism, and epistemic vagueness. Top-tier philosophers of mind and computational cognitive scientists (e.g., MacFarlane, 2020; Morrison, 2012) have systematically dismantled the assumption that vagueness requires an ontological gap or points to a non-physical primitive.

### Category Error: Epistemic Resolution Limits vs. Ontological Voids

In contemporary representationalist and predictive processing accounts, vagueness is understood as a fundamental feature of the *computational format* of the cognitive system, not a metaphysical property of the universe or an irreducible dualist "seam." The human visual, sensory, and conceptual systems encode information probabilistically, relying heavily on noisy population codes across neural networks. When a subject introspects and encounters phenomenal "vagueness" (e.g., the indistinct boundary of a color patch in peripheral vision, the exact onset of a feeling of anger, or the precise categorization of a Sorites-susceptible object), they are simply experiencing the system's own probabilistic uncertainty.

To formalize this in contemporary computational terms: if a neural population encodes a stimulus feature as a probability density function P(x) with a specific variance σ2, the phenomenological output will inherently possess a resolution limit directly proportional to that variance. The manuscript commits a severe philosophical category error by confusing *epistemic resolution limits* (the inability of the introspective system to read its own sub-personal probability distributions with infinite precision) with *ontological voids*.

The three faces of the vagueness void (Sorites, introspective, higher-order)  do not require a "seam-preserving merger" that defies physicalist or functionalist explanation. They are perfectly and exhaustively explained as the joint consequences of a single, well-understood architectural feature: the physical brain operates as a probabilistic inference engine limited by metabolic bandwidth, synaptic noise, and representational capacity constraints.

By failing to engage with any contemporary literature on computational models of vagueness, probabilistic population coding, or semantic contextualism , the manuscript sets up physicalism as a fragile straw man. It defeats a simplistic version of materialism that no serious cognitive scientist or philosopher of mind holds in the 2020s, rendering the entire "Vagueness Void" cognate philosophically inert.

## Structural and Logical Deficiencies: Bracketed Premises and Untested Counterarguments

A pervasive and fatal methodological weakness running throughout the entire manuscript is its tendency to "bracket" highly contested premises rather than defending them against standard contemporary counterarguments. This intellectual evasion is most evident in the manuscript's engagement with illusionism, meta-problem approaches to consciousness, and artificial intelligence.

### Shadowboxing with Illusionism

When the manuscript encounters the illusionist challenge—which argues that introspective reports of non-physical qualia are cognitive illusions generated by the brain's modeling processes—it simply classifies this as "Mode Two" reasoning. The author asserts that the illusionist's attempt to relocate the quale to a mere "seeming" (e.g., "seeming pastness") still leaves an unexplained residue, arguing that "seeming is still experiencing".

This retort completely ignores the extensive 2020-2025 literature on the meta-problem of consciousness. Strong illusionists do not leave a phenomenological residue; they provide detailed computational accounts of why the cognitive architecture inevitably *judges* and *reports* that there is a residue. By refusing to engage with the actual mechanism of the meta-problem—how introspection mischaracterizes its own representational formats as irreducible properties—the manuscript's "rejection cases"  are fundamentally hollow. The author is shadowboxing against weak, outdated versions of physicalism that lack sophisticated metacognitive components, ensuring the "dualist" alternative appears victorious only by default.

### The AI Introspection Control Fallacy

Furthermore, the manuscript's treatment of Artificial Intelligence as an "introspection control"  demonstrates a severe disconnect from contemporary computer science. The author argues that because an AI system's provenance, sampling, confidence, and source tags are structurally inspectable while the human brain's mechanisms are "dark," AI serves as a perfect contrast instrument to study introspective opacity.

This creates a false dichotomy that ignores the rapidly evolving field of artificial neural network interpretability. The internal feature representations of modern large language models (LLMs) and advanced neural networks are famously opaque, suffering from their own profound versions of polysemanticity and superposition that perfectly mimic human introspective opacity. The "darkness" of the brain is not a unique biological signature of a dualist interface; it is a generic mathematical consequence of highly complex, distributed, non-linear processing architectures. The manuscript's reliance on this supposed contrast demonstrates a lack of engagement with 2024-2026 mechanistic interpretability research, severely damaging its claim that human introspective opacities require a uniquely non-reductive, biological, or dualistic explanation.
