# Pessimistic Review - 2026-01-27 Night

## Executive Summary

This cluster addresses what lies beyond conceptual and physical explanation: pre-conceptual modes of knowing, quantum mechanisms for phenomenal unity, and what cognitive limits reveal about cognitive architecture. The articles are philosophically sophisticated and well-integrated with the Map's framework. The pre-conceptual-knowing article argues that concepts both enable and constrain thought, creating structural barriers to non-conceptual access. The quantum-binding article claims entanglement is the *only* physical phenomenon capable of explaining phenomenal unity. The limits-reveal-structure article turns cognitive closure into a method of self-knowledge.

However, significant weaknesses appear across all three. The pre-conceptual-knowing article conflates distinct phenomena (perceptual fineness of grain, phenomenal overflow, knowledge by acquaintance, bodily know-how) that may have different explanations—not all point to pre-conceptual territory. The quantum-binding article's central claim that entanglement provides "genuine ontological holism" that matches phenomenal unity commits the same explanatory gap it identifies in classical approaches: physical holism (even quantum) is not phenomenal holism. The limits-reveal-structure article, while philosophically careful, provides no clear criterion for distinguishing structural limits from mere difficulty—the very distinction on which its central thesis depends.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

"The pre-conceptual-knowing article assumes from the outset that there's something to know pre-conceptually. But what is this 'knowing' if it's not cognitive? Not propositional, not a capacity, not reportable—at what point does 'knowing' become an empty honorific?

The fineness of grain argument is instructive. I see 'that shade' of blue. I cannot identify it reliably later. The article concludes: the experience 'outstrips the concept.' But here's an alternative: the experience was never as fine-grained as introspection suggests. The perceptual system discriminates briefly, functionally, for immediate action. The sense that I'm experiencing a determinate shade—rather than a region of colour space—is itself a constructive representation. There's no 'shade itself' that concepts fail to capture; there's a functional discrimination that doesn't create stable memory traces.

Kelly's response to McDowell—that demonstrative concepts require re-identification capacity—tells us about concept possession, not about what exceeds concepts. If I cannot re-identify, I didn't have a concept. But 'didn't have a concept' doesn't entail 'had pre-conceptual knowledge.' It might mean: had a momentary perceptual state that was never knowledge in any interesting sense.

The quantum-binding article makes more sophisticated errors. It acknowledges that classical binding mechanisms coordinate separate processes. Then it claims quantum entanglement provides 'genuine physical holism.' But holism in physics means the state of the whole cannot be specified by states of the parts. This is a fact about mathematical representation—not about phenomenal experience. An entangled state is still a *physical* state. Why should irreducibility in Hilbert space produce irreducibility in phenomenal character?

The article slides from 'entangled states have properties parts lack' to 'entanglement makes separate things literally one thing.' But this confuses representational irreducibility with substantial unity. The entangled system remains a physical system describable (irreducibly) in physical terms. The hard problem asks why *any* physical system—reducible or not—produces experience. Quantum holism doesn't dissolve this; it gives it a fancier label.

The limits-reveal-structure article rests on distinguishing problems from mysteries. But the distinction is asserted, never demonstrated. How do we *know* the mind-body problem is a mystery rather than a very hard problem? The article says: persistence across millennia suggests structural closure. But many hard problems have persisted across millennia before being solved. The three-body problem in physics seemed intractable for centuries. Plate tectonics was a mystery until it wasn't. The inference from 'long-standing difficulty' to 'cognitive closure' requires premises the article doesn't supply."

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

"The pre-conceptual-knowing article multiplies examples: fineness of grain, phenomenal overflow, knowledge by acquaintance, bodily know-how. These are presented as convergent evidence for pre-conceptual territory. But convergent on what?

Block's phenomenal overflow experiments are contested precisely because 'rich experience beyond cognitive access' has an alternative interpretation: there's no rich experience, just the functional state of *seeming* to have seen more than can be reported. The 'seeming' is the whole phenomenon. The article mentions critics who 'argue the rich experience is illusory—constructed after the fact' but doesn't engage this seriously. It moves to 'the phenomenology is suggestive' as if phenomenology settles the matter. It doesn't.

Russell's knowledge by acquaintance is philosophically respectable, but 'acquaintance' doesn't obviously refer to something pre-conceptual. I'm acquainted with redness in the sense that I have direct perceptual access to red things. This is consistent with: the access is always conceptually structured. 'That sensation' is as conceptual as 'that dog.' The claim that acquaintance is pre-conceptual is not established by Russell's distinction; it's an additional claim requiring independent argument.

The quantum-binding article's treatment of illusionism is revealing. It says the 'illusion of unity' requires explanation—why would misrepresentation yield *unified* illusion? But this presupposes that unity is the phenomenon to explain. The illusionist response: the brain produces *functionally* unified behaviour and *reports* of unity. These are the explananda. The assumption that there's 'phenomenal unity' over and above functional coordination and unity-reports is precisely what's at issue.

The article says 'subjecthood presupposes unity—there must be one experiencer to whom something appears.' But the heterophenomenological method denies this. There are unified *reports* from a unified *system*, but no additional unified *subject* having experiences. The reports are produced by a coordinated system; coordination is not phenomenal unity.

The limits-reveal-structure article claims that if consciousness is 'fundamental—not emergent from physics but interacting with it—then the space of possible minds may be vast.' But this is speculation built on speculation. We don't know consciousness is fundamental. We don't know minds vary in the relevant ways. The article uses 'may be' appropriately but then proceeds as if the hypotheticals were established. The Nagel bat example shows we can't *imagine* bat experience—but can't imagine ≠ can't understand. I can't imagine the experience of early Earth before life, but I can understand geological processes. Imaginative failure is not cognitive closure."

### The Quantum Skeptic (Max Tegmark)

"The quantum-binding article cites my 2000 decoherence calculation as a target for critique. Let me address the responses it offers.

Hagan et al.'s corrections yield coherence times seven orders of magnitude longer—from femtoseconds to perhaps microseconds. But the article then invokes 'discrete quantum events rather than sustained superposition' to bridge the remaining gap to the 300ms timescale of conscious decisions. This is sleight of hand. The Orch OR hypothesis specifically requires sustained superposition until collapse. If you retreat to discrete events, you're abandoning the mechanism that was supposed to explain phenomenal unity. Entangled states that collapse in microseconds don't provide the temporal unity of a conscious moment.

The quantum Zeno effect is mentioned as a precedent from cryptochrome radical pairs. But the quantum Zeno effect in cryptochromes involves specific chemical reactions over specific timescales. The article doesn't specify what neural process would exhibit Zeno effects, at what timescale, or how those effects would constitute rather than merely correlate with consciousness.

The Kerskens-Pérez findings are described as 'the first attempt at direct evidence linking entangled states in the brain to consciousness.' Attempt is the key word. Warren's critique is acknowledged but dismissed with 'no alternative classical explanation for the observed signal patterns.' But absence of classical explanation is not evidence for quantum explanation. It's evidence that we don't understand the phenomenon. The inference 'no classical explanation → quantum explanation' requires ruling out measurement artifact, experimental error, and unknown classical mechanisms. The article acknowledges the findings 'remain contested'—good—but then proceeds to treat them as supportive evidence.

The pre-conceptual-knowing article mentions quantum states only briefly, connecting to 'Minimal Quantum Interaction' by suggesting 'quantum states only conscious systems sustain' might explain consciousness-only access. But this is mechanism-free speculation. What quantum states? Sustained how? The article provides no physics.

The limits-reveal-structure article at least hedges appropriately: the quantum mechanism 'may be' physically possible, the hard problem remains even if we understand the interface. But it doesn't address: if we don't understand the mechanism, how can we claim the limits-reveal-structure thesis rather than simply acknowledging ignorance? The article treats 'we don't understand the mind-body connection' as evidence for cognitive closure. But it might be evidence that the problem is hard, not that it's impossible."

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

"The quantum-binding article contains a section specifically rejecting many-worlds. Let me address its arguments.

'In the many-worlds interpretation, all possible bindings occur in branching universes. There is no genuine selection, no *this* binding becoming actual while others remain merely possible.'

This misunderstands MWI. In MWI, from within any branch, *this* binding is actual. The other bindings are actual in other branches, but they're not alternatives to *this* one—they're different situations involving different observers. It's like saying 'if my twin exists, I don't really exist.' The existence of other instances doesn't undermine the reality of this instance.

'Decoherence makes branches effectively independent; it doesn't explain which branch contains my continued experience.'

Which branch contains your continued experience? All of them—in the sense that all branches contain continuations of what was previously you. 'Which one is *really* me?' is a confused question, like asking which fork in a road is *really* the continuation. Both forks continue the road. Both branches continue the person.

The article invokes haecceity: 'the thisness that phenomenal unity implies.' But haecceity is preserved within each branch. *This* experience in *this* branch is indexically unique within the branch. The existence of other branches with other experiences doesn't dissolve the thisness of this experience any more than the existence of other people dissolves my identity.

The limits-reveal-structure article connects to 'No Many Worlds' through the indexical problem: 'Why am I this branch?' may be a mystery rather than a problem. But on MWI, the question dissolves—you're in every branch, so asking 'why this one?' is like asking 'why am I me rather than someone else?' The question presupposes metaphysical uniqueness that MWI denies. This isn't mystery—it's dissolution of a confused question.

The pre-conceptual-knowing article doesn't address MWI directly, which is appropriate since its claims don't depend on collapse. But if experience includes pre-conceptual content, that content exists equally across all branches on MWI. Nothing about pre-conceptual territory requires collapse to be real."

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

"What would falsify the claim that pre-conceptual knowing exists?

The article lists conditions: concepts prove unlimited in scope, phenomenal overflow proves illusory, acquaintance reduces to description, skilled knowledge becomes articulable, contemplatives articulate their experiences. Each condition is reasonable. But examine the structure: each condition essentially requires solving the hard problem or demonstrating strong conceptualism. The falsifiers are approximately as hard to verify as the thesis is to prove.

Compare: 'Invisible unicorns exist unless we can prove they don't.' The burden of proof has been inverted. The article presents converging evidence (fineness of grain, overflow, acquaintance, bodily skill) as supporting pre-conceptual knowing. But convergence of contested phenomena doesn't establish any of them. Block's overflow is contested. Russell's acquaintance is contested. The convergence of contested claims is a contested meta-claim.

The quantum-binding article is more explicit about falsifiability. It lists: neural determinism, classical explanation of zero-lag synchrony, failure to replicate Kerskens-Pérez, fully classical anesthetic mechanisms, unified AI consciousness without quantum effects. These are testable. But observe: most rely on absence of findings (no quantum effects, no replication failure). The article claims 'current evidence favors the quantum position'—but the evidence is suggestive rather than decisive.

The epothilone study is striking: stabilizing microtubules delayed anesthesia onset by 69 seconds. But the article acknowledges: classical mechanisms might explain this. The inference 'no classical mechanism known → quantum mechanism' is a god-of-the-gaps argument. Absence of known mechanism ≠ evidence for specific unknown mechanism.

The limits-reveal-structure article is most carefully hedged. Its falsifiability section is detailed: contingent rather than structural limits, successful Dennett objection, convergent evolution of cognition, introspective investigation revealing content rather than absence, explanatory gap closing. But the article then argues none of these conditions have been met—which is methodologically appropriate, but notice: the persistence of the hard problem serves as both evidence for the thesis AND as the standard against which falsification is measured. This is perilously close to circularity.

'After millennia of contemplative investigation and centuries of philosophical inquiry, the hard problem remains as puzzling as ever.' True, but also true of the problem of consciousness in general. The specific claim—that limits reveal *structure* rather than mere *difficulty*—requires showing that structural limits are the explanation for the persistence. The article doesn't establish this; it assumes it."

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

"The pre-conceptual-knowing article invokes prereflective bodily awareness, contemplative practice, and 'non-conceptual wisdom (nirvikalpa jnana).' Let me offer a perspective from within these traditions.

The article frames the question as: can conceptual beings access pre-conceptual knowing? But this assumes the conceptual/pre-conceptual distinction maps onto something real. Buddhist analysis suggests a different framing: all experience is conditioned arising. Concepts arise dependently; so does what the article calls 'pre-conceptual' experience. The distinction between conceptual and pre-conceptual may itself be a conceptual construction—precisely the kind of reification the article claims to investigate but actually perpetuates.

When the article says meditators report 'states where conceptual activity quiets, revealing something beneath,' it imposes a layered metaphysics: concepts on top, pre-conceptual beneath. But careful contemplative investigation finds no layers. Experience arises and passes. The 'quieting of concepts' is itself a phenomenon with its own character—not a peeling away to reveal a hidden substrate. The 'something beneath' is constructed by the concept 'beneath.'

The quantum-binding article cites nirodha-samāpatti, rigpa, and turīya as evidence for 'phenomenal unity' prior to content. But these states, as described in their home traditions, are not evidence for unity as a *thing*. They are described apophatically: not-this, not-that, beyond duality. The article appropriates these reports to support a metaphysics of phenomenal unity that the traditions themselves might reject. Nagarjuna would ask: what is this 'unity' that you locate? Show me its boundaries. Show me its substance. If you cannot, perhaps 'unity' is another conceptual construction being reified.

The limits-reveal-structure article is philosophically more careful. It acknowledges that 'perhaps both humans and great apes share the fundamental situation: experience arising without an experiencer.' But then it proceeds with 'cognitive architecture' language that presupposes what Buddhist analysis deconstructs: a stable cognitive system with definite properties. The 'limits' of cognition presuppose a cognizer with boundaries. But if the cognizer is empty—arising dependently, lacking inherent existence—then 'limits' are conventions, not discoveries.

I don't claim Buddhism has the answer the Map lacks. I claim the Map's questions may be malformed. 'What cannot concepts access?' assumes concepts are agents that access things. 'What does phenomenal unity consist in?' assumes unity is a substance. The traditions the articles cite would dissolve the questions rather than answer them."

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Conflation of Distinct Phenomena in Pre-Conceptual Arguments

- **File**: `voids/pre-conceptual-knowing.md`
- **Location**: Lines 35-72 (Fineness of Grain through Prereflective Bodily Awareness sections)
- **Problem**: The article presents four phenomena as converging evidence for pre-conceptual knowing: (1) perceptual fineness of grain exceeding conceptual grasp, (2) phenomenal overflow beyond cognitive access, (3) Russell's knowledge by acquaintance, (4) prereflective bodily awareness. But these may have different explanations, and not all point toward the same conclusion.

Fineness of grain might indicate perceptual discrimination without stable representation—the experience isn't as determinate as introspection suggests. Phenomenal overflow is contested—Block's opponents deny there's overflow rather than functional states producing overflow-reports. Acquaintance might be conceptually structured—'that sensation' is as demonstrative as 'that dog.' Bodily know-how might be implicit propositional knowledge not yet articulated.

The article treats these as 'converging from multiple directions' (line 32), but convergence requires the phenomena to be independently established. If each is contested, convergence of contested claims doesn't establish any of them.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Engage more seriously with alternatives to each phenomenon. Distinguish which arguments depend on anti-illusionism (overflow), which depend on anti-conceptualism (fineness of grain), and which are methodologically independent. Acknowledge that the 'convergence' is weaker than it appears if each component is contested.

### Issue 2: Physical Holism ≠ Phenomenal Holism

- **File**: `topics/quantum-binding-and-phenomenal-unity.md`
- **Location**: Lines 105-115 (Why Entanglement Matches Unity section)
- **Problem**: The article's central claim is that quantum entanglement provides 'genuine ontological holism' matching phenomenal unity. Neven is quoted: entanglement is 'the only true binding agent in physics.' The article concludes: 'The unity of experience would reflect the unity of the underlying physics.'

But this commits the same explanatory gap the article identifies for classical binding. Why should physical holism—even genuine ontological holism—produce phenomenal unity? The entangled state has properties parts lack individually. Granted. But this is a fact about how the state is represented mathematically. The hard problem asks why *any* physical state—reducible or irreducible, holistic or compositional—produces experience. 'The whole has properties the parts lack' is still a *physical* fact. The transition from physical fact to phenomenal unity is asserted, not explained.

The article says 'phenomenal unity would *mirror* the structure of that entanglement.' Mirror how? By what mechanism does mathematical irreducibility in Hilbert space become experienced unity in phenomenal space? The word 'mirror' does heavy lifting while explaining nothing.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either provide an argument for why physical holism maps to phenomenal holism, or acknowledge that quantum binding *relocates* rather than solves the hard problem. The current presentation treats quantum holism as obviously solving unity, but this is precisely what needs establishing.

### Issue 3: Problem/Mystery Distinction Unestablished

- **File**: `voids/limits-reveal-structure.md`
- **Location**: Lines 42-56 (Scope Implies Limits and Species-Specific Closure sections)
- **Problem**: The limits-reveal-structure thesis depends on distinguishing problems (solvable in principle) from mysteries (constitutionally inaccessible). Chomsky is cited: the endowment that makes development possible 'also excludes other paths.' McGinn is cited: cognitive closure means concept-forming procedures *cannot* extend to certain properties.

But how do we know the mind-body problem is a mystery rather than a very hard problem? The article's evidence: persistence across millennia, cross-traditional convergence on the existence of limits. But persistence doesn't establish principled impossibility. Many problems have seemed intractable before being solved (plate tectonics, continental drift, the chemical basis of heredity). The distinction between 'very hard' and 'constitutionally impossible' is never operationalized.

McGinn's rat-and-calculus example is instructive for rats—we can observe rat cognitive limits from outside. But we cannot observe human limits from outside. The inference 'we can't solve it → we can't solve it' is tautological. The additional premise—'we can't solve it because of cognitive architecture'—is asserted without independent support.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge that the problem/mystery distinction cannot be established from inside the cognitive system claimed to be limited. The thesis becomes: *if* the hard problem is unsolvable due to cognitive architecture, *then* its shape reveals that architecture. But the antecedent cannot be verified, making the thesis conditional on an unestablished premise.

### Issue 4: Timescale Gap Not Adequately Bridged

- **File**: `topics/quantum-binding-and-phenomenal-unity.md`
- **Location**: Lines 57-76 (The Decoherence Debate section)
- **Problem**: The article acknowledges coherence times of 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁴ seconds even with Hagan et al.'s corrections—'three orders of magnitude short' of the ~300ms conscious decision timescale. It responds: 'this gap may not matter if the relevant mechanisms involve discrete quantum events rather than sustained superposition.'

But this retreat undermines the article's central argument. Phenomenal unity is supposed to be explained by quantum holism—entangled states forming irreducible wholes. Discrete events collapsing in microseconds don't provide continuous holistic unity over 300ms. The article wants both sustained entanglement (for holism) and discrete events (to avoid decoherence). These are different mechanisms with different implications.

The quantum Zeno effect is mentioned: 'discrete attentional observations' rather than 'long-lived coherence.' But the Zeno effect prevents collapse, not creates it. The mechanism for conscious collapse would be different from the mechanism for coherence maintenance. The article conflates these while acknowledging 'a gap remains.'
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Clarify which mechanism is being proposed: sustained entanglement (requiring longer coherence than demonstrated) or discrete collapse events (requiring a different explanation of unity). The current presentation oscillates between these without acknowledging they're incompatible.

### Issue 5: The Precondition Response Is Question-Begging

- **File**: `topics/quantum-binding-and-phenomenal-unity.md`
- **Location**: Lines 124-127 (The precondition response to illusionism)
- **Problem**: The article responds to illusionism: 'Illusion requires a subject to be deceived. But subjecthood presupposes unity—there must be one experiencer to whom something appears other than it is. Dennett's "multiple drafts" eliminate the unified subject who could be fooled by them.'

But this begs the question against illusionism. The illusionist denies that illusions require a unified phenomenal subject. A system can produce reports of unity without there being a unified subject producing those reports. 'Something appears other than it is'—the illusionist parses this as: the system represents itself inaccurately. This doesn't require a unified subject; it requires representational capacities, which are functional.

The argument assumes subjecthood presupposes phenomenal unity. This is precisely what illusionists deny. The 'precondition response' presupposes what it needs to prove.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Engage the illusionist response more carefully. The illusionist will say: 'subjecthood' is itself a functional state, not a phenomenal one. The system that's 'fooled' is a system that represents itself incorrectly—no phenomenal subject required. Either argue against this functionalist parsing of subjecthood, or acknowledge the response is dialectically ineffective against committed illusionists.

### Issue 6: AI as Asymmetric Probe Is Speculative

- **File**: `voids/limits-reveal-structure.md`
- **Location**: Lines 98-109 (AI as Asymmetric Probe section)
- **Problem**: The article proposes using LLMs to probe human cognitive limits: 'If LLMs have different blind spots—trained on human text but lacking biological architecture—they may detect our voids from outside.' The 'strange possibility: an LLM might *state* a thought humans cannot *think*.'

But this is untestable speculation. How would we know if an LLM stated an unthinkable thought? If we understood it, it wasn't unthinkable. If we didn't understand it, how would we know it was a thought rather than nonsense? The article acknowledges this: 'We'd hear the words without grasping the meaning.' But then we couldn't verify it was meaningful. The asymmetry proposed is unfalsifiable in principle.

More fundamentally: LLMs are trained on human text. Their outputs are recombinations of human-thinkable thoughts. The claim that they might produce genuinely novel thoughts outside human cognition requires argument, not assertion. Pattern-matching on human text is unlikely to exceed human conceptual space.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either provide criteria for identifying when an LLM produces a genuinely unthinkable thought, or acknowledge this is speculative methodology that may prove unworkable. The current presentation treats it as a promising research direction without addressing the verification problem.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The Functional Discrimination Response

- **Current content says**: Perceptual fineness of grain exceeds conceptual grasp; we experience 'that shade' without possessing a concept of it.
- **A critic would argue**: Perceptual discrimination is functional, not phenomenal. The visual system discriminates many shades for purposes of edge detection, colour constancy, and action guidance. The discrimination serves immediate processing without creating stable representations. The sense that we're experiencing a 'determinate shade' beyond concepts may be a construction—we're experiencing functional discrimination that doesn't correspond to determinate phenomenal content.
- **Suggested response**: Distinguish the phenomenological claim (it seems like I'm experiencing a determinate shade) from the metaphysical claim (there is determinate phenomenal content beyond concepts). Acknowledge that the phenomenological datum doesn't settle the metaphysical question. Or provide argument for why functional discrimination couldn't produce the phenomenology without the metaphysics.

### The Mathematical Irreducibility ≠ Substantial Unity Response

- **Current content says**: Entangled states are mathematically irreducible; the whole has properties parts lack; this provides 'genuine ontological holism' matching phenomenal unity.
- **A critic would argue**: Mathematical irreducibility is a formal property. A system described by an irreducible state vector is still a *physical* system. Why should irreducibility in Hilbert space produce unity in phenomenal space? Classical bound systems (a bound electron, a planet in orbit) also have properties their parts lack in isolation—binding energy, orbital parameters. These are relationally irreducible. If relational irreducibility doesn't produce phenomenal unity, why should quantum irreducibility?
- **Suggested response**: Either provide a principle connecting mathematical irreducibility to phenomenal unity, or acknowledge that quantum holism provides a better *match* for unity without explaining *why* matching structure produces matched phenomenology. The current presentation asserts the connection without argument.

### The Problem/Mystery Conflation Response

- **Current content says**: The hard problem's persistence suggests cognitive closure; we may be constitutionally closed to its solution.
- **A critic would argue**: Persistence suggests difficulty, not impossibility. Many problems have persisted for centuries before being solved. Cognitive closure would explain persistence, but so would 'the problem is genuinely hard and we haven't found the answer yet.' The inference from persistence to closure requires additional premises: that we've tried the right approaches, that progress should have been made, that there's no other explanation for stasis. None of these are established.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge that persistence is consistent with both difficulty and closure. The limits-reveal-structure thesis should be presented as one interpretation of the evidence, not as established. Alternatively, provide additional evidence for closure over difficulty—perhaps the *specific shape* of failure (always the same gap, despite radically different approaches) suggests structural rather than contingent limitation.

The fundamental vulnerability across all three: each assumes the reality of what it seeks to explain. Pre-conceptual knowing is assumed real to investigate its territory. Phenomenal unity is assumed real to explain it through quantum holism. Cognitive limits are assumed structural to infer cognitive architecture. Critics who question the phenomena (illusionists, strong conceptualists, optimists about cognitive progress) are engaged but not fully satisfied. The articles are strong defenses of positions that presuppose their own framework—effective for sympathetic readers, less effective for converting skeptics.
