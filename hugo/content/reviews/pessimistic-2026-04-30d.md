## Executive Summary

The article synthesises a substantial literature accurately and the prose is disciplined. The exposed flanks are conceptual rather than empirical: (1) the **improper-mixture opening** is asserted to provide a place where consciousness can act, but the reduced density matrix is FAPP-indistinguishable from a proper mixture for any local observer — the distinction the article relies on is not locally accessible; (2) the **three "post-decoherence mechanisms"** (movable cut, relocated Stapp, actualisation) are not three mechanisms but three vocabularies for the same minimal commitment, and the article's typology obscures this; (3) the **selection-criterion problem** is acknowledged in passing but is the most damaging issue for the entire forward-in-time programme — it generates a regress the article does not engage; (4) the **prebiotic-collapse handling** introduces a *dual-mechanism* story (objective collapse for cosmology, consciousness-modulation for neurons) that is not parsimonious despite being framed as the Map's "standard resolution"; (5) the **five-considerations list** mixes weak grounds (phenomenological fit, which the article itself notes is non-conclusive) with grounds the article cannot defend without assuming what it is trying to establish (compatibility with objective collapse, which actually *increases* the mechanism count); (6) the **structural seam** of the coalesce remains visible — the article alternates between a "forward vs retrocausal" frame and a "pre- vs post-decoherence" frame without integrating them. A seventh concern is **falsifiability asymmetry**: the article admits post-decoherence selection makes weaker empirical predictions than pre-decoherence, then continues to label it the Map's "primary response" — converting unfalsifiability from cost to virtue.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

This article is a textbook case of folk-psychological vocabulary inflated to metaphysical status. *Selection*, *preference*, *intervention*, *attention holding a pattern*, *experiencing the actualisation* — every active verb attributed to consciousness in this article is precisely the kind of pre-scientific mentalese that mature neuroscience will replace. The post-decoherence framework's central claim — "consciousness selects which element of the mixture becomes experientially actual" — is folk-psychological volition with quantum-flavoured language layered over it. There is no neural correlate of *selection-as-such*; there are only specific gating mechanisms (basal ganglia thresholding, predictive-coding precision-weighting, attentional gain-control) that produce the *appearance* of unified selection. When you say "consciousness contributes only the final determination," you are reifying that appearance.

Worse, the "improper mixture" argument retreats from physics into philosophy precisely where the empirical case fails. Tegmark's calculation kills pre-decoherence neural superposition; rather than abandon the programme, the article relocates the same folk vocabulary to a stage where there is *nothing physical left for it to do*. Decoherence has done the work; what remains is, neurologically, the firing of one pattern rather than another, fully explicable through standard cognitive neuroscience. The "selection criterion problem" is admitted but not pursued — because pursuing it reveals the regress: whatever determines consciousness's preference is *the actual mechanism*, and consciousness becomes a label for that mechanism rather than an additional ingredient. Drop the mentalese and the post-decoherence framework reduces to: "after decoherence, the brain produces the firing pattern that the brain produces, and we call the result experience." Which is true, and trivial, and requires no new physics.

The Hagan et al. (2002) and 2025 *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* citations particularly illustrate the methodology. Both are explicitly hedged as "contested" and "unreplicated," yet their plausibility is invoked to keep pre-decoherence options open. This is asymmetric crediting: extraordinary evidence is treated as suggestive while ordinary skepticism is treated as merely "raised by critics." The folk concept of consciousness is being protected from elimination by selectively credited fringe experiments.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

The improper-mixture argument is the heterophenomenological mistake at the foundations-of-physics level. You are taking a *mathematical formalism* whose interpretation is contested (does the reduced density matrix represent ignorance or genuine indeterminacy?) and treating one interpretation as licensing a metaphysical claim (there is something for consciousness to do post-decoherence). But the FAPP results are devastating: from the perspective of any local observer with finite resources, the improper mixture and the proper mixture produce identical predictions for all physical observables. The "opening" you describe is invisible from inside any system that could interact with it. If consciousness is the kind of thing that lives somewhere in the physical chain (and the article never abandons this, since it preserves bidirectional interaction with neurons), it is on the wrong side of the FAPP veil to detect the distinction it is asked to act on.

The "three post-decoherence mechanisms" are particularly telling. Read carefully: the von Neumann movable cut says consciousness *can* be the location of selection; the relocated Stapp framework says consciousness *is* the selector but loses its physical mechanism (Zeno) in the relocation; the actualisation model says consciousness *actualises* with no physical mechanism at all. These are not three mechanisms. They are three rhetorical postures for the same minimal commitment: post-decoherence indeterminacy, plus consciousness, equals definite outcome. Strip the labels and the article has one mechanism (a placeholder labelled "selection") that you have re-described three times. The taxonomy creates the appearance of theoretical richness where there is one undeveloped move.

The "selection criterion problem" deserves more attention than the article gives. If consciousness selects pattern A over pattern B, either (i) it has internal reasons (in which case the *reason-bearing structure* is the actual mechanism, and consciousness is folk-psychological dressing on a deeper functional state); (ii) it selects randomly (in which case the model has gained nothing over GRW + Born-rule statistics); or (iii) it selects according to some non-reducible "preference" that has no further account (in which case you have introduced an unanalysable primitive, which is the explanatory failure the article was supposed to remedy). The article does not face this trilemma. It presents the selection-criterion problem as one open question among four, when it is the one that determines whether the entire framework has any explanatory traction.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Max Tegmark)

The article's strategy is to retreat from my decoherence calculation by relocating consciousness's role to *after* decoherence has done its work. But this concedes my point: pre-decoherence neural superpositions cannot survive long enough for consciousness to act on them. Then it asks me to accept that consciousness acts on the *output* of decoherence — which is, by construction, classical for all observable purposes. There is no quantum problem left at that stage to solve.

The improper-mixture opening rests on a foundational-physics distinction (proper vs improper mixtures) that has *no observational consequences* for anyone embedded in the system. Schlosshauer's review, which the article quotes, is precisely the place where physicists like me argue that this gap is closed by environment-induced selection plus the interpretation one prefers — the *gap* is interpretive, not dynamical. There is no measurement you can perform from inside a decohered system that distinguishes the two. So when you say "consciousness selects which element of the improper mixture becomes actual," you are asking consciousness to perform a discrimination that no physical apparatus can perform. Either consciousness is a non-physical entity with access to information no physical detector has (which is naked dualism, not a mechanism), or it cannot perform the selection you describe.

The 2025 *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* claim about coherence domains over 10¹¹ glutamate molecules is an extraordinary claim asserted without independent replication. Cite it once and hedge it — fine. But the article keeps the door open for pre-decoherence selection by leaning on this single contested study at exactly the place where the empirical case is weakest. That is not balanced framing; that is preserving optionality through credulity. If the 2025 claim fails to replicate, the entire pre-decoherence side of the four-mechanism taxonomy collapses to "ruled out by basic thermodynamics." The article should be willing to say so, rather than treating the contested claim as load-bearing for the article's structure.

A direct calculation challenge: at the post-decoherence stage, what is the quantum *operator* by which consciousness "selects"? Either you can write down a Hamiltonian or projection-style action consciousness performs on the environment-decohered density matrix (in which case we can analyse decoherence-of-consciousness's-action and recover the timescale problem at the next level), or you cannot (in which case "selection" is a placeholder, not a physical mechanism). The article never writes the operator. It cannot, because consciousness has no Hilbert-space representation in the framework. So we are back to Georgiev's objection against Stapp, which the article quotes but does not refute: introducing the mind as an external agent acting on quantum formalism from outside is *negation* of QM, not *building* on it.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

The article's rejection of MWI is brisk: "If all elements of the decohered mixture were equally real (as Everett holds), there would be no selection to explain." This is the entire treatment. But the assumption smuggled in is that the *thing requiring selection* is the post-decoherence mixture rather than the indexical question "why am I in this branch." On the MWI reading, decoherence produces all branches; the indexical question is malformed because there is no branch-independent "I" to ask it; consciousness is fully explained as the experience-of-being-in-a-branch with no further selection required. The Map insists indexical identity matters (No Many Worlds tenet), but the article does not engage the anthropic-bias objection: that MWI rejection itself is anthropocentric, treating the experiential singleness of one branch as evidence about ontology rather than as evidence about what it is like to *be* a particular branch-history.

More damagingly: the article praises post-decoherence selection for "minimality" while requiring the additional ontological commitment that post-decoherence mixtures contain *genuine* indeterminacy (not just observer-relative ignorance). On MWI, there is no such indeterminacy — the wavefunction simply continues to evolve, and "indeterminacy" is the experience of being in a branch where one outcome has occurred. The article's framework requires an *additional* metaphysical postulate (real, ontologically open possibilities post-decoherence) that MWI does without. Yet the article repeatedly invokes Minimal Quantum Interaction and parsimony. The minimality is asymmetric: it counts the simplicity of the *interaction* but not the underlying ontology.

The "Phenomenological fit" consideration is especially weak from the MWI side. Phenomenology presents experience as choosing among present possibilities — but on MWI, that *is* what is happening: each branch instantiates one of the possibilities, and the observer in that branch experiences the choosing. Phenomenological fit does not discriminate between MWI and forward-in-time selection; it is satisfied by both. The article counts it as a forward-in-time virtue without showing it disfavours MWI.

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

The falsifiability asymmetry is the article's most damaging structural feature. It admits, in the section "Why Pre-Decoherence Faces Pressure That Post-Decoherence Does Not," that "post-decoherence models operate at the interpretive level — the gap between formalism and outcome — where empirical access is indirect." It then continues to position post-decoherence selection as the Map's *primary* response and *strongest* version of forward-in-time selection. This converts unfalsifiability from a cost into a virtue. A theory whose primary virtue is that no empirical result can refute it is not even wrong.

The actualisation model is the limit case. The article writes: "It is invulnerable to any empirical objection about decoherence timescales, quantum coherence in biology, or energy conservation, because it makes no physical claims at all." This is presented as a "distinctive advantage." It is not. A theory that makes no physical claims and cannot be empirically distinguished from its negation has no scientific content. The article notes the philosophical vulnerability ("incoherent or redundant") but treats this as a separable concern. It is not separable. If the theory's only vulnerability is philosophical, and its philosophical content is "consciousness actualises possibilities," it has retreated entirely from the explanatory project. Calling this a *mechanism* is a category error.

The "selection criterion problem" should be the *first* open question, not the last, because it determines whether any of the proposed mechanisms have testable consequences. If consciousness's selection criterion has no specifiable structure (from outside or inside), there is no prediction the framework can make about *which* outcome will be selected, only that *some* outcome will be — which is what every other interpretation also predicts. The framework has zero predictive content over and above the Born rule. What experiment, in principle, could distinguish a world in which post-decoherence selection occurs from a world in which it does not? The article does not answer.

The contested empirical citations (Hagan 2002 microtubule decoherence revision; 2025 macroscopic coherence-domain claim) are credited with sustaining plausibility while their hedges deny them probative force. This is the *unfalsifiable middle*: claims hedged enough to escape refutation while invoked confidently enough to do supportive work. Either the contested findings are evidence — in which case their hedging is inconsistent with their argumentative role — or they are not, in which case they should not appear in the structural argument.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nāgārjuna)

The article reifies a self that selects. The "consciousness contributing only the final determination" is described as a coherent agent acting on an array of physical alternatives — but the very framework of *agent confronting alternatives* is precisely the conceptual structure (subject-object duality, ātman) that Madhyamaka deconstructs. There is no consciousness *over here* and array of post-decohered possibilities *over there* with consciousness reaching across. Both poles are dependently arisen; both are empty of inherent existence; the "selection" the article describes is a conceptual construction layered over a process that has no privileged decomposition into selector and selected.

The article's commitment to indexical identity (No Many Worlds tenet) doubles down on the reification. The insistence that "the Map's framework is compatible with both retrocausal and forward-in-time selection, and need not choose between them" preserves the optionality precisely by maintaining a self stable enough to *have* options. But the more carefully you specify the selection mechanism — whether pre- or post-decoherence, whether Stappian or actualisation-style — the more the framework requires a self that persists through deliberation, attends to alternatives, and selects. This is exactly the constructed self emptiness aims to dissolve. The article cannot have its dualism without reifying what the Buddhist tradition spends two millennia teaching one to release.

The "actualisation model" comes closest to seeing the issue — it treats selection as a transition from possibility to actuality without specific physical mechanism — but it stops at the boundary. The next step is to recognise that *actualisation* itself is a conceptual imposition. The post-decoherence state is neither possibility-laden nor actuality-bearing intrinsically; both descriptions are dependently arisen relative to a viewpoint that asks the question. The article uses "actualisation" as a placeholder for what would be, on a more rigorous deconstructive reading, the absence of any inherent actualisation-versus-possibility structure.

### Issue 4: Prebiotic-collapse handling introduces dual mechanisms despite framing as unified

- **Problem**: The article says forward-in-time selection handles the prebiotic problem through "the Map's standard resolution: objective reduction with consciousness modulation." But this is a *dual-mechanism* story: physical mechanisms (gravitational collapse, spontaneous localisation) provide baseline selection in the absence of consciousness; consciousness modulates selection in the presence of a brain. These are two mechanisms doing the same job (producing definite outcomes) with different triggers (universal vs neural). This is *less* parsimonious than a single-mechanism account, not more. ## Counterarguments to Address

### The improper-mixture argument and the FAPP veil
- **Current content says**: The improper-mixture distinction is the philosophical opening through which consciousness can act post-decoherence (lines 76–82).
- **A critic would argue**: From any local observer's perspective, the improper mixture and the proper mixture produce identical predictions. The "opening" is invisible to anything embedded in the physical chain; consciousness can only act on it if it has access to information no physical apparatus has — which is a strong dualism the article does not own up to.
- **Suggested response**: The article could acknowledge the FAPP gap explicitly and frame the post-decoherence opening as something only consciousness (qua non-physical) has access to. This is consistent with the Dualism tenet but tightens the argument's commitments. Currently the article speaks as if the opening is a feature of the physics that consciousness happens to be able to act on; the critic's reading exposes that it must be a non-physical access. Owning the access claim makes the argument internally consistent.

### MWI and the indexical question
- **Current content says**: MWI is rejected because indexical identity matters and "if all elements of the decohered mixture were equally real, there would be no selection to explain" (line 200).
- **A critic would argue**: MWI handles the indexical question by saying it is malformed — there is no branch-independent self to ask "why this branch" of. The article assumes the indexical question is well-formed in order to reject the answer that says it is not. This is anthropocentric: privileging the experiential singleness of one branch as evidence about ontology rather than as evidence about what it is to be a particular branch-history.
- **Suggested response**: The article could add a sentence acknowledging that the indexical question's well-formedness is itself contested, and that MWI rejection presupposes a position on this prior question. The Map's commitment to indexical identity is a foundational tenet, but the article should make visible that this foundational commitment, not the current passage's brisk dismissal, is doing the work.

### Functional reduction of "selection"
- **Current content says**: Consciousness selects among alternatives, contributing the final determination (line 120 and elsewhere).
- **A critic would argue**: There is no neural correlate of selection-as-such; the appearance of unified selection is produced by specific gating mechanisms (basal ganglia thresholding, attentional gain-control, predictive-coding precision-weighting). The article reifies the appearance.
- **Suggested response**: The article could add a passage engaging the functionalist counter directly: what is consciousness contributing *over and above* the gating mechanisms cognitive neuroscience identifies? The selection-criterion problem (Issue 3) is one place to address this, but the article should frame the functionalist objection as the strongest current alternative rather than as an unstated background.

### The unfalsifiability of the actualisation model
- **Current content says**: The actualisation model is "invulnerable to any empirical objection" (line 134) — described as a distinctive advantage.
- **A critic would argue**: A theory that makes no physical claims and cannot be empirically distinguished from its negation has no scientific content. Calling this an advantage inverts methodological norms.
- **Suggested response**: Recharacterise the actualisation model's unfalsifiability as a *cost* the framework accepts in exchange for invulnerability to the timing-gap objection. The article should explicitly say that the actualisation model is the most metaphysically modest *and* the least empirically tractable, and that these are paired — not separate — features.
