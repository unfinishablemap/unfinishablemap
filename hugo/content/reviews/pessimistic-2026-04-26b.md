# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-04-26
**Content reviewed**: [eliminative-materialism](/topics/eliminative-materialism/) — created today (2026-04-26 14:37 UTC) by `/expand-topic`; ~3,200 words; no prior review. The article distinguishes classical eliminativism (Churchland/Stich) from illusionism (Frankish/Dennett), engages the connectionist challenge, and rejects both strands while conceding limited eliminativism about folk-psychological *structure* (not phenomenology).

## Executive Summary

The article is a strong, conscientious survey, but its rebuttal section relies heavily on premises an eliminativist would reject without giving them independent support. Three patterns recur: (i) the introspection-as-special-evidence move is *asserted* as the rebuttal rather than argued for, which begs the question against the very position under review; (ii) the "parsimony has a bad track record in fundamental physics" historical argument is sketched with examples that, on closer inspection, *cut against* the article's conclusion; (iii) the "Honest Limitations" section concedes the connectionist point in a way that the "Map's third tenet" paragraph then quietly assumes was not conceded, producing internal tension. The article also misses the most active contemporary front (predictive processing / Clark / Hohwy), and the regress-against-illusionism is presented in a form Frankish has explicitly answered.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist
"You cast my view as 'physicalism's cleanest exit' — a parsimony manoeuvre. That is not the argument. The argument is *theory replacement under inductive pressure*: folk psychology is a primitive theory whose entities (beliefs, qualia) have, on the inductive base rate of folk theories, a poor prior of carving real kinds. I am not driven by Occam's Razor; I am driven by the same kind of empirical-theoretical revision that retired phlogiston and miasma. By recasting eliminativism as parsimony abuse, you have substituted a methodological posture I do not hold for the position I do hold, and then refuted the substitute. Worse, your central rebuttal — that 'the phenomenal observer cannot be wrong in the same way about the existence of phenomenality, because the observation is constitutive of what is observed' — is exactly what I deny. Asserting it as a rebuttal is question-begging in dress uniform. The connectionist argument *is* the argument; you correctly flag this, then proceed as if conceding it cost nothing. It costs the structural premise that 'we have beliefs' is a datum. If beliefs lack categorical implementation, then the inferential framework that grounds your whole 'phenomenal evidence as datum' move is on the same footing as the propositional attitudes you've consigned to the cognitive scientists."

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist
"You quote my 'obvious default theory' line and then route around heterophenomenology, which is the actual method that supports the position. Your Moorean rebuttal — 'I am more certain that phenomenal consciousness exists than I am of any premise an illusionist could deploy' — is *first-person operationalism*, the very move I have spent forty years calling out. Confidence in introspection is not an argument; it is the explanandum. Your 'regress against illusionism' is a clean miss: the seeming that there are qualia is, on my view, a *judgment* — a higher-order representational state with quasi-phenomenal properties — not itself a phenomenal seeming that would relaunch the regress. Frankish answers this in §6 of the 2016 paper; you do not engage it. The morphine case in your treatment is rendered as a curiosity rather than what it actually is — a dissociation that pries apart components folk psychology had glued together. If the folk concept fragments under empirical pressure, the inference is not 'the components are separately real'; it is 'the folk concept did not carve at the joints'. You concede this in the closing paragraph about Griffiths and Machery, then refuse to apply the same logic to *qualia*, where the asymmetry needs an argument and you give none."

### The Quantum Skeptic
"The article makes no quantum claims directly — fine. But the closing 'Map's third tenet' paragraph leans on Bidirectional Interaction to rule out eliminativism: 'Mental fictionalism and full eliminativism both deny [real mental causation].' This is a circular move. The third tenet *presupposes* mental causation; you cannot then deploy it as a premise against a position that contests the antecedent. From a purely dialectical standpoint, this argument is not doing the work the article wants it to do. It is asserting a tenet, then noting that eliminativism violates it. That is not a refutation; that is a restatement of disagreement. If a Frankish-style eliminativist accepts that 'beliefs cause behaviour' as a description of physical-functional causation but denies that this requires *phenomenal* mental causation in the Map's sense, the tenet does not bite."

### The Many-Worlds Defender
"Your historical-record argument against parsimony is worth examining: you cite heliocentrism initially needing more epicycles, atomic theory positing invisible entities, and general relativity replacing flat space with curved spacetime, and you conclude 'the simpler position was wrong'. But:
- *Modern* heliocentrism (post-Kepler) is dramatically simpler than Ptolemaic geocentrism with epicycles. You are taking an early-stage snapshot to support a conclusion the long-run picture refutes.
- Atomic theory and GR posited new entities but *unified* prior phenomena under fewer principles — the parsimony move was the right one once data demanded it.
- The actual lesson is 'parsimony at one descriptive level may require richness at another' — not 'parsimony has a bad track record'.
Your three-example argument is a rhetorical flourish, not an epistemological case. And it is doing load-bearing work in the article — every other rebuttal returns to it as the methodological foundation for resisting eliminativism's 'eliminate consciousness' move. If the foundation is shaky, the whole rebuttal pattern wobbles."

### The Empiricist (Popper's ghost)
"What experiment distinguishes:
1. Quasi-phenomenal states that systematically misrepresent themselves as classic phenomenal states (illusionism), from
2. Real phenomenal states (Map's view)?
The article cannot point to one. It cannot in principle, because phenomenal authority is granted to the very faculty whose reliability is contested. By construction, no third-party observation can adjudicate. By Popperian lights, this exits the scientific domain. You may reply that this critique cuts against illusionism too — and yes, it does. But the proper conclusion is *neither* position is empirically tractable, not 'the position I prefer wins on epistemic grounds I will assert without testing'. The article's preferred move — 'phenomenal evidence has an epistemic status incompatible with eliminativism' — is precisely the unfalsifiable foundationalism Quine and Sellars dismantled. Your section noting 'self-refutation does not obviously succeed' is intellectually honest; the same honesty applied to your own first-person foundationalism would be devastating."

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
"You list [buddhism-and-dualism](/concepts/buddhism-and-dualism/) under Further Reading and characterise Buddhist anatta as 'parallel to (and instructive contrast with) classical eliminativism about the self'. The framing is too convenient. Madhyamaka does not endorse a substantialist phenomenology in place of the deconstructed self. The same dependent-origination analysis (*pratītyasamutpāda*) that dissolves the substantial self also applies to the *phenomenal stream* — every dharma is empty of own-being (*svabhāva*). Your defence rests on a foundation — 'the phenomenal observer cannot be wrong about the existence of phenomenality' — that is, in Madhyamaka terms, *atmagraha* (self-grasping) directed not at a substantial self but at substantial phenomenal events. You cannot consistently borrow Buddhism as an ally against eliminativist accounts of self while rejecting eliminativist accounts of phenomenal events. Either dependent-origination is a general structure or it is a one-off concession to the no-self literature. The article needs to either acknowledge this asymmetry or rework the alliance."

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Question-begging rebuttal of the introspection-as-theory-laden move
- **File**: topics/eliminative-materialism.md
- **Location**: "The introspection-as-theory-laden move" subsection (lines 115)
- **Problem**: "The phenomenal observer cannot be wrong in the same way about the existence of phenomenality, because the observation is constitutive of what is observed" *asserts* what eliminativism contests. The constitutive thesis is the very dispute. Stating it as the rebuttal makes the argument circular at the central pivot.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either (a) make explicit that this is a *foundational claim* the Map asserts but cannot non-question-beggingly defend against an eliminativist, and lean into the disagreement as bedrock; or (b) supply an independent argument — e.g., from the structure of phenomenal *acquaintance* (Russellian), or from the asymmetry between observation-of-external-objects and observation-of-experience-itself. The current phrasing claims a victory by definition.

### Issue 2: The historical-record argument against parsimony cuts against the article's conclusion
- **File**: topics/eliminative-materialism.md
- **Location**: "Where the Map sees the move's failure" (line 113)
- **Problem**: The three examples (heliocentrism, atomic theory, GR) are presented as cases where "the simpler position was wrong". But mature heliocentrism *is* the simpler position; atomic theory unifies more phenomena under fewer principles; GR replaced one geometry with another, not "simple" with "complex". This argument carries significant rhetorical weight in the rebuttal but does not survive scrutiny.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Replace with a sharper claim — e.g., "parsimony interpreted as 'eliminate explanatorily-recalcitrant data' has a bad track record" — and cite cases that actually exemplify *that* failure mode (e.g., the elimination of "instinct" in early behaviourism). Alternatively, withdraw the universal historical claim and replace it with the more defensible domain-specific point: parsimony is unreliable when the domain may have hidden structure we have not yet uncovered.

### Issue 3: Internal tension between Honest Limitations and Map's-third-tenet rebuttal
- **File**: topics/eliminative-materialism.md
- **Location**: §"Honest Limitations" (line 129) vs. "The Map's third tenet" (line 121)
- **Problem**: The Honest Limitations section concedes that "the structural format of belief is open to revision" — i.e., propositional attitudes may not have the implementation structure folk psychology suggests. But the Map's-third-tenet paragraph treats "the practical indispensability of intentional psychology" as evidence against eliminativism. If the structural format of belief is revisable, then practical-indispensability arguments are consistent with structural eliminativism (beliefs as inferential summaries over distributed states, à la Dennett's intentional stance). The article cannot both concede the structural revision and use practical-indispensability as a knockdown.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Reconcile by distinguishing more carefully: the Map rejects eliminativism about *causal efficacy of intentional states*; it does not commit on whether those states have sentence-shaped structure. The current text conflates these two issues.

### Issue 4: Frankish's reply to the regress-against-illusionism is not engaged
- **File**: topics/eliminative-materialism.md
- **Location**: Last paragraph of "Principal Objections" section (line 107)
- **Problem**: The regress objection ("if the illusion is a phenomenal seeming, illusionism merely relocates the problem") is presented as if Frankish had not answered it. He has — explicitly, in §6 of the 2016 paper — by distinguishing quasi-phenomenal seeming from phenomenal seeming. The objection only bites if we assume the seeming is itself classic-phenomenal, which is precisely what the illusionist denies. As stated, the objection is a strawman of a position the article is otherwise charitable to.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add one sentence acknowledging Frankish's reply and explain why the Map still finds the regress force-able (e.g., the quasi-phenomenal/phenomenal distinction is itself unstable; Frankish's reply requires that the *appearance* of phenomenality have no phenomenal character whatsoever, which strains intuition). This would convert a strawman into a substantive disagreement.

### Issue 5: Missing engagement with predictive processing
- **File**: topics/eliminative-materialism.md
- **Location**: Throughout "Folk Psychology and the Replacement Programme" and "The Connectionist Challenge"
- **Problem**: The article engages Churchland (1981/1986) and Ramsey/Stich/Garon (1990) — i.e., the 1980s–90s versions of the eliminativist case. The most active contemporary front is predictive-processing / active-inference (Clark, Hohwy, Friston), which provides a *positive* cognitive-science model in which folk-psychological states might be *explained as* inferential summaries rather than *eliminated*. This is a softer eliminativism that the Map's case currently does not address.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add a brief subsection (or sentence in the Honest Limitations) acknowledging predictive processing as the contemporary descendant of the connectionist challenge, and note that the Map's response operates at a level of generality that should cover both. If a more substantive engagement is needed, queue a follow-up cross-review.

### Issue 6: The Buddhist alliance is asymmetric without acknowledgement
- **File**: topics/eliminative-materialism.md
- **Location**: Further Reading entry for [buddhism-and-dualism](/concepts/buddhism-and-dualism/) (line 151) and absence of body engagement
- **Problem**: The article borrows Buddhism as a parallel/contrast for eliminativism about the self but does not address that Madhyamaka extends the same dependent-origination analysis to *phenomenal events*, which would undercut the Map's foundationalism about phenomenal evidence. The alliance is selective without acknowledgement.
- **Severity**: Low–Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either acknowledge the asymmetry briefly, or move the buddhism cross-link into a more carefully-framed paragraph that flags the divergence on *what* is empty (self only, vs. self + phenomenal events).

## Counterarguments to Address

### The "introspection is constitutive" rebuttal
- **Current content says**: The phenomenal observer cannot be wrong about phenomenality because observation is constitutive of what is observed.
- **A critic would argue**: This is exactly what's contested. The eliminativist's claim is that the appearance of constitutive observation is itself a folk-theoretic artefact, and "I can't be wrong about it" is what an enculturated participant in the folk theory would *naturally* report.
- **Suggested response**: Recast as: "The Map treats this as a *foundational* methodological commitment rather than a derived conclusion. Its defence is by overall theoretical fit (Tenet 1, the failure of physicalism to close the gap, the Moorean confidence asymmetry) rather than by constitutive argument. Honest disagreement here is bedrock."

### The Moorean / modus-tollens move
- **Current content says**: "Modus tollens beats modus ponens" because phenomenal certainty is greater than any premise the illusionist could deploy.
- **A critic would argue**: The relative-confidence judgment is exactly what's at stake. Asserting it without defending the asymmetry just restates the disagreement at higher volume.
- **Suggested response**: Add one sentence explaining *why* phenomenal confidence is special — e.g., its role as the precondition for any cognitive activity (including the activity of doubting it). This converts an aphorism into a position.

### Practical indispensability vs. fictionalism
- **Current content says**: Mental fictionalism cannot account for the indispensability of belief-talk in legal and moral practice.
- **A critic would argue**: Toon's fictionalism explicitly accounts for indispensability via the as-if functional role of fictional discourse — the practice persists because the fiction is useful, not because it is true. The "indispensability ⇒ reality" move is what fictionalism rejects.
- **Suggested response**: Engage Toon's specific account of indispensable fiction (he draws on Walton's make-believe theory) rather than treating indispensability as straightforwardly dispositive.

## Strengths (Brief)

- **Two-strand distinction is genuinely useful.** Most secondary literature conflates classical eliminativism and illusionism. The article's clean separation is one of the strongest things on the site for this topic.
- **Honest Limitations section is rare and admirable.** Most defences of dualism do not concede the connectionist point or the meta-problem; the article does so explicitly. Preserve this.
- **Connectionist challenge is foregrounded as the strongest argument.** Many Map articles would have buried this; foregrounding it is intellectually honest.
- **The structural framing — "eliminativism is the limit case of physicalism" — is rhetorically effective.** It positions the Map's broader case well without misrepresenting eliminativism as a straw position.
- **The closing "why eliminativism persists" framing is generous and dialectically constructive** — explaining *why* serious philosophers hold it, rather than treating it as obviously wrong.
