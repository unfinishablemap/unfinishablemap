### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)

Kane would find the laws-and-dispositions refine's *pain asymbolia compound-property dissociation* the cluster's most consequential Kanean achievement. Pain asymbolia is the standard Kanean test case for whether *quality-power identity* survives empirical pressure: if asymbolic patients retain pain's sensory aspect but lose its motivational aspect, the identity theory appears falsified at its strongest test. The 02:29 UTC refine installs at line 168 a sophisticated reading: "asymbolia separates the compound rather than dissociating quality from power within a single property... what we colloquially call 'pain' is a compound of two distinct powerful qualities — sensory localisation (the bodily-mapped detection of damage) and aversive valence (the hurtfulness that motivates withdrawal). Asymbolic patients retain the sensory powerful quality and lose the aversive one, so the behavioural change tracks the loss of one quality-power identity rather than a dissociation within a single quality." This is the *preserve-the-position-by-getting-the-property-individuation-right* discipline Kane's own work has been moving toward: the apparent counterexample is reread by *finer-grained property individuation* rather than by abandoning the position. Each component property remains a unified powerful quality; what dissociates is the compound, not the identity of quality with power. Kane would single out *the behavioural change tracks the loss of one quality-power identity rather than a dissociation within a single quality* — the discipline of asking, when an alleged dissociation arises, whether the property being individuated was actually two properties from the start.

The imagery-void's function-phenomenology face (lines 50–54) is Kanean in a different way. The Kay, Keogh & Pearson (2024) finding that "aphantasics complete classical mental rotation tasks slower but more accurately than controls, with both groups showing the standard linear relation between angular disparity and response time" (line 52) creates a wedge with three options: "(a) imagery is a phenomenal accompaniment that does not do the cognitive work it appears to do; (b) two phenomenally distinct architectures perform isomorphic work; or (c) introspective reports of imagery systematically misdescribe the underlying process" (line 54). Each option is metaphysically loaded; none is verifiable from inside. Kane has long warned against frameworks that prematurely *select* among such options to preserve a preferred metaphysical position. The article's discipline of *naming the wedge without forcing a selection* — and explicitly stating "Each option is metaphysically loaded; none is verifiable from inside" — is the right Kanean response: preserve the genuine open question rather than collapse it into one's favoured horn.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)

McGinn would find the imagery-void's *Würzburg-aphantasia recurrence argument* the cluster's most centrally Mysterian achievement. The article observes (line 66): "The Würzburg-aphantasia recurrence is itself evidence the void is structural rather than methodological. The same phenomenon surfaces twice across a century with the same controversy structure, under different vocabularies and instruments. The territory keeps producing this shape because the shape is real." This is the kind of *historical-recurrence-as-evidence* discipline McGinn's own work has been moving toward. The Mysterian position holds that some questions are unsolvable for human cognition because the structure of the problem exceeds our cognitive resources for resolution; one diagnostic for such structural unsolvability is *recurrence across instruments and vocabularies that should have dissolved the problem if it were merely methodological*. The Würzburg School's introspection-controversy was retired with behaviourism; the post-2015 aphantasia literature reopens the same controversy with neuroimaging and behavioural-signature instruments. The fact that *the same shape* surfaces again under disjoint instruments is McGinnian evidence that the shape tracks something stable in the territory rather than something fragile in the method.

The article's §"Approaches to the Edge" (lines 76–90) is McGinnian in a different way. The article catalogues six approaches (behavioural signatures, neuroimaging, vividness questionnaires, apophatic mapping, cross-modal probes, linguistic divergence analysis) and names which face of the void each approach can and cannot reach. The closing observation — "None of these reach the fidelity face directly. They detect *whether something imagery-shaped is happening*, not *how accurate the image is*. The fidelity face is the most resistant of the three, and probably permanent" (line 90) — is the kind of *limits-are-real-and-rank-orderable* discipline at its most mature. McGinn would single out *probably permanent* as the right epistemic posture: not "permanent" (which would overclaim) and not "currently inaccessible" (which would underclaim), but *probably permanent* — the strongest claim the evidence supports without overreaching. The article's discipline of *ranking the faces by probable-permanence* — fidelity most resistant, inter-subjective somewhat tractable through behavioural divergence, function-phenomenology already partially mapped through aphantasia research — is the discipline of *honest cartography of the limits* rather than uniform mystery.
