# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-07-19
**Content reviewed**: `obsidian/topics/consciousness-and-the-ontology-of-temporal-becoming.md`

## Executive Summary

The article is a strong, well-defended synthesis that explicitly pre-empts several standard objections (circularity, rate-of-passage, relativity). Its remaining weaknesses are not sloppiness but over-reach: a deflationary argument (Price) is inverted into a realist one; the constitution-over-causation move rests on a near-tautological premise; the reflective-equilibrium defence leans on a fringe interpretation (Wigner/Stapp consciousness-collapse) presented as "independent motivation"; and the creativity thesis asserts a contestable in-principle limit on computation as settled. Citations are accurate and correctly attributed — no fabrication found.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist
The whole apparatus of "felt passage," "durée," and "autonoetic settledness" is folk phenomenology dressed as ontology. The article's second argument for *constitution over causation* — "removing consciousness leaves no phenomenological arrow at all" — is trivially true of anything defined as conscious experience and proves nothing about the structure of reality. You have relabelled "consciousness causes the appearance of passage" as "consciousness constitutes the phenomenological arrow" and called the relabelling a metaphysical discovery.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist
The B-theorists you cite (Prosser, Hoerl) already grant everything real here — local ordering, change, the veridical representation of succession — while declining the metaphysical inflation. You concede they are "more sophisticated than crude illusionism" and then treat their position as a *cost* rather than as a live rival that may simply be correct. Calling illusionism "every moment of experienced passage becomes a kind of mistake" is a rhetorical move: a representation being non-veridical about *metaphysical passage* does not make the *experience* a mistake, any more than seeing a stationary rainbow is an error about optics.

### The Quantum Skeptic
"Consciousness modulates collapse in neural systems" is asserted, never quantified. Where is the decoherence-timescale calculation showing a window in which conscious modulation could bias an outcome? The article offloads all mechanism to `[[collapse-and-time]]` and `[[conservation-laws-and-mental-causation]]`, so as a standalone page it presents a physics-laden claim with zero physics.

### The Many-Worlds Defender
The "No Many Worlds" section refutes MWI purely by tenet incompatibility ("Temporal becoming requires that possibilities genuinely narrow") — which is honest framework-marking, since it sits in *Relation to Site Perspective*, but it means the article never engages the Everettian's actual reply: branch-relative observers *do* experience narrowing indexically. The article's strongest anti-MWI premise is exactly the one an Everettian denies.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
What observation could distinguish "consciousness constitutes the phenomenological arrow" from "consciousness merely represents a physical arrow it did not constitute"? The article's own constitution-argument turns on a counterfactual (remove consciousness → no phenomenological arrow) that is unobservable by construction. The MQI "biasing without injecting energy" is engineered to be undetectable. This is metaphysics, and the article should own that rather than borrow the measurement problem's empirical credibility.

### The Buddhist Philosopher
The "settled self" that autonoetically owns its past and faces an open future is precisely the reified continuant that dependent origination dissolves. The article makes the temporally-extended self a *truth-maker* ("retention and causal inheritance are genuine relations to real things"), doubling down on the very substantialism a process view (which it elsewhere invokes via Whitehead) should undercut.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Price's deflationary argument inverted into a realist one
- **File**: consciousness-and-the-ontology-of-temporal-becoming.md
- **Location**: "The Puzzle of Time's Arrow" — the causal-arrow paragraph (line 65)
- **Problem**: Price argues the causal asymmetry is *perspectival — an anthropocentric projection* (i.e. it is *not* a mind-independent feature of the world). The article "draws a different lesson": because "agents' temporal orientation shapes how causation appears, consciousness may play a *constitutive role in temporal asymmetry more broadly*." This recruits an anti-realist premise (asymmetry is mere projection/appearance) to support a realist conclusion (consciousness genuinely constitutes asymmetry). It is an epistemic-reading → metaphysical-reading equivocation of the kind flagged in `[[evidential-status-discipline]]`: "how causation *appears*" (epistemic/appearance) is silently upgraded to "constitutes temporal asymmetry" (metaphysical constitution).
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either (a) drop the appeal to Price as support and present the constitutive claim on its own footing, or (b) explicitly acknowledge the inversion — "the Map agrees with Price that agent-perspective is doing the work, but reads the resulting asymmetry as constituted-and-real rather than projected-and-unreal" — and supply the bridging argument for why a projection should be reclassified as a constitution.

### Issue 2: "More broadly" over-reaches past the article's own later restriction
- **File**: consciousness-and-the-ontology-of-temporal-becoming.md
- **Location**: line 65 ("constitutive role in temporal asymmetry more broadly") vs the "Consciousness as Constitutive of the Phenomenological Arrow" section (lines 115-121)
- **Problem**: The later section is careful to restrict the constitution claim to the *phenomenological* arrow and explicitly concedes the *physical* asymmetry is consciousness-independent ("collapse happened before life existed"; "The physical arrow is necessary but not sufficient"). The early phrase "temporal asymmetry more broadly" claims exactly the wider scope the article then disowns. Internal tension.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Narrow line 65 to "consciousness may play a constitutive role in the *phenomenological* arrow specifically" so the framing matches the defended thesis.

### Issue 3: The "mark of constitution" premise is near-tautological
- **File**: consciousness-and-the-ontology-of-temporal-becoming.md
- **Location**: "Consciousness as Constitutive of the Phenomenological Arrow," second consideration (line 119)
- **Problem**: The argument distinguishing constitution from causation runs: "a cause can be absent while the effect is merely diminished, but a constitutive element's absence eliminates the constituted phenomenon" — then notes that removing consciousness eliminates the phenomenological arrow entirely. But "the phenomenological arrow" is *defined* as the experienced/felt directionality; that removing consciousness removes conscious experience is true of every mental phenomenon and cannot by itself adjudicate constitution vs causation. The premise smuggles the conclusion. (The first and third considerations — the from-the-inside point and the no-physical-correlate point — carry more weight and should lead.)
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Demote or rework the second consideration; the eliminativist reads it as a definitional trick. Anchor the constitution claim on the "what collapse is like from the inside" argument (first consideration) and the qualitative-character argument (third), which are not merely definitional.

### Issue 4: Wigner/Stapp consciousness-collapse presented as "independent motivation"
- **File**: consciousness-and-the-ontology-of-temporal-becoming.md
- **Location**: "The Mutual Support Question" (line 109)
- **Problem**: The anti-circularity defence depends on the claim that "consciousness participates in quantum collapse has independent motivation from the measurement problem." Consciousness-*causes*-collapse (Wigner/Stapp) is a fringe-minority interpretation, not a neutral consequence of the measurement problem; most responses to measurement (decoherence, GRW, Bohm, Everett, QBism) invoke no consciousness at all. Presenting it as free-standing "independent motivation" overstates its standing and weakens the reflective-equilibrium argument — a critic will say both legs of the "equilibrium" lean on the same contested dualist prior.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Downgrade to honest framing: "the consciousness-collapse tradition (Wigner, Stapp) is a minority interpretation of the measurement problem, but one whose motivation predates and does not presuppose growing-block ontology." That preserves the non-circularity point without inflating the interpretation's acceptance.

### Issue 5: Creativity thesis asserts a contestable in-principle limit on computation
- **File**: consciousness-and-the-ontology-of-temporal-becoming.md
- **Location**: "From Temporal Becoming to Creativity" (lines 133-135); echoed in the lead ("genuine creativity no atemporal process can replicate")
- **Problem**: The claim that computational systems "operate over fixed representational primitives" and cannot achieve "generation of *new* primitives" is stated as an in-principle limit, but it is empirically contestable — representation-learning systems arguably induce new primitives, and the article gives no argument foreclosing this beyond assertion. This is a load-bearing empirical claim (it connects explicitly to `[[ai-consciousness]]` and `[[topics/free-will]]`) presented as settled. The single hedge "appears to require" at line 135 does not cover the unhedged lead claim.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add one clause acknowledging the ML representation-learning rejoinder and specifying what the Map means by "new primitive" such that learned features do not count (e.g. primitives that restructure the space rather than being selected within a pre-given architecture) — or soften the lead's "no atemporal process can replicate" to match the hedged body.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The rate-of-passage "category error" reply
- **Current content says**: Asking the rate of the block's growth "commits a category error—like asking how fast space extends spatially" (line 147).
- **A critic would argue**: The objection survives reframing without a rate: is growth *uniform*? Could it have been faster or slower? Any answer reintroduces a comparison standard (a meta-time), which is what the objection targets. Calling the question a category error asserts, rather than shows, that growth is not a process-in-time.
- **Suggested response**: Concede the objection has a non-rate form and answer it directly — e.g. that "uniformity" is ill-posed because there is no second becoming to compare against — rather than dissolving it by analogy.

### The B-theorist is a rival, not a cost
- **Current content says**: Eternalism's cost is that "every moment of experienced passage becomes a kind of mistake" (line 83).
- **A critic would argue**: Prosser/Hoerl explicitly deny that non-veridicality-about-passage makes the experience a mistake; the experience is veridical about change and succession. The article's framing caricatures the sophisticated B-theory it elsewhere says it "takes seriously."
- **Suggested response**: Replace "a kind of mistake" with the sophisticated B-theorist's actual position and locate the disagreement precisely (metaphysical passage), preserving the concession already made at line 81.
