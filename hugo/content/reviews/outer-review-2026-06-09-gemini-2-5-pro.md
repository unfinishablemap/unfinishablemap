## Introduction and Scope of the Audit

The submitted manuscript under review attempts to formulate a definitive defense of non-reductive, phenomenally real consciousness—specifically advocating for an agent-causal, interactionist dualist framework—by leveraging the psychological phenomenon of "choking under pressure." The manuscript asserts that because "you cannot confabulate interference," the systematic tracking of physical motor disruption by the phenomenal "feel" of effortful monitoring proves that phenomenal consciousness exercises irreducible, top-down causal efficacy within the physical domain. The author explicitly claims this bidirectional interaction satisfies the rigorous requirements for agent causation and decisively defeats the eliminativist challenges posed by physicalist illusionists.

This review undertakes an exhaustive, hostile pre-publication audit of the manuscript’s empirical claims, literature citations, structural framework, and logical inferences. The analysis assesses the submission strictly against the state of the art in the philosophy of mind, cognitive neuroscience, computational modeling of consciousness, and sports psychology, with a deliberate focus on the 2020–2025 period. The audit reveals a manuscript that is structurally anachronistic, empirically fragile, and philosophically naive. The author systematically conflates functional attentional modulation with phenomenal consciousness, evades the most sophisticated iterations of strong illusionism (specifically those articulated by Frankish and Kammerer in 2025), entirely brackets leading computational alternatives such as the Attention Schema Theory (AST), and relies on obsolete models of motor control from the 1980s and early 2000s while ignoring the vast modern physiological and affective neuroscience literature.

The author’s core argumentative strategy relies on what they term the "Causal Tracking Problem" and "Mode Asymmetry." The manuscript argues that if phenomenology were an empty misrepresentation, the precise, systematic correlation between the phenomenal feel of effortful monitoring and the physical degradation of motor execution would constitute an inexplicable, massive coincidence. Furthermore, the manuscript leans on Endel Tulving’s 1985 hierarchy of anoetic and autonoetic consciousness to claim that different conscious modes possess radically distinct causal profiles, a phenomenon that supposedly defies explanation under a purely functionalist or illusionist paradigm.

The ensuing sections systematically dismantle the manuscript's core arguments. The critique exposes the author's definitional sleights of hand regarding phenomenal realism, their failure to engage with the contemporary "distortion thesis" of illusionism, their empirical conflation of distinct neurocognitive processes, and their reliance on a truncated, highly curated reading of the behavioral sciences that ignores the profound role of the autonomic nervous system in performance degradation. Ultimately, the manuscript operates within a closed conceptual loop, arguing against a straw-man version of materialism while completely shielding itself from the empirical and philosophical rigors of the current decade.

## The Metaphysics of Interference: Definitional Fallacies and Phenomenal Realism

The manuscript’s primary philosophical ambition is to utilize the choking phenomenon as an empirical wedge against strong illusionism. Illusionism, the radical physicalist stance that phenomenal consciousness does not exist but merely *seems* to exist due to an introspective cognitive error, is purportedly defeated by the manuscript's invocation of the "Causal Tracking Problem." The manuscript asserts that the systematic correlation between the feeling of conscious interference and actual physical degradation is proof of phenomenological causal efficacy. This argument fundamentally misunderstands the illusionist project, commits a severe epistemic-to-metaphysical slide, and collapses under the weight of contemporary philosophical literature regarding the nature of phenomenal definitions.

### The Strait of Definitions and the Scylla of Implicitly Loaded Concepts

The manuscript assumes that the "feel" of conscious effort during an athletic choking event is a metaphysically innocent datum that requires a top-down causal explanation. By framing the debate around why different "misrepresentations" reliably produce different motor consequences, the author begs the question against the strong illusionist. This precise definitional fallacy has been rigorously formalized in the recent literature, most notably in the 2025 work of François Kammerer.

Kammerer (2025) outlines how phenomenal realists attempt to corner illusionists by deploying "innocent" definitions of phenomenal consciousness that implicitly encode metaphysically loaded, anti-physicalist assumptions. In "Defining consciousness and denying its existence: Sailing between Charybdis and Scylla," Kammerer utilizes a nautical metaphor to map the dialectical space. On one side lies Charybdis, representing the strategy of defining "phenomenal consciousness" in an overtly loaded, philosophically demanding manner (e.g., defining it as essentially non-physical, ineffable, or possessing haecceity). While this renders illusionism highly attractive—because physicalists can easily deny the existence of such magical properties—it risks committing a straw-man fallacy against phenomenal realists who claim they only mean the everyday "what it is like" aspect of experience. On the other side lies Scylla, representing the strategy of defining phenomenal consciousness "innocently," simply as the subjective feel of an experience, such as the feeling of effort during a golf putt.

Kammerer demonstrates that Ulysses (the strong illusionist) must sail directly toward Scylla. The supposedly innocent definition of phenomenal consciousness is the trap that the manuscript currently falls into. The concept that captures what the phenomenal realist means by "feel" is explicitly innocent but *implicitly loaded*. When the author claims that "disruption occurs specifically when there is the phenomenological 'feel' of effortful, self-focused monitoring" , the argument implicitly assumes that the *feel itself*, defined as a non-functional, qualitative state, is the causal relatum responsible for the motor disruption.

The strong illusionist, however, operates on a purely functional and representational paradigm. In the illusionist model, the biological organism instantiates complex, top-down prefrontal control algorithms that interfere with automated cerebellar and basal ganglia motor schemas. The organism's introspective system monitors this functional conflict and generates a highly compressed, schematic representation of it. This functional representation is then conceptualized by the system as possessing an ineffable, phenomenal "feel." The causal work—the actual disruption of the physical performance—is done entirely by the computational conflict between the prefrontal and procedural functional architectures. The "feel" is merely the introspective system's inaccurate, quasi-phenomenal gloss on that functional conflict.

The manuscript fails entirely to demonstrate why a functional architecture cannot exhibit systematic tracking between an internal state conflict and a subsequent behavioral degradation. Instead, it chooses to declare the phenomenon a victory for agent causation by definitional fiat. By treating the phenomenological report as unassailable proof of metaphysical efficacy, the author commits an epistemic-to-metaphysical slide: inferring from the fact that we *know* our mental states as phenomenal, that they must *be* phenomenal to exert force.

## Introspective Inaccuracy and the Distortion Thesis

The most glaring logical leap in the manuscript is the triumphant, supposedly incontrovertible assertion: "you cannot confabulate interference". The author contends that while an epiphenomenalist or illusionist might dismiss a positive action (e.g., a person deciding to raise their arm and then doing so) as post-hoc confabulation—claiming the brain made the physical decision first and then generated the illusion of a conscious choice—the disruption of a physical system by a putatively inert monitoring state is logically impossible unless that state possesses actual causal power. The manuscript argues that there is no logical reason why a causally inert conscious self-monitoring would systematically disrupt motor execution.

This argument fundamentally mischaracterizes what the strong illusionist claims is being confabulated. It is a profound category error. The illusionist does not claim, nor have they ever claimed, that the *physical interference* is an illusion. The breakdown of the athlete's motor execution is a verifiable, objective, physical event. What is confabulated—or more accurately in contemporary terms, what is *distorted*—is the underlying nature of the state that is causing the interference.

### The Dimensions of Introspective Misrepresentation

The mechanics of this cognitive illusion have been explicitly addressed and formalized in the recent literature. Maxim Gorbachev and Keith Frankish (2025) have proposed what they term the "distortion thesis". The distortion thesis is central to the illusionist approach; it is the specific claim that introspective mechanisms systematically misrepresent neural processes in a way that strongly inclines the cognitive subject to judge that they possess phenomenal properties, thereby creating the persistent "illusion" of phenomenality.

Gorbachev and Frankish recognize that the illusion of consciousness is not a simple hallucination. They map out eight distinct patterns of introspective distortion based on three critical dimensions: positiveness, conceptuality, and dispellability.

1. **Positiveness** refers to whether the introspective system adds illusory features to the representation or merely omits physical details, creating a gap that the subject misinterprets as non-physicality.
2. **Conceptuality** concerns whether the distortion occurs at the level of sensory representation or at the level of conceptual judgment.
3. **Dispellability** examines whether the illusion can be cognitively overridden (like a mirage) or if it is cognitively impenetrable (like the Müller-Lyer illusion).

When the analytical framework of the distortion thesis is applied to the choking phenomenon, it provides a comprehensive, non-phenomenal explanation that the manuscript entirely ignores. During a high-pressure sporting event, the organism's cognitive system allocates explicit working memory and explicit monitoring resources (which are entirely functional, physical states) to a task normally governed by implicit, highly automated procedural systems. This functional shift causes a massive computational bottleneck, leading to degraded physical performance.

Simultaneously, the organism's introspective mechanisms monitor this functional shift and *distort* the functional reality according to the dimensions outlined by Gorbachev and Frankish. The introspective system generates a highly positive, conceptually rich, and undisspellable representation of the event, forcing the subject to believe that an immaterial, phenomenal "self" is effortfully interfering with the body. The introspective system confabulates the *phenomenality* of the monitoring process, not the reality of the monitoring process itself.

The manuscript attempts to circumvent this by claiming that the "Infinite Regress Problem" undermines illusionism—arguing that if physical matter cannot generate consciousness, it cannot generate the illusion of consciousness, because "misrepresentation presupposes presentation". Frankish’s 2020-2025 work explicitly dismantles this phenomenological regress. Frankish argues that illusions need not be phenomenal illusions at all; they can be purely cognitive, theoretical, or dispositional errors. Just as pre-scientific cultures were entirely wrong about the ontological nature of heat or life without experiencing a "phenomenal illusion" of phlogiston or vital élan, a cognitive system can be completely wrong about the nature of its own functional processing. Our introspective systems represent our experiences as having properties they simply do not possess. This representation is itself just functional information processing, not a phenomenal experience of an illusion.

Because the manuscript operates on a straw-man version of eliminativism, it fails to engage with the sophisticated, multi-dimensional models of introspective distortion published in 2025. The manuscript demands to know why different "misrepresentations" would reliably produce different motor consequences (the so-called Mode Asymmetry). The distortion thesis answers this effortlessly: different cognitive processing regimes (implicit vs. explicit working memory) have radically distinct physical causal profiles. The introspective system merely tracks these distinct physical profiles and applies different illusory phenomenological labels to them. The systematic correlation is fully preserved without conceding an inch to phenomenal realism or agent causation.

## Functionalist Blindspots: The Attention Schema Theory as a Defeater

The manuscript assumes a binary, artificially constrained theoretical landscape: either phenomenal consciousness is a genuine, top-down causal agent that interferes with physical processes, or it is an epiphenomenal byproduct that can be easily dismissed by the absurdity of a purely coincidental correlation. This false dichotomy is sustained only by the manuscript's systematic and deliberate exclusion of leading computational and functionalist frameworks developed over the last decade. Foremost among these inexcusable omissions is the Attention Schema Theory (AST), originally proposed by Michael Graziano and widely adopted in artificial intelligence, cognitive modeling, and neurobiology between 2020 and 2025.

### The Architecture and Ontology of the Attention Schema

The manuscript repeatedly utilizes the concept of "attention"—specifically, explicit, self-focused monitoring—as a direct proxy for phenomenal consciousness. The author argues that if attention disrupts skilled performance, then phenomenal consciousness is causally efficacious, thereby proving interactionist dualism. This uncritical conflation of attention and phenomenal experience is precisely the conceptual knot that AST untangles.

According to AST, the brain does not merely allocate attention as a raw physical resource; it constructs a compact, integrated, and constantly updated internal model of its own attentional state. To understand this, one must look at the precedent of the "body schema." The brain does not control the limbs or navigate the body through space by continuously calculating the complex physics and biomechanics of every muscle fiber, tendon, and joint angle in real time. Such a computational load would be biologically impossible. Instead, it constructs a "body schema"—a highly simplified, functional understanding of the body's current state and configuration.

Similarly, the brain must control its own information processing resources. It does this by constructing an "attention schema" to monitor, predict, and control its own deployment of cognitive focus. Crucially, this attention schema is a data structure. It is a purely physical, functional computation. Because the schema is a highly simplified model designed for rapid, real-time control, it does not represent the complex neural machinery of synapses, neurotransmitters, and competitive lateral inhibition that actually constitutes attention. Instead, it depicts attention as a mysterious, ethereal essence, an invisible property that the mind projects onto objects. In other words, the brain’s physical control model *computes* the illusion of phenomenality in order to efficiently manage its data streams. Phenomenal consciousness, under AST, is not a metaphysical property; it is the specific informational content of a cybernetic control model.

### AST as a Complete Physicalist Explanation for the Choking Phenomenon

The omission of AST destroys the manuscript's claim that choking under pressure strictly necessitates an agent-causal or interactionist dualist explanation. Viewed through the lens of AST, the choking phenomenon is an entirely predictable, mathematically necessary consequence of a physical control architecture operating out of bounds.

When an expert athlete performs fluidly—a state the manuscript anachronistically terms "anoetic consciousness" following Tulving —the brain's procedural motor schemas are operating efficiently without requiring top-down attentional modulation. The highly detailed, high-resolution models in the cerebellum and motor cortex execute the physical sequence seamlessly. However, under high-stress conditions, the brain's attention schema is explicitly directed toward the kinematics of the motor action.

The attention schema is a high-level, low-resolution data structure designed for general cognitive control and novel problem solving. When this low-resolution schema attempts to override or heavily modulate the highly detailed, high-resolution motor schema, it creates a severe computational conflict and a bandwidth bottleneck. The low-resolution controller cannot process the micro-adjustments required for expert execution, resulting in the physical degradation of performance known as choking.

The system accurately recognizes that a top-down control process (the attention schema) is interfering with a bottom-up process (the motor schema). However, because the attention schema represents itself to the broader cognitive and linguistic systems as an ethereal, subjective "feeling" rather than a computational data structure, the human subject verbally reports that "my conscious awareness interfered with my body."

The causal work is being done entirely by the physical properties of the attention schema. The manuscript's claim that functionalist accounts struggle to explain why the functional distinction maps onto the phenomenological distinction is definitively, comprehensively answered by AST: the phenomenological distinction *is* the functional distinction, as represented by the brain's internal control models. By completely ignoring AST—a framework that saw massive integration into AI consciousness assessments, multidimensional indicator validations, and robotic modeling between 2024 and 2025 (e.g., Elamrani et al., 2025; Sorensen, 2025)—the manuscript has bracketed the exact competing framework that cleanly solves its central riddle.

## The Empirical Dissociation of Attention, Working Memory, and Consciousness

The manuscript's entire argument rests on a rigid, unbreakable correlation between conscious attention and motor interference. The author explicitly assumes that the "explicit monitoring" that disrupts choking is unequivocally a state of phenomenal consciousness. However, the cognitive neuroscience and philosophy of the 2020s have forcefully demonstrated that top-down attention, working memory, and phenomenal consciousness are dissociable neurocognitive processes. By conflating these distinct mechanisms, the manuscript misattributes the causal power of attentional networks to phenomenal consciousness, rendering its empirical claims invalid.

### The Cognitive Access vs. Phenomenal Content Debate

The distinction between cognitive access (which includes top-down attention, working memory, and global broadcast) and phenomenal consciousness (the subjective qualitative experience) has been a cornerstone of empirical consciousness research. This distinction, championed prominently by Ned Block, has been rigorously tested and heavily debated in the current decade. The manuscript assumes a paradigm where attention and consciousness are identical, or at least perfectly correlated, such that an attentional interference is logically and empirically equivalent to a phenomenal interference.

Contemporary research categorically refutes this assumed equivalence. The literature robustly demonstrates that attention can modulate sensory processing and motor execution regardless of whether the stimulus or the monitoring process is phenomenally experienced. For example, in affective blindsight, patients process, evaluate, and behaviorally respond to basic emotional stimuli without any phenomenal consciousness of the stimulus. Similarly, in somnambulism (sleepwalking), complex, top-down motor navigation, obstacle avoidance, and even highly coordinated activities occur in the complete absence of phenomenal awareness.

If complex, coordinated motor action can be both executed and disrupted by non-conscious attentional and affective processing, the manuscript's central premise—that the disruption observed in choking must be uniquely attributed to the *phenomenal* aspect of the monitoring—is empirically unsupported. The causal work is done by the cognitive access mechanisms, not the phenomenal content.

### Electroencephalographic and Perturbational Evidence

The dissociation between attention and consciousness is not merely theoretical; it is structurally and temporally mapped in the human brain. The manuscript relies entirely on outdated behavioral models from 2001 (such as Beilock & Carr's explicit monitoring theory) without integrating any modern electrophysiological metrics.

Recent studies utilizing independent component analysis and decoding of electroencephalography (EEG) have successfully isolated the neural profiles of attention from those of conscious experience. Hatamimajoumerd et al. (2022) demonstrated distinct temporal and spatial EEG decoding patterns that separate feedforward processing, local recurrent interactions, and broad top-down attentional access. Their findings explicitly delineate the attentional mechanisms of consciousness from the phenomenological stages. The brain architectures responsible for attentional load, working memory allocation, and explicit monitoring are largely orthogonal to those responsible for phenomenal experience.

Furthermore, data derived from the Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI)—which has emerged as the most sensitive, specific, and empirically-validated electrophysiological index of consciousness in the 2020s—provides devastating counter-evidence to the manuscript's assumptions. Research utilizing PCI demonstrates that patients with severe prefrontal damage (the exact cortical regions responsible for explicit working memory, top-down attention, and the "explicit monitoring" that causes choking) can still yield PCI values comparable to healthy, conscious individuals, provided their posterior-central cortical regions remain spared.

This creates a powerful double dissociation. It proves that the prefrontal explicit monitoring networks responsible for "choking under pressure" are distinct from the posterior-central networks that instantiate phenomenal consciousness. Therefore, when a golfer attempts a high-pressure putt and their explicit monitoring disrupts the automated swing, it is the prefrontal working memory network causing the physical disruption, not the phenomenological network. The manuscript's assertion that phenomenal consciousness itself is performing the causal work is flatly contradicted by the neuroanatomical, neurofunctional, and electrophysiological data of the current decade. The author is attempting to solve a metaphysical problem using the wrong anatomical variable.

## The Obsolescence of the "Explicit Monitoring" Paradigm in Motor Control

The final, and perhaps most empirically fatal, structural flaw of the manuscript is its reliance on a severely antiquated, highly sanitized model of the "choking under pressure" phenomenon. The article bases its entire causal sequence on the "explicit monitoring theory" proposed by Beilock and Carr in 2001, which itself builds on Baumeister's initial sociological work from 1984. It marries this behavioral theory to Endel Tulving's 1985 tripartite hierarchy of consciousness (anoetic, noetic, autonoetic) to argue that shifting from a lower, absorbed conscious mode to a higher, self-aware mode disrupts performance.

This theoretical framing completely ignores the massive paradigm shifts in sports psychology, motor control, affective neuroscience, and evolutionary biology that have occurred over the last twenty years, particularly the explosion of interdisciplinary research in the 2020s.

### The Rejection of Pure "Explicit Monitoring" and the Role of the Autonomic Nervous System

The manuscript presents choking as a purely cognitive, phenomenological event: attention smoothly shifts to procedural execution, disrupting the procedural system via conscious intentionality. However, rigorous empirical replications and extensions in the 2020–2025 period have demonstrated that choking is rarely, if ever, a purely cognitive phenomenon isolated to explicit monitoring.

Modern research highlights the profound, indispensable role of physiological arousal, affective distress, and the sympathetic nervous system in performance degradation. A comprehensive 2025 study on "Mind-Body Synergy" evaluating athlete stress levels explicitly redefines choking under pressure as a catastrophic systemic interaction. It is not merely a shift in attentional focus; it involves a "spiraling of anxious thoughts triggering a sympathetic surge, leading to muscle tension and impaired fine motor control, which then feeds back into more catastrophic thinking".

This sympathetic surge includes measurable changes in neuroendocrine markers, cortisol spikes, inflammation, and cardiovascular dynamics that directly impair the neuromuscular junction and fine motor unit recruitment. Similarly, interdisciplinary research into academic choking (such as severe mathematics anxiety) published in 2025 demonstrates that anxiety actively drains the mental resources required for logical reasoning, indicating that the disruption is caused by emotional and affective flooding of the working memory system, not merely "self-focused monitoring" of a skill.

By excising the entire autonomic and affective nervous system from its analysis, the manuscript presents a sterile, mathematically clean version of choking that perfectly fits its philosophical agenda but bears absolutely no resemblance to the biological reality of the phenomenon as understood in the 2020s. The disruption is not caused by the irreducible "phenomenology of mental effort"; it is caused by a massive, systemic neurochemical and sympathetic cascade triggered by deep-seated threat perception.

### The Obsolescence of Tulving's Rigid Hierarchy in Evolutionary Context

The manuscript relies heavily on the rigid assertion that skilled automaticity occurs in "anoetic" consciousness (which it defines as experiential but strictly non-reflective), while choking is triggered by the sudden intrusion of "autonoetic" (self-knowing) consciousness. This reliance on Tulving’s 1985 cognitive hierarchy to explain embodied action is fundamentally incompatible with modern affective neuroscience, which has fundamentally restructured our understanding of subcortical processing and the evolutionary origins of consciousness.

In his 2025 magnum opus "On the Evolution, Science and Metaphysics of Consciousness," Walter Veit synthesizes contemporary affective neuroscience to demonstrate that the evaluative, affective, and conscious systems of vertebrates are rooted deeply in subcortical structures. Drawing on the legacy of Jaak Panksepp's affective neuroscience (which established the subcortical basis of primary emotional systems), Veit and others argue that the seamless, "flow" state of expert performance is not merely "anoetic" cognitive processing as defined by Tulving. Rather, it is a highly integrated, affectively valenced state of subcortical and cortical synchronization.

When a subject chokes under pressure, it is not merely a clean transition from anoetic to autonoetic cognition. Rather, evolutionary threat-detection mechanisms in the amygdala, periaqueductal gray, and related subcortical networks override the synchronized flow state, forcing the prefrontal cortex to attempt manual, top-down control over a musculoskeletal system that is simultaneously being flooded with stress hormones. The manuscript’s attempt to map a complex, whole-brain, affective-motor crisis onto a simplistic, 40-year-old cognitive hierarchy demonstrates a profound disconnection from current neurobiological, evolutionary, and psychological literature. It uses antiquated maps to chart modern territory.
