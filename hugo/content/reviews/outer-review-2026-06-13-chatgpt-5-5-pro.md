## Major claim-audit findings

### 1. “Empirically established across the primary literature” is overstated

The article’s central comparative schema depends on four broad tradition-level summaries: Buddhist equanimity, Bhakti/Sufi ecstatic love, Christian consolation/aridity, and Daoist naturalness. The page cites primary classics, but it does not provide source passages, page-specific references, or secondary scholarship adequate to the breadth of the claim. A safer formulation would be: “Authoritative practice texts in these traditions foreground contrasting affective ideals.” That would preserve the article’s insight while avoiding the stronger claim that the divergence is already empirically settled across traditions.

### 2. The Buddhist section conflates fourth-jhāna neutrality with Buddhism generally

The Buddhist evidence for fourth-jhāna neutrality is real. Canonical jhāna descriptions include first and second jhānas suffused with rapture and pleasure, the third as a pleasant abiding, and the fourth as neither pleasure nor pain with purified equanimity. The Visuddhimagga also treats fourth-jhāna feeling as neither painful nor pleasant and equanimity as purified. [Access to Insight+1](https://www.accesstoinsight.org/ptf/dhamma/sacca/sacca4/samma-samadhi/jhana.html)

But that same evidence weakens the article’s broader “Buddhist affective flatness” framing. Buddhist advanced practice includes pleasurable, rapturous, equanimous, compassion-based, devotional, and sometimes challenging or destabilizing affect. Crucially, jhāna is concentrative absorption meditation, not simply open monitoring. A 2024 systematic definition and classification paper describes the jhānas as advanced concentrative absorption states and notes significant disagreements across contemporary jhāna manuals, while a 2025 Cerebral Cortex study describes Theravāda jhāna as ACAM-J, a series of eight advanced concentrative absorption states whose neuroscience remains underexplored. [Springer+1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12671-024-02367-w)

The better revision is to distinguish: fourth-jhāna affective neutrality, open-monitoring equanimity, brahmavihāra equanimity, and witness-consciousness phenomenology. They may converge, but the article currently compresses them.

### 3. Bhakti and Sufi “maximal positive valence” needs serious narrowing

The Bhakti comparison is plausible for some Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava sources, but the target article treats bhakti-rasa too monolithically. The Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu structure includes sādhana-bhakti, bhāva-bhakti, prema-bhakti, and a graded rasa taxonomy, not a uniform ecstatic state. Likewise the Sufi material lumps Junayd, Hallāj, and Rūmī as if they all straightforwardly occupy the ecstatic-love pole. Junayd is often treated as emblematic of sober Sufism, contrasted with intoxicated Sufi strands; the Routledge Handbook excerpt available online explicitly says Junayd and his followers prefer sobriety to intoxication. [topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions/)

A stronger version would separate “intoxicated/ecstatic Sufi lineages and texts” from “sober Sufi fanā’/baqā’ disciplines,” and would not use Junayd as evidence for maximal positive valence without qualification.

### 4. Christian and Daoist profiles are suggestive but under-defended

The Christian oscillation profile is intuitively plausible, but the article needs a stage model and source differentiation. John of the Cross’s aridity, Teresa’s delights, and The Cloud’s naked intent do not obviously form a single “Christian affective signature” unless the article explains whether it is describing endpoints, intermediate stages, or distinct schools. The Daoist “naturalness” profile likewise needs source-dating and compilation caveats. The parent article already notes that Daoist and Indian contemplative streams are not clearly independent, and flags the Sino-Indian channel as the weakest independent-pair claim. The target article should import that warning directly, because it currently says the profiles are well attested across “multiple unconnected cultures.” [topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions/)

## Citation and literature-currency audit

The empirical paragraph is stale for a 2026 article. Its core contemplative-science support is Lutz et al. 2008, Fredrickson et al. 2008, and Zeng et al. 2015. Those are relevant, but they are no longer sufficient for claims about style-specific affective profiles, loving-kindness outcomes, neuroplasticity, or advanced meditative states. [topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions/)

The loving-kindness claim needs active-control caveats. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 23 randomized controlled studies found positive effects of loving-kindness and compassion meditation but emphasized methodological limitations and the need for active controls. The article should not present loving-kindness outcomes as settled style-specific affective differentiation evidence. A 2020 Scientific Reports study comparing focused attention, open monitoring, and loving-kindness meditation in experienced meditators found both shared and distinct neural coupling patterns across the three states. That directly supports the article’s weaker claim that practice style matters, while not supporting the stronger dualist interface claim. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-64324-6)

The article should engage the 2024–2026 advanced-meditation wave. Current publications now include 2024 jhāna classification work, multiple 2025 jhāna neurophenomenology and 7T fMRI case studies, 2025 reviews on long-term and advanced meditation, and 2026 reviews on advanced meditation, consciousness science, functional neuroimaging, and the neuroscience of advanced meditation. The Meditation Research Program’s live publications list makes clear that this literature is moving quickly. [Springer+2OUP Academic+2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12671-024-02367-w)

The article should also acknowledge meditation adverse effects, because affective tone is not always positive. A 2022 representative U.S. survey reported high rates of unusual meditation-related experiences, substantial rates of adverse-effect endorsement, and 9.1% functional impairment from adverse effects. [Springer+2PLOS+2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12671-022-01915-6)

The target article’s “neuroplasticity findings” language should also be narrowed. The surrounding neurophenomenology article already contains more caution than the target: it distinguishes functional findings from disputed structural-change claims and mentions active-control-sensitive structural null results. The target should either inherit that caution or avoid “neural architecture” language. ## Counterargument coverage

The article names one serious rival: affective divergence may track ordinary neural affective architecture rather than dualist interface mode. That is necessary but not sufficient. [topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions/)

A first missing counterargument is the stage-mismatch objection: the compared affective profiles may be artifacts of comparing different attainment stages or different practice modes rather than tradition-level constants. The parent article already raises stage-mismatch as a source of apparent variation; the target should turn that admission into an explicit objection section. [topics/comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions/)

A second missing counterargument is report-construction. Affect is especially vulnerable to doctrinal framing, ritual expectation, translation choice, and socially learned reporting conventions. The parent article discusses cross-pollination and the Katz-style objection that mystical experience is theory-laden, but the target page does not sufficiently import this challenge into the affect-specific context. [topics/comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions/)

A third missing counterargument is dimensionality. “Flat,” “ecstatic,” “arid,” and “easy” are not all values on a single pleasant/unpleasant axis. The article should distinguish hedonic valence, arousal, motivational salience, emotional reactivity, devotional object-directedness, and engagement register; otherwise the four-way comparison risks comparing incommensurable quantities. The article’s proposed single-practitioner prediction is good, but it needs to be embedded in a design that controls for instruction, bodily practice, social ritual, and prior belief. Experimental phenomenology is an available model here because it proposes systematic variation of attention and attitude under controlled instructions. [topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions/)

## Five-tenet audit

### Dualism

The target article handles Dualism honestly but protectively. It says the interface-mode hypothesis presupposes dualism and that the affective data are equally available to an identity theorist. That is the right epistemic posture, but it means the article is not defending dualism; it is offering a conditional model inside a dualist framework. Since the Tenets page says the five commitments are chosen starting points rather than proofs, the target should label the model more explicitly as “conditional on Map tenets,” not as something the affective data support. [tenets](https://unfinishablemap.org/tenets/)

### Minimal Quantum Interaction

MQI is invoked only loosely. The selection-only-channel article defines the strictest MQI reading as mind selecting among brain-prepared alternatives without contributing to the candidate set, with information-theoretic constraints; the target should state whether affective “engagement register” is a phenomenological overlay, a proposed channel-state variable, or merely a metaphor. [concepts/selection-only-channel](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/selection-only-channel/)

### Bidirectional Interaction

The Bidirectional Interaction section is disciplined but slightly overreaches. It says the hypothesis connects at the correlation register, not the mechanism register, which is correct. But it also says conscious practice shapes affective experience and “neural architecture.” Given active-control caveats and structural-neuroplasticity disputes, “functional neural dynamics,” “behavioral-affective outcomes,” or “training-sensitive neural correlates” would be safer than “neural architecture.” [topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/affective-tone-divergence-across-meditative-traditions/)

### No Many-Worlds

No Many-Worlds is not really engaged by this article, which is fine; the article should simply mark it as “not load-bearing here” rather than leave it implicit. The Tenets page itself frames No Many-Worlds around indexical identity, not affective phenomenology. [tenets](https://unfinishablemap.org/tenets/)

### Occam-has-limits

Occam Has Limits is implicit but not articulated. The article resists the simpler readings “all traditions report the same state” and “all differences are cultural construction,” but it also should not let the dualist interface hypothesis benefit from explanatory neatness. The Occam tenet says simplicity is not a reliable truth guide under incomplete knowledge; that should bind the Map’s own clean interface-mode explanation as much as it binds physicalist or constructivist rivals. [tenets](https://unfinishablemap.org/tenets/)
