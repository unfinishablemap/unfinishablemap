## Executive Summary

The Map's recent work has consolidated two major strengths. First, the empirical programme has deepened decisively: the new experimental consciousness science survey integrates COGITATE adversarial results, brain biophotons, split-brain unity, and quantum field models into a unified case that physicalist theories face not just philosophical but empirical difficulties. Second, the anaesthesia article—now one of the Map's longest and most sophisticated pieces—has been refined with recovery evidence that transforms what was already strong pharmacological analysis into a comprehensive interface architecture argument. The recovery sequence (hierarchical restoration, neural inertia, stochastic emergence, the bootstrapping problem) provides a second empirical axis that is independently compelling: consciousness does not passively reappear as chemicals dissipate, and the Map's framework predicts exactly the structured, asymmetric, agent-specific patterns observed. The primary expansion opportunities lie in developing the phenomenology of anaesthetic recovery as a standalone topic, exploring biological computationalism's inadvertent support for dualism, and formalising the Map's growing "convergence from independent methodologies" epistemological strategy.

## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)

The experimental consciousness science article delivers what Chalmers has argued the field needs: empirical results that expose the explanatory gap rather than paper over it. The COGITATE consortium's own admission—"no single experiment would decisively refute either theory"—is not a failure of experimental design but a revelation about the nature of the problem. IIT and GNWT disagree fundamentally about *what consciousness is* while making overlapping predictions about *where it correlates*. The article's identification of this as a "consciousness measurement problem" parallels Chalmers' meta-problem: the difficulty is not finding correlates but understanding what correlation means when the explanandum is subjective experience. The biological computationalism finding is equally significant from Chalmers' perspective: if consciousness requires specific physical substrates and cannot be replicated through abstract computation, functionalism—the strongest philosophical ally of physicalism—fails. Milinkovic and Aru arrive at this conclusion as physicalists, making their inadvertent support for the explanatory gap all the more striking.

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)

The anaesthesia article's refined recovery section provides the most detailed empirical mapping of the consciousness-brain interface in the Map's corpus. The Bodart et al. (2021) finding—frontal-parietal dynamics normalising *prior to* connected consciousness—is precisely what Stapp's framework predicts: the neural side must prepare the quantum-sensitive channels before consciousness can resume its selective activity. The active reboot is not a generator powering up but a channel being cleared for an agent to re-enter. The stochastic emergence finding in genetically identical mice goes further: deterministic pharmacokinetic models fail where stochastic models succeed, consistent with the interface operating at a level where quantum indeterminacy shapes the moment of reconnection. Stapp would also note with satisfaction the biophoton detection—quantum phenomena (individual photons) emitted by the brain that change with cognitive tasks. If biophotons play a functional role, quantum-level events participate in cognition at a scale accessible to new measurement techniques, opening an empirical programme that could eventually test quantum consciousness hypotheses directly.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)

The enactivism article exemplifies what Nagel has long practised: taking an opposing position's strongest form seriously enough to discover that it secretly supports your conclusion. The three-strand analysis (autopoietic, sensorimotor, radical) engages each variant on its own terms before identifying the structural convergence: every strand acknowledges experience's irreducibility while failing to explain *why* embodied activity is accompanied by subjective experience. Thompson's "transcendental character" of experience is, as the article shows, a phenomenological commitment that sits more naturally in dualism's territory than in the physicalism enactivists officially endorse. The coherence question—deflationary and revisionist aims pulling in opposite directions—is a distinctively Nagelian observation: the tension between wanting to dissolve a problem and wanting to offer a new metaphysics is itself evidence that the problem is real.

The self-stultification article's recent refinement deserves praise from Nagel's perspective for its intellectual honesty about the AI exception. A lesser treatment would suppress this limitation; the Map faces it directly and shows how the argument's scope limitation (it proves *human* consciousness must be causally efficacious) does not undermine its force where it applies.

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)

The anaesthesia article's recovery analysis reads as a processual account of consciousness-brain interaction. The hierarchical restoration sequence—autonomic first, then reflexive, then executive, then full connected awareness—traces the interface from its most basic to most sophisticated components. This is temporal process: each level of the interface has its own characteristic becoming, and the sequence reveals the layered structure of actual occasions at the consciousness-matter boundary. The neural inertia finding—hysteresis between induction and emergence—captures what Whitehead called the "stubborn fact": the interface has its own persistence conditions that resist simple reduction to either consciousness or neural activity alone. The bootstrapping problem is the processual question par excellence: what initiates the creative advance when the channels through which consciousness acts are suppressed? The article's honest acknowledgment that this remains open is itself a process-philosophical virtue—premature closure would betray the subject matter.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)

The experimental consciousness science article's treatment of biological computationalism provides indirect support for Kane's libertarianism that the article itself does not highlight. If consciousness requires specific biological substrates—if functionalism fails—then consciousness is not a computational property but something tied to the physical particulars of biological brains. This means the freedom consciousness provides (if any) is not duplicable through computation, which strengthens the case that human agency is genuinely unique rather than a generic feature of sufficiently complex information processing. The covert consciousness finding (25% of vegetative patients may harbour awareness) reinforces Kane's intuition that agency and consciousness can persist even when all behavioural evidence of them vanishes—the inner source of action may be intact even when the output channels are severed.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)

The experimental consciousness science article's framework void observation—competing theories proving "empirically incommensurable even under shared protocols"—is the measurement-problem manifestation of cognitive closure. The COGITATE study was designed to adjudicate between theories, yet the theories' assumptions differ so fundamentally that shared experimental protocols cannot discriminate between them. This is not a fixable methodological shortcoming but may be a structural feature of the problem space: consciousness may be such that the standard tools of empirical science cannot fully grasp it, not because the experiments are bad but because the explanandum is of a different character than the explananda science was built to handle. The bootstrapping problem in anaesthesia recovery—what initiates the return of consciousness?—is another instance of the same closure: we cannot observe the moment of reconnection from either side, because consciousness is needed to observe and the interface is what consciousness needs to act.
