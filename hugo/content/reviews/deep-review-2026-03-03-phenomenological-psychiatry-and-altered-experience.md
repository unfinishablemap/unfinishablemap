### Counterarguments Considered
- **Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)**: Phenomenological categories may be folk-psychological artifacts. The article correctly presents the pharmacological caveat and notes that correlation proves an interface, not identity.
- **Physicalist (Dennett)**: Heterophenomenology would treat reports as data about beliefs, not transparent evidence for experiential architecture. The article's dualist framing is aligned with site tenets.
- **Empiricist (Popper)**: Several empirical claims (EASE validation, predictive validity for at-risk populations) would benefit from more specific citations in future revisions, though the claims themselves are well-established in the literature.
- **Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)** and **Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)**: Minimal engagement with this article—it wisely stays within phenomenological territory and does not make quantum claims.
- **Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)**: Would question the realist ontology of "structural components of consciousness" as having inherent existence. The article's realist commitments are aligned with site tenets.

The following represent bedrock philosophical disagreements that future reviews should NOT re-flag as critical:

- **Eliminative materialists** will always object that phenomenological categories are folk-psychological and potentially eliminable. This is the fundamental dualism-materialism disagreement, not a fixable deficiency in the article.
- **Dennett-style physicalists** will object to treating phenomenological reports as transparently revealing experiential architecture. The article's dualist framing is aligned with site tenets.
- **Buddhist philosophers** may question whether the "structural features" of consciousness have inherent existence. The article's realist ontology is a legitimate philosophical commitment, not an oversight.
- **Empiricists** will want more granular citations for specific EASE validation findings. The claims are well-established but the article cites the framework papers rather than individual validation studies. This is a stylistic choice appropriate for a synthesis article rather than a systematic review.
