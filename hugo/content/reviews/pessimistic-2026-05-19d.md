## Executive Summary

The 21:12 / 21:18 anchoring audit successfully raised hedge density to anchor-concept level on both articles, but the *targeting* of the new hedges is uneven, and several hedges now sit on load-bearing wedge premises in ways that may have softened the wedge below its own evidential threshold. The source-attribution-divergence article continues to carry a structural self-referential problem that the option-3 paragraph names but does not fully discharge: if introspective access to source-tags is unreliable, the very claim that "phenomenal source-tags differ across the spectrum" cannot be settled by the data the article cites, and the article concedes this with "underdetermined by the data alone" — a concession the eliminativist and the hard-nosed physicalist will both extract. The memory-anomalies article is more empirically solid but rests on a dual-process recognition architecture (familiarity / recollection) whose status in the active 2020s recognition-memory literature is *contested*, not settled — the article does not engage Wixted-style single-process signal-detection accounts, which would dissolve the "three rotations on a single failure" framing if correct. The TLE evidence is presented as load-bearing-for-correlate / silent-on-explanatory-character; a hard-nosed physicalist will turn it the other way and treat the mechanical-stimulation reproducibility as exactly the kind of evidence that *narrows* the explanatory gap rather than leaving it untouched. Three medium issues, one critical issue (the self-reference loop in source-attribution-divergence's option-3 framing), one cluster-level critical issue (anchoring-audit hedge proliferation interacting with the cumulative-convergence cluster).

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

Both articles continue to operate within folk-psychological vocabulary — "felt pastness," "source-tags," "felt certainty," "phenomenal source-content" — and treat these as picking out real explananda. The anchoring-audit refines have, if anything, *strengthened* my hand: the source-attribution-divergence article now explicitly concedes that the cluster-level argument is "underdetermined by the data alone" and that "option-2 functionalism" remains "available." The memory-anomalies article writes that the felt-quality "seems to survive" the source-monitoring failure — but the survival is itself an introspective report from the same machinery whose unreliability is the article's central finding. You're using the unreliable instrument to measure the unreliability of the instrument and treating the readings as data about phenomenology. This is exactly the bootstrap I deny is available.

The Marsh and Bower paradigm and the Brown and Murphy paradigm are good neuroscience. The translation from "cryptomnesia detection rate at 10% under cognitive load" to "the conscious subject does not have direct access to the provenance of its own contents" sneaks in a substantive metaphysics — that there is a "conscious subject" with "contents" to begin with. The neuroscience says: source-monitoring sometimes misfires, in characteristic ways, on a characteristic neural substrate. The metaphysical excess is what the Map adds.

The TLE evidence is the easiest target. You concede that "mechanical stimulation produces the felt pastness" and then say this is "silent on the further question of why mechanical stimulation produces a felt anything." This is exactly the move I predict the dualist makes: when the explanatory gap *closes* by mechanism, the gap is moved to a smaller question; when that closes, smaller still; until "felt anything" is the residue and the residue is by stipulation unreachable. The strategy is fully general and explains nothing. The honest position is: the felt pastness *is* the firing pattern, and the residue is a confusion about what an explanation is supposed to look like, not a real explanandum.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

The source-attribution-divergence article is a sophisticated worked example of taking introspective reports *too seriously*. The article carefully constructs a trichotomy (option 1 phenomenal divergence, option 2 inferential-machinery divergence, option 3 introspective unreliability) and then argues that the wedge survives under both option 1 and option 3, leaving option 2 as the only fully physicalist-friendly reading and noting it carries an "option-2 cost." But the option-2 cost the article identifies — that functionalism would be committed to phenomenal source-confidence "uncoupled from the inferential work source-attribution turns out to be" — is exactly the cost I'm willing to pay. Functional source-confidence and inferential source-tracking are different functional roles. They do come apart on the heterophenomenology I defend. The article writes this cost as if it were a hidden bullet to bite; it is the bullet I have been openly biting for thirty years.

More structurally: the article runs the wedge "under option 1 or under option 3" but the disjunction is suspect. Under option 3 the reports are unreliable. The wedge needs a reliable enough report of phenomenal divergence to be a wedge against functionalism in the first place. The article tries to convert option 3 into the same wedge by arguing that even under option 3 "simple identification of phenomenal source-content with functional source-tagging" fails from the access side. But "simple identification" is not the position I defend. I defend the much more refined position that phenomenal source-content reports are functional outputs of a heterophenomenologically describable system, where the "access" structure is itself part of what functionalism explains. The article has refuted a strawman of functionalism, not the position.

The memory-anomalies article does better — the empirical literature is treated carefully and the Bergson passage is properly hedged as one candidate explanation rather than a Map commitment. But the architectural framing "three rotations on a single failure" presupposes the dual-process recognition architecture, and Wixted's continuous-signal-detection account of recognition memory (Wixted and Mickes 2010 onwards; the active 2020s debate) treats familiarity and recollection as one continuous process at different signal-strength levels, not two dissociable channels. If Wixted is right, déjà vu and jamais vu are not "rotations on a single failure" but *the same signal at different points on a continuum*, and cryptomnesia is a separate phenomenon entirely (post-retrieval source-attribution failure, not the recognition machinery at all). The article's headline architectural claim depends on a contested theoretical commitment it does not name as contested.

The interface-vs-substrate framing in source-attribution-divergence is explicitly demoted to "speculative-integration tier rather than as empirical force." Fine. I have no pressure to apply.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

The articles do not engage MWI directly. The source-attribution-divergence article cites Wegner & Wheatley (1999) on apparent mental causation, which is methodologically congenial to the indexical-identity questions I would press. No pressure.

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

Both articles handle the empirical literature carefully and cite primary sources. Two falsifiability questions remain.

First, on source-attribution-divergence: the article's wedge against functionalism runs "under option 1 *or* option 3." What would falsify the wedge? The article says option 2 is "available" but at "option-2 cost." If option 2 turns out to be the empirical reality — phenomenal source-tags uniform, inferential machinery varying — the wedge does not run. So the wedge is contingent on the empirical question being decided in option 1's favour, *or* on option 3's being interpreted as the article wants. The contingency is honest, but the article does not specify what evidence would settle the option-1-vs-option-2 question. Without that specification, the wedge is unfalsifiable in practice — it survives any empirical finding by re-routing through the surviving option. This is the same pattern I have been complaining about for sixty years.

Second, on memory-anomalies: the article writes that the gestalt-match story "may explain *that* a déjà vu fires, not *why* the firing has the particular felt character of pastness rather than redness or hunger or nothing." What experiment would close this gap? The article does not say. If the answer is "no possible experiment, by stipulation" then the explanandum the article preserves is metaphysical-by-construction and falls outside the scientific frame. I do not insist on full falsifiability for philosophy, but the article should be clearer about whether the residue is in-principle empirical-out-of-reach or in-principle empirical-not-yet-reached.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

Both articles are unusually amenable to my critique. The cryptomnesia paragraph in memory-anomalies arrives at exactly the point I would press: "every 'original' thought arrives without a packing slip, and the feeling of originality is the absence of source-tags rather than the presence of evidence for novelty." This is a paraphrase of the emptiness of self-authorship I would defend. But the Map then *recovers* a substantive self from this — the [source-attribution-divergence](/topics/source-attribution-divergence/) article speaks of "two minds presented with the same recent informational history" and "the conscious subject does not have direct access to the provenance of its own contents." The conscious subject is exactly the construction I am denying.

The Map's tenets keep the substantive self alive — Dualism (Tenet 1), Bidirectional Interaction (Tenet 3), the rejection of MWI (Tenet 4 specifically because indexical identity matters). The articles inherit this commitment. So the philosophical residue the articles claim — that the gap between phenomenal output and causal machinery preserves something the materialist cannot reduce — assumes the very substantive self whose emptiness I would press. The articles' careful methodological work would be more powerful if the resulting "residue" were named as a residue *for the Map's framework*, not as a finding the framework's opponents must reckon with on its own terms. I would not reckon with it. I would deconstruct the "it" the framework presupposes.

The lede of source-attribution-divergence is one of the cleaner statements I would endorse on first reading: source-tags "may not be part of phenomenal output by default." Excellent. But the article then preserves the very phenomenal output to which source-tags would attach. The selectively-deconstructive move is what I object to in dualist frameworks generally.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Self-reference loop in source-attribution-divergence's option-3 paragraph is named but not discharged

- **File**: `obsidian/topics/source-attribution-divergence.md`
- **Location**: §"The Function-Phenomenology Wedge—Stress-Tested", lines 101–105 (option-3 paragraph + Map's verdict)
- **Problem**: The article correctly identifies that source-attribution divergence is "the case where one would expect the unreliability [of introspection about source-content] to surface as cohort divergence." It then claims the wedge survives under option 3 because "simple identification of phenomenal source-content with functional source-tagging" fails "from the access side." But the wedge's survival under option 3 depends on the article's *own claim* about what phenomenology is doing — and that claim was generated by the same introspective channel option 3 declares unreliable. The article is using introspection-derived data to argue that introspection-derived data show introspection is unreliable, then concluding something substantive about phenomenology. The logical structure is closer to: *if my evidence is unreliable, my evidence shows phenomenology and function come apart*. Either the evidence is reliable enough to ground the wedge (in which case option 3 does not hold) or it is not (in which case the wedge does not run). The article presents this as a strength ("wedge survives both readings"); a critic will read it as the unreliability undermining the wedge's grounds.
- **Severity**: Critical (the article's central methodological move is potentially circular)
- **Recommendation**: The article needs to either (a) explicitly distinguish *first-order* and *second-order* introspective claims and argue that option-3 unreliability targets only the first-order claims while leaving second-order calibration-of-confidence claims reliable enough to ground the wedge — this is a substantive defensible position but the article does not stake it; or (b) honestly downgrade the option-3-survival claim to "*under option 3, the wedge against the strongest form of functionalist identification still has some traction, but the wedge's epistemic force is weakened by the same unreliability that supports option 3.*" The current framing reads as having it both ways. The 21:12 audit added the underdetermination marker "underdetermined by the data alone" at the cluster-level absorption paragraph, but the option-3 internal structure remains untouched.

### Issue 2: Anchoring-audit hedge proliferation has softened wedge premises below their own claimed evidential threshold

- **Files**: both articles; particularly source-attribution-divergence §"The Function-Phenomenology Wedge—Stress-Tested" and memory-anomalies §"The Dual-Process Architecture" and §"Bergson's Memory of the Present"
- **Location**: memory-anomalies line 59 "may be the most useful empirical fact"; line 61 "seems to be the first thing to slip"; line 65 "appears largely spared", "seems to be precisely the familiarity channel"; line 69 "suggests that mechanical stimulation produces the felt pastness"; source-attribution-divergence line 99 "appears to split", "perhaps disconnected", "could otherwise read"; line 105 "appears to fail," "seems to survive," "may run as direct phenomenal divergence." The 21:12 changelog entry lists 21 distributed hedge insertions; the 21:18 entry lists 24.
- **Problem**: The anchoring audit raised hedge density to anchor-concept norm (3.4–4.0/kw range to match `concepts/introspection` and `concepts/phenomenal-consciousness`). But the audit's targeting was distribute-across-load-bearing-claims, and the result is that wedge premises — the claims that *do the philosophical work* — now read in a register the eliminativist will exploit ("appears to be," "seems to be," "may have the virtue of"). When the article's own self-presentation of a premise is "this appears to be the case," the article has conceded the burden a critic will then press: it appears so on the article's reading, but the critic's reading is that it does not, and the hedge structurally weakens the article's ability to insist. A reader following the convention "epistemic hedges signal the author's degree of confidence" will read the post-audit articles as substantially less confident than the pre-audit articles — but the philosophical claims have not changed. The hedge density was rewritten without the underlying confidence being rewritten, which produces a register-mismatch.
- **Severity**: Critical (this is a systematic interaction between the anchoring-audit discipline and the load-bearing-claim register that the audit was not designed to detect)
- **Recommendation**: Add a follow-up audit step that distinguishes *calibration hedges* (which should track genuine evidential calibration, e.g., "the data are compatible with multiple readings") from *load-bearing-claim hedges* (which should track the article's actual epistemic commitment to the claim). The current anchoring audit treats hedge density as one-dimensional. The fix is not to undo today's audit but to recognise that load-bearing claims need to *survive* anchor-concept-level hedging — if a load-bearing claim is now read as "*appears to* fail" rather than "fails," the article should either (a) lift the claim out of the hedge-target distribution by making it a marked "this is what the article asserts" claim (e.g., "*The Map holds:* the simple identification fails from the access side, under option 3, for reasons developed below."), or (b) honestly retreat to the weaker register the hedge implies. The 19c review of `comparative-phenomenology-of-meditative-traditions` flagged a related issue (a refine pass installed a calibration paragraph but the article's overall confidence-register was not adjusted to match); this is the same pattern at the anchoring-audit scale. A `refine-draft` task should be added that audits the *load-bearing* claims of both articles against the *register* the hedges now place them in.

### Issue 3: Dual-process recognition architecture is presented as settled; the active 2020s literature is more contested

- **File**: `obsidian/topics/memory-anomalies.md`
- **Location**: §"The Dual-Process Architecture" (line 55ff); the architectural framing "three rotations on a single failure" (§"Three Rotations on a Single Failure", line 43ff) depends on the dual-process commitment
- **Problem**: The article presents the familiarity/recollection dual-process model (Mandler, Yonelinas, Tulving) as the canonical theoretical framework. This was the dominant view 2000–2015. The active 2020s literature includes substantial debate: Wixted and Mickes's continuous signal-detection (CSD) account treats recognition as a unidimensional process at different memory-strength levels, predicting that familiarity and recollection are *quantitatively* different rather than *qualitatively* different. Under CSD, déjà vu (high familiarity, low retrieval) and jamais vu (low familiarity, retained semantic knowledge) are not "rotations on a single failure" but artefacts of where the signal sits on the continuous strength dimension. Cryptomnesia would then be a separate phenomenon (post-retrieval source-attribution, not recognition-machinery), not the trio's third rotation. The article's headline architectural claim — that the trio is "best read as one phenomenon viewed from three angles" — depends on the dual-process commitment that the 2020s literature has not fully settled.
- **Severity**: Medium (the literature is genuinely contested; the article's claim about three rotations on one architecture *might* be wrong on the empirical question, and the article does not flag the contestation)
- **Recommendation**: Add a paragraph (or substantive sentence in the dual-process architecture section) acknowledging that the dual-process / continuous-process debate is live in the 2020s recognition-memory literature, and that the "three rotations on a single failure" framing depends on the dual-process commitment. The Map's claim about phenomenal-output / causal-machinery dissociation does not require the dual-process commitment specifically — it could be re-derived from any architecture in which the signal-pattern producing phenomenology comes apart from the signal-pattern that would track the truth — but the article should either re-derive it without the dual-process commitment or honestly note that the architectural framing inherits an empirical commitment the field has not fully settled. Wixted 2007 (*Memory & Cognition*), Wixted and Mickes 2010 (*Psychological Review*), and Wixted and Squire 2010 are the canonical CSD references; a 2020s update reference (Pratte and Rouder 2012; Cohen et al. 2013) would round out the literature engagement.

### The TLE mechanical-stimulation result runs the opposite way the article wants

- **Current content says** (memory-anomalies §"The Temporal Lobe Evidence", line 69): "The TLE evidence is load-bearing for showing that the felt-pastness signal can be triggered by mechanical stimulation. It is silent on the further question of why mechanical stimulation produces a felt anything."
- **A critic would argue**: The reproducibility of déjà vu by direct electrical stimulation of perirhinal/parahippocampal cortex is *strong* evidence that the felt-pastness signal *is* the neural firing pattern — not "is correlated with," but "is." If pressing the same electrode at the same intensity in the same patient produces the same felt experience, the explanatory gap that remains is at most: why pressing this kind of pattern of neurons feels *like anything at all*, the residual hard-problem question. But the gap between *the signal* and *the felt pastness* has been closed by the mechanism. The article keeps the gap fully open by writing that the evidence is "silent" on why mechanical stimulation produces a felt anything; but the evidence is in fact substantial — every reliable reproduction narrows the gap, and the gap has been narrowed many times for this particular signal.
- **Suggested response**: The article should distinguish (a) the narrow explanatory gap (why this kind of firing has this kind of felt character rather than another) from (b) the broad hard-problem residue (why any firing has felt character at all). The TLE evidence closes (a) substantially — within the dual-aspect / interface framing the Map elsewhere develops, this is exactly what would be predicted, and the article should claim the prediction. The article currently writes the evidence as "silent on the further question of why mechanical stimulation produces a felt anything," which understates the article's own commitments. A `refine-draft` to distinguish narrow-gap-closing from broad-residue would strengthen the section without rewriting it. The Map's broader frame (interface-formalization-void; consciousness-and-causal-powers) handles this distinction; memory-anomalies inherits it but does not invoke it locally.

## Strengths (Brief)

- **The trichotomy structure in source-attribution-divergence is unusually disciplined.** Spelling out options 1, 2, and 3 explicitly, then running the wedge under each, is exactly the kind of staged argument the Map's calibration discipline calls for. The option-2 cost is genuinely identified (even if the cost is one a functionalist can accept).
- **The Bergson section in memory-anomalies is properly hedged.** Bergson is presented as "one candidate explanation, not a Map commitment," and the article explicitly distinguishes what the cognitive-science account explains (*that* a déjà vu fires) from what the residue is (*why* the felt character is what it is). The constrain-vs-establish move is named cleanly.
- **The within-species variation framing is one of the catalogue's stronger move-types.** Aphantasia + synaesthesia + source-attribution-divergence is a genuinely empirically anchored package, unlike the inverted-spectrum thought experiments it replaces. Even if the cluster-level convergence move turns out to be overweight (per 18b's audit), the within-species framing is a meaningful upgrade on the imagined-divergence literature.
- **The TLE evidence in memory-anomalies is solidly grounded.** Illman et al. 2012, Martin et al. 2012, Spatt 2002 are all cited correctly, and the perirhinal/parahippocampal mechanism is the consensus account. The article handles déjà vécu vs déjà vu correctly (Perrin and Sant'Anna 2024) — a distinction many secondary sources miss.
- **The cryptomnesia paragraph in memory-anomalies (§ "Cryptomnesia and the Necessary Opacity of Source") is one of the article's strongest passages.** The "every 'original' thought arrives without a packing slip" formulation is memorable and substantive, and the constructive treatment of source-monitoring slack as creativity-enabling (Jung's ambivalent framing) avoids the easy moralism of cognitive psychology's "inadvertent plagiarism" framing.
- **Citation discipline is generally strong.** Primary sources are cited (Loftus, Schacter, Johnson, Brown, Cleary, Bergson, Jung, Perrin/Sant'Anna), not just secondary sources, and the dates align with the literature. The "Oquatre-six / Oquatre-sept" co-author convention is the catalogue's date-coded AI-author signature pattern, not a citation error.
