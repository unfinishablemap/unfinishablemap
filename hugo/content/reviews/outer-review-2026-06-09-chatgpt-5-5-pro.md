## High-confidence factual and citation problems

### 1. The “Pereboom & Frankish (2021)” reference appears wrong

The Illusionism page cites “Pereboom, D. & Frankish, K. (2021). ‘Illusionism about Phenomenal Consciousness.’ Review of Philosophy and Psychology” and the body attributes a distortion-thesis development to “Pereboom and Frankish (2021).” That reference does not line up with the bibliographic record. The relevant Review of Philosophy and Psychology article is **Daniel Shabasson, “Illusionism about Phenomenal Consciousness: Explaining the Illusion,” 13(2):427–453 (2022)**. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

There is a relevant Pereboom article, but it is **Derk Pereboom, “Illusionism and Anti-Functionalism about Phenomenal Consciousness,” Journal of Consciousness Studies 23(11–12):172–185 (2016)**, and it is a critical engagement with Frankish, not a co-authored 2021 Review of Philosophy and Psychology paper. [PhilPapers](https://philpapers.org/rec/PERIAA-6)

This is especially concerning because the sibling Functional Seeming page already has the corrected source cluster: Frankish 2016, Frankish 2021, Pereboom 2016, Shabasson 2022, and Kammerer 2022. The site’s own Functional Seeming deep review also identifies the same “Pereboom & Frankish” misattribution as an attribution error. [concepts/functional-seeming](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/functional-seeming/)

### 2. Graziano 2024 is cited with the wrong venue

The Illusionism page lists Graziano’s 2024 “Illusionism Big and Small” as appearing in **Journal of Consciousness Studies**. Princeton’s publication metadata gives the article as **eNeuro, volume 11, issue 10, article ENEURO.0210-24.2024**, DOI 10.1523/ENEURO.0210-24.2024. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

The page’s substantive use of Graziano is broadly plausible, but the reference should be corrected to **“Illusionism Big and Small: Some Options for Explaining Consciousness,” eNeuro 11(10)**. Graziano’s abstract emphasizes that illusionism is a broad framework, not merely its most extreme form, and that internal models are never fully accurate. That nuance supports the page’s “big/small” distinction but also means the page should avoid treating all Graziano-style illusionism as equivalent to “no referent at all.” [Princeton University](https://collaborate.princeton.edu/en/publications/illusionism-big-and-small-some-options-for-explaining-consciousne/)

### 3. The Kammerer citation is mismatched to the body claim

The article invokes Kammerer in connection with whether the illusion of consciousness is “rich” and persistent. The cited Kammerer 2022 item, however, is **“How Can You Be So Sure? Illusionism and the Obviousness of Phenomenal Consciousness,” Philosophical Studies 179(9):2845–2867**, whose abstract concerns the Moorean argument and whether its crucial premise begs the question against illusionism. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

The “rich illusion” discussion belongs to Kammerer’s **“How Rich is the Illusion of Consciousness?”**, published in Erkenntnis, volume 87, pages 499–515. That article explicitly distinguishes rich illusion from sparse illusion and classifies illusionist theories along that divide. [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10670-019-00204-4)

The fix is not hard: cite both Kammerer papers, but use them in the right sections. “How Can You Be So Sure?” belongs in the Moorean-obviousness section; “How Rich is the Illusion of Consciousness?” belongs in the functional-seeming / illusion-richness section.

### 4. The Chalmers 2022 sentence is misleading

The page says Chalmers “himself (2022) later judged this flawed,” in context after presenting Chalmers’s 2018 Moorean argument against illusionism. The evidence I found supports a narrower and different claim: Loginov reports that Chalmers 2022 criticized **Moore’s original external-world argument**, and Loginov then applies that Chalmers-style criticism to Chalmers’s anti-illusionist Moorean argument. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

That distinction matters. Chalmers’s 2020 “Debunking Arguments for Illusionism about Consciousness” still says the Moorean argument is the strongest argument against illusionism, although illusionists can reject its premises and need to do more to defang its intuitive force. [Consciousness.net](https://consc.net/papers/debunking.pdf)

Recommended rewrite: **“Loginov (2024) applies Chalmers’s 2022 criticism of Moore’s original external-world proof to Chalmers’s 2018 Moorean anti-illusionism argument, arguing that the anti-illusionist premise is too theory-laden to function as common sense.”** That preserves the point without implying that Chalmers recanted his own Moorean anti-illusionist argument.

## Characterization of Frankish, Dennett, and Kammerer

### Frankish

The page is broadly right that Frankish denies phenomenal properties and accepts quasi-phenomenal properties, but its opening formulation risks sounding stronger than Frankish’s own clarifications. Frankish’s public summary says illusionism rejects phenomenal realism while holding that conscious experiences are real; the illusory items are the supposed special phenomenal properties, not all consciousness in every sense. [Keith Frankish](https://www.keithfrankish.com/illusionism/)

Frankish’s 2016 paper also gives the more precise picture: illusionists deny that experiences have phenomenal properties, allow that we are introspectively aware of sensory states, and require that the relevant introspective representations be explained in broadly functional terms. [Keith Frankish](https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_Illusionism%20as%20a%20theory%20of%20consciousness_eprint.pdf)

The article should therefore keep its “phenomenal consciousness” framing, but add an early qualifier: **illusionists do not deny consciousness in every ordinary or functional sense; they deny phenomenal consciousness as traditionally conceived.** This would prevent a common misreading that Frankish explicitly warns against. [Keith Frankish+1](https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_What%20is%20illusionism_eprint.pdf)

### Dennett

The page’s “late Dennett user-illusion” framing is directionally fair, but the phrase “no further fact beyond the report-contents” is too report-centric. Dennett’s 2015 paper is not merely a report-behavior theory; it argues against a “second medium” or “MEdium” into which neural spike trains are retransduced, rejects a Cartesian Theater, and explains the seeming of qualia through access, expectations, and public/second-person pressures. [Open MIND+1](https://open-mind.net/papers/why-and-how-does-consciousness-seem-the-way-it-seems/at_download/paperPDF)

A stronger Dennett paragraph would say: **Dennett’s reply is that the demand for an inner medium of subjective color, sound, and feel is a mistaken “double transduction” picture; intuitions of qualia are data to be explained, not limits on scientific explanation.** Dennett explicitly says intuitions against his view are important data but should not be treated as showing a limit of science. [Open MIND](https://open-mind.net/papers/why-and-how-does-consciousness-seem-the-way-it-seems/at_download/paperPDF)

Minor currentness point: wording that groups Dennett among figures who “continue” the 2020s programme should be handled carefully, because Dennett died in April 2024. It is fine to call him a central late contributor, but not an active current developer. [Ingenta Connect](https://www.ingentaconnect.com/content/imp/jcs/2024/00000031/f0020007/art00014)

### Kammerer

Kammerer is not miscast as pro-illusionist; the problem is source placement. His “How Can You Be So Sure?” paper directly supports the page’s Loginov-style Moorean discussion, because it argues that the obviousness premise cannot do the required anti-illusionist work without begging the question. [PhilArchive](https://philarchive.org/rec/KAMHCY-2)

The “rich illusion” claim should be sourced to “How Rich is the Illusion of Consciousness?” and used to sharpen the article’s taxonomy: is the illusion a cognitively impenetrable incorrect representation, or an incomplete representation that leads to false judgments? [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10670-019-00204-4)

## Missed or underdeveloped strong illusionist replies

The article does engage Frankish’s strongest reply better than many anti-illusionist treatments, but it still underplays several available illusionist counter-moves.

First, Frankish has a direct reply to the epiphenomenalism-convergence charge. He says illusionists can hold that phenomenal concepts track causally effective properties, or that phenomenal properties are causally potent as intentional objects—like ideas, stories, theories, and memes—because they figure in intentional states. The page currently acknowledges a version of this reply but dismisses it too quickly as “what physical processes were already doing.” That is a dualist verdict, not a neutral description of Frankish’s position. [Keith Frankish+1](https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_Illusionism%20as%20a%20theory%20of%20consciousness_eprint.pdf)

Second, Frankish’s reply to “what-it-is-likeness” is stronger than “reports and reactions.” He grants ordinary what-it-is-like talk about contents, reactions, memories, emotions, and psychological significance; he denies only a further intrinsic, nonfunctional, nonrepresentational property over and above those. The article’s Mary / intrinsic-quality section should make clear that Frankish will ask the realist to identify this “third thing” without simply naming phenomenal feel again. [Keith Frankish](https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_What%20is%20illusionism_eprint.pdf)

Third, Shabasson should be added as a serious illusion-problem resource, not hidden behind the erroneous “Pereboom & Frankish” reference. Shabasson proposes that introspective opacity, an infallibility intuition, and a justification constraint explain why we draw erroneous unconscious inferences about sensory states. This is directly relevant to the page’s “illusion problem” and “meta-representational bridge” sections. [PhilArchive](https://philarchive.org/rec/SHAIAP-5)

Fourth, the page should engage Kammerer’s anti-Moorean challenge in the Moorean section itself: if illusionism is coherent and supported by independent arguments, the anti-illusionist cannot simply invoke obviousness unless the relevant premise has non-question-begging support. [PhilArchive](https://philarchive.org/rec/KAMHCY-2)

Fifth, the Dennett section should include the “no double transduction” argument. This is one of the strongest illusionist replies to the charge that functional processing leaves out the felt medium of experience: Dennett says the felt medium is precisely the imaginary posit to be rejected. [Open MIND+1](https://open-mind.net/papers/why-and-how-does-consciousness-seem-the-way-it-seems/at_download/paperPDF)

## Evidence and overstatement audit

The article’s “debate talks past itself” claim is partly right but overstated. It says anti-illusionists often defend “zero qualia” while illusionists attack “classic qualia.” Frankish does distinguish quasi-phenomenal or “zero qualia” properties from strong phenomenal properties, but the anti-illusionist literature is not merely defending what Frankish already accepts. The 2016 JCS special issue includes direct anti-illusionist contributions by Nida-Rümelin, Pereboom, Prinz, Schwitzgebel, Tartaglia, and others, many of which target the eliminative move itself rather than only “classic qualia” caricatures. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

The page’s structural-convergence-with-epiphenomenalism section is rhetorically powerful but should be softened. Under illusionism, “what-it-is-likeness” is not causally excluded in the same way as epiphenomenalist qualia; it is reinterpreted as representational content, psychological significance, or quasi-phenomenal functional structure. The Map can argue that this is not enough, but it should not state the convergence as if Frankish has no reply. [Keith Frankish+1](https://keithfrankish.github.io/articles/Frankish_Illusionism%20as%20a%20theory%20of%20consciousness_eprint.pdf)

The “What would challenge this view?” section is good methodology in principle, but the contemplative-practice criterion needs better sourcing and a better illusionist reply. An illusionist can say meditation alters attention, access, reportability, and model transparency; it does not follow that it reveals intrinsic phenomenality. The surrounding AST page makes stronger contemplative claims about witness consciousness and trained introspectors, but those claims currently read as internal-site phenomenology rather than independently supported evidence. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

## Tenet-protective bracketing

The page mostly avoids crude tenet-protective bracketing, but two places need tightening.

The strongest example of good discipline is the intrinsic-quality caveat: the page explicitly says that if one rejects intrinsic phenomenal character, the objection is a framework-boundary disagreement rather than a materialist-internal refutation. That is exactly how a dualist project should handle bedrock disputes. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

The weaker spot is that the Tenet 1 section later says the self-representation and intrinsic-quality objections “show” that the functional account cannot close the gap without presupposing what it denies. That should be downgraded. The related Constitutive vs Referring Observation page is more disciplined: it says illusionism can reconstruct introspection as representational and that the Map cannot dislodge that view from premises the illusionist already accepts. The Illusionism page should import that language. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)

The AST page is less careful than the Illusionism page. The Illusionism page grants that the bare regress objection begs the question; the AST page still says that if the schema “seems” to generate consciousness, something must experience that seeming, and that saying the schema “just is” the seeming begs the question. That should be harmonized with the more charitable treatment on the Illusionism page. [concepts/illusionism](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/illusionism/)
