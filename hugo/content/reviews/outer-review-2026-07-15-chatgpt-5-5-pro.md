# Outer review: “Trumping preemption”

## Executive verdict

The 13 July 2026 reframing makes the article **substantially more accurate**. Its most important correction is sound: Vaassen’s 2024 proposal is not a preemption account. It treats the mental state and its physical basis as genuine causes under a lightweight interventionist conception of causation. Bealer and Saad, by contrast, really do assign the effect to the mental cause by preempting a physically sufficient or default physical candidate. [Springer Nature Link+3Umeå University Digital Repository+3Umeå University Digital Repository+3](https://umu.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2%3A1832320/FULLTEXT03.pdf)

The revised article is nevertheless not yet fully reliable as a guide to the literature. Three material problems remain:

1. **The Bealer priority claim is stronger than its evidence.** Saad’s notes establish that Bealer is an important predecessor, not that he was historically the first.
2. **Saad’s technical proposal is compressed misleadingly.** His experiential causal profile is a *subset* of the physical default profile, not simply something that “matches” it; the preempted physical state is not a second actual cause.
3. **Vaassen is still placed in the wrong taxonomic neighbourhood.** Vaassen 2024 expressly argues that the mental and physical causes do not constitute overdetermination under the criterion he adopts. Vaassen 2026 contrasts preemption with the strategy he favours; it does not subsume both under one lawful-co-causation or benign-overdetermination family. [concepts/trumping-preemption](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/trumping-preemption/)

My overall assessment is therefore:

> **The correction gets the central three-way contrast right, but the article should be revised again before presenting the Bealer–Saad–Vaassen triangle as historically settled or taxonomically clean.**

## 1. What the revised article now gets right

The current article distinguishes three broad possibilities:

- physical and mental candidates are both genuine causes;
- the mental cause preempts the physical candidate;
- the physical story alone supplies the actual cause.

That is a useful organising contrast. The article is particularly right to withdraw the earlier presentation of Vaassen as a form of mental-over-physical preemption. Vaassen’s diagram explicitly represents both pain and its physical basis as causes of wincing, and the surrounding argument says that lightweight interventionism can recognize both causal relations while rejecting the exclusion principle. [Umeå University Digital Repository+1](https://umu.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2%3A1832320/FULLTEXT03.pdf)

The article is also right that Bealer and Saad belong in the preemption branch. Bealer invokes the major–sergeant example as a case of “trumping preemption” and argues that mental laws can trump competing physical-law routes. Saad develops a much more explicit psychophysical mechanism in which an experiential causal profile preempts a corresponding part of a physical state’s default causal profile. [Springer Nature Link+4PhilPapers+4PhilPapers+4](https://philpapers.org/archive/BEAMC.pdf)

The revised page therefore repairs the most serious substantive defect identified in the site’s own review: its former claim that Vaassen’s proposal was preemptive. The site’s deep-review document candidly records that correction and attributes the immediate reconsideration to correspondence with Vaassen. [reviews/deep-review-2026-06-26-trumping-preemption](https://unfinishablemap.org/reviews/deep-review-2026-06-26-trumping-preemption/)

## 2. Is Bealer really the earliest preemption defence?

### What Bealer actually argues

Bealer’s paper is unequivocally relevant. He grants a form of physical sufficiency: every relevant effect may have a physical event or condition sufficient for it. His central contention is that causal sufficiency does not entail being the actual cause. The major’s order and the sergeant’s matching order are used to illustrate how one sufficient route can screen off or trump another. Bealer then applies this structure to psychophysical and mental laws, proposing that their modal or law-governed strength gives the mental route trumping power over the physical candidate. [PhilPapers+2PhilPapers+2](https://philpapers.org/archive/BEAMC.pdf)

This is not merely a passing analogy. The major–sergeant structure is integrated into Bealer’s proposed test for causal selection, his distinction between weaker and stronger closure principles, and his positive account of mental-to-physical causation. He even describes the resulting mental causation as a previously unnoticed species of trumping preemption. Thus the revised article is justified in upgrading Bealer from a peripheral precursor to a central predecessor.

### What Saad’s notes establish

Saad’s notes 24 and 27 do verify two narrower propositions:

- Bealer appealed to the major–sergeant case in developing an account of mental causation.
- Bealer proposed a nonreductive account of mental causation as preemption, which Saad regards as underexplored and too intricate to address fully in that paper. [Springer Nature Link+1](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s11098-025-02290-3.pdf)

They **do not**, however, say that Bealer was the first person to defend the preemption route. They are acknowledgements of intellectual precedence, not a historical priority study. Accordingly, the current wording that presents Bealer as the earliest sustained defence “per Saad 2025, notes 24 and 27” attributes more to those notes than they contain.

### What can responsibly be claimed

The strongest historically defensible formulation is approximately:

> “Bealer 2007 is the earliest explicit and sustained application of Schaffer-style trumping preemption to dualist mental causation located in the present literature search.”

That formulation is well supported. Schaffer introduced trumping as a proposed variety of preemption in 2000, and Bealer’s 2007 treatment is explicit, sustained, and directed at mental causation. Earlier dualist work does not automatically defeat that scoped claim. Pietroski’s 1994 proposal, for example, treats bodily behaviour as having both mental and neural causes—indeed as being “twice caused”—rather than selecting the mental cause by preempting the neural one. It is an important antecedent to the both-are-causes branch, not an earlier trumping account. [PhilPapers+2newdualism.org+2](https://philpapers.org/rec/SCHTP-12)

What is not yet justified is the unrestricted statement that Bealer is simply “the earliest sustained defence of the preemption route.” Historical priority claims are unusually demanding: one must specify the relevant tradition, terminology, publication types and search range, and one cannot infer exhaustiveness from Saad’s two footnotes. The article should therefore preserve Bealer’s priority provisionally and explicitly delimit it to **post-Schaffer, explicit trumping/preemption accounts of dualist mental causation**.

## 3. Does the article represent Bealer fairly?

Broadly, yes—but the article’s “authority” vocabulary conceals important machinery.

The page presents trumping as the separation of counterfactual sufficiency from “causal authority.” That is a potentially helpful Map-level gloss. Bealer certainly exploits the difference between being sufficient for an effect and being the cause of it. But Bealer does not merely posit an unspecified authority layer. He offers an objective causal-selection proposal involving counterfactual screening-off and relations among laws of different modal strength. [concepts/trumping-preemption](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/trumping-preemption/)

That difference matters. “Authority” can sound conventional, explanatory or attributional—as though we simply choose which sufficient process receives causal credit. Bealer intends a claim about objective causation. The mental event is supposed to be the actual cause because the relevant mental and psychophysical laws trump the physical candidate, not because an interpreter or explanatory practice awards it priority.

The article should therefore say expressly:

- **“Authority layer” is the Map’s heuristic vocabulary.**
- In Bealer, the proposed realizer of that vocabulary is a hierarchy among laws together with a screening-off criterion.
- The physical event may remain causally or nomically sufficient in a weak sense without being an actual cause of the effect.
- Bealer accepts something like weak physical sufficiency while rejecting the stronger inference that every physical effect must have a physical cause.

The current statement that “both levels remain sufficient, but only one is authoritative” is tolerable as a high-level gloss of Bealer only after these senses of sufficiency are separated. Otherwise it invites the mistaken picture of two actual causes followed by an additional act of causal ranking.

## 4. Does the article represent Saad fairly?

### The central classification is correct

Saad’s delegatory dualism is indeed a preemption theory. The physical state is the nomic guarantor or default causal candidate, while the experience preempts the relevant part of its default causal contribution. Saad explicitly places this under “preemptive dualism” and uses the major–sergeant case as an analogy. [Springer Nature Link+1](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s11098-025-02290-3.pdf)

The article also correctly reports an important consequence: Saad does not preserve causal closure without qualification. His proposal allows actual failures of physical causal closure, but the failures are designed to be observationally undetectable because the experiential contribution produces outcomes within the relevant physical default profile. [Springer Nature Link](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s11098-025-02290-3.pdf)

### “Matches” should be replaced by “is a subset of”

The page presently says that Saad’s Subset Law* makes the experiential causal profile “match” the corresponding physical default profile. Saad’s technical condition is more specific: the experiential causal profile is a **subset** of the physical state’s default causal profile, and the Delegatory Law preempts the corresponding subset of that physical profile. [Springer Nature Link](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s11098-025-02290-3.pdf)

This is not merely terminological. “Matches” naturally suggests equivalence or one-for-one duplication. Saad needs only controlled inclusion. The experiential state need not inherit or duplicate everything the physical state would otherwise do.

A more faithful sentence would be:

> “Under Subset Law*, an experience’s causal profile is contained within the corresponding physical state’s default causal profile; the Delegatory Law assigns the matching portion of that default profile to the experience instead.”

### The physical state is not a second actual cause

The phrase “both levels remain sufficient” also needs qualification in Saad’s case. The physical state remains a nomic guarantor and possesses a default or would-be causal profile. It is not, with respect to the preempted effect, an additional actual cause alongside the experience. That is exactly what distinguishes Saad from Vaassen.

The article should distinguish at least four notions:

1. a condition’s guaranteeing the occurrence of an effect under the total laws;
2. possession of a default causal power or profile;
3. counterfactual sufficiency in selected circumstances;
4. actual causation of the token effect.

Saad’s proposal allows the physical base to retain roles corresponding to the first three while denying it the fourth for the preempted contribution.

### Saad’s own qualifications deserve more prominence

Saad acknowledges that ordinary major–sergeant cases involve a familiar mechanism—commands, ranks and obedient subordinates—whereas delegatory dualism posits direct law-based preemption. He also recognizes that widespread systematic preemption may appear baroque or coincidental, although he argues that Subset Law* supplies a principled structure and that preemption avoids the additive problems of straightforward overdetermination. [Springer Nature Link+1](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s11098-025-02290-3.pdf)

The current article discusses some of these costs, but it would be fairer to make clear that they are not merely objections imposed by external critics. Saad himself identifies and attempts to answer them.

## 5. Does the article now represent Vaassen 2024 fairly?

### The central correction is correct

Vaassen’s 2024 paper is a both-are-causes proposal. Under his version of interventionism, a relation can qualify as causal when it supports suitably robust interventionist dependence across the relevant nomically possible circumstances. The account does not require causation to consist in energy transfer, production or necessitation. Both the pain and its physical basis can therefore be causes of the wince. [Umeå University Digital Repository+1](https://umu.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2%3A1832320/FULLTEXT03.pdf)

The revised article is also right to characterize this as a rejection of exclusion rather than an attempt to make the mental cause displace the physical one.

### It should not call Vaassen’s result “overdetermination” without qualification

Vaassen explicitly considers the overdetermination objection and applies Bennett’s test. On that test, the pain and its physical basis are not overdeterminers, because the counterfactual circumstances in which one occurs without the other do not support the requisite independent causal sufficiency. Vaassen therefore concludes that his case involves two causes but not overdetermination. [Umeå University Digital Repository+1](https://umu.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2%3A1832320/FULLTEXT03.pdf)

This makes the current pairing with a “lawful-co-causation family” potentially misleading. A site-created taxonomy may group Vaassen with Mills, Lowe and other both-are-causes views at a sufficiently abstract level. But the article then says that systematic connection removes the bad coincidence from the “resulting overdetermination.” That is not Vaassen’s own classification.

A fair formulation would be:

> “Vaassen belongs to the broader both-are-causes branch, but unlike standard benign-overdetermination theories, he argues that the mental–physical pair fails an influential test for overdetermination.”

That would preserve the Map’s comparative framework without attributing to Vaassen a label he expressly argues against.

### The physicalist dialectical setting should be stated

Vaassen’s paper is not presented as his personal endorsement of substance dualism or property dualism. Its dialectical contention is that physicalists who accept a lightweight interventionist theory of causation cannot deny dualists the same causal standard merely because the candidate cause is mental. The article currently makes his proposal sound more like a positive dualist doctrine than an intervention in a dispute about dialectical consistency. [Umeå University Digital Repository+1](https://umu.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2%3A1832320/FULLTEXT03.pdf)

That context affects how much weight the proposal can bear within The Unfinishable Map. Vaassen supplies a conditional route to mental causation: **given** this permissive but independently motivated causal theory, standard dualism need not make the mental causally idle. He does not thereby establish the dualist ontology, a production relation, or mental causal “oomph.”

The article’s “points of control” language is defensible as an interpretive gloss on interventionism, provided it is not taken to mean that mental states can be surgically varied independently of their physical bases. Vaassen’s argument concerns robust dependence useful for manipulation, prediction and control, not necessarily free-standing mental intervention variables.

## 6. Does Vaassen 2026 support the article’s taxonomy?

Only partly.

Vaassen’s 2026 overview divides dualist responses to exclusion into broad strategies involving the rejection or acceptance of physical causal self-sufficiency. Preemption appears on the rejection side, specifically among “masking” strategies that allow mental efficacy while explaining why departures from physical self-sufficiency are difficult to detect. Vaassen contrasts this with approaches that embrace physical self-sufficiency and deny that it excludes additional mental causation; the overview judges the latter family more promising. [Wiley Online Library+3Wiley Online Library+3Wiley Online Library+3](https://compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/phc3.70109)

Accordingly:

- It is true that the 2026 paper places preemption within a wider taxonomy of dualist mental-causation strategies.
- It is misleading to say that it places preemption within the same “lawful-co-causation family” as Vaassen’s 2024 interventionist proposal.
- The overview uses the two as contrasting strategic families: Saad-style masking preemption denies physical self-sufficiency at the causal level, whereas Vaassen-style co-causation accepts physical self-sufficiency and rejects exclusion.
- The overview is not neutral between them. Vaassen treats widespread preemption as vulnerable to a baroque-structure objection resembling the objection to widespread overdetermination and recommends greater attention to the acceptance strategy. [Wiley Online Library+2Wiley Online Library+2](https://compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/epdf/10.1111/phc3.70109)

The article’s sentence should therefore be replaced by something like:

> “Vaassen’s 2026 survey classifies Saad-style preemption as a masking denial of physical self-sufficiency and contrasts it with the both-are-causes strategy defended in Vaassen 2024, which accepts physical self-sufficiency while rejecting exclusion.”

That single revision would remove the largest remaining philosophical distortion.

## 7. The Bealer–Saad–Vaassen triangle

The literature supports the following cleaner comparison:

| Position | Physical candidate | Mental candidate | Main strategy |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Bealer 2007** | May be physically sufficient but is trumped as the cause | Actual cause under stronger mental/psychophysical laws | Trumping preemption; distinguish sufficiency from causation |
| **Saad 2025** | Nomic guarantor with a default causal profile; relevant subset is preempted | Actual cause of the delegated effects | Systematic law-based preemption; hidden failures of causal closure |
| **Vaassen 2024** | Genuine cause | Genuine cause | Lightweight interventionism; reject exclusion; deny that the pair constitutes overdetermination |

This is not one family with three implementations. It is a disagreement about **where to resist the exclusion argument**:

- Bealer resists the move from physical sufficiency to physical causation.
- Saad allows a default physical causal route but systematically preempts it.
- Vaassen accepts the physical cause and denies that its presence excludes a mental cause. [PhilPapers+2Springer Nature Link+2](https://philpapers.org/archive/BEAMC.pdf)

The phrase “same destination” is acceptable only in the weak sense that all three make room for efficacious mental properties or events. Their accounts of closure, causal competition and the number of actual causes are importantly different.

## 8. Secondary problems in the concept article

### “Authority” is useful but underdefined

The article’s “authority layer” is original interpretive vocabulary rather than an established term shared by Schaffer, Bealer, Saad and Vaassen. It can illuminate Bealer and Saad, but it fits Vaassen poorly because Vaassen does not select one cause as authoritative over the other. The page should mark the term as a Map-level heuristic whenever it moves from exposition to synthesis. [concepts/trumping-preemption](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/trumping-preemption/)

It also needs a metaphysical interpretation. Does causal authority mean:

- being the actual producer;
- surviving a screening-off test;
- occupying the higher position in a hierarchy of laws;
- being selected by a structural-equation model;
- grounding the best causal explanation; or
- receiving causal attribution for pragmatic purposes?

Bealer and Saad need an objective version. Leaving the term between metaphysics and explanatory practice weakens the proposed dualist application.

### The analogy cases are not homogeneous

The article says that the authority-selecting rule is stipulated “by agents” in both the magic and military cases. That is not quite right. Military rank is an agent-created convention. The magical case involves a stipulated law of the imagined world, not an in-world convention created by the relevant agents. Bealer also notes the preference some philosophers have for natural-law examples over merely conventional command structures. [PhilPapers](https://philpapers.org/archive/BEAMC.pdf)

This matters because Saad’s Delegatory Law is supposed to be a fundamental or natural psychophysical law, not a conventional instruction hierarchy.

### Hitchcock is described too favourably

The statement that Hitchcock “partially rehabilitates trumping” needs a qualification. A contrastive framework can recover some of the causal judgments associated with trumping cases, but Hitchcock does not thereby vindicate trumping as a distinctive species of causal preemption. His conclusion is substantially more deflationary than the word “rehabilitates” suggests. [Springer Nature Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-010-9799-y)

Bernstein is represented more accurately: her analysis challenges trumping’s status as a separate category by treating the cases as forms of overdetermination or early preemption. [Springer Nature Link+1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12136-014-0231-y)

### “The brain is sufficient” is too unqualified

The article concludes that “the brain is sufficient” is true and uncontroversial. That formulation suppresses precisely the distinctions on which the article depends. Physical, nomological, counterfactual and causal sufficiency are not interchangeable. The claim also depends on the grain of the physical state, background conditions, temporal extent and assumptions about physical determinism or completeness.

A safer formulation is:

> “Even granting the physical-sufficiency premise used in the exclusion argument, it remains open whether the physically sufficient condition is the sole actual cause.”

### Site methodology

1. **Institute a primary-source gate for load-bearing interpretive claims.** No claim that an author defends preemption, overdetermination, exclusion or closure should be marked verified until the relevant definition, diagram or argumentative conclusion has been checked in the primary text.
2. **Maintain a claim–source ledger.** For every major sentence, record the exact page or section and label the claim as one of: author’s explicit position, conventional scholarly classification, Map-level synthesis, or reviewer inference.
3. **Adopt a special protocol for priority claims.** State the scope, databases and terms searched; distinguish “earliest found” from “earliest”; inspect cited predecessors; and avoid presenting acknowledgements in footnotes as exhaustive historiography.
4. **Separate author self-description from external taxonomy.** A view may be grouped by the Map under “co-causation” while its author rejects “overdetermination.” Both facts should be reported rather than allowing the site’s category to overwrite the author’s.
5. **Use an author-recognition test.** Before publication, ask whether the author would recognize the attributed conclusion in a one-sentence summary. Vaassen’s explicit “both cause” diagram should have failed the former preemption characterization immediately.
6. **Treat correspondence as supplementary evidence.** Record its date, whether quotation or paraphrase is authorized, and what it clarifies, but independently verify the interpretation against the published work whenever possible.
7. **Require cross-page synchronization after conceptual corrections.** A change to a central classification should automatically trigger review of the concept article, research note, deep-review report, bibliography, related concept pages and comparison tables.
8. **Make the changelog article-specific and diff-oriented.** Every substantive correction should name the affected page, summarize the old and new positions, state the reason, and link to the supporting review. The 13 July Vaassen correction should be added explicitly.
9. **Add automated consistency checks for metadata.** Flag cases in which the changelog contains entries later than its displayed modification date, or an article’s modification date has no corresponding substantive changelog entry.
