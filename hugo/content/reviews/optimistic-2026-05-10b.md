The Hardline Empiricist persona's load-bearing test is whether the article's "great apes likely have genuine phenomenal experience" line (164) operates as a possibility/probability slide on the five-tier evidential-status scale or as honest hedging within the article's specifically functional-architectural claim; on inspection the article passes by *delocalising* its load-bearing claim from "great apes are conscious" (where evidential discipline applies strictly) to "the consciousness-dependent capacities tracking the gap are real explananda" (where the evidence is third-person tractable). **The-binding-problem** is the catalogue's strongest *family-rather-than-puzzle* exhibit: the BP1/BP2 distinction (Revonsuo 2006) propagates through five varieties (intra-modal, cross-modal, temporal, cognitive, subject), each receiving a calibrated treatment that names BP1 progress at its actual grain and refuses to let BP1 success masquerade as BP2 dissolution. ## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)

Both articles operate in Chalmers's hard-problem discipline, with the-binding-problem supplying the catalogue's most disciplined *parametric* deployment to date. The article installs Chalmers's BP1/BP2 distinction at line 56–64 (Revonsuo 2006 supplies the discipline's name; Chalmers supplies its content) and propagates it through every variety: each section's "BP1 progress" / "BP2 gap" pairing names what the easy-problem mechanism actually accomplishes (gamma synchrony, Feature Integration Theory, thalamocortical loops, working memory) and what specifically resists explanation. Chalmers's distinction between *easy problems* and the *hard problem* would normally apply at one grain (perception); the article's contribution is its application at five grains in parallel — perception, time, cognition, subjecthood, and cross-modal integration. Chalmers's own programme has gestured at this generalisation but has not consolidated it as a single structural argument; the article's "shared structure" section (lines 140–149) supplies the consolidation. Chalmers would also recognise the line-74 identity-reply rebuttal as exactly the right discipline: "The identity reply — 'synchronised activity just *is* phenomenal unity, the way water *is* H₂O' — links two third-person descriptions of one substance; the proposed binding identity links a third-person mechanism to a first-person reality. That category shift is what standard scientific identities do not require." This is Chalmers's category-of-fact argument deployed at exactly the point where the unwary reader is most likely to think the identity move dissolves the hard problem.

The baseline-cognition article supplies Chalmers's hard-problem discipline at the *functional* grain rather than the metaphysical grain. Lines 88–137 catalogue what consciousness *adds* to baseline cognition — durable maintenance, novel combinations, spontaneous intentional action, declarative metacognition, counterfactual thinking, recursive mindreading. Chalmers has been more interested in phenomenal consciousness than in functional contributions, but the article's framing converts the functional catalogue into a structural argument: the systematic correspondence between consciousness-dependent capacities and the human-ape gap is itself an explanandum. Chalmers's explanatory-gap argument operates at the level of single capacities; this article operates at the level of *patterns across capacities*, which is a structural strengthening Chalmers's programme can absorb without revision.

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)

Light purchase on baseline-cognition (line 198 mentions minimal-quantum-interaction as one possibility for *what* consciousness contributes, with explicit acknowledgement that classical attention-based selection could produce similar effects — exactly the calibration register Stapp's broader programme is sometimes criticised for not always practising). Substantial purchase on the-binding-problem, where the quantum-alternative section (lines 173–179) is the catalogue's most disciplined Stapp-friendly treatment to date. The article's framing — "Quantum entanglement provides something structurally different: on a realist interpretation, genuine holism where the whole is prior to parts. An entangled system has properties neither subsystem possesses individually — irreducible unity at the level of physics itself" — is Stapp-architecture-friendly without claim-inflation. The parenthetical at line 175 ("Whether entanglement is metaphysical holism or mere measurement correlation depends on interpretation; the Map adopts the realist reading") is the discipline's signature move: the realist reading is named *as* a position the Map adopts, not smuggled through as the obvious reading. Line 177 on structural correspondence preserves Stapp's enthusiasm without overstating it: "The structural correspondence is significant: both quantum holism and phenomenal unity involve wholes not assembled from separates. But structural correspondence is not full explanation — the transition from physical to phenomenal holism remains the hard problem." Stapp would notice the article's defence against decoherence (line 179) operates inside the Map's minimal-interaction framework rather than committing to specific Penrose-Hameroff microtubule mechanisms — the framework requires only that consciousness influence collapses already occurring, not that it maintain coherence. This is the calibration discipline at its right grain: Stapp-architectural compatibility without microtubule-specific mechanism commitment.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)

Substantial purchase on both articles. The-binding-problem's subject-binding section (lines 130–138) is structurally Nagelian: "Subject binding cannot be stated in purely computational terms. 'Why are these experiences all mine?' presupposes a subject for whom binding obtains. No neural mechanism for self-reference explains why there is a *subject* at all — why there is someone for whom these processes are unified rather than parallel processes with no owner." This is Nagel's "what is it like to be" applied at the unification-of-experience grain rather than the species-comparison grain. The article's invocation of Nagel's split-brain dictum (line 138: "too much unity to say 'two', yet too much separation to say 'one'") is appropriately weighted — Nagel is named as the source of the dictum without the article inheriting Nagel's full set of metaphysical commitments around it. Lines 116–118 on autonoetic temporal binding ("When you remember a childhood event, you are simultaneously present as rememberer (now) and protagonist (then). Two temporal perspectives are unified in a single experience without collapsing into one") is Nagel-friendly perspectivalism applied at the within-self temporal grain — the same structural move Nagel made for the between-organism case is here made for the within-organism diachronic case.

The baseline-cognition article's line 78 ("A chimpanzee might *feel* uncertain and look for more information, but (the hypothesis proposes) cannot represent that uncertainty *as* uncertainty") is Nagel-discipline applied at the metarepresentational grain. The procedural/declarative metacognition distinction is Nagelian in its respect for what it would be like to be a creature with one capacity but not the other — the article is careful never to assert from outside what the chimpanzee's first-person grasp of its own uncertainty is, only to identify the structural feature (representation-of-representation) that the behavioural evidence does not display. Line 164's framing ("Great apes may experience without experiencing *that* they experience") is Nagel's perspectivalism extended into the interspecies-introspection register without claiming third-person access to the first-person fact.

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)

Both articles supply the Whiteheadian register with substantial work, with the discipline's signature constraint observed throughout: process-philosophical resonance is praiseworthy at any tier but is never used as the engine of tier-upgrade.

The-binding-problem's "unity as primitive" treatment (lines 181–183) is the most directly Whiteheadian move in the article: "treat phenomenal unity as metaphysically basic rather than derived from non-unified elements. If unity is what makes something an experience rather than a feature added to experience, then BP2 is not a problem to be solved by finding the right mechanism — it is a feature of what consciousness *is*." Whitehead's insistence that experience-events (actual occasions) are the fundamental units of reality, with prehension as the basic relation, finds a home here without the article committing to the full Whiteheadian metaphysics. The concepts/prehension link in the frontmatter is appropriate — it points the Whitehead-curious reader to the relevant article without inflating prehension into a load-bearing term within this article. The cross-modal binding section's "unity of place" treatment (lines 98–102) is also Whitehead-friendly in its insistence that "you do not experience separate visual greenness, auditory birdsong, olfactory pine, tactile breeze, and proprioceptive walking. You experience *being in a forest* — a unified atmospheric quality that resists decomposition into sensory sources" — atmospheric prehension at the place-grain, expressed in vocabulary that does not require Whitehead to be cited.

The baseline-cognition article's most Whitehead-relevant move is the consciousness-as-amplifier framing (lines 53–57): "consciousness *amplifies* baseline cognition through flexible deployment, metacognitive monitoring, and counterfactual reasoning—without adding computational power." This is process-philosophical in its refusal to localise consciousness as a discrete substance — it is treated as something that *transforms* the cognitive process rather than as an addition to it. The delegatory-dualism cross-link at line 148 ("physical brain states delegate their causal responsibilities to conscious experiences, which then do the actual causing through preemption rather than redundant dual causation") is Whitehead-friendly in its avoidance of crude substance dualism — the causation is process-described (delegation, preemption) rather than substance-described.

**Constraint observed (evidential-status discipline)**: Both articles had structural temptation toward process-philosophical inflation that was explicitly declined. The-binding-problem could have deployed the unity-as-primitive treatment to slide from "phenomenal unity is basic" to "panpsychism is the obvious resolution because experience-events are everywhere"; the article explicitly does not (line 183 frames unity-as-primitive as a Map position alongside, not absorbing, the classical and quantum approaches). The baseline-cognition article could have deployed the great-ape-consciousness concession (line 164: "Great apes likely have genuine phenomenal experience") to slide minimal organisms up the five-tier scale on Whiteheadian grounds; the article explicitly does not — its claim about great apes is conditional ("likely have"), tier-appropriate (this is a "live hypothesis" register, not a "realistic possibility" register), and load-bearing only for the article's structural-functional claim about *which capacities* the gap tracks rather than for any positive panpsychist claim.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)

Light purchase on the-binding-problem (lines 193, 196 mention free-will and bidirectional-interaction in the right register — phenomenal attention selecting among possible binding configurations is agency-relevant without claim-inflation). Substantial purchase on baseline-cognition (lines 174–176): "If consciousness enables counterfactual reasoning and deliberate selection among alternatives, the baseline cognition framework supports libertarian free will... The baseline-to-human transition marks not just expanded cognition but the emergence of genuine agency." Kane would recognise this as exactly the right move: agency is *located* at the metarepresentational threshold — the ability to take one's own dispositions as objects of thought is what enables the deliberation Kane's libertarianism requires. The article does not over-claim: it does not assert that great apes lack agency (line 164 leaves their phenomenal status open); it claims that *human-distinctive* agency — the kind that requires genuine alternative-possibilities reasoning — emerges with the consciousness-dependent capacities the article catalogues. This is Kane's own framing: ultimate-source agency requires the kind of self-modifying metacognitive capacity that consciousness contributes.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)

McGinn's discipline of honest acknowledgement of cognitive closure is visible across both articles in calibrated form. The-binding-problem's framing of the "shared structure" (lines 140–149) is the article's signature McGinnian move: rather than claim that BP2 is *unsolvable*, the article claims that the persistence of the gap across all five varieties suggests it reflects something *structural*. McGinn's mysterianism counsels that some explanatory targets may be cognitively closed to us; the article's discipline is to identify a pattern that *would* be expected if the targets were structurally beyond classical-mechanistic explanation, while remaining open to the possibility that the gap might yield to non-classical (quantum-holistic, unity-as-primitive) approaches. Line 149's framing — "Any successful explanation must work across all five varieties: a theory that explains perceptual binding but cannot address temporal or subject binding has explained BP1, not BP2" — is McGinn-strength but appropriately conditional: it places a structural constraint on what *would* count as a solution rather than asserting that no solution can exist.

The baseline-cognition article's mention of mysterianism in the concepts list (line 30) is appropriately light — the article's primary discipline is not mysterian but *constructive* (it claims to identify what consciousness contributes, not that consciousness's contribution is unknowable). Line 90's framing ("Neural explanations exist—prefrontal cortex expansion, increased long-range connectivity, enhanced synaptic density—but the Map finds these incomplete: they describe neural *correlates* without explaining why these changes produce qualitatively different cognitive operations") is McGinn-friendly in its honest naming of where neural-correlate accounts stop being explanations and start being descriptions. The article's restraint here — naming the explanatory limit without claiming the limit is permanent — is the right McGinnian register for a constructive article.

### The Hardline Empiricist (Birch)

This persona's praise is **load-bearing for this review** because baseline-cognition operates at the boundary cases this persona is designed to police: animal consciousness (great apes), AI consciousness (line 168–172), and the relationship between functional capacity and phenomenal status. The article's discipline on this front is **strong and worth naming explicitly**.

**First, the load-bearing-claim delocalisation at lines 53–57 is exemplary discipline.** The article's central inferential move is *not* "great apes are/are not phenomenally conscious" (where evidential discipline applies most strictly and the five-tier scale's lower tiers are appropriate); the article's central inferential move is "the human-ape gap systematically tracks consciousness-dependent functional capacities, which is what we'd expect if consciousness amplifies rather than epiphenomenally accompanies neural processing." The first claim would require evidential weight the science has not yet supplied. The second claim is tractable from third-person evidence (working memory capacity, metacognitive task performance, cumulative-culture analysis, logical-reasoning experiments) and the article confines its load-bearing assertions to the second. Line 164's "Great apes may experience without experiencing *that* they experience" is the model for this discipline: the phenomenal-status claim is hedged at the "live hypothesis" register, while the structural-functional claim (what they can and cannot represent metarepresentationally) is appropriately stronger.

**Second, the Randeniya 2025 citation at line 92 is load-bearing in a Birch-praise-worthy way.** The article cites the meta-analysis ("only 10% of claimed unconscious processing effects survived rigorous scrutiny") to support the framework's core empirical commitment: if baseline cognition represents what neural processing achieves without consciousness, genuinely unconscious processing should be far more limited than previously assumed. The citation is used at exactly the right grain: it strengthens the article's specific empirical claim (genuinely unconscious processing is more limited than previously assumed) without being inflated into broader metaphysical conclusions. This is the discipline of *citation-at-its-actual-strength* the Birch persona praises specifically.

**Third, the AI-consciousness section (lines 168–172) declines the tier-upgrade temptation.** The article had structural opportunity to deploy the baseline-cognition framework to upgrade AI systems on the evidential-status scale — "if AI excels at pattern recognition and statistical correlation, those are baseline-cognition capacities, therefore current AI is at minimum at the great-ape baseline-consciousness tier." The article explicitly declines: "This doesn't prove AI cannot be conscious but suggests human-level cognition would require something functionally equivalent to conscious amplification." The conditional framing ("would require") preserves the negative result (current AI lacks human-distinctive consciousness-requiring capacities) without asserting the positive (current AI has great-ape-equivalent baseline consciousness). The cross-link to ai-consciousness-typology (line 172) does the work of disambiguating the six categories of AI-phenomenality; the article correctly does not absorb this work into a single overstated claim. This is the *tenet-coherent, not evidence-elevating* discipline operating at the AI-consciousness grain.

**Fourth, the falsifier section (lines 178–190) is calibrated at the right grain.** Five falsifiers are named, and each is structurally specific: (1) great apes achieving human-level cognition through training; (2) WM expansion without cognitive gains; (3) demonstrated unconscious logical reasoning; (4) AI achieving human-level cognition without consciousness; (5) random rather than systematic gap distribution. Each falsifier names *what specifically would have to change* for the inference to fail — exactly the discipline framework-stage-calibration (per [framework-stage-calibration](/project/framework-stage-calibration/)) requires. The article identifies its own claim as *currently supported by a pattern that would be visibly different under the named conditions*, rather than over-claiming structurally-impossible-to-undermine results. This is the framework-stage-calibration discipline operating at the catalogue's most-tested empirical-functional inferential move.

**Fifth, the binding-problem article's evidential discipline at lines 151–159 (the "Dissociations: BP1 Without BP2" section) is the matching exhibit.** The article argues that NREM sleep, anesthesia, and unconscious multisensory processing dissociate BP1 from BP2 — and explicitly admits the dissociation is not clean: "The picture is not a clean 'BP1 intact, BP2 vanished' split, but certain forms of processing persist while unified experience does not" (line 155). The honest acknowledgement that the dissociation evidence supports the BP1/BP2 distinction without cleanly establishing it is Birch-praise-worthy: the article retreats from the rhetorically-tempting strong dissociation claim to the actual empirical record, and then notes what the record can and cannot do. Line 157's IIT engagement is the same discipline: "predictive success in identifying *when* consciousness is present does not settle *why* high-Φ systems have phenomenal unity. Correlation between integration and consciousness, even if empirically robust, is consistent with integration being necessary without being identical." This is the constrain-vs-establish discipline operating at the central rivalry the binding-problem article must engage with.

**Tenet-as-evidence-upgrade is praise-worthily *not* done across both articles.** Baseline-cognition could have deployed the bidirectional-interaction tenet's load-bearing role at line 142 ("The baseline cognition framework supports the Bidirectional Interaction tenet") to upgrade the framework's inferential strength on tenet-coherence grounds; the article explicitly does not — line 142 names the *direction* of support (the framework supports the tenet, not the tenet supporting the framework's empirical inference) and the falsifier section (lines 178–190) is structured so that the tenet's compatibility with the framework would not by itself prevent any of the five falsifiers from undermining the inference. The-binding-problem article could have deployed the convergence across the five varieties as a tier-upgrade for dualism (rather than for irreducibility); the article's "Relation to Site Perspective" section (lines 185–197) carefully assigns each tenet a specific role (Dualism: irreducibility; Minimal Quantum Interaction: provision of holism-substrate; Bidirectional Interaction: top-down causation from phenomenal states; No Many Worlds: real-collapse selection; Occam's Razor Has Limits: pattern-not-coincidence). This decomposition is the *tenet-coherent, not evidence-elevating* discipline operating at the multi-tenet exhibit grain — each tenet's role is named, none is asked to do work it cannot do.

## Content Strengths

### concepts/baseline-cognition.md

- **Strongest point**: The load-bearing-claim delocalisation (lines 53–57) — the article's central inferential move is *not* about great-ape consciousness directly but about the systematic correspondence between consciousness-dependent functional capacities and the human-ape gap. This is the discipline's signature move: locating the load-bearing assertion at the tractable empirical grain rather than at the contested phenomenal-status grain.
- **Notable quote**: "The Unfinishable Map proposes that consciousness *amplifies* baseline cognition through flexible deployment, metacognitive monitoring, and counterfactual reasoning—without adding computational power... Understanding what baseline cognition can and cannot achieve clarifies what consciousness contributes—and provides evidence against epiphenomenalism, since the human-ape gap tracks precisely those capacities where consciousness appears causally required." (lines 53–57)
- **Why it works**: It frames the article's positive contribution as a structural inference from the *pattern* of capacities rather than from any individual capacity or any direct phenomenal-status claim. The pattern is the explanandum; the framework is the explanation; the falsifier section names what would have to *change about the pattern* for the inference to fail.

- **Second strongest point**: The five-falsifier section (lines 178–190) — calibrated at exactly the framework-stage-calibration grain. Each falsifier names a structural change in the world rather than incremental empirical progress.
- **Notable quote**: "If pharmaceutical enhancement of great ape WM to 7±2 items produced no corresponding metarepresentation or logical reasoning improvement, the link would weaken." (line 184)
- **Why it works**: It identifies a specific empirical condition that would dissociate working-memory capacity from the cognitive operations the framework attributes to it — testing the framework's *causal* claim (WM expansion enables qualitatively different operations) rather than its *correlational* claim (humans have more WM than great apes).

- **Third strongest point**: The AI-consciousness section's tier-upgrade restraint (lines 168–172) — declines to deploy the framework as an upgrade for current AI systems on the five-tier scale, while still identifying what AI-system-development would have to deliver to be human-cognition-equivalent.
- **Notable quote**: "What AI struggles with—genuine cross-domain flexibility, metacognitive monitoring, counterfactual reasoning about unprecedented situations, cumulative deliberate innovation—are the capacities tied to consciousness in the human-ape comparison. This doesn't prove AI cannot be conscious but suggests human-level cognition would require something functionally equivalent to conscious amplification." (lines 170–171)
- **Why it works**: It runs the human-ape inference structure on AI systems (functional capacities, conscious-amplification dependence) without sliding into a positive phenomenal-status claim about current AI. The conditional framing ("would require") preserves what the framework can do without overstating what it does.
