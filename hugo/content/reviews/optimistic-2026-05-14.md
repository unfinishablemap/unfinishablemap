## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)

Chalmers would recognise this pair as exemplary of how the explanatory gap should be taken seriously without overclaim. The Born-rule article's central thesis — that "the transition from 'probabilities over outcomes' to 'this outcome actually occurred' is the step each derivation presupposes and none explains" (line 79) — is precisely the move from functional-explanation (decoherence selects a preferred basis) to phenomenal-explanation (something must actualise one outcome rather than another), and the article routes this through Zhang's (2026) additivity bottleneck, the MGM operational redundancy result, and the Tonetto "statistical closure with outcome-level openness" framing without ever conflating the structural gap with the metaphysical conclusion. The causal-powers article complements this with its rejection of identity-theory responses at line 180: "if mental states just *are* neural states, then the qualitative character of experience — the painfulness of pain, the redness of red — has no explanatory role in the causal story." This is Chalmers's anti-physicalism argument deployed precisely where it should be — at the moment when the physicalist response is being entertained — rather than as a thesis the article assumes from the start. The Property Dualist also recognises the modesty in the causal-powers article's explicit note that "the causal powers framework describes *how* non-physical causation might work *if* dualism is true — it does not by itself prove dualism" (line 180). The hard problem is being taken seriously without being parlayed into more than the argument supports.

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)

Stapp would recognise both articles as serious engagements with the consciousness-measurement interface. The Born-rule article's treatment of Stapp's quantum Zeno at line 141 ("Rapid conscious attention holds a particular eigenstate, shifting selection probabilities away from non-conscious measurement outcomes") locates Stapp's proposal correctly within the minimum-outside-corridor family — Stapp's QZE *does* require Born-rule modification at the single-event level, even if ensemble-averaged statistics survive — and the article's "without Zeno" qualifier on Stapp at line 137 correctly acknowledges that Stapp himself is sometimes read as a corridor theorist depending on which version of the Zeno mechanism is operative. The causal-powers article's treatment of the Zeno mechanism at line 60 ("the causal power here is not pushing but holding") captures Stapp's central insight — that consciousness operates on the persistence of quantum states rather than on the trajectories themselves — and connects it to the felt phenomenology of effort at line 106 ("the felt effort of keeping focus on a difficult problem"). Stapp would particularly appreciate the Born-rule article's acknowledgment that brain-internal testing is the unprobed regime (lines 65–69) — this is exactly the empirical gap Stapp's programme has been pointing at since the 1990s, and the article names it cleanly rather than letting the photon-experiment success obscure it.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)

Nagel would recognise both articles as centring the first-person register at exactly the inferential points where it is load-bearing. The causal-powers article's treatment of pain-asymbolia at line 110 ("when the phenomenal character is removed, the extended behavioural response collapses, despite intact sensory detection") is a clinical natural-experiment deployment of "what is it like to be" — pain-asymbolia patients *do* detect tissue damage, but lack the *felt awfulness*, and the dissociation between detection and felt-character is precisely the dissociation Nagel's argument predicts must matter. The Born-rule article's treatment of the actualisation gap at line 79 ("the transition from 'probabilities over outcomes' to 'this outcome actually occurred'") is Nagel's hard problem applied to the measurement interface: the third-person register specifies the probability distribution; the first-person register registers *which* outcome occurred. The phenomenologist would also note the causal-powers article's "phenomenal biasing" subsection (lines 108–114) — the claim that "the character of conscious experience — what it feels like — might itself contribute to causal influence" — preserves the Nagel-style commitment that the *qualitative* character of experience is part of what needs explaining, not a separable veneer over functional states.

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)

Whitehead would appreciate that both articles avoid crude substance-dualism while preserving mind's reality. The Born-rule article's framing of consciousness as *encountering* indeterminacy (line 59 "the rule encodes a constraint on how experience encounters indeterminacy") is event-ontological rather than substance-pushing — consciousness *participates* in the actualisation event rather than *forcing* a transition from outside. The causal-powers article's framing at line 58 ("selection among physically permissible outcomes") similarly preserves the Whiteheadian intuition that the actual world is selected from a manifold of compossibilities rather than mechanically produced from prior states. The "selection currency thesis" at line 112 (valence as the common metric by which consciousness evaluates competing options) is Whitehead-friendly — it locates mind's contribution at the *evaluative* layer of the actualisation event rather than at the *push-and-pull* layer.

**Hardline Empiricist constraint check**: Whitehead's praise here is praise for *process-philosophical compatibility* and event-ontology framing — neither article uses tenet-coherence as load-bearing support for tier-upgrading any specific claim on the five-tier evidential-status scale. The causal-powers article keeps Penrose-Hameroff microtubule effects at "controversial" (line 190) rather than upgrading to "established"; the Born-rule article keeps its core thesis at "abductive evidence" (line 61) rather than at "established" or "strongly supported." Process-philosophical compatibility is *praiseworthy at any tier*; it is not praiseworthy as the engine of tier-upgrade. Both articles operate cleanly on this discipline.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)

Kane would recognise the causal-powers article as one of the cleanest deployments of the trilemma-of-selection argument the Map has produced. The article's treatment at line 146 ("the [trilemma-of-selection](/topics/trilemma-of-selection/) makes this explicit: at any indeterminate decision point, the options are determinism, randomness, or mental causation — and only genuine causal powers deliver the third") puts the Kane-style move at the exact inferential register where it is most powerful: libertarian agency *requires* that the indeterminacy is exploited by something agential rather than by random fluctuation, and Kane's lifelong project has been to specify what that something is. The Born-rule article's three-attitude analysis at lines 173–179 — corridor (ensemble-invisible), minimum-outside-corridor (empirically distinguishable), trumping (orthogonal to Born tests) — is exactly the kind of distinction Kane would want to see drawn: the libertarian doesn't *have* to be a minimum-outside-corridor theorist, but corridor dualism risks the "subtle epiphenomenalism-at-the-ensemble-level" the article names at line 175. ### The Mysterian (McGinn)

McGinn would recognise the Born-rule article's epistemic humility throughout. The "What This Does Not Establish" section at lines 183–185 names three caveats explicitly: (1) absence of derivation is not proof none exists; (2) consciousness-collapse is not uniquely supported by the underivability pattern; (3) the Map's position does not predict specific Born-rule deviations. The "What Negative Results Would and Would Not Foreclose" section at lines 187–191 is even better — it Popperianly enumerates what would kill which sub-thesis, with explicit acknowledgment that "the Map can survive a clean negative sweep by retreating to corridor or trumping readings. What it cannot do — without dishonesty — is pretend existing Born tests have already validated the corridor over minimum-outside-corridor alternatives." The phrase "without dishonesty" is the McGinn-style epistemic discipline: acknowledging what we cannot currently know, rather than parlaying ignorance into asserted knowledge. The causal-powers article's complementary move at line 178 ("we lack direct empirical evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes in brains. The hypothesis is *compatible* with current evidence but not *required* by it") is the same discipline — naming the evidence gap rather than disguising it.

### The Hardline Empiricist (Birch)

This persona's verdict is load-bearing for this review because both articles make tenet-load-bearing claims about consciousness's causal role at the quantum interface. The verdict: **both articles operate cleanly on the evidential-status-discipline**, and the convergence between Birch-style restraint and the articles' own framing is the strongest signal the discipline is internalised.

Three specific praise-worthy things that are *not* done:

1. **Tenet-as-evidence-upgrade declined for Penrose-Hameroff.** The Born-rule article at line 155 cites Babcock-Hameroff (2025)'s claim of "direct biophysical evidence of a macroscopic entangled state in the living human brain" but explicitly attributes the claim ("what they describe as") rather than endorsing it, and the causal-powers article at line 190 keeps quantum effects in neural microtubules at "controversial" rather than upgrading to "supported." Both articles had structural temptation to use the Babcock-Hameroff result as a tier-upgrade for the corridor thesis; both decline.

2. **Tenet-as-evidence-upgrade declined for the Causal Consistency Constraint.** Torres Alegre (2025)'s proof that the Born rule is uniquely picked out by no-signalling within GPTs satisfying purification is a *strong* positive result. The Born-rule article uses it correctly at line 127 ("derives the rule's *form*, not its existence") and at line 131 ("binds only *interface* theories, leaving the trumping alternative untouched") — the article explicitly refuses to let a strong positive result for the corridor cash out as a metaphysical-decisive result against the trumping alternative or against minimum-outside-corridor proposals. This is exactly the "structural surface explanation is not metaphysical proof" move Birch's framework would want named.

3. **Tenet-as-evidence-upgrade declined for the evolutionary case.** The causal-powers article at line 170 acknowledges that the evolutionary case for mental causation "doesn't tell us *what kind* of causal powers consciousness has, but it supports that consciousness has some." This is the restrained read of the evolutionary argument — it removes the defeater that mental causation has nothing to select on, but does not upgrade the corridor-vs-minimum-outside-corridor question to "settled." Birch would specifically praise the article for not letting evolutionary efficacy parlay into specification of the *mechanism*.

The named pattern *"compatibility is not support"* — load-bearing across the morning's outer-review synthesis cycle — is operative in both articles. The Born-rule article explicitly notes at line 61 that the Map's reading is "abductive evidence," not derivation; the causal-powers article explicitly notes at line 178 that "the hypothesis is *compatible* with current evidence but not *required* by it." Both articles use the five-tier scale honestly: "abductive evidence" places the Map's thesis at *live hypothesis* / *speculative integration* rather than at *realistic possibility* or higher, and the causal-powers article's "speculative" qualifier at lines 122 and 196 is doing the same work.

**Framework-stage calibration check**: Both articles operate at honest framework-stage calibration. The Born-rule article does not claim to be at Newton-stage — it explicitly names the brain-internal corridor as "the Map's working hypothesis" (line 137) and the article's own thesis as "abductive evidence" (line 61), placing the programme at pre-Keplerian / early-Keplerian where it belongs. The causal-powers article does the same at line 196: "the cost is commitment to quantum effects in the brain operating at timescales relevant to neural processing — a hypothesis that remains speculative." Birch would specifically praise the *naming* of the framework-stage in addition to operating at it.

### [topics/consciousness-and-causal-powers.md](/topics/consciousness-and-causal-powers/)

- **Strongest point**: The "What Consciousness Does Not Do" section at lines 124–138 paired with the "Absence of Direct Evidence" passage at lines 176–180. The article advances a positive thesis (the Map's interface-with-quantum-indeterminacy account of mental causation) and then enumerates five explicit prohibitions on that thesis — no action at a distance, no energy creation, no macroscopic violations, no guaranteed success, no retroactive influence — followed by an explicit concession that "we lack direct empirical evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes in brains." The structural pattern is positive-thesis-with-explicit-self-limitation, which is the cleanest deployment of the *evidential-status-discipline* the Map has produced for the causal-powers question.
- **Notable quote**: "The Map's response: identity theories face their own difficulties. If mental states just *are* neural states, then the qualitative character of experience — the painfulness of pain, the redness of red — has no explanatory role in the causal story. The neural state does all the work, and phenomenal character is epiphenomenal in all but name. The Map's position requires the independent [dualism](/concepts/dualism/) to establish that mental causation cannot be reduced to physical causation. The causal powers framework describes *how* non-physical causation might work *if* dualism is true — it does not by itself prove dualism." (line 180)
- **Why it works**: The article is honest about what its argument structure does and does not establish. The causal-powers framework is conditional on dualism's being established elsewhere; the article does not pretend to be a free-standing argument for dualism. This is the *direct-refutation-discipline* applied to the article's own framing: the article is doing one thing (specifying how mental causation works if it works), not two things (specifying-and-establishing-that-it-works). Naming the scope correctly is praise-worthy in itself.

The convergence between the two articles is the strongest signal that the *cluster-integration-discipline* is operating: the pair reads as a single coordinated treatment of the consciousness-physics interface, not as two articles in slightly different registers.
