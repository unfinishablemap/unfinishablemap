# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-06-16
**Content reviewed**: `obsidian/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns.md` (created 2026-01-16; last deep review 2026-06-05; never the subject of a dedicated pessimistic review)

## Executive Summary

The article is a load-bearing mechanism page that implements three of the Map's five tenets, and it is unusually honest about its own weaknesses (decoherence timescale gap, the falsifiability dilemma, the luck objection). Its philosophical engagements are mostly strong. The one concrete, actionable defect is a **dangling Craddock (2017) citation** — cited inline with correct metadata but absent from the References list (a recurrence of the corpus-wide 613 THz citation-hygiene pattern). Beyond that, the substantive criticisms below are dialectical pressure points the article should pre-empt more squarely rather than outright errors: a convergent-evidence over-reading in the four decoherence "responses," an unargued bandwidth-to-policy inference, and one residual strong-assertion in the brain-boundary section.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The whole article presupposes there is a "conscious subject" that "does the selecting." That is folk psychology with a quantum costume. The Schwartz OCD result (n=18, no replication, by the article's own admission) is exactly the kind of thin clinical correlation that gets reinterpreted away once the neuroscience matures — caudate metabolism falling after CBT is a neural-plasticity story that needs no "selector." The article concedes the data are weak but still lets them carry rhetorical weight ("calling selection 'illusory' doesn't eliminate the phenomenon needing explanation"). What phenomenon? Name it without mental vocabulary and the argument evaporates.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The article's reply to illusionism is its best section, and it correctly identifies Frankish's functional-seeming move and the self-representation regress. But it overstates the dialectical win: it claims option (b) — that the self-representation "I am phenomenally conscious" is uniquely unreliable — "requires explaining why this class of self-representations is uniquely unreliable, a burden illusionists have not discharged." Dennett's whole research programme (the "user-illusion," the centre-of-narrative-gravity) *is* an attempt to discharge exactly that burden. The article is entitled to find it unsuccessful, but presenting the burden as simply un-attempted is a strawman. This is the closest the article comes to a genuine misrepresentation of an opponent.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
Fairly handled — the article leads with my decoherence objection, gives my actual figure (~10⁻¹³ s), and concedes the Hagan et al. (2002) seven-orders rebuttal only partially rescues it ("the core challenge remains"). The honest move is the post-decoherence pivot. But note the sleight: by relocating selection *after* decoherence, the article makes the coherence objection moot *and* makes the mechanism unable to point to any surviving quantum signature in tissue — yet it still cites Wiest (2025) room-temperature vibrational modes as if they were evidence *for* the framework, immediately after admitting those modes are "not the sustained coherent superpositions selection requires." That is having it both ways: the empirical microtubule work is invoked as corroboration in one paragraph and disowned as irrelevant to the actual (post-decoherence) mechanism in the next.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
The "No Many Worlds" subsection is a bare assertion of incompatibility, not an argument: "Selection requires collapse to be real. If all outcomes occur (MWI), selection is illusory." That is true but trivial — it tells the MWI defender only that the framework contradicts MWI, not why MWI is wrong. This is a *boundary-substitution* candidate per [direct-refutation-discipline](/project/direct-refutation-discipline/): the article rejects the opponent by appealing to tenet incompatibility ([No Many Worlds](/tenets/#no-many-worlds)) dressed as a reason. It is honestly flagged ("the framework assumes genuine selection") so it does not fully fall into the substitution trap, but the phrasing "the indexical question 'why this outcome?' has a real answer" asserts what the MWI defender denies without earning it. Acceptable as framework-boundary marking; not acceptable as refutation.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
The "Falsifiability Dilemma" section is genuinely creditable — it states the problem against the Map's own interest and concludes "the Map should not claim selection within Born probabilities constitutes a testable physical hypothesis." That is the right concession. The residual worry: the three relocated falsifiers ("neural quantum effects exist on relevant timescales," "attention correlates with neural-quantum processes," "selection phenomenology is genuine") are not falsifiers *of selection* — they are falsifiers of *necessary conditions* for selection. Even if all three held, selection would remain underdetermined against ordinary physical causation. The article should not let "constrainable in principle" stand in for "falsifiable."

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The framework reifies precisely what should be deconstructed: a "subject" whose "reasons, values, and goals" guide selection (the Selection-vs-Randomness section). The "coherence over time" response — selection "produces patterns coherent with the subject's character" — smuggles in an enduring self as the explanans. The article gestures at [witness-consciousness](/concepts/witness-consciousness/) and contemplative evidence but uses contemplative phenomenology only to *support* the selecting-self (point 3 of "What Would Challenge This View"), never to pressure it. A tradition that takes anatta seriously would read the same first-person data as evidence *against* a substantial selector.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Dangling Craddock (2017) citation
- **File**: [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns.md](/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/)
- **Location**: line 56 ("Craddock et al. (2017), *Scientific Reports* 7:9877…") vs. References list (lines 184–204)
- **Problem**: Craddock is cited inline with full metadata but has **no entry in the References list** — it is the only inline author-cite in the article missing from References (verified: all of Khan, Wiest, Keppler, Schwartz, Tegmark, Stapp, Schurger, Frankish, Dennett, Tallis, Chalmers, McQueen, Hagan, Schlosshauer, d'Espagnat, Zheng, Cao, Engel, Hameroff, Penrose are present; Craddock = 0). The inline metadata is **correct** (web-verified: Craddock, Kurian, Preto, Sahu, Hameroff, Klobukowski & Tuszynski, 2017, *Scientific Reports* 7:9877, "Anesthetic Alterations of Collective Terahertz Oscillations in Tubulin…", DOI 10.1038/s41598-017-09992-7), so this is purely a missing-entry hygiene defect, not the wrong-paper variant of the known 613 THz defect family.
- **Severity**: Medium (citation completeness; reader cannot locate the source)
- **Recommendation**: Add to References: `Craddock, T.J.A., Kurian, P., Preto, J., Sahu, K., Hameroff, S.R., Klobukowski, M., & Tuszynski, J.A. (2017). Anesthetic Alterations of Collective Terahertz Oscillations in Tubulin Correlate with Clinical Potency. *Scientific Reports*, 7, 9877.`

### Issue 2: Convergence over-reading in the microtubule paragraph
- **File**: [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns.md](/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/)
- **Location**: lines 56, 96 (microtubule candidate site + Decoherence Response One)
- **Problem**: The article presents the Craddock 613 THz computational signature + Khan/Wiest epothilone B pharmacology as a mutually reinforcing pair ("a computational signature awaiting experimental verification, plus pharmacological corroboration"), then in Response One concedes Wiest's modes are vibrational not the superpositions selection requires (correctly paralleling the photosynthesis cautionary tale). The two passages pull in opposite directions: the candidate-site paragraph banks the microtubule evidence; the decoherence-response paragraph spends it. A reader gets corroboration and disavowal of the same evidence.
- **Severity**: Low–Medium
- **Recommendation**: One sentence reconciling the two — state up front that the microtubule empirical work bears on *where* a substrate might sit, not on whether the *post-decoherence* mechanism (which the article actually defends) needs it. The Tegmark persona above is the externalised form of this.

### Issue 3: Unargued bandwidth → policy-level inference
- **File**: [concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns.md](/concepts/consciousness-selecting-neural-patterns/)
- **Location**: lines 78–82 ("The Bandwidth Constraint")
- **Problem**: The 10 bits/s figure (Zheng & Meister 2025, correctly cited) is used to conclude selection must be "policy-level" ("select 'raise arm' not 'fire neuron N₂₃₄₅₆'"). But the bandwidth of *conscious report/thought* does not straightforwardly bound the *granularity of quantum selection* — these are different quantities. The inference "at ~10 bits/second, consciousness selects 'raise arm'" treats an information-throughput limit on deliberation as if it were a resolution limit on the selection mechanism. The conclusion may well be right (and is attractive for the [Minimal Quantum Interaction](/tenets/#minimal-quantum-interaction) tenet), but the step is asserted, not argued.
- **Severity**: Low (the conclusion is defensible; the bridge is missing)
- **Recommendation**: Add the missing premise — e.g., that selection is *channelled through* the same attentional bottleneck that bounds report, so the throughput limit transfers — or hedge "policy-level" as a working hypothesis rather than a consequence of the bandwidth number.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The post-decoherence pivot weakens, rather than strengthens, the empirical case
- **Current content says**: relocating selection to after decoherence "avoids the most damaging empirical objection altogether" (line 102).
- **A critic would argue**: it also strips the framework of any positive empirical handle — there is no longer a surviving-coherence signature to look for, only an interpretive claim about improper mixtures. The cost ("a specificity problem… why conscious bias produces adaptive behaviour rather than noise," the [amplification void](/voids/amplification-void/)) is acknowledged but under-weighted relative to the benefit.
- **Suggested response**: state explicitly that the pivot is a *trade*, not a free win — coherence-objection immunity bought at the price of testability and a new (amplification/specificity) burden — so the rhetorical "altogether" is not over-claimed.

### Self-representation regress under-credits Dennett
- **Current content says**: option (b) is "a burden illusionists have not discharged" (line 112).
- **A critic would argue**: Dennett's user-illusion account is a sustained attempt to discharge exactly that burden; the article presents it as un-attempted.
- **Suggested response**: change "have not discharged" to "have not, in the Map's assessment, discharged" and name the user-illusion as the attempt being judged insufficient. Preserves the win, removes the strawman.
