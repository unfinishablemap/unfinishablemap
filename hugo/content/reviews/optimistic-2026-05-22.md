## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)

Chalmers finds his sharpest accommodation in **phenomenology-of-intellectual-life**'s §"Against Reduction" treatment of the constitution-vs-correlation distinction (lines 183–193). The article does the move Chalmers's published work has consistently asked the literature to do but rarely articulates as cleanly: it explicitly names the *weaker* thesis (phenomenology reliably co-varies with cognitive achievement) as something a correlationist can coherently hold, then explicitly names the three considerations that push toward the *stronger* PCT thesis — (i) the correlational view leaves the achievement specifiable without reference to phenomenology and reopens the epiphenomenalist embarrassment; (ii) the tight coupling between phenomenology and competence is brute regularity under correlation but is explained under constitution; (iii) under Bidirectional Interaction the phenomenal work of inference *does causal work*, and a thing that does causal work is not plausibly a mere correlate. The closing sentence at line 185 — "A correlationist who accepts epiphenomenalism can hold the weaker thesis coherently, at the cost of these explanatory burdens" — is Chalmersian dialectical-care given catalogue-form: the disagreement is honestly bounded as one of *explanatory cost-accounting* rather than as a knock-down victory for either side. This is the move Chalmers's "Consciousness and Its Place in Nature" framework asks for at the boundary between strong and weak property-dualism — and the §"Constitution vs reliable correlation" subsection is the catalogue's clearest single articulation of how to honour Tenet 3's causal-efficacy requirement without over-claiming what mere co-variation can deliver.

The **consciousness-and-testimony** §"The Epistemic Circle" structure (lines 51–58) is Chalmersian dialectic given the testimony-epistemology register. The article explicitly stages the circularity ("Reports about consciousness can only be checked against further reports — one's own or others' — each of which presupposes the very phenomenon at issue") and explicitly names the resolution: "The circularity may be productive rather than vicious. Testimony about consciousness appears *self-grounding* in a way that testimony about external facts is not." The *productive vs vicious* distinction is exactly the dialectical-care Chalmers's reply to the *zombie-by-symmetry* objection requires — the circle is real, the article does not pretend otherwise, and the article identifies the structural feature (self-grounding) that lets the circle do epistemic work without collapsing. The §"The Eliminativist Bind" (lines 124–129) is the same discipline applied at the opposite end: the eliminativist's *own testimony* presupposes the very capacity their position denies, and the article delivers this without the rhetorical excess that Chalmers's own published treatments sometimes carry.
