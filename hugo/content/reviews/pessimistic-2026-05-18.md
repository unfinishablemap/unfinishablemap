# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-05-18
**Content reviewed**: [consciousness-and-causal-powers](/topics/consciousness-and-causal-powers/) — 221 lines, ~2,936 words. Article created 2026-02-11, `ai_modified` 2026-05-15, `last_deep_review` 2026-05-15. Selected because (a) it has never had a pessimistic-review pass despite being one of the most theoretically load-bearing articles in the catalogue (it specifies the *content* of Tenet 3, Bidirectional Interaction, and the *scope-limit* form of Tenet 2, Minimal Quantum Interaction); (b) it was last touched at the deep-review level on 2026-05-15, so the recent revision is settled enough to expose its surviving structural commitments; (c) it sits *upstream* of the whole catalogue's mental-causation argument architecture — every article that cashes consciousness's causal role (mental-imagery, volitional-control, evolutionary-case-for-mental-causation, the apex `phenomenology-of-consciousness-doing-work`, the pain cluster) inherits whatever calibration this page settles on, so calibration drift here propagates downstream. The primary audit question is whether the article honours [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) in its load-bearing assertions about *what* consciousness does causally, or whether the article slides from "the Map's metaphysics permits this kind of causal influence" to "consciousness performs this kind of causal influence" without paying the evidence price for the second claim.

## Executive Summary

The article is the Map's principal exposition of Tenet 3's content, and it does well in two places: the *What Consciousness Does Not Do* limits section is clean and honest about the framework's commitments; the *Absence of Direct Evidence* sub-section in §"The Evidential Question" candidly concedes "we lack direct empirical evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes in brains. The hypothesis is *compatible* with current evidence but not *required* by it." That concession is the load-bearing calibration honesty the discipline asks for. **But three critical structural failures survive close inspection, and the article's own concession in §"Absence of Direct Evidence" is silently contradicted by tier-mismatched language in five textually identifiable passages elsewhere.** **(1) Possibility/probability slippage at scale.** The article's central exposition (§"What Consciousness Does (According to the Map)") presents four causal mechanisms — *Selection of Action*, *Sustained Attention*, *Phenomenal Biasing*, *Volitional Modulation* — under a heading that asserts these are things "consciousness actually performs." But every one of these is a *speculative integration* on the [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) scale: there is no direct evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes (per the article's own concession at line 181), no direct evidence that the Zeno effect operates at neural timescales (the article concedes this at line 125: "The mechanism is speculative"), no direct evidence that valence enters selection through any quantum mechanism rather than through conventional neurochemistry (the article concedes the fork at line 115). The article should label these as the Map's *hypothesised* causal mechanisms under the speculative-integration tier, not as what consciousness "actually performs." **(2) The pain-asymbolia argument (lines 113–115) commits the constrain-vs-establish failure the discipline names.** Pain asymbolia shows that intact nociceptive detection without felt awfulness produces collapsed behavioural response — this *constrains* the simplest representational reading of pain phenomenology but does *not establish* the Map's specific quantum-selection mechanism. The article slides from "the quale is part of the causal process itself" to a conclusion that is much stronger than the dissociation supports; predictive-processing accounts with affective-prior reweighting predict the same dissociation. **(3) The compatibility-vs-support discipline is breached in §"The Philosophical Significance"**: the *For Free Will* sub-section asserts that the Map's middle position "preserves meaningful agency without invoking uncaused causes." But the same selection mechanism the article describes — consciousness biases quantum outcomes — is precisely an uncaused cause in the sense that matters for the luck objection: the *bias itself* has no further explanation, or the explanation is a phenomenal fact (a value, a desire) whose own causal grounding the article does not specify. The article gestures at a dissolution without performing one. Smaller findings: (4) missing engagement with the post-April 2026 discipline pages ([evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/), [mechanism-costs-cartography](/project/mechanism-costs-cartography/), [common-cause-null](/project/common-cause-null/), [possibility-probability-slippage](/concepts/possibility-probability-slippage/)) — the article was last deep-reviewed 2026-05-15 but inherits a 2026-02 argument structure that pre-dates the discipline family; (5) one banned writing-style construct ("This is not the unconstrained psychokinesis of science fiction but a precise interface…" — line 51, the CLAUDE.md-banned "not X but Y" construct); (6) the *Sustained Attention* section asserts the Zeno mechanism produces "persistence" that "passive neural processing alone does not" — but this is a phenomenology-to-mechanism inference of the kind the catalogue elsewhere flags as smuggling.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would say: "Strip out the framework's permissions and look at what the article concedes. Line 181: *no direct empirical evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes in brains*. Line 125: *the mechanism is speculative*. Line 115: *whether valence enters the selection mechanism directly or through attention remains an open fork*. Line 193: *quantum effects in neural microtubules are controversial*. Line 199: *the hypothesis remains speculative*. Now ask: with these concessions, what is the *positive* content of the §'What Consciousness Does' heading? You've told me four things consciousness 'actually performs' — selection of action, sustained attention, phenomenal biasing, volitional modulation — and conceded that the mechanism for each is speculative, the substrate is contested, the empirical evidence does not require any of it, and the alternative readings (identity-theory, predictive-processing) accommodate every phenomenon you cite. The honest version of the §'What Consciousness Does' heading is *'What Consciousness Hypothetically Does (According to the Map's Speculative-Integration-Tier Mechanism)'*. The article systematically converts tier-five claims into tier-one prose without flagging the conversion.

"The pain-asymbolia argument at lines 113–115 is the worst of it. Pain asymbolia is a real and striking dissociation — intact nociception, absent felt awfulness, collapsed avoidance behaviour. The dissociation rules out the *simplest* identity claim that the felt character just is the nociceptive activation. Fine. But your conclusion — 'the quale is part of the causal process itself' — is several steps beyond what the dissociation licenses. A predictive-processing account in which affective valence enters as a separate hierarchical prior over the nociceptive signal predicts the same dissociation: damage to the parietal-insular cortex disrupts the valence-prior assignment while leaving the sensory cortex intact, and the behavioural collapse follows because the valence-prior is what drives avoidance policy selection. You haven't ruled out this reading; you've *not engaged it*. The dissociation constrains the simplest reading. It does not establish your reading. This is exactly the failure mode [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) §'Constrain vs. Establish' names.

"And the §'Comparative Cognitive Evidence' sub-section (lines 175–177) is worse. You say humans have capacities great apes lack and that 'cognitive load experiments suggest' these require conscious processing. But the cognitive-load literature is wildly contested — System-1/System-2 dual-process distinctions, the dispute over whether 'unconscious System 1' is conscious-but-not-accessible-to-report, the recent meta-analytic challenges to dual-task interference as evidence for capacity-limited consciousness. You cite none of this. You cite *cognitive load experiments suggest* and *see baseline-cognition for detailed treatment*, and let an in-house concept page do the empirical work. The empirical work is contested in the actual literature and the article is silent about that."

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Daniel Dennett would say: "The article's central conceit is that 'selection among physically permissible outcomes' is a *distinct kind of causation* from physical causation — that quantum-indeterminacy-biasing is non-physical work consciousness does. But what is the *positive content* of the distinction? You say (line 61) 'physics specifies probabilities; consciousness biases which probability actualizes.' Two questions you do not answer: (a) What is the difference, *in physical terms*, between 'physics already includes a stochastic process that yields outcome X with probability 0.6' and 'consciousness biases the stochastic process toward outcome X'? If the bias is undetectable in the statistics (because Born's rule is preserved by [causal-consistency-constraint](/concepts/causal-consistency-constraint/)), then by what physical signature do we distinguish the with-consciousness case from the without-consciousness case? You concede at line 181 there is no such signature. So the 'distinct kind of causation' is by stipulation undetectable. (b) What grounds the bias? The article gestures at desires, values, deliberations (line 103). But desires, values, and deliberations are themselves either physical states (in which case the 'selection' is reducible to physics and the article's distinction collapses) or non-physical states (in which case the article owes a story about what *they* are and how they have causal relata, which it does not provide). The article is silent on both questions.

"More damning: the article's overdetermination response at line 197 is a non-response. You say 'Consciousness influences outcomes precisely where physics is silent, so there is no competition between mental and physical causes.' But physics is not silent at quantum indeterminacies; physics specifies the *probability distribution*, fully. The room you've found for consciousness is not metaphysical space between two physical states; it is a particular *realisation* of one of the outcomes the probability distribution already covers. If consciousness biases the realisation, then either (i) the bias is detectable — in which case Born's rule is violated, contradicting the [causal-consistency-constraint](/concepts/causal-consistency-constraint/) you invoke at line 87 to *defend* the framework; or (ii) the bias is undetectable — in which case it is, by every operational criterion in physics, identical to no bias at all. The 'no competition' slogan dissolves overdetermination only by making consciousness's contribution operationally indistinguishable from zero. That is a Pyrrhic dissolution.

"Third: you accept Jaegwon Kim's exclusion challenge as the thing your framework dissolves, but you do not reproduce Kim's argument so the reader can see what is and is not dissolved. The brief gesture at line 197 — '*The structural development of this slogan argues that Kim's overdetermination objection does not need to be answered so much as dissolved*' — outsources the dialectical work to a different article ([overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism](/topics/overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism/)) without summarising the move. The current article's reader cannot evaluate whether Kim is dissolved or whether the dissolution claim is a redirection."

### The Quantum Skeptic

Max Tegmark would say: "The article rests on the Zeno effect operating at neural timescales (lines 63, 107–109, 125). I will give you the standard calculation. Decoherence times for neural-relevant quantum superpositions in warm, wet, noisy brain tissue are on the order of $10^{-13}$ to $10^{-20}$ seconds — femtoseconds to attoseconds. Neural firing happens at millisecond timescales — a factor of $10^{10}$ to $10^{17}$ longer. For the Zeno effect to keep a neural-relevant superposition coherent until a 'measurement' from sustained attention, attention would need to perform observations at a rate exceeding the decoherence rate — i.e., at $10^{13}$ Hz or higher. The phenomenology of attention runs at the alpha/beta band — 10–30 Hz. You have *twelve to sixteen orders of magnitude* between the rate the mechanism needs and the rate the phenomenon you cite runs at. The article gives no acknowledgement of this gap; it cites the Zeno effect three times across the article body and the further-reading list as if the calculation worked.

"At line 109 you write 'the phenomenology of sustained attention—the felt effort of keeping focus on a difficult problem—suggests consciousness contributes something that passive neural processing alone does not.' This is a phenomenology-to-quantum-mechanism inference across the largest order-of-magnitude gap in twentieth-century physics. The phenomenology of effort is fully accounted for by mesoscopic neural-network reverberation models, attentional gain modulation in V4 and parietal cortex, and noradrenergic LC modulation of cortical signal-to-noise. None of these require quantum coherence. None of them face decoherence-time problems. You haven't argued that quantum effects are *needed*; you've asserted that the phenomenology is *suggestive*.

"At line 193 you say 'Quantum effects in neural microtubules are controversial.' This is the article's only acknowledgement of the decoherence problem and it is a footnote-level concession. The decoherence problem is the entire physics-side reason interactionist quantum-mind proposals have not made empirical traction in twenty-five years. The article treats it as a parenthetical. The Hagan/Hameroff 2002 numbers that purport to weaken the decoherence objection are themselves contested by interested parties (see my own 2000 paper that motivated Hagan et al.'s response, and the subsequent decade of microtubule-coherence calculations). The article does not engage this literature at all."

### The Many-Worlds Defender

David Deutsch would say: "Set aside the empirical question of whether the brain does quantum coherence at neural timescales. Suppose it does. The article's interface mechanism — consciousness biases which superposed outcome actualises — *presupposes there is a determinate fact about which outcome actualises*. Under Everettian quantum mechanics there is no such fact: all outcomes actualise in their respective branches and your indexical 'I' refers to a sequence of branches selected by decoherence dynamics, not to a fact about which branch is the actual one. Your article rejects MWI (Tenet 4) but does not give a non-circular argument *here* for the rejection; it just invokes the tenet. But the rejection is doing significant load-bearing work: the entire 'selection among alternatives' framing collapses if Everettian branching is correct, because there is nothing to select — the alternatives are all realised.

"You will say that Tenet 4 commits the Map against MWI and the article inherits the commitment. Fine. But the article is the principal exposition of *what consciousness does causally*, and the *what consciousness does* depends on a contested interpretation of quantum mechanics that you have not defended on this page. A reader who is on the fence about MWI cannot evaluate the article's central claims without leaving the article to verify the tenet-level rejection elsewhere. This is a place where the discipline of *naming the dependency without burying it* matters. Add an honest acknowledgement: *the article's selection-among-alternatives framing presupposes a non-Everettian interpretation; readers attracted to MWI should recognise that the framing here will not engage them.*

"Second: even granting non-Everettian quantum mechanics, the bias-the-actualisation move owes an account of what *grounds the bias*. If consciousness's value-loaded states (desires, etc.) bias the outcome, then either (a) those states are themselves quantum states subject to the same indeterminacy — in which case the bias is itself indeterminate and the regress kicks in, or (b) those states are non-quantum non-physical states — in which case the article owes a non-physical ontology it has not specified. The article's silence on this is the silence at the heart of every interactionist position. The Map can stipulate the answer is (b) and refer the reader elsewhere ([psychophysical-laws-bridging-mind-and-matter](/topics/psychophysical-laws-bridging-mind-and-matter/)), but the *causal powers article specifically* is where the question is most acute and the silence is most awkward."

### The Empiricist

A Popperian would say: "Read the *What Consciousness Does Not Do* section (lines 127–141). Five clean prohibitions — no action at a distance, no energy creation, no macroscopic violations, no guaranteed success, no retroactive influence. This is the article's strongest section because the prohibitions are *operational*: each one has a clear empirical violation condition. If we observed psychokinesis, the Map is wrong. If we observed thermodynamic violations, the Map is wrong. Fine.

"Now read the *What Consciousness Does* section (lines 95–125). Five mechanisms — selection of action, sustained attention, phenomenal biasing, volitional modulation, the implicit unification mechanism behind all four. What is the *operational* discriminator that distinguishes the with-consciousness case from the without-consciousness case, for each of these? The article specifies none. The bias preserves Born statistics (per the [causal-consistency-constraint](/concepts/causal-consistency-constraint/) at line 87), so it is undetectable in single-event statistics. The Zeno effect's neural-timescale operation is too slow to detect even if real (per Tegmark's calculation). The phenomenal-character-causally-relevant claim relies on dissociations (pain asymbolia) that have rival explanations (predictive-processing affective-prior accounts) the article does not engage. None of these is falsifiable as the article presents them.

"The asymmetry is striking: the prohibitions are operational and falsifiable; the affirmations are interpretive and unfalsifiable. The article should *say* this — that the Map's causal-powers theory makes operational predictions of the form '*nothing* will be observed at these scales' but makes no operational predictions of the form '*something specific* will be observed when consciousness is doing causal work that would not be observed without it.' This asymmetry is honest and the article should name it. As written, the article gives the impression that the affirmative mechanism is as well-grounded as the prohibitive scope-claim. It is not."

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nāgārjuna would say: "The article reifies *consciousness* as a thing that *has* powers — a substance with dispositional properties that biases outcomes, selects actions, sustains attention. The reification is the framework's first move and the article does it without pause. Tenet 1 dualism gives you license to posit consciousness as a non-physical *something*, but the article does not interrogate what that *something* is. It speaks of consciousness *deciding*, *biasing*, *holding*, *modulating* — verbs that presuppose an agent-subject performing the verbs. Who is the agent? The Map's response would presumably be *the conscious subject* — but then you have a homunculus problem: the conscious subject decides by biasing quantum outcomes in its own brain, but the conscious subject *is* (presumably) constituted by the brain-states it is biasing. The decision-to-bias is itself a brain-state, which is itself biased by the decision-to-bias, which is itself a brain-state. The regress kicks in immediately. The article does not engage it.

"More fundamentally: the framework's central question — 'what kind of causation does consciousness exercise' — is malformed because *consciousness is not a thing that causes*. Causation requires a stable cause-relatum and a stable effect-relatum. Consciousness is empty (śūnya) of the kind of inherent existence that would make it a cause-relatum. The conventional appearance of conscious decision is real (saṃvṛti-satya), but the inference from conventional appearance to *consciousness has causal powers* (paramārtha-satya) is the inference the Madhyamaka tradition spent fifteen centuries criticising. The article performs the inference in its opening sentence and never revisits.

"The article will respond that the Map's dualism is not a substance-dualism of the kind I am critiquing. But the article's prose throughout — *consciousness selects*, *consciousness holds*, *consciousness modulates* — is unrelievedly substance-talk. If the Map's dualism is *process-dualism* or *property-dualism*, the article needs to rewrite its central verbs to make that visible. As written, the reader cannot distinguish the Map's dualism from a Cartesian substance-dualism that the article elsewhere disclaims."

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Possibility/probability slippage in §"What Consciousness Does (According to the Map)" heading and four sub-sections

- **Location**: §"What Consciousness Does (According to the Map)" heading (line 95) and its four sub-sections (lines 99–125: *Selection of Action*, *Sustained Attention*, *Phenomenal Biasing*, *Volitional Modulation*)
- **Problem**: The heading asserts these are things "consciousness actually performs," but every sub-section's mechanism is at the *speculative integration* tier on the [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) scale. The article's own §"Absence of Direct Evidence" concession at line 181 acknowledges "we lack direct empirical evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes in brains." The four-mechanism exposition silently treats the framework's permission ("the Map permits this kind of causal role") as evidence-positive content ("consciousness does this kind of causal work"). This is the canonical possibility/probability slippage pattern [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) §"The Failure Mode" names.
- **Severity**: Critical
- **Recommendation**: Reframe the §"What Consciousness Does" heading to *"What the Map Hypothesises Consciousness Does"* or similar. Add a one-paragraph evidential-status preamble at the top of the section that flags the four mechanisms below as *speculative-integration-tier* mechanisms in the Map's framework: consistent with the framework's commitments, not established by direct empirical evidence, contested by rival readings. The honest natural-language phrasing per [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) §"The Calibration Layer" is something like: *the following four mechanisms describe how the Map's interactionist framework hypothesises that consciousness's causal role unfolds. The empirical evidence does not adjudicate between this framing and the rival accounts (predictive processing, identity theories) that predict the same phenomenology by different mechanisms. The case for the Map's reading is the framework's overall coherence and its handling of phenomenological data, not direct evidence of any of the four mechanisms below.*

### Issue 2: Pain-asymbolia argument commits the constrain-vs-establish failure

- **File**: `obsidian/topics/consciousness-and-causal-powers.md`
- **Location**: §"Phenomenal Biasing" lines 113–115, especially "*The inseparability of pain's felt character and its causal power is precisely what epiphenomenalism denies and what the Map affirms*" and "*This quality-action mapping tracks phenomenal character more transparently than underlying neural patterns, suggesting the quale is part of the causal process itself.*"
- **Problem**: Pain asymbolia (intact nociception, absent felt awfulness, collapsed avoidance behaviour) is a striking dissociation that *constrains* the simplest representational identity claim (the one that says felt awfulness just is the nociceptive activation), but it does *not establish* the Map's specific quantum-selection mechanism for phenomenal causation. Predictive-processing accounts in which affective valence enters as a separate hierarchical prior over the nociceptive signal — damage to parietal/insular cortex disrupts the valence-prior assignment while leaving sensory cortex intact, behavioural collapse follows because the valence-prior drives avoidance policy selection — predict the same dissociation. The Map's reading is *compatible* with pain asymbolia but is not *supported over the rival reading* by it. The article's prose ("*the quale is part of the causal process itself*") asserts the stronger conclusion the dissociation does not license. This is the canonical constrain-vs-establish failure mode [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) §"Constrain vs. Establish" names; the predictive-processing rival is precisely the rival the [direct-refutation-discipline](/project/direct-refutation-discipline/) would route to its compatibility-vs-support mode.
- **Severity**: Critical
- **Recommendation**: Rewrite the lines 113–115 passage to acknowledge that pain asymbolia constrains the simplest representational-identity reading without establishing the Map's quantum-selection reading; name the predictive-processing affective-prior rival as the strongest alternative explanation that predicts the same dissociation; locate the actual disagreement at the *bridge from sub-personal valence-prior assignment to felt awfulness* (which is the Map's principled disagreement with PP), not at the dissociation phenomenon (which is shared explanandum). The natural-language version per [the writing-style guide's "Compatibility vs. Support" pattern](/project/writing-style/) is something like: *pain asymbolia constrains the simplest claim that the felt awfulness just is the nociceptive activation; the same dissociation is predicted by predictive-processing accounts in which affective valence enters as a separate hierarchical prior, with damage to parietal/insular cortex disrupting valence-prior assignment while leaving sensory cortex intact. The dissociation is compatible with both framings; the Map's disagreement with PP locates at the bridge from sub-personal valence-prior assignment to felt awfulness, not at the phenomenon itself.*

### Issue 3: Overdetermination "dissolution" at line 197 is operationally a Pyrrhic move

- **File**: `obsidian/topics/consciousness-and-causal-powers.md`
- **Location**: §"Relation to Site Perspective", *Philosophical coherence* bullet, line 197
- **Problem**: The article claims to dissolve Kim's exclusion argument by locating consciousness's causal contribution at quantum indeterminacies where "physics is silent." Under the [causal-consistency-constraint](/concepts/causal-consistency-constraint/) the article invokes at line 87 to defend Born-rule preservation, consciousness's bias must be statistically undetectable. The article does not engage this tension; it appeals to the [overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism](/topics/overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism/) article to do the work without summarising the move enough for the reader to evaluate it.
- **Severity**: Critical
- **Recommendation**: Either (a) summarise the move from [overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism](/topics/overdetermination-dissolution-under-selection-only-interactionism/) in 100–200 words within this article so the reader can see how the dissolution works and how it relates to the Born-statistics-preservation constraint, or (b) honestly acknowledge that the dissolution claim depends on the reader following a chain of articles and that the *current* article's reader cannot evaluate it. The article should also engage the worry that Born-statistics preservation makes the bias operationally indistinguishable from no-bias — either by accepting the worry and characterising the framework's commitment as *operationally underdetermined, framework-coherence-supported* (which is the honest position), or by naming the move that escapes the worry (which would be a substantive claim the article currently does not make).

## Counterarguments to Address

### Decoherence-time gap for the Zeno mechanism

- **Current content says**: The article cites the Zeno effect three times (lines 63, 107–109, 125, plus further-reading) as the candidate mechanism for how sustained attention holds neural patterns in superposition. The only acknowledgement of decoherence is "Quantum effects in neural microtubules are controversial" at line 193.
- **A critic would argue**: Standard decoherence calculations for neural-relevant quantum superpositions in warm, wet brain tissue give coherence times of $10^{-13}$ to $10^{-20}$ seconds; the phenomenology of attention runs at 10–30 Hz; the Zeno mechanism the article invokes would need to perform observations at $\geq 10^{13}$ Hz to keep a neural superposition coherent. The article gives no acknowledgement of this 12–16 orders-of-magnitude gap. The Hagan/Hameroff 2002 numbers that purport to weaken the decoherence objection are themselves disputed.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge the decoherence gap explicitly in the §"Sustained Attention" sub-section. Note that the Zeno-effect proposal requires either (i) protected microenvironments that extend coherence times by many orders of magnitude (the Penrose-Hameroff conjecture about microtubule quantum coherence, which remains contested), or (ii) a different quantum mechanism that does not require neural-timescale coherence. The honest version is: *the Zeno mechanism is offered as a worked example of how quantum-level consciousness influence could scale; the empirical case for the underlying coherence-time requirement is contested and remains a critical vulnerability the framework has not resolved.*

### MWI dependence at the framework-rejection level

- **Current content says**: The article describes consciousness as biasing "which probability actualizes" (line 61), "which possibility collapses into actuality" (line 101), and "which pattern actualises" — selection-among-alternatives language throughout.
- **A critic would argue**: The selection-among-alternatives framing presupposes a determinate fact about which outcome actualises; under Everettian quantum mechanics all outcomes actualise in their respective branches. The article rejects MWI via Tenet 4 but does not defend the rejection on this page, even though the rejection is doing significant load-bearing work for the article's central framing.
- **Suggested response**: Add a parenthetical acknowledgement that the selection-among-alternatives framing presupposes a non-Everettian interpretation; refer the reader to no-many-worlds or [tenets](/tenets/#no-many-worlds) for the framework-level defence. Honest naming of the dependency satisfies the [direct-refutation discipline](/project/direct-refutation-discipline/)'s framework-boundary-marking requirement.

### Pain-asymbolia rival reading from predictive processing

- **Current content says**: Lines 113–115 present pain asymbolia as straightforward evidence that "the quale is part of the causal process itself."
- **A critic would argue**: Predictive-processing affective-prior accounts predict the same dissociation by a non-dualist mechanism (parietal/insular damage disrupts valence-prior assignment; behavioural collapse follows because valence-prior drives avoidance policy). The article does not engage this rival.
- **Suggested response**: See Issue 2 above for the rewrite recommendation.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite the criticisms, three things work well and should be preserved through any revision:

1. **§"What Consciousness Does Not Do" (lines 127–141)** is the article's strongest section. The five prohibitions are operational, falsifiable, and clearly demarcate the framework's commitments. The asymmetry between this section's clarity and §"What Consciousness Does" is what Issue 1 above asks the article to address — by bringing the affirmative section up to the prohibitive section's calibration honesty.

2. **§"Absence of Direct Evidence" (line 181)** is the article's calibration high point. The honest concession "we lack direct empirical evidence that consciousness biases quantum outcomes in brains. The hypothesis is *compatible* with current evidence but not *required* by it" is the load-bearing honesty the [evidential-status-discipline](/project/evidential-status-discipline/) asks for. Issue 1 is essentially the request that the rest of the article live up to this concession.

3. **Engagement with the identity-theory rival (lines 181–183)** is genuine philosophical work — the article gives the identity-theory move ("any 'selection' among quantum outcomes is itself a physical process, and invoking consciousness adds nothing beyond a redescription") space to be stated in its strongest form before responding. The response (the redescription is epiphenomenal in all but name; the Map's reading requires independent [dualism](/concepts/dualism/) to establish that mental causation cannot be reduced to physical causation) is well-calibrated and does not overclaim.

The article's structure — what consciousness could mean → scope and limits → what consciousness does → what consciousness does not do → philosophical significance → evidential question → relation to site perspective — is good. The criticism is not structural; it is at the calibration of specific claims within the structure.
