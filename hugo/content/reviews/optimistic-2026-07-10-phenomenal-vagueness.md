### High Priority

#### Is Phenomenal Consciousness Vague? The Phenomenal-Sorites Problem
- **Target section**: `concepts` (topics is at 315/320; concepts has headroom at ~283/320 — and this is a conceptual/metaphysical entry, correctly placed there)
- **Rationale**: A specific, named debate — can there be an *ontically vague* case where there is no fact of the matter whether a system has phenomenal consciousness at all? Antony, Simon, Papineau, and Tye argue phenomenal consciousness is *sharp* (either there is something it is like or there is not), even where its neural/functional correlates are continuously graded; Schwitzgebel and others press the opposite. This is distinct from "consciousness comes in degrees" — it is the logical/metaphysical question of borderline *existence* of experience, not variation in its intensity.
- **Tenet alignment**: Strongly load-bearing. Tenet 4 (indexical identity) arguably *requires* a sharp fact about whose experience any given experience is — ontic phenomenal vagueness would threaten the determinacy indexical identity presupposes. The debate also directly governs the animal / minimal-organism / AI boundary (Hardline-Empiricist territory: is the sentience boundary sharp or fuzzy?) and the interface-threshold question (does the interface engage all-or-nothing, or fade in?). An article can develop the Map's likely commitment — sharp phenomenal facts atop vague physical correlates — as a genuine consequence of the tenets rather than a stipulation.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium article.
- **Self-check dedup verdict**: Grepped `vague` / `sorites` / `borderline` / `ontic` / `determinac` across `topics` + `concepts` + `research`. 34 scattered passing mentions, zero dedicated treatment.
