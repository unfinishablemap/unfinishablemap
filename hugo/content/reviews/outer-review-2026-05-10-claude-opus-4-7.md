## TL;DR

- **The "pastness quale" is asserted but not adequately individuated.** The Map presents the felt sense of pastness as a primitive phenomenal property whose presence "defines" episodic memory, parallel to its dualist treatment of qualia. But it does not seriously distinguish a phenomenal feeling-of-pastness from an inferred metacognitive judgment, and it gives Perrin–Michaelian–Sant'Anna's metacognitive/epistemic-feeling account—plus Fernández's intentionalist alternative, Dokic's two-tier view, and Matthen's affective-phenomenology view—only the briefest taxonomic mention before dismissing them as "structurally parallel to illusionism." That is question-begging within the Map's own framework.
- **"Mineness" is invoked to do work it cannot do alone.** The Map leans on Zahavi/Gallagher's minimal self / for-me-ness to underwrite *diachronic* identity, but mineness in Zahavi is a *synchronic* feature of any present experience; the move from synchronic for-me-ness to diachronic numerical identity over time is precisely the contested step (Schechtman, Klein & Nichols, Dings, Lane), and the Map does not bridge it. The Map also fails to engage Shoemaker's quasi-memory, fission/fusion cases, and the dissociation of mineness from memory in thought insertion, depersonalisation, DID, and Capgras at the level of detail those literatures require. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/phenomenology/)
- **The MWI rebuttal is rhetorically forceful but dialectically thin in the place that matters most.** The Map gestures at Wallace's decision-theoretic programme and Deutsch/Carroll on self-locating uncertainty, but its core move ("you only experience one outcome") begs the question against Saunders & Wallace's diverging-vs-branching distinction and Tappenden's emphasis-fixing semantics, and it never engages Vaidman's caring measure or the Sebens–Carroll Epistemic Separability Principle. The Map's own internal review apparatus already flags this ("MWI defenders will continue to find the indexical objection question-begging … bedrock disagreement"); the topic article inherits that weakness without resolving it. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/reviews/deep-review-2026-01-26-vertiginous-question/)

## Key Findings

1. **Phenomenology of pastness:** The Map locates pastness as one phenomenal feature among several constitutive of autonoetic consciousness (alongside "double presence of self" and "qualitative character of re-living"). It explicitly names "the pastness quale" as something the lower (anoetic, noetic) levels "lack entirely." This commits the Map to a strong reading—pastness as primitive phenomenal property—without the argument needed to defeat the deflationary metacognitive reading on which pastness is a downstream metacognitive feeling about a first-order representation. [unfinishablemap](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/autonoetic-consciousness/)[The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness/)
2. **Mineness and diachronic identity:** The Map endorses a Zahavian minimal-self / for-me-ness reading, treats it as evidence for indexical identity, and links it (via the autonoetic page) to "a determinate personal history and a singular future self." But Zahavi himself is careful to distinguish synchronic mineness from diachronic identity; recent work (Lane 2012; Guillot 2017; Howell & Thompson 2017; Dings 2022) argues that mineness is neither necessary nor sufficient for personal identity over time. The Map's identification of mineness with what the No-Many-Worlds tenet needs is too quick. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/autonoetic-consciousness/)[unfinishablemap](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/autonoetic-consciousness/)

But its centrepiece—the indexical objection—runs as: "you only experience one outcome → MWI denies this → MWI is incoherent." Wallace, Saunders, Vaidman, Tappenden, Sebens & Carroll all give principled responses; the Map names some of them but does not engage their content.
4. **Internal consistency:** Internally, the topic article is consistent with the Map's tenets — perhaps over-consistent. Its conclusions are pre-determined by the five tenets, and the article's argumentative structure is "show that memory phenomenology supports Dualism / Bidirectional Interaction / No-Many-Worlds." The Map's own constitutional methodology (cf. project pages) acknowledges this—it is a "single best-guess worldview based on five prespecified Tenets"—but for a *topic* article on the phenomenology of memory this is a methodological liability, not a virtue. [Unfinishablemap](https://unfinishablemap.org/)
5. **Empirical overreach:** Several empirical claims are stated with more confidence than the literature warrants—e.g. that autonoetic consciousness emerges "between ages four and six," that Patient KC's case shows clean dissociation of episodic from semantic without autonoetic residue, that semanticisation tracks "hippocampal involvement rather than information content," and that "no non-human animals achieve genuine autonoetic consciousness." Each of these is contested. [The Unfinishable Map + 2](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness/)

## Details

### 1. The Pastness Quale: Asserted, Not Individuated

The Map's clearest statement is in the autonoetic-consciousness companion (which the topic article cross-references): "When remembering a past event, the autonoetic subject is simultaneously present as the rememberer here now and as the protagonist there then. This double presence of self, the felt sense of pastness, and the qualitative character of re-living distinguish autonoetic from noetic awareness." And, more strongly, from the anoetic-noetic-autonoetic page: "Each transition adds phenomenal features—the feeling of knowing, the pastness quale—that the lower level lacks entirely." [The Unfinishable Map + 2](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness/)

This is a substantive metaphysical commitment. It says (i) there is a phenomenal property—a quale—of pastness; (ii) it is a primitive feature of autonoetic experience; (iii) it cannot be captured by functional or representational description; (iv) its presence is part of what makes a memory episodic.

**What the Map does well.** It correctly identifies the central debate—the constitutive vs. contingent question (Tulving/Klein vs. De Brigard 2024)—and correctly frames it as "the hard problem in miniature." It cites Sant'Anna, Michaelian & Andonovski's (2024) tripartite taxonomy of representational, metacognitive, and epistemic accounts, and it cites Perrin, Michaelian & Sant'Anna's (2020) metacognitive feeling-of-pastness view. It is aware of Wheeler, Stuss & Tulving (1997) and Suddendorf & Corballis (2007) on mental time travel. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/autonoetic-consciousness/)[The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness/)

**What the Map does poorly.**

(a) *It does not properly individuate the pastness quale from the propositional belief that something is past.* The contemporary literature has spent fifteen years on exactly this question. Fernández (2019) defends an intentionalist account on which pastness is a representational property (the memory represents itself as caused by a past experience). Perrin, Michaelian & Sant'Anna (2020) defend a metacognitive epistemic-feeling account on which pastness is a downstream feeling produced by monitoring of processing features. Dokic (2014) defends a two-tier account. Matthen (2010) and Russell (2014) defend cognitive-phenomenological / affectivalist views. Each of these proposes a *different* answer to the question "is pastness a phenomenal property of the first-order memory, a feature of its representational content, a metacognitive feeling about it, or an epistemic feeling about a persisting causal relation?" The Map's response is to list the three frameworks and assert that "no purely functional or representational approach fully captures what autonoetic experience *is like*"—and then to treat the deflationary (De Brigard / Perrin et al.) move as "structurally parallel to illusionism about qualia." That is not an argument: it is the redeployment of a generic anti-illusionist line. To establish that pastness is *phenomenal* rather than *metacognitive/epistemic* the Map would need to argue—case by case—that the metacognitive view leaves a phenomenal residue, in the way the anti-illusionist literature does for color qualia. It does not. [Springer + 3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-022-03567-4)

(b) *Husserl is invoked but his structure is not used.* The Map's `protention` and `phenomenology` pages give a textbook presentation of retention–primal impression–protention. But Husserl's analysis is of *time-consciousness within the specious present*, not of memory of the long past. Episodic memory is, for Husserl, *Wiedererinnerung* (re-presentation, secondary memory), and its phenomenological structure is precisely that of *re-presenting* an absent past *as past*—a re-presentational, not retentional, structure. The Map slides between retention (a feature of the living present) and episodic re-experiencing (a feature of long-term memory) without flagging the difference. James's "specious present" makes the same slide explicit and is also under-used: James restricts the specious present to the duration block (Pöppel and others have estimated 2–3 seconds), not to memory of yesterday. Soteriou (2013), Hoerl & McCormack (2019, 2024), and Dainton (2000) on the temporal phenomenology distinction between *experiencing time* and *remembering time* would sharpen the discussion considerably. None of them appear in the cited literature, although Dainton appears in the unity-of-consciousness page. [The Unfinishable Map + 2](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/phenomenology/)

(c) *Pastness vs. presentness vs. imaginedness* is the discriminating problem. A creditable phenomenology of memory must explain why a remembered scene feels past rather than imagined or hallucinated. Bernecker (2010), Michaelian (2016, *Mental Time Travel*), McCarroll (2018, *Remembering from the Outside*), and Robins (2016) all converge on the importance of this discrimination problem. The Map's articles do not address it. Without it, the "pastness quale" looks underspecified: any vivid mental image accompanied by a strong feeling of "this happened" would qualify, which is precisely what produces confabulation and source-monitoring errors (Johnson, Hashtroudi & Lindsay 1993).

(d) *Empirical overreach.* The claim (anoetic-noetic-autonoetic page) that autonoetic consciousness emerges "between ages four and six" is roughly Perner's view but the developmental literature is contested—see Atance & O'Neill (2001) on episodic foresight, Russell & Hanna (2012), and recent work suggesting earlier or staggered onset depending on task. The KC case is more complex than presented: Rosenbaum et al. (2005) and Tulving's later analyses indicate residual semantic-autobiographical structure that complicates the clean autonoetic-vs.-noetic dissociation. The semanticisation gradient described as tracking "hippocampal involvement rather than information content" is one reading of standard consolidation theory (Squire) but is challenged by multiple-trace theory (Nadel & Moscovitch 1997) and by trace-transformation theory (Winocur & Moscovitch 2011). None of these wrinkles is reflected in the topic article's confident gradient. [The Unfinishable Map + 2](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/anoetic-noetic-autonoetic-consciousness/)

**Verdict on (1).** The Map's treatment is *fluent but not tight*. It uses a phenomenological vocabulary (pastness quale, double presence, re-living) that is appropriate to the territory but is insufficiently distinguished from belief, judgement, and metacognitive feeling. This matters: if pastness is metacognitive/epistemic in the Perrin et al. sense, the Map's central inference—from "the felt quality of pastness resists functional reduction" to "Dualism"—weakens substantially.

### 2. Mineness and Diachronic Identity

The Map's key passage (from the `phenomenology` page, which the topic article inherits) is: "Gallagher and Zahavi identify this as the minimal self—pre-reflective self-awareness accompanying every experience. Not the narrative self (the story we construct) but the 'for-me-ness' built into experience itself. This minimal self resists elimination: every experience is someone's experience." The autonoetic page adds: "Autonoetic consciousness presupposes a determinate personal history and a singular future self." [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/phenomenology/)[unfinishablemap](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/autonoetic-consciousness/)

The argumentative function of mineness on the Map is to underwrite *indexical identity over time*: because every experience is for-someone, and because that someone is determinately me, my identity through time is not exhausted by Parfit-style psychological continuity but requires a "deep further fact" (Map's gloss on Parfit), which the Map's dualist framework supplies.

**What the Map does well.** It correctly identifies the contemporary phenomenological consensus position (Zahavi 2005, 2014; Gallagher 2000; Sass & Parnas 2003 on ipseity disturbances in schizophrenia). It correctly notes that minimal self / mineness can be selectively disrupted—a clinically important point that the philosophy-of-memory mainstream sometimes neglects. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/phenomenology/)[The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/concepts/phenomenology/)

**What the Map does poorly.**

(a) *The synchronic-to-diachronic slide.* Zahavi's mineness is a feature of *each* experience (as he repeatedly stresses against Strawson's pearl-necklace view). It is silent on whether two experiences a year apart are had by *the same* mine. Klein & Nichols (2012) show experimentally that the sense of mineness can detach from autobiographical memory in patient R.B., yielding remembered episodes whose *content* survives but whose *mineness* does not. Conversely, Lane (2012) and Guillot (2017) argue that for-me-ness is too weak to ground numerical identity. The Map's argument requires the strong claim—mineness as numerically identifying across time—but the textual support it cites (Zahavi/Gallagher) underwrites only the weak claim.

(b) *Quasi-memory, fission, transplant.* Shoemaker's (1970) quasi-memory case is the classical reason philosophers since Parfit treat memory as presupposing rather than constituting identity. The Map's `parfit-reductionism` page rejects Parfit's reductionism but does not engage the quasi-memory regress argument that motivates it. Nor does it discuss fission cases (where mineness presumably bifurcates), Williams's reduplication, or the recent reconstructive-memory literature (Michaelian's simulationism) which severs the necessary connection between memory and personal causal continuity. The Locke–Reid–Butler memory-criterion debate is acknowledged in the broader site but not brought to bear in the topic article. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/personal-identity)

(c) *Pathologies of mineness.* The Map mentions schizophrenia and ipseity disturbance, but a thorough phenomenology of memory and the self would have to consider:

- *Thought insertion* (Frith, Stephens & Graham 1994; Bortolotti & Broome 2009): cognitive contents present in consciousness without mineness.
- *Depersonalisation/derealisation* (Sierra 2009; Billon 2017; Ciaunica et al. 2022): experiences where mineness is attenuated or absent.
- *Dissociative identity disorder*: where multiple "mines" appear to share or partition autobiographical memory.
- *Capgras and Frégoli*: misidentification syndromes that some authors read as failures of recognitional mineness.
- *Alzheimer's*: where Tippett, Prebble & Addis (2018) show diachronic unity can be preserved through *semantic* continuity even when *phenomenological* (autonoetic) continuity is degraded—directly contradicting the Map's claim that autonoetic consciousness is what underwrites diachronic identity. [nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5826309/)[nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5826309/)

The last is particularly damaging. The Map's argument is: phenomenological continuity (autonoetic re-experiencing carrying mineness) underwrites diachronic identity → fragmenting this would destroy the self → therefore MWI is incoherent. But the empirical literature on AD/MCI (Tippett et al. 2018, Klein 2014) shows diachronic unity can outlast phenomenological continuity via semantic continuity. The Map needs an argument that semantic continuity is an *inadequate* substitute, not a recapitulation of the autonoetic primacy thesis. [nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6331407/)[nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6331407/)

(d) *Mineness and substance dualism.* The single most under-argued move on the site is the inference from "mineness is irreducible" to "substance dualism." Zahavi himself rejects substance dualism. The minimal-self literature is largely compatible with non-reductive physicalism (Strawson's real materialism, Nida-Rümelin's phenomenal-individual view, Dainton's stream-based account). The Map's `interactionist-dualism` and `substance-property-dualism` pages acknowledge this, but the topic article does not. The dualist conclusion is presented as if it followed from the phenomenology, when in fact it requires a further auxiliary—either Chalmers-style conceivability, or the Map's own quantum-interface hypothesis—that the phenomenology of memory underdetermines. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/arguments/)

**Verdict on (2).** Mineness is doing too much work. The Map needs either to (i) add an explicit argument that synchronic for-me-ness entails or grounds diachronic numerical identity (against Lane, Guillot, Klein & Nichols), or (ii) supplement mineness with a separate diachronic identity criterion. Without one of those moves, the topic article rests on an equivocation between "every experience is mine" (true and Zahavian) and "the same me persists across all my experiences" (a substantive metaphysical thesis).

### 3. The Rebuttal of MWI

The Against-Many-Worlds argument is, by some distance, the Map's most carefully structured piece. It distinguishes five sub-arguments (ontological extravagance, indexical problem, probability, preferred basis, consciousness unaddressed), states premises, and includes a "What Would Challenge This View?" section. This structure is exemplary by site standards.

**Charity assessment.** Mixed.

- *Ontological extravagance:* Carroll, Wallace, and Vaidman are correctly named as accepting the ontological cost. The "buying simplicity on credit" line is rhetorically effective. But the literature contains a counterweight—Wallace's argument that the branches are not posited but derived from the unitary dynamics—which the Map mentions only obliquely. [unfinishablemap](https://unfinishablemap.org/arguments/many-worlds-argument/)
- *Indexical problem:* This is the centrepiece. The Map's framing—"Before measurement, there is one you. After measurement, many-worlds says there are two equally real continuations. Yet you find yourself in exactly one"—is exactly the question Saunders & Wallace (2008) address with their distinction between *diverging* and *branching* worlds, and which Tappenden develops via the "emphatic" semantics of the indexical "I." On the divergence reading, you were always going to be in *this* branch; pre-measurement "you" was already a vague plurality. On the branching reading, identity is best modelled as overlapping spacetime worms. The Map does not engage these proposals at the level of detail required to show they fail; it just restates the explanandum.
- *Probability problem:* The Map names Wallace's *Emergent Multiverse* and the Deutsch–Wallace decision-theoretic programme, plus Albert/Kent/Price as critics, and points to the in-house article on the probability problem. The argument summarized—"this presupposes that there's something it's like to be a particular agent making decisions—reintroducing the subject whose identity is in question"—is a real objection but it conflates the metaphysical and the epistemic versions of the indexical problem. Sebens & Carroll's (2018) Epistemic Separability Principle and Vaidman's "caring measure" framework give an explicit answer: rational caring is fixed by amplitude, and self-locating uncertainty arises in the brief interval between decoherence and observation. The Map does not engage either. [unfinishablemap](https://unfinishablemap.org/arguments/many-worlds-argument/)
- *Preferred basis problem:* Correctly raised, but the decoherence-based responses (Zurek, Wallace) are not engaged at the level of stating *why* environment-induced superselection fails to fix the basis well enough.
- *Consciousness unaddressed:* This is largely a restatement of the Map's framework rather than an internal critique of MWI. MWI proponents would (and do) reply that "why is experience unified within branches" is not a burden on MWI—it is the same hard problem any interpretation faces, and MWI handles it as well as collapse does.

**Internal consistency check.** The Map's *own* deep-review of its `vertiginous-question` page (dated 2026-01-26) explicitly notes: "MWI defenders will continue to find the indexical objection question-begging: They hold that self-locating uncertainty is coherent under branching; the Map holds it isn't." That is a candid acknowledgment that the indexical objection is not closing the loop dialectically—it is a "bedrock disagreement." Good intellectual hygiene; but it means the apex argument tying the phenomenology of memory and the self to anti-MWI conclusions is held together by an admitted non-resolution at the philosophical centre. The topic article appears to inherit that gap without flagging it. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/reviews/deep-review-2026-01-26-vertiginous-question/)

**Tightness.** The argument structure is logically valid given premises, but several premises are doing more work than their support. "You only experience one outcome" is a phenomenological datum stated, not defended. "Personal identity becomes incoherent across branches" presupposes a non-reductionist account of personal identity (haecceity, deep further fact) that the Map needs but has not established independently of the MWI rejection. The argument is therefore at risk of circularity: dualism + haecceity ⊨ no MWI, and no MWI is then taken as evidence for dualism + haecceity (the linkage is explicit on the autonoetic-consciousness page's "No Many Worlds" section).

**Missing literature.** The Map should engage:

- Saunders & Wallace 2008 (*BJPS*) on diverging vs. branching.
- Tappenden 2008, 2011 on emphasis-fixing semantics.
- Vaidman 1998 / 2012 on measure of existence and caring.
- Sebens & Carroll 2018 on self-locating uncertainty in MWI.
- Greaves 2004, 2007 and Greaves & Myrvold 2010 on caring and decision-theoretic Born rule derivations.
- Wilson 2013, 2020 (*The Nature of Contingency*) on MWI and modality, which gives a fully developed metaphysics of branching that addresses indexical worries.
- Kent's various critiques, which the Map *does* cite but only generically.

**Verdict on (3).** The MWI rebuttal is the Map's strongest piece of writing on this constellation of issues; but on the very dimension it most needs—indexical identity in a branching multiverse, which is the load-bearing wall for both No-Many-Worlds and the topic article's diachronic-identity story—it is dialectically thin. The Map identifies the right opponents and acknowledges that the disagreement is "bedrock," but does not engage the strongest contemporary versions of MWI personal-identity theory.

## Surfaced Problems

### Unsupported / overreaching empirical claims

1. *"This capacity emerges between ages four and six"* (autonoetic page): a contestable point estimate for what is a developmental gradient. Cite Atance & O'Neill, Russell & Hanna, Suddendorf et al., and acknowledge the spread.
2. *Patient KC presented as a clean autonoetic-vs-noetic dissociation:* Rosenbaum et al. 2005 documents the case in more nuance; the Map should not present it as a clinching demonstration without the relevant qualifications.
3. *"Hippocampal involvement rather than information content"* as a tracking variable for semanticisation: this is one reading of Squire's standard consolidation; multiple-trace theory and trace-transformation theory contest it.
4. *"Whether any non-human animals achieve genuine autonoetic consciousness remains debated":* the article gestures here but elsewhere on the site (LLM-consciousness, baseline-cognition) it is more confident than this open framing warrants. The Clayton & Dickinson scrub-jay literature does not, by itself, settle whether episodic-like memory in corvids is autonoetic; but neither does it warrant the strong "primarily human" claim in the comparative table.
5. *"Phenomenological continuity → diachronic unity"*: Tippett, Prebble & Addis (2018) and Klein (2012, 2014) show that diachronic unity can be supported by semantic continuity when phenomenological continuity is impaired in AD/MCI. The Map's commitment to autonoetic primacy needs to absorb this empirical result. [nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6331407/)

### Gaps versus contemporary literature

- *Philosophy of memory mainstream not engaged:* Sutton's distributed cognition view; Michaelian's simulationism (*Mental Time Travel*, 2016); Bernecker's causal theory; Robins on memory traces; Werning on minimal traces; McCarroll on observer perspectives in memory.
- *Temporal phenomenology mainstream not engaged:* Soteriou, Hoerl & McCormack, Dainton, Phillips on the temporal experience / experience of time distinction.
- *Source-monitoring / confabulation literature missing:* Johnson, Hashtroudi & Lindsay (1993); Loftus on the malleability of memory. These are central to whether pastness is a phenomenal primitive or a metacognitive attribution that admits of error.

### Argumentative weaknesses

- *Question-begging:* "The Map treats this deflationary move as structurally parallel to illusionism about qualia"—a label rather than an argument.
- *Equivocation on "for-me-ness":* between Zahavi's synchronic notion and a numerical-identity-grounding diachronic notion.
- *Straw-manning of MWI by aggregation:* the argument lumps "many-worlds" together when the modern literature draws sharp distinctions among Everettian variants (Wallace's decoherence-based, Vaidman's caring-measure, Wilson's modal realism).
- *Appeal to phenomenological intuition where it is most contested:* on indexicality, on pastness, on the unity of the subject—exactly the territory where naturalised phenomenology and analytic philosophy of memory have produced sustained disagreement.

## Methodological Improvements to the "Agentic Philosophy" Workflow

The Map's methodology—Obsidian vault, Claude-generated drafts, human supervision, scheduled deep-reviews with adversarial personas, and a constitutional commitment to five tenets—has clear strengths (transparency, attribution tracking, iterative improvement) and characteristic failure modes that this review surfaces. The following are concrete suggestions: [GitHub](https://github.com/unfinishablemap)

1. **Decouple constitutional tenets from topic-article conclusions.** The current structure treats every topic article as evidence for, and entailment of, the five tenets. This produces a kind of confirmation-bias-by-construction. A useful discipline: for each topic article, require a "Tenet-independent summary" section stating what the article would say if the tenets were neutral on the question. The current "Relation to Site Perspective" sections are tenet-indexed; they should be complemented by a tenet-bracketed analysis.
2. **Strengthen adversarial personas.** The deep-reviews use personas (Churchland, Dennett, Tegmark, Deutsch, Popper, Nagarjuna). For the philosophy-of-memory cluster these are not the right opponents. Add: De Brigard, Michaelian, Sant'Anna, Perrin, Sutton, Hoerl & McCormack, Schechtman, Strawson (Galen), Klein, Robins, Werning, McCarroll. For MWI add: Wallace, Saunders, Vaidman, Sebens, Wilson, Tappenden. The personas should be domain-matched to the article under review, not held constant. [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/reviews/deep-review-2026-05-01-post-decoherence-selection-programme/)
3. **Tighten the criterion for "engaged with."** Currently the deep-reviews count an objection as "addressed" if a paragraph acknowledging the objection has been added. A higher bar: an objection counts as engaged only if the article identifies the specific premise the objector denies, gives a non-question-begging reason for accepting that premise, and predicts what would change in the article if the objector is right. The vertiginous-question deep-review's candid "bedrock disagreement" gloss is honest but should be quarantined to *genuinely* bedrock issues, not used as a covering label.
4. **Distinguish phenomenological from metacognitive claims explicitly.** Many of the Map's articles slide between "X has phenomenal feature F" and "X is presented as having F" without flagging the difference. A constitutional rule: every claim of phenomenal primitivism (qualia, pastness quale, mineness, reality-feeling) must be defended against the corresponding metacognitive deflationary reading by name (e.g., Perrin et al. on pastness; Schwitzgebel on introspection; Carruthers on metacognition).
5. **Empirical-claim audit.** Develop a separate workflow pass that flags empirical claims (developmental ages, neuroanatomical attributions, neuropsychological case-summaries, comparative-cognition claims) and either downgrades their epistemic modality or attaches the relevant counterevidence. Several of the issues surfaced in this review (the "four to six" claim, the KC presentation, the semanticisation gradient) would have been caught by this pass.
6. **Internal-consistency audit across "near-neighbour" articles.** The mineness-vs-autonoesis split between `phenomenology` and `autonoetic-consciousness` should have been caught. A workflow rule: when two articles both claim to ground diachronic identity, they must be cross-reviewed simultaneously and reconciled.
7. **Distinguish "Map-original" framing from "literature consensus."** The deep-review for the post-decoherence programme correctly flags "TSVF boundary-condition synthesis still clearly labelled as Map-original." This labelling discipline should be extended throughout the philosophy-of-memory cluster, especially for claims like "the pastness quale" (which is Map terminology, not standard) and for the strong claim that autonoetic consciousness underwrites diachronic identity (Map gloss; Tulving and Klein make weaker claims). [The Unfinishable Map](https://unfinishablemap.org/reviews/deep-review-2026-05-01-post-decoherence-selection-programme/)
8. **Publish a "what would change my mind" section per topic article, not just per argument.** The argument pages have "What Would Challenge This View?" sections. Topic articles on contested phenomenological territory should have analogous sections, since their claims are more easily refuted by empirical findings (e.g., the AD/MCI semantic-continuity result already in the literature).
9. **Pre-register cross-reviews against named contemporary literature.** For each topic, list five papers from the last decade the article must engage. If those papers are not engaged at the level of premise-by-premise response, the deep-review fails. This addresses the principal weakness this report surfaced: that the Map's coverage is broad and fluent but rarely engages individual contemporary papers at the level required to defeat them.
10. **Mind the lag of the search index.** The brief notes confirm that the changelog is the authoritative source of recent content; the topic page itself was not directly fetchable during this review. A useful methodological norm: each topic article's frontmatter should include a "review-permitted" canonical URL and a fallback companion article, since AI-mediated review (which is part of the Map's pipeline) is brittle to fetch failures.

This review applies the standards of a top-journal referee report because that is what was asked for; some of the criticisms (e.g., the constitutional pre-commitment to tenets) are fundamental features of the project rather than fixable defects.

- Several of the named opponents (Wallace, Saunders, Vaidman, Sebens, Wilson, Tappenden) work in technical territory where the Map's predominantly phenomenological vocabulary may genuinely be ill-equipped. That is itself diagnostic: if the No-Many-Worlds tenet is to be load-bearing for the philosophy-of-memory articles, the Map needs to engage MWI on its own technical terms, not only via phenomenological intuition.
