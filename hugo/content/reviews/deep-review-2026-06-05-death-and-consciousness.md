## Pessimistic Analysis Summary

### Critical Issues Found (corrected)

1. **AWARE II number/stance cross-attribution (CRITICAL — calibration, fixed).** The article stated "AWARE II reporting 39% of survivors had recalled experiences of death." Verification against Parnia et al. 2023 (*Resuscitation* 191, 109903) and the NYU Langone / Elsevier releases: ~40% of interviewed survivors reported a *perception of consciousness*; only ~20% described features consistent with a *recalled experience of death (RED)*; the 39.3% figure is 11/28 reporting "memories/perceptions suggestive of consciousness" — the broad-consciousness bucket, NOT the RED bucket. The article had pinned the wrong (higher) number to the stronger ("recalled experience of death") claim — exactly the cross-attributed-number defect this zone is prone to. Rewritten to "around 40% recalled some perception of consciousness and roughly 20% described features consistent with a 'recalled experience of death.'"

2. **Xu/Borjigin 2023 neural-signal vs. phenomenology conflation (CRITICAL — calibration, fixed).** Verified Xu et al. 2023 (*PNAS* 120(19), pnas.2216268120): four **comatose patients during withdrawal of ventilatory support**, two showed gamma-connectivity surges in the posterior "hot zone." These patients **did not survive and reported no experience** — the surge is a neural correlate, not confirmed conscious experience. The article's original "gamma wave surges in dying brains—heightened neural organisation … why failing neural function sometimes produces *heightened* consciousness" risked reading the EEG signal as heightened experience. Reworded to specify comatose ventilator-withdrawal patients, posterior-hot-zone coupling, and explicitly: "these patients did not survive to report any experience, so the surge is a neural signal rather than confirmed phenomenology." This is the honest calibration the task flagged (gamma-surge-in-comatose ≠ experience). NOT cardiac-arrest-with-experience — confirmed the cohort.

### Medium Issues Found (corrected)

Article said "compared over 1,000 cases." Corrected to "survey responses from over 1,000 doctors and nurses … reporting deathbed observations." The cross-cultural deceased-person-predominance claim itself stands.
