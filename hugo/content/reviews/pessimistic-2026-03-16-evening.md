# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-03-16
**Content reviewed**: obsidian/topics/consciousness-and-counterfactual-reasoning.md

## Executive Summary

This article makes an ambitious case that counterfactual reasoning—imagining what isn't—provides distinctive evidence for dualism and bidirectional interaction. While philosophically engaging, the argument suffers from a central equivocation between the *phenomenology* of counterfactual reasoning and the *capacity* for it, several unsupported empirical claims about great apes, and a recurring pattern of presenting physicalist explanations only to dismiss them with "but why is it conscious?"—which is just the hard problem restated, not a new argument from counterfactuals specifically.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would target the article's assumption that counterfactual reasoning has a distinctive, irreducible phenomenology. The article claims there is "something it is like to imagine a world where you chose differently—a felt quality of exploring an alternative that is neither perception nor memory nor hallucination." But this carves phenomenology too finely. The feeling of imagining might simply be the feeling of reactivating perceptual circuits in novel configurations—which the article itself acknowledges neuroscience confirms. Calling the result "sui generis" is folk-psychological inflation. Every cognitive process feels somewhat different from every other; that doesn't make each one a separate argument for dualism. By this logic, the phenomenology of mental arithmetic, of trying to remember a name, and of earworms would each constitute independent evidence for non-physical minds.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would point out that the article's central argument is circular. It amounts to: "Counterfactual reasoning is conscious. Consciousness can't be physical. Therefore counterfactual reasoning shows something non-physical." The counterfactual reasoning isn't doing independent argumentative work—it's just a new venue for restating the hard problem. The article even concedes this: "The pattern is familiar from the hard problem." If the pattern is familiar, what does the counterfactual case *add*? The article promises a "distinctive argument for dualism: not from qualia or the explanatory gap, but from the mind's ability to grasp what doesn't exist." But the argument *is* from the explanatory gap—just applied to a particular cognitive capacity rather than to raw feels.

Dennett would also challenge the claim that "physical systems track what is actual." Software systems routinely represent counterfactual states—game engines simulate physics that doesn't obtain, weather models explore scenarios that don't materialise, planning algorithms evaluate unchosen paths. The article's metaphysical claim that only consciousness "reaches beyond actuality" is simply false as a description of what computational systems do.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Tegmark would find the article's invocation of quantum indeterminacy entirely gratuitous. The claim that "consciousness evaluates quantum possibilities through counterfactual simulation before selecting which becomes actual" has no mathematical grounding whatsoever. The timescales are wrong: quantum decoherence in warm neural tissue occurs in femtoseconds, while deliberation occurs over seconds. The article concedes this is "speculative" but still places it in the structurally prominent position of connecting counterfactual reasoning to the Minimal Quantum Interaction tenet. Calling something speculative doesn't license including it as though it were an argument.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

Deutsch would challenge the article's claim that under many-worlds, "counterfactual reasoning would be reasoning about real (though inaccessible) branches of reality." This mischaracterises MWI. Under many-worlds, counterfactual reasoning is reasoning about *other branches that are not inaccessible in principle*—they are part of the same quantum state. The article frames this as problematic, but a many-worlds advocate would say it *explains* why counterfactual reasoning works: we evolved to track the structure of a branching reality. The article's claim that counterfactuals represent "genuine non-being" under the No Many Worlds tenet is actually *harder* to explain than the many-worlds alternative—how can we successfully reason about things that don't exist at all?

### The Empiricist

A Popperian would ask: what experimental finding could falsify the claim that counterfactual reasoning requires non-physical consciousness? The article's argument structure is: if physicalism explains it → "but why is it conscious?"; if physicalism can't explain it → "see, you need dualism." This is unfalsifiable. The article identifies no empirical prediction that distinguishes its dualist account from a physicalist one. If neuroscience eventually provides a complete computational account of counterfactual reasoning—including its phenomenology—would the article's author accept that dualism is unnecessary here? The argument's structure suggests not.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nagarjuna would question the article's assumption that counterfactual reasoning requires a unified, persisting subject. The article treats "holding two incompatible representations simultaneously" as requiring a single conscious self to do the holding. But this assumes what needs to be shown. Buddhist philosophy offers accounts of complex cognition without a substantial self—momentary mental events in causal sequence can produce the *appearance* of a unified deliberator without requiring an ontologically primitive subject. The article briefly acknowledges Buddhist philosophy in the related panpsychism article but doesn't engage with this challenge directly. The "someone" doing the counterfactual reasoning may be a convenient fiction rather than a metaphysical necessity.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: The Great Ape Empirical Claims Are Overstated

- **File**: consciousness-and-counterfactual-reasoning.md
- **Location**: "The Dual Representation Problem" and "Counterfactual Reasoning as Evidence for Bidirectional Interaction"
- **Problem**: The article claims great apes show "limited counterfactual sophistication" and that they "respond to present stimuli and learned associations rather than constructing and comparing alternative futures." This significantly understates the primate cognition literature. Great apes pass false-belief tests (Krupenye et al., 2016), engage in deceptive behaviour suggesting they model others' mental states, and show anticipatory planning (Mulcahy & Call, 2006). These capacities involve representing non-actual states. The clean division between human counterfactual reasoning and ape stimulus-response is empirically contested.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Qualify the ape claims significantly. Acknowledge that some forms of non-actual representation exist in great apes, and focus the argument on the *degree* and *phenomenal richness* of human counterfactual reasoning rather than claiming a categorical absence in apes.

### Issue 2: Central Argument Reduces to the Hard Problem

- **File**: consciousness-and-counterfactual-reasoning.md
- **Location**: "Why Physicalism Struggles"
- **Problem**: Each physicalist account is dismissed with a variant of "but why is it conscious?" (simulation account: "why does that tagging produce a felt distinction?"; computational account: "says nothing about why the process is conscious"; predictive processing: "doesn't explain why prediction feels like something"). This is the hard problem applied to counterfactual reasoning—not a new argument *from* counterfactual reasoning. The article's opening promise of a "distinctive challenge for physicalist accounts" is not delivered.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either identify what is specifically harder about explaining counterfactual phenomenology (as distinct from the general hard problem), or honestly frame the article as exploring how the hard problem manifests in this domain rather than claiming a new argument.

### Issue 3: "Physical Systems Track What Is Actual" Is False

- **File**: consciousness-and-counterfactual-reasoning.md
- **Location**: "The Ontological Status of Counterfactuals" section
- **Problem**: The claim "Physical systems track what is actual. Conscious minds alone seem to reach beyond actuality into the space of unrealised possibility" is straightforwardly false. Computational systems routinely represent counterfactual states: simulation engines, planning algorithms, model-based reinforcement learning systems, theorem provers exploring hypothetical premises. The article needs to explain why these don't count, and doing so will likely require appealing to phenomenology—which again collapses the argument back into the hard problem.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Distinguish between *representing* counterfactual states (which computers do) and *phenomenally experiencing* counterfactual states (which the article claims is distinctive). Make this distinction explicit and argue for why the phenomenal dimension matters, rather than claiming physical systems cannot represent non-actuality at all.

### Issue 4: The "7±2" Working Memory Claim Is Outdated

- **File**: consciousness-and-counterfactual-reasoning.md
- **Location**: "The Dual Representation Problem"
- **Problem**: The article states "Humans maintain roughly 7±2 items" in working memory. Miller's (1956) "magical number seven" has been substantially revised. The current consensus, following Cowan (2001), is approximately 4±1 items—a figure the responsibility gradient article correctly uses. Using the outdated figure undermines the article's credibility on empirical matters.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Update to "approximately 4±1 items" and cite Cowan (2001).

## Counterarguments to Address

### Computers Represent Counterfactuals

- **Current content says**: "Physical systems track what is actual" and "the capacity to represent non-actuality suggests a mental dimension that exceeds the physical"
- **A critic would argue**: Software systems routinely represent and reason about counterfactual states. AI planning systems evaluate unchosen action sequences. Game engines simulate physics that doesn't obtain. Modal logic provers manipulate possible worlds formally. None of these require consciousness.
- **Suggested response**: Distinguish computational counterfactual representation (manipulating data structures tagged as "hypothetical") from phenomenal counterfactual imagination (the felt experience of inhabiting an alternative scenario). Argue that the phenomenal dimension is what's distinctive—but acknowledge this makes the argument a variant of the hard problem applied to a specific cognitive domain.

### Evolutionary Explanation Suffices

- **Current content says**: The article acknowledges evolutionary utility but claims it doesn't explain the "phenomenal dimension"
- **A critic would argue**: This is an argument from incredulity. Evolution doesn't need to explain why processes are conscious—it only needs to explain why they're functional. If the functionality is explained, the "why conscious?" question is the general hard problem, not specific to counterfactuals.
- **Suggested response**: Strengthen the argument that counterfactual phenomenology is *particularly* puzzling from an evolutionary standpoint—e.g., that the phenomenal richness far exceeds functional requirements, or that the felt distinction between imagined and actual serves a purpose that simpler mechanisms couldn't achieve.

### Great Apes Do Engage in Counterfactual-Adjacent Cognition

- **Current content says**: Great apes show "limited counterfactual sophistication" and "respond to present stimuli and learned associations"
- **A critic would argue**: Great apes pass anticipatory looking false-belief tests, cache food against future theft (suggesting they model others' future actions), and demonstrate deceptive behaviour requiring representation of another's (non-actual) mental state. These are counterfactual-adjacent even if not fully developed.
- **Suggested response**: Reframe the comparison as one of degree rather than kind. Focus on the phenomenal richness and recursive depth of human counterfactual reasoning (counterfactuals about counterfactuals) rather than claiming a categorical absence in apes.
