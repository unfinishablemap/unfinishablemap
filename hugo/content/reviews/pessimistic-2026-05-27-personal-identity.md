## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The article treats "the haecceity intuition persists through reflection" (line 168, point 3) as evidence that it "tracks something real." This is exactly the move eliminativism predicts folk psychology will make: a robust, reflection-stable intuition is precisely what a deeply-wired but non-referring representation would feel like. Persistence-through-reflection is equally consistent with the intuition being an evolved fixed point with no metaphysical referent. The article's own point (2) — that the haecceity intuition "discriminates between teletransportation and ordinary death" — would, to Churchland, just be a description of how the death-avoidance circuit fires, not evidence of a tracked particular.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The recurring "seeming requires a subject" regress (lines 98, 164, 166) is the article's load-bearing anti-illusionist weapon, deployed three times. Dennett's reply is that "the subject for whom it seems" can itself be a represented content (the center of narrative gravity), not a metaphysically primitive experiencer. The article asserts the regress terminates in a real particular but never engages the deflationary reading where the apparent terminus is itself part of the illusion. Deploying the same regress three times without confronting its standard rebuttal inflates a single contested intuition into apparent triple-support.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
The Decoherence Challenge section (lines 170-174) lists three mitigations but does no calculation. "Decoherence calculations may be off by orders of magnitude" is asserted without the femtosecond-vs-required-timescale arithmetic Tegmark demands. The article partly defuses this by saying "even if the quantum mechanism proves untenable, the conceptual point stands" — which is honest, but invites the question of why the quantum machinery is invoked at all if the identity argument survives its removal. The retreat-to-conceptual-point makes the quantum framing look ornamental.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
The article's central anti-MWI claim — "you find yourself in exactly one branch ... seems factual, but many-worlds says it is not" (line 76) — assumes the very indexical primitive Deutsch denies. He would say the question "which you?" is malformed in the same way "which of the two post-fission people is really me?" is malformed, and the article actually grants this for fission (line 132-134) while refusing it for branching. That asymmetry is unargued: the article uses Parfit's "identity can't branch" logic to dissolve fission but rejects the structurally identical dissolution for quantum branching. This is the sharpest internal tension in the piece.

### The Empiricist (Popper's ghost)
The five challenge conditions (line 202) are commendable, but four of the five are about rival *theories* succeeding (an illusionist explanation, a Born-rule derivation, a neural account of unity) rather than direct observations against the view. Only condition (3) — teleportation experiments where replicas report no discontinuity — is a genuine empirical test, and the article elsewhere concedes the replica would of course report continuity (it has the memories), so condition (3) may be unfalsifiable in practice too. The falsification section is more rhetorically reassuring than operationally decisive.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The article handles anatta more carefully than most Map articles — it explicitly distinguishes Vedantic witness-persistence from Buddhist no-self (lines 146-150) and flags "process haecceitism" as the Map's own construction, not a Buddhist position (line 102). Good. The residual problem: process haecceitism wants particularity of a *stream* without a substrate, but Nagarjuna would press that a "stream" individuated as *this* rather than *that* smuggles back the very svabhava (inherent existence) the Map claims to avoid. Calling it a process rather than a substance relocates the reification without dissolving it.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Fission/branching asymmetry is unargued
- **File**: personal-identity.md
- **Location**: lines 76 (branching) vs 132-134 (fission)
- **Problem**: The article dissolves the fission question ("neither resulting person is you") using the logical point that identity can't branch, but refuses the structurally identical dissolution for quantum branching ("which branch am I in?" is treated as a meaningful, answer-having question). The two cases share the same form — one pre-split subject, multiple post-split continuers — yet receive opposite treatments. A Deutsch-style critic gets free leverage here.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add one paragraph distinguishing the cases on principled grounds (e.g., fission is a physical division of substrate while branching is a putative division of the indexical perspective itself; or concede the symmetry and argue the dissolution is unacceptable in *both* cases for the same reason). Either resolves it; the current silence is the problem.

Repeating an unrebutted argument three times reads as triple-counting a single contested move.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The causal-history individuation argument (line 92, 116, 214)
- **Current content says**: A replica's consciousness "would begin its causal engagement from creation, not from my birth. Causal history individuates."
- **A critic would argue**: Causal history individuates *tokens* trivially (any two distinct things have distinct histories) — but that is true of two qualitatively identical billiard balls too, and we do not think they have haecceity. The argument as stated proves too little: it establishes numerical distinctness, not the metaphysically loaded "thisness" the Map wants. Distinct causal history is necessary but the article treats it as if sufficient.
- **Suggested response**: Clarify that causal-history is offered as what *gives content* to an already-posited indexical particular, not as what *generates* haecceity from nothing — otherwise it collapses into ordinary numerical non-identity that even Parfit accepts.

### The conceivability-to-haecceity bridge (line 90)
- **Current content says**: zombie conceivability shows consciousness outruns qualitative description, and "a further step" extends this to non-qualitative individuation.
- **A critic would argue**: The "further step" is doing enormous work and is stated as a likelihood ("is also unlikely to be qualitatively determined"), not an entailment. Conceivability arguments are themselves contested (the phenomenal-concept strategy, which the Map has an article on, is the standard physicalist block). Resting haecceity on conceivability inherits all of conceivability's vulnerabilities.
- **Suggested response**: Cross-link `[[phenomenal-concepts-strategy]]` at line 90 and acknowledge the bridge is an inference-to-best-explanation, not a proof — the article does this for haecceity generally but not at this specific step.
