## Executive Summary

The article is genuinely well-calibrated at the framework-boundary level after the day's six refines — neuromatrix and predictive processing are engaged as the strongest contemporary materialist competitors, both ceded essentially all empirical commitments, with the disagreement properly located at the bridge-to-felt-content step. The common-cause-null audit of the three pillars (lines 100–108) is excellent: it does the structural work the outer reviewers asked for in the article body rather than only in editor-internal review. **However**, two genuinely critical findings survive: **(1) the Guémann 2023 RCT-only systematic-review citation flagged P1 by two converging outer-reviewers (2026-05-16) is not in the article body** — the mirror-therapy section still cites only Chan 2007 (n=18) and Foell 2014, with no acknowledgement of the strongest recent null systematic review and no update to falsifier #2 (which the same outer-reviewers noted is arguably already partially met by Guémann 2023); the todo task is marked completed but the work did not land. **(2) The compatibility-vs-support discipline is breached in the §"Relation to Site Perspective" Bidirectional Interaction subsection**: after the article has worked carefully throughout to flag mirror therapy as "compatible with materialism", the closing clause "is the kind of pathway interactionism predicts" is doing predictive-success work the discipline elsewhere refuses to license. ## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would say: "You've cleaned up the worst of the strawmen — fine. You now concede that neuromatrix theory and predictive processing predict everything you observe and that 'the felt body is constructed by the brain' is the convergent view of modern pain science rather than something Map-distinctive. Good. But then what *work* is the article doing? You retreat to: 'the bridge from production conditions to felt content is promissory.' This is the same promissory note materialists have been writing since 1850. Why does it bite *now*? You answer: because predictive processing has not yet specified why hierarchical Bayesian inference is accompanied by felt burning, itching, or impossible-posture cramp. But this is just the standard hard problem dressed in phantom clothing — *phantoms add nothing* once you concede the central-construction story is the explanandum.

"Your common-cause-null audit confesses this: you collapse the three pillars to one observation, and then claim the 'residual specification-to-felt-content gap sits at the framework boundary.' That residual is identical to the residual in vision, hearing, touch, and ordinary embodied experience. Why have a *phantom* article at all then? You should fold this into a generic hard-problem entry and stop pretending phantoms specifically embarrass anyone. The article's load-bearing structural work shrinks to: phantoms make a vivid pedagogical example. Fine — but stop calling that 'philosophically loaded.'"

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Daniel Dennett would say: "You've improved enormously by ceding the empirical ground to neuromatrix and PP. But now you have a different problem. The predictive-processing section's fifth paragraph (line 98) borrows phenomenal-aboutness language while claiming the framework borrows it without paying for it — but you do not specify what the framework would need to pay for it to count as paid. This is rhetorical asymmetry: when PP cashes out aboutness as hierarchical inference over body-configuration priors, you say it is 'derived' and 'metaphysically derivative.' When the Map cashes out aboutness as somatic-interface contact with consciousness, you say nothing at all about what *that* relation consists in. The Map owes the same accounting it demands. Your own interface story owes a bridge from interface-architecture to felt content, and that bridge is no less promissory than the one you are charging predictive processing for not yet paying.

"Second, the line 'the felt impossible-posture pain has the phenomenal character of *being-about-an-unrealised-bodily-state*' is doing a lot of philosophical work in a single embedded clause. Heterophenomenologically: the *report* has aboutness. Whether the underlying state has aboutness independently of the report is what is in dispute. You are smuggling the disputed conclusion into the description of the explanandum."

### The Quantum Skeptic

Max Tegmark would say: "I am surprised by how restrained this article is — phantom limbs don't actually require quantum vocabulary, which is admirable. But the article never engages the question whether phantoms could be evidence for *anything* about the consciousness-physics interface at all. The Map's framing is that phantoms make the somatic interface vivid because the limb is absent. But if the interface is doing causal work at the quantum-statistical level (per Tenets 2 and 3), the article should specify whether phantom *content* selection is the kind of event the interface is supposed to be biasing — or whether phantom content selection is below the interface's grain, sitting at the conventional neural-decoding level. If the latter, phantoms are not actually evidence for the interface; they are evidence for *the architecture the interface plugs into*, which is exactly what materialists already concede. The article never settles this and so escapes the empirical-fragility scrutiny [mqi-empirical-fragility](/project/mqi-empirical-fragility/) would otherwise apply."

### The Many-Worlds Defender

### The Empiricist (Popperian)

A Popperian would say four things, two sympathetic and two pointed.

Sympathetic first: Falsifier #3's reformulation (lines 128, predictive content-specific correlations) is genuinely improved over the prior god-of-the-gaps version. The acknowledgement that 'the bar is demanding, not impossible' is exactly what calibration discipline requires.

Pointed first: **Falsifier #2 is dishonestly framed.** It reads: "If well-powered randomised trials returned null results for visual-feedback interventions, the demonstrative force of the top-down modulation argument would diminish." Guémann, Olié, Raquin, Courtet & Risch 2023 (*European Journal of Pain* 27(1):3–13) is precisely a placebo-controlled-RCT-only systematic review that returned no evidence for clinical effectiveness. Two of the three converging outer-reviewers on 2026-05-16 explicitly flagged this as a partial-meeting of the article's own falsifier. The article should either cite Guémann 2023 and acknowledge the partial-meeting, or specify why the methodological restriction in Guémann's review does not count. As it stands, the falsifier is a moving target: the literature has moved, and the article has not.

Pointed second: The thesis "the bridge will not be redeemed within physicalist resources" (line 88, paraphrased) is unfalsifiable as stated, because what counts as 'within physicalist resources' is itself contested. Predictive-processing theorists could redeem the bridge by some future hierarchical-inference account of phenomenal-aboutness and the Map could rejoin that the redeemed account is no longer physicalist 'in the relevant sense.' The article needs at least one concrete specification of what 'redemption within physicalist resources' would look like for it to bite as a falsifier.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

Nagarjuna would say: "You have done admirable work locating the felt body in the brain's construction rather than in the periphery. But the article still reifies a 'somatic interface' — a *thing* through which consciousness contacts the body. The Buddhist analysis is that the felt body is *empty* in exactly the way you describe — there is no peripheral referent — but you immediately re-substantialise: you propose that *another* thing (the somatic interface) is doing the construction, with consciousness on one side and brain architecture on the other. The phantom case is *more* radical than your reading: the body whose felt experience requires no peripheral referent also requires no metaphysical somatic-interface referent. ## Counterarguments to Address

### Mirror therapy as evidence for top-down causation

- **Current content says**: "*Phenomenological modulation* — vivid felt movement and sometimes immediate change in pain quality — is well-attested. *Clinical efficacy* across patient populations is more contested" (line 62)
- **A critic would argue**: Guémann 2023 is precisely the well-powered RCT-only review and finds no evidence for clinical effectiveness. The distinction between "phenomenological modulation" (well-attested) and "clinical efficacy" (contested) is the right move — but the article does not perform it cleanly. The phenomenological modulation is reported in single-subject studies and small-n case series; the systematic RCT evidence is for the clinical effect, which has not survived. If the article rests on phenomenological modulation, it needs to specify which studies establish the phenomenological effect under conditions that exclude placebo, attention-shifting, expectancy, and the contemporaneous use of graded motor imagery / VR — none of which are uniquely "visual feedback of phantom." Rajendram et al. 2022 found mirror therapy and VR equivalent, which complicates the specific "visual feedback of phantom" causal story.
- **Suggested response**: Cite Guémann 2023 and Rajendram et al. 2022; explicitly separate the phenomenological modulation evidence (Ramachandran 1996 and follow-ups; clinical case series) from the clinical-efficacy evidence (Chan 2007; Foell 2014 vs. Guémann 2023); acknowledge that the equivalence with graded motor imagery and VR weakens the "specifically visual feedback of phantom" causal-shape claim, and reformulate the philosophical work mirror therapy does for the article as resting on phenomenological modulation under controlled attention/expectancy conditions rather than on clinical efficacy. The pathway-shape argument can survive this, but the article needs to do the work.

### Predictive processing redeems phenomenal-aboutness

- **Current content says**: "What predictive processing does not specify is why hierarchical Bayesian inference is accompanied by felt burning, itching, or impossible-posture cramp. The bridge from prediction error to phenomenal character is promissory" (line 96)
- **A critic would argue**: The internal PP response — that phenomenal-aboutness is itself a derived feature of hierarchical inference at sufficient depth and recurrence — is acknowledged in the article (line 98, "internally available") but treated as if mere acknowledgment of the response refutes nothing. A serious PP theorist (Friston-style) would argue that the article concedes the framework's full empirical apparatus, then complains that the framework has not yet completed a project no theory has completed for any phenomenon. This is unfair scoring: by the same standard, every theory in cognitive science would be 'promissory.' The Map needs to specify why the phantom case sharpens this standard load specifically — what phantom-specific feature makes the promissory note worse here than in (say) vision or hearing.
- **Suggested response**: Either acknowledge the article is exhibiting the standard hard-problem load in a clinical-vivid setting (and lose the implicit claim that phantoms specifically embarrass PP) — which is the honest move and already partially in the text — or specify the phantom-specific feature that makes the promissory load worse for phantoms than for vision. The clearest candidate is the absence-of-peripheral-correlate condition: in vision, the felt-content target is causally upstream of the brain; in phantoms, it is not. But the article would need to argue this rather than gesture at it.

### The somatic interface recapitulates the dualist mistake

- **Current content says**: "Phantoms do *not* support consciousness existing independently of the brain. The phantom is brain-mediated; its conditions of existence are neural, even when the limb is not. The Map's dualism claims consciousness is not reducible to the brain, not that it operates without one." (line 120)
- **A critic would argue**: The Buddhist deflationary reading (above) and a Strawsonian pansychist reading would both say: the conclusion 'the phantom is brain-mediated' is correct but the inference to 'somatic interface' is the same kind of reification the article elsewhere refuses. If consciousness is irreducibly tied to brain-mediated conditions of existence even when the limb is not present, why posit a *thing* (the interface) at the contact point rather than treating consciousness as constitutively neural-architectural?
- **Suggested response**: This is at the framework-boundary level and properly belongs in the [somatic-interface](/concepts/somatic-interface/) article rather than the phantom-limb article. But the phantom-limb article should at least briefly acknowledge that the somatic-interface ontology is being imported as a framework commitment rather than independently earned by the phantom case (per Issue 5). One sentence suffices.
