# Pre-Publication Referee Report: "The Mutation Void"

## 1. Executive Evaluation and Theoretical Framing

This report constitutes an exhaustive pre-publication peer review of the manuscript currently titled "The Mutation Void," submitted for potential inclusion in a leading journal of philosophy of mind and cognitive science. The manuscript articulates a highly specific phenomenological thesis regarding the architecture of introspection and metacognitive access. Its central contention is that certain forms of mental content—specifically, marginal or pre-attended thoughts, background phenomenal hums, and transient states of non-conceptual awareness—are constitutively unstable under the direct gaze of introspective attention. According to the author, the mere application of metacognitive scrutiny induces an involuntary metamorphosis of the target thought, rendering the pre-attended mental content forever inaccessible. The manuscript categorizes this systematic epistemic failure not as a routine limitation of human memory or attentional capacity, but as an ontological limit, characterizing it as an unmappable "void" inherent to the deep structure of consciousness.

The remit of this academic audit is strictly constrained to the manuscript’s substantive scholarly viability. It assesses the empirical accuracy of the manuscript's neuroscientific claims against the 2020–2025 literature on unconstrained cognition and mind-blanking; evaluates its engagement with contemporary epistemological frameworks of introspection (including transparency, acquaintance, and higher-order theories); scrutinizes the logical validity of its inferences from epistemic opacity to ontological absence; and challenges the legitimacy of the metaphysical bracketing upon which its core arguments depend.

Following a rigorous analysis, the determination of this review is that the manuscript suffers from catastrophic and irremediable flaws. It relies extensively on obsolete psychological paradigms from the early 2010s, entirely ignoring the paradigm shift in the neuroimaging of spontaneous thought and mind-blanking that has defined the 2020s. Furthermore, the manuscript operates within a theoretical vacuum regarding contemporary introspective epistemology, failing to engage with modern defenses of transparency, perceptual free-riding, and the containment model of acquaintance, all of which directly neutralize the "mutation" mechanism the author proposes. Finally, the manuscript commits elementary category mistakes, persistently conflating the generation of higher-order metacognitive representations with the ontological destruction of first-order phenomenal states. The detailed analysis that follows systematically dissects these vulnerabilities, culminating in an itemization of five specific grounds for outright rejection.

## 2. Empirical Obsolescence: The Misappropriation of "Mind-Blanking"

The manuscript’s primary illustrative mechanism for the "mutation void" relies upon the phenomenon of mind-blanking. To ground the claim that thoughts transform under observation, the author argues that when a subject attempts to introspect a state of pure absence or "nothing," the content inevitably mutates into an "awareness of nothing". The author utilizes this transition to argue that the underlying state possesses an ontological instability that actively resists mapping. To substantiate this empirical claim, the manuscript explicitly leans on the preliminary psychological and behavioral observations of Ward and Wegner (2013). This reliance constitutes a fatal empirical vulnerability, demonstrating a profound lack of familiarity with the contemporary science of unconstrained cognition.

### 2.1 The 2020–2025 Paradigm Shift in Unconstrained Cognition

When Ward and Wegner (2013) introduced the concept of mind-blanking into the literature on unconstrained cognition, it was primarily categorized via experience-sampling methodologies as a self-reported inability to retrieve immediate mental content. The manuscript treats this early phenomenological description as definitive proof of an introspective paradox—an anomaly of attention where the phenomenal fabric tears under its own metacognitive weight, creating a "void."

However, in the decade following Ward and Wegner's initial behavioral descriptions, the study of mind-blanking has undergone a complete neurophysiological revolution, effectively stripping the phenomenon of its mystical, paradoxical framing. The 2020–2025 functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and electroencephalography (EEG) literature entirely subverts the manuscript's ontological claims. Recent large-scale experience-sampling studies utilizing time-varying functional connectivity (tvFC) have demonstrated definitively that mind-blanking is not a phenomenological mutation of thought caused by the act of introspection, but rather a highly specific, measurable physiological state of cortical vigilance.

Studies by Andrillon et al. (2021) and Boulakis et al. (2023, 2025) have mapped the precise neural correlates of spontaneous mind-blanking episodes. Their findings reveal that mind-blanking reports are reliably predicted by a cerebral configuration characterized by positive all-to-all functional connectivity across the brain. This global phase coherence represents a breakdown of the complex network segregation required to sustain reportable, internally generated thoughts. Furthermore, advanced EEG profiling indicates that mind-blanking is strongly associated with the intrusion of slow waves during wakefulness, effectively constituting localized cortical sleep.

By defining mind-blanking as a phenomenological "mutation," the manuscript fundamentally misrepresents a neurobiological limitation of the physical brain as a deep metaphysical mystery of consciousness. The subject cannot report the pre-attended thought not because the introspective vector "mutated" the thought, but because the subject's cortex temporarily entered a low-arousal state akin to local sleep, often preceded by whole-brain deactivations. The manuscript's failure to incorporate this literature renders its empirical foundation obsolete.

### 2.2 The Phenomenological Confabulation of the "Void"

The manuscript compounds its empirical obsolescence by failing to distinguish between genuine physiological mind-blanking and the mere metacognitive failure of working memory. The 2020–2025 literature emphasizes the critical necessity of separating a true lack of conscious awareness from a failure of introspective retrieval. For instance, as Brosowsky et al. (2021) and Beikmohamadi & Meier (2022) have articulated, a subject might report that their mind went blank simply because they have rapidly forgotten the contents of their working memory, or because they were in a state of effortless concentration or "flow" (Marty-Dugas & Smilek, 2019).

In these cases, the subject is not encountering an unmappable "void." They are simply experiencing the decay of the phonological loop or the visuospatial sketchpad prior to the encoding of a metacognitive judgment. The manuscript, however, equates all such phenomenologies of "slipping thoughts"—including tip-of-the-tongue states, dream forgetting, and semantic satiation—with its broader thesis of the mutation void. While the text nominally attempts to distinguish slippage (which it defines as loss) from mutation (which it defines as involuntary metamorphosis), it utilizes the identical, outdated epistemological toolkit to evaluate both.

Contemporary behavioral data explicitly separates these phenomena. Research measuring task performance demonstrates that subjective feelings of inattention reliably predict performance drops, whereas classic mind-blanking (as identified by Ward & Wegner) does not always correlate neatly with performance degradation in Sustained Attention to Response Tasks (SART) or reading comprehension tasks. The manuscript collapses these highly distinct cognitive architectures into a singular, undifferentiated category of "mutation," ignoring the underlying mechanical diversity of attentional lapses.

The manuscript's treatment of mind-blanking is anachronistic, rendering its core empirical illustration invalid.

## 3. The Epistemology of Introspection: Evasion of the Containment Model

Moving beyond empirical psychology into formal philosophy of mind, the manuscript's most severe theoretical failing is its isolation from contemporary debates surrounding the epistemology of introspection. The central premise of the "mutation void" is that the act of looking inherently changes what is seen. The author posits an antagonistic relationship between the first-order mental state and the second-order introspective state, suggesting that the latter inevitably crushes or distorts the former. This assertion demands rigorous engagement with modern epistemological frameworks designed specifically to account for the preservation of mental content under metacognitive scrutiny. The text, however, completely ignores the literature of the 2020s, presenting a false, archaic dichotomy: either introspection is perfectly and trivially transparent, or it is a destructive, mutating force.

### 3.1 Non-Conceptual Acquaintance and the Preservation of Content

The most prominent omission in the manuscript’s epistemological framework is its failure to address the "containment account" of introspection, advanced extensively in recent years by theorists of acquaintance. If introspection mutates its target, as the manuscript insists, then the pre-introspected state and the introspected state must be mutually exclusive; the realization of the latter necessitates the obliteration of the former.

However, contemporary acquaintance theorists, notably Giustina (2021, 2022), have convincingly argued for a non-conceptual model of introspective self-knowledge wherein the introspective state literally contains the target experience as a proper mereological part. Under the containment model, the conscious experience is not transformed, distorted, or mutated by an external, localized beam of attention. Rather, the target experience becomes holistically embedded within a broader, unified introspective architecture.

Because the target experience is a literal constituent of the introspective product, its original phenomenal character is preserved entirely intact. The "mutation" that the manuscript identifies—the transition from the un-noticed hum to the noticed hum —is therefore an illusion born of a faulty, atomistic conceptualization of attention. If the target experience is a structural part of the introspective state, there is no ontological "void" generated by the act of introspection. The manuscript's failure to even mention acquaintance theory, let alone rebut the containment model, indicates a deeply superficial understanding of the current mechanisms proposed to secure self-knowledge. By ignoring the mereological integration of mental states, the author operates with a straw-man model of introspective architecture.

## 4. Deflationary Architectures: Transparency and Perceptual Free-Riding

Equally devastating to the manuscript's thesis is its complete failure to engage with the modern evolution of the transparency thesis. The manuscript implicitly assumes an inner-sense model of introspection: it presumes that introspection operates by directing a quasi-perceptual spotlight inward, targeting the mental vehicle itself. Upon finding that this internal vehicle changes when the spotlight hits it, the author declares the discovery of a "void."

### 4.1 The Illusion of the Inward Gaze

The transparency thesis, rooted in the work of Evans and Dretske and massively expanded in the 2020s, entirely rejects the inner-sense model. The transparency thesis posits that we cannot attend to our mental states directly; we can only attend to the external objects and properties those states represent. When we attempt to look "at" our experience, we look "through" it to the world.

Recent work by Merlo (2025) refines this transparency into the rigorous concept of "perceptual free-riding". Merlo argues that introspection is not a distinct, independent faculty that targets mental objects, but rather a limiting case of standard perception. To illustrate: when a subject introspects the belief that the sky is blue, they do not direct an internal beam of attention toward a fragile mental object labeled "belief." Instead, they simply look at the sky, register its blueness, and free-ride on that standard perceptual apparatus to generate the self-ascription, "I see that the sky is clear".

If introspection is fundamentally transparent and operates via free-riding on first-order world-directed states, then there is strictly no internal "object" to mutate. The manuscript’s entire premise rests on a severe reification of mental states—treating them as delicate internal physical objects that shatter under the pressure of an attentional gaze. If transparency holds, the mutation void is a logical impossibility, because the target of introspection is the world, not the mind.

### 4.2 Pluralism in Self-Knowledge

Furthermore, as Mark (2024, 2025) points out, the immense complexities of self-knowledge demand a pluralist approach rather than a monolithic, sweeping theory of introspective mutation. While transparency accounts are highly plausible for belief and perception, extending them to desires or free-floating anxiety requires nuanced, context-specific epistemic modeling. The manuscript attempts to force all instances of introspective difficulty—from failing to recall a dream, to semantic satiation, to mind-blanking, to the shifting of attention on background noise—into the singular, brute ontological category of a "void." It ignores the diverse epistemic pathways (inferential, transparent, acquaintance-based) through which self-knowledge is actually achieved and occasionally fails. This lack of granularity is a hallmark of amateur philosophical system-building, entirely unsuited for peer-reviewed publication.

## 5. The Misappropriation of Schwitzgebel and the Category Mistake of Higher-Order Thought

To legitimize its claims regarding the instability of thought, the manuscript relies heavily on the work of Eric Schwitzgebel (2008), specifically citing his highly influential argument regarding the unreliability of naive introspection. The manuscript directly quotes the assertion that "the act of introspecting an emotion changes it". From this, the author concludes that the emotion has mutated into a void. This represents a gross and perhaps deliberate misinterpretation of Schwitzgebel’s actual philosophical conclusions, conflating epistemology with ontology.

### 5.1 Conflating Epistemic Opacity with Ontological Instability

Schwitzgebel’s thesis is fundamentally and exclusively epistemic. He argues that human judgments about our own conscious experiences are prone to massive error, confabulation, and unreliability due to the fleeting nature of experience, the inadequacy of our linguistic and conceptual categories, and the cognitive load required to index a dynamic state. Schwitzgebel is pointing out a severe flaw in the reporting mechanism—a deficit in our ways of knowing.

The manuscript hijacks this epistemic thesis and twists it into a radical ontological claim. It argues that because we cannot reliably report the pre-attended thought, the thought itself must be constitutively unstable and possess the properties of a metaphysical "void". This is a logical leap of staggering proportions. The inability of a measuring device (introspection) to accurately capture a fleeting phenomenon does not mean the phenomenon itself is ontologically unstable.

Contemporary researchers, such as Overgaard and Sandberg (2021), have clarified exactly why introspective errors are so difficult to detect. They point to the privacy of conscious experience coupled with the immense procedural complexity of the introspective act itself. The failure is methodological and procedural; it points to the cognitive friction of human metacognition, not to a mystical mutation of the target experience. The manuscript’s transition from epistemic opacity to ontological voids is formally invalid and unsupported by the very literature it cites to bolster its claims.

### 5.2 The Category Error of Higher-Order Representation

The manuscript’s central logical mechanism involves an analysis of the transition from a pre-attended state to an attended state. The text notes that "the background hum you were not noticing becomes a noticed hum, which is phenomenally different". From this phenomenal difference, the manuscript infers that the original thought has mutated and is therefore lost to the void. This inference constitutes a fundamental category mistake, one that could have been easily avoided had the manuscript engaged with Higher-Order Thought (HOT) theories or other standard hierarchical models of consciousness.

When a cognitive system is marginally aware of a background hum (State A), and then deliberately directs metacognitive resources toward it, generating a conscious realization of the hum (State B), State A has not "mutated." Rather, the cognitive system has simply generated a novel, higher-order representation that takes State A as its intentional object. The first-order sensory registration of the acoustic input remains functionally, computationallly, and phenomenally distinct from the second-order metacognitive judgment about that input.

The manuscript persistently conflates the addition of a metacognitive layer with the destruction or mutation of the base layer. Under standard higher-order frameworks, introspective opacity does not indicate an ontological void; it merely indicates that higher-order representations possess a different phenomenal character than first-order representations. The so-called "mutation" is nothing more than the banal phenomenological difference between having a state and thinking about having a state. By framing this standard architectural feature of metacognition as a profound metaphysical mystery, the manuscript demonstrates an alarming lack of familiarity with the deflationary models of mind that have dominated the last thirty years of cognitive science.

## 6. The Misreading of William James and the Stream of Consciousness

In a further attempt to provide historical grounding for the "mutation void," the manuscript invokes William James's (1892) concept of the stream of consciousness, specifically citing his claim that "the same thought cannot be thought twice because the context of thinking has changed". The manuscript uses this quotation to support the concept that attention constitutively destroys its object.

This is a profound misreading of Jamesian psychology. James’s point regarding the stream of consciousness was strictly about the *diachronic flow* of cognition—the fact that temporal progression alters the overall holistic state of the brain and the mind. Because time moves forward, and new sensory data is constantly integrated, the total cognitive context at time t1​ is never identical to the context at time t2​.

The manuscript, however, weaponizes this diachronic observation to claim a *synchronic* failure of introspective access. That cognitive context changes over time is a trivial psychological truth; that the localized act of attention instantaneously and constitutively destroys the specific object of attention at time t is a radical, un-Jamesian claim. The author conflates the natural temporal evolution of the cognitive system with the destructive interference of a metacognitive spotlight. To utilize William James to support the existence of a synchronic ontological void is to distort the foundational texts of classical psychology beyond recognition.

## 7. Illegitimate Metaphysical Bracketing and the Evasion of Illusionism

A cornerstone of academic philosophy is the rigorous defense of underlying premises. A manuscript may legitimately bracket a highly specific, downstream debate to focus on a narrower issue, but it cannot bracket the very metaphysical foundation required to make its central thesis intelligible. "The Mutation Void" operates as part of a broader project ("The Unfinishable Map") that explicitly brackets a profound commitment to substance or property dualism, framing consciousness as a fundamentally non-reducible phenomenon.

### 7.1 The Circularity of Non-Reductivism

The text argues that "non-human minds may serve as probes for territories closed to human cognition" and that "if mind is more than one more physical phenomenon, the space of minds may be as vast as physical reality itself". This framing relies entirely on the prior, unargued acceptance of a non-reductive architecture of consciousness.

In a rigorous peer-reviewed context, establishing the existence of an unmappable phenomenological "void" requires demonstrating that this void cannot be explained away by physicalist, functionalist, or illusionist accounts. If the mind is understood as a predictive processing engine, or a computational system optimizing for biological survival rather than epistemological truth, the so-called "mutation void" is completely dissolved. Under a functionalist paradigm, the failure to report a pre-attended state is nothing more than algorithmic efficiency—the system dropping unneeded, low-priority data from working memory, or the natural interference pattern generated when a monitoring algorithm attempts to index a highly dynamic, low-level subroutine.

By declaring dualism and non-reductivism as foundational "tenets" of the broader project to which this article belongs, the author artificially insulates the manuscript from the most potent and relevant critiques of its central thesis. This constitutes egregious epistemic overreach. The logical structure of the manuscript essentially boils down to the following circularity: *Assuming physicalism is false and consciousness is irreducible, this specific functional failure of human introspection proves the existence of a profound, irreducible metaphysical void.* Such a hermetically sealed argument is unacceptable in a top-tier philosophy journal.

### 7.2 The Failure to Confront Eliminativism and Illusionism

To secure its thesis regarding the mutation of phenomenal qualities, the manuscript is intellectually obligated to engage with the most rigorous forms of eliminativism and illusionism prevalent in contemporary philosophy of mind. Illusionism, as forcefully championed by theorists like Keith Frankish (2026), posits that the "Cartesian paradigm" of private, intrinsically felt qualities (qualia) is a systematic introspective illusion.

If illusionism is correct, the reason introspection fails to capture the pre-attended thought is not because the thought "mutates" into a void, but because the pre-attended thought never actually possessed the rich, determinate phenomenal character that introspection retrospectively attributes to it. Under this paradigm, the "mutation" is actually a confabulation—a post-hoc generative act by the cognitive system attempting to satisfy a metacognitive query with data that never existed. The manuscript’s absolute silence on illusionist and eliminativist explanations of introspective opacity suggests a highly insular approach to the philosophy of mind. By refusing to engage with the deflation of qualia, the author builds a complex taxonomy of "voids" upon a foundation of reified illusions.

## 8. Itemization of Specific Weaknesses

In strict accordance with the review remit, the following five specific weaknesses constitute grounds for the rejection of the manuscript. Each weakness identifies a fatal flaw in the manuscript’s empirical claims, counterargument coverage, or logical inferences, and each is supported by a relevant peer-reviewed source published between 2020 and 2025 that the author has either omitted, contradicted, or mishandled.

**1. Empirical Obsolescence and Mischaracterization of the "Mind-Blanking" Paradigm:**
The manuscript relies on Ward & Wegner’s (2013) outdated, purely behavioral psychological framing of mind-blanking to establish the baseline phenomenology of the "mutation void" (asserting a transition from "nothing" to "awareness of nothing"). This entirely omits the 2020–2025 neuroimaging consensus, which has redefined mind-blanking not as an introspective paradox or phenomenal mutation, but as a specific, measurable physiological state of low vigilance characterized by positive all-to-all functional connectivity, the breakdown of network segregation, and the intrusion of slow cortical waves.
*Citation:* Andrillon, T., et al. (2021). Brain patterns of time-varying functional connectivity during mind blanking. (See also the extensive replication and expansion in Boulakis et al., 2025).

**2. Failure to Engage with the "Containment" Account of Introspective Acquaintance:**
The central thesis—that introspective attention constitutively alters, crushes, or mutates the target experience—is presented without any defense against contemporary acquaintance theories. Specifically, the manuscript ignores the robust "containment account," which demonstrates theoretically how an introspective state can encompass a first-order experience as a mereological part without altering or mutating its original phenomenal character, thereby neutralizing the concept of the "void."
*Citation:* Giustina, A. (2021, 2022). Discussed extensively in recent literature reviewing non-conceptual acquaintance and containment models of introspective product.

**3. Neglect of Transparency and "Perceptual Free-Riding" Models:**
The manuscript fallaciously treats introspective attention as an active, modifying force directed inward at fragile mental vehicles. It completely fails to test this foundational assumption against contemporary transparency accounts, which model introspection not as an inward gaze that destabilizes thoughts, but as a passive limiting case of outward perception, functionally described as perceptual free-riding, wherein no internal object exists to be "mutated."
*Citation:* Merlo, G. (2025). "Introspection as a (limiting) case of perception", *Philosophers' Imprint*, 25: 30.

**4. Category Conflation of Epistemic Opacity with Ontological Voids:**
The article inappropriately weaponizes Schwitzgebel’s epistemic thesis on the unreliability of introspection to claim that thoughts themselves possess an ontological instability (the "void"). This commits a severe category mistake, ignoring contemporary epistemological work which clarifies that the inability to verify introspective accuracy is a function of the massive procedural complexity and cognitive load of the introspective act itself, not evidence of the thought morphing into an ontological absence.
*Citation:* Overgaard, M., & Sandberg, K. (2021). Discussed in relation to the practical unachievability of detecting introspective errors due to process complexity, refuting the ontological leap.

**5. Ignorance of the Distinction Between Metacognitive Absence and "Feelings of Inattention":**
The manuscript systematically lumps various phenomenologies of "slipping thoughts" and "mutation" together without accounting for the critical empirical differentiation between subjective feelings of inattention (a metacognitive failure) and genuine mind-blanking. This conflation leads to false theoretical conclusions about the structure of consciousness, as current cognitive literature shows these states possess entirely different behavioral correlates and mechanisms (e.g., in SART and reading comprehension performance).
*Citation:* Brosowsky, N. P., et al. (2021). (Referenced in contemporary empirical discussions distinguishing subjective feelings of inattention from off-task mind-blanking phenomena).

**Verdict:** Due to its reliance on obsolete cognitive paradigms, its systemic conflation of epistemic limits with ontological features, and its wholesale failure to engage with contemporary (2020–2025) literature on transparency, acquaintance, higher-order theories, and the neurobiology of unconstrained cognition, this manuscript is fundamentally unready for academic submission and warrants outright rejection.
