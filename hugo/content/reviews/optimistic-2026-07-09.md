## Executive Summary

The free-will wave is one of the strongest article clusters the Map has built: four
technically precise, exceptionally well-calibrated pieces that state the incompatibilist
arguments at full strength, name their own dialectical vulnerabilities, and repeatedly draw
the line between "clears the ground" and "vindicates the Map." The cluster's discipline about
what each argument does and does *not* deliver is model work. The main opportunities are
outward: the cluster refers to several major adjacent positions (event-causal vs agent-causal
libertarianism, source-vs-leeway incompatibilism, semicompatibilism, the reactive-attitudes
tradition, free-will skepticism's constructive ethics) that it engages substantively in prose
but which the Map has no dedicated home for.

## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)
The cluster keeps the hard-problem stakes visible without leaning on them illegitimately.
Frankfurt-cases and akrasia both explicitly quarantine the metaphysics: akrasia states outright
that "what the Map adds—that the controller is genuinely non-physical—rests on its independent
arguments (the hard problem, the conceivability argument), not on akrasia." That is exactly the
division of labour a property dualist wants — the explanatory gap is carried by the arguments
that can bear it, not smuggled into every topic.

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)
The consequence-argument article's reading of Lewis's local miracle is the standout. It reframes
Lewis's "divergence miracle" not as an adversary but as a structural cousin of the Map's
interface: "Where Lewis posits an unowned miracle to save compatibilism, the Map posits a
minimally owned one to ground libertarian selection." That is a genuinely original connection
between the compatibilist literature and the interactionist picture, and it engages the
physics-consciousness interface seriously rather than decoratively.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)
Akrasia centres the first-person datum honestly — "I chose X while judging Y better" — and then,
rather than treating the report as self-certifying, foregrounds the Socratic denial that the
datum even exists. Centring first-person experience *while* flagging that the experience's
reality is contested is the mature version of the phenomenological move.

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)
The cluster avoids crude substance-dualism throughout by locating agency at a *selection*
interface rather than positing a homuncular chooser. The akrasia piece's control-theoretic
framing — consciousness as "a low-bandwidth controller that selects among neural options the
brain prepares, rather than computing outcomes from scratch" — preserves mind's reality while
keeping it continuous with process rather than parked in a separate substance.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)
The cluster takes agency and origination seriously as the thing worth wanting. Frankfurt-cases'
retreat-to-source move is precisely a Kane-friendly instinct: what matters is "that the agent be
the genuine originator of the selection." Notably, the manipulation article gives Kane's
event-causal libertarianism a fair hearing as the target of the disappearing-agent objection
rather than a strawman — though (see below) Kane's positive theory has no dedicated page.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)
Epistemic humility is the cluster's signature. Every "Relation to Site Perspective" section
ends by conceding that the opposing hand is strong: the manipulation article says the Map "places
its bet there knowingly, and grants that the opponent's hand is strong"; frankfurt-cases calls
the source-retreat "a *retreat*" that "costs the Map the clean leeway picture." This is
intellectual humility deployed as method, not as decoration.

### The Hardline Empiricist (Birch)
This cluster is a showcase for the restraint this persona exists to praise, and the praise is
load-bearing here because the whole wave turns on tenet-load-bearing claims about whether
harnessed indeterminism is control.

- **Tenet-as-evidence-upgrade is praise-worthily NOT done.** The manipulation article had every
  structural opportunity to convert Tenet 5 into positive support for agent causation and
  explicitly declined: it concedes that blocking Pereboom's parsimony objection "buys no positive
  support for agent causation either," and that the wild-coincidences worry "is a real cost the
  Map's framework pays, not one it has discharged." The consequence-argument article is equally
  disciplined: "Presenting the argument as though it *proved* the Map's dualism would be exactly
  the over-reading to avoid."
- **Register separation is explicit.** The manipulation article names the Bidirectional Interaction
  tenet's own status honestly — "a live hypothesis pending a toy model, not an established result"
  — and admits the Map "does not have a worked model" for token-level bias amounting to settling.
  This is the five-tier scale used honestly, with the load-bearing claim held at "live hypothesis"
  rather than nudged upward on tenet-coherence.
- **The named pattern is present in substance.** Akrasia's entire "Relation to Site Perspective"
  is a worked example of "tenet-coherent, not evidence-elevating": it offers the selection reading
  as "one lens among the framework-neutral accounts," states that "a physicalist can accept every
  word" of the exposition, and lists three independent reasons the reading proves nothing
  non-physical. This is the honest register the persona rewards.
- **No Process-Philosopher/Empiricist conflict detected.** The two personas *converge* across this
  cluster: the process-friendly selection-interface framing and the evidential-discipline framing
  praise different aspects of the same passages without tension, which is the signature of a
  cluster that has resolved the possibility/probability slippage honestly. No calibration flag is
  warranted for these four articles.

## Content Strengths

### the-consequence-argument-for-incompatibilism.md
- **Strongest point**: The relentless "establishes incompatibilism *only*" discipline — the article
  states four separate times, in four registers, that the argument does not deliver libertarianism,
  culminating in the observation that "the hard determinist and the libertarian *share* the
  Consequence Argument."
- **Notable quote**: "Where Lewis posits an unowned miracle to save compatibilism, the Map posits a
  minimally owned one to ground libertarian selection."
- **Why it works**: It resists the single most tempting over-read in the entire free-will literature
  (treating incompatibilism as if it were libertarianism) and turns the resistance into positive
  clarity about the Map's own division of labour.

### the-manipulation-argument-and-hard-incompatibilism.md
- **Strongest point**: Framing hard incompatibilism as "a *partial ally*" — the most dangerous
  opponent precisely because it grants the incompatibilist premise and attacks only the second
  front. This is a sophisticated dialectical self-location most treatments miss.
- **Notable quote**: "The honest position is that hard incompatibilism and the Map agree on the
  incompatibilist premise and disagree, at the edge of what anyone can currently establish, on
  whether harnessed indeterminism can be control."
- **Why it works**: It correctly identifies the one place the Map genuinely has to fight, and fights
  there rather than everywhere.

### frankfurt-cases-and-the-principle-of-alternate-possibilities.md
- **Strongest point**: The source-vs-leeway pivot as the Map's survival route — "the Map does not
  have to win the Frankfurt debate to survive it." Recasting the libertarianism as a sourcehood view
  that survives even a successful PAP refutation is a durable structural insight.
- **Notable quote**: "the Map's deepest commitment was to origination, and only derivatively to
  leeway."
- **Why it works**: It converts a prima facie threat (a refutation of alternatives-based freedom)
  into a clarification of what the Map was really committed to all along, without pretending the
  retreat is costless.

### akrasia-and-weakness-of-will.md
- **Strongest point**: The four-position front-loading (Socratic / Aristotelian / Davidsonian /
  Holtonian) and the rigorous refusal to let the selection-model reading masquerade as evidence.
- **Notable quote**: "the descriptive facts underdetermine the analysis, and simplicity is an
  unreliable guide when the phenomenon's very existence is contested."
- **Why it works**: It treats a phenomenon that *looks* tailor-made to support a non-physical will
  and explicitly declines to cash it in — the hardest and most honest thing to do with congenial
  material.

## Expansion Opportunities

### High Priority

#### Event-causal vs agent-causal libertarianism (a taxonomy article)
- **Builds on**: the manipulation argument (which turns entirely on the two-family distinction and
  the disappearing-agent objection), `concepts/agent-causation`, `concepts/luck-objection`
- **Would address**: The Map has no article on the distinction between event-causal libertarianism
  (Kane, Balaguer, Ekstrom) and agent-causal libertarianism (O'Connor, Clarke), even though the
  manipulation article's whole case against libertarianism is structured around it and Kane's
  self-forming actions and "ultimate responsibility" are referenced repeatedly across the corpus
  with no home page. A dedicated treatment of the two libertarian families would strengthen
  `free-will`, `agent-causation`, and the manipulation article.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium
- **Tenet alignment**: Directly serves Tenets 2 and 3 by locating the Map's agent-causal commitment
  within the space of libertarian options.

#### Source vs leeway incompatibilism (the organizing distinction)
- **Builds on**: frankfurt-cases (which introduces the distinction but has no page to point at),
  the consequence argument, `concepts/moral-responsibility`
- **Would address**: The source/leeway distinction is the central organizing axis of contemporary
  free-will debate and the frankfurt-cases article's load-bearing survival move depends on it, yet
  the Map has no dedicated article on it. A treatment of source incompatibilism (Pereboom, Stump,
  Zagzebski) versus leeway incompatibilism would give the retreat-to-source argument a proper
  anchor.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium
- **Tenet alignment**: Clarifies whether the Map's libertarianism is fundamentally a leeway or a
  source commitment — a question Tenets 2–4 leave partly open.

#### The reactive-attitudes tradition (Strawson's "Freedom and Resentment")
- **Builds on**: the manipulation argument, `concepts/moral-responsibility`
- **Would address**: The Map has no article on P.F. Strawson's "Freedom and Resentment" or the
  reactive-attitudes approach to moral responsibility, despite this being the most influential
  compatibilist alternative of the last half-century and the direct backdrop to Pereboom's
  forward-looking, desert-free responsibility. Its absence is a real dialectical gap — the Map
  engages hard incompatibilism's *conclusion* but not the Strawsonian tradition it is reacting to.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium
- **Tenet alignment**: Tests the Map's desert-entailing responsibility commitment against the
  leading naturalistic rival to it.

### Medium Priority

#### Semicompatibilism and guidance control (Fischer & Ravizza)
- **Builds on**: frankfurt-cases (which calls Fischer's view "the forceful rival" and "a genuine
  rival, not a defeated one"), `concepts/reasons-responsiveness`
- **Would address**: Fischer's semicompatibilism — free will incompatible with determinism but
  moral responsibility compatible with it — is engaged as a serious opponent in two articles but
  has no dedicated treatment. The Map has a `reasons-responsiveness` concept page but no article on
  the guidance-control/regulative-control distinction or the semicompatibilist position that rests
  on it. A dedicated treatment of semicompatibilism would strengthen the cluster's fairness to its
  strongest actual-sequence rival.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium
- **Tenet alignment**: Probes whether responsibility really needs the non-physical origination the
  Map posits, or whether an actual-sequence account suffices.

#### Free-will skepticism's constructive ethics (life without basic desert)
- **Builds on**: the manipulation argument (which explicitly defers "Pereboom's constructive
  project" as "outside the scope of this article")
- **Would address**: The Map has no article on the positive ethics of free-will skepticism —
  Pereboom's and Caruso's forward-looking, non-retributive responsibility, the public-health /
  quarantine model of wrongdoing, and meaning without basic desert. The manipulation article names
  this as explicitly uncovered. A dedicated treatment would let the Map show what is practically at
  stake in defending libertarian agency: what the world looks like if the skeptic is right.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium
- **Tenet alignment**: Sharpens the practical stakes of Tenets 2–4 by mapping the ethical
  alternative the Map is arguing against.

#### The hierarchical / mesh theory of the will (Frankfurt 1971)
- **Builds on**: frankfurt-cases (which flags that the 1971 higher-order-desire Frankfurt is
  "distinct from the 1969 PAP paper"), `concepts/moral-responsibility`
- **Would address**: The Map has no dedicated article on the hierarchical (mesh) theory of the will
  — Frankfurt's 1971 second-order-desire account of the person, with Watson's valuational and
  Bratman's planning-agency variants. This is a foundational compatibilist account of autonomy that
  the cluster references but never treats. It would also feed the `control-theoretic-will` and
  `volitional-control` concepts.
- **Estimated scope**: Medium
- **Tenet alignment**: Bears on what makes a selection the *agent's own* — the sourcehood question
  Tenets 2–4 depend on.

### Ideas for Later

- **A neutral survey of compatibilism.** The Map covers incompatibilist *arguments* thoroughly but
  the compatibilist position itself appears only as an opponent; a dedicated, fair-minded overview
  of compatibilism would improve dialectical balance (the Map has `compatibilist-symmetry-challenge`
  as a narrow concept but no overview).
- **Motivational internalism vs externalism about moral judgement.** The akrasia article's "Two
  Standing Debates" leans on the internalism/externalism dispute about whether evaluative judgements
  necessarily motivate; it is treated only inside `moral-implications-of-genuine-agency` and would
  benefit from a dedicated concept page (it also underwrites the Socratic-denial horn in akrasia).
- **Manipulation arguments beyond Pereboom.** Mele's zygote argument and its relation to the
  four-case argument are uncovered and would deepen the manipulation article.

## New Concept Pages Needed

- **basic-desert moral responsibility**: The pivotal notion the entire manipulation argument targets,
  currently a single mention inside `moral-responsibility`. It deserves a concept page distinguishing
  basic desert from forward-looking, contractualist, and consequentialist responsibility, so the
  manipulation and Frankfurt articles have a precise anchor.
- **disappearing-agent objection**: Referenced in the manipulation article and `luck-objection` as a
  named objection but with no page; it is distinct enough from the present-luck rollback framing to
  warrant its own concept treatment.
