# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-02-21
**Content reviewed**: concepts/collapse-and-time.md

## Executive Summary

"Collapse and the Arrow of Time" is one of the Map's most ambitious articles, arguing that consciousness helps *constitute* time's direction through its role in wavefunction collapse. While the article is well-structured and honestly presents competing interpretations, it makes several inferential leaps that critics from multiple traditions would challenge. The core vulnerability is that the article's central claims—consciousness constituting time, retrocausal dissolution of the Libet problem, agent identity through collapse patterns—are speculative superstructure built atop a disputed foundation (collapse realism), and the article's own "positive predictions" are too vague to function as genuine empirical tests.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would find this article a prime example of inflated folk psychology dressed in physics vocabulary. "Consciousness selects among possibilities"—what *exactly* is doing the selecting? When you say consciousness "helps constitute" time, you've smuggled in an agent with causal powers and given it a role in fundamental physics, all based on the pre-theoretical intuition that time "feels open." This is folk psychology with equations nearby.

The article's phenomenological claims—that temporal flow "feels like creation not discovery," that the future "feels open, genuinely undetermined"—are treated as data constraining physical theory. But decades of neuroscience show that how things feel is a terrible guide to how things work. The feeling of temporal flow is produced by neural mechanisms (prediction error processing, memory consolidation, attentional updating). Explaining the mechanism explains the phenomenology. You don't need to make consciousness a participant in quantum collapse to explain why time seems directional.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would identify a characteristic inflation throughout this article: moving from "how things seem" to "how things are" without justification. The article states that temporal flow "isn't discovery; it's creation. The future doesn't feel like a region we haven't visited yet; it feels open, genuinely undetermined." But this is precisely the kind of phenomenological assertion Dennett would challenge. Of *course* the future feels undetermined—that's what cognitive uncertainty feels like from the inside. It doesn't follow that the future *is* undetermined, let alone that consciousness is what determines it.

The "Agent Identity Across Collapse" section commits the same error at a deeper level. The claim that "the agent is the selecting activity itself" sounds deep but is either trivially true (the pattern of brain activity constituting decision-making is what we *call* agency) or inflated metaphysics (there's a non-physical selector choosing quantum outcomes). The article slides between these readings without settling on either.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Max Tegmark would have several pointed objections:

1. **The numbers don't add up.** The article claims "revised microtubule coherence estimates (10-100 microseconds) multiplied across collapse events could produce the ~100-750ms specious present." This is not a calculation—it's numerology. "Multiplied across collapse events" is not a mathematical operation with a defined procedure. How many collapse events? What's the multiplication rule? Where's the model? This sentence gestures at quantitative prediction while providing none.

2. **Decoherence is glossed over.** The article acknowledges decoherence but then moves past it too quickly. Decoherence in neural tissue occurs on timescales of 10^-13 seconds (Tegmark's original estimate). Even the disputed "revised estimates" of 10^-5 to 10^-4 seconds are still orders of magnitude too short for the neural processes the article needs. The article leans on "decoherence doesn't solve the measurement problem" as a response, but this conflates two issues: whether quantum coherence persists long enough for consciousness to interact with it (a physics question with clear answers) and whether the measurement problem has a philosophical solution (a separate question).

3. **"Collapse timing" is undefined.** The framework presupposes that collapse events have well-defined timing, but this is precisely what's disputed. In GRW theory, spontaneous localization rates are specified; in Penrose's objective reduction, the timing depends on gravitational self-energy differences. The article treats "collapse timing" as a single concept when it varies dramatically across competing theories.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

David Deutsch would challenge the article's dismissal of MWI on three fronts:

1. **The Past Hypothesis objection is symmetric.** The article argues that MWI "inherits" the Past Hypothesis from thermodynamics rather than explaining it. But collapse realism has exactly the same problem—it also requires low-entropy initial conditions. This isn't a critique of MWI specifically; it's a feature of all cosmological physics.

2. **"The future feels open" is anthropocentric bias.** The article treats the phenomenology of openness as evidence that the future is genuinely undetermined. But on MWI, the branching structure *explains* why the future feels open: because from any branch's perspective, future branching is genuinely unpredictable. The phenomenology is equally well explained without invoking collapse.

3. **The "indexical identity" objection is confused.** What determines which outcome "I" experience? On MWI: both outcomes are experienced by continuations of you. The question "why this one and not that one?" presupposes that only one continuation is real—exactly what MWI denies. The article calls this "treating branch-identity as primitive," but it's no more primitive than treating conscious selection as primitive, which is exactly what the Map does.

### The Empiricist

Karl Popper's ghost would level a devastating objection: the article's "testable predictions" are not genuinely testable.

- "The specious present should show structure consistent with collapse timing." What structure? Consistent with which collapse theory's timing? With what precision must they match? Without specifying these, this is not a prediction but an aspiration.

- "Contemplative practices that alter collapse involvement should alter temporal phenomenology." Meditation *does* alter temporal phenomenology—through well-understood neurological mechanisms (altered default mode network activity, reduced interoceptive prediction error). A materialist neuroscientist can explain every meditation-related change in temporal experience without invoking collapse. The prediction doesn't distinguish between the Map's framework and straightforward neuroscience.

- "Advanced meditation states like cessation—where temporal experience reportedly stops—may reflect voluntary withdrawal from collapse participation." What would distinguish "withdrawal from collapse participation" from "altered neural dynamics"? Without a discriminating test, this is metaphysics, not science.

The article's falsification conditions are similarly weak. "Definitive vindication of Many-Worlds" is not an experiment one can perform. "Discovery that fundamental physics is time-asymmetric at base level" would require physics we don't have. These conditions make the framework unfalsifiable in practice, even if falsifiable in principle.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nagarjuna would see a deep irony in the "Agent Identity Across Collapse" section. The article claims to avoid both substance dualism and bundle theory by treating the agent as "constituted activity rather than enduring thing." But this is precisely the sort of move Buddhism would interrogate.

If the agent is the "pattern of selections," what makes this pattern a unity? The article says each collapse "incorporates its predecessors as the background against which selection occurs." But this is just another form of reification—turning a process into a thing by naming the pattern. The pattern of waves on a shore is not a surfer. The pattern of selections is not a self. The article has replaced one form of clinging (to a permanent substance) with another (to a constituted pattern), and called it progress.

Moreover, the entire framework presupposes that consciousness is something that *does things*—selects, constitutes, participates. But what if consciousness is not an agent but an appearing? The article never considers that what it calls "consciousness selecting" might be empty of the agency it imputes—that the selection is happening, but there is no selector.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Uncited "Revised" Coherence Estimates

- **File**: concepts/collapse-and-time.md
- **Location**: "Falsifiability and Predictions" section
- **Problem**: "Revised microtubule coherence estimates (10-100 microseconds)" is stated without citation. The tenets page cites Hagan et al. (2002) for revised estimates of 10^-5 to 10^-4 seconds, but the "10-100 microseconds" figure in this article is not sourced. Additionally, the claim that these can be "multiplied across collapse events" to produce the specious present has no mathematical model behind it—it's a hand-wave disguised as quantitative reasoning.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either cite the specific source for these estimates and provide an actual mathematical model linking them to the specious present, or remove the claim and replace with honest acknowledgment that no quantitative prediction exists yet.

### Issue 2: Retrocausal Libet Resolution Asserted Without Argument

- **File**: concepts/collapse-and-time.md
- **Location**: Opening paragraph, second sentence
- **Problem**: "This connects directly to the retrocausal resolution of the Libet problem. If consciousness operates outside linear time when selecting outcomes, questions about whether neural activity precedes or follows conscious intention dissolve." This is a bold claim placed in the opening summary—the most truncation-resilient position—but it receives no argument in the article body. The Libet problem is never discussed again after this sentence. The resolution is merely asserted: if consciousness is atemporal, temporal ordering doesn't matter. But this is circular—it assumes the framework to dissolve the problem the framework was partly motivated to address.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Either develop the Libet resolution argument in its own section with actual engagement with the experimental literature, or remove it from the opening and replace with a more supportable claim.

### Issue 3: Modified Growing Block Asserted Rather Than Argued

- **File**: concepts/collapse-and-time.md
- **Location**: "Collapse and the Philosophy of Time" section
- **Problem**: The article presents the modified growing block ("Past and present exist; the future doesn't yet. The 'growth' of the block *is* collapse") as the Map's position, but this ontological stance is simply stated, not argued for. The article maps collapse realism to A-theory and decoherence to B-theory, which is helpful exposition, but then the modified growing block is introduced without explaining why it should be preferred over other A-theoretic positions (presentism, for example) or why "growth = collapse" is more than a metaphor.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add a subsection explaining why the modified growing block is preferable to competing temporal ontologies, engaging with the standard objections (e.g., the growing block's difficulty with the special status of "now," the rate-of-passage problem).

### Issue 4: Microtubule Reference Risks Style Guide Violation

- **File**: concepts/collapse-and-time.md
- **Location**: "Falsifiability and Predictions" section
- **Problem**: The writing style guide (Composition Guidance, "Subjects Requiring Restraint") explicitly warns against making microtubules central to arguments. The article references "revised microtubule coherence estimates" as the basis for a quantitative prediction linking collapse timing to the specious present. This makes a speculative mechanism load-bearing in the prediction structure—exactly what the style guide cautions against.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Reframe the prediction without making it dependent on microtubule coherence specifically. The prediction about collapse timing and the specious present could reference multiple candidate mechanisms rather than foregrounding microtubules.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Consciousness as Time-Constituter

- **Current content says**: Consciousness helps constitute time's direction through its role in collapse, rather than merely experiencing it.
- **A critic would argue**: This confuses correlation with constitution. Even if consciousness is involved in collapse, and collapse introduces temporal asymmetry, it doesn't follow that consciousness *constitutes* time's direction. Collapse could introduce asymmetry and consciousness could participate in collapse without consciousness being constitutive of temporal direction. The article needs to distinguish between "consciousness is involved in a process that produces temporal asymmetry" and "consciousness constitutes temporal direction."
- **Suggested response**: Clarify what "constitution" means in this context and distinguish it from weaker claims (correlation, participation, supervenience).

### The Dependence on Collapse Realism

- **Current content says**: "Only if collapse is real does consciousness have a role. Only if collapse introduces asymmetry does that role connect to temporal experience."
- **A critic would argue**: This makes the entire framework contingent on an interpretation of quantum mechanics that is currently a minority position among physicists. The article honestly acknowledges this, but the boldness of its claims (consciousness constituting time!) sits uneasily with the fragility of its foundations (collapse realism is one of several competing interpretations, and not the dominant one).
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge the contingency more prominently, perhaps in the opening summary, so readers understand the conditional nature of the claims from the start.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite the criticisms above, this article has genuine strengths worth preserving:

1. **Honest presentation of alternatives.** The article presents decoherence, time-symmetric, and MWI interpretations fairly before taking a position. It doesn't strawman competing views.

2. **Strong structural organization.** The mapping of collapse interpretations to temporal ontologies (A-theory, B-theory, complications for both) is genuinely illuminating philosophical exposition.

3. **The "What Un-Collapse Reveals" thought experiment** is a creative and effective way to sharpen the differences between interpretive positions. Each interpretation gives a distinct answer, revealing what's really at stake.

4. **Self-aware about contingency.** The article explicitly acknowledges that the framework is "speculative but not unfalsifiable" and states conditions under which it would be undermined. This is good epistemic practice, even if the falsification conditions could be sharper.

5. **The Müller et al. citation** is handled well—noting the experiment "confirms fluctuation relations rather than directly proving collapse causes time's arrow." This is appropriately cautious treatment of empirical evidence.
