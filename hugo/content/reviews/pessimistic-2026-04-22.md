# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-04-22
**Content reviewed**: [trumping-preemption](/concepts/trumping-preemption/) — newly created today (2026-04-22), ~1700 words, no prior review. The article presents Schaffer's (2000) fourth species of redundant causation as a "structural template" for dualist mental causation preserving observable physical closure.

## Executive Summary

The article is scholarly, correctly cites the central literature, and handles the primary objections honestly. The weakness is not in the exposition but in the load-bearing rhetorical move: it acknowledges that Bernstein's (2014) reductive strategy may dissolve trumping into overdetermination, acknowledges that the framework is "deliberately designed to be invisible at the trajectory level," and *still* concludes that the experience-as-authoritative-cause channel is "a genuine addition the evidence warrants." The gap between the defensive middle and the confident ending is where every critic below will press. Two further problems warrant revision: the jump from fiction-derived authority (Merlin, rank) to psychophysical laws is never bridged, and one counterfactual-dependence characterisation is technically loose.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Churchland would find the entire article to be an exercise in reifying folk-causal vocabulary. Schaffer's cases are *fiction* — "laws of magic," military *convention* — and their intuitiveness depends on our practice of attributing causation in ways that match our practices of attributing authorship, responsibility, rank, and blame. Treating this as "load-bearing" for a metaphysics of mental causation is to mistake the grammar of attribution for the structure of nature. The "authority layer" the article isolates is not an additional natural feature; it is what humans do when asked *which* of several sufficient processes they wish to credit. Churchland would add: the article needs a psychophysical law to pick experience as authoritative. Such a law is either (i) reducible to physical facts about brain state dynamics, in which case eliminativism wins, or (ii) irreducible, in which case it is a brute stipulation no more respectable than "first spell of the day." The article gestures at this worry in the "Costs of the Template" section but does not answer it.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would like parts of this article — especially the sufficiency/authority decoupling, which is adjacent to his own style of analysis. He would pick one precise fight. The article says the experience is *the* authoritative cause while the physical base is "counterfactually sufficient" but not authoritative. What, he asks, is *authority* doing here that is not purely linguistic? If two descriptions (neural-level and phenomenal-level) pick out the same causal fact, calling one "authoritative" is a stylistic choice, not a metaphysical discovery. If, instead, the phenomenal description picks out a *different* fact with its own causal work, the article owes us an account of what that work is — and the article concedes there is no trajectory-level difference. That concession empties the "authority" of causal content, at least as Dennett uses the word "cause." The article rescues itself by relying on a technical sense of "authority" drawn from Schaffer's metaphysics, but it does not *argue* that this technical sense is what we want when we ask whether consciousness causes things.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Tegmark has no direct stake in Schaffer's metaphysics, but the article ties trumping to the Map's attention–motor-planning–quantum-interface work: "Whether that authority is realised at a quantum-collapse interface … or at a higher-level psychophysical interface remains open." Tegmark would press the obvious question. If authority is picked out by a law and leaves no trajectory-level trace, the *quantum* realisation is doing no extra work — you have simply relocated the ghost to a smaller room. Either trumping makes the physics different in some detectable way (in which case show the calculation), or it does not (in which case adding a quantum-interface story is cosmetic). The article does not flag this tension between its two modes of framing: trumping as invisibility-preserving elegance vs. quantum-interface as a potential empirical signature. Those aims cut against each other.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

Deutsch would note that Schaffer's cases require a *principled* higher-order rule. "First spell of the day" is stipulated by the fictional world; rank is stipulated by a military hierarchy. In both cases, the authority-selecting rule is itself a *convention* tied to agents capable of stipulation. When the article imports this template into psychophysical metaphysics, it needs a principled rule that is not a convention — and the article does not provide one. It points to Saad's Subset Law and Delegatory Law, but these are themselves candidate rules, not a derivation. Deutsch would say: if you want dualism to be something other than anthropocentric commitment, you must articulate the authority-selecting law with the same precision you would demand of any physical law. The article concedes the debt ("there has to be a principled authority-selecting law; without one, 'trumping' collapses into brute stipulation") but does not discharge it. Deutsch would also note that MWI's critics lean on intuitions about indexical identity; he would flip this on the article — isn't the intuition that the experience rather than the base is *the* cause equally an indexical, first-person anchoring?

### The Empiricist

Popper's sharpest possible critique lives in a single sentence the article writes about itself: "Trumping offers no new empirical signature. Schaffer's structure is deliberately designed to be invisible at the trajectory level: both processes complete, and the authoritative one is picked out by a law, not a trace." The article treats this as a cost and moves on. A Popperian treats it as a verdict. If no observation could discriminate between trumping-dualism and a physicalist story with the same trajectory, the two proposals are, operationally, the same proposal — and the Map's additional commitment is a metaphysical flourish on top of a physical description that already covers the data. The article's response ("a trumping-based dualism therefore inherits this invisibility and must earn its keep on theoretical grounds") simply relocates the question to theoretical virtues, but those virtues — simplicity, explanatory economy — cut the *other* way for the physicalist. The article needs to say what the theoretical virtue of trumping-dualism *is*, in coin Popper accepts. Preserving the tenets is a commitment, not a virtue.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nāgārjuna would observe that the article's core move depends on a stable, unified *experience* whose causal authority can be picked out by a law. The article treats "the experience" as though it were a single entity with a determinate causal profile — a profile specific enough that a Subset Law can match it to the physical state. Madhyamaka analysis would dissolve this picture. Experience is not a thing with an essence; it is a dependently-arisen flow with no identity condition apart from what is imposed on it. If so, no law can select "the experience" as authoritative, because there is no bounded entity for the law to range over. The article's entire architecture — authority layer, psychophysical law, matched causal profiles — presupposes the very reification Madhyamaka denies. One need not accept Nāgārjuna's metaphysics to feel the point: the article's confidence that experiences have enough structure to be matched and selected is itself a substantial commitment the article does not argue for.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Fiction-to-nature gap is never bridged

- **File**: `concepts/trumping-preemption.md`
- **Location**: §"Schaffer's Cases" through §"Applying the Template to Mental Causation"
- **Problem**: Schaffer's cases are fictional (magic) or conventional (military rank). Both are authored by agents who stipulate the authority rule. The article imports the *structure* into psychophysical metaphysics without explaining why we should expect nature to supply a non-stipulative analogue of the "first spell of the day" rule. The "Costs of the Template" section acknowledges the need for a principled law but does not engage with the deeper worry that all Schaffer-style examples trade on our practices of authority-assignment, which are themselves intentional.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add a paragraph (perhaps at the start of §"Applying the Template") explicitly flagging the disanalogy between stipulated-law cases and natural-law cases, and noting that the argument for psychophysical authority laws must come from elsewhere (the tenets, the explanatory gap, etc.) rather than from Schaffer's structure itself. The structure shows *how* such a dualism could be articulated; it does not show that it is *warranted*. The article's final paragraph already says something close to this but lets the fiction-nature gap pass without comment.

### Issue 2: Counterfactual-dependence characterisation is loose

- **File**: `concepts/trumping-preemption.md`
- **Location**: §"Schaffer's Cases", sentence: "Counterfactual dependence fails in both directions: had Merlin not cast, Morgana's spell would have sufficed; had Morgana not cast, Merlin's would have sufficed."
- **Problem**: This is correct as written but invites a reader to miss what is actually going on in the dialectic. The standard complaint is that *each* candidate cause fails to be counterfactually necessary for the effect — not that dependence "fails in both directions" in some symmetric sense. The sentence works but a sceptical reader may take it as misstating the symmetry: in overdetermination, counterfactual dependence also fails symmetrically, so the cited feature does not distinguish trumping from overdetermination on its own.
- **Severity**: Low
- **Recommendation**: Tighten to: "Counterfactual necessity fails for both candidates (had Merlin not cast, Morgana's would have sufficed; and vice versa), yet the asymmetry in our causal judgement survives." Adds half a sentence of precision without bloating.

### Issue 3: Defensive middle, confident ending

- **File**: `concepts/trumping-preemption.md`
- **Location**: §"Relation to Site Perspective", final paragraph
- **Problem**: The article's middle sections ("Lewis Response and its Limits," "Costs of the Template") are honestly hedged, acknowledging that (a) Lewis's influence theory may accommodate the cases, (b) Bernstein may reduce trumping to overdetermination with insensitive effects, (c) there is no empirical signature, and (d) the dualist inherits an overdetermination objection if reduction succeeds. The final paragraph then states "the second channel — experience as authoritative cause — [is] a genuine addition the evidence warrants." This is a strong claim given what the article has just conceded, and readers will feel the tonal break.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either (a) soften the closing to "a genuine addition the Map's commitments require" (making the claim tenet-driven rather than evidence-driven, which is closer to what the article has actually shown), or (b) add a sentence connecting the closing confidence to specific evidential considerations not dependent on trumping itself (the hard problem, the Map's other commitments), so the reader sees the source of the confidence.

### Issue 4: Saad's work is described but one claim is reported as settled

- **File**: `concepts/trumping-preemption.md`
- **Location**: §"Applying the Template to Mental Causation", sentence: "Saad argues that only unobservable violations of physical closure occur; observable closure is preserved."
- **Problem**: Reported in the narrator's voice as Saad's claim, but the phrase "observable closure is preserved" has been contested (by Tim Crane-style challenges to the very distinction between observable and unobservable violations, and by critics who argue that "unobservable violation" is either an oxymoron or concedes that closure does not really hold). The article treats this as Saad's position rather than flagging that this is itself a philosophically contentious frame.
- **Severity**: Low
- **Recommendation**: Add a brief hedge: "Saad argues — controversially — that only unobservable violations … ; the observable/unobservable distinction itself bears weight that critics (e.g., Crane) have pressed."

## Counterarguments to Address

### Trumping collapses into early preemption or overdetermination (Bernstein 2014)

- **Current content says**: The article cites Bernstein (2014) and notes the reductive strategy, then offers Mills (1996) and Lowe (2003) as response material: "systematic psychophysical laws make any residual co-causation non-coincidental, but this is a response to a live objection rather than a refutation of it."
- **A critic would argue**: "Non-coincidental co-causation" is still overdetermination. Calling the co-causation non-coincidental (because lawful) does not change the fact that two processes are both producing the effect, which is the thing the dualist needs to avoid. The Mills/Lowe move exchanges *brute* overdetermination for *lawful* overdetermination without changing the ontology.
- **Suggested response**: The article should make the Mills/Lowe argument explicit rather than alluding to it. If the claim is that lawful overdetermination is ontologically thinner than brute overdetermination (because the two processes are not metaphysically independent), the article should say so and cite Lowe's argument that observational closure is what matters. This is a case where the article's brevity is costing it argumentative force.

### The authority/sufficiency decoupling is linguistic, not metaphysical

- **Current content says**: "This is the first worked-out philosophical model of a causal structure in which authority and sufficiency are decoupled."
- **A critic would argue**: The decoupling is a feature of the *vocabulary* of causal attribution (what we call "the cause"), not of nature. If two sufficient processes both complete, the effect has two causes; picking one as "authoritative" is stipulation whether or not a law does the stipulating.
- **Suggested response**: The article could address this by distinguishing metaphysical from attributional readings of Schaffer and explicitly taking a side. As written, it occupies both positions at once — treating trumping as a real structural feature while also deferring to law-like "authority selection" in a way that reads as stipulative.

### The "principled law" requirement is not discharged

- **Current content says**: "There has to be a principled authority-selecting law; without one, 'trumping' collapses into brute stipulation. Saad's candidate is the pair of laws summarised above."
- **A critic would argue**: Saad's Subset Law and Delegatory Law are candidate laws, not principled derivations. Without an account of why *these* laws rather than others obtain, the dualist has relocated the explanatory burden rather than lifted it.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge more explicitly that Saad's laws are proposals, not derivations, and note the open question of what would warrant them over alternatives. The article is closer to honest on this than most such pieces but stops just short.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite the critiques above, the article has real virtues worth preserving during revision:

1. **Accurate scholarship**: The Lewis-Schaffer exchange, Bernstein's reductive critique, Hitchcock's contrastive accommodation, and Saad's two-stage development are reported correctly and in the right proportions.
2. **Honest acknowledgement of costs**: §"Costs of the Template" concedes the three most serious objections (need for a principled law, Bernstein's reduction, no empirical signature). Most dualist expositions do not.

4. **Correctly distinguishes template from commitment**: The sentence "The Map treats trumping as a conceptual resource, not a settled metaphysics" correctly calibrates how much weight the Map should put on Schaffer's framework.
5. **Tenet integration is substantive, not ornamental**: Each of the four tenets gets its own concrete mapping to the trumping structure.

The fix list is short. Issues 1–4 above, plus the language tweaks, would leave the article substantially stronger without altering its core argument.
