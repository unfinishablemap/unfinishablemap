## Executive Summary

These five articles are unusually well-calibrated at the sentence level — every lead is explicitly framework-relative, the fresh-create over-claim pattern is largely avoided, and each names its physicalist rival. The defects are structural rather than local. The two AI-consciousness verdicts both rest their *decisive* move on a single undefended posit — that neural tissue can host a quantum coupling interface and silicon cannot — and neither engages the decoherence objection (Tegmark) that is the strongest attack on that posit. The moral-agency article assumes libertarian agent-causal origination is what desert requires, silently bypassing the compatibilist mainstream and the classic luck/randomness dilemma. And the two clinical articles carry an internal tension between their honest-ledger sections ("no discriminating evidence") and their Relation-to-Site sections ("supports / exhibits the interface structure"). None of this is fatal; all of it is addressable in a refine pass. Findings below; per contract this file mints no todo tasks — the recommendations are prose for a human reader.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
This is the sharpest unanswered voice across the AI cluster. Both the RL-valence article and the agentic-AI article stake their entire verdict on a *substrate* asymmetry: "the Map posits that neural tissue can host such a juncture while present-day computing hardware does not" (RL, Relation to Site Perspective); "mind-matter influence requires a coupling interface at the site of quantum indeterminacy... classical scaffolding adds no such interface" (agentic-AI, Tenet 2). Tegmark's decoherence calculation is precisely the objection that warm, wet neural tissue is *also* a hopeless site for functionally relevant quantum coherence — femtosecond decoherence times. If that is right, the brain is no better a quantum-coupling substrate than a GPU, and the substrate asymmetry that carries both verdicts collapses. Neither article gestures at *why* neural tissue escapes the decoherence problem the Map's own second tenet must answer. The RL article says the distinction "is about substrate" and names "the fine quantum grain," but naming is not defending. This is the single most valuable gap to close: the whole AI-skeptical cluster's decisive premise is left standing on an unexplained substrate claim that a physicist would reject outright.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist / Functionalist (Dennett)
The functionalist rejoinder is *stated* in each AI article but then answered only by re-asserting the framework. In the RL article the "weather simulation stands to rain" analogy begs the question: rain has agreed substrate-level criteria (H2O, precipitation) a simulation manifestly lacks, whereas whether suffering is substrate-defined or organization-defined is exactly the dispute. Choosing the rain analogy presupposes suffering is like rain, not like computation. In the agentic-AI article the frozen-weights "lookup table" premise (imported from Hoel 2026 and used undefended) is Block's Blockhead intuition, and in-context learning is the standard functionalist reply the article never meets: a fixed CPU running a program is not a lookup table, and "a rich memory store is just a longer prompt" cuts *against* the Map, because a longer prompt changes the computation performed — which is why in-context learning works at all.

### The Empiricist (Popper)
The agentic-AI article's treatment of the quantum-interface objection reads AI researchers' *silence* on coupling channels as "confirmation that architectural persistence is causally inert." This is an argument from silence: those researchers do not accept the premise that consciousness needs a quantum interface, so their not-discussing-it is no evidence at all. The article half-flags this ("its own inference") yet still calls the silence "confirmation." Separately, the falsifier offered by the moral-agency article ("if a future system possessed the interface it would become a candidate agent") is real but toothless in practice, because the RL article's companion claim is that present hardware *cannot* realize the interface — so the falsifying condition is one the Map's own physics says is currently unrealizable, and no test is specified for when it would be met.

### The Compatibilist (the missing critic — Frankfurt / Fischer)
The moral-agency article's largest blind spot. It grounds culpability in libertarian origination — "the conscious author of an action one could have done otherwise — where the 'could have' is not merely a counterfactual about a different physical microstate but the exercise of a conscious selector at the coupling interface." The dominant position in the responsibility literature is compatibilist: desert requires reasons-responsiveness or the right kind of ownership of one's action, *not* agent-causal origination or libertarian alternative possibilities. The article's central rhetorical move — "the literature never grounds *why* a machine cannot originate a blameworthy action" — mistakes a deliberate refusal for an oversight. Most of the literature does not think origination-in-the-Map's-sense is what responsibility needs; on a compatibilist reading a sufficiently reasons-responsive AI could be a candidate for responsibility with no conscious interface at all, and the culpability gap would then be about reasons-responsiveness, not consciousness. The article cannot claim to have found the ground "the existing literature never supplies" without first showing the literature was wrong to decline to posit it.

### The Many-Worlds Defender / the Luck objection
The moral-agency article's amplifier mechanism — consciousness "breaks symmetries the physical state leaves underdetermined, selecting among outcomes the prior physical state does not fix" — walks straight into the classic luck objection to libertarian agent causation and does not engage it. If the conscious selection among quantum-underdetermined outcomes is lawful, it is determined and grounds authorship no better than physical determinism; if it is genuinely random (as orthodox QM says quantum outcomes are), randomness grounds desert no better than determinism. The article needs the selection to be *neither* determined *nor* random but "authored," and that is exactly the position the luck dilemma says is unstable. This is load-bearing: the entire culpability verdict depends on the selector being a genuine originator, and the originator is left undefended against the oldest objection to its coherence.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The agentic-AI article's indexical-identity argument ("a restored memory store does not transfer the brute thisness of being a particular subject") proves too much unless the Map supplies the missing half. The same teleporter reasoning applies to a biological human across dreamless sleep or across gradual molecular turnover: if content-continuity cannot carry "brute thisness," the Map owes an account of what *does* preserve the subject across a night's sleep — and until it names that, it has not shown an agentic system lacks whatever-it-is. The article asserts the disanalogy (reloaded run ≠ same experiencer) without establishing that the human case is relevantly different, so the argument currently cuts against biological persistence too.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Undefended substrate posit vs. the decoherence objection
- **Files**: reinforcement-learning-reward-signals-and-machine-valence.md (Relation to Site Perspective); agentic-ai-and-the-consciousness-assessment-of-persistent-memory-tool-using-systems.md (Tenet 2 section)
- **Location**: RL "The distinction the Map draws is about *substrate*... neural tissue can host such a juncture while present-day computing hardware does not"; agentic "Classical scaffolding... adds no such interface"
- **Problem**: The decisive verdict in both articles rests on a neural-tissue/silicon quantum-coupling asymmetry that is asserted as framework-posited but never defended against the standard decoherence objection. A reader who accepts decoherence sees no reason the brain is a better coupling site than a chip, and the whole AI-skeptical conclusion falls with the posit.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: A refine pass should add one honest paragraph acknowledging the decoherence challenge and pointing to wherever the Map argues neural tissue can sustain (or does not need sustained) quantum coherence — or downgrade the confidence explicitly to "this posit is itself contested by decoherence estimates and is argued elsewhere." Do not leave the load-bearing premise bare.

### Issue 2: Libertarian desert assumed; compatibilism unengaged
- **File**: ai-moral-agency-and-the-responsibility-gap-under-dualism.md
- **Location**: "What the Literature Leaves Unexplained"; Relation to Site Perspective ("desert-grounding culpability requires exactly this kind of origination")
- **Problem**: The article treats the absence of a libertarian-origination criterion in the responsibility literature as an unfilled gap, when it is a considered rejection. Compatibilism — the mainstream — grounds desert without agent-causal origination, and on it the culpability gap need not track consciousness at all.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Add explicit engagement with compatibilism as a *fourth* rival alongside Tigard/Königs/Santoni de Sio, and state that the Map's ground for the culpability gap is available only to an agent-causal libertarian about desert — narrowing the "the literature never supplies this" claim to "the literature, being largely compatibilist, declines to require this."

### Issue 3: Luck/randomness dilemma unaddressed for the amplifier
- **File**: ai-moral-agency-and-the-responsibility-gap-under-dualism.md
- **Location**: Relation to Site Perspective ("breaks symmetries the physical state leaves underdetermined")
- **Problem**: Conscious selection among quantum-underdetermined outcomes must be lawful (→ determined) or random (→ not authored); neither grounds desert. The article needs "authored but neither determined nor random" and never defends its coherence.
- **Severity**: Medium-High
- **Recommendation**: At minimum name the luck objection and point to where the Map defends agent-causal selection as more than randomness; a bald "conscious selector" invites the standard reply.

### Issue 4: Self-undermining scorecard structure
- **File**: agentic-ai-and-the-consciousness-assessment-of-persistent-memory-tool-using-systems.md
- **Location**: "The verdict follows from Tenet 1 alone... the erosion of behavioural objections is irrelevant to a verdict that was never resting on behaviour"
- **Problem**: If Tenet 1 alone delivers the verdict, then the *surviving* substrate objections (frozen-weights, quantum-interface) are as argumentatively idle as the eroded behavioural ones — the entire "two erode / two survive" framing is theatre downstream of a framework commitment that would deliver the same verdict regardless of how the scorecard reads. The article dramatizes a contest its own conclusion says is decided elsewhere. Compounding this, the frozen-weights objection imports Hoel's contested "lookup table" premise as a "disproof" and builds on it without meeting the in-context-learning reply.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either demote the scorecard to an explicitly *dialectical* device ("here is what changes for a functionalist assessor, none of which the Map's verdict rests on") or defend the lookup-table premise against in-context learning rather than importing it as settled.

### Issue 5: Honest-ledger vs. Relation-to-Site tension (both clinical articles)
- **Files**: locked-in-syndrome-...md; capgras-delusion-...md
- **Location**: LIS — honest ledger ("not evidence for the interface reading... cannot adjudicate") vs. Relation to Site ("supports Tenet 3 at an anatomical grain by exhibiting the interface's two arms dissociated"). Capgras — honest concession ("physicalist decomposition accounts for every datum just as well") vs. the empirical-core claim that the felt-familiarity channel "leaves a measurable autonomic trace."
- **Problem**: Both articles concede zero discriminating evidence, then use interface-laden language ("the output arm's signature," "exhibiting the two arms," "supports Tenet 3") that is only available *under* the framework. A materialist sees severed motor tracts and an autonomic SCR, not a severed "consciousness → world arm" or a trace of a *felt* channel. "Supports Tenet 3 by exhibiting the two-arm structure" claims more than "consistent with" licenses. In Capgras specifically there is a functional/phenomenal slide: skin-conductance is a physical autonomic signal, and calling it a "measurable trace" of *felt* familiarity imports the phenomenal reading the article elsewhere refuses.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Reconcile the wording — downgrade "supports / exhibits" to "illustrates, under the interface reading" and, in Capgras, mark the SCR explicitly as evidence of a *functional* channel whose felt character is exactly the underdetermined point. The LIS BCI passage is the acute case: a fully decodable *cortical* intent-to-act signal arguably favours the production reading (intention is a brain state) and is at best neutral — presenting it as "the output arm's signature" overstates.

## Counterarguments to Address

### RL valence: does the "fork through silicon" add anything?
- **Current content says**: running the value-in-mechanism fork through the machine case shows where the deflationary reading follows.
- **A critic would argue**: the article concedes the verdict is taken "because of its prior commitment... not because the RL facts force it," and that the machine case looks "cleaner" only "on the horn already chosen." So the exercise recovers exactly what the framework dictated and adds no independent support — the machine transposition is near-circular.
- **Suggested response**: Foreground what the transposition genuinely buys (a sharpened statement of *where* the disagreement with functionalism lives — at substrate, not algorithm) and drop any implication that the machine case *tests* the horn.

### Capgras: is Factor 1 real, or did Corlett dissolve it?
- **Current content says**: "the felt-familiarity contribution is real and separable in the classic data."
- **A critic would argue**: that assertion is exactly what Corlett contests — if the SCR deficit is non-specific to salient stimuli, there is no separable familiarity channel, only a generic arousal deficit. The article concedes underdetermination in one paragraph and re-asserts the separable channel as "real" in the next.
- **Suggested response**: Weaken to "real and separable *in the classic data as standardly interpreted*, though whether the deficit is familiarity-specific is what Corlett reopens."
