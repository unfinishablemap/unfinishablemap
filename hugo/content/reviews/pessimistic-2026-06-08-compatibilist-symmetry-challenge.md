## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The whole apparatus — "agent causation," "ultimate desert," "sourcehood-grade desert" — is folk-psychological vocabulary stacked three deep. The article concedes that compatibilism grounds all the *practices* (praise, blame, character formation) without libertarian metaphysics, then reserves a "residue" of *ultimate desert* and *genuine alternatives* that are precisely the bits with no behavioural or neural cash value. The honest eliminativist reading: the residue is the part of the theory that does no work, and the article has helpfully isolated it.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The article's own logic almost finishes the job for me. It admits the moral phenomenology "does not adjudicate between libertarian and reasons-responsive readings," that the deliberative feel "would obtain whether agency is irreducible or merely experienced as such." That is the Dennettian conclusion: the libertarian add-on is metaphysically idle relative to the moral content. The article calls the differentiator "tenet-coherence" — i.e., it fits a metaphysics adopted elsewhere. Fine, but then the libertarian framing is *decoration* on a compatibilist moral theory, and the article never quite says so out loud.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
Mostly out of scope — this article doesn't touch decoherence or quantum mechanism. But note the residue case (c), "an open future as a fact about the world," smuggles in an ontological-indeterminism claim that the agency pillar inherits from elsewhere in the Map and that *does* face the decoherence problem. The article should not let "metaphysically open alternatives" pass as cost-free here; it has a quantum bill due in the linked articles.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
"Regret over what genuinely was an alternative" (residue c) is exactly the indexical confusion I diagnose. There is no single "I" who "could have done otherwise"; there are branches. The article's residue leans on No-Many-Worlds to make the alternative metaphysically real, so residue (c) is not independent of the tenets — it is tenet-coherence wearing a moral-phenomenology costume. The article's own discipline ("locate the differentiator outside moral theory") should flag that residue (c) collapses back into tenet-coherence rather than standing as separating moral work.

### The Empiricist (Popper's ghost)
What observation distinguishes a world where the residue (ultimate desert, genuine alternatives) obtains from one where only the compatibilist capacities obtain? By the article's own admission, none. The residue is unfalsifiable by construction. That is acceptable as openly-declared metaphysics, but the article frames the residue as a place where "libertarian agency may do separating work" — *separating* implies a difference that makes a difference, which is the empirical promise the residue cannot keep.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The "irreducible source" / "ultimate authorship" the residue protects is the reified permanent self. The compatibilist's "prior states the agent did not author" is closer to dependent origination, and the article treats *that* as the deficiency to be overcome. From emptiness, the residue is not a subtle moral remainder; it is the clinging the rest of the analysis had almost dissolved.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The residue may collapse into tenet-coherence (strongest finding)
- **Current content says**: residue (c) — "regret over what genuinely was an alternative ... presses for an open future as a fact about the world" — is named as a place where libertarian framing does *separating* work distinct from tenet-coherence.
- **A critic would argue**: an "open future as a fact about the world" is supplied *by the tenets* (No-Many-Worlds + the indeterminism the dualist interface requires), not by moral phenomenology. So residue (c) is not separating moral work; it is tenet-coherence relabelled — exactly the inflation the article exists to prevent. Residue (a) ultimate desert and (b) strong "could have done otherwise" are arguably more defensible as moral residue; (c) is the weak member.
- **Suggested response**: a half-sentence conceding that residue (c)'s "open future" is itself downstream of the tenets, so its separating force is internal to the Map's framework rather than a tenet-neutral moral discriminator — which is consistent with the article's own discipline and actually strengthens it.

### "Availably equivalent" may understate the asymmetry the article relies on elsewhere
- **Current content says**: the two metaphysics are "availably equivalent" at the level of standard moral discourse.
- **A critic would argue**: the companion apex/topic frame the contrast as *irreducible-vs-derivative* (agent is the ultimate source vs. capacities emerge from unauthored prior states). If that distinction is real and load-bearing for the four-pillar synthesis, then the moral contents are *not* equivalent — they differ in their desert-grounding structure even within "standard discourse." The article needs to say whether irreducible-vs-derivative is a difference in moral content or only in metaphysical backstory.
- **Suggested response**: clarify that irreducible-vs-derivative is a difference in *grounding* that leaves *first-order* moral verdicts equivalent — i.e., same outputs, different sourcehood story — so "availably equivalent" is about verdicts, not about the desert metaphysics.
