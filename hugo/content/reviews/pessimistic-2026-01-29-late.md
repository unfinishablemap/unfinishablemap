## Executive Summary

This cluster addresses three topics where the Map extends its framework into contested empirical and theoretical territory: altered states of consciousness (lucid dreaming), critique of a major competing theory (IIT), and a speculative metaphysical framework (simulation hypothesis). The articles are philosophically sophisticated and engage seriously with empirical literature. The lucid dreaming article draws on 2025 Demirel study findings to argue for distinct neural signatures of lucidity; the IIT critique identifies genuine weaknesses in Tononi's framework; the simulation article thoughtfully explores how the hypothesis relates to mind-matter interaction.

However, the cluster shares a systematic vulnerability: each article presents the Map's framework as escaping problems that critics would argue apply equally to the Map itself. The lucid dreaming article's argument for bidirectional interaction from dream control faces the same interpretation problem as all introspection-based evidence—feeling like you're causing something doesn't establish that phenomenal properties do causal work. The IIT critique's "passivity problem" rebounds: the Map claims consciousness is causally efficacious, but specifies no mechanism that doesn't face analogous objections. The simulation article treats the hypothesis as supporting the Map's tenets when it actually remains neutral between physicalism and dualism—a sufficiently sophisticated simulation could implement consciousness physically. The cluster would benefit from acknowledging that the Map faces structurally similar challenges to those it raises against competitors.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Patricia Churchland)

"Three articles building a case for dualism through altered states, theoretical critique, and metaphysical speculation. Let me trace the reasoning.

The lucid dreaming article claims that 'becoming lucid adds something further—proof that consciousness isn't simply identical to whatever brain activity happens to be occurring.' But what exactly is added? Metacognitive activation. Prefrontal engagement. Enhanced gamma connectivity. The article documents these neural correlates in detail—then leaps to the conclusion that something non-physical must be involved. Why? Because the article assumes consciousness is non-physical, then interprets the neural evidence through that lens.

The 2025 Demirel study shows that lucid dreaming has distinctive neural signatures. Wonderful—this means lucid dreaming is a brain state, detectable through EEG. The article treats this as supporting dualism because the state 'differs from both ordinary REM and waking.' But differentiable brain states don't imply non-physical components. We have distinctive neural signatures for meditation, for flow states, for hypnosis. These are all brain states with different functional profiles. Nothing about lucid dreaming's neural signature requires positing consciousness as something beyond those neural patterns.

The IIT critique is more substantive—Tononi's framework does face serious problems. But the 'passivity problem' the article identifies cuts both ways. The article asks: if consciousness is identical to integrated information, how can it 'do' anything beyond what the physics does? Fair question. But turn it around: if consciousness is non-physical, how can it 'do' anything to the physical? The article's response—quantum selection—faces all the problems the article raises against IIT's identity theory. At least IIT specifies what consciousness *is* (integrated information). The Map specifies what consciousness *does* (quantum selection) without specifying what it is.

The simulation article is the most revealing. It argues that simulation would make mind-matter interaction 'unsurprising' because simulations 'routinely support bidirectional interaction between conscious operators and simulated physics.' But the conscious operators in our simulations are physical beings—humans with brains. The simulation hypothesis doesn't establish that non-physical consciousness could interface with simulated physics. It establishes that physical consciousness can interface with simulations. This is evidence *for* physicalism about consciousness, not against it.

The article's treatment of illusionism is instructive: 'simulating an illusion that depends on *seeming* requires implementing whatever makes seeming possible.' But illusionism doesn't deny that seeming occurs—it denies that seeming requires phenomenal properties. Functional states can seem without genuine phenomenality. The simulation framework actually supports this: we could simulate a system that represents itself as having phenomenal properties without the simulation itself being phenomenal. The regress the article claims doesn't arise."

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Daniel Dennett)

"The lucid dreaming article builds an elaborate case for bidirectional interaction from dream control. The argument: lucid dreamers exert 'causal influence over dream content through intention.' They can execute pre-agreed actions while remaining asleep. Therefore consciousness is 'not epiphenomenal observation but causal participation.'

This argument confuses levels. When a lucid dreamer intends to fly and then dreams of flying, what caused the dream content to change? The dreamer's brain, which generated both the intention and the dream. The intention is a brain state; the dream content is another brain state; brain states cause brain states. Where does non-physical consciousness enter?

The article anticipates this: 'Even if attention is "merely" a brain process, the phenomenology of effortful monitoring—the felt sense of watching oneself—must appear *to* something.' But this is the classic homunculus fallacy dressed up in phenomenological language. There doesn't need to be a *something* to whom appearances appear. There are just information states causing other information states, including states that represent themselves as appearances to a subject. The subject is part of the representation, not something outside receiving it.

The IIT critique raises the 'temporal gap'—IIT calculates phi for instants, but consciousness extends through time. The article claims the Map's quantum approach 'handles this differently' because 'if consciousness involves collapse processes, each collapse has duration.' But this is hand-waving. Quantum collapse (if it exists) is instantaneous in standard interpretations. Decoherence is effectively instantaneous at biological temperatures. Where does the experienced duration come from? The Map hasn't explained this any better than IIT—it's just gestured at different unexplained mechanisms.

The simulation article claims the hypothesis 'provides precedent for consciousness-physics interaction.' But precedent in what sense? In simulations we create, consciousness interacts with simulated physics through *physical mechanisms*—keyboard inputs, controller signals, neural interfaces. The simulation hypothesis doesn't establish that non-physical consciousness could interact with simulated physics. It establishes that *embodied* consciousness can interact with *representations* of physics. These are different things.

More troubling: the article acknowledges that 'a simulation running on physical substrate is still physical—the computer exists in base reality.' So even if we're simulated, the simulation runs on physical stuff. Consciousness in the simulation would be patterns in that physical stuff. The simulation hypothesis, properly understood, is a form of physicalism: consciousness is computation, and computation is physical. The article's dualist spin requires the additional assumption that consciousness operates at the 'substrate level' rather than the simulation level—but this is asserted, not argued."

### The Quantum Skeptic (Max Tegmark)

"The lucid dreaming article proposes that 'dream control might operate through mechanisms analogous to the quantum Zeno effect—sustained attention stabilising certain neural patterns against decoherence.' Let me assess this claim.

The quantum Zeno effect requires repeated measurements at intervals shorter than the system's evolution time. For the effect to stabilise neural patterns, measurements would need to occur at nanosecond or faster timescales. But neural firing operates at millisecond timescales. What performs these ultra-rapid measurements?

The article says consciousness does—through 'sustained attention.' But attention is a slow cognitive process operating at timescales of hundreds of milliseconds. Attention cannot perform the rapid measurements the Zeno effect requires. The timescales are off by at least six orders of magnitude.

The article acknowledges this obliquely: 'The incomplete reliability of dream control fits this model: consciousness influences outcomes within the margin quantum indeterminacy allows, but cannot override the brain's bulk classical dynamics.' But this is post-hoc accommodation. When dream control works, quantum selection explains it. When dream control fails, classical dynamics explain it. The framework is unfalsifiable because it can accommodate any outcome.

The IIT critique raises the temporal problem but offers no quantum solution. It says the Map's approach 'may be speculative' but 'addresses what IIT ignores.' Speculation that addresses a problem is still speculation. The article claims that 'if retrocausality operates, past and future constrain the present in ways that generate genuine temporal extension.' But retrocausality is even more speculative than quantum consciousness. Invoking one speculative mechanism to support another doesn't strengthen the position—it compounds the speculation.

The simulation article's treatment of quantum mechanics is more careful. It notes that 'if the simulation implements quantum mechanics faithfully, consciousness would still need to interface with quantum indeterminacy at appropriate timescales.' This is correct—the simulation hypothesis doesn't dissolve the decoherence objection. But then the article claims the simulation framework 'suggests such an interface was *designed*.' This is theological reasoning: we don't understand how the interface works, so someone must have designed it to work. This explains nothing; it relocates the mystery to a designer.

The cluster's quantum appeals share a pattern: invoke quantum mechanics when arguing against competitors (IIT ignores temporal extension; physical closure has quantum gaps), then retreat from quantum specifics when challenged on mechanism (the Map's quantum framework is 'speculative'; the simulation might have been 'designed'). This is not scientific reasoning."

### The Many-Worlds Defender (David Deutsch)

"The simulation article claims the hypothesis provides 'an alternative to many-worlds that explains quantum indeterminacy: The simulator calculates one outcome per measurement. Other "branches" are simply not computed.' This is interesting but faces problems the article doesn't address.

First, simulating quantum mechanics without computing all branches requires choosing which branch to actualise. What makes this choice? The article says 'consciousness' or 'the simulator.' But if the simulator is a quantum computer (the most plausible way to simulate quantum physics), it faces the same branching structure. The simulation hypothesis doesn't escape MWI; it pushes the question to the substrate level.

Second, the article concedes: 'If faithfully simulating quantum mechanics requires quantum computation, and quantum computation implies branching, then MWI may apply within the simulation.' This is the crux. Classical computers cannot efficiently simulate quantum systems (unless P=BQP, which is unlikely). Quantum computers can—but quantum computers operate according to the same quantum mechanics, including superposition. The simulation hypothesis faces a dilemma: either the substrate is classical (and can't simulate quantum physics) or quantum (and faces MWI at the substrate level).

The article's response—'The hypothesis is most powerful when paired with objective collapse interpretations at the substrate level'—concedes the point. The simulation hypothesis doesn't resolve the MWI question; it depends on assuming the answer.

The lucid dreaming article's treatment of MWI is more direct but equally problematic. It claims that 'the phenomenology of foreclosure—of possibilities genuinely closing rather than continuing elsewhere—supports collapse interpretations where selection is real.' But MWI doesn't deny the phenomenology of foreclosure. Within each branch, the observer experiences one outcome and perceives the others as not taken. The subjective experience of choosing is identical on MWI and on collapse interpretations—both produce observers who perceive single outcomes.

The article claims the lucid dreamer 'experiences the non-flying option as genuinely not taken, not as branching off into another self.' But how would this experience differ if branching occurred? The article provides no criterion. It treats phenomenology as evidence for metaphysics without establishing that the phenomenology distinguishes between competing metaphysics.

The IIT critique notes that the Map 'preserves indexical identity' by rejecting MWI. But indexical identity is preserved within each MWI branch. The question 'why am I this instance rather than another?' has the same answer on both interpretations: because you're the one having this experience right now. On MWI, other instances exist; on collapse, they don't. But your identity as *this* experiencer is equally determinate either way."

### The Empiricist (Karl Popper's Ghost)

"Let me assess the falsifiability structure of each article.

**Lucid Dreaming**: The article includes a 'What Would Challenge This View?' section listing five conditions. Let me examine them.

(1) 'Neural correlates prove sufficient': If neural signatures were shown to be constitutive rather than correlated, the case would weaken. This is a genuine falsifier—but note it requires proving a negative (that nothing beyond neural patterns exists), which is extremely difficult.

(2) 'Lucidity without phenomenal change': If accurate dream-awareness occurred without qualitative shift, the link would weaken. This is testable in principle but hard to operationalise—how do we detect 'phenomenal change' independently of reports?

(3) 'Dream control perfectly reliable': Oddly, the article says this would *complicate* the framework by removing brain autonomy. So partial control supports dualism; complete control... also needs explaining. This is not a falsifier but a win-win for the theory.

(4) 'Artificial induction of lucidity': If pure physical intervention induced lucidity without training, this would suggest the state is 'fully determined by brain activity.' This is the strongest falsifier—but note the article already discusses that electrical stimulation can induce lucidity-like experiences. The threshold for falsification is unclear.

(5) 'Illusionism vindicated': If illusionism explained the phenomenological shift, the case would dissolve. But this makes falsifiability depend on a philosophical debate, not empirical findings.

**IIT Critique**: The article doesn't include a falsifiability section—it's critiquing IIT, not defending a positive claim. But the positive alternative (quantum selection) inherits the falsifiability problems the article raises against IIT. 'We cannot see why integrated information should feel like anything'—we also cannot see why quantum selection should produce feeling. The critique applies reflexively.

**Simulation**: The article's 'What Would Challenge This View?' section lists five conditions, but most are about whether the hypothesis is *true*, not whether the Map's use of it is valid. The section conflates 'the simulation hypothesis might be wrong' with 'the simulation hypothesis doesn't support dualism.' Even if we're not simulated, the framework's *possibility* still illuminates contingency—so the hypothesis can't be falsified by evidence that we're not simulated.

The cluster's falsifiability pattern: lucid dreaming evidence is interpreted through a dualist lens regardless of findings. The IIT critique doesn't defend an alternative that's more falsifiable. The simulation hypothesis is explicitly presented as a 'conceptual tool' rather than a testable claim. The overall position—that consciousness is non-physical and causally efficacious—remains unfalsifiable because each article reinterprets evidence to fit rather than specifying what would disconfirm."

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)

"The lucid dreaming article claims that 'becoming lucid adds something further' to the dream state—specifically, a metacognitive capacity where 'the dreamer becomes aware not just of dream content but of themselves *as the one dreaming*.' This presupposes what Buddhist practice investigates: is there a self to become aware of?

When I enter the lucid state and investigate the sense of 'I am dreaming,' what do I find? A thought arises: 'this is a dream.' A sense of lucidity arises. But do I find the *one* who is dreaming, or just the *sense* of being one who dreams? These are different. The sense of selfhood is another experience, not an experiencer behind experience.

The article invokes 'witness consciousness' from Tibetan traditions: 'the witness remains while the witnessed transforms.' But this is a particular interpretation. Other contemplatives report that when witness consciousness is investigated, the witness is not found. There is witnessing—but no witness separate from the witnessing. The lucidity that arises in dreams is not *owned* by a self; it arises dependently like all other dream content.

The article's process philosophy section acknowledges this partially: 'the dreamer grasps "this might not be waking reality" as a genuine alternative.' Whitehead's framework does allow for selfless prehension—actual occasions don't require a substantial self. But the article then claims lucidity 'feels like *recognition* rather than *inference*,' implying direct acquaintance with one's own nature. Direct acquaintance with *what*? If there is no substantial self, what is directly acquainted?

The IIT critique objects that IIT makes consciousness 'passive'—a property of structures rather than an agent that shapes outcomes. But Buddhism also denies that consciousness is an agent. There is no self wielding consciousness like a tool. There are just processes of experience arising dependently. The article assumes agency is a feature consciousness must have; Buddhist analysis suggests agency is another experience arising within consciousness, not consciousness's intrinsic nature.

The simulation article explores 'where consciousness resides'—in the simulation, at the substrate, or both. But Buddhist analysis questions the metaphor of residence. Consciousness doesn't reside anywhere; it arises dependently when conditions are present. Whether those conditions are simulated or 'base' reality, consciousness would arise dependently in either case. The simulation hypothesis doesn't address the question Buddhism poses: what is the nature of awareness when investigated directly?

The cluster treats consciousness as something—a substance, a property, a causal agent—that exists and acts. Buddhist investigation reveals no such thing when looked for directly. There is experiencing, but no experiencer over and above the experiences. The Map's framework reifies what contemplative investigation reveals as empty."

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Dream Control Evidence Doesn't Establish Phenomenal Causation

- **File**: `topics/lucid-dreaming-and-consciousness.md`
- **Location**: Lines 89-98
- **Problem**: The article claims dream control provides 'direct support' for bidirectional interaction because 'lucid dreamers exert causal influence over dream content through intention.' But this conflates two claims: (1) something called 'intention' causally affects dream content, and (2) phenomenal consciousness specifically does causal work. The epiphenomenalist accepts (1)—functional intention-states cause changes—while denying (2). The article provides no evidence that phenomenal properties rather than functional states do the causal work. The neural evidence (gamma connectivity, prefrontal activation) is entirely consistent with functional states causing both the phenomenology and the dream-content changes via common causation.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge that dream control evidence is consistent with both interactionism and sophisticated epiphenomenalism. The evidence shows that brain states associated with metacognition affect dream content; it doesn't establish that the associated phenomenology has independent causal power. The article should distinguish between 'intention causes' and 'phenomenal intention causes.'

### Issue 2: IIT Passivity Objection Applies Reflexively

- **File**: `topics/integrated-information-theory-critique.md`
- **Location**: Lines 35-44
- **Problem**: The article claims IIT renders consciousness 'causally inert' because identifying consciousness with integrated information means 'physics alone does all the causal work.' But the Map's alternative—consciousness selecting quantum outcomes—faces a parallel objection. If consciousness operates only at quantum indeterminacies (to satisfy causal closure elsewhere), then physics plus quantum chance determines all outcomes, and consciousness merely accompanies the selection. What does 'selection' add beyond the quantum probability distribution? The article claims IIT is 'epiphenomenalism by another name' without acknowledging that critics make the same charge against quantum selection theories.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: The critique should acknowledge that the passivity objection is generic to theories that try to locate consciousness's causal role. IIT places consciousness as identical to certain physical structures; the Map places it as selecting among quantum alternatives. Both face the question: what does consciousness *add* beyond what the physical substrate provides? The critique would be stronger if it showed why selection escapes the passivity charge rather than assuming it does.

### Issue 3: Simulation Hypothesis Is Neutral Between Physicalism and Dualism

- **File**: `concepts/simulation.md`
- **Location**: Lines 79-98
- **Problem**: The article presents the simulation hypothesis as supporting the Map's tenets, particularly dualism and bidirectional interaction. But the simulation hypothesis is actually neutral. A sufficiently sophisticated simulation could implement consciousness physically—consciousness could be identical to certain computational states within the simulation, satisfying physicalism. The article argues that simulation 'creates conceptual room for consciousness to be something genuinely different from simulated matter.' But simulated matter *is* computation, and consciousness could be certain computations without being 'different' from them. The conceptual room the article claims is assumed, not established by the hypothesis.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge that the simulation hypothesis is compatible with both physicalism and dualism about consciousness. If consciousness is identical to certain computational patterns, the simulation implements physical consciousness. If consciousness is something beyond computation, the simulation raises questions about where that something interfaces. The hypothesis doesn't adjudicate; it rephrases the question.

### Issue 4: Quantum Zeno Mechanism Faces Timescale Mismatch

- **File**: `topics/lucid-dreaming-and-consciousness.md`
- **Location**: Lines 159-160
- **Problem**: The article proposes that dream control might operate through quantum Zeno effect mechanisms—'sustained attention stabilising certain neural patterns against decoherence.' But the quantum Zeno effect requires measurements at timescales faster than the system's evolution. Attention operates at hundreds of milliseconds; quantum decoherence in neural systems occurs at femtoseconds to microseconds. The timescale mismatch is at least six orders of magnitude. The article doesn't address how 'sustained attention' (slow cognitive process) could perform the rapid measurements the Zeno effect requires.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either remove the quantum Zeno speculation from the lucid dreaming article or acknowledge the timescale problem explicitly. The article could note that while the Zeno effect is a proposed mechanism for quantum consciousness generally, its application to dream control faces significant challenges that remain unresolved. As currently written, the passage suggests a mechanism that doesn't work on the relevant timescales.

### Issue 5: MWI Rejection Arguments Don't Distinguish Phenomenologies

- **File**: `topics/lucid-dreaming-and-consciousness.md`, `concepts/simulation.md`
- **Location**: lucid-dreaming:163-164, simulation:116-118
- **Problem**: Both articles claim that the 'phenomenology of foreclosure'—experiencing choices as genuinely closing alternatives—supports collapse interpretations over MWI. But MWI predicts identical phenomenology within each branch. When I choose to fly in a lucid dream, the 'me' in this branch experiences the non-flying option as not taken. In other branches, other mes chose differently. The phenomenology of selection is identical whether branching occurs or not. The articles don't provide a criterion for distinguishing 'genuinely foreclosed' from 'branched but locally foreclosed.'
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Either develop an argument for why the phenomenology differs between interpretations or acknowledge that phenomenology doesn't adjudicate. The current argument relies on intuitions about what foreclosure 'really' is—but both interpretations predict the same experience. The Map's MWI rejection should rest on other grounds (perhaps the indexical identity argument) rather than on phenomenology that's interpretation-neutral.

### Issue 6: Process Philosophy Interpretation Contested

- **File**: `topics/lucid-dreaming-and-consciousness.md`
- **Location**: Lines 132-140
- **Problem**: The article presents Whitehead's process philosophy as supporting the Map's interpretation of lucid dreaming—'the transition to lucidity involves a distinctive kind of prehension: the dream experience prehends *itself as dream*.' But process philosophy doesn't require dualism; Whitehead's framework is often interpreted panpsychistically, with experience as a feature of all actual occasions, not a non-physical addition. The article treats process philosophy as supporting dualist conclusions when process philosophy is actually compatible with multiple interpretations, including physicalist panpsychism.
- **Severity**: Low
- **Recommendation**: Note that process philosophy is compatible with multiple metaphysical interpretations. Whitehead's framework illuminates the structure of experience without adjudicating between dualism and panpsychism. The article should present process philosophy as offering conceptual tools rather than as endorsing the Map's dualism specifically.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The Common Causation Response to Dream Control

- **Current content says**: Dream control demonstrates bidirectional interaction—consciousness causally influences dream content.
- **A critic would argue**: The brain generates both the intention and the dream content. Intention-states (functional states correlated with phenomenology) cause changes in dream-generating regions. The phenomenology is epiphenomenal; the functional state does the causal work. No evidence distinguishes phenomenal causation from common causation.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge the common causation interpretation is possible. Argue that the best explanation for why intention reliably changes dream content is that the phenomenal aspect of intention—not just its functional profile—has causal efficacy. The phenomenology provides information about what the dreamer wants; information processing is most naturally understood as involving the informational content (phenomenal) not just the physical vehicle (functional). This doesn't prove interactionism but provides reasons to prefer it.

### The Reflexive Passivity Objection

- **Current content says**: IIT makes consciousness passive because identifying consciousness with integrated information means consciousness doesn't 'do' anything beyond what physics does.
- **A critic would argue**: The Map's quantum selection faces the same objection. If consciousness operates at quantum indeterminacies, it selects among alternatives that quantum mechanics makes equally probable. But selection that preserves probability distributions is causally inert—it doesn't change what physics predicts. Consciousness 'selecting' a quantum outcome is indistinguishable from physics plus chance producing that outcome.
- **Suggested response**: Distinguish between type-level and token-level causation. At the type level, consciousness doesn't change statistical regularities—Born rule predictions remain valid. At the token level, consciousness determines *which* particular outcome actualises. This matters for agency: the specific pattern of outcomes determines the course of my life, even if the statistical distribution remains unchanged. Token-level determination is genuine causation even without type-level deviation.

### The Simulation Physicalism Response

- **Current content says**: The simulation hypothesis supports dualism by showing that mind-matter interaction is 'architecturally possible' in simulated contexts.
- **A critic would argue**: In simulations we create, conscious operators are physical beings interacting with representations. The simulation hypothesis shows that physical consciousness can interface with simulated physics, not that non-physical consciousness can. If we're simulated, consciousness in the simulation is computational—patterns in the simulator's substrate. This is physicalism, not dualism.
- **Suggested response**: The simulation hypothesis is neutral between physicalism and dualism about consciousness within the simulation. What it establishes is that the *physical laws of the simulation* are contingent—they could have been otherwise. Causal closure within the simulation doesn't preclude interaction with the substrate level. This doesn't prove dualism but removes one objection to it: the simulation framework shows that apparent causal closure can coexist with non-physical influences from a more fundamental level.

## Strengths (Brief)

Despite criticisms:

1. **Empirical grounding**: The lucid dreaming article draws substantively on 2025 neuroscience findings (Demirel study, Max Planck structural research, Biorxiv microstate analysis). The empirical detail strengthens the philosophical argument by anchoring it in current science.

2. **Honest about limitations**: The IIT critique fairly presents what IIT gets right (taking phenomenology seriously, rejecting functionalism, empirical engagement). The simulation article acknowledges the hypothesis is 'not established fact' but a 'conceptual tool.'

3. **Multiple philosophical frameworks**: The lucid dreaming article draws on predictive processing, process philosophy, and contemplative traditions. The simulation article engages Buddhist, process, and analytic perspectives. This breadth prevents the appearance of cherry-picking supporting frameworks.

4. **Clear falsifiability sections**: The lucid dreaming and simulation articles include explicit 'What Would Challenge This View?' sections. Even where the falsifiers have problems (identified above), the sections demonstrate intellectual honesty about the position's vulnerabilities.

5. **Integration with Map framework**: All three articles connect naturally to other Map content through extensive internal linking. The cluster demonstrates the framework's coherence even where individual arguments face objections.

The fundamental vulnerability: the cluster identifies problems for competing views (passivity for IIT, physicalism's dependence on causal closure) without fully acknowledging that structurally similar problems face the Map's own framework. Dream control evidence doesn't distinguish phenomenal causation from functional causation. Quantum selection faces the same passivity objection as identity theories. The simulation hypothesis is neutral rather than dualism-supporting. The articles would be stronger if they positioned the Map's framework as having *different* challenges, not as escaping the challenges facing competitors.
