## 3. Epistemological and Logical Structural Failures

### 3.1 The Epistemic-to-Metaphysical Slide

The foundational logical error animating the entire manuscript is an overt and pervasive epistemic-to-metaphysical slide. The text routinely identifies a limit in subjective phenomenological access—a boundary concerning what a specific mind can experience or conceptualize—and illicitly substitutes this for a claim about the objective, structural non-existence of mapping mechanisms in reality at large.

This slide is most glaringly evident in the text's conflation of *knowledge by acquaintance* with *propositional knowledge*. In tracing the lineage of the philosophy of mind from Bertrand Russell to Frank Jackson's knowledge argument (Mary's Room) and Thomas Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (which the manuscript explicitly attempts to appropriate ), the distinction between directly experiencing a state and possessing structural knowledge about that state is paramount. The manuscript collapses this distinction entirely. It insists that an agent "cannot know those dimensions exist" because the agent cannot formulate the question of what is missing without possessing the phenomenal concepts those unaccessed dimensions would theoretically provide.

This represents a profound misunderstanding of contemporary cognitive science. The fact that a non-synesthete lacks direct, phenomenological acquaintance with a specific synesthetic colour-sound dimension does not logically entail that the non-synesthete "cannot know those dimensions exist." Contemporary neuroscience, psychophysics, and computational modeling routinely identify, describe, and map these dimensions propositionally. By asserting that the lack of subjective acquaintance equates to an absolute, unbridgeable barrier to all forms of knowledge, the text massively overstates its case. It transforms a localized epistemic limitation—the inability to vividly imagine another's qualia—into a universal metaphysical "void," a leap unsupported by formal logic or empirical observation.

Aphantasia (the imagery void) represents a specific structural deficit in voluntary, top-down visual recall and mental representation. The manuscript confidently asserts that the mere existence of the "synesthetic void"—where identical physical sensory inputs purportedly produce different phenomenal states across different minds—permanently defeats representationalist and functionalist accounts of consciousness. Eliminative materialists argue that our very vocabulary of "qualia," "phenomenal dimensions," and "mind-space" is derived from a deeply flawed, prescientific folk psychology.

From the eliminativist perspective, the "synesthetic void" does not defeat functionalism; it merely describes a physical, topological variation in neural wiring that our archaic folk-psychological language is ill-equipped to describe accurately. The manuscript does not even attempt to counter Churchland's eliminativism.
