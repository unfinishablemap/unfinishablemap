## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
Bedrock framework-boundary disagreement: the article's entire structure presupposes the hard problem "marks a genuine ontological boundary, not merely an unsolved puzzle" (line 45). To the eliminativist this is the unargued premise the whole comparison rests on — both Russellian monism and the Map are pre-scientific to her. The article does not (and need not, for a comparison piece) defend the shared anti-physicalist premise here; it is honestly out of scope. Not critical.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
Same boundary disagreement, sharper on one point: line 43-45 asserts "structural description cannot capture what it is like to be conscious" as the shared starting point. Dennett would say this just begs the hard problem by assuming there is a residue structure leaves out. The article correctly frames this as the *shared* premise of both compared positions, so the comparison is fair even if the premise is contested. Out of scope.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
"Biasing among physically indeterminate outcomes does not require energy injection" (line 113, also line 87) is the load-bearing physics claim and is framed as the Map's position, not consensus. PASS — but the Tegmark objection that decoherence leaves no exploitable macroscopic indeterminacy at neural scales is *not even mentioned* in this article. It is reasonably deferred to interactionist-dualism / born-rule articles, but a reader arriving here sees the interaction mechanism asserted as available without the decoherence objection acknowledged. Low severity (scope), but the "interaction problem is real" admission (line 87) could carry one clause acknowledging the *thermal/decoherence* form of the difficulty, not just the bare distinctness-of-substance form.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
Well-handled. Lines 99-101 now explicitly frame Everett-compatibility as a *cost the Map's own commitments* impose, not a defect in Russellian monism by its own lights, and the "a Russellian monist who shares the No-Many-Worlds intuition could add a collapse mechanism" concession is present. The disagreement is honestly boundary-marked, not dressed as refutation. PASS.

### The Empiricist (Popper)
Falsifiability of bi-aspectual dualism is out of scope for a comparative article; reasonably deferred. But note the article makes a *symmetry* claim it could be held to: Pautz's parity argument (line 77) says "no evidence bears on this modal question" — the Popperian would observe this cuts against the Map too, since the Map's contingent-linking-principles version is equally evidence-underdetermined. The article presents parity as a problem *for monism* (line 147, "the parsimony is illusory") without noting the underdetermination is symmetric. See Counterarguments below.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The no-self objection to "fundamental individual subjects" (line 63: "if individual conscious subjects are fundamental") is the deepest unaddressed pressure. The article's escape from the combination problem *depends* on individual subjects being ontologically basic — exactly what Nagarjuna denies. The article concedes this is "a relocation of the explanatory burden rather than its elimination" (line 63), which is honest, but the relocation is to "why unified subjects exist at all," and that is precisely where emptiness-of-self bites. Out of scope per prior reviews, but it is the article's softest load-bearing assumption.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The trajectory/"stable endpoint" thesis is question-begging (the article's central organising claim)
- **Current content says**: The dual-aspect tradition (Spinoza → Fechner → Pauli-Jung → Bohm-Pylkkänen) shows "a consistent trajectory" each step of which "moves closer to genuine causal interaction," with bi-aspectual dualism as "the final step the monist tradition stops short of" (lines 131-133).
- **A critic would argue**: This is a narrative imposed on the tradition, not extracted from it. The same four thinkers can be read as a tradition that *repeatedly and deliberately refused* interaction for principled reasons (Spinoza's conceptual barrier, Fechner's conservation argument) — i.e., as evidence that serious dual-aspect theorists keep concluding interaction is *not* the place to land. The "approaching but never reaching" framing pre-loads the destination. A monist reads the identical history as "four independent confirmations that you can have two aspects without interaction."
- **Current mitigation**: The article *does* concede this — line 133 explicitly calls the non-entailment "the load-bearing claim ... one a committed monist will contest" and says the trajectory is "offered here as a perspicuous way to organise the tradition, not as a proof." This is good calibration. **Residual problem**: the *frame elements* (description frontmatter: "the stable endpoint monism cannot reach"; opening line 39: "the stable endpoint that the entire tradition approaches but cannot occupy"; section heading "Approaching but Never Reaching Interaction") still assert the conclusion flatly, where the *body* hedges it. This is a frame-vs-body calibration mismatch of the same family flagged on decision-void (2026-06-10) and others. The teleological "trajectory" reading is stated as fact in the structure and hedged only in the prose closer.
- **Suggested response**: Soften the *frame* to match the body's own concession. The description and the section heading assert what the body admits is contestable. E.g. heading "Approaching but Never Reaching Interaction" → "A Tradition That Weakens the Ban on Interaction" (descriptive, not teleological); description "the stable endpoint monism cannot reach" → "the interactionist endpoint the dual-aspect tradition repeatedly declines to take." This does not weaken the argument — it aligns the article's strongest self-criticism (already in the body) with its packaging.

### Pautz parity underdetermination is symmetric but presented as one-sided
- **Current content says**: Pautz shows monism needs the same brute linking principles as dualism, so "the parsimony advantage may be illusory" (line 77); Relation-to-Site reads this as a win: "Ontological simplicity that generates explanatory complexity ... is false economy" (line 147).
- **A critic would argue**: If "no evidence bears on this modal question" (line 77), the underdetermination is *mutual*. The Map's framework also rests on linking principles (contingent ones); parity means neither side gets a parsimony edge, not that the Map wins. Line 147's "false economy" verdict quietly converts a *no-advantage* result into a *Map-advantage* result.
- **Suggested response**: Line 77's body is already balanced ("Russellian monists who think [necessity] can [be motivated] will resist the parity verdict"). The Relation-to-Site bullet (line 147) is the overreach — it should say parity *removes monism's claimed parsimony advantage* rather than implying the Map is simpler. The Map's claim under Tenet 5 ("Occam's Razor Has Limits") is actually *better served* by the honest "neither side wins on parsimony" reading than by the "monism's economy is false" reading.
