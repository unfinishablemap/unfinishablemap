## Executive Summary

A clean, well-organized concept hub. The citations all verify and every wikilink target resolves. The substantive weaknesses are argumentative, not factual: the article leans on the dissociation evidence (blindsight, inattentional blindness, lucid dreaming) as if it discriminated between dualism and physicalism, but each case is comfortably explainable on a purely physical access/phenomenal-processing distinction. There is one borderline overstatement (blindsight "processing is intact") and one mild straw-manning of predictive processing. No critical issues; two medium issues worth a light refine pass.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The article's central inference — "if perception were identical to processing, this dissociation should not occur" — equivocates on "processing." Blindsight shows that *one kind* of visual processing (dorsal-stream, fast spatial discrimination) survives V1 damage while *another kind* (ventral/recurrent processing supporting report) is lost. That is a dissociation between two neural subsystems, not between "processing" and "something beyond processing." Folk-psychological "phenomenal experience" is doing no explanatory work the two-stream story doesn't already do. The article quietly assumes the very thing in dispute: that "experience" names a non-neural residue rather than a yet-uncharacterized processing mode.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
"Sincerely reporting no visual experience" is exactly the heterophenomenological datum I'd predict from a system whose reporting subsystem has no access to the discriminating subsystem. The article treats the *absence of report* as *absence of experience* — precisely the inference heterophenomenology warns against. Likewise the inattentional-blindness paragraph already half-concedes this (citing phenomenal overflow: "unseen or merely uncatalogued"), which undercuts the dissociation argument the section is built to support. If overflow is live, the gorilla case cannot be cited as "processing complete, conscious perception absent."

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
The Minimal Quantum Interaction paragraph is appropriately hedged ("remains speculative"). No overreach to attack here — but note it provides no mechanism connecting "ambiguous decision points" in *perception* (bistable percepts) to quantum-scale events; the link is asserted, not even sketched.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
Not directly engaged; not relevant to this article. No issue.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
The interface model is presented as sharing direct realism's "genuine contact" and indirect realism's "neural mediation" while being distinct from both. What observation would distinguish the interface model from sophisticated indirect realism (predictive processing with a strong-prior story about constancies)? The article does not say. The four "failure signatures" (graceful degradation, priority preservation, compensatory generation, anaesthesia dissociation) are each independently predicted by ordinary bandwidth-limited neural models. As stated, the interface model risks being a redescription, not a rival with a discriminating prediction.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
The article reifies "the experiencing subject" who owns the novel phenomenal properties of degraded vision (perceptual-degradation paragraph) and "perceptual ownership" (Bidirectional Interaction paragraph). The ownership claim is asserted as evidence for an interface recipient, but ownership/mineness is itself contested and could be a constructed feature rather than a primitive of a receiving consciousness. The argument assumes the recipient it sets out to demonstrate.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The dissociation argument proves less than claimed
- **Current content says**: "If perception were identical to processing, this dissociation should not occur" (blindsight); processing-without-experience and experience-without-input "establish that these are separable capabilities."
- **A critic would argue**: All three cases are dissociations *between neural subsystems* (discrimination vs. report; retinal input vs. attentional selection; endogenous activation vs. sensory drive). None requires a non-physical relatum. A physicalist who distinguishes access-processing from phenomenal-processing predicts every one of them.
- **Suggested response**: Reframe the section's thesis from "processing vs. something beyond processing" to "experience tracks a *specific* processing signature, not gross stimulus reception" — and then state explicitly what the interface model adds that the two-subsystem story does not. The honest move is to mark this as evidence *consistent with* dualism that does not by itself *select* dualism, rather than as confirmation ("blindsight... confirm this" in Relation to Site Perspective overstates).

### Predictive processing mildly straw-manned
- **Current content says**: bistable percepts are "a phenomenon that predictive-processing frameworks struggle to explain without granting consciousness a role beyond passive Bayesian updating."
- **A critic would argue**: Mainstream PP accounts (Hohwy, Clark) explain bistability via precision-weighting and active inference — perceptual switches as the system resolving high model-uncertainty. PP does not treat the perceiver as "passive"; active inference is the opposite of passive. Calling PP "passive Bayesian updating" is a strawman.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge the active-inference reply, then locate the genuine residual disagreement (whether *voluntary* top-down biasing of the switch needs a contributor PP cannot supply from priors alone). That is a stronger, defensible claim.
