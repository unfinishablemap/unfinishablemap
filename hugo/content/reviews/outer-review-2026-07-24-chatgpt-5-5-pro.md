# Outer review: “Volitional Control”

**Review state:** 24 July 2026  

**Recommendation:** **Major revision**

The live article remains dated 17 July 2026. I checked the recent-activity changelog through its entries of 24 July and found no subsequent substantive revision to this page. The 17 July review was primarily a publisher-of-record and bibliographic-verification exercise: it corrected a misattributed Wolpe reference and two quotation-fidelity problems, then declared the article “converged” with no remaining items. It did not conduct a full claim-level comparison with the current volition and sense-of-agency literature. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

## Overall verdict

The article succeeds at two limited but important tasks:

1. It shows why the classic Libet result does **not** by itself prove that consciousness is causally idle.
2. It shows that agency, ownership, motor preparation, intention, inhibition and action monitoring are dissociable components supported by identifiable neural systems.

It does **not** establish its stronger headline claim that the evidence points toward “genuine conscious causation,” still less that it supports a non-physical agent selecting neural outcomes. Most of the cited evidence establishes that agency has structured neural and computational correlates. That finding is expected under physicalist, functionalist, predictive-processing and dualist models alike. The article repeatedly moves from:

> agency experience tracks causal architecture

to:

> agency experience is itself a cause within that architecture

without supplying the evidence needed for the second proposition. Its own calibration section briefly recognizes this gap, but the introduction and conclusion continue to make the stronger inference. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

## 1. The article conflates four different theses

A clearer assessment emerges if the argument is separated into four levels.

### A. The anti-Libet thesis

The onset of the readiness potential does not straightforwardly mark an unconscious completed decision, and the reported W-time does not provide an uncontested timestamp for the beginning of conscious intention.

This thesis is well supported.

### B. The architecture thesis

Voluntary action depends on multiple distinguishable processes: action selection, preparation, inhibition, predictive monitoring, self-attribution and execution.

This is also well supported.

### C. The phenomenal-causation thesis

The phenomenal experience of deciding, intending or trying makes a causal contribution that would not occur without the experience.

The article does not establish this. Evidence that a neural process covaries with reported effort or agency does not determine whether the experience causes the process, is realized by it, is produced by it, or shares a common cause with it.

### D. The dualist-interface thesis

A non-physical conscious subject selects among physically open neural alternatives, perhaps through quantum-indeterminate outcomes.

None of the evidence reviewed in this article discriminates that thesis from physicalist alternatives. The article itself labels the proposed quantum mechanism a hypothesis at a speculative evidential tier, but then uses the same hypothesis to answer the regress objection to conscious veto. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

The article is strongest at A and B. Its central weakness is the unmarked passage from A–B to C–D.

## 2. Libet and the readiness potential

The article is right that the traditional inference from “readiness potential precedes W-time” to “the brain has already decided before consciousness appears” is not warranted. Schurger’s stochastic-accumulator account remains an important alternative, and subsequent modelling has shown how slow ramping can emerge from spontaneous neural fluctuations. A 2024 spiking-network study reproduced slow ramping and confirmed a model-derived prediction in human medial-frontal single-neuron data. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-51401-x)

But “dissolution” and “dismantled” are too strong. A 2025 study by Gavenas, Schurger and Maoz says that the RP’s cognitive significance and relationship to conscious intention remain unclear because of conflicting findings. Their results favour stochastic accumulation and indicate that early RP buildup does not reflect reported awareness of motor preparation, but they offer a metacognitive account of how accumulated activity may nevertheless become accessible. A 2026 study instead describes the lateralized RP as resembling a late effector-specific motor gate; it also found no reliable waveform differences between voluntary and forced choices. These results revise what the RP means rather than closing the subject. [MIT Press Direct+2Chapman University Digital Commons+2](https://direct.mit.edu/imag/article/doi/10.1162/imag_a_00465/127590/Probing-for-Intentions-The-Early-Readiness)

The treatment of Braun, Wessler and Friese’s 2021 meta-analysis is also selective. The article foregrounds the high uncertainty surrounding one crucial comparison based on six studies. That qualification is legitimate, but the larger meta-analysis did not amount to a wholesale failure of Libet-style temporal findings; it presented a mixed record containing both broadly recurrent timing patterns and fragile or underpowered components. The defensible conclusion is therefore:

> Libet-style experiments do not identify the onset of a completed decision and do not establish that consciousness is causally idle.

The article instead implies:

> The empirical literature has collectively dismantled the Libet result and thereby removed the principal empirical case against conscious causation.

The former follows. The latter does not. The 2020s literature is actively refining the functional meaning of preparatory signals rather than declaring the issue dissolved. [ScienceDirect+2ScienceDirect+2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0149763421002578)

## 3. The Sjöberg evidence is substantially overstated

The page calls Sjöberg’s 2024 publication an “analysis” providing “independent confirmation.” It is classified by *Brain* as an **Essay**, not an original research report. It draws on an earlier clinical cohort rather than supplying an independent replication. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

The underlying 2020 study involved nine patients with transient postoperative declines in speech or movement after left dorsal-medial frontal surgery: eight underwent glioma surgery and one underwent removal of an adjacent meningioma. The patients did not interpret their transient disabilities as a loss of volition. This is interesting evidence that felt effort or wanting can remain when normal initiation or execution is impaired. [PubMed+1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32198926/)

It does not show that phenomenology “survives the destruction of the neural machinery it was supposedly identical to.” That sentence assumes that the resected SMA region constituted the entire relevant neural realization of volition. Sjöberg’s own preferred account instead treats the SMA as a gateway between higher-order decision systems and motor execution. His 2021 narrative review expressly concluded that the philosophical question of free will could not be definitely resolved from the resection evidence. Recovery from SMA syndrome is also ordinarily transient and has been associated with compensatory reorganization and distributed white-matter pathways. [PMC+3Springer+3PMC+3](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s00701-021-04748-9.pdf)

The cases therefore support a narrower result:

- conscious effort and distal decision processes are not identical to activity in one SMA execution gateway;
- the SMA is not the sole “seat” of willing;
- impairment of execution need not eliminate the experience of trying.

They do not establish that volitional phenomenology lacks a neural realization, that the readiness potential has no role in voluntary action, or that a non-physical subject remains intact after its physical basis has been removed.

## 4. Clinical dissociations support computational structure, not dualist causation

The agency–ownership distinction is a legitimate and useful organising framework. Anarchic hand, delusions of control and motor-awareness disorders do show that ownership, intention, movement and attribution can dissociate.

There is, however, a concrete citation problem. The article cites Blakemore, Wolpert and Frith’s 1998 healthy-participant self-tickling study and then states, without a separate citation, that “schizophrenic patients can tickle themselves.” The 1998 paper did not study such patients. The relevant clinical work is the 2000 study of self-produced sensations in patients with auditory hallucinations and passivity experiences. Even there, the defensible formulation concerns reduced sensory attenuation in relevant symptom groups, not a categorical capacity shared by all people diagnosed with schizophrenia. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

More importantly, the page argues that a confabulatory mechanism would have to “either always attribute agency or always deny it.” That is a false dilemma. Contemporary physicalist models are not binary after-the-fact story generators. A 2023 recurrent-neural-network model reproduced both excessive and diminished agency judgements by delaying sensorimotor prediction signals. A 2024 hierarchical study separated motor control, control detection and self-attribution, finding impairments at lower levels alongside compensatory judgement biases that could preserve overt self-attribution. Such models predict selective, bidirectional and context-sensitive errors. [Nature+1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41537-023-00403-7)

Clinical dissociations therefore count against an extremely crude “the brain indiscriminately narrates agency after every movement” theory. They do not count against sophisticated predictive, Bayesian, hierarchical or functionalist theories. Indeed, those theories were developed partly to explain exactly these dissociations.

The article’s conclusion that agency “tracks causal structure” is reasonable if it means that agency judgements respond to predictive and control processes. But tracking a process is not evidence that the phenomenal feeling of agency causes that process.

## 5. Desmurget’s stimulation evidence is important but theory-neutral

Desmurget and colleagues’ 2009 findings are accurately described at a basic level: in seven awake-surgery patients, parietal stimulation could produce reported desires or beliefs about movement without detected muscle activity, while premotor stimulation could produce movements that patients did not report initiating. [Science](https://www.science.org/doi/pdf/10.1126/science.1169896)

The article draws two stronger conclusions:

- conscious intention and execution occupy cleanly distinct neural territory;
- the finding undermines accounts in which willing is a physical interpretation of action-related processing.

Neither conclusion follows straightforwardly.

First, the sample consisted of seven patients undergoing surgery, and direct electrical stimulation has complex local and network effects. A contemporaneous technical comment noted that stimulation may disrupt or deactivate tissue rather than simply activate a discrete cognitive module. Later whole-brain work based on 1,537 stimulated cortical sites in 67 participants found that stimulation effects are strongly predicted by intrinsic network architecture. [Science+1](https://www.science.org/doi/pdf/10.1126/science.1183735)

Second, a physicalist model can readily distinguish neural representations of intended action, predicted sensory consequences, motor commands and actual movement. Finding distinct physical components is not evidence for a non-physical component. The study strongly rejects a simple model in which agency experience is nothing more than direct detection of completed muscle output. It does not reject models in which intention is a higher-order neural representation constructed before, during or after motor preparation.

## 6. The conscious-veto section confuses a neural correlate with phenomenal causation

Brass and Haggard’s 2007 study identified dorsal frontomedian activation associated with intentionally withholding a prepared action. The authors described it as a **candidate brain area** reflecting the decision whether to act. The study was correlational fMRI, not evidence that the dFMC itself “exerts top-down inhibition,” and still less that conscious experience independently exerts that inhibition. [Journal of Neuroscience+1](https://www.jneurosci.org/content/27/34/9141)

The more recent literature depicts inhibition as a distributed process. A 2021 meta-analysis located intentional decisions across medial and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, left insula and inferior parietal cortex. Multimodal work has implicated right inferior frontal cortex, beta-band signalling and directed interaction with pre-SMA, while other research finds shared right-prefrontal mechanisms for suppressing actions and thoughts. [PMC+2Nature+2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8463837/)

These studies support the existence of active stopping mechanisms. They do not answer the philosophical question of whether:

- conscious experience causes the stopping;
- the conscious experience is the phenomenal aspect of the stopping process;
- both are effects of another process; or
- a non-physical subject influences the network.

The article’s answer to the regress objection is a clear example of **tenet-protective bracketing**. It says the objection assumes that every neural event has a sufficient prior neural cause, but it does not provide empirical evidence that the veto is an exception. Instead, it inserts the Map’s quantum-selection hypothesis, which the next sentence concedes has not been shown empirically. That identifies a possible escape from physical closure; it does not answer the objection or provide evidence for the escape. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

## 7. The intentional-binding section is the most conspicuously stale

The page presents intentional binding as an implicit, non-introspective measure of agency and treats Moore and Obhi’s 2012 review as establishing that it reflects predictive agency mechanisms rather than retrospective authorship inference. That is too confident even as a description of the older literature, and it is not tenable against work published since 2020. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

A 2020 construct-validity analysis argued that “sense of agency” research mixes several different constructs and experimental procedures, that implicit measures often fail to correlate with explicit ones, and that standard measures have generally low construct validity. Intentional binding may index temporal prediction, causal linkage or performance on a timing task without measuring the phenomenal character of agency. [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nc/article/2020/1/niaa019/5890345)

Subsequent findings sharpen the problem:

- A 2023 study concluded that apparent relations between binding and agency may result from procedural confounds and generic temporal grouping. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37166936/)
- A 2024 paper found substantial temporal binding but no evidence that intention itself was the relevant factor, attributing the result instead to sensory integration and temporal prediction. [hal.science+1](https://hal.science/hal-04784538/document)
- A 2025 registered study found that canonical binding depended on conscious access to the action outcome, but explicitly cautioned that binding is not equivalent to sense of agency and interpreted its outcome as most compatible with an illusionist account. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44271-025-00323-5)

The 2025 result could be incorporated into an argument that awareness alters sensorimotor processing. It still would not establish dualist causation. More damagingly, the article’s own second proposed disconfirmer is that intentional binding might prove to be an artefact of motor preparation or timing. The current literature has not conclusively abolished the effect, but it has made that challenge a live and serious possibility. The page neither cites this work nor reduces its confidence accordingly. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

## 8. The neuroplasticity argument contains a causal error

The page argues that, if conscious effort were epiphenomenal, the same neural changes should be producible without the phenomenology of effort. That does not follow.

An epiphenomenalist can posit:

N→EN \rightarrow EN→E

and

N→PN \rightarrow PN→P

where a neural control process NNN produces both experienced effort EEE and plastic change PPP, without EEE causing PPP. Effort and plasticity could therefore covary perfectly while experience remained causally idle. Evidence distinguishing that model from E→PE \rightarrow PE→P would require an intervention that changes conscious experience while holding the relevant neural control variables fixed, or some comparably discriminating design.

The article’s companion page on clinical neuroplasticity now acknowledges exactly this problem. It says the data do not adjudicate between mental-causal and identity/common-neural-cause readings, that compatibility is not confirmation, and that removing a defeater does not upgrade evidence for the preferred interpretation. The stronger wording in “Volitional Control” is therefore internally inconsistent with the article it directs readers to for the “honest assessment.” [topics/clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation/)

The Rajan citation also contains claim-level errors. The paper compared willed with instructed attention. It found **greater**, not uniquely present, frontal theta power and frontoparietal interaction in the willed condition. The theta difference began at about 470–510 milliseconds after the cue, not approximately 300 milliseconds. The study did not establish absence during “automatic attention.” Its interpretation concerned additional decision and conflict-processing demands in the willed condition. [OUP Academic+2OUP Academic+2](https://academic.oup.com/cercor/article/29/7/2832/5041999)

The neuroplasticity material shows that cognitive therapies and internally guided tasks have measurable neural implementations. It does not show that their phenomenal character is an independently efficacious cause.

## 9. Wegner and the reportability objection are not treated fairly

Nahmias’s objection is sound as far as it goes: demonstrating some illusions of agency does not prove that agency experience is always illusory. But “the experience can malfunction” also does not prove “the experience normally has a causal role.” A speedometer can accurately track speed, malfunction selectively and remain causally irrelevant to the engine.

The article characterises Wegner’s evidence principally as pathological exceptions—automatisms, hypnosis and alien-hand phenomena. Wegner and Wheatley’s apparent-mental-causation account also rested on experiments in which normal participants could be induced to attribute a forced action to themselves, and on the proposed conditions of priority, consistency and exclusivity. An adequate reply should test that inferential model rather than dismiss the evidence as pathology. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

The reportability argument is also presented as a knockdown: an epiphenomenalist allegedly cannot rationally report or write about conscious will. But a physicalist or functionalist can say that the same neural state realizes the experience and produces the report; an epiphenomenalist can appeal to reliable common causes; and phenomenal-concept strategies dispute what sort of causal connection knowledge of experience requires. The site’s “Bidirectional Interaction” page was revised on 22 July precisely to soften this argument from “cannot be rationally held” to a serious but conditional objection, explicitly recognizing the phenomenal-concept response. “Volitional Control” retains the superseded knockdown formulation. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

## 11. Tenet-protective bracketing

Tenet-protective bracketing is a material problem in this article. It appears in at least six forms.

### Calibration that does not govern the verdict

The calibration section correctly says that phenomenology does not discriminate dualism from physicalism and that removing the Libet defeater is not positive evidence for non-physical volition. But the introduction nevertheless says the evidence points toward genuine conscious causation, and the conclusion says that clinical and experimental evidence collectively dismantles the opposing conclusion. The caveat is locally present but globally inert. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

### Compatibility counted as confirmation

The observation that several findings are “compatible with the Map” is repeatedly allowed to carry evidential weight. Compatibility is weak evidence when all leading rivals predict the same observations. The relevant question is comparative: does the Map assign a substantially higher probability to the result than its physicalist competitors do?

### Objections set aside at the framework boundary

The regress objection to veto is described as assuming physical causal sufficiency. But because the Map supplies no independent evidence for the proposed exception, identifying the opponent’s assumption does not answer the objection. The article substitutes an unverified interface hypothesis at exactly the point where evidence is required.

### Opponents reduced to brittle versions

The article primarily tests itself against:

- an RP interpreted as a completed unconscious decision;
- an agency system that indiscriminately confabulates;
- a detector of completed motor output;
- a veto localised to one region;
- an illusion theory based on pathology;
- an epiphenomenalism unable to explain any effort–plasticity covariance.

Current physicalist models are stronger and more flexible than all six constructions.

### Asymmetric falsifiers

The proposed disconfirmers are generally stacked against detection. Requiring intact agency phenomenology after the comparator, parietal circuits, dFMC and plasticity pathways have all been disrupted simultaneously is neither realistic nor a distinctive prediction of physicalism. Requiring a system known independently to lack phenomenal consciousness is circular when the presence of consciousness is inferred from the same behaviour. Meanwhile, the intentional-binding disconfirmer may already be partly activated by current research, yet the article has not been downgraded. [topics/volitional-control](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/volitional-control/)

### Premature convergence locking

The 17 July review explicitly said there were no currency claims requiring an empirical sweep, declared the page “converged” with no remaining items, advised future reviewers not to re-flag the physicalist calibration issue, and allowed future citation verification to be skipped. Yet that review also identifies itself as a targeted publisher-of-record verification pass. A metadata-oriented review should not lock claim fidelity, opponent representation or literature currency. [GitHub+3GitHub+3GitHub+3](https://github.com/unfinishablemap/unfinishablemap/commit/1b19fe1b4d961cd5e866cd757a60d4c1b521c351)

The site’s methodology has since been improved by distinguishing bibliographic existence, claim-attribution fidelity and literature currency. Applying that newer three-stage standard to this article would likely have caught several of the defects above. [workflow/changelog](https://unfinishablemap.org/workflow/changelog/)

## 12. Integration with surrounding articles

The page is well integrated at the level of links and terminology. It connects volitional control with the agency–ownership distinction, mental causation, quantum selection, clinical neuroplasticity, control theory and the philosophy of trying.

The epistemic integration is weaker.

- **Clinical Neuroplasticity Evidence for Bidirectional Causation** says the therapy evidence is compatible with both interpretations, that the data do not adjudicate between them, and that compatibility is not positive confirmation. “Volitional Control” says the evidence points away from epiphenomenalism. [topics/clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/clinical-neuroplasticity-evidence-for-bidirectional-causation/)
- **Consciousness and Motor Selection** uses the more defensible formulation that the Libet challenge has been weakened and the playing field levelled rather than the issue settled. “Volitional Control” escalates this to “dissolution” and “collectively dismantled.” [topics/motor-control-quantum-zeno](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/motor-control-quantum-zeno/)
- The site’s quantum-motor articles acknowledge that neural-timescale coherence and the proposed interface remain unestablished. “Volitional Control” repeats that caveat but nevertheless deploys the mechanism as its answer to the veto regress. [topics/motor-control-quantum-zeno](https://unfinishablemap.org/topics/motor-control-quantum-zeno/)
- **Bidirectional Interaction** now treats reportability and self-stultification as conditional rather than decisive. The target article still states that the opposing position cannot rationally be held. [workflow/changelog](https://unfinishablemap.org/workflow/changelog/)
- **Control-Theoretic Will** and related interface pages often reuse the target article’s veto and effort material. This risks circular convergence: one evidential stream is restated across several articles and later counted as support from several directions.

The site therefore has semantic integration but significant **confidence-version skew**. Its more cautious qualifications have not consistently propagated into this article’s lead, conclusion and dependent pages.

## Bottom line

The article’s claims hold up only in restricted form.

The evidence is sufficient to show that simplistic readings of Libet are unwarranted and that volitional agency consists of multiple dissociable neural and computational processes. It is not sufficient to show that phenomenal willing is an independently efficacious cause, and it supplies no discriminating evidence for a dualist or quantum-selection interpretation.

The counterarguments are not fairly represented at their contemporary strongest. The page engages crude post-hoc-confabulation, output-detector and local-veto models while largely omitting hierarchical predictive processing, Bayesian cue integration, distributed control networks, common-cause explanations and functional realization.

The reference metadata is now mostly sound, but several body claims remain unsupported, overstated or inaccurate. The reference base is also too old for an empirical-convergence article in 2026.

The page is coherent with the site’s architecture but not with the calibration level of several surrounding articles. Its principal methodological defect is that caveats are inserted locally while the title, opening, transitions and final synthesis continue to deliver a tenet-favouring verdict that those caveats do not warrant.

4. **`topics/volitional-control` — update the RP literature.** Add the 2024 spontaneous-ramping network work, the 2025 probe study of RP and conscious preparation, recent motor-gate/evidence-accumulation findings, and methodological work on W-time.
