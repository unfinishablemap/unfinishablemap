## Notes

### topics/free-will.md
- **Tenet adjacency**: T1 (Dualism)
- **Issue**: The article states twice that "Libertarian Free Will... requires [substance dualism](/tenets/#dualism): consciousness must be a persisting *thing* capable of exercising causal powers" (line 64) and the Further Reading entry for `agent-causation` describes "substance causation" (line 190). The dualism tenet itself is explicitly neutral: "This commitment is neutral between [substance and property dualism](/concepts/substance-property-dualism/)... What matters is irreducibility, not the specific ontological framework." The article takes a stronger position than the tenet sanctions, then links back to the tenet under an alias ("substance dualism") that misrepresents the tenet's scope.
- **Why it is not a violation**: An article may legitimately take a stronger position on a specific philosophical question than the tenet framework requires, *provided* it does not represent that stronger position as the tenet itself. The substantive philosophical claim — that agent causation requires the agent to be a persisting substance — is debatable but defensible; some agent-causal theorists hold that property dualism plus the right metaphysics of properties suffices, while others (Chisholm, O'Connor) maintain substance dualism is needed. Either reading is internally coherent.
- **Recommendation**: Change the wikilink alias from `[substance dualism](/tenets/#dualism)` to `[dualism](/tenets/#dualism)` (preserving the article's substantive position), and add a clarifying clause: "the Map's interpretation of agent causation as requiring substance dualism is one defensible reading of Tenet 1's commitment to irreducibility, not a direct entailment of the tenet itself." This preserves the article's philosophical position while keeping the tenet's neutrality intact.

## Non-Violation Tensions Worth Monitoring

These are *not* tenet violations but are pressure points visible in this window worth tracking:

1. **Substance vs property dualism within agent-causal arguments (T1, presentation-level):** The free-will note above flags this. The deeper structural pressure: agent-causal arguments often need a persisting substantive subject to ground causal initiation, while T1 is neutral between substance and property dualism. Articles that carry agent causation explicitly (`free-will.md`, `agent-causation`, `the-epiphenomenalist-threat.md`, `responsibility-gradient-from-attentional-capacity.md`) need to be careful about whether they represent the substance-dualism reading as a free choice within T1's neutrality (correct) or as what T1 itself requires (incorrect). Most carry this distinction well; `free-will.md` is the outlier in this window.
2. **Explanatory failure as evidence vs as gap-argument (T1):** `placebo-effect-and-mental-causation.md` engages this directly and well — explicitly acknowledging that "this is a gap argument" charge from a critic, and replying that the tight content-specific correlation between conscious expectation and physiological cascade is an *argument from structure* rather than from ignorance. The Map's case for T1 will increasingly turn on this distinction; articles that lean on explanatory-gap-style reasoning need to make the structural-argument framing explicit. The current cluster does this well; future articles should preserve the discipline.
3. **Functionalist responses and their concessions (T1, T3):** Multiple articles in this window engage functionalism by quoting the strongest functionalist reply, then arguing the reply concedes more than it preserves. The pattern is correct but the gradient between "explained" and "explained away" is delicate. `pain-consciousness-and-causal-power.md`, `placebo-effect-and-mental-causation.md`, and `the-epiphenomenalist-threat.md` handle this carefully; the discipline should generalise.
4. **Open-label placebos and the limits of content-specificity (T3):** `placebo-effect-and-mental-causation.md` honestly concedes that open-label findings "weaken the strongest version of the content-specificity argument" while arguing they do not eliminate mental causation. This is the right register but creates a falsifiability handle: if richer studies of open-label placebos showed the effect operating through pathways genuinely independent of conscious meaning, T3's empirical case would weaken. Worth watching.
5. **The agency-void verification gap (T3):** `phenomenology-of-agency-vs-passivity.md` and `free-will.md` both invoke the agency void to mark that introspective verification of consciousness's causal power is structurally circular — every check is performed by the very faculty under investigation. This is correctly framed as a structural limit on T3's introspective evidence, not as a defeat of T3. The Map's framework therefore rests on coherence with phenomenology and neural data rather than on an introspective proof of efficacy. Articles that carry T3 should preserve this distinction.
