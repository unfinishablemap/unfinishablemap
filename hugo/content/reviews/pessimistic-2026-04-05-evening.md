# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-04-05
**Content reviewed**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`

## Executive Summary

The delegatory dualism article is well-structured and clearly presents Saad's theory, but it contains a fundamental epistemological vulnerability: the theory is designed to be empirically indistinguishable from its rivals, which undercuts the article's claims about its "advantages." The preemption analogies also have structural disanalogies that the article doesn't acknowledge, and several philosophical attributions need tighter sourcing.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist

Patricia Churchland would target the article's uncritical use of folk psychological categories. The entire delegation framework assumes that "pain," "wincing," and "visual experience" are natural kinds with determinate causal profiles. But these categories come from folk psychology—pre-scientific taxonomies that neuroscience is progressively replacing. What Saad calls "the causal profile of pain" is a heterogeneous collection of neural processes (nociceptive signalling, affective evaluation, motor planning, autonomic response) that don't form a unified causal kind. Building a metaphysical framework on folk categories is like building physics on the four classical elements. The Subset Law* requires experiences to have clean causal profiles that map onto physical profiles—but if "pain" isn't a natural kind, there's no determinate profile to match.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist

Dennett would argue that delegation is a verbal trick that smuggles in the conclusion. When Saad says "pain causes the wince by taking over causal work the physical state would otherwise perform," the physicalist asks: what *exactly* changed? The physical state fires. The muscles contract. The neural pathways are identical. Saad says "the experience did the causal work, not the physical state"—but this is a relabelling of the same causal chain, not a discovery about it. The delegation framework adds a metaphysical layer (experiences "preempting" physical causes) without any observable difference. This is precisely the kind of theoretical epicycle that should be shaved by Occam's razor. Dennett's heterophenomenology would say: we can explain all the verbal reports, all the behavior, without positing delegation. The "explanatory advantages" over epiphenomenalism are illusory—both views predict identical observations.

### The Quantum Skeptic

Max Tegmark would focus on the Map Integration section (Part II). The article claims quantum indeterminacy could provide "the physical mechanism" while delegation provides "the causal structure." But this integration is hand-waving. What specific quantum system is being delegated to? At what timescale? The entanglement binding hypothesis is invoked as if it were an established framework, but large-scale neural entanglement faces devastating decoherence objections that the article simply ignores when integrating it with delegation. The article's claim that "entanglement provides the physical holism; delegation explains how that unified state's causal work gets taken over by experience" combines two speculative frameworks without showing that their combination is even coherent, let alone physically plausible.

### The Many-Worlds Defender

David Deutsch would note that the article's treatment of MWI is cursory and dismissive. The claim that "the Map's quantum interactionism requires outcomes to be *selected* (not merely branched)" begs the question against MWI. In the Everettian framework, all outcomes occur—the appearance of selection is explained by decoherence and self-locating uncertainty. The article doesn't engage with this; it merely states the Map's rejection as if it were obvious. Deutsch would also point out that delegatory dualism's compatibility with quantum interpretations is a weakness, not a strength: if the theory works regardless of interpretation, it has no explanatory purchase on the actual physics of mind-body interaction. The "integration" in Part II is purely optional, making it decorative rather than substantive.

### The Empiricist

This is the most devastating critique. The article's own "Testability and Limitations" section admits that delegatory dualism is "in the same predictive boat" as epiphenomenalism, overdeterminist dualism, and physicalism. The theory is *designed* to satisfy Observational Closure—no detectable anomalies. This means: (1) No experiment can distinguish delegation from its competitors. (2) The theory makes zero distinctive predictions. (3) The claimed advantages (explaining reports, tracking function) are philosophical preference, not empirical advantage—a Churchland-style eliminativist or a Dennett-style physicalist can explain the same phenomena with different theoretical machinery. The article tries to spin this as the "advantages are theoretical rather than predictive," but Popper would recognise this as metaphysics masquerading as theory. If your theory is constructed to be observationally identical to its rivals, the claim that it's "better" reduces to aesthetic preference. The article should be far more explicit about this limitation rather than treating it as a minor footnote.

### The Buddhist Philosopher

Nagarjuna would challenge the article's unexamined assumption that "experiences" are discrete entities with determinate causal profiles that can "take over" work from physical states. This reifies experience into a thing—a substance-like entity that acts. But in the Madhyamaka tradition, both the experiencer and the experience arise dependently; neither has intrinsic existence. Pain doesn't have a fixed causal profile because pain is empty of self-nature—it arises in dependence on conditions and is constituted by its relations. Saad's framework treats experiences as fully formed causal agents that plug into physical slots. This is a deeply Western, substance-metaphysical picture that assumes exactly what Buddhist philosophy denies: that there are ultimate bearers of causal power. The delegation framework, far from solving the mind-body problem, merely moves the mystery from "how do minds interact with bodies?" to "how do reified experiences preempt physical causes?"

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Self-Undermining Unfalsifiability

- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: "Testability and Limitations" section (line 148-153)
- **Problem**: The article acknowledges the theory makes no distinctive predictions, then pivots to claiming "theoretical advantages"—but the advantages cited (explaining reports, tracking function) are themselves contested. A physicalist explains reports through physical causation; an illusionist explains them through self-modelling. The article presents delegation's explanations as uncontested when they're not.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Strengthen this section by explicitly acknowledging that every claimed theoretical advantage has a rival explanation. Frame delegation's contribution as offering *coherence within a dualist framework* rather than advantages over all competitors.

### Issue 2: Preemption Analogy Structural Disanalogy

- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: Lines 102-113
- **Problem**: Both analogies (major/sergeant, assassin A/B) involve causes of the *same ontological type*—both are physical events in the same causal order. Delegatory dualism posits preemption across ontological boundaries (non-physical experience preempting physical state). The analogies assume what needs to be explained: that cross-ontological preemption works like same-type preemption. The article doesn't flag this disanalogy.
- **Severity**: High
- **Recommendation**: Add a paragraph acknowledging that the analogies are illustrative of the *structure* of preemption, not the *mechanism*, and that cross-ontological preemption is a distinctive claim requiring its own justification.

### Issue 3: Unexamined Assumption About Causal Profiles

- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: Lines 88-96 (Subset Law*)
- **Problem**: The theory assumes experiences have determinate causal profiles, but never explains how non-physical entities acquire causal profiles in physical terms. If an experience's causal profile is defined by what physical effects it produces, this seems circular: the experience's identity is defined by what it causes, but what it causes is determined by the physical state it's matched to via the Subset Law*.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Address the circularity concern explicitly. Saad may have a response in his paper; if so, cite it. If not, flag it as an open question.

### Issue 4: Map Integration Compounds Speculation

- **File**: `topics/delegatory-dualism.md`
- **Location**: Part II, lines 181-237
- **Problem**: Part II integrates delegation with quantum interactionism and the entanglement binding hypothesis. Both are speculative. Combining two speculative frameworks doesn't produce a stronger position—it multiplies the assumptions. The article reads as if the integration strengthens both frameworks, but it actually increases the total burden of proof.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add explicit acknowledgment that the integration is exploratory and that each framework must stand independently before the combination has force.

## Counterarguments to Address

### Theoretical Advantages Are Contested

- **Current content says**: Delegatory dualism explains why introspective reports track experiences (unlike epiphenomenalism) and avoids redundant causation (unlike overdeterminism).
- **A critic would argue**: Physical self-modelling explains reports without positing non-physical causation. The "advantage" over epiphenomenalism only holds if you've already accepted dualism—it's not an argument *for* dualism, just for one type of dualism over another.
- **Suggested response**: Reframe the claimed advantages as *intra-dualist* advantages rather than advantages over physicalism. The article sometimes slides between these.

### Default Causal Profiles Are Counterfactual

- **Current content says**: A physical state's "default" causal profile is what it would cause if delegation didn't occur.
- **A critic would argue**: This is an unfalsifiable counterfactual. We can never observe the "default" because delegation always occurs (wherever experience is present). The baseline against which preemption is defined is itself unobservable.
- **Suggested response**: Acknowledge the counterfactual nature explicitly. Saad may justify this via the Organizational Invariance constraint (zombie duplicates would exhibit the default profile), but this presupposes that zombies are possible—another contested claim.

- **Clear two-part structure** separating Saad's theory from Map interpretation is exemplary. This honest framing prevents the reader from conflating Saad's claims with the Map's extensions.
- **Thorough objection/response section** that doesn't shy away from hard questions shows intellectual integrity.
- **The opening summary** is well-crafted and front-loaded—it would survive truncation effectively.
- **Accurate citations** with DOIs and specific page references to Saad's publications.
