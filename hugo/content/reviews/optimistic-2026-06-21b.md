## Executive Summary

Both articles are mature, well-calibrated exemplars of different corpus virtues. `vertiginous-question` is the strongest worked instance in the corpus of *honest boundary-marking against one's own preferred conclusion*: it concedes openly that the indexical objection alone does not discharge the No Many Worlds tenet against Christian List's first-personally centred many-worlds, and names the separate bet (rejection of modal realism) that actually does the work. `dual-domain-capabilities-in-proprioception-and-spatial-imagination` is a model of *evidential bookkeeping discipline*: it explicitly downgrades its own "three independent confirmations" framing to "one explanatory-gap pattern recurring across three sites," keeping the inferential count honest. Opportunities are downstream and link-only — two genuine one-directional cross-link gaps surfaced, and neither article warrants a new page (both targets are foundational siblings that already exist).

## Praise from Sympathetic Philosophers

### The Property Dualist (Chalmers)
`vertiginous-question` generalises the explanatory gap into a *second-order* gap Chalmers would recognise as orthogonal to his own: Roberts' "Even Harder Problem" — "harder than Chalmers' hard problem because it adds a layer of specificity." The hard problem asks why physical processes yield experience at all; the vertiginous question asks why, given that experiences exist, *this* one is mine. The article keeps the gap intact even against dualism itself ("Adding souls to the ontology doesn't explain why I am associated with one soul rather than another"), which is exactly the discipline that prevents dualism from cheaply declaring victory.

`dual-domain-capabilities` keeps Chalmers' gap sharpening rather than closing as mechanism improves: "the more precisely we map receptor pathways and cortical representations, the more conspicuous it becomes that mechanism and experience are different kinds of thing." The imagery section states the move cleanly — physical overlap between imagery and perception "does not dissolve the hard problem for imagination — it sharpens it."

### The Quantum Mind Theorist (Stapp)
`vertiginous-question` engages the physics-consciousness interface with restraint Stapp would respect: the [indexical-identity-quantum-measurement](/topics/indexical-identity-quantum-measurement/) thread proposes that "if collapse selects *this* outcome from many possibilities, and consciousness selects *this* viewpoint, the two selections may share a common structure" — offered as a structural analogy, explicitly tagged "a philosophical proposal rather than established science." `dual-domain-capabilities` extends the quantum interface to proprioceptive neurons (microtubule distribution "throughout the nervous system... not just cortical ones") but immediately fences it: "This is speculative; the dual-domain argument rests on the explanatory gap, not on any specific mechanism." Both keep the physics as illustration, not load-bearing support.

### The Phenomenologist (Nagel)
`vertiginous-question` is deeply Nagelian — "the view from nowhere" cited directly, and the phenomenology-of-vertigo section traces the felt sequence (apparent triviality → dawning inadequacy → conceptual vertigo → oscillation) that keeps the first-person encounter primary rather than collapsing it into third-person description. `dual-domain-capabilities` centres lived embodiment through Merleau-Ponty (the body as "vehicle of being in the world") and the Christina case (Sacks), keeping "felt embodiment" load-bearing as a distinct domain from the body schema.

### The Process Philosopher (Whitehead)
Neither article over-reaches into process metaphysics, which is itself the right discipline. `dual-domain-capabilities` treats mind and matter as making "distinguishable contributions" to a single capability rather than as crude warring substances — close to the two-aspect spirit Whitehead would endorse — without inflating any organism or capability up an evidential tier. `vertiginous-question` keeps haecceity as "a tool for locating the void's boundary, not for crossing it," resisting the temptation to convert process-friendliness into a positive metaphysical result.

### The Libertarian Free Will Defender (Kane)
`vertiginous-question` does Kane's work through the moral-responsibility connection: "I am responsible for *my* actions because I am this particular subject who performed them — not merely someone qualitatively similar," and flags that pattern-identity theories allowing branching/fusing selves "face difficulties with desert and punishment." `dual-domain-capabilities` grounds genuine mental causation in the bidirectional tenet — the sculptor's chisel following the imagined form, mental-practice transfer — without overstating it beyond the replicated sports-psychology evidence base.

### The Mysterian (McGinn)
`vertiginous-question` is exemplary here. It treats the indexical void as a candidate *cognitive* boundary (structural circularity: "the explanandum appears in the explanans") while refusing to declare the wall permanent — the deflationary accounts (Hofstadter, Parfit) are given "serious advocates" status and engaged on the merits, not dismissed. The "What AI Might See" section is a fine mysterian touch: an AI "might formally characterise the void's structure... without experiencing the vertigo," and "this gap between formal characterisation and phenomenal encounter is itself informative."

### The Hardline Empiricist (Birch) — load-bearing verdict for `dual-domain-capabilities`
This is where `dual-domain-capabilities` earns its highest marks, and the verdict is load-bearing because the article's core move is an *inductive generalisation* ("the pattern recurs across three capabilities, therefore dual-domain architecture is general") — precisely the structure most prone to over-counting evidence. **The article polices its own induction.** The General Pattern section contains the key self-correction: "One qualification keeps the count honest: the three capabilities share a single upstream — the explanatory gap itself, which each dissociation presupposes — so they are not three fully independent confirmations of dual-domain architecture so much as one explanatory-gap pattern recurring across three sites." It then states exactly what the recurrence *does* legitimately add — *substrate divergence* (engrams vs. receptors vs. parietal machinery cannot share capability-specific mechanism, so a deflation of one wouldn't automatically deflate the others) — and weights it correctly: "worth the weight of one cross-substrate pattern, not three." This is the named "tenet-coherent, not evidence-elevating" discipline applied to an *empirical* induction rather than a metaphysical one: the article had the structural opportunity to claim three independent confirmations and explicitly declined. That restraint is itself the strength to name.

The aphantasia section is a second piece of evidential honesty: rather than reading aphantasia as proof of a non-physical imager, the article reads it two-sidedly — "If spatial cognition were purely physical computation, the phenomenal dimension... should be irrelevant... If spatial cognition were purely phenomenal, aphantasics should fail entirely. They do not" — and lands on dissociable contributions, the calibrated reading. The phantom-limb passage refuses the over-claim too: phantoms "sharpen the dual-domain question without by themselves establishing a non-neural bearer of embodiment."

For `vertiginous-question`, the Birch verdict is lighter (the article trades in metaphysics of indexicality, not animal/AI evidential tiers) but strongly positive on a *different* axis the persona cares about — honest framework-stage calibration. The List passage is the corpus's clearest worked example of declining to let a favoured tenet over-reach: "against List specifically, the case against many worlds rests on the rejection of modal realism, not on the vertiginous question — an honestly open boundary the Map marks here rather than papers over." Naming the actual load-bearing commitment, and conceding the indexical objection is "supplementary, not sufficient" against centred-worlds views, is exactly the register-separation discipline this persona exists to praise.

**Process Philosopher / Hardline Empiricist convergence:** No conflict in either article. In `dual-domain-capabilities` the personas praise different aspects of the same induction-policing move; in `vertiginous-question` both find honest boundary-marking. No possibility/probability slippage and no calibration concern arises in either — so no `refine-draft` is warranted on calibration grounds. The only opportunities are link-reciprocity gaps below.

## Content Strengths

### topics/vertiginous-question.md
- **Strongest point**: The List treatment (the "Connections to Other Problems" → "Many-worlds branching" passage and its echo in "Relation to Site Perspective"). The article distinguishes the easy target (Everettian branch-egalitarianism, which the indexical objection *does* defeat) from the hard target (List's first-personally centred many-worlds, which "answers the vertiginous question by privileging one present per subject"), and openly states that against the hard target the tenet "leans on a distinct commitment — the rejection of the modal realism" rather than on the indexical objection. It even names the one-world rival that honours the same first-person realism (Hare's egocentric presentism). This is steelmanning the strongest opponent and conceding the limits of one's own best argument — rare and valuable.
- **Notable quote**: "That commitment is itself a substantive bet rather than a result the indexical objection delivers on its own."
- **Why it works**: Front-loads the question in the first sentence (truncation-resilient), grounds every claim in named live scholarship (Hellie, Roberts, Nagai, List 2023/2024, Conitzer, Adams, Hare, Hofstadter, Parfit), and threads the No Many Worlds tenet through as a void that *generates* the tenets rather than merely following from them. The List (2023) *Noûs* and List (2024) *Phil Quarterly* cites carry DOIs/volumes.

### topics/dual-domain-capabilities-in-proprioception-and-spatial-imagination.md
- **Strongest point**: The self-auditing "General Pattern" section. Most multi-case inductive arguments in any corpus quietly bank each case as independent support; this one explicitly demotes "three confirmations" to "one pattern across three substrate-divergent sites" and explains precisely what partial independence does and does not buy. The summary table (Capability × Physical × Phenomenal × Key Dissociation) is a genuinely effective LLM-friendly compression of the whole argument.
- **Notable quote**: "They are not three fully independent confirmations of dual-domain architecture so much as one explanatory-gap pattern recurring across three sites... worth the weight of one cross-substrate pattern, not three."
- **Why it works**: Each capability is anchored in a real dissociation pair (Christina vs. phantom limbs; aphantasia vs. hyperphantasia), citations are live and specific (Shepard & Metzler 1971, Kay/Keogh/Pearson 2024, Sacks 1985), and the article correctly routes the canonical phantom-limb mechanism treatment out to [phantom-limb-phenomena](/topics/phantom-limb-phenomena/) rather than re-deriving it, keeping itself focused on the dissociation it needs.
