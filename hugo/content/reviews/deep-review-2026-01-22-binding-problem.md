## Pessimistic Analysis Summary

### Critical Issues Found

1. **Illusionist dismissal was too quick**: Original treatment (one paragraph) rejected Dennett's position without adequate engagement. Illusionism (Dennett, Frankish, Graziano) offers a powerful challenge: phenomenal unity might be introspective misreporting of computational binding, not a genuine feature requiring explanation.

2. **Decoherence debate presented one-sided**: Article cited Hagan et al. 2002 correction of Tegmark without mentioning Reimers et al. 2009 response or the ongoing exchange. This made the quantum position seem more settled than it is.

3. **Epothilone study quantum claim unsupported**: Study showed microtubule stabilization delays anesthesia, but classical explanation (mechanical effects on synaptic transmission) wasn't ruled out. No quantum measurement was made.

4. **Many-Worlds objection was weak**: Article claimed MWI can't account for phenomenal unity because "all bindings occur in branches," but didn't address the Everettian response that each branch has definite, real unity—it's just branch-relative.

5. **Phenomenological appeals lack empirical grounding**: Repeatedly asked "why does X feel unified?" without acknowledging that phenomenological intuitions alone are insufficient for empirical claims.

4. **Twin study lacked methodology**: "Entanglement of qubits in stimulus configurations" was unexplained—readers couldn't evaluate the claim.

5. **C. elegans trace-conditioning claim** needed citation.

6. **Buddhist no-self not engaged**: Mentioned but didn't address the challenge that binding is only a problem if you reify unity.

- **Eliminativist**: Unity is cognitive construction, "user interface" over parallel processes
- **Physicalist**: Sleep removes wakefulness/global availability, not some special phenomenal-unity ingredient

- **Buddhist**: Binding problem assumes reified unity; dissolves under no-self analysis
