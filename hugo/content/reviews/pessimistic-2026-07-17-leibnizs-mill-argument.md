# Pessimistic Review

**Date**: 2026-07-17
**Content reviewed**: `obsidian/topics/leibnizs-mill-argument.md`

## Executive Summary

A well-structured, citation-clean article (Lodge 2014 and the Latta translation of *Monadology* §17 both verify). Its principal weaknesses are dialectical rather than factual: an unacknowledged tension between "all successor arguments share the mill's core insight" and the later use of their convergence as *independent* evidence, and an epistemic/metaphysical equivocation in the argument's "absence claim" that the article restates rather than bridges. The neutrality boast ("doesn't require dualist premises") is stronger than the reconstruction supports.

## Critiques by Philosopher

### The Eliminative Materialist (Churchland)
The whole argument is a spatial parlour trick. "Walk through the mill and you won't *find* perception" trades on the metaphor that perception, if real, would be a locatable object among the gears. That is exactly the folk-psychological reification neuroscience is destined to dissolve. You don't "find" digestion by looking for a digestion-lump among the stomach's parts either, yet digestion is nothing over and above those parts working on one another. The article's reply to the computation analogy concedes the analogy holds for computation and then simply *asserts* that "something is left over" for perception. That assertion is the entire disputed conclusion.

### The Hard-Nosed Physicalist (Dennett)
The article claims the argument "doesn't require dualist premises" because it "starts from materialist assumptions." False. Premise 3 — "among these parts we would *never* find perception" — is not a neutral observation report; it presupposes that perception is not identical to any arrangement of the parts. A physicalist denies precisely that. The mill only looks empty to someone who already expects perception to be an *extra ingredient*. Strip that expectation and the mill is full: the perception is the organised activity, viewed from inside. The article's "different kinds of discourse" move (the intuition-reliability caution) quietly re-imports the dualism it says it isn't assuming.

### The Quantum Skeptic (Tegmark)
Little purchase here — the article does not lean on quantum timing. But note the "even at molecular or quantum scales... reveals only physical processes" aside is doing rhetorical work it hasn't earned: it gestures that no *future* physics could help, which is the promissory-materialism charge turned around into promissory-dualism.

### The Many-Worlds Defender (Deutsch)
Not directly engaged by the content; no MWI-relevant claims.

### The Empiricist (Popper's Ghost)
What observation could show that perception *is* among the parts? None is specified — indeed the article says "no matter how complete our physical knowledge," the gap remains. That is an admission the thesis is insulated from any physical finding. The mill argument is presented as surviving *all possible* neuroscience; a claim no evidence could touch is metaphysics, and should be labelled as such rather than dressed as a result "derived from within" materialism.

### The Buddhist Philosopher (Nagarjuna)
Leibniz's escape hatch — the partless *monad* as the only possible subject of perception — reifies exactly what should be deconstructed. The article reports the monadology neutrally and disclaims it, but inherits its deepest assumption: that perception needs a *simple subject* to belong to. Drop the subject and the "where is it?" question dissolves; there is no one for the redness to appear *to*.

## Critical Issues

### Issue 1: Convergence treated as independent while premises are shared
- **File**: `leibnizs-mill-argument.md`
- **Location**: "Historical Lineage" section vs. "Relation to Site Perspective"
- **Problem**: The lineage section states each successor argument "isolates a different facet" but "all share the mill's core insight: physical inventory does not yield experience." The closing section then treats the fact that "Leibniz, Levine, Jackson, Nagel, Chalmers arrived at the same conclusion through different routes" as evidentially significant convergence. If the arguments share the mill's premise, they are not independent lines and their agreement double-counts a single intuition. This is the convergence-independence problem the Map's own [epistemology-of-convergence-arguments](/topics/epistemology-of-convergence-arguments/) is supposed to guard against. The article links that node approvingly without acknowledging that it *undercuts* the present case.
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Recommendation**: Add one or two sentences conceding the tension — either argue the successor arguments introduce genuinely independent premises (Jackson's epistemic framing, Chalmers' conceivability apparatus) that do more than restate the mill, or downgrade the convergence claim to "successive sharpenings of one insight" rather than independent confirmations.

### Issue 2: Epistemic/metaphysical equivocation in the "absence claim"
- **File**: `leibnizs-mill-argument.md`
- **Location**: "The Argument's Structure" (premise 3) and "The knowledge objection" response
- **Problem**: Premise 3 has an epistemic reading (inspection does not *reveal* perception to the walker) and a metaphysical reading (perception is not *constituted by* the parts). The conclusion (premise 4) needs the metaphysical reading. The knowledge objection raises exactly this ("limited epistemic position, not an ontological gap"), and the article's reply — "no matter how complete our physical knowledge, it will consist entirely of parts and their interactions, precisely what Leibniz claimed cannot explain perception" — restates the metaphysical conclusion rather than bridging from the epistemic evidence to it. Per the evidential-status discipline, evidence for the epistemic reading is being recruited to assert the metaphysical one.
- **Severity**: Medium-High (interpretive equivocation)
- **Recommendation**: `refine-draft` pass splitting the two readings explicitly and either supplying the bridging argument (why inspection-failure entails constitution-failure) or conceding the reply is a standoff of intuitions rather than a refutation.

### Issue 3: "Explain" conflated with "locate"
- **File**: `leibnizs-mill-argument.md`
- **Location**: "The inspection premise" / "The absence claim"
- **Problem**: The argument slides between "we would never *find* perception" (spatial/locational) and "never anything by which to *explain* a perception" (explanatory). Explanation is not spatial location; many real explananda (computation, per the article's own example) are not "found" as objects yet are fully explained. The vividness the article praises comes partly from this conflation — the mill makes us look for a *thing*, then reports its absence as an explanatory failure.
- **Severity**: Low-Medium
- **Recommendation**: Acknowledge that the locational framing is a heuristic, and that the real claim is about explanatory transparency (Levine's point), not about failing to spot an object.

## Counterarguments to Address

### The neutrality boast
- **Current content says**: "It doesn't require dualist premises. The argument starts from materialist assumptions... and derives a problem for materialism from within."
- **A critic would argue**: The absence claim is not materialist-neutral; it presupposes perception is not identical to any arrangement of parts, which is the dualist thesis. The argument is valid only if that premise is granted, so it does not derive the problem "from within" materialism.
- **Suggested response**: Weaken to "the argument does not *explicitly* invoke a dualist ontology," and add a sentence conceding that physicalists reject the absence claim precisely because they identify perception with the organised activity.

### The digestion/life parallel
- **Current content says**: The wetness analogy fails because wetness is structurally explicable while felt quality is not.
- **A critic would argue**: Vitalists once said the same about life — "walk through the cell, you find only chemistry, never *life itself*." The gap closed. Why is the phenomenal gap different in kind rather than merely not-yet-closed?
- **Suggested response**: The article gestures at this with the "promissory materialism" reply but does not confront the *historical* track record of dissolved gaps. A sentence distinguishing the phenomenal case from the life/heat cases would strengthen it.
