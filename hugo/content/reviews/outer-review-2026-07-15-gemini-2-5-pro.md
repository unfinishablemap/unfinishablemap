## Executive Summary of the Manuscript's Theoretical Deficits

Navigating the notoriously treacherous waters of Jaegwon Kim's causal exclusion argument, the author posits that a non-physical experiential state can serve as the "authoritative" cause of a physical effect while its corresponding physical neural correlate remains merely "sufficient" in the counterfactual sense. To substantiate this thesis, the manuscript draws heavily upon contemporary and historical scholarship, specifically invoking Bradford Saad's delegatory dualism, Bram Vaassen's interventionist accounts, and George Bealer's major-sergeant screening-off analogies.

A rigorous audit of the manuscript's substance, however, reveals a text plagued by profound scholarly, exegetical, and dialectical frailties that render it entirely unfit for publication in a leading philosophical venue. The manuscript engages in a persistent pattern of theoretical gerrymandering. It surgically strips rigorous causal models of their requisite ontological dependencies, forcibly welds them to incompatible, highly speculative quantum-collapse mechanisms, and systematically evades the very metaphysical critiques it introduces by retreating into dogmatic, undefended postulates bracketed by its own idiosyncratic commitments. The resulting paper is less a novel contribution to the mental causation debate and more a Procrustean bed into which diverse, mutually exclusive philosophical frameworks have been violently coerced. ## The Metaphysical Architecture of Trumping Preemption and Dialectical Evasion

To understand the magnitude of the manuscript's failure, one must first trace the historical and conceptual lineage of trumping preemption and how the manuscript attempts to wield it against the causal exclusion problem. Jaegwon Kim's canonical exclusion argument posits that four principles are jointly inconsistent: the reality of mental causation, the causal closure of the physical domain, the irreducibility of the mental, and the ban on systematic causal overdetermination. Non-reductive physicalists and dualists alike are thus forced into a trilemma: accept epiphenomenalism, embrace reductionism, or swallow the bitter pill of ubiquitous, coincidental overdetermination.

The manuscript attempts to thread this needle by invoking Jonathan Schaffer's (2000) paper "Trumping Preemption," published in the *Journal of Philosophy* (97: 165–181). Schaffer introduced trumping as a fourth species of redundant causation, distinct from early preemption, late preemption, and symmetric overdetermination. In Schaffer's originating thought experiment, Merlin and Morgana both cast spells to turn a prince into a frog. The laws of magic dictate that the first spell of the day is efficacious. Merlin casts at noon, Morgana at 6:00 PM; at midnight, the prince transforms. Counterfactual dependence fails in both directions, as the prince would have transformed had either wizard abstained, yet our causal intuitions firmly select Merlin as the cause. Schaffer's insight was that the laws of nature (or magic) can resolve causal ties between two completed, sufficient processes by conferring "authority" on one while sidelining the other.

The manuscript correctly identifies this separation of *sufficiency* from *authority* as a potent structural template for dualist mental causation. If a psychophysical law selects the mental state as the authoritative cause of a bodily movement, the physical brain state can remain counterfactually sufficient without resulting in brute overdetermination. However, the manuscript's execution of this strategy collapses under the weight of its own metaphysical evasions.

Since Schaffer's publication, trumping has been subjected to intense scrutiny, primarily regarding whether it constitutes a genuinely distinct causal kind. David Lewis (2000) famously responded to Schaffer with his "Causation as Influence" framework (also in the *Journal of Philosophy* 97: 182–197), arguing that fine-grained alterations to the time or manner of the cause would reveal a hidden dependence. Later, Alyssa Bernstein's critiques—specifically her 2014 paper in *Philosophia* (42(4): 935–948) and her 2015 paper in *Acta Analytica* (30(1): 41–57)—persuasively argued that Schafferian trumping reduces to either early preemption with a hidden cutting point or, more damagingly, symmetric overdetermination with causally insensitive effects.

The manuscript correctly acknowledges Bernstein's critique, explicitly noting the devastating implication for its own project: if trumping merely collapses into overdetermination, then a dualist who appeals to trumping has not solved the causal exclusion problem, but merely relocated Kim's overdetermination worry. Given that the entire manuscript is predicated on using trumping to *avoid* overdetermination, one would expect a rigorous, original metaphysical defense of trumping as a *sui generis* causal structure against Bernstein's reductionist critique.

Instead, the manuscript engages in an astonishing act of philosophical abdication. It concedes the potential collapse of trumping into overdetermination and retreats into an undefended postulate, stating that its response would simply be "Mills/Lowe-style — overdetermination between mental and physical is non-coincidental because of systematic psychophysical laws". This refers to Eugene Mills (1996) and E.J. Lowe (2000), who argued that systematic mind-body dependencies render overdetermination benign.

This concession fundamentally destroys the manuscript's central thesis. If the author's ultimate fallback defense against the exclusion problem is to simply bite the bullet and stipulate that systematic overdetermination is philosophically acceptable, then the entire extensive detour through Schaffer's trumping preemption is dialectically inert. The manuscript utilizes the complex architecture of trumping merely as window dressing. When pressed on the metaphysical viability of that architecture, the text abandons it entirely in favor of brute-force overdetermination justified by fiat. A manuscript cannot claim to solve a deep metaphysical problem using a novel structural mechanism, admit that the mechanism fails under scrutiny, and then claim victory by stipulating that the original problem was never actually problematic due to pre-existing framework commitments. Such a maneuver is the hallmark of dogmatism, not rigorous philosophical inquiry.

## The Misappropriation of Bradford Saad and the Quantum Category Error

The load-bearing pillar of the manuscript's positive proposal is its reliance on Bradford Saad's delegatory dualism, articulated in his 2025 paper "A Dualist Theory of Experience," published in *Philosophical Studies* (182(3-4): 939–967). The manuscript's attribution of the venue, date, and volume for Saad's work is demonstrably accurate. Furthermore, the text correctly identifies the mechanics of Saad's preemptive dualism. It notes that Saad's framework operates via two fundamental laws: a *Subset Law**, which assigns experiences to physical states with matching causal profiles, and a *Delegatory Law*, which dictates that when a match occurs, the experience preempts the physical state's causal contribution and becomes the authoritative cause of the subsequent behavior.

The manuscript also accurately reports that Saad's framework replaces the strict, universal causal closure of the physical with "Observational Closure," a weaker constraint permitting unobservable violations of physical closure while maintaining strict empirical adequacy.

| Causal Framework Attribute | Universal Causal Closure (Standard) | Observational Closure (Saad 2025) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Metaphysical Requirement** | Every physical event has a sufficient, strictly physical cause. | Every physical event *appears* empirically to have a sufficient physical cause. |
| **Exclusion Problem Status** | Generates the trilemma; mental causation requires overdetermination or reduction. | Dissolves the trilemma; unobservable mental preemption is permitted. |
| **Empirical Detectability** | No violations of physical laws are permitted at any scale. | Violations occur, but are systematically hidden beneath existing physical trajectories. |

However, having correctly outlined Saad's structural logic, the manuscript commits a severe scholarly malpractice by artificially assimilating Saad's highly abstract, theory-neutral model into a parochial and empirically speculative commitment to quantum wave-function collapse.

Saad's delegatory dualism is fundamentally a structural thesis concerning the formal mapping of causal profiles. It is explicitly engineered to remain neutral regarding the lower-level physical mechanisms that might underwrite Observational Closure. In Saad's framework, the delegation of causal authority from a physical state to an experiential state is governed by sui generis psychophysical laws that require no specific quantum-mechanical interpretation to function logically.

By contrast, the manuscript attempts to "solve" the interaction problem by insisting that this causal delegation physically occurs at the locus of quantum indeterminacy, claiming that consciousness biases undetermined quantum outcomes without injecting energy, frequently citing Henry Stapp's Quantum Zeno Effect and Penrose-Hameroff's Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch-OR). The text explicitly posits a "delegation-selection bridge" arguing that Saad's default causal profile "just is the Born-rule distribution," thereby making delegation the structure and quantum selection the mechanism of a single causal event.

This conflates a macroscopic structural causal model with a highly specific, heavily contested microphysical hypothesis. The manuscript effectively uses Saad's rigorous formal architecture to epistemically launder the speculative physics of quantum interactionism. Saad's Observational Closure accommodates interactionist dualism precisely because it brackets the physics, demonstrating that formal models of preemption can satisfy the constraints of mental causation purely at the level of property matching. The manuscript shatters that careful bracketing by forcing a "Minimal Quantum Interaction" requirement onto the delegatory framework—a move that Saad's 2025 paper neither supports, requires, nor entails. The failure to distinguish between a structural causal template and a highly contingent microphysical implementation represents a profound category error, revealing a reliance on framings merely bracketed by the author's prior commitments rather than organic derivations from the cited literature.

## The Superficial Framing of Bram Vaassen's Interventionism

The prompt specifically mandates an assessment of how the manuscript characterizes Bram Vaassen's 2024 paper, "Mental Causation for Standard Dualists," published in the *Australasian Journal of Philosophy* (102(4): 978–998), and whether it correctly frames his view as an interventionist "both are causes" account rather than a case of preemption.

At a purely taxonomic level, the manuscript passes this test. It correctly identifies the venue, date, and volume of the publication. Furthermore, the research notes embedded within the manuscript explicitly acknowledge that Vaassen (2024) develops an interventionist defense of standard dualism where both mental and physical states are causes with *no preemption involved*, correctly situating it as a contrast case to the trumping route that belongs to the "lawful-co-causation" family.

Taxonomic correctness, however, does not equate to philosophical comprehension. The manuscript categorizes Vaassen as merely another flavor of dualist mental causation, failing entirely to grasp the devastating implications of Vaassen's methodology for the manuscript's own trumping-based project.

| Feature | Trumping Preemption (Schaffer/Saad) | Insensitive Interventionism (Vaassen) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Causal Relationship** | Competition; one cause must preempt the other. | Cooperative; both variables exhibit robust correlation. |
| **Causal Sufficiency** | Relies heavily on traditional metaphysical definitions of sufficiency. | Discards strict physical necessity in favor of dependency and counterfactual manipulation. |
| **Resolution of Exclusion** | Reallocates causal authority via a higher-order meta-law. | Dissolves exclusion by relaxing "holding fixed" conditions in causal modeling. |
| **Ontological Heaviness** | Requires "oomphy" productive causation to necessitate a trumping mechanism. | Operates on lightweight, difference-making dependency structures. |

Vaassen (2024) secures dualist mental causation not by playing within the traditional rules of the Kimian exclusion argument—rules which demand a strict account of "causal sufficiency" and physical "oomph"—but by adopting a lightweight, dependency-based interventionist framework. Vaassen develops "insensitive interventionism," which deliberately weakens the "holding fixed" requirements of standard Woodwardian interventionism to allow higher-level mental phenomena to act as legitimate causes in virtue of exhibiting robust patterns of correlation, completely independent of physical production or strict metaphysical sufficiency.

The manuscript's entire architecture is built on Schafferian trumping preemption, a concept whose sole philosophical utility in this context is to separate *causal sufficiency* (retained by the physical base) from *causal authority* (granted to the mental state). Yet, if one adopts the interventionist paradigm that Vaassen champions, the very notion of "causal sufficiency" that generates the exclusion problem—and thus necessitates the highly complex trumping solution—dissolves entirely. As Vaassen makes explicitly clear in his work, lightweight interventionist accounts do not require causes to physically necessitate or suffice for their effects in the traditional, production-heavy sense.

By citing Vaassen merely as an alternative "co-causation" outcome, the manuscript ignores that Vaassen's interventionist framework renders the manuscript's own trumping solution structurally obsolete. The author attempts to hold onto the heavy, productive metaphysics of causal sufficiency (to make the trumping preemption mechanism necessary) while simultaneously giving a passing nod to Vaassen's interventionism as valid parallel scholarship. The manuscript utterly fails to realize that these two frameworks rely on mutually exclusive theories of causation. One cannot invoke an interventionist co-causation model to legitimize the dualist enterprise while simultaneously resting one's actual thesis on a preemption model designed specifically to solve problems that interventionism denies exist.

## The Anachronistic Extrapolation of George Bealer (2007)

The manuscript's historical and scholarly credibility is further damaged by its treatment of George Bealer. The author cites Bealer's 2007 paper, "Mental Causation," published in *Philosophical Perspectives* (21(1): 23–54), lauding it as the "earliest sustained preemption-based account of mental causation in contemporary literature" and highlighting Bealer's use of the major-sergeant hierarchical command analogy to explain how mental causes can preempt physical ones.

The citation details are accurate, but the philosophical attribution constitutes an egregious decontextualization of Bealer's project. Bealer's 2007 paper does utilize a major-sergeant screening-off test, borrowed from Bas van Fraassen, to illustrate how one might empirically isolate an authoritative cause when faced with simultaneous, ostensibly sufficient events.

However, Bealer is explicitly working within the confines of *nonreductive functionalism*—or at most, a highly specific property dualism—predicated on his earlier (1997) "Self-Consciousness" arguments against standard ontological functionalism. More damningly, Bealer's 2007 account is strictly predicated on the assumption of *weak causal closure*—the premise that every actual event has some physical event causally sufficient for it. Bealer explicitly identifies mental properties with the unique properties that make an appropriately general psychological theory true, arguing that primary psychological laws hold with metaphysical necessity.

The manuscript under review advocates for an interactionist substance dualism that explicitly *denies* causal closure at the locus of quantum indeterminacies. To rip Bealer's epistemological screening-off test out of its nonreductive functionalist, weak-closure context, and blindly transplant it into a substance dualist, quantum-interactionist framework that rejects the very premises Bealer assumed, borders on intellectual dishonesty.

The manuscript treats the major-sergeant analogy as a floating metaphysical mechanism that can be plugged into any ontology, ignoring the strict logical dependencies Bealer established to make the screening-off test function in his 2007 paper. Bealer's model relies on the metaphysical necessity of psychological laws to ensure that the mental state acts as the true cause during counterfactual substitution; the manuscript relies on contingent psychophysical matching (Saad) intersecting with stochastic quantum mechanics (Stapp/Penrose). These frameworks are mathematically and metaphysically incompatible, rendering the manuscript's invocation of Bealer anachronistic and highly misleading.

## Specific Weaknesses and Omissions Against the 2020–2025 Literature

Each point highlights a critical omission or mishandling of peer-reviewed sources from this period, exposing untested counterarguments and confirming the manuscript's reliance on undefended prior commitments.

### 1. Mishandling of Saad (2025) via Unwarranted Quantum Assimilation

The manuscript fatally misrepresents the ontological commitments of Bradford Saad's delegatory dualism. The text asserts that Saad's model acts as the causal mechanism for its own "Minimal Quantum Interaction" tenet, forcing a microphysical wave-function collapse mechanism onto a theory explicitly engineered to remain agnostic about physical implementation.

- **Source Mishandled:** Saad, B. (2025). "A Dualist Theory of Experience." *Philosophical Studies* 182(3-4): 939–967. Saad rigorously defines "Observational Closure" as permitting unobservable physical violations, but he does *not* require those violations to occur via quantum indeterminacy or any specific microphysical mechanism. The manuscript's aggressive co-optation of Saad to legitimize highly speculative quantum biology (e.g., Stapp's Zeno Effect) constitutes a severe misreading of Saad's structural methodology and a reliance on framings merely bracketed by the author's own prior dualist commitments.

### 2. Omission of Vaassen (2021) on Causal Sufficiency

The manuscript's central theoretical problem—the need to meticulously separate causal *sufficiency* from causal *authority* using the complex machinery of trumping preemption—relies entirely on an antiquated reading of the exclusion argument. It ignores vital recent literature demonstrating that strict causal sufficiency is a flawed and entirely unnecessary requirement for formulating or solving the exclusion problem.

- **Source Omitted:** Vaassen, B. (2021). "Causal Exclusion without Causal Sufficiency." *Synthese* 198: 10341–10353. Vaassen demonstrates that traditional exclusion arguments rely on a highly problematic notion of causal sufficiency (requiring causes to be spatially enormous and maximally specific), and that valid, modern exclusion arguments can and should be formulated without it. By omitting this foundational work, the manuscript wastes thousands of words attempting to bypass a concept of "sufficiency" that the contemporary philosophy of causation has largely discarded.

### 3. Omission of Vaassen (2022) on Proportionality Constraints

The manuscript asserts that mental states can usurp causal authority from their physical bases via a "Delegatory Law," but it completely fails to address the proportionality of those mental states to their physical effects. When dealing with high-level causation versus micro-level physical realizers, contemporary philosophy of causation requires a rigorous account of proportionality to avoid arbitrary or overly broad causal attributions—an issue the manuscript's reliance on a simple "Subset Law*" handwaves.

- **Source Omitted:** Vaassen, B. (2022). "Halfway Proportionality." *Philosophical Studies* 179(9): 1–21. Vaassen analyzes the proportionality principle—that causes should be neither too general nor too specific for their effects—arguing that while half of the principle is overly demanding, the other half is vital for providing a nuanced picture of higher-level causation. The manuscript's failure to engage with the proportionality literature leaves its application of causal delegation completely untethered from the actual constraints of difference-making accounts of causation.

### 4. Contradiction of Vaassen's (2024) Interventionist Assumptions

While the manuscript correctly labels Bram Vaassen's 2024 account as "co-causation" rather than preemption, it fundamentally misunderstands the causal semantics required to make Vaassen's account function, resulting in a deep ontological contradiction within the text.

- **Source Mishandled:** Vaassen, B. (2024). "Mental Causation for Standard Dualists." *Australasian Journal of Philosophy* 102(4): 978–998. The manuscript assumes a production-heavy, "oomphy" theory of causation to generate the necessity for trumping preemption, while simultaneously citing Vaassen as a parallel valid model. However, Vaassen's "insensitive interventionism" explicitly denies that causes must physically necessitate their effects. The manuscript contradicts Vaassen's core thesis by forcing his interventionist model into a broader architectural framework that presupposes the very strict physical sufficiency that Vaassen's methodology was explicitly designed to dismantle.

### 5. Omission of Zhong (2023) and Competing Non-Reductive Frameworks

The manuscript operates as though Schafferian trumping preemption and brute-force Mills/Lowe overdetermination are the only viable metaphysical avenues for securing mental causation against physicalist reductionism. It entirely ignores contemporary, bleeding-edge non-reductive physicalist arguments that successfully bypass the exclusion problem without resorting to the baroque complexities of preemption or the empirically perilous waters of interactionist substance dualism.

- **Source Omitted:** Zhong, L. (2023). "A new causal argument for physicalism." *Asian Journal of Philosophy* 2(54). Zhong provides a sophisticated, modern causal argument that establishes a non-reductive physicalism (where mental phenomena are metaphysically necessitated by physical phenomena without being identical to them) without falling prey to the traditional flaws of the exclusion argument or relying on outdated difference-making accounts. The manuscript's complete failure to address such competing, highly recent non-reductive frameworks renders its aggressive defense of trumping preemption myopic, incomplete, and dialectically obsolete.
